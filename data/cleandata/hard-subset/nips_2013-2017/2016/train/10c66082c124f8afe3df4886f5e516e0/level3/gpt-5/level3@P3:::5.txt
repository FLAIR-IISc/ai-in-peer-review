Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The method learns (i) a linear transformation A applied to the word embedding space (word2vec), and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. Supervision is injected via an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective. Because exact WMD gradients would entail a costly nested LP, the paper uses the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) problem as a tractable proxy to compute approximate gradients. The dual variables (α, β) are recovered from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors to derive gradients w.r.t. A and w. Practical speedups include initializing A with a supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) metric learned on document centroids and restricting neighborhoods using a cheap WCD prefilter. On 8 datasets against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised), S-WMD reports the best average kNN performance.

The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15) and Cuturi’s entropy-regularized OT (NIPS’13), and relates to metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and prior attempts to learn ground metrics for EMD/OT (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.). Its novelty lies in bringing supervised OT-based distances to document classification by jointly learning A and w with an NCA objective and Sinkhorn-based gradients.

Strengths
- Empirical thoroughness: evaluated on 8 diverse datasets vs 26 strong baselines, with careful hyperparameter tuning and code release. S-WMD attains the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets.
- Technical clarity: the paper is very well written, with clean derivations for the A and w gradients and a pragmatic description of Sinkhorn-based computation and batching.
- Practicality: the combination of Sinkhorn, WCD-based neighbor filtering, and S-WCD initialization yields a method that is significantly faster than exact WMD-based learning while retaining strong accuracy.
- Interpretability: learned word weights (w) provide insight into task-relevant vocabulary.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Limited novelty relative to WMD and existing OT/metric-learning literature. The core ideas—learn a (generalized) Euclidean ground metric and use entropic OT for speed—are known; the contribution is primarily an effective instantiation for text with a solid training objective.
- Entropic regularization (λ) is under-discussed. In Eq. (2) the −(1/λ)h(T) term implies that λ directly controls both approximation bias and computational behavior. The paper fixes λ=10 without justification or sensitivity analysis. Yet λ can dominate the learned distances and gradients:
  - Small λ (strong entropy) yields blurry transport and can materially change the geometry—this is no longer close to true Wasserstein.
  - Large λ (weak entropy) behaves more like unregularized WMD but may slow convergence and affect numerical stability.
  - Most practitioners choose small λ for speed, which biases the model toward the entropic/Sinkhorn regime rather than pure Wasserstein. The paper should clarify which regime S-WMD effectively operates in and how that impacts behavior and results.
  - Please provide: (i) accuracy vs λ and time vs λ curves; (ii) a discussion of whether λ is tuned per dataset; (iii) guidance on stable scaling and stopping criteria.
- Objective mismatch and clarity: the loss is written in terms of DA,w (WMD), but gradients are computed with the Sinkhorn solution T*λ and corresponding duals. Please make explicit whether the distances in the NCA probabilities are also entropic (i.e., consistent with the gradients), or quantify the impact of this approximation.
- Word-importance vector w: the paper does not state whether w is constrained to be nonnegative. Since d̃ involves normalization, negative entries could be problematic. Recommend enforcing w≥0 (e.g., via projection or exp-parameterization) and reporting whether sparsity or regularization on w is used.
- Overuse and framing of WCD: While WCD is a standard lower bound and a useful initializer/filter, repeated references to Word Centroid Distance and the introduction of S-WCD as a baseline are somewhat distracting and risk confusing readers about the main contribution. Tighten the presentation to emphasize WCD’s role as an engineering device rather than a conceptual pillar.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics:
  - Separate effects of learning A vs w (e.g., A-only, w-only, both).
  - Sensitivity to rank of A and to neighbor set size M used in gradient approximation.
  - Comparison to exact WMD gradients/subgradients on small problems to validate the proxy.
  - Clarify the choice of k in kNN and whether it is tuned consistently across methods.

Quality
Overall technically sound with clear math and sensible engineering choices. The λ issue is a notable hole given its centrality to Eq. (2). Empirics are strong and fairly conducted, but additional ablations would increase confidence.

Clarity
Excellent exposition, notation, and organization. Main suggested clarity improvements: explicitly align the optimized distance with the approximated gradients; detail the λ trade-offs and selection; clarify constraints on w; de-emphasize WCD to avoid confusion.

Originality
Moderate. The work combines known ingredients (NCA, OT metric learning, Sinkhorn) in a new, effective way for document distances. Incremental conceptual novelty; strong practical impact.

Significance
High practical significance for kNN-style document retrieval/classification and as a supervised alternative to WMD. The empirical wins and efficiency suggest adoption value. The work also helps bridge OT and NLP.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical results across many datasets/baselines
- Clear, well-motivated method with practical speedups
- Interpretable learned weights; code available
- Advances supervised OT-based document metrics

Cons for acceptance
- Limited conceptual novelty over WMD and prior OT metric-learning
- Insufficient treatment of λ and the entropic vs Wasserstein behavior
- Some objective/implementation details under-specified (constraints on w, loss/gradient consistency)
- Overemphasis on WCD may confuse readers

Constructive suggestions
- Add λ sensitivity analysis and guidance; discuss the method’s regime (entropic vs near-Wasserstein) and its implications.
- Report ablations for A vs w, rank of A, neighbor truncation M, and k in kNN.
- Clarify constraints/regularization on w and A; consider an ℓ1 penalty on w and Frobenius/low-rank constraints on A.
- Validate the Sinkhorn-gradient proxy on small instances with exact WMD.
- Streamline discussion of WCD to its practical roles (initialization, filtering).
