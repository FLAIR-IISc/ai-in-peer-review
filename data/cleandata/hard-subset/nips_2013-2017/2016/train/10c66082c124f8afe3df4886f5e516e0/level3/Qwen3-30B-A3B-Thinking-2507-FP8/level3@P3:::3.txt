

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (NIPS 2016)

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that learns a supervised metric by parameterizing the ground cost matrix with a Mahalanobis distance and learning histogram reweighting coefficients. The authors build upon the WMD framework (Kusner et al., 2015), which computes document distances as optimal transport problems between word embeddings, and introduce a novel supervised learning approach that minimizes leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error.

The main contribution is a practical and efficient optimization method that provides gradients for NCA-type objectives applied to the WMD framework. Rather than directly computing the expensive nested optimization for WMD gradients, the authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance approximation (Cuturi, 2013) to achieve O(q²) complexity instead of O(q³ log q). The paper also introduces a histogram reweighting vector w that emphasizes words important for classification, addressing the limitation of the unsupervised WMD.

The paper's strongest aspect is its compelling empirical evaluation across eight text classification datasets. The authors compare against 26 baselines, including multiple document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with supervised metric learning methods (NCA, ITML, LMNN). S-WMD consistently outperforms all baselines on five of eight datasets and achieves the lowest average error across all datasets. The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) demonstrates that the method successfully identifies semantically relevant words for classification tasks.

The paper's approach is technically sound, combining existing techniques in a straightforward manner. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their method extends the WMD framework, and the optimization strategy is well-justified. The algorithm is efficient, with training times comparable to or better than baseline metric learning methods.

However, the paper has several areas for improvement. First, there is a notational inconsistency: Equation (1) uses the Euclidean distance (‖x_i - x_j‖), while Equation (6) uses squared Euclidean (‖A(x_i - x_j)‖²₂), making it unclear whether the authors are using 1-Wasserstein or 2-Wasserstein. The paper should explicitly describe the WMD as p-Wasserstein to avoid confusion.

Second, the mathematical derivations could be more rigorous. The authors claim that the gradient with respect to A can be computed as in Equation (8), but the proof is not provided. The paper should include a more detailed derivation of the gradient expressions, particularly for the dual variables. An algorithmic box would also greatly improve clarity.

Third, the reweighting mechanism in Equation (5) is not novel. The authors should cite Lebanon's work on histogram reweighting (Lebanon, 2006) and the related work by Le and Cuturi (2014) on histogram reweighting for optimal transport.

Fourth, the paper could better address the interplay between regularized and unregularized optimal transport formulations. The authors use the Sinkhorn approximation (regularized) for efficiency, but they don't discuss how this affects the optimization objective compared to the unregularized WMD.

Fifth, the paper lacks insights into the empirical importance of the learned weight vector w. The authors should analyze which words receive high weights on different datasets and explain why these words are particularly informative for classification.

Finally, the related work section should more clearly distinguish S-WMD from Cuturi & Avis (2014) and Wang et al. (2015). While Wang et al. learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance, they do not consider histogram reweighting, which is a key differentiator in this work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the document similarity literature. The idea is simple yet effective, combining existing techniques in a novel way to solve an important problem. The empirical results are compelling and thoroughly evaluated. The paper is well-written, and the method is efficient and easy to implement.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational inconsistencies, mathematical rigor, and related work clarity. The paper's main contribution - learning a supervised metric for WMD through Mahalanobis distance and histogram reweighting - is significant and well-supported by the experiments. The authors have made a meaningful advancement in the field of document classification metrics, and I believe this work will be influential in future research on text representation and metric learning.
