

This paper proposes a supervised extension of Word Mover's Distance (WMD) called Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), which learns word-specific weights and uses Sinkhorn distance as a faster approximation to the Wasserstein distance. The authors frame the problem as minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error while learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights. The method is evaluated on 8 text classification datasets against 26 baselines, reporting best average performance.

The paper's strengths are significant: it is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical exposition, the evaluation is comprehensive across multiple datasets and baselines, and the implementation details are thorough. The authors demonstrate that their approach consistently outperforms both unsupervised WMD and various document representation methods.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that affect its contribution. The novelty is limited compared to prior WMD work, as the core idea of incorporating supervision into optimal transport distances is not substantially new. More critically, the paper poorly discusses the role of the entropic regularizer λ (equation 2), despite its dominant effect on the method's behavior. The manuscript fails to clarify whether the method operates closer to pure Wasserstein (large λ) or Sinkhorn (small λ), which is crucial for understanding the method's behavior. Most users employ small λ for speed, but the paper does not explain how this choice impacts results or provide guidance on λ selection.

The references to Word Centroid Distance (WCD) are confusing and irrelevant, as WCD is a lower-bound approximation to WMD, not a related technique that informs the proposed method. This creates unnecessary confusion for readers.

Regarding the four key criteria: Quality is good with solid experimental results, but the lack of discussion on λ undermines the theoretical foundation. Clarity is excellent overall, though the WCD references and λ discussion detract from this. Originality is limited, as the work builds incrementally on prior WMD research without sufficient differentiation. Significance is moderate, as the method shows consistent improvements but doesn't address a fundamentally new problem.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the λ parameter and removing irrelevant references to WCD. While the method is practical and well-executed, the limited novelty and theoretical gaps prevent it from being a transformative contribution to the field.
