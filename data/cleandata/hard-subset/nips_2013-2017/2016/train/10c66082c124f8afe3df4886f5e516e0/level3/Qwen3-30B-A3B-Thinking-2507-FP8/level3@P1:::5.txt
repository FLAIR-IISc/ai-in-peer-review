

This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) called Supervised WMD (S-WMD), which learns word-specific importance weights and uses a Sinkhorn approximation to efficiently compute the distance. The authors frame the problem as learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and a word importance vector to minimize leave-one-out kNN classification error. The paper builds directly on Kusner et al.'s WMD work [19] while introducing supervised learning through metric learning techniques.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming 26 baselines across 8 text classification tasks. The evaluation is thorough, with consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD (reducing errors by up to 51% on some datasets), and the method achieves the lowest average error across all datasets. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between computational efficiency and approximation quality, with training times comparable to other metric learning approaches.

The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical exposition. The technical details of the gradient computation and the Sinkhorn approximation are presented with appropriate rigor, making the method reproducible. The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) effectively demonstrates how the method captures class-relevant words.

However, the paper's novelty is limited compared to the prior WMD work [19]. The core idea of extending WMD with supervision is a natural progression, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from the original WMD framework. The authors' claim that "this paper proposes an algorithm for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover's Distance" understates the incremental nature of the contribution, as the supervised metric learning approach directly extends existing metric learning techniques [15, 36] to the WMD framework.

The manuscript contains several confusing references to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) that don't advance the core argument. The paper repeatedly discusses "S-WCD" as an initialization method, but the WCD is a lower bound of WMD that the authors themselves acknowledge is inferior to the WMD in their prior work [19]. This creates unnecessary confusion about the relationship between the proposed method and prior work.

Most critically, the role and choice of the entropic regularizer parameter λ are poorly discussed. The paper states "we use λ = 10" but provides no justification for this choice or analysis of its impact on results. The value of λ dramatically affects the behavior of the method: large λ approaches the pure Wasserstein distance while small λ yields the Sinkhorn approximation. The authors don't clarify whether they're using a value that approximates the true WMD (large λ) or prioritizes speed (small λ), which is crucial for understanding their method's behavior. This omission is particularly problematic given that the entire computational efficiency hinges on this parameter choice.

The paper would benefit from a more careful discussion of the trade-offs between λ values, including ablation studies showing how performance changes with different λ settings. The authors should also clarify whether their method produces distances closer to the true WMD (with large λ) or the Sinkhorn approximation (with small λ), as these represent different computational and theoretical regimes.

The paper's significance is strong due to the consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets, making it a valuable contribution for document classification tasks. The method leverages high-quality word embeddings effectively and provides a practical approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the incremental nature of the contribution and the lack of theoretical analysis about how the supervised learning affects the underlying optimal transport problem.

In summary, the paper makes a solid empirical contribution to document classification with a well-executed experimental evaluation. The method is practical, efficient, and consistently outperforms baselines. However, the novelty is limited, and the paper would be significantly stronger with better justification for the choice of λ, clearer differentiation from the original WMD work, and more thoughtful discussion of the relationship between the Sinkhorn approximation and the true Wasserstein distance.

Strengths: Strong empirical results across multiple datasets, clear mathematical exposition, practical implementation with efficient training.

Weaknesses: Limited novelty compared to prior WMD work, poor justification for λ parameter choice, confusing references to Word Centroid Distance, lack of theoretical analysis of the supervised learning process.

Overall, this paper represents a good technical contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in document classification. It's well-executed and provides a useful tool for practitioners. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets and the practical efficiency of the method make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the novelty is incremental but the empirical results are strong.
