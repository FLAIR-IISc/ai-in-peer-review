This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), termed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), which learns weights on the distance and a linear transformation of the word embedding space. The core idea is to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error, leveraging recent advances in efficient optimal transport computation (Sinkhorn distance) to make the training tractable. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on eight datasets, consistently outperforming a wide range of baselines.

The work builds upon the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2016] as an effective document distance metric, acknowledging its unsupervised nature as a limitation. Prior work in metric learning [Davis et al., 2011; Weinberger & Saul, 2009] often suffers from scalability issues with high-dimensional text data, which S-WMD avoids by operating directly on word embeddings. While recent work has explored learning ground distances for EMD [Cuturi & Avis, 2014; Wang et al., 2016], S-WMD uniquely combines this with learning word importance weights, providing increased flexibility.  The paper also appropriately references related work in word embeddings [Mikolov et al., 2013] and document representation learning (LSI, LDA, mSDA).

**Strengths:**

* **Soundness & Technical Novelty:** The approach is technically sound and elegantly addresses the limitations of unsupervised WMD. The combination of learning a transformed word embedding space *and* word importance weights is a key contribution. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is a crucial practical element.
* **Extensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a *very* extensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets and 26 baselines. This rigorous evaluation convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD.  The ablation study with "S-WMD init" is also helpful in understanding the contribution of the full training procedure.
* **Clarity & Detail:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The method is explained with appropriate technical detail, including the derivation of the gradients and a discussion of the complexity. The inclusion of pseudocode for the algorithm is appreciated.
* **Significant Performance Improvement:** The experimental results show substantial performance gains over all baselines. The margin of improvement is significant, demonstrating that the proposed method represents a genuine advance.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of initialization strategies and batch gradient descent demonstrates awareness of practical considerations for scaling the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of the squared Euclidean distance within the optimal transport problem might be a limitation. While justifiable, a discussion of alternative distance metrics and their potential impact would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While Bayesian optimization is used for hyperparameter tuning, the paper doesn't detail the range of values explored or the sensitivity of the results to specific hyperparameter settings.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the visualization with t-SNE is helpful, a more detailed error analysis of cases where S-WMD fails would provide valuable insights.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of WMD – its unsupervised nature.
* The proposed method is technically novel and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing approaches.
* The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
* The inclusion of an efficient approximation for gradient computation makes the method practical for large-scale datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on squared Euclidean distance might limit the method's generality.
* The lack of detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is a minor concern.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically effective.  I was particularly pleased to see someone applying discriminative training to learn a domain-specific metric from WMD. The extensive experimental evaluation and clear writing further strengthen the paper. I believe this paper is suitable for presentation at NIPS and recommend acceptance.
