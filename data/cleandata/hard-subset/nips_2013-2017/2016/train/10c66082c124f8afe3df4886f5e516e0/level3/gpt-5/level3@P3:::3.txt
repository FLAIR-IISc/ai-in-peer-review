Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which learns the ground metric and histogram weights that define Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The ground metric is parameterized as a Mahalanobis distance A on word embeddings, and document histograms are reweighted by a positive vector w, producing a transport cost cij = ||A(xi − xj)||^2 and renormalized histograms d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w^T d). To fit A and w, the paper optimizes an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective where pairwise distances are WMDs. The key computational idea is to approximate gradients through the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) formulation; the authors use Sinkhorn scaling to obtain T*λ and associated dual variables, which enable efficient gradients for A and w. A practical training recipe includes an S-WCD (centroid) initialization, mini-batch optimization, and neighbor subsampling via WCD. Experiments on eight datasets versus 26 baselines show consistent improvements, highlighting the empirical strength of the approach.

Relation to prior work: WMD (Kusner et al.) frames document distance via OT over word embeddings. Entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi) provides fast O(q^2) solvers used here for training. The paper is related to ground metric learning for EMD (Cuturi & Avis) and to ITML/LMNN/NCA for Euclidean metric learning; it is closest to Wang et al., who learn an EMD ground metric for images but do not learn histogram reweighting and rely on pairwise constraints rather than a kNN/NCA objective. The reweighting of histograms itself is not new; supervised term reweighting on the simplex (e.g., Lebanon’s work on supervised learning for multinomial models/information geometry) and OT-related reweighting/linear parameterizations (e.g., Le & Cuturi) should be cited and contrasted.

Strengths
- Clear and effective proposal: learn both the OT ground cost (via Mahalanobis A) and word-importance weights w; the combination makes intuitive sense for text tasks.
- Methodologically coherent: NCA-type objective, efficient gradients via entropic OT, solid initialization with S-WCD, and neighbor subsampling for scalability.
- Empirical section is strong: broad set of datasets and 26 baselines; consistent gains with convincing visualizations; code availability increases impact.
- Practical relevance: leverages pre-trained embeddings; yields improvements without rewriting retrieval/classification pipelines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Interplay between regularized and unregularized OT: The training uses entropy-regularized OT for gradients, but the inference objective and its dependence on λ are not deeply analyzed. Please:
  - Clarify whether evaluation uses regularized or unregularized OT, and if mismatched, how bias induced by λ during training affects test-time distances.
  - Provide sensitivity/ablation over λ and number of Sinkhorn iterations; discuss convergence vs bias trade-offs.
  - Consider reporting both Sinkhorn (regularized) and unregularized OT at test time.

- Mathematical rigor and notation:
  - Some derivations (e.g., gradient for w via dual potentials) are sketched. Please tighten assumptions (uniqueness, subgradient conditions), and explicitly state when you use relaxed dual variables from Sinkhorn.
  - Add a concise algorithm box summarizing the full training pipeline (init via S-WCD, batching, WCD neighbor filtering, Sinkhorn settings, stopping criteria).
  - Notational inconsistency: Eq. 1 uses Euclidean costs while Eq. 6 is based on squared Euclidean (2-Wasserstein-type cost). To avoid confusion, describe the distance uniformly as p-Wasserstein with cost exponent ρ and specify your choice (e.g., p=1 with cost ρ=2). Make explicit whether you optimize 1-Wasserstein with quadratic ground cost or true 2-Wasserstein.

- Prior work on reweighting: The idea in Eq. 5 (learning word weights) has precedents in supervised term reweighting and metric learning on the multinomial simplex (e.g., work by Lebanon) and in OT with linear parameterizations and class-dependent weights (e.g., Le & Cuturi). Please cite and position your contributions relative to these.

- Related work clarity:
  - Cuturi & Avis: clearly differentiate from “ground metric learning” which learns a (possibly continuous) ground metric with linear parameterizations and convex formulations; also note differences in regularization (entropic vs none), discrete vocabulary setting, and the NCA objective used here.
  - Wang et al.: discuss limitations (no histogram reweighting, pairwise constraints/ITML vs kNN/NCA objective, weaker scalability to text, no Sinkhorn-based efficiency).

- Empirical analysis of w: Provide insights on the learned w beyond a word cloud. Suggested analyses:
  - Ablations: w fixed to 1, A fixed to I, and learning only one of them.
  - Stability: variation of w across folds and datasets; sparsity and concentration (are stop-words downweighted, domain-specific terms upweighted?).
  - Impact: changes in accuracy/nearest neighbors when permuting or zeroing top-weighted words.

Quality
- Generally sound and well-supported by experiments. The main technical caveat is the reliance on entropy-regularized approximations during training without a thorough analysis of their effect on the unregularized objective. Derivations could be more precise and self-contained.

Clarity
- The paper is readable and well-organized. Addressing the notation inconsistency, adding an algorithm box, and tightening derivations would significantly improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
- The contributions are a simple, effective combination of existing components (NCA, Sinkhorn OT, Mahalanobis learning, histogram reweighting). Novelty lies in bringing them together for WMD with an efficient end-to-end training recipe for text. Conceptual originality is moderate; practical novelty and execution are strong.

Significance
- Strong empirical gains across diverse datasets, with an approach likely to be adopted by practitioners working with OT/WMD for text. The method advances the state of the art in a practical and extensible way.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to λ, number of Sinkhorn iterations, and neighbor set size N? Please include sensitivity plots.
- Do you evaluate with regularized or unregularized OT at test time? If both, how do results compare?
- Provide ablations for learning only A, only w, and both; and report computational overhead of learning w.
- What is the geometric rank of A learned (effective dimensionality)? Any benefit from low-rank constraints?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance; simple, practical method; efficient optimization; clear potential impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical discussion of regularization mismatch; derivations/notation need tightening; prior art on reweighting not fully acknowledged.

Overall assessment
A straightforward, well-executed idea that combines known techniques to deliver substantial empirical improvements. With clearer treatment of regularized vs unregularized OT, more rigorous derivations, and expanded discussion of related reweighting work, the paper would be even stronger. I support acceptance.
