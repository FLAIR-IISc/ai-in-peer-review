

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (NIPS 2016)

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), extending Kusner et al.'s unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) to a supervised metric learning framework. The authors introduce two key innovations: a linear transformation matrix A applied to word embeddings and a word importance weight vector w that re-weights word frequencies in document representations. S-WMD learns these parameters to minimize stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error, using an efficient approximation based on the Sinkhorn distance to avoid the cubic time complexity of the original WMD. The method is evaluated on 8 document classification tasks, consistently outperforming 26 baselines including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and supervised metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN).

**Strengths**

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by addressing a key limitation of the original WMD: its unsupervised nature. The authors provide a theoretically sound approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, which is particularly valuable since document classification tasks often require task-specific distance metrics. The technical innovation of using the Sinkhorn distance approximation to enable efficient gradient computation is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements across diverse datasets (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification), and the authors provide a clear implementation (with Matlab code available). The paper also builds effectively on prior work, particularly the NCA framework for metric learning and the WMD itself.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper is technically sound, several points require clarification for better understanding and reproducibility:

1. The intuition behind Eq (5) (the definition of w) is not sufficiently explained. The authors should clarify how w interacts with the document representation and why it's necessary beyond the linear transformation A. More importantly, the paper does not clearly identify which component (A vs w) drives the reported improvements, which is crucial for understanding the method's mechanics.

2. There is confusion regarding the use of λ in Eq (2) versus the λ referenced at line 170. The authors should explicitly confirm whether these are the same parameter and clarify when Eq (2) is used in the optimization process.

3. The claim that S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316) requires more concrete justification. The authors should provide specific examples or additional analysis to support this claim.

4. The reference to "average error across datasets" at line 257 is unclear; it should be explicitly stated whether this value is presented in Table 3.

5. Table 2's columns "n" and "ne" need proper description to understand the reported results. Similarly, the paper lacks details on how k (for k-NN classification) was chosen—whether it was dataset-dependent or method-dependent.

6. While the authors clarified that comparing against simple classifiers was irrelevant (as the focus is on distance metrics), the paper could better emphasize how S-WMD complements rather than replaces standard classification pipelines.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper demonstrates high technical quality with a well-supported theoretical foundation and robust experimental results. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets, with the reported improvements being statistically significant in most cases. The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a practical, efficient approach to learning document distances that can be applied to numerous text classification tasks. The ability to leverage high-quality unsupervised word embeddings while incorporating task-specific supervision represents an important step forward in document representation learning.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but the ambiguities mentioned above reduce its clarity. The mathematical formulations are precise, but the implementation details and interpretation of results would benefit from greater elaboration. The authors' careful clarifications in the rebuttal addressed many concerns, demonstrating their thorough understanding of the work.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The S-WMD approach successfully extends the promising WMD framework to a supervised setting with meaningful improvements across multiple benchmarks. While some implementation details require clarification, these are minor concerns that could be addressed in the final version. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the field of metric learning for text.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper addresses an important limitation of the WMD framework with a novel and effective solution, demonstrated through rigorous evaluation. The method's efficiency and consistent performance across diverse datasets make it a valuable addition to the NIPS 2016 proceedings.
