This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), extending the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to a supervised learning setting. The authors address the lack of supervision in the original WMD by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights, minimizing leave-one-out kNN classification error. The core contribution lies in an efficient approximation to the gradient computation, enabling practical application despite the computational challenges of the WMD. The paper is well-motivated, building on recent work in document distances (Kusner et al., 2016) and metric learning, and demonstrates strong performance across eight datasets, consistently outperforming numerous baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently superior performance compared to a comprehensive set of 26 baselines across diverse datasets. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD.
* **Practical Approximation:** The efficient gradient approximation is a significant contribution, making the application of supervised learning to WMD feasible.  This avoids the prohibitive computational cost of directly optimizing the original WMD objective.
* **Clear Motivation & Connection to Prior Work:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of unsupervised approaches like WMD and position their work within the broader context of metric learning and document distance techniques.
* **Code Release:**  The promised release of Matlab code will significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.
* **Careful Rebuttal:** The reviewer notes positive experience with the authors' clarifications during rebuttal, suggesting responsiveness and a willingness to address concerns.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Intuition for ‘w’:** The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the word importance weights (w). While Eq. (4) is defined, a deeper discussion of *why* certain words might be upweighted for specific tasks would be valuable.  The connection between these weights and potential biases in word embeddings also isn't explored.
* **A vs. w Contribution:** The review correctly points out the need to dissect the contribution of the linear transformation (A) versus the word weights (w) to the observed improvements. While the results demonstrate overall improvement, understanding the individual impact of each component would provide further insight.  An ablation study focusing on this aspect would be highly valuable.
* **Lack of Justification for ‘Forgiving’ Claim:** The claim that S-WMD is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” (ln316) lacks sufficient justification.  While intuitively plausible, providing examples or a more detailed explanation of how the learned metric achieves this would strengthen the argument.
* **Missing ‘Average Error’ Reference:** The missing reference to the “average error across datasets” (ln257) is a minor editorial issue but should be addressed. Checking its presence in Table 3 as suggested is a good starting point.
* **Table 2 Clarification:** Defining the “n” and “ne” columns in Table 2 is crucial for understanding the experimental setup. This is a straightforward clarification that needs to be included.
* **k Selection:** The lack of discussion about how ‘k’ (for k-NN classification) was chosen introduces a potential confounding factor. Was it dataset-dependent, method-dependent, or fixed across all experiments? Reporting the selection process and potentially performing a sensitivity analysis would improve rigor.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution and compelling results. The technical details are sound, and the authors address a relevant problem in document distance learning. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding explanation and justification, the overall quality of the work is high. 

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Significant performance improvements over numerous baselines.
* Practical and efficient algorithm for supervised WMD learning.
* Well-motivated and connected to existing literature.
* Code release promised, fostering reproducibility.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* Limited intuition provided for the learned word weights.
* Lack of dissection of the contributions of A and w.
* Insufficient justification for key claims regarding conceptual similarity.
* Minor clarity issues regarding referenced data and experimental setup.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The authors should address the points raised regarding intuition for 'w', the relative contributions of 'A' and 'w', justification of the “forgiving” claim, clarification of Table 2, and the k-selection process. Addressing these concerns will further strengthen an already high-quality paper.
