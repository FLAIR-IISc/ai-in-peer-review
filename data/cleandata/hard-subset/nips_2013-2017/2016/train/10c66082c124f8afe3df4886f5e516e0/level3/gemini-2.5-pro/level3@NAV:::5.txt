This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document metric. Building upon the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which frames document similarity as an optimal transport problem, this work extends it to the supervised setting by learning an affine transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights. To make the optimization tractable, the authors leverage the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for the WMD, allowing them to minimize a stochastic nearest-neighbor classification loss. The proposed method is evaluated extensively on eight text classification datasets, where it achieves the best average performance against 26 competitive baselines.

The primary strengths of this paper are its exceptional clarity and strong empirical results. The mathematical exposition is thorough and well-presented, making the proposed method easy to understand and reproduce. The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation that convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach, outperforming a large number of unsupervised and supervised methods on average. The work addresses a clear limitation of the original WMD—its unsupervised nature—and provides a practical and effective solution.

The paper’s main weaknesses relate to its novelty and the technical treatment of a key hyperparameter. While the engineering is solid, the contribution is an incremental, albeit useful, extension of prior work, combining WMD with established metric learning and optimization techniques. A more significant concern is the insufficient discussion of the entropic regularizer, lambda. The choice of lambda fundamentally dictates the behavior of the transport problem; a small value prioritizes entropy (and speed), while a large value approximates the true Wasserstein distance. The paper does not adequately analyze this trade-off or justify the chosen value (lambda=10). It is unclear whether the method's success stems from learning a metric for the Wasserstein distance or for the qualitatively different Sinkhorn distance, a critical distinction that is overlooked. This lack of ablation on a parameter that can dominate the objective function is a significant omission. Additionally, the references to the Word Centroid Distance for initialization, while pragmatic, feel somewhat ad-hoc and slightly confuse the core contribution.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with strong empirical validation for a practically useful method. However, its novelty is limited, and the lack of a rigorous analysis of the crucial lambda hyperparameter weakens its technical contribution. Addressing the role and impact of lambda on the learned metric would substantially strengthen the paper.
