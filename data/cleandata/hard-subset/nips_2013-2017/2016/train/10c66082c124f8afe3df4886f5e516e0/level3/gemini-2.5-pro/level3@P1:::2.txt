This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The original WMD provided a powerful, unsupervised metric for document similarity by framing the distance as an optimal transport problem between word embeddings. The key limitation of WMD is its inability to incorporate task-specific supervision. This work addresses that gap by learning a document metric that is optimized for a given classification task. The proposed method learns two components simultaneously: 1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to adjust the geometry of word relationships, and 2) a vector of word-importance weights to re-scale the contribution of each word in a document's representation.

The authors formulate the learning problem by adapting the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective, which seeks to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error. A significant technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD metric is computationally prohibitive due to a nested optimization problem and potential non-differentiability. To overcome this, the authors make a crucial contribution by replacing the exact transport problem with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance). This relaxation yields a strictly convex and differentiable objective that can be solved efficiently, allowing for a practical gradient-based optimization of the transformation matrix and the word weights. The paper further proposes an intelligent initialization scheme based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and efficient batch-based training to make the approach scalable.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD. Applying discriminative training to learn a domain-specific metric for WMD is a logical and valuable next step for this line of research. The proposed method of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space and word-importance weights is a novel and powerful combination. Given the strong performance of WMD, a supervised version that further improves its accuracy is a significant contribution that is likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers in text analysis, information retrieval, and NLP.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The main technical contribution—the use of the Sinkhorn distance to derive an efficient and differentiable approximation for the WMD gradient—is elegant and effective. This clever approximation is what makes the entire framework computationally feasible. The derivations for the gradients with respect to both the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w` are clearly presented. The overall approach, including the NCA-based loss function and the practical optimization strategies (initialization, batching), constitutes a complete and well-thought-out piece of work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background on word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning, making the paper accessible. The motivation for the problem is clear, and the proposed solution is explained with appropriate mathematical detail. The description of the experimental setup and the results is thorough, aiding in the reproducibility of the work.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. The authors evaluate their method on eight diverse datasets, covering tasks from news categorization to sentiment analysis. They compare S-WMD against an extensive list of 26 competitive baselines, including classic methods (TF-IDF, LSI), modern representation learning techniques (mSDA), and the combination of these representations with multiple standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The results are compelling, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on five of the eight datasets and achieves the best average rank overall, often outperforming the next best method by a significant margin. The included visualizations also provide good qualitative support for the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Sensitivity to Optimization:** While the method performs extremely well on average, the authors honestly note that the unsupervised WMD outperforms S-WMD on the CLASSIC dataset. They speculate this might be due to a poor initialization or the specific choice of a squared Euclidean distance in the embedding space. This suggests that, like many non-convex optimization problems, the performance can be sensitive to initialization and hyperparameter settings, which could be a minor practical hurdle for new users.

2.  **Computational Complexity:** The authors have done an excellent job of making the training process efficient. However, the complexity of computing the gradient at each step remains notably higher than for simpler metric learning approaches applied to fixed document vectors (e.g., NCA on TF-IDF). While the reported training times are practical, the scalability to datasets with extremely large documents (large numbers of unique words) or a very large number of training instances could still be a concern.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant limitation of the influential WMD by introducing a supervised, task-specific variant.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound solution, using a clever relaxation of the optimal transport problem to enable efficient gradient-based learning.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, rigorous, and demonstrates state-of-the-art results across a wide range of tasks and strong baselines.
*   The paper is clearly written and presents a complete piece of work that is likely to have a high impact on the field.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The performance can be sensitive to initialization, as shown on one dataset.
*   The computational cost, while much improved, is still higher than simpler baselines.

**Recommendation:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a novel, significant, and high-performing method. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work makes a clear contribution to the fields of metric learning and text analysis and is highly suitable for presentation at NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
