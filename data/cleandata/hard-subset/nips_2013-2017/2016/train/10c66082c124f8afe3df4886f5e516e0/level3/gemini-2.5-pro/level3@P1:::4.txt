This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which defines the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance in an unsupervised setting, it cannot incorporate label information.

The authors propose to learn two components to make the metric supervised: 1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that reshapes the word embedding space to better separate classes, and 2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weights the normalized bag-of-words (BOW) representation of each document. The learning objective is to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out k-NN classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework. A key technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD metric is computationally prohibitive and non-differentiable. The authors provide a practical solution by relaxing the WMD optimal transport problem with an entropy regularizer (leading to the Sinkhorn distance), which yields a differentiable and efficiently computable approximation. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on eight text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 baselines and demonstrating superior performance on average.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. It provides a clear and concise background on word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning, making the work accessible. The motivation is clear, and the proposed method is explained logically.

2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are a major strength. The authors evaluate their method on eight diverse datasets, comparing it against a large and competitive set of 26 baselines that include classic representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), neural methods (mSDA), and combinations of these with various metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The consistently strong performance of S-WMD across these tasks provides compelling evidence for its effectiveness.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The approach of using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient approximation for the WMD gradient is a clever and sound solution to a difficult problem. This makes the proposed learning framework practical. The inclusion of a smart initialization scheme (S-WCD) and batching with nearest-neighbor filtering further demonstrates careful engineering for efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Conceptual Justification of Word Re-weighting (`w`):** The motivation and implications of learning the word-importance vector `w` are not sufficiently discussed. By re-weighting the document's word distribution (`d_a` to `d_tilde_a`), the method is no longer measuring the distance between the original documents but between modified versions of them. This raises a conceptual question: does this re-weighting alter the fundamental content or semantics of a document? For example, if `w` down-weights important nouns and up-weights stop words, the resulting `d_tilde_a` might not represent the original document's topic. A more detailed justification is needed for why this modification is a desirable property, beyond improving kNN accuracy. An ablation study showing the performance of S-WMD with only the matrix `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) would be highly valuable to disentangle the contributions of the space transformation and the distribution re-weighting.

2.  **Novelty:** The core idea of learning a linear transformation `A` and feature weights `w` using an NCA-style objective is a well-established technique in the metric learning literature. The primary novelty here lies in its specific application to the WMD distance and the technical contribution of deriving a practical gradient via the Sinkhorn relaxation. While this is a valuable engineering and application contribution, the conceptual advance in terms of metric learning itself is somewhat limited.

3.  **Ambiguous Terminology:** The paper, particularly in the conclusion, states that S-WMD captures differences when "two words may be similar in one sense but dissimilar in another, depending on the articles in which they are contained." The abstract also refers to learning a distance that matches the "semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels." This use of "semantic" can be misleading. The model learns to separate documents based on their given labels (e.g., topic, author, sentiment), which does not always align with general semantic similarity. For instance, two news articles about opposing political candidates are semantically very similar (both about politics), but for a topic classification task, they should be far apart. It would be clearer to state that the method learns a task-specific, discriminative distance rather than a more general "semantic" one.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high quality. Claims are well-supported by extensive experiments. The technical approach is sound, and the authors are honest about the need for approximations to make the method computationally feasible.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. As noted above, the paper is a model of clarity in its writing and organization.
*   **Originality:** Moderate. The originality lies in successfully adapting supervised metric learning to the WMD framework, including the key technical contribution of the efficient gradient approximation. However, the constituent ideas (NCA loss, linear transforms, feature weighting) are not new.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant. WMD is a powerful unsupervised baseline, and this work provides an effective and practical method to adapt it for supervised tasks. Given its strong performance, researchers and practitioners in text classification and retrieval are likely to use and build upon this method. It demonstrably advances the state of the art for kNN-based document classification.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and effective method for supervised document metric learning. The empirical results are impressive, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. My main reservations concern the conceptual justification for the word re-weighting component `w` and the somewhat incremental nature of the core learning formulation. However, the technical contribution of making this framework trainable is non-trivial, and the practical utility of the final S-WMD method is high.

I would be more confident in recommending acceptance if the authors could provide a more robust justification for the `w` vector and its effect on document representation, ideally supported by an ablation study. Clarifying the terminology around "semantic" similarity would also strengthen the paper. Nevertheless, given the paper's strengths, I lean towards acceptance.
