Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric for document comparison that builds on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a global word-importance vector w used to reweight document histograms. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out (LOO) kNN objective in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To make optimization practical, the authors approximate WMD with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance for both forward passes and gradients, derive gradients w.r.t. A using the relaxed optimal transport T*λ, and w.r.t. w using the relaxed dual variables (α*, β*) inferred from Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u, v). To further accelerate, they initialize A via NCA on document centroids (S-WCD) and use batch training with WCD-based neighbor filtering. On eight classification datasets, S-WMD outperforms or matches 26 baselines (unsupervised document representations, WMD/WCD, and those combined with supervised metric learning such as NCA/LMNN/ITML after PCA), with code released.

The work sits at the intersection of: (a) WMD/Earth Mover’s Distance for documents (Kusner et al., Cuturi), (b) supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), and (c) Sinkhorn-based approximations for optimal transport. Prior supervised EMD-style learning in vision has learned ground metrics (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), but this paper adapts those ideas to text by learning both a word-space transform and global histogram weights, leveraging modern word embeddings.

Strengths:
- Clear exposition and organization, with a solid literature review that situates the contribution within optimal transport and metric learning.
- Practical, well-engineered training strategy: Sinkhorn relaxation, dual-based gradients for w, S-WCD initialization, and neighbor filtering.
- Extensive experiments across 8 datasets and 26 baselines; consistent improvements on most datasets and competitive elsewhere; helpful visualizations (t-SNE) and qualitative analysis of learned word weights.
- Released code and discussion of training complexity/times, which supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Motivation and justification for the global reweighting vector w are underdeveloped. Reweighting document histograms can, in effect, change document content distribution; the paper should better argue why this operation preserves semantics while improving class separation, and how it differs from or improves upon standard IDF/stopword reweighting.
- Novelty feels incremental: combining NCA-style supervision with a linear transform of embeddings and word reweighting, trained via Sinkhorn approximations. While the engineering is strong, the conceptual advance beyond prior supervised EMD/ground metric learning seems modest.
- Practical clarity: It is unclear how w is used at test time for unseen documents and whether w is global or document-specific. The text sometimes suggests “per-document level” optimization, which could be read as per-document parameters. Please clarify that A and w are global parameters and that test documents are reweighted by the same w.
- Ambiguity in terminology: the abstract’s “semantic difference” seems to refer to task-specific label similarity (topic/sentiment/author). Using “task-specific similarity” would be clearer and avoid suggesting the method captures a universal semantic notion.
- Limited analysis of contributions of A vs w: There is no ablation showing S-WMD with A only, w only, or effects of initialization. On some datasets WMD or NCA+TF-IDF perform similarly or better; understanding when w helps or hurts would strengthen the case for the design.
- Theoretical properties: After learning A and w and using the Sinkhorn relaxation, does the resulting distance remain a metric? Some discussion of properties and any bounds on the approximation-induced bias for training would be useful.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound and the optimization choices are sensible. The gradient derivations under the Sinkhorn relaxation are coherent, and the training heuristics are effective.
- Experimental support is strong: many datasets and baselines, careful tuning, and reporting of ranks and error rates. A few methodological clarifications (see questions) would further improve rigor.

Clarity:
- Generally very clear. Suggested clarifications: precise role and scope of w (global vs per-document), what distance is used at test time (exact WMD vs Sinkhorn), positivity/regularization constraints on w, and rephrase “semantic difference.”

Originality:
- Moderate. Learning a ground metric with supervision and Sinkhorn-based training is known; the combination with NCA-style loss and global word reweighting in the text domain is a practical and useful integration, but the conceptual jump is not large.

Significance:
- The empirical gains and practical training pipeline are valuable for kNN-style document classification and retrieval with WMD. Given WMD’s adoption, a supervised, efficient variant is likely to influence practice. The work advances the state of the art on several benchmarks.

Questions for the authors:
1) Is w a single global vector shared across all documents? Please clarify how w is applied to unseen documents at test time and confirm there are no per-document parameters.
2) What constraints/regularization are imposed on w (e.g., nonnegativity, sparsity, entropy) to avoid degenerate solutions (e.g., collapsing mass to a few words)? Any sensitivity analyses?
3) Do you evaluate with exact WMD (using learned A and w) at test time, or with the Sinkhorn distance? If exact WMD, how much does the choice affect accuracy/runtime?
4) Can you provide ablations: S-WMD with A-only, w-only, with/without S-WCD initialization, and sensitivity to λ, N (neighbor count), and batch size?
5) On datasets where S-WMD underperforms WMD or NCA+TF-IDF, what failure modes do you observe (e.g., very long documents, noisy vocabularies)? Any guidance for practitioners?
6) Please clarify “per-document level” phrasing and replace “semantic difference” with “task-specific similarity” or similar.

Minor comments:
- Discuss OOV words handling and transfer robustness if embeddings are trained on a different domain.
- Please state explicitly that w ≥ 0 and normalized via d̃ to maintain valid histograms.
- Consider adding a brief discussion on whether DA,w is a metric after learning (symmetry, triangle inequality).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed paper addressing a timely problem with strong empirical results and released code.
- Practical training innovations make supervised WMD feasible and fast.
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines on diverse datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Conceptual novelty is limited; largely an effective integration of known components.
- Insufficient motivation and analysis for the histogram reweighting w and limited ablation to isolate contributions.
- Some ambiguities in terminology and practical usage at test time.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept / weak accept, contingent on clarifying the role and constraints of w, test-time usage, and providing ablations that justify the design choices.
