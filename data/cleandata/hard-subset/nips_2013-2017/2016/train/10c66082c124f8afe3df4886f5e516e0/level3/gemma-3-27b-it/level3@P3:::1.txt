This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric based on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear projection of word embeddings alongside document-specific word re-weightings, minimizing kNN classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem – the Sinkhorn distance – and a probabilistic filtering strategy. The paper demonstrates strong performance on eight text classification tasks, consistently outperforming numerous baselines.

The work builds upon the recent success of WMD (Kusner et al., 2016) in kNN-based text classification, directly addressing its limitation of being entirely unsupervised. It connects to the broader field of metric learning (e.g., ITML, LMNN, NCA – Davis et al., 2011; Weinberger & Saul, 2009; Goldberger et al., 2004) by applying these techniques within the WMD framework. The use of the Sinkhorn distance is inspired by Cuturi (2013) and subsequent work on efficient optimal transport computation. 

The paper is strong in several aspects. The proposed approach is a natural and insightful extension of WMD, effectively incorporating supervision. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating superior performance across a diverse set of datasets and consistently beating a large number of competitive baselines. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making it a pleasure to read. The authors thoughtfully address computational efficiency with the Sinkhorn approximation and batch gradient descent, presenting a practical method. The inclusion of ablation analysis in the results (Table 3) and visualizations (Figures 1 & 2) further strengthens the presentation.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. A key concern is the limited impact of the full supervised learning process. Table 3 reveals that initializing the metric learning via a "word centroid distance" (S-WCD) achieves performance comparable to the full S-WMD learning process. This suggests that the benefits of the more complex, fully supervised approach are marginal. While the authors acknowledge this, a deeper investigation into *why* S-WCD performs so well would be beneficial.

A crucial missing baseline is an evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings (as used in S-WCD) *with* existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would establish a clearer understanding of whether the gains from S-WMD stem from the metric learning itself or from the specific combination with the WMD framework and word re-weighting.

Furthermore, the paper lacks an ablation study to isolate the contributions of the word-specific weights versus the linear projection matrix A. While both are learned simultaneously, understanding their individual effects would provide more insight into the method's mechanism. Is the improvement primarily driven by the re-weighting, the projection, or their interaction?

The originality primarily lies in the optimization approach (lines 156–175) and applying metric learning *within* the EMD/WMD framework. While interesting, this is a somewhat limited novelty compared to, for example, entirely novel metric learning algorithms. The paper adequately references related work, but a mention of recent advances in optimal transport for machine learning beyond the cited works might be useful. 

There are minor clarifications needed: Line 152 refers to “The authors”; it should explicitly state which paper is being referenced (Kusner et al., 2016). Equation 11 likely needs stars on the α and β variables to indicate that they are the optimal dual variables derived from the relaxed transport. Additionally, the use of Sinkhorn scaling, referenced earlier, should be explicitly noted around line 170 when discussing the optimization procedure.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance across multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Practical and efficient optimization approach.
*   Natural and insightful extension of WMD.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited impact of full supervised learning (S-WCD performs similarly).
*   Missing baseline: evaluation of word-centroid embeddings with standard metric learning methods.
*   Missing ablation study to isolate the contributions of word weights vs. projection matrix.
*   Somewhat limited novelty in the core algorithmic contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper. While the impact of the full supervised learning process is somewhat limited, the method is effective, and the presentation is excellent. The missing baselines and ablation study represent relatively straightforward additions that could further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing these minor concerns.
