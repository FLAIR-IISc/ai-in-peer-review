## Review of “Supervised Word Mover’s Distance”

This paper presents a compelling extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by introducing a supervised learning framework to optimize both word embeddings and word importance weights. The core idea – learning a domain-specific metric from WMD via discriminative training – is a valuable contribution. The authors effectively address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into the WMD calculation by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering approach. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD) method with appropriate mathematical detail. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the connection to existing work in metric learning and optimal transport is well-established. The extensive experimental evaluation is a significant strength. The authors demonstrate consistent and substantial performance gains over a comprehensive set of 26 baselines across eight diverse datasets. This thoroughness convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of ablation studies (showing the benefit of initialization) and analysis of learned word weights further strengthens the empirical validation.

A minor weakness is the reliance on the squared Euclidean distance within the optimal transport problem. While justified, exploring alternative distance metrics within this framework could be a direction for future work. Additionally, the discussion of limitations could be expanded. For example, the potential for overfitting with limited labeled data or the computational cost for extremely large vocabularies could be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Performance:** S-WMD consistently and significantly outperforms a large number of competitive baselines.
*   **Novelty:** Applying discriminative training to learn a metric directly within the WMD framework is a novel and impactful contribution.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The proposed method is theoretically sound and practically efficient, addressing the computational challenges effectively.
*   **Extensive Evaluation:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness and generalizability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   None significant. The paper is of high quality and addresses a relevant problem in a well-executed manner.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and thoroughly evaluated. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS. The work is likely to be influential and inspire further research in this area.
