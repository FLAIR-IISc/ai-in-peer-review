This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The learning objective is an NCA-style leave-one-out kNN surrogate that encourages documents with the same label to be close under the transport distance DA,w. Direct differentiation through the WMD LP is intractable, so the authors adopt an entropy-regularized OT relaxation (Sinkhorn) to obtain efficient, unique solutions for both the primal transport T*λ and approximate dual variables (α*λ, β*λ). This yields closed-form gradients for A and w, enabling batch SGD. They initialize A by applying NCA to Word Centroid Distance (WCD) representations (S-WCD), restrict gradient computation to a candidate neighbor set obtained via WCD, and report state-of-the-art kNN accuracy on 8 datasets against 26 baselines.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al.) and entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi) and is conceptually related to supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and prior work on learning ground costs for EMD (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.). The novelty is to (a) combine supervised ground metric learning in the embedding space with (b) word-level histogram reweighting and (c) a practical, differentiable Sinkhorn-based training procedure tailored to document distances.

Quality: The method is technically sound overall, with a clear derivation of gradients using the Sinkhorn relaxation and NCA loss. The complexity analysis and practical accelerations (initialization, neighbor pruning) are reasonable and borne out by training times. Empirically, S-WMD achieves the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets. That said, several aspects merit strengthening:
- The incremental gains over the S-WCD initialization appear modest on multiple datasets; the paper would benefit from a more systematic accounting of when S-WMD substantially surpasses S-WCD and why.
- Ablations disentangling the contribution of A vs. w are missing; it is unclear whether the histogram reweighting or the linear transform drives most improvements.
- Sensitivity to λ (regularization in Sinkhorn), neighbor set size, and the choice of squared vs. unsquared Euclidean ground costs is not analyzed.
- The selection of nearest neighbors using WCD for gradient computation could bias training; a brief empirical justification (e.g., recall of true neighbors under WCD) would help.

Clarity: The paper is very well written and easy to follow. Minor issues:
- Please clarify the antecedent of “the authors” around line ~152.
- In Eq. (11), α and β should be denoted with stars to match the optimal dual variables (α*, β* or α*λ, β*λ).
- Explicitly state near the gradient derivations that dual variables are obtained via Sinkhorn scaling (referencing the earlier section).
- State constraints on w (non-negativity) and any regularization; since d̃ uses (w ◦ d)/(w>d), negative entries seem undesirable.

Originality: The combination of Sinkhorn-based gradients with NCA for WMD and the joint learning of A and w for document transport is a meaningful, if incremental, contribution. Prior work has explored learning ground metrics for EMD, but the adaptation to word embeddings with histogram reweighting and an efficient training loop for text classification is novel and practical.

Significance: The approach is likely to be useful for kNN-style text classification and retrieval with OT distances and can influence subsequent supervised OT methods in NLP. However, if S-WCD (i.e., WCD + NCA) performs nearly as well in practice, the incremental benefit of full S-WMD may be limited for some applications. Clearer evidence of unique gains (e.g., on short-text regimes, cross-domain splits, or low-resource settings) would strengthen impact.

Missing baselines and analyses:
- Report ITML and LMNN applied to WCD representations (the paper presents S-WCD via NCA, but parity with other metric learners on WCD is important).
- Ablate A-only (fix w = 1), w-only (fix A = I), and full model.
- Compare against supervised fine-tuning of embeddings (e.g., learning an affine transform via triplet or contrastive losses) combined with WMD, to isolate the value of the transport objective per se.
- Provide sensitivity to λ and neighbor set size M; report robustness across these hyperparameters.

Additional questions and suggestions:
- Provide details on constraints/regularization for w (non-negativity, sparsity). Inspect learned weights across datasets; do they align with IDF?
- Discuss failure cases (e.g., CLASSIC where WMD > S-WMD) and whether using L1 ground distance improves results there.
- Clarify whether neighbors for gradient computation are refreshed as A and w evolve.
- Include a brief convergence discussion or empirical convergence plots (loss vs. iterations).

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated extension that marries WMD with supervision.
- Elegant use of Sinkhorn to enable efficient, differentiable training.
- Strong average performance across diverse datasets; public code.
- Clear exposition and useful visualizations.

Cons against acceptance:
- Incremental gains over a strong WCD + NCA baseline appear modest on several datasets.
- Missing ablations and some supervised baselines (ITML/LMNN on WCD).
- Limited analysis of design choices and sensitivity; unclear contribution of w vs. A.
- Scope largely confined to kNN; broader impacts not demonstrated.

Overall assessment: A solid, well-executed paper with practical value and clear empirical benefits, though with somewhat modest novelty and missing analyses that would clarify where S-WMD is most advantageous. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can add the ablations and WCD+ITML/LMNN comparisons and address the minor clarity issues.
