This paper proposes a supervised learning approach to improve the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a document metric that leverages word embeddings. The authors introduce the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD) metric, which learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector. The S-WMD metric is trained to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error on a per-document level.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a thorough literature review that provides a clear understanding of the context and related work. The authors provide extensive experimental results on several novel datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The key contribution of this paper is the proposal of a supervised learning approach to improve the WMD metric. The authors show that the S-WMD metric outperforms the unsupervised WMD and other baseline methods on several document classification tasks. The use of a linear transformation and a word-importance weight vector allows the S-WMD metric to capture more nuanced relationships between words and documents.

However, there are some limitations and concerns with the proposed approach. The motivation for reweighting the word-importance vector is not well-explained, and it is unclear how this reweighting affects the document semantics. Additionally, the contribution of the paper is limited, as applying a linear transformation and reweighting with an NCA objective is not a significant novelty.

Another practical concern is that it is unclear how to compute the reweighting for a completely new or unseen document. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how to handle this scenario, which is important for real-world applications.

Furthermore, the terminology used in the abstract is ambiguous, and the term "semantic difference" appears to refer to label/topic/authorship rather than true semantics. This ambiguity may lead to confusion among readers.

Despite these limitations, the paper is well-written, and the experimental results are impressive. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed approach and its advantages over existing methods.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, and the organization is well-structured. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques. The use of a linear transformation and a word-importance weight vector is a new approach to improving the WMD metric.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results are important, and other researchers or practitioners are likely to use these ideas or build on them.

Overall, I would not object to the acceptance of this paper, provided that the authors address the concerns and limitations mentioned above.
