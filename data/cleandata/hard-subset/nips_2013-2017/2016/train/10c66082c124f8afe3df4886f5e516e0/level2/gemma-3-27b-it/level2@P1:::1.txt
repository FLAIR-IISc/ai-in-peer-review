This paper proposes a supervised variant of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) called S-WMD, aiming to improve document distance learning by incorporating supervision. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering technique. The method is evaluated on eight datasets, consistently outperforming numerous baselines.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear limitation of the original WMD – its unsupervised nature. The problem of incorporating supervision into document distance metrics is important, as the optimal distance measure often depends on the specific task. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and position their work as a natural extension to WMD. The proposed approach is technically sound, leveraging the Sinkhorn distance to make the optimization tractable. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including a large number of competitive baselines and demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The ablation studies (reporting results for S-WMD initialization) are also helpful in understanding the contribution of the learning process. The clarity of the paper is generally good. The mathematical notation is well-defined, and the explanations are mostly accessible. The authors provide sufficient details to allow for reproducibility.

However, there are a few areas for improvement. While the paper claims efficiency, the empirical evaluation focuses primarily on accuracy. Providing a more detailed analysis of the runtime performance, particularly compared to the original WMD and other metric learning approaches, would strengthen the claims. For instance, a plot showing training time versus dataset size could be informative. The ablation study, while useful, could be expanded. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance? What is the impact of the batch size and the number of nearest neighbors used in the stochastic gradient descent? 

Furthermore, the discussion of related work, while comprehensive, lacks depth in certain areas. For example, the authors mention ITML and LMNN but don’t deeply connect their work to these methods beyond stating they are used as baselines. Discussing how S-WMD differs conceptually from these methods (e.g., in terms of the objective function or the constraints imposed) would add value. While the authors do discuss Wang et al. [35], a more detailed comparison could be beneficial. 

Finally, the conclusion could be strengthened by outlining potential future research directions. For example, exploring non-linear transformations of the word embedding space or investigating methods for automatically selecting the regularization parameter λ could be promising avenues for future work.  The motivation behind re-weighting the histograms (word importance) is stated, but an investigation into *which* words are consistently weighted higher (and why) could provide further insight.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** S-WMD consistently outperforms a large number of competitive baselines across multiple datasets.
*   **Novel approach:** The method presents a novel way to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework.
*   **Technical soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering provides a practical solution to the computational challenges.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited runtime analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational performance of S-WMD.
*   **Shallow discussion of related work:** The related work section could be more in-depth, particularly in comparing S-WMD to other metric learning methods.
*   **Limited exploration of hyperparameters:** The sensitivity of the performance to key hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The technical innovations are sound, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly concerning the runtime analysis and a more detailed discussion of related work and hyperparameter sensitivity.
