Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns task-specific document distances for kNN classification. WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15) computes the optimal transport cost between two documents represented as distributions over pre-trained word embeddings. S-WMD introduces two supervised components: (i) a linear transformation A applied to the word embedding space, effectively learning a Mahalanobis ground metric, and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The parameters (A, w) are learned by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss in the style of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), where neighborhood probabilities are defined using the WMD distance.

Directly differentiating WMD is expensive and non-smooth. The authors leverage entropy-regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn distances; Cuturi, NIPS’13) to obtain a differentiable and efficient surrogate. They compute gradients w.r.t. A using the relaxed transport plan T*λ, and w.r.t. w using dual potentials (α, β) obtained from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u, v). Training is further accelerated by (a) initializing A via NCA on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) representation (S-WCD), (b) minibatching, and (c) restricting neighborhood computations to WCD-based nearest neighbors. Across eight text classification datasets, S-WMD outperforms 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised, including NCA/LMNN/ITML on BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA) on average and achieves the best test error on 5/8 datasets. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on WMD, entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi; Cuturi & Doucet), and NCA (Goldberger et al.). It is closely related to learning ground metrics for EMD (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al., supervised EMD for vision), but adapts these ideas to text with word embeddings and adds learned histogram reweighting. It also relates to supervised embeddings for sentiment (Tang et al.), though the focus here is metric learning for kNN.

Strengths
- Well-motivated and principled formulation that marries OT-based document distances with supervised metric learning.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn-based gradients, strong initialization, and neighbor filtering; complexity analysis provided.
- Learning both a ground metric (A) and word weights (w) increases adaptability to task semantics.
- Extensive empirical evaluation across 8 datasets against 26 well-tuned baselines; consistent average improvements and state-of-the-art results on most datasets.
- Useful qualitative analyses (t-SNE with S-WMD distances; visualization of high-weight words) and code release.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Approximation gap: The loss is optimized using gradients computed from the entropy-regularized OT. It would be helpful to clarify whether forward distances in the loss also use the regularized cost or the exact WMD, and to discuss the effect of λ on bias/variance and accuracy. Any empirical sensitivity to λ beyond the fixed choice of 10?
- Constraints and regularization on w: Since d is reweighted and renormalized, w should be constrained nonnegative to ensure valid distributions; please clarify the constraint and any regularization (e.g., L1/L2) to prevent pathological weightings (e.g., overfitting to rare or stop words). An ablation of learning A only vs. w only vs. both would be informative.
- Identifiability/scaling: There is a potential scaling ambiguity between A and λ, and normalization in d̃ partially removes scaling in w. A short discussion of identifiability and any normalization of A (e.g., Frobenius norm, rank r) would improve clarity.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are reported for kNN classification only. While this matches the method’s goal, it would strengthen the case to show (i) sensitivity to k, (ii) whether the learned A induces improvements in linear or kernel classifiers built on document centroids, and (iii) inference scalability (per-query time for kNN with S-WMD vs. WMD).
- Baselines: In 2016 there exist competitive neural document encoders (CNN/RNN with pre-trained embeddings); even a representative comparison on a subset would be useful context. The paper does include mSDA, which is strong for sentiment, but neural baselines would further support significance.
- Clarity of derivations: The gradient w.r.t. w via dual potentials is elegant; a brief appendix derivation and conditions under which the approximation α*λ, β*λ from u, v is accurate would help readers reproduce results. Minor notation issues (e.g., dimensions of 1 and p) could be tightened.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound; leveraging Sinkhorn for tractable gradients is appropriate. Some theoretical aspects (approximation gap and constraints on w) could be elaborated. Empirical methodology is careful: multiple datasets, well-tuned baselines, and runtime analyses.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Adding explicit constraints/regularizers, ablations, and a succinct derivation of the w-gradient would further improve reproducibility.
- Originality: Learning a supervised ground metric and word reweighting for WMD on text, with efficient Sinkhorn-based training and NCA-style objective, is a novel and meaningful combination relative to prior EMD metric learning in vision and to WMD itself.
- Significance: Results are strong and the approach is practical. Given the broad use of WMD in retrieval/classification, a supervised version is likely to be adopted and extended.

Questions for the authors
- Are entries of w constrained to be nonnegative? Is any regularization applied to w or A? How is r (the rank/rows of A) selected?
- Do you use regularized OT for both training and test-time distance computation, or exact WMD at test time?
- Sensitivity of performance to λ, N (neighbor set size), and batch size? Any ablations for learning A only vs. w only?
- How does S-WMD scale to very long documents (large q) and large retrieval pools at test time (per-query latency)?

Pros
- Strong, principled method with efficient optimization.
- Broad and competitive empirical evaluation; best average rank.
- Code release and practical training details.

Cons
- Limited analysis of approximation effects and sensitivity to λ.
- Constraints/regularization on w and identifiability not fully specified.
- Evaluation restricted to kNN; missing contemporary neural baselines.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper provides a substantial and practical advance over WMD with clear empirical gains, and the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on supervised OT-based document metrics. Addressing the noted clarifications and adding small ablations would further strengthen the submission.
