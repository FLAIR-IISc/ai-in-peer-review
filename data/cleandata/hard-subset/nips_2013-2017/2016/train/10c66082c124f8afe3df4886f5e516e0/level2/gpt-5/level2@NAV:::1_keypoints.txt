- Proposal: S-WMD learns an affine transform of word embeddings plus a word-importance vector to produce a supervised optimal-transport document metric, trained with a stochastic leave-one-out kNN (NCA) loss and Sinkhorn approximation; initialized via S-WCD.

- Implementation: Uses entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) for efficient, differentiable updates with batching and neighborhood filtering.

- Experiments: Evaluated on eight text classification datasets against 26 baselines (including WMD and various metric-learning/representation baselines), showing consistent improvements and competitive training time.

- Interpretability: Learned word weights provide interpretable importance scores for retrieval/classification.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML), and complements supervised OT research in vision by addressing text and jointly learning ground metric and histogram reweighting.

- Technical quality: Method is sound, well-motivated, and implemented efficiently.

- Empirical rigor: Strong empirical support with sensible baselines and hyperparameter tuning.

- Missing analyses: Lacks ablations (e.g., A-only, w-only, effect of initialization) and sensitivity studies.

- Sinkhorn concerns: No detailed discussion of approximation error or convergence behavior from the Sinkhorn surrogate.

- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized with helpful background and algorithm summary, but contains a few typos, minor citation glitches, and underspecified practical details (embedding source, preprocessing); code release mitigates some issues.

- Originality: Novel combination for text—supervised learning of both the ground metric and per-word weights within OT trained via NCA and Sinkhorn gradients.

- Significance: Useful for kNN-based classification/retrieval and advances WMD, but limited scope due to focus on kNN and lack of comparisons to neural document encoders.

- Pros: Principled supervised extension of WMD, strong empirical gains, interpretability, code availability, and solid engineering (initialization, batching, neighbor filtering).

- Cons: Missing ablations/sensitivity studies, evaluation restricted to kNN (no neural encoder baselines), some reproducibility details underspecified, and computational cost remains quadratic in unique words per document.
