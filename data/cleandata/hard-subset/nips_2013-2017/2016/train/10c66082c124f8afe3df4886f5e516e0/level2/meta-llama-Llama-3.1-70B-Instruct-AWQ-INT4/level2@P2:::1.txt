The paper "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance" proposes a novel method for learning a supervised document distance metric, which improves upon the state-of-the-art Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision into the distance computation. The authors introduce a technique for learning a linear transformation of the word representations and a word-importance weight vector, which enables the WMD to capture the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method, including the mathematical formulation and the optimization procedure. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which outperforms 26 competitive baselines on 8 real-world text classification tasks.

The main strength of the paper is its ability to leverage the strengths of the WMD while addressing its limitations. The WMD is a powerful distance metric that can capture the semantic meaning of documents, but it is entirely unsupervised and lacks a mechanism to incorporate supervision when available. The proposed method, Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), addresses this limitation by learning a supervised metric that can capture the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels.

Another strength of the paper is its efficient optimization procedure, which enables the method to scale to large datasets. The authors propose a fast gradient computation process and a batch gradient descent algorithm, which significantly reduce the computational complexity of the method.

The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the related work, including a discussion of the limitations of existing methods and how the proposed method addresses these limitations.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the method relies on the quality of the word embeddings, which may not always be available or accurate. However, the authors address this limitation by using a state-of-the-art word embedding technique, word2vec, and demonstrating that the method can still achieve good performance even with a relatively simple initialization.
