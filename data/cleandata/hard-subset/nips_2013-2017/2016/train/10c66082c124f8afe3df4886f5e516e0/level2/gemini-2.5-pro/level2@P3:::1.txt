This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach extends the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to calculate the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance in unsupervised settings, it cannot incorporate label information. S-WMD addresses this limitation by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space, and (2) a vector of word-importance weights. These are learned by minimizing a stochastic approximation of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out classification error, following the framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).

A key technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD metric is computationally intractable, as it involves a nested optimization problem. The central contribution of this work is an efficient and differentiable approximation of this gradient. This is achieved by replacing the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version, also known as the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation allows for the derivation of a practical and efficient update rule. The work also proposes effective optimization strategies, including a sensible initialization using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent procedure that filters neighbors to reduce computational cost. The proposed method is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, demonstrating superior performance against a comprehensive suite of 26 baseline methods.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of the problem is clear, and the motivation for extending WMD with supervision is well-justified. The primary technical contribution—the approximation of the WMD gradient using the Sinkhorn distance—is elegant and sound. It successfully transforms a computationally prohibitive optimization problem into a practical algorithm. The derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation matrix `A` and the word weight vector `w` is well-explained. The use of the dual variables from the relaxed transport problem to compute the gradient with respect to `w` is particularly clever.

The experimental evaluation is extensive and convincing. The comparison against 26 baselines, including strong document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) paired with leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML), provides strong evidence for S-WMD's effectiveness. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning across all methods ensures a fair comparison. The results are impressive, with S-WMD achieving the best performance on five of the eight datasets and the best average rank overall. The inclusion of training time analysis demonstrates the practical feasibility of the proposed method.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of document distance metrics and metric learning. Section 2 provides the necessary background on word2vec, WMD, regularized transport, and NCA, making the paper largely self-contained and accessible to readers familiar with the area. The proposed method in Section 3 is detailed logically, from the definition of the S-WMD distance and loss function to the derivation of the fast gradient approximation and the overall optimization algorithm. The results in Section 4 are presented transparently with clear tables and informative visualizations.

**Originality**

This work is highly original. While it builds upon several existing ideas—WMD, NCA, and the Sinkhorn distance—their combination to create a supervised document distance is novel and non-trivial. The problem of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored before, but this paper's approach of simultaneously learning a linear transformation and re-weighting the histograms in the specific context of document classification with word embeddings is a new contribution. It significantly advances the WMD framework by making it adaptable to supervised tasks, which was its most apparent limitation. The work is well-situated in the literature and related work is adequately cited and distinguished.

**Significance**

The results presented are significant and likely to have a considerable impact. WMD is a powerful but unsupervised metric. This work provides a practical and effective way to leverage supervision, making the WMD framework applicable to a much broader range of real-world classification tasks. Given the state-of-the-art results on a variety of datasets, researchers and practitioners working on text classification are likely to adopt or build upon these ideas. The method provides a powerful new tool for document analysis that combines the strengths of pre-trained word embeddings with task-specific metric learning.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is very strong, but could be slightly improved with minor additions:

1.  **Sensitivity to Regularization (λ):** The work sets the Sinkhorn regularization parameter λ to 10, noting it is a trade-off between approximation accuracy and convergence speed. A brief sensitivity analysis or discussion on how the choice of λ impacts final classification performance would be a valuable addition. This would provide practitioners with a better understanding of this hyperparameter's importance.

2.  **Discussion on the Ground Metric:** The paper notes that the use of squared Euclidean distance might be suboptimal for one dataset. Expanding this point with a brief discussion on the potential of incorporating other ground metrics within the S-WMD framework could be beneficial for future work.

3.  **Visualization of Down-weighted Words:** The word cloud in Figure 2 effectively shows which words are deemed important. It might also be insightful to visualize some of the words that receive the *lowest* weights. This could help illustrate that the model is learning to ignore common or task-irrelevant words, providing a more complete picture of the learned importance vector.

These are minor suggestions and do not detract from the overall high quality of the work.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a clear and important limitation of the highly successful WMD metric.
    *   Presents a novel, technically sound, and elegant solution to a difficult optimization problem.
    *   The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough and provides compelling evidence of state-of-the-art performance.
    *   The work is significant and likely to be influential in the field of text analysis and metric learning.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   There are no strong arguments against acceptance. The minor points for improvement are suggestions for refinement rather than fundamental flaws.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field.
