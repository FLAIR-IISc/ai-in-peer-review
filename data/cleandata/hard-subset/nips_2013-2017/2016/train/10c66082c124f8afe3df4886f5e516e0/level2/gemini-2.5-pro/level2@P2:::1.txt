### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to compute the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance for kNN classification, it is an entirely unsupervised metric.

The key contributions of this paper are:
1.  A method to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework by jointly learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector.
2.  An efficient and practical optimization scheme. The authors recognize that computing the exact gradient of the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and non-differentiable. They overcome this by approximating the WMD with the regularized Sinkhorn distance, which is differentiable and can be computed much faster.
3.  The proposed method, S-WMD, is trained by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The training is further accelerated through a clever initialization using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent procedure that only considers a small subset of nearest neighbors for each document.

The authors conduct an extensive empirical evaluation on eight real-world text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 competitive baselines. The results demonstrate that S-WMD consistently outperforms these baselines, achieving state-of-the-art kNN classification error on most datasets.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed approach is well-motivated, addressing a clear limitation of the original WMD. The main technical challenge—the intractability of differentiating through the optimal transport problem—is identified and addressed with a principled and well-justified approximation using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance. This is a clever application of recent advances in computational optimal transport to the problem of metric learning for text. The optimization strategy, including the NCA-based loss function, the initialization via S-WCD, and the neighbor-filtered batch gradient descent, makes the overall algorithm practical and efficient.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a very wide range of baselines, including classic document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), a more modern one (mSDA), and the original WMD/WCD. Furthermore, they strengthen these baselines by combining them with three different metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The consistent and significant improvements shown by S-WMD across eight diverse datasets strongly support the paper's claims. The inclusion of t-SNE visualizations and an analysis of learned word weights adds further credibility to the results.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide a clear and concise background on the necessary concepts, including word2vec, WMD, the Sinkhorn distance, and NCA. The motivation for S-WMD is clearly articulated, and the derivation of the method, from the loss function to the approximate gradients and optimization procedure, is presented logically and is easy to follow. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented clearly in tables and figures. The pseudo-code in Algorithm 1 is a helpful summary of the training process.

### Originality

The paper presents a novel and significant contribution. While the idea of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored before in computer vision, this paper is the first to apply this concept to the WMD for text documents. Crucially, the paper goes beyond just learning a transformation of the embedding space by also learning a word-importance vector, which is a novel and well-suited addition for the text domain. The combination of WMD, NCA-style metric learning, and efficient Sinkhorn-based gradient approximation is a novel synthesis of ideas from different subfields that results in a powerful new algorithm. This work is a natural and important next step after the original WMD paper and is clearly distinct from prior work.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant. WMD provided a new paradigm for document similarity that significantly outperformed traditional bag-of-words models. By successfully incorporating supervision, S-WMD makes this powerful framework applicable to a wider range of supervised tasks and demonstrates substantially improved performance. Given the strong empirical results, practitioners and researchers working on text classification, retrieval, or other tasks requiring a robust document similarity metric are likely to find this work very useful. The proposed technique for efficiently differentiating through the WMD is also a valuable contribution that could inspire further research into supervised optimal transport-based models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces a highly novel and effective method for supervised document metric learning, addressing a key limitation of the powerful WMD framework.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient proxy for WMD is a clever and well-executed technical contribution.
*   **Extensive and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against a large suite of strong baselines on diverse datasets. The results robustly demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Ablation Study:** The method learns two components: the transformation matrix `A` and the word weight vector `w`. It would strengthen the paper to include an ablation study showing the performance of the model when only learning `A`. This would help quantify the contribution of the novel word re-weighting component.
*   **Hyperparameter Discussion:** The paper mentions using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning but provides little detail on the sensitivity to key parameters of the S-WMD algorithm itself, such as the Sinkhorn regularization parameter `lambda` or the nearest neighbor set size `N`. A brief discussion on the choice and impact of these parameters would be beneficial for reproducibility and practical application.
*   **Choice of Distance Norm:** The authors speculate that the use of squared Euclidean distance might be suboptimal on the CLASSIC dataset. It would be useful to elaborate on why this norm was chosen over the L1 or L2 norm used in the original WMD paper. Was this primarily for compatibility with the NCA framework and mathematical convenience in differentiation, or is there another reason? Briefly discussing this design choice would be helpful.

Overall, the weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions. This is an excellent paper that is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically strong. It represents a significant advance in the area of document similarity and metric learning.
