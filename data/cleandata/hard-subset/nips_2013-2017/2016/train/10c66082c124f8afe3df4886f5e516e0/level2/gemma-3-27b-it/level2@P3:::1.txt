This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. Extensive experiments on eight datasets demonstrate that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large number of baselines.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem. WMD is a powerful document distance metric, but its unsupervised nature limits its performance in supervised tasks. The proposed S-WMD directly tackles this limitation in a principled manner. The technical contributions are solid: the use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is clever, and the inclusion of word importance weights adds flexibility to the model. The extensive experimental evaluation, with 26 baselines, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized, with detailed explanations of the mathematical derivations and algorithmic details.

A strength of the work is the thorough experimental evaluation. The inclusion of a large number of baselines, including state-of-the-art methods and variations of WMD, provides a strong benchmark for comparison. The ablation study (comparing S-WMD to its initialization, S-WCD) is also helpful in understanding the contributions of the different components of the model. The authors also clearly state limitations, for instance, the potential suboptimality of using squared Euclidean distance for certain datasets. 

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. While the explanation of the Sinkhorn distance is present, the connection to why it *enables* efficient gradient computation could be more clearly articulated. Specifically, it would be helpful to explain how the relaxed problem simplifies the gradient derivation compared to the original WMD formulation. The discussion of related work could also be expanded. While the authors mention metric learning and recent work on learning ground distances, a more detailed comparison to related methods, particularly those that also address computational challenges in optimal transport, would be beneficial. 

The choice of the regularization parameter λ (set to 10) is somewhat arbitrary. While the authors mention a trade-off between speed and approximation accuracy, a more systematic evaluation of different λ values, perhaps through a validation set, would strengthen the results. Additionally, the dependence on PCA for dimensionality reduction in some baseline methods isn’t thoroughly discussed and could be a confounding factor. The paper assumes familiarity with several concepts (optimal transport, metric learning, etc.), and although explanations are provided, some readers might benefit from more introductory material.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to incorporating supervision into WMD.
*   Strong empirical results on a diverse set of datasets.
*   Technically sound and well-motivated.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of WMD.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation with many baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection between Sinkhorn distance and gradient efficiency could be better explained.
*   The choice of the regularization parameter λ is somewhat arbitrary.
*   The related work discussion could be more comprehensive.

**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The proposed S-WMD is a powerful and efficient method that outperforms a large number of baselines. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. This reviewer recommends acceptance. The suggested revisions – providing a more detailed explanation of the gradient computation, exploring different values of λ, and expanding the related work discussion – would further strengthen the paper.
