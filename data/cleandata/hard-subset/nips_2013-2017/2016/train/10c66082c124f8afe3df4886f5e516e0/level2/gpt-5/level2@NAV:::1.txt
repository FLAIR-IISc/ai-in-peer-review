Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which learns an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance vector to induce a supervised optimal-transport document metric. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (à la NCA) and uses an entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) approximation to obtain efficient and differentiable updates. An initialization via NCA on word centroids (S-WCD) further stabilizes training. Experiments on eight text classification datasets against 26 baselines (including WMD, TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA with and without NCA/ITML/LMNN) show consistent improvements, with competitive training time and interpretability via learned word weights. The paper builds on WMD (Kusner et al.), Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi), and metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML), and complements supervised EMD work in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas) by addressing text, learning both the ground metric and histogram reweighting.

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The Sinkhorn-based gradient surrogates are reasonable and implemented efficiently with batching and neighborhood filtering. Empirical support is strong across diverse datasets, with sensible baselines and hyperparameter tuning. However, the paper lacks ablations (e.g., only A, only w, effect of initialization), sensitivity analyses (λ, neighborhood size, r), and discussions of approximation error or convergence.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with a useful background section and algorithm summary. A few typos and minor citation glitches appear, and some practical details (embedding source, preprocessing) could be clearer for full reproducibility, though code release mitigates this.

Originality: The combination is novel for text: learning a supervised ground metric plus per-word weights within OT, trained with an NCA objective and Sinkhorn-based gradients. Prior supervised OT/EMD methods are acknowledged and contrasted.

Significance: The approach is practically useful for kNN-based classification and retrieval with interpretability benefits. It advances WMD materially, but scope is limited to kNN; broader impact would be clearer with comparisons to contemporary neural document encoders and retrieval metrics.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled extension of WMD with supervision and efficient training.
- Strong empirical gains over a wide set of baselines on eight datasets.
- Interpretability via learned word importance; code available.
- Good engineering: initialization, batching, and neighbor filtering.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing ablations and sensitivity studies; limited analysis of Sinkhorn approximation effects.
- Evaluation restricted to kNN; no comparisons to neural encoders (e.g., CNN/RNN/fastText).
- Some reproducibility details underspecified; minor clarity issues.
- Computational cost remains quadratic in unique words per document.
