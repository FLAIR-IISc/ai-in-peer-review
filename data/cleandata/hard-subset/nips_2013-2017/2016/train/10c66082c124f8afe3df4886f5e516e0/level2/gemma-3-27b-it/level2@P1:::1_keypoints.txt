- Proposes S-WMD, a supervised variant of Word Mover’s Distance that learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights.
- Core objective: minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error using learned embedding transform and histogram re-weighting.
- Addresses computational issues by using Sinkhorn (relaxed OT) distance and a probabilistic filtering technique for tractability.
- Evaluated on eight datasets and consistently outperforms numerous competitive baselines.
- Well-motivated: tackles the important limitation of WMD being unsupervised and adapts the distance to task-specific needs.
- Technically sound: use of Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering makes supervised optimization practical.
- Experiments are thorough with many baselines and helpful ablation (S-WMD initialization) for understanding contributions.
- Paper is clear and reproducible: notation and explanations are well-defined with sufficient implementation detail.
- Lacks detailed runtime/efficiency analysis; empirical focus is mainly on accuracy rather than computational cost.
- Ablation studies are limited: need sensitivity analysis for Sinkhorn regularization λ, batch size, and number of nearest neighbors in SGD.
- Related work discussion is somewhat shallow; should better contrast S-WMD conceptually with metric learning methods like ITML and LMNN.
- Requires deeper comparison to Wang et al. [35] and clearer positioning among related supervised OT or metric-learning approaches.
- Suggests future work: explore non-linear embedding transforms, automatic selection of λ, and analysis of which words receive higher importance weights.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong empirical results, novel supervision integration into WMD, technical practicality, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited runtime analysis, superficial related-work discussion, and insufficient hyperparameter sensitivity exploration.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address runtime evaluation, related-work depth, and hyperparameter analyses.
