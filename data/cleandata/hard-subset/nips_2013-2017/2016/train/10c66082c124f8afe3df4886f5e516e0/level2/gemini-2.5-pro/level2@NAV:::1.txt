This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document metric. It builds upon the powerful unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which formulates document distance as an optimal transport problem between word embeddings. The authors' main claims are twofold: first, that supervision can be incorporated into WMD by jointly learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector; second, that this can be done efficiently. The technical core of the paper is an elegant solution to the computational challenge of differentiating through the WMD optimization. By replacing the exact transport problem with an entropy-regularized approximation (the Sinkhorn distance), the authors derive an efficient and practical gradient-based update rule. This approach effectively combines ideas from metric learning, specifically the NCA loss function, with recent advances in computational optimal transport.

The paper's strengths are numerous and significant.
**Pros:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem is well-motivated; creating a supervised version of a state-of-the-art unsupervised metric like WMD is a natural and important research direction. The proposed method for achieving this is novel and technically sound.
*   **Quality of Evaluation:** The experimental validation is exceptionally thorough. S-WMD is evaluated on eight diverse datasets and compared against an extensive suite of 26 competitive baselines. The results are compelling, demonstrating that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on the majority of tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are explained clearly, making the work easy to understand and appreciate.

The paper has very few weaknesses.
**Cons:**
*   **Analysis of Limitations:** While the method performs exceptionally well overall, it is outperformed on a few datasets (e.g., WMD is better on CLASSIC). The authors provide plausible hypotheses, but a more in-depth analysis of these failure cases could further strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights into the method's limitations.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the regularization parameter λ is mentioned as a trade-off between speed and accuracy. A brief sensitivity analysis showing its effect on performance would have been a welcome addition, perhaps in the supplementary material.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field. It convincingly demonstrates how to effectively supervise a powerful non-parametric distance metric for text, supported by a rigorous and comprehensive empirical study. The work is likely to be highly influential and widely used by researchers and practitioners. I strongly recommend acceptance.
