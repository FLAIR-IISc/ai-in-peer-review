

The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification performance. Building on Kusner et al.'s WMD, which computes document distances as optimal transport problems between embedded words, the authors introduce a supervised learning framework that jointly optimizes an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights. The method efficiently approximates gradients using the Sinkhorn distance to avoid the cubic complexity of the original WMD.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across eight text classification datasets, showing consistent improvements over 26 baselines including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The authors provide a thorough evaluation with proper statistical analysis, including average ranks across datasets and visualizations of learned word weights.

The work exhibits strong technical quality with a well-motivated approach to addressing the limitation of unsupervised WMD for classification tasks. The gradient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance is a practical contribution that enables efficient training, and the method is shown to scale well with batch processing. The experimental design is robust, with multiple datasets spanning different text classification tasks and proper hyperparameter tuning.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The background section effectively introduces necessary concepts (word embeddings, WMD, metric learning), and the proposed method is clearly explained with sufficient mathematical detail. The organization is logical, progressing from motivation to method to evaluation. The authors appropriately cite prior work, including Kusner et al.'s WMD and standard metric learning approaches.

The originality of the work is notable. While WMD itself was a significant contribution, extending it to a supervised framework through affine transformation and word weighting represents a meaningful advance. The paper distinguishes itself from standard metric learning approaches by operating directly on word embeddings rather than document representations, which avoids the high dimensionality issues of traditional methods. The combination of optimal transport with supervised learning for text classification is novel.

The significance of the work is strong. The consistent improvements across multiple datasets and baselines demonstrate that the method provides a practical and effective solution to a real problem in text classification. The ability to leverage pre-trained word embeddings while improving performance through supervised learning is particularly valuable, as it avoids the need for extensive retraining on labeled data. The computational efficiency of the method, with training times comparable to standard metric learning approaches, enhances its practical utility.

The paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed analysis of the cases where S-WMD underperforms (e.g., CLASSIC dataset), which would help understand the method's limitations. A more thorough investigation of the hyperparameter λ (set to 10 without justification) would also be valuable. While the paper mentions the importance of word weights, a more detailed analysis of how these weights correlate with task-specific semantics would add depth.

The paper makes a compelling case for the value of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated across multiple datasets. The authors effectively address the computational challenges of the problem through their efficient approximation technique. The work advances the state of the art in document distance metrics and provides a practical tool for text classification tasks that can leverage existing word embeddings.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality: it is technically sound with strong experimental validation, clearly written and well-organized, presents a meaningful extension to prior work with appropriate novelty, and demonstrates significant practical value. The minor limitations noted above do not detract from the overall contribution, which represents a substantial improvement over existing document distance metrics for classification tasks.

The paper's focus on text classification is well-aligned with NIPS topics, and the method has clear potential for broader application in natural language processing. The results are impressive, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on five out of eight datasets and the best average performance across all datasets. Given the comprehensive evaluation and strong theoretical foundation, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field.
