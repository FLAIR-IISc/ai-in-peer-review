## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights by minimizing the leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the recent success of WMD and addressing its limitation of being unsupervised. The proposed approach is novel in its combination of learning both a transformation of the word embeddings *and* word importance weights. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on eight datasets, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various metric learning approaches. The ablation study showing the benefit of the learned transformation (S-WMD init) is also valuable. The inclusion of training time analysis is a practical addition.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the computational complexity, the explanation of the gradient computation and the approximations used (Sinkhorn distance, probabilistic filtering) could be more detailed and accessible. The choice of λ=10 feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification beyond a trade-off statement. Furthermore, the discussion of why S-WMD doesn't perform as well on the CLASSIC dataset feels incomplete – a deeper analysis of the dataset characteristics might be warranted. The writing, while generally clear, occasionally feels dense and could benefit from more concise phrasing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance across multiple datasets.
*   Novel combination of learned word embedding transformation and importance weights.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of WMD (lack of supervision).
*   Practical considerations regarding computational efficiency.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited explanation of gradient computation and approximation choices.
*   Arbitrary choice of λ without strong justification.
*   Incomplete analysis of performance discrepancies on specific datasets.
*   Dense writing style in certain sections.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity. The empirical results are compelling, and the proposed approach is both novel and practical. While there are some areas for improvement in terms of clarity and justification, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, expanding the explanation of the gradient approximation and providing a more thorough analysis of the CLASSIC dataset would significantly strengthen the paper.
