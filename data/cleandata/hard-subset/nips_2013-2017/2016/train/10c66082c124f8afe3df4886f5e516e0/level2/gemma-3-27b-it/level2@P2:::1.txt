This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric on top of the unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance weighting vector to minimize k-nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering approach. They demonstrate strong empirical performance on eight real-world text classification tasks, consistently outperforming a large number of baselines.

The paper is well-motivated. WMD is a powerful unsupervised document distance metric, and extending it to a supervised setting is a natural and valuable direction. The problem is clearly defined, and the proposed solution is well-articulated. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of incorporating supervision and propose a clever approximation using the Sinkhorn distance, a significant contribution. The extensive experimental evaluation, comparing against 26 baselines, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The ablation study of initialization (S-WMD init.) is also valuable.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. First, while the authors mention the non-convexity of the loss function and use batch gradient descent, the details of the optimization process (learning rates, batch size, convergence criteria) are somewhat lacking. More details on the optimization setup would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding the practical aspects of the method. Specifically, the sensitivity of performance to the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance approximation could be explored.

Second, the connection between the learned word importance weights and interpretability is not fully explored. While the authors visualize the top weighted words for one dataset, a more systematic analysis of these weights across different datasets would offer further insights into what the model learns and how it differs from unsupervised methods. Does the learned weighting consistently highlight topic-specific keywords, or are the patterns more complex?

Third, the impact of the dimensionality of the word embeddings (d in Rd) on performance is not explicitly investigated. While the authors state they use word2vec embeddings, the embedding size isn't consistently reported, and a brief discussion of its influence would add valuable context.

Finally, the paper’s clarity suffers from occasionally dense mathematical notation and a somewhat disorganized presentation. While the core ideas are sound, the flow could be improved by breaking down complex equations into smaller steps and providing more intuitive explanations. For instance, a more detailed explanation of how the dual variables are obtained from u and v would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of learning a supervised metric on top of WMD is novel and addresses a limitation of the original unsupervised method.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance to address the computational complexity is a well-justified and effective approximation.
*   **Empirical Performance:** Extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates strong performance compared to a large number of baselines.
*   **Practicality:** The authors demonstrate that S-WMD is computationally efficient, making it a viable option for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Optimization Details:** Lack of detailed information regarding optimization setup (learning rates, convergence criteria).
*   **Interpretability:** Limited analysis of the learned word importance weights.
*   **Embedding Dimensionality:** Impact of word embedding dimensionality is not explored.
*   **Clarity:** Some sections are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in document distance learning.
*   The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and achieves state-of-the-art results.
*   The extensive experimental evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks detailed information regarding the optimization process.
*   The interpretability of the learned word importance weights is not fully explored.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the issues related to optimization details, interpretability of the learned weights, and clarity of presentation. Specifically, adding a table of hyperparameters used, a more in-depth analysis of the word weights, and simplifying some of the mathematical derivations would significantly enhance the paper.
