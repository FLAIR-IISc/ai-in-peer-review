

This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), called Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), which learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to improve document classification. The authors address a clear limitation of the original unsupervised WMD by incorporating task-specific supervision, which they claim consistently outperforms 26 baselines across 8 text classification tasks.

The paper's main strengths are its clear motivation, thorough experimental evaluation, and practical implementation. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature, acknowledging the limitations of unsupervised WMD and distinguishing their approach from prior metric learning work. The theoretical foundation for their gradient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance is sound, and the implementation details (including the efficient batch gradient descent approach) are well-explained. The experimental section is comprehensive, with results on 8 diverse datasets and 26 baselines, demonstrating that S-WMD achieves the lowest average error across all datasets.

However, there are several important weaknesses that need addressing. First, the paper lacks a statistical analysis of the performance improvements over baselines. While the results show S-WMD outperforms baselines on average, the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for these comparisons, which is a standard practice in machine learning evaluation. This weakens the claim of "consistent" superiority.

Second, the paper doesn't sufficiently analyze why S-WMD works better than the unsupervised WMD. The results show improvement on 5/8 datasets but not all, yet the paper doesn't explore the characteristics of datasets where S-WMD excels versus those where it doesn't. A case study of the learned word importance weights (e.g., showing how weights differ between datasets like TWITTER versus CLASSIC) would provide valuable insights into the method's behavior.

Third, the paper uses the squared Euclidean distance in their formulation (Equation 5), while the original WMD paper (Kusner et al., 2015) used L1 distance. The authors don't discuss why this change was made or how it affects the results. This is a significant omission as the distance metric choice is fundamental to the WMD approach.

The initialization approach (S-WMD init.) performs remarkably well, often close to the final S-WMD results. The paper doesn't explore whether this initialization alone could be sufficient for some applications, which would be valuable for practitioners. The paper also doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of λ=10 for the Sinkhorn approximation, which could affect reproducibility.

The related work section is generally good but misses some key recent papers on supervised word embeddings, particularly Tang et al. (2015) which uses label information for sentiment-specific word embeddings. While this might not change the main claims, it would strengthen the context of the contribution.

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations. For example, the paper states S-WMD outperforms WMD on most datasets, but doesn't acknowledge that WMD was previously state-of-the-art, making this a significant improvement. The authors could also better address the trade-off between training time (Table 3) and the marginal performance gains.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear practical value, but the claims of consistent superiority would be much stronger with statistical validation of the results and deeper analysis of the method's behavior. The technical approach is sound, and the results are impressive, but the paper would be significantly improved by addressing these points. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field of document distances, and with minor revisions to address these concerns, it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
