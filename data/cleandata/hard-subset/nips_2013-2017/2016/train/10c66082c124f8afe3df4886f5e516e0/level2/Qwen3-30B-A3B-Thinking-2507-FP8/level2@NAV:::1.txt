

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision for improved document classification. The authors address a key limitation of WMD (which is entirely unsupervised) by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out classification error. The method leverages the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make training computationally feasible.

The paper makes a clear and well-supported contribution. The authors demonstrate S-WMD's superiority across 8 text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 baselines (including various document representations and metric learning methods). The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with appropriate baselines, multiple datasets, and detailed analysis of results. The visualization of learned word weights (Fig. 2) provides compelling evidence that the method correctly identifies discriminative terms. The paper also provides clear algorithmic details and implementation notes, making it reproducible.

The technical approach is sound, with proper justification for the Sinkhorn approximation and efficient gradient computation. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., suboptimal performance on CLASSIC dataset due to initialization) and discuss the trade-offs between speed and approximation accuracy.

The paper builds effectively on prior work, particularly Kusner et al.'s WMD and metric learning approaches like NCA. It makes a meaningful contribution by adapting supervised metric learning to the WMD framework, addressing a gap in the literature. The results are statistically significant, with the method achieving the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank.

However, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of when S-WMD might not be appropriate (beyond the CLASSIC example) and a clearer comparison of computational complexity with alternative supervised approaches. The authors should also explicitly address how their method differs from Wang et al.'s work on EMD metric learning.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear novelty, solid experimental validation, and practical utility for document classification tasks. The paper is well-written and makes a significant improvement over unsupervised WMD. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
