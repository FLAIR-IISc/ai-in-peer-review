Summary
The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The approach learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space (a Mahalanobis-type ground cost) and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights term histograms, to better align document distances with label-based similarity. The objective minimizes a stochastic relaxation of the leave-one-out kNN classification error (NCA-style). To make optimization tractable, the work replaces exact optimal transport (OT) with Sinkhorn-regularized OT, uses the corresponding transport plan and dual potentials to obtain gradients, and employs WCD-based initialization, mini-batching, and neighbor filtering to reduce computation. Experiments on eight text classification datasets compare against 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD/WCD and several representation + metric learning combinations), with S-WMD achieving the best average rank and the lowest error on 5/8 datasets. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on: WMD for document distances via OT over word embeddings [Kusner et al., ICML’15]; Sinkhorn distances for fast OT [Cuturi, NIPS’13; Cuturi & Peyré, SIAM JIS’16]; NCA for optimizing kNN LOO risk [Goldberger et al., NIPS’04]. It is related to supervised learning of ground metrics in EMD [Cuturi & Avis, 2014; Wang & Guibas, ECCV’12] and metric learning methods such as LMNN [Weinberger & Saul, JMLR’09] and ITML [Davis et al., ICML’07]. The novelty lies in applying supervised ground metric learning and histogram reweighting to OT in the NLP setting, with an NCA-based objective and a practical Sinkhorn-based gradient scheme.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: WMD is strong but unsupervised; incorporating labels is natural and important for task-specific document similarity.
- Sound formulation: learning A and w within an OT framework is principled; the objective directly targets kNN performance via a well-established stochastic neighborhood loss.
- Efficient optimization: leveraging Sinkhorn for both primal transport and dual potentials is an effective and standard route to differentiable, scalable approximations; neighbor filtering and WCD-based initialization are pragmatic and well-justified.
- Empirical effectiveness: consistently competitive or superior performance across eight datasets and many baselines; average rank and multiple best-in-class results are compelling.
- Reproducibility: implementation is publicly available; datasets and baselines are described with hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Constraints and regularization on w: the method reweights histograms via d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w>d). Nonnegativity of w and any explicit regularization are not clearly stated. Negative entries would break probability constraints and potentially the metric properties of OT. A positivity parameterization (e.g., softplus) or explicit constraints should be clarified.
- Overfitting control on A and w: beyond initialization and batch training, there is little discussion of norm or rank constraints on A, or regularization on w. Given the nonconvex NCA objective, a brief overfitting analysis would strengthen the claim of robustness.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: the method fixes λ=10 and uses neighbor truncation (N=200) and batch size B=32. Empirical sensitivity analyses for λ, N, and the choice of squared vs. Euclidean ground cost would be valuable, especially as performance on CLASSIC is noted to be sensitive.
- Test-time complexity: although training speed is discussed, kNN classification with S-WMD still requires many OT computations. The paper uses WCD for filtering, but a quantitative analysis of test-time latency vs. baselines (and impact of neighbor pruning on accuracy) would be helpful for practical deployment.
- Ablations: while S-WCD and “S-WMD init.” are reported, the relative contributions of A vs. w are not isolated. Ablations with (A only), (w only), and joint (A,w) would clarify what drives the gains and when reweighting is most beneficial.
- Scope of comparisons: the study is comprehensive within classical text representations; however, as of 2016, comparisons to supervised text models (e.g., CNN-based document classifiers or doc2vec variants) would contextualize the benefits of a nonparametric metric-learning approach.
- Minor clarity issues: section numbering (3.2), a few typos (e.g., “word-imporance”), and notation inconsistencies (e.g., p vs. q vs. vocabulary/doc sizes; formula for dual potentials α*,β* includes unclear “1/p 1” term) slightly hinder readability.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and builds on well-established tools. Using Sinkhorn yields differentiability, and invoking Danskin/envelope arguments justifies ignoring implicit derivatives of the transport plan and dual potentials under uniqueness/strong convexity. The approximations are standard and appropriate. The empirical evaluation is broad and supports the central claims, although additional ablations and sensitivity analyses would improve the scientific rigor.

Clarity
Overall presentation is clear and well-structured. The optimization pipeline and complexity are explained, and the intuition behind each design choice (initialization, neighbor filtering) is persuasive. Fixing minor typographical and notation issues would further improve readability.

Originality
Supervising WMD for text via joint ground metric learning and histogram reweighting, optimized with an NCA objective and Sinkhorn-based gradients, is a novel and meaningful contribution in NLP. While related ideas exist in vision for EMD, the specific combination and adaptation to word embeddings and document distances adds originality.

Significance
Given the popularity of WMD and OT in NLP, a supervised, efficient variant with consistent empirical gains is likely to be impactful. The method is practical and likely to be adopted for kNN retrieval, clustering, and as a distance for downstream tasks.

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized)
- Essential: clarify and enforce nonnegativity/regularization for w; add a brief regularization discussion for A (e.g., Frobenius norm, low-rank).
- Essential: report test-time complexity and accuracy/latency trade-offs with neighbor filtering; include sensitivity to λ and N.
- Important: add ablations for (A only) vs. (w only) vs. (A,w), and for squared vs. unsquared ground cost.
- Nice-to-have: compare against at least one supervised neural text classifier to contextualize the gains.
- Minor: fix typos/notation and provide a short note on the theoretical justification (Danskin) for using T* and (α*,β*) in gradients.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (supervised WMD) with a principled, efficient method.
- Strong empirical results across diverse datasets with extensive baselines.
- Practical contributions (initialization, neighbor filtering, released code).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of test-time scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Missing ablations disentangling contributions of A vs. w.
- Scope of comparisons could include more supervised text models.

Overall, this is a solid and practically useful contribution that advances OT-based document metrics and merits consideration for acceptance, pending clarifications and minor additions.
