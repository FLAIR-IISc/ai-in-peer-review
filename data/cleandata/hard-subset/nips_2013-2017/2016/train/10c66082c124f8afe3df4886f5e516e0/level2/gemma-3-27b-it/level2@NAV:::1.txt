## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by incorporating supervision into the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance vector to minimize k-nearest neighbor classification error. A key contribution lies in devising an efficient approximation to the gradient calculation, leveraging the Sinkhorn distance to overcome the computational challenges of the original WMD.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the success of WMD while addressing its limitation of being purely unsupervised. The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the existing landscape of document distance metrics and metric learning. The proposed approach is technically sound, and the use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient computation is a significant contribution, enabling practical application of supervised learning within the WMD framework. The extensive experimental evaluation, encompassing eight datasets and comparison against 26 baselines, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD.  The ablation study, showing the impact of initialization, further validates design choices. 

However, the clarity could be improved in a few areas. The mathematical notation, particularly around the optimization and gradient calculations, feels dense and could benefit from more explanation. While the authors mention limitations, a more thorough discussion of potential failure modes – for instance, scenarios where the linear transformation assumption might not hold – would be valuable. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of λ in the Sinkhorn approximation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform numerous baselines.
*   Addresses a key limitation of WMD (lack of supervision).
*   Efficient gradient approximation makes supervised learning feasible.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Mathematical notation can be dense and challenging.
*   Limited discussion of potential limitations and failure modes.
*   Sensitivity analysis of the λ parameter is missing.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD is a compelling method with demonstrated effectiveness. Despite minor areas for improvement in clarity and discussion, the paper is of high quality and warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to enhance clarity and address the suggested limitations.
