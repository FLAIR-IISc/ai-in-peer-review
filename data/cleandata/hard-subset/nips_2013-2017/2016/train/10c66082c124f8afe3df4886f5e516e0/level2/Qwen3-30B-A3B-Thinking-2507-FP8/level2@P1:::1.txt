

The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an elegant extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates label information to learn a discriminative document metric. The key innovation lies in jointly learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights, while efficiently approximating the gradient using Sinkhorn regularization to avoid the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over 26 baselines across 8 text classification tasks, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on 5/8 datasets.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a strong technical contribution by addressing a meaningful gap in the WMD literature—incorporating supervision without sacrificing the semantic richness of word embeddings. The use of Sinkhorn approximation (Cuturi, 2013) for gradient computation is well-justified and enables practical scalability, with empirical complexity O(TBN[p² + d²(p + r)]), which the authors validate in Table 3. The experimental design is thorough: they evaluate against diverse baselines (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD) and include both unsupervised and supervised metric learning variants. Crucially, they provide clear visualization (Figures 1–2) showing S-WMD’s improved cluster separation and meaningful word weights. The initialization via NCA on WCD (S-WCD) is a thoughtful touch that significantly reduces training time.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
1. **Theoretical Clarity:** The gradient derivation (Section 3.1) is mathematically sound but dense. For readers unfamiliar with optimal transport duality, the transition from Eq. (9) to Eq. (10) could be clarified with a brief intermediate step (e.g., "Using the envelope theorem, the derivative of the primal objective with respect to w follows from the dual variables").  
2. **CLASSIC Dataset Anomaly:** S-WMD underperforms WMD on CLASSIC (Table 2), which the authors attribute to "poor initialization" and "suboptimal squared Euclidean distance." A more systematic analysis—e.g., comparing initialization sensitivity across datasets or testing alternative word distance metrics (e.g., cosine)—would strengthen the argument.  
3. **Computational Efficiency:** While Table 3 shows S-WMD is fast, it lacks comparison to the *total* training time of baselines (e.g., training mSDA or LDA). Including this would better contextualize the efficiency claim.  
4. **Scalability Limits:** The paper notes that the method scales with document vocabulary size (p), but doesn’t discuss how it handles extremely large vocabularies (e.g., >100k words), which is common in real-world text. A brief mention of vocabulary pruning strategies would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that advances the state-of-the-art in document metrics. The combination of theoretical insight (efficient gradient approximation), practical implementation (batch gradient descent with neighbor filtering), and strong empirical results makes it a compelling contribution to NIPS. The work is clearly distinct from prior metric learning (e.g., NCA, LMNN) and related to WMD (Kusner et al., 2015), with thorough related work coverage. The significance is clear: S-WMD is a powerful, scalable tool for text classification that leverages pre-trained embeddings without requiring task-specific retraining.  

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), novel in its supervised WMD framework (Originality), and impactful for text applications (Significance). Minor suggestions for clarity and context would elevate an already strong submission. I recommend acceptance.
