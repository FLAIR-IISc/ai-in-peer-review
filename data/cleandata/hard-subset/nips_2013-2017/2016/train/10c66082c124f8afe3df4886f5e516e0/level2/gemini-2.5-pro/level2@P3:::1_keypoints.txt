- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of Word Mover's Distance that learns a linear embedding transform and word-importance weights for task-specific document distance.  
- Learns parameters by minimizing a stochastic k-NN leave-one-out classification loss in the Neighborhood Components Analysis framework.  
- Main technical challenge: WMD gradient is intractable due to a nested optimal transport problem.  
- Central contribution: uses entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to obtain an efficient, differentiable approximation of the WMD gradient.  
- Derives practical update rules and uses dual variables from the relaxed transport problem to compute gradients, notably for the word-weight vector.  
- Proposes sensible optimization strategies: supervised WCD initialization, batch gradient descent with neighbor filtering to cut computation.  
- Evaluated on eight datasets against 26 strong baselines, with S-WMD achieving best results on five datasets and best average rank.  
- Experimental setup is extensive and fair, using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning and reporting training time.  
- Paper is technically solid: clear problem formulation, sound derivations, and an elegant solution to a computationally hard problem.  
- Writing and organization are clear; background and method sections make the paper largely self-contained.  
- Highly original: novel combination of WMD, NCA, and Sinkhorn regularization to enable supervised ground-metric learning for documents.  
- Significant impact: makes WMD applicable to supervised tasks and likely to influence text classification and metric-learning work.  
- Suggested improvements: analyze sensitivity to the Sinkhorn regularization parameter λ.  
- Suggested improvements: discuss alternative ground metrics beyond squared Euclidean distance.  
- Suggested improvements: visualize lowest-weighted words in addition to important words to show what the model ignores.  
- Acceptance rationale: addresses an important limitation of WMD, presents a sound and novel solution, and provides thorough empirical evidence.  
- No strong arguments against acceptance; listed issues are minor refinements rather than fundamental flaws.
