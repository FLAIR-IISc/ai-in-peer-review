**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to compute an optimal transport cost between documents. The key limitation of WMD is its unsupervised nature. This paper addresses this by learning two components from labeled data: 1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to better suit the classification task, and 2) a vector of importance weights for each word in the vocabulary. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA). A significant technical contribution is a practical way to compute the gradient of this objective. Differentiating through the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and faces issues with non-differentiability. The authors cleverly circumvent this by using an entropy-regularized version of the WMD (based on the Sinkhorn distance), which provides a close, differentiable, and efficiently computable approximation. The paper includes several practical optimizations, such as a strong initialization scheme and a batching strategy, making the approach feasible. The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation on eight datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 strong baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in kNN-based text classification.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-founded and addresses a clear limitation of prior work. The main technical challenge—computing the gradient through the WMD's nested optimization problem—is identified and solved with an elegant and well-justified approximation using the entropy-regularized optimal transport problem. The derivation of the gradients for both the embedding transformation `A` and the word weights `w` appears sound.

The experimental evaluation is extensive and convincing. The authors compare against a very wide array of baselines, including classic methods (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), modern representation learning (mSDA), and the original WMD. Crucially, they also apply state-of-the-art metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN) to the representation-based baselines, making the comparison fair and challenging. The results consistently show that S-WMD is either the top-performing method or highly competitive across eight diverse datasets. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds) provides valuable qualitative insight into what the model learns. The reporting of training times is also a welcome addition, showing the practicality of the method.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear background on word2vec, WMD, and NCA, making the paper accessible. The motivation for the work is stated plainly, and the proposed S-WMD model is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner. The mathematical notation is consistent and clear. The description of the gradient approximation and the optimization speed-ups are detailed enough for an expert to understand the implementation choices. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the overall training procedure.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. While it builds upon existing ideas (WMD, NCA, Sinkhorn distances), the combination is novel and non-trivial. It is the first work, to my knowledge, to successfully incorporate supervision into the WMD framework in a principled and computationally efficient manner. While learning the ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored before (e.g., Wang et al., 2015), this paper introduces the additional, and powerful, idea of simultaneously learning word importance weights, which is a key contribution tailored to the text domain. The solution to the difficult gradient computation problem is a significant contribution in its own right.

**Significance**

The work is highly significant. The original WMD was a major advance in document similarity, and this paper presents a substantial improvement by making it adaptable to specific tasks. Given the impressive performance demonstrated across multiple datasets, S-WMD is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners for kNN-based text classification tasks. The ideas presented—particularly the use of Sinkhorn distances to create differentiable and efficient versions of optimal transport-based losses—could also inspire similar approaches in other domains where WMD/EMD is applicable, such as computer vision. This work clearly advances the state of the art in document distance metrics.

**Pros:**
*   **Novel and important problem:** Addresses the key limitation of the powerful WMD metric by incorporating supervision.
*   **Elegant technical solution:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for the WMD is a clever and effective way to enable gradient-based learning.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The method is shown to outperform an extensive list of 26 competitive baselines on 8 diverse text classification datasets, establishing a new state of the art.
*   **High clarity and organization:** The paper is very well-written, making complex ideas easy to understand.
*   **Practicality:** The authors propose several optimizations (initialization, batching) that make the method computationally feasible, and they provide code for reproducibility.

**Cons / Questions for authors:**
*   The method uses the squared Euclidean distance in the word embedding space. The original WMD paper suggested other norms might be effective. Could the authors comment on this design choice? The paper notes this may be suboptimal for the CLASSIC dataset, suggesting it is a relevant factor.
*   The hyperparameter λ for the Sinkhorn approximation is set to 10. It would be useful to understand how sensitive the model's performance and training stability are to this choice. A brief analysis in the supplementary material would strengthen the paper.
*   The paper provides a strong comparison against other metric learning methods. It would be interesting to also understand how S-WMD compares against end-to-end deep learning models for text classification (e.g., CNNs or RNNs), which also learn task-specific representations from word embeddings. A brief discussion of this alternative approach would help to position the work in the broader landscape.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research. The minor points above are primarily suggestions for further clarification and do not detract from the strength of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
