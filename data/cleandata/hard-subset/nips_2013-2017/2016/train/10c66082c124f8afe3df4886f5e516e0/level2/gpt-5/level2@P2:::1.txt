Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance that learns (i) a linear transformation A of the underlying word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance weight vector w that reweights document histograms. Supervision is incorporated by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (à la NCA) where pairwise document distances are computed via optimal transport over the transformed embeddings and reweighted histograms. Because differentiating through the exact WMD is computationally expensive and not always smooth, the paper uses the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to obtain an efficient, unique transport plan and dual potentials, yielding practical gradients. Training is further accelerated via an S-WCD (NCA over word centroids) initialization and neighborhood truncation based on WCD. Across eight text classification datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised metric learning over various document representations), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and is state-of-the-art on 5/8 datasets; code is released.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), entropy-regularized optimal transport (Cuturi, NIPS’13), and NCA for metric learning. It relates to supervised EMD learning in vision (e.g., ITML-based ground metric learning) but differs by learning both a ground metric on word embeddings and histogram reweighting specific to text classification. Compared to classic text features (BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA) and neural autoencoders (mSDA), the approach leverages pre-trained embeddings and optimal transport as a document metric rather than learning a document representation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective is well-motivated and the approximations are standard. Using Sinkhorn to obtain T* and dual variables for gradients is sensible and yields an efficient procedure. The gradient w.r.t. A is straightforward given the transport plan. The gradient w.r.t. w via dual potentials is reasonable, with the uniqueness (up to constants) addressed by centering.
- A few technical concerns merit clarification:
  1) Training–evaluation mismatch. The loss is written in terms of the exact WMD DA,w, but gradients use the Sinkhorn-regularized solution. Do you also use the regularized distance when computing the loss/probabilities during training, or only for the gradient? At test time, do you report kNN with the exact WMD or the regularized variant? Please clarify, and if different, report sensitivity.
  2) Constraints/regularization on w and A. The formulation appears unconstrained; as d̃a involves normalization by w>da, zero or negative weights could cause instability. Are there positivity constraints (w ≥ 0) and any regularization (e.g., L2 on A and w) to avoid degenerate solutions and control scale? Empirically, how sensitive is performance to the norm of A (given the exp(−D) softmax)?
  3) Notation and dual details. The symbol p is overloaded (norm exponent vs vocabulary size/unique words). The expressions for α*, β* from u, v would benefit from a clearer derivation or citation to the smoothed dual (Cuturi & Peyré). The centering constants should be unambiguous.
  4) Complexity and stopping. Sinkhorn complexity depends on the number of iterations and regularization λ; please report typical iterations, tolerance, and sensitivity to λ (you fix λ=10).
- Empirics: The evaluation is extensive, with tuning via Bayesian optimization and a large set of baselines, including supervised metric learning after PCA. The gains are consistent on short-text datasets; performance lags on some long-document datasets (BBCSPORT/OHSUMED), which is discussed. Training times are reasonable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with clear motivation, method description, and practical training accelerations. However:
- Clean up notation collisions (p used for multiple concepts).
- State constraints/regularization on A and w explicitly.
- Specify whether r < d is used and how r is selected.
- Clarify whether distances in training and testing are exact WMD or regularized, and maintain consistency in naming.

Originality
Incorporating supervision into WMD by jointly learning a ground metric on embeddings and per-word importance weights, optimized via an NCA-style loss with Sinkhorn-based gradients, is a novel and interesting combination. While supervised EMD learning has antecedents in vision, applying it to text with word embeddings and histogram reweighting is a meaningful advance.

Significance
The approach is practically useful: it improves kNN classification across diverse datasets, is efficient enough to train, and releases code. Given the popularity of WMD, a principled supervised extension is likely to be adopted. The idea of learning both A and w inside an OT-based document distance could influence further work (e.g., domain adaptation, multi-task, or end-to-end embedding fine-tuning).

Strengths
- Clear problem and well-motivated method; leverages strengths of WMD while addressing lack of supervision.
- Sound use of Sinkhorn regularization for differentiable, efficient training.
- Clever initialization and neighbor truncation strategies that materially reduce cost.
- Broad empirical comparison with thorough hyperparameter tuning; consistent gains.
- Qualitative analyses (t-SNE, important word visualization) support the claims.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Provide ablations: A only, w only, and both; impact of initialization; effect of λ; number of neighbors N; dimensionality r.
- Discuss and, if possible, include a regularization term on A and w; report its effect on stability and generalization.
- Fairness and external data: explicitly state the pretraining corpus for word2vec and discuss whether competing baselines could benefit similarly from pretrained embeddings (e.g., TF-IDF+NCA vs embedding-based).
- For long documents where TF-IDF+NCA is strong, consider hybrid features (e.g., IDF-informed w initialization) or non-squared ground distances; report results.
- Clarify training/test distance choice and report if exact WMD vs Sinkhorn leads to differences in accuracy and runtime.
- Minor: fix typos/formatting (e.g., “word-imporance”, inconsistent notation), and provide more details on Sinkhorn stopping criteria.

Questions for the authors
- Do you enforce w ≥ 0 and/or add regularization on A and w? How do you prevent degenerate scaling?
- What is r in practice? Do you learn a dimensionality-reducing A? How is r chosen?
- Are loss probabilities computed with exact WMD or Sinkhorn-regularized distances during training? What about at test time?
- How sensitive are results to λ and to the neighbor truncation size N?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of a widely used distance with a principled, efficient method.
- Strong empirical results across many datasets and baselines.
- Technically solid combination of OT and metric learning; likely to impact practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Some gaps in clarity and theoretical justification of training–evaluation mismatch.
- Limited ablation/diagnostics and regularization discussion.
- Weaker performance on some long-document datasets without a clear mitigation.

Overall recommendation
A solid, practical contribution with good empirical support and a clear path to adoption. With clarifications and minor additions (ablations, constraints/regularization), this would be a strong NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
