

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. The authors establish that these methods can be readily extended to saddle-point problems while addressing key challenges related to non-uniform sampling, problem structure, and acceleration. They demonstrate theoretical improvements over existing batch methods and provide empirical validation on machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers. The work connects to foundational literature on saddle-point problems (Rockafellar, 1976) and recent advances in stochastic optimization (SAGA, SVRG).

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction methods in the saddle-point setting. The authors demonstrate how the analysis differs from convex minimization by leveraging monotonicity properties rather than strong convexity. This is particularly valuable as saddle-point problems arise in many machine learning contexts where convex minimization methods don't apply directly.

2. **Practical Significance:** The paper identifies important machine learning applications where existing methods fail (e.g., non-separable losses like ROC-AUC surrogates or structured regularizers), making the proposed algorithms the only scalable options. The experimental results on real datasets (sido, rcv1) convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of non-uniform sampling and acceleration over existing methods.

3. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-structured, with clear theorems and proofs. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) significantly broadens the applicability of the approach beyond saddle-point problems. The paper carefully distinguishes between different types of problem splits (factored vs. individual) and demonstrates their computational implications.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear definitions and consistent notation. The examples in Section 2.1 effectively contextualize the problem. The algorithms (1 and 2) are described precisely, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. **Assumption of Strong Convexity-Concavity:** The paper assumes strong convexity-concavity constants are known, which limits applicability to problems where these constants are difficult to estimate. While the authors mention future work on adaptivity, they could provide more concrete guidance on how to estimate these constants in practice. For instance, they might add a brief discussion of how to estimate λ and γ from data or provide a simple heuristic for their choice.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experiments on non-separable problems are compelling, the comparison with primal methods on the separable rcv1 dataset (Figure 1, right) is somewhat limited. The authors could strengthen this by including additional datasets where both primal and dual approaches are applicable, or by providing more detailed comparisons of computational efficiency (wall-clock time) rather than just number of passes.

3. **Non-Uniform Sampling Explanation:** The paper references [25] for the need of resampling with non-uniform sampling, but doesn't explain why this is necessary. A brief clarification of this technical point would improve the paper's accessibility. The authors might add a sentence or two explaining that non-uniform sampling requires resampling to maintain the correct expectation properties when using the variance reduction technique.

4. **Connection to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it could better contextualize how their approach differs from existing stochastic methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., [11, 12]). A more detailed comparison of their approach with these methods would help readers understand the specific advantages of their variance reduction techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for solving a broad class of machine learning problems. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical analysis is novel and the empirical results support the claims. While the assumption of known strong convexity-concavity constants is a limitation, it's common in this line of work and the authors acknowledge future work on adaptivity.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, significance, and originality. The main weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revised version. The work advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning, particularly for problems involving non-separable losses and regularizers.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to optimization theory with clear practical relevance. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.
