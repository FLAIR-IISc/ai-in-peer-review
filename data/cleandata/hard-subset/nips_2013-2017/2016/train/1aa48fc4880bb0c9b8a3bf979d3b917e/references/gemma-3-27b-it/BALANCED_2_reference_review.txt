This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for solving saddle-point problems, a common formulation in machine learning arising from duality. The authors extend these algorithms, initially designed for convex minimization, to this broader setting and provide a convergence analysis. A key contribution is framing the saddle-point problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator, enabling a more general convergence result. They also highlight the importance of splitting the problem appropriately and demonstrate the benefits of non-uniform sampling. Finally, they present an acceleration technique based on the "catalyst" framework.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant and important problem in machine learning optimization. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a natural and valuable direction. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence guarantees, is a strong point, particularly the connection to monotone operator theory which broadens the applicability of the results.  The authors carefully consider different splitting strategies and demonstrate the advantages of non-uniform sampling, supported by experimental results. The inclusion of examples in machine learning, particularly related to non-separable losses and regularizers, strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The extensive related work section demonstrates a good understanding of the field. The thorough experimental validation, including comparison with existing methods and ablation studies, is commendable.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses, primarily in terms of clarity and presentation. The sheer volume of mathematical notation and different algorithm variations (batch, stochastic, accelerated, with/without resampling, different sampling strategies) makes it difficult to follow the core ideas.  The extensive use of assumptions (A), (B), (C) without consistently referencing them within the derivations hinders readability. The connection between the theoretical results and the specific machine learning examples provided could be made more explicit.  While the authors mention adaptivity to strong convexity-concavity constants as future work, a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to these constants in the experiments would be beneficial. The experimental section, while comprehensive, could benefit from clearer visualizations and a more concise presentation of the results. The performance gains on the rcv1 dataset, being applied to a separable problem, feels somewhat less compelling given the existence of specialized primal-dual methods. The paper also makes several references to appendices for detailed proofs, which, while reasonable, can discourage a quick read-through and understanding of the core contributions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with theoretical guarantees is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis is rigorous and builds upon well-established concepts from monotone operator theory.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers is highly relevant.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper includes extensive related work, a detailed experimental evaluation, and analysis of different splitting strategies.
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to impact the optimization landscape for a broad class of machine learning problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Clarity:** The paper is dense and mathematically challenging, hindering accessibility.
* **Presentation:** The large number of variations and assumptions make it difficult to grasp the key takeaways.
* **Reproducibility:** While the paper provides details, the complexity might make reproduction challenging without significant effort. 
* **Limited Insight from rcv1:** The results on the separable rcv1 dataset are less compelling.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. While the clarity and presentation could be improved, the theoretical soundness, novelty, and practical relevance are strong enough to warrant acceptance. However, addressing the clarity issues would significantly increase the impact and accessibility of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** 

Specifically, I recommend:

* **Streamlining the notation and derivations:** Reduce redundancy and consistently reference assumptions.
* **Improving the clarity of the experimental section:** Use more concise visualizations and focus on the key insights.
* **Adding a discussion on the sensitivity to strong convexity/concavity constants.**
* **Providing more explicit connections between the theoretical results and the machine learning examples.**
* **Consider a more concise presentation of the various algorithm variants.**  Perhaps a table summarizing the differences in key components would be helpful.
