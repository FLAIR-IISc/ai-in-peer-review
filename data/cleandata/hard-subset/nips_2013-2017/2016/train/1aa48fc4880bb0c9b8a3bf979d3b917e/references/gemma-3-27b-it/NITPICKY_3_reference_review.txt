This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG) and stochastic average gradient (SAGA) methods to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating improvements over existing batch algorithms. The work is framed within the broader context of monotone operator theory, highlighting the generality of the proposed approach. While the ideas are generally sound and the theoretical results appear correct, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws in presentation, notation, and justification, impacting its overall clarity and readability.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a valuable contribution, as existing methods often struggle with non-separable objectives.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis appears thorough and provides a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of the algorithms. The connection to monotone operator theory is a plus.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The presented experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms, particularly the accelerated version, on relevant machine learning problems.
*   **Generality:** The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the work beyond saddle-point problems.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Notation:** The paper is dense and suffers from inconsistent notation. For example, the meanings of symbols like `λ` and `γ` shift subtly depending on the context, making it difficult to follow. The frequent use of inequalities without clear justification for equality cases is distracting.
*   **Overly Detailed Justifications:** The paper sometimes spends an excessive amount of space justifying relatively minor points. For instance, the detailed discussion of norms for bilinear problems (Section 2.4) is unnecessarily lengthy and detracts from the core contributions.
*   **Redundancy & Repetition:** Several ideas are repeated throughout the paper, making it feel verbose. The connection between the saddle-point problem and convex minimization is revisited multiple times without adding significant new information.
*   **Inconsistent Level of Detail:** Some proofs are highly detailed, while others are relegated to appendices with minimal explanation. The justification for specific algorithmic choices (e.g., step-size selection) is sometimes lacking.
*   **Formatting and Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typos detract from the professional appearance of the paper. (e.g. inconsistent equation numbering, occasional grammatical errors)
*   **Experiments:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and baseline comparisons. The claim that primal methods are inferior *without* showing a comparison of equivalent effort (similar number of oracle calls) is weak.

**Specific Concerns & Examples:**

*   **Assumption (A):** The definition of strongly (λ, γ)-convex-concave is unusual and potentially confusing. Standard definitions often involve a single parameter. The justification for needing separate parameters is weak and adds complexity.
*   **Section 2.3:** The discussion of sampling probabilities is unclear. The meaning of “good probability vectors” is not well-defined. The explanation of how different sampling schemes affect the smoothness constant `L̄(π)` is convoluted.
*   **Section 2.4:** The detailed discussion and bounding of various matrix norms (op, F, max) for the bilinear case feels excessive and doesn’t significantly contribute to the main results. The claims about improved bounds require careful scrutiny.
*   **Section 3 & 4:** The algorithmic descriptions are dense and lack clear pseudocode. The discussion of memory requirements for SAGA with factored splits is unclear.
*   **Section 5:** The statement that the accelerated algorithm is “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” is a strong claim that requires more robust justification.
*   **Section 7:** The experimental setup is not described in sufficient detail. The choice of hyperparameters, the initialization scheme, and the evaluation metrics should be clearly specified.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems.
*   The algorithms demonstrate practical improvements over existing approaches.
*   The connection to monotone operator theory broadens the applicability of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues.
*   The level of detail is inconsistent and sometimes excessive.
*   The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail and justification.
*   Numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its technical merits, I am leaning towards **rejection**. While the core ideas are valuable, the paper's numerous flaws in presentation, notation, and justification significantly hinder its readability and impact. The excessive detail on minor points, combined with the lack of clarity in key areas, makes it difficult for the reader to grasp the main contributions of the work. A major revision addressing these issues is necessary before this paper could be considered for acceptance. The authors need to prioritize clarity, conciseness, and consistency in their presentation. A more thorough experimental evaluation with clear baseline comparisons would also strengthen the paper.
