

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is convex-concave and smooth, and M is non-smooth but simple. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, showing how the analysis differs from convex minimization. They demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, provide an accelerated version via the "catalyst" framework, and extend their results to monotone operators. The paper shows applications to machine learning problems including supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning. The work builds on prior NIPS papers on variance reduction for convex optimization (SAGA, SVRG) but extends them to a fundamentally different problem class.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis for saddle-point problems, which is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The authors correctly identify key differences between convex minimization and saddle-point settings, particularly regarding the role of non-uniform sampling and the need for different step-size analysis.

2. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates clear advantages of their approach through well-designed experiments on real-world machine learning problems. The results show significant improvements over existing batch and stochastic methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

3. **Broad Applicability**: The extension to monotone operators is a valuable theoretical contribution that broadens the scope of the work beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, making the results more widely applicable.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions of assumptions, theorems, and algorithms. The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical implementation is well-explained.

## Weaknesses
1. **Strong Assumptions**: The paper assumes strong convexity-concavity (assumption A), which is a significant limitation for many practical applications. While the authors mention future work on adaptivity, they don't address how to handle cases where these constants are unknown, which is a common practical issue.

2. **Comparison with Relevant Work**: The paper could better contextualize its contribution relative to existing saddle-point optimization methods. For example, the work of Chambolle and Pock (2011) on primal-dual methods for saddle-point problems isn't sufficiently contrasted with the proposed approach. It would be valuable to clarify how the proposed methods differ from and improve upon these existing techniques.

3. **Experimental Scope**: While the experiments are well-executed, they are limited to two datasets. More comprehensive experiments across a wider range of machine learning problems would strengthen the claims about practical significance. The paper could also include a more detailed comparison with the most relevant recent methods for saddle-point problems.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that "for bilinear saddle-point problems, we only need to store O(n+d) elements for the factored splits," but doesn't fully explain how this storage advantage translates to computational efficiency in practice, especially compared to alternative methods.

## Overall Assessment
This is a solid theoretical contribution that extends a well-established class of optimization methods to a fundamentally different problem class. The paper demonstrates clear theoretical advantages and practical benefits through well-executed experiments. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption limits the immediate applicability to some problems, the authors correctly identify this as a limitation for future work.

The paper meets NIPS's standards for quality and significance, with a clear theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in optimization methods for machine learning. The originality is strong, as the extension to saddle-point problems is novel and the analysis differs significantly from existing work on convex minimization.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. The authors should address how to handle cases where the strong convexity-concavity constants are unknown, perhaps by discussing adaptive variants or providing experimental evidence of robustness to parameter choices.

2. A more thorough comparison with relevant existing methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., Chambolle-Pock, Arrow-Hurwicz variants) would strengthen the paper's positioning in the literature.

3. Expanding the experimental section to include additional datasets and more comprehensive comparisons would further validate the practical significance of the approach.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the storage advantages mentioned in the factored splits section.

## Final Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that extends stochastic optimization methods to saddle-point problems with clear practical benefits. The work is well-executed and meets the high standards of NIPS. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, it's a common starting point in optimization literature, and the authors appropriately acknowledge this as a direction for future work. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
