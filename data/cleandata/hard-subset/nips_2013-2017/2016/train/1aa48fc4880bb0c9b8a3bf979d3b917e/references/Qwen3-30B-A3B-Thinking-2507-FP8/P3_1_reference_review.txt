

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, which are fundamental in machine learning through duality formulations. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point context, demonstrating that the analysis differs significantly from convex minimization due to the monotonicity properties of saddle-point problems. They show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency and extend their methods with acceleration using the "catalyst" framework. The paper also establishes connections to monotone operator theory, broadening the applicability of their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper provides the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting, which is a significant contribution. The authors correctly identify that saddle-point problems require a different analysis than convex minimization, leveraging monotonicity properties rather than strong convexity. This distinction is well-justified and properly developed in Section 6.

2. **Practical Significance**: The authors convincingly demonstrate the importance of their approach through multiple machine learning examples where non-separable losses or regularizers prevent the use of standard convex optimization techniques (Section 2.1). The experiments (Section 7) clearly show that their methods outperform existing batch and stochastic algorithms, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

3. **Comprehensive Coverage**: The paper thoroughly addresses the key aspects of the problem: problem formulation, assumptions, algorithms, theoretical analysis, extensions to monotone operators, and experimental validation. The connection to monotone operator theory in Section 6 is particularly valuable, showing the broader applicability beyond saddle-point problems.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3), and the theoretical results are well-motivated and explained. The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem setup to analysis to experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The analysis assumes strong convexity-concavity (Assumption A), which limits the applicability to problems where this property does not hold. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, it would be valuable to discuss potential extensions to non-strongly convex-concave cases, which are common in practice.

2. **Limited Connection to Existing Saddle-Point Literature**: The paper mentions several existing batch algorithms for saddle-point problems (Section 2.2) but doesn't sufficiently contrast their approach with other recent stochastic methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., the stochastic primal-dual methods in [11, 12]). A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

3. **Complexity of Theoretical Analysis**: While the theoretical results are rigorous, the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* the saddle-point analysis differs from convex minimization. Section 6's discussion of monotone operators is technically sound but could be made more accessible to readers less familiar with operator theory.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, but the experimental section could provide more details on how the sampling probabilities were computed in practice (beyond the theoretical bounds), which would help practitioners implement these methods.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by experimental validation. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, making it a complete and honest contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithms are clearly presented. Some of the theoretical explanations could be made more intuitive for a broader audience.

**Originality**: This is a novel application of stochastic variance reduction to saddle-point problems, with a significantly different analysis than convex minimization. The connection to monotone operators further extends the novelty.

**Significance**: Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning, and the proposed methods enable efficient optimization for non-separable problems where existing techniques fail. The acceleration framework provides a substantial practical improvement, making this a significant contribution to the field.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to optimization for machine learning, extending the successful stochastic variance reduction techniques to the saddle-point setting where they had not been previously applied. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experiments are well-designed, and the practical significance is clear. While the restriction to strongly convex-concave problems is a limitation, this is common in the literature and the authors appropriately acknowledge it.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel and technically sound solution. It advances the state of the art in optimization methods and provides both theoretical insights and practical algorithms.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (primarily to clarify the connection to existing saddle-point optimization literature and to provide more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical distinctions).

## Key Arguments for Acceptance
- First rigorous analysis of SVRG/SAGA for saddle-point problems
- Demonstrates practical advantages through compelling experiments
- Establishes a connection to monotone operator theory, broadening applicability
- Provides a theoretically grounded approach to non-separable problems

## Key Arguments for Rejection (None)
The paper has no major weaknesses that would warrant rejection; the limitations noted are common in the field and appropriately acknowledged.
