This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating practical improvements over existing methods. The core idea – adapting established variance reduction techniques to a non-convex setting – is conceptually straightforward, yet the analysis and demonstration of its applicability beyond standard convex minimization (to monotone operators) is a noteworthy contribution. However, while the theoretical extensions are presented with rigor, the empirical validation feels somewhat incomplete and doesn't fully justify the theoretical complexity.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting appears correct and extends the existing theory to a broader class of problems (monotone operators). The connection to finding zeros of monotone operators provides a useful generalization.
* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning, particularly in duality-based formulations. Efficient algorithms for these problems are valuable.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the presentation of the algorithms is concise.
* **Identifies Key Considerations:** The paper correctly highlights the importance of appropriate splitting strategies for the operator *B* and the impact of non-uniform sampling.

**Weaknesses & Concerns:**

* **Incremental Innovation:** While the extensions are valid, the core idea of applying SVRG/SAGA to a saddle-point problem isn’t a radical departure. The paper’s significance hinges heavily on the analysis and the improvements realized in practice, which aren't fully convincing. The contribution feels more like a careful application and generalization of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic direction.
* **Overly Optimistic Experimental Setup:** The experiments are tailored towards situations where the proposed methods *should* excel – specifically, problems with efficiently computable proximal operators and a structure suitable for non-uniform sampling. The chosen datasets and problem formulations feel somewhat contrived to highlight the method’s strengths.  More diverse and challenging benchmarks (including those where the theoretical advantages are less pronounced) are needed. Comparing against genuinely competitive alternatives (e.g., accelerated primal-dual methods specifically designed for saddle-point problems, beyond the accelerated forward-backward used as a baseline) is also crucial.
* **Complexity Discussion Lacks Practicality:** While the paper details the theoretical complexity, the practical implications of parameters like *L* and *L̄* aren’t fully addressed. Estimating these parameters in real-world applications can be challenging, and the sensitivity of performance to these estimates isn't discussed.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results for several variants (SAGA, SVRG, accelerated versions, uniform vs. non-uniform sampling). However, a more thorough ablation study would be helpful to understand the individual contributions of each component.  For example, what is the performance gain from acceleration when *L* is small?
* **Catalyst Framework Discussion is Brief:** The extension of the “catalyst” framework is presented as a significant advantage, but the explanation is somewhat cursory. A more detailed justification for its effectiveness in this setting would be appreciated.




**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems, filling a gap in the existing literature.
* The extension to monotone operators is a valuable generalization.
* The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate potential improvements over existing methods in specific settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core idea is incremental, and the novelty is primarily in the analysis rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.
* The experimental validation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed methods.
* The complexity discussion lacks practical guidance for real-world applications. 
* The paper feels overly focused on a specific class of problems (those well-suited to non-uniform sampling and efficient proximal operators).




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical work is sound and the extensions are valid, the paper doesn’t present a sufficiently compelling case for its practical significance. The experimental evaluation is too narrow, and the theoretical complexity analysis doesn’t translate into clear guidance for practitioners. The paper is a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of stochastic optimization, but it falls short of the originality and impact expected for acceptance at NIPS.  I would encourage the authors to broaden their experimental evaluation and provide a more practical discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and sensitivities.
