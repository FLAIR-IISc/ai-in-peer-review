This paper investigates stochastic variance reduction methods applied to saddle-point problems, extending existing techniques like SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The authors provide convergence analysis and demonstrate the applicability of their algorithms to problems arising in machine learning, particularly those with non-separable loss or regularizers. They also explore different splitting strategies and propose an accelerated variant. While the topic is relevant and the extensions of SVRG/SAGA are a natural progression, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Saddle-point problems are increasingly common in machine learning, particularly in duality-based approaches.
*   **Natural Extension:** Applying variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems is a logical and potentially impactful direction.
*   **Convergence Analysis:** Providing convergence guarantees is a valuable contribution, although the analysis relies on strong assumptions.
*   **Acceleration Framework:** The extension of the catalyst framework is a positive addition.
*   **Detailed Discussion of Splits:** The exploration of different splitting strategies is insightful.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Pedantic Notation & Definitions:** The paper is burdened with excessively detailed and, at times, redundant notation. For example, defining weighted Euclidean and dual norms with λ and γ (A) is unnecessarily verbose and clutters the presentation. The definition of B(x,y) is repeated and unnecessarily complex.
*   **Inconsistent Use of Notation:** The authors frequently redefine concepts without clear indication. The meaning of 'L' shifts contextually. The definition of `Ω` and `Ω*` are inconsistent across the sections.
*   **Missing Proof Details:** Several claims, particularly those in the appendices, lack sufficient detail. For instance, the proof sketch in Appendix B.1 is inadequate.
*   **Unjustified Claims of Superiority:** The statement that the accelerated algorithm is "always superior" to accelerated batch algorithms (Introduction) is a strong claim requiring more rigorous justification than presented.
*   **Lack of Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup is inadequately described. The choice of parameters, the specific implementation details, and a more thorough analysis of the results are needed. The scaling of the results is not clearly shown (log scale is requested but not always implemented).
*   **Redundant Discussion of Bilinear Cases:** The detailed analysis of bilinear saddle-point problems is overly lengthy and dominates the paper, overshadowing the broader applicability to general convex-concave problems.
*   **Minor Mathematical Errors:** Several instances of missing or incorrect indexing were observed. For example, the indexing in Eq. (2) is unclear.
*   **Formatting Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, inconsistent equation numbering) distract from readability.
* **Lack of clear motivation for some design choices:** Why were certain sampling schemes chosen over others? What are the practical implications of these choices?

**Specific Comments:**

*   **Introduction:** The sentence "While the algorithmic extension is straightforward..." is misleading. The challenges associated with saddle-point problems are significant, and the extension is not trivial.
*   **Section 2.2:** The discussion of the forward-backward algorithm and its acceleration is unnecessarily detailed and doesn't add substantial insight.
*   **Section 2.4:** The tables of complexities are difficult to interpret and lack clear explanation of the assumptions. The bounds are also too strong (e.g. comparing nd and max{n,d}).
*   **Section 3 & 4:** The repetition of notation and the lack of concise summaries make these sections difficult to follow.
*   **Section 5:** The analysis of the accelerated algorithm lacks clarity and requires more detailed explanation.
*   **Section 7:** The experimental results are presented in a confusing manner. The selection of the parameters is not sufficiently justified. The authors should also provide error bars or confidence intervals.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem and extends existing algorithms to a new setting.
*   The convergence analysis provides some theoretical justification for the proposed methods.
*   The exploration of different splitting strategies is insightful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly verbose, with numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.
*   The lack of experimental detail and the weak experimental results raise concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The strong claims of superiority are not adequately supported.
*   The overall presentation is lacking in clarity and conciseness.
*   The extensive focus on bilinear cases obscures the general applicability of the methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting contributions, the numerous flaws and inconsistencies significantly detract from the paper's overall quality. While the technical contributions are present, they are buried under a layer of unnecessary complexity and a lack of polish. I believe that the paper, in its current state, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision, addressing the issues outlined above, would be necessary to make it suitable for publication.

**Recommendation:** **Reject** (with strong encouragement for revision and resubmission). The authors should significantly simplify the presentation, provide more rigorous experimental results, and address the identified weaknesses before resubmitting.
