This paper presents a significant and timely contribution by extending stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to the general class of composite saddle-point problems. The core idea is to apply the variance reduction principle, which has proven highly successful for finite-sum convex minimization (e.g., SVRG, SAGA), to the gradient operator associated with the saddle-point formulation. While the algorithmic adaptations are conceptually straightforward, the main novelty and strength of this work lie in its theoretical analysis. The authors depart from the standard analyses for convex minimization and instead leverage the framework of monotone operators. This not only provides a sound basis for proving linear convergence but also demonstrates that the proposed methods are applicable to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper further introduces practical improvements, including different ways to decompose the problem ("factored splits") and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling, and shows how to achieve acceleration via a "Catalyst"-like approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **High Novelty and Significance:** This work is the first to provide linearly convergent, incremental gradient algorithms for general saddle-point problems. This is a major step forward. Saddle-point formulations are fundamental to many areas of machine learning, including robust optimization, structured prediction with non-separable losses (like AUC), and duality in general. By making these problems amenable to large-scale SVR techniques, this paper unlocks the potential for training a much wider class of models efficiently. This is not an incremental improvement but a foundational contribution that opens a new direction for algorithm design.
*   **Bold and Elegant Theoretical Framework:** The choice to analyze the algorithms through the lens of monotone operators is a key strength. This approach is more general and powerful than a direct (and likely more cumbersome) extension of existing SVR proofs. It demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying optimization principles and broadens the paper's impact beyond saddle-point problems. Proving linear convergence using only monotonicity properties, without relying on co-coercivity, is a notable technical achievement that distinguishes this work from previous efforts to generalize SVR methods.
*   **Practical Relevance and Insights:** The paper successfully connects theory to practice. The discussion on "factored splits" versus individual element splits for bilinear problems is insightful and offers practical guidance. The theoretical and empirical demonstration that non-uniform sampling is not just helpful but essential for outperforming batch methods is a critical finding. The inclusion of an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework further strengthens the practical utility, yielding an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly states its assumptions, presents the algorithms, and articulates its contributions. The summary table of complexities (Table 1) is particularly effective at contextualizing the proposed methods against existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Somewhat Incomplete Theoretical Components:** While the overall theoretical contribution is strong, a few pieces feel incomplete. The acceleration framework is formally proven only for SVRG, with the authors noting it "should work for SAGA." While this is a reasonable claim, a formal proof would have made the contribution more complete. Similarly, the analysis of SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step, which is often avoided in practice, creating a small gap between theory and the experimental implementation.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while convincing in their message, are conducted on only two datasets. They effectively demonstrate the superiority of accelerated SVRG over batch methods and highlight the failure of uniform sampling. However, a broader set of experiments on different problem types (e.g., robust optimization) or a more direct comparison with other stochastic saddle-point methods could have further strengthened the paper's empirical claims.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to optimization for machine learning. Its primary strength is its high degree of novelty and significance. It bridges the gap between the highly successful field of stochastic variance reduction and the broad, important class of saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is both novel and powerful, providing a new perspective via monotone operators.

The identified weaknesses—namely the lack of a formal acceleration proof for SAGA and the limited experimental suite—are minor in the context of the paper's overall contribution. I view these as opportunities for future work rather than critical flaws that undermine the validity or importance of the current results. As a reviewer who prioritizes bold new ideas and directions, I find this paper exemplary. It tackles a difficult and relevant problem, introduces a novel analytical approach, and delivers algorithms that demonstrably advance the state of the art.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **First to show linear convergence for SVR methods on saddle-point problems.** This is a major theoretical and practical breakthrough.
2.  **Novel analysis via monotone operators** is elegant, general, and will likely influence future work in this area.
3.  **Addresses a clear need** for efficient solvers for problems with non-separable losses and regularizers, which are common but were previously difficult to scale.
4.  **Provides a complete package:** SVRG, SAGA, non-uniform sampling, and an acceleration scheme, all backed by strong theory and convincing experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The acceleration proof is not provided for all proposed variants (i.e., SAGA).
2.  The experimental validation, while positive, could be more comprehensive.

Given the substantial strengths, which lie in conceptual novelty and significance, the weaknesses are easily outweighed. This paper will be highly cited and will spur further research in this domain. I strongly recommend acceptance.
