Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes variance-reduced stochastic algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex-concave with a tractable proximal operator, and K is smooth. The authors extend SVRG and SAGA to this setting by operating on the monotone operator B(x,y)=(∂_x K, -∂_y K), allowing B to be decomposed into Lipschitz components without requiring each component to be a gradient of a scalar function. They provide linear convergence rates under strong convexity-concavity and introduce non-uniform sampling schemes that exploit problem structure (e.g., bilinear K with “factored” row/column splits). They further adapt the “catalyst”/proximal-point acceleration idea to their setting, claiming improved complexities, and argue the framework extends to more general monotone operator inclusions beyond saddle-point problems.

This sits at the intersection of several lines: SVRG/SAGA for finite sums (Johnson & Zhang ’13; Defazio et al. ’14), primal-dual and forward-backward methods for saddle points (Chambolle–Pock ’11; Rockafellar; Nesterov acceleration), stochastic forward-backward/VI methods (Rosasco–Villa–Vũ ’14; Nemirovski’s Mirror-Prox), and stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods for separable saddle points (Zhang & Xiao ’14; Zhu & Storkey ’15). The paper’s main claim is to provide the first linear convergence analysis of variance-reduced methods for non-separable saddle-point problems, and to handle monotone operators without relying on co-coercivity.

Strengths:
- Theoretical development: Extending SVRG/SAGA to composite saddle-point problems with a clear monotone operator viewpoint is well motivated. The analysis that avoids co-coercivity and still achieves linear rates under strong convexity/concavity is non-trivial and, to my knowledge, was not standard at the time.
- Structural sampling: The discussion on non-uniform sampling and “factored” splits for bilinear K is thoughtful, and the resulting L̄(π) bounds help explain when non-uniform strategies give tangible gains.
- Acceleration: The proximal-point/catalyst-style outer loop combined with SVRG inner loops is natural here; the analysis via proximal-point linear contraction is clean, and the complexity trade-offs are explicit.
- Broader scope: Framing the methods as solving monotone operator inclusions increases potential applicability (e.g., variational inequalities), albeit the experiments are focused on ML tasks.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical validation is limited. Only two datasets are used, one with non-separable structures, and evaluations are primarily in terms of “passes over data” rather than wall-clock time. There are no comparisons to strong contemporary primal-dual baselines beyond basic (accelerated) forward-backward, e.g., Chambolle–Pock variants, Mirror-Prox, SPDC/APCG-type methods, or stochastic PDHG/SPDHG. Given the claims of practical superiority (e.g., “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms”), a more thorough empirical study is needed.
- Theory–practice mismatch in SAGA experiments. The linear-rate guarantee for non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step, but the experiments follow a heuristic mixture without resampling. It is unclear whether the theoretical rates translate to the implemented variant.
- Assumption burden and constants. The analysis requires known strong convexity/concavity parameters (λ, γ), precise Lipschitz constants (L, L̄), and tractable prox for M. Many ML-relevant non-separable proximals are not O(n+d), and some complexities hide substantial logarithmic/constant factors. The cost of computing sampling probabilities (e.g., row/column norms) and memory/storage overhead for SAGA under different splits are not thoroughly quantified in wall-clock terms.
- Novelty is somewhat incremental from an algorithmic viewpoint. The algorithms are direct adaptations of SVRG/SAGA to the operator B with a proximal step for M. The main novelty lies in the analysis and sampling; the “catalyst” acceleration is a fairly straightforward application of a known framework.
- Claims are occasionally too strong. Statements like “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” are not universally substantiated, particularly for non-bilinear K and in the presence of strong accelerated PD baselines.

Quality:
The paper is technically solid overall, with clear theorems and careful definitions. However, most proofs are deferred to the appendix; I did not fully verify all steps. The use of monotonicity rather than co-coercivity is interesting. The reliance on known problem constants and exact prox operators limits robustness. Empirical support falls short of the theoretical scope.

Clarity:
Generally well written, but dense. The choice of weighted norms and scaling (λ, γ) is sensible but could be explained more intuitively. Some claims could be toned down. Implementation details (tuning, mini-batch sizes, prox runtimes) need more transparency. There are minor presentation issues (e.g., references without full bibliographic info, leftover “th Conference” artifact).

Originality:
Moderate. Extending variance-reduction to composite saddle points and general monotone inclusions without co-coercivity appears novel and useful. The sampling analysis for bilinear/factored splits is a nice addition. The acceleration component is less original.

Significance:
Potentially impactful for non-separable saddle-point problems where existing separable-coordinate methods do not apply. Yet, the limited empirical evidence and missing comparisons to strong baselines weaken the case for broad practical significance at this stage.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid and general theoretical contribution (linear rates for SVRG/SAGA-style methods in saddle-point/monotone settings).
- Insightful treatment of non-uniform sampling and factored splits.
- Clean acceleration via proximal-point outer loop.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is limited and omits key baselines; wall-clock gains are not convincingly demonstrated.
- Practical variants used in experiments deviate from the theoretically justified versions.
- Strong claims not fully supported; reliance on problem constants and exact prox may limit applicability.

Questions and suggestions:
- Include wall-clock comparisons and ablations (uniform vs non-uniform sampling, minibatch sizes), and add baselines: Chambolle–Pock (and accelerated variants), Mirror-Prox, SPDC/APCG, SPDHG.
- Report memory/computation overheads for SAGA under different splits; quantify the one-time cost to compute non-uniform probabilities.
- Clarify how λ, γ, L, and L̄ are estimated in practice, and the sensitivity of performance to mis-specification; discuss adaptivity.
- Evaluate on additional tasks (e.g., large-scale bilinear problems, variational inequalities) and provide code.
- Reconcile the resampling requirement in theory with the heuristic used in experiments, or provide guarantees for the implemented variant.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theory is interesting and could be significant, but the empirical validation and comparisons are too limited for NeurIPS standards, and some claims are stronger than the evidence provided.

Confidence: High on the empirical assessment; medium on the fine details of the theoretical proofs.
