### Summary

This paper presents the first extension of stochastic variance reduction gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods to the setting of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The primary motivation is to handle machine learning objectives with non-separable loss functions or regularizers, which cannot be addressed by standard SVRG/SAGA applied to the primal or dual convex minimization problems. The authors show that the algorithmic extensions are straightforward, but the main contributions lie in the novel convergence analysis and the broader implications.

The key contributions are:
1.  Providing the first linear convergence proofs for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting. The analysis is framed within the more general theory of monotone operators, which does not require the co-coercivity assumption used in many analyses for convex minimization, thus broadening the applicability of the results.
2.  Demonstrating that the saddle-point formulation allows for different ways to decompose the problem (e.g., "factored" splits) and highlighting the critical role of non-uniform sampling for achieving state-of-the-art theoretical rates and practical performance.
3.  Showing that these methods can be systematically accelerated using a simple application of the "Catalyst" framework, leading to an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods under certain conditions.
4.  Empirically validating the proposed methods on a binary classification task with non-separable loss and regularizer, demonstrating significant practical gains over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Detailed Review

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It bridges the gap between the highly successful variance reduction techniques for finite-sum problems and the important class of saddle-point problems.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs provided in the appendix. The analysis via monotone operators is elegant and powerful, providing a more general foundation for these algorithms than a direct extension of existing SVRG/SAGA proofs might have. This approach correctly identifies that co-coercivity is not present in the saddle-point setting and relies only on monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the resulting complexity bounds (summarized effectively in Table 1) are rigorously derived. The experiments are carefully designed to showcase the exact setting where the proposed methods are most valuable—problems where primal methods are not applicable. The authors are honest about the limitations, such as the need to know problem constants, and correctly position their work relative to SOTA methods for separable problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, introduces the problem formulation and assumptions systematically, and then presents the algorithms and their theoretical guarantees in a logical progression.
*   Section 2 does a great job of setting the stage by defining the problem class, providing concrete ML examples (non-separable losses/regularizers, robust optimization), and reviewing existing batch and stochastic methods. This context is invaluable.
*   The inclusion of Table 1 is extremely helpful, providing a concise summary of the theoretical complexities and making the advantages of the proposed methods immediately apparent.
*   The algorithms are presented clearly. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is a highlight, effectively communicating the generality of the theoretical contribution.
*   The experimental section is clear, and the plots effectively convey the main message.

A minor suggestion for improvement could be to briefly mention the monotone operator perspective in the introduction, as it is a cornerstone of the paper's theoretical novelty.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is high. While the SVRG and SAGA algorithms themselves are direct adaptations of existing templates, the key novelty lies in:
1.  The formulation and application of these methods to the general class of decomposable saddle-point problems. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this problem class.
2.  The convergence analysis is highly original. By leveraging the framework of monotone operators, the authors provide a proof technique that is distinct from the standard potential function analyses for SVRG/SAGA and is more broadly applicable. This is a significant theoretical insight.
3.  The analysis of different splitting strategies (e.g., "factored") and the rigorous incorporation of non-uniform sampling are novel in this context and prove to be crucial for performance.
The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related work in convex optimization ([2, 3, 7]), saddle-point optimization ([20]), and monotone operators ([29, 30]).

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is substantial. Variance reduction methods have had a major impact on large-scale machine learning, but their application has been largely confined to problems with separable structure. This work breaks down that barrier, making these powerful techniques available for a much wider range of problems, including:
*   Structured output prediction with non-decomposable losses (e.g., AUC maximization).
*   Problems with complex, non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso).
*   Robust optimization and game theory settings that naturally lead to minimax formulations.

The theoretical framework based on monotone operators is also significant, as it provides a unified way to understand variance reduction and may inspire further work on extending other optimization techniques beyond the convex setting. Researchers and practitioners working on the problems mentioned above are very likely to use and build upon these ideas. The work demonstrably advances the state of the art in stochastic optimization for machine learning.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novelty and Impact:** It is the first paper to provide linearly convergent stochastic variance-reduced methods for a general class of saddle-point problems, significantly broadening the applicability of these important algorithms.
2.  **Strong Theory:** The convergence analysis via monotone operators is elegant, general, and technically sound. It represents a significant theoretical contribution beyond a simple adaptation of existing proofs.
3.  **Comprehensive Scope:** The paper thoroughly covers the topic, including two core algorithms (SVRG, SAGA), acceleration, analysis of different sampling schemes, and a general theoretical extension.
4.  **Clear Practical Relevance:** The methods are validated on well-chosen, realistic machine learning problems where existing SVRG/SAGA methods fail, demonstrating substantial empirical improvements over baseline algorithms.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The proposed algorithms are direct analogues of SVRG and SAGA. The core innovation is in the analysis and application domain, not in the algorithm design itself. (This is a minor point, as the contribution is still very strong).
2.  **Dependence on Unknown Constants:** Like many similar methods, the algorithms require knowledge of strong convexity/concavity and Lipschitz constants to set step sizes. The paper acknowledges this and suggests adaptive methods as future work, but it remains a practical limitation of the current work.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a well-executed piece of work that solves an important problem with both theoretical depth and practical relevance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
