Summary
This paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) from finite-sum convex minimization to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems with a smooth part K and proximable M. The key idea is to operate on the monotone operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK), allow decompositions B = ∑i Bi that need not be gradients of scalar functions, and plug variance-reduced (unbiased) estimators of B into a forward–backward framework. The analysis gives linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity, with constants depending on the operator Lipschitz constants (L) and sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π). The paper also proposes non-uniform sampling schemes (e.g., for bilinear K with row/column norms), a SAGA variant with factored storage, and an acceleration via a proximal point (“catalyst”) outer loop, claiming improvements over batch forward–backward and its acceleration. The viewpoint extends to finding zeros of a strongly monotone A plus a monotone B, thus relating to variational inequalities.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned relative to SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization, primal–dual methods (Chambolle–Pock, Arrow–Hurwicz), stochastic forward–backward with O(1/t) rates, and separable-coordinate primal–dual methods (e.g., SPDC) that require separability. The monotone operator framing connects to classic Rockafellar and modern splitting literature. To my knowledge (for the 2016 timeframe), a general variance-reduced analysis for non-separable saddle points is not present in prior NIPS work; existing VR primal–dual methods mostly target separable finite sums. The catalyst acceleration follows Lin–Mairal–Harchaoui but the authors argue a simpler outer-loop analysis suffices in the saddle-point setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting variance reduction directly on the operator B, without requiring Bi to be (co-)coercive gradients, is clean and broadens applicability beyond separable finite sums.
- Theory: linear convergence guarantees for both SVRG- and SAGA-style algorithms under strong convex–concavity, with detailed constants and sampling-dependent L̄(π). The extension to general monotone inclusions is noteworthy.
- Practical sampling: careful treatment of non-uniform sampling for bilinear problems, with factored vs elementwise splits and explicit norm bounds, is useful. The storage reduction for factored SAGA (O(n + d) vs O(nd)) is pragmatic.
- Acceleration: the proximal-point (“catalyst”) outer loop is a reasonable way to improve constants; the complexity discussion tying τ to the relative costs T(B) and T(Bi) is informative.
- Experiments: non-separable losses/regularizers are a relevant and under-served regime. Results suggest clear gains of (accelerated) VR methods over batch FB and over uniform-sampling SAGA.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited. Only two datasets are shown; one is separable (to compare against primal methods), one non-separable. There is no comparison to stronger and widely used primal–dual baselines in the saddle-point literature, e.g., Chambolle–Pock variants with linesearch/adaptive step sizes, extra-gradient/mirror-prox type methods, or optimistic gradient methods. Given the strong claims (“always superior to accelerated batch algorithms”), broader experiments (more datasets, varying conditioning, sparse/dense regimes) and stronger baselines are needed.
- Theory-practice gap in sampling. The linear-rate SAGA bound for non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step; the experiments explicitly omit it (using a heuristic mixture of uniform/non-uniform). The paper should either provide guarantees for the practical variant or ablations showing when the theory-mandated resampling helps/hurts.
- Parameter knowledge and tuning. Step sizes require L and L̄(π); probabilities π rely on quantities like (KK^T)jj or Kjk^2. The paper does not discuss how these are estimated in practice, the overhead to maintain them, and robustness to misspecification. In many ML settings, exact L or L̄(π) is unknown. An adaptive scheme (linesearch, backtracking, or online estimation of L) would substantially improve practicality.
- Scope of acceleration. The accelerated batch FB rate is only proven for bilinear K. The outer-loop catalyst result is given for SVRG; the claim that it “should work for SAGA” is plausible but unproven here. Moreover, the “always superior” claim hinges on τ choice and the frequency of full B evaluations; wall-clock trade-offs deserve empirical corroboration.
- Clarity/notation issues. In Theorem 2, µ appears in the rate but is not clearly defined in the main text (presumably related to λ, γ via the Ω-norm). Some constants (e.g., number of inner iterations proportional to log(4)×(L^2+3 L̄^2/m) in Algorithm 1) and references to Table 1 are hard to interpret without the appendix. A more self-contained presentation would help.
- Generality vs. assumptions. Linear rates require strong convexity–concavity of M and Lipschitz continuity of B; while standard in analysis, this excludes important convex–concave but not strongly so cases. The paper mentions future adaptivity, but currently results do not address this broader regime.

Quality
The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound, with monotone operator tools appropriately used to avoid cocoercivity. However, some definitions and algorithmic parameters need clearer exposition in the main text. Empirical support is promising but not yet comprehensive enough to substantiate broad superiority claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable for experts. I recommend:
- Define all constants (µ, L, L̄) precisely where first used; unify notation across theorems.
- Summarize the main complexity bounds in a self-contained table with explicit assumptions and costs (T(B), T(Bi), Tprox).
- Provide pseudocode with practical defaults (how to pick m, σ) and discuss how to estimate L, L̄ in practice.

Originality
Extending SVRG/SAGA to general saddle-point problems via a monotone-operator perspective, allowing non-gradient Bi and variance reduction with non-uniform sampling, is a nontrivial and useful contribution beyond separable ERM. The catalyst adaptation is incremental but sensible.

Significance
If adopted, these methods could become standard tools for non-separable composite saddle-point problems, which do arise in robust optimization and structured losses/regularizers. The impact will hinge on practical implementability (sampling/tuning) and demonstrated empirical gains over strong primal–dual baselines.

Questions for authors
1) Please clarify the definition of µ in Theorem 2 and relate it to λ, γ and Ω.
2) How do you set L and L̄(π) in practice? Do you backtrack or overestimate? Sensitivity?
3) What is the overhead of computing/maintaining non-uniform sampling distributions and of resampling in SAGA?
4) Can you compare against adaptive CP (with linesearch), mirror-prox/extra-gradient, and optimistic gradient methods on your non-separable setup?
5) Do you have experiments on non-bilinear smooth K to support generality?
6) Can you provide an ablation on the resampling step and mini-batch size m?

Pros
- Solid theoretical extension of variance reduction to saddle-point/monotone operators
- Thoughtful non-uniform sampling and factored storage design
- Clear linear rates under standard strong assumptions
- Practical acceleration via proximal-point outer loop

Cons
- Limited empirical validation and missing strong baselines
- Dependence on hard-to-know constants and nontrivial sampling overhead
- Some gaps between theory and the implemented variants
- Clarity issues (undefined constants, reliance on appendices)

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying parameter/tuning issues. The contribution aligns with established methodology, has solid analysis, and addresses a relevant gap; with improved experimental rigor, it would make a valuable addition.
