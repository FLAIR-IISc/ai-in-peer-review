This paper presents an extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of composite, strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The core idea is to apply the variance reduction principle not to a gradient, but to the monotone operator associated with the smooth part of the saddle-point objective. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis showing linear convergence for these new algorithms, explore the impact of different data-splitting and sampling strategies, and demonstrate how to further accelerate the methods using a "Catalyst"-style framework. The analysis is framed more generally in the context of finding zeros of monotone operators, highlighting that the results apply beyond saddle-point problems.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported and represent a complete piece of work.

*   **Strengths:** The convergence analysis appears sound and is a key contribution. Moving beyond the standard analysis for convex minimization, which often relies on co-coercivity, to a more general analysis based only on monotonicity is a significant theoretical step. This allows the framework to correctly handle saddle-point problems, where co-coercivity does not hold. The authors are careful to detail their assumptions (A-C) and define the relevant constants (L, L̄(π)) that govern the convergence rates. The experimental evaluation is strong: it uses realistic, non-separable objectives (AUC surrogate loss, clustering-inducing regularizer) that genuinely motivate the need for this work, as standard primal SVRG/SAGA would not apply. The results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) over batch and standard stochastic competitors. The comparison against a primal SAGA solver on a separable problem is an honest evaluation of the method's performance relative to specialized algorithms when they are applicable.

*   **Weaknesses:** The paper assumes knowledge of the strong-convexity-concavity parameters (λ, γ) for setting the step sizes. While common in theoretical analyses, this can be a practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this and suggest adaptivity as future work. The presentation of the acceleration in Section 5 is quite condensed and relies heavily on the reader's prior knowledge of the Catalyst framework. A bit more intuition on why the proximal-point-like updates lead to acceleration in this context could be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, though technically dense.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction provides excellent motivation and clearly outlines the paper's contributions. The problem setup in Section 2 is precise and lays a solid foundation. The distinction between individual and "factored" splits is explained well and is an important practical detail for this problem class. The algorithms are presented clearly in pseudo-code.

*   **Weaknesses:** The paper demands a high level of expertise from the reader, assuming familiarity with saddle-point optimization, variance reduction methods, and monotone operator theory. Section 2.4, which discusses the various norms and sampling schemes, is particularly dense. While all the information is there, a more intuitive summary of the key takeaway—that non-uniform sampling leverages the data structure to yield better theoretical bounds—could improve readability for a broader audience.

**Originality:** The work is highly original in its analysis and application domain.

*   **Strengths:** While the algorithmic templates for SVRG and SAGA are familiar, this is the first work to successfully adapt them and provide a rigorous linear convergence proof for this general class of saddle-point problems. The key originality lies in the analysis. By leveraging the framework of monotone operators, the authors develop a proof technique that does not require co-coercivity, which was a major roadblock in extending previous analyses. This is a novel and important insight. Furthermore, the systematic study of splitting schemes (e.g., "factored" splits) and the theoretical and empirical validation of non-uniform sampling are novel contributions specific to the saddle-point structure.

*   **Weaknesses:** The core algorithmic ideas themselves (variance reduction, outer-loop acceleration) are borrowed from prior work in convex minimization. The novelty is in the synthesis, application, and, most importantly, the new analysis required for this different problem domain.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:** Saddle-point formulations are becoming increasingly central to modern machine learning, with applications in robust optimization, adversarial training, AUC maximization, and generative modeling. This paper provides a new class of powerful, efficient, and theoretically-grounded solvers for these problems. The demonstrated practical performance gains over existing methods are substantial. By showing that variance reduction can be as effective here as it is for standard convex minimization, the paper advances the state of the art in large-scale optimization. The general analysis for monotone operators also broadens the potential impact to other areas like variational inequalities and game theory. Researchers and practitioners working on problems with non-separable objectives are very likely to use and build upon these ideas.

*   **Weaknesses:** The reliance on strong convexity-concavity limits the immediate application to some high-profile non-convex-concave problems like training GANs. However, this work provides a crucial foundation upon which future analysis for non-monotone or weakly-monotone settings can be built.

**Summary and Justification for Rating:**

**Pros:**
*   First work to provide linear convergence rates for SVRG/SAGA on general composite saddle-point problems.
*   Novel and powerful proof technique based on monotonicity, which is more general than prior analyses and correctly handles the saddle-point structure.
*   Demonstrates the critical importance of non-uniform sampling, both in theory and practice.
*   Includes an effective acceleration scheme, leading to a state-of-the-art algorithm.
*   Strong empirical results on realistic problems that cannot be tackled by standard primal variance-reduced methods.

**Cons:**
*   Assumes knowledge of problem parameters (e.g., strong convexity constants).
*   The algorithmic ideas are adaptations of existing ones; the main novelty is in the analysis for the new domain.
*   The analysis is restricted to the strongly-convex-concave setting.

This is an excellent paper that addresses an important and timely problem. It successfully bridges the gap between the highly successful variance reduction techniques and the increasingly relevant class of saddle-point problems. The theoretical contribution is substantial and insightful, and the empirical results clearly validate the approach. This work provides both a new set of practical tools for machine learning practitioners and a solid theoretical foundation for future research in this area. It is a clear accept.
