Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes stochastic variance-reduced algorithms for composite convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is proximable and strongly convex-concave and K is smooth. The core ideas are:
- Extend SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point/forward–backward setting, with linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity.
- Allow decompositions at the level of partial derivatives/operators Bi, not necessarily gradients of individual convex-concave functions, enabling factored row/column splits for bilinear K and broader “sketches.”
- Analyze non-uniform sampling and mini-batching; show it is key in theory and practice.
- Accelerate via a proximal-point/Catalyst outer loop, yielding improved complexities and a conceptually simpler analysis than classical Nesterov acceleration in this setting.
- Generalize the analysis beyond saddle-point problems to finding zeros of strongly monotone operators A plus a sum of Lipschitz operators Bi with monotone sum, without cocoercivity assumptions on Bi.

This positions the work beyond batch FB/Chambolle–Pock (and their accelerations), beyond stochastic FB with O(1/t) rates, and beyond separable primal–dual methods like SPDC and stochastic PD coordinate descent that assume separability/coordinate structure. It also differs from SMART and related monotone-operator VR methods that rely on cocoercivity; here the step-sizes and proof techniques do not assume cocoercivity and handle non-function Bi.

Quality:
- Theory: The linear convergence proofs for saddle-point SVRG/SAGA under strong convexity–concavity are sound and carefully tied to a monotone operator viewpoint. The allowance for operator-level splits (not necessarily gradients) is a meaningful theoretical generalization. The non-uniform sampling analysis and the complexity bounds for bilinear problems (including factored vs individual splits) are detailed and informative. The Catalyst/PP outer loop is well-motivated and yields clean trade-offs with τ.
- Experiments: The empirical section shows consistent improvements of SAGA/SVRG and especially the accelerated SVRG over batch and stochastic FB variants, with non-uniform sampling clearly beneficial. The non-separable loss/regularizer examples are appropriate to showcase the claimed advantages. That said, the experimental scope is modest (two datasets) and baselines could be broader (e.g., SPDC, stochastic Chambolle–Pock/extragradient with or without VR).

Clarity:
Generally well-written and organized; the operator notation and norms are clearly defined. The factored vs individual splits and their storage/complexity implications are explained. A few places could be clearer:
- Assumption (C) and the bullet “does need splits into convex-concave terms (as opposed to convex minimization)” seems inconsistent with the earlier emphasis that Bi need not derive from convex-concave Ki; I suspect this is a typo and should read “does not need.”
- Practical guidance on estimating/using L and L̄ (or adapting to them) would aid reproducibility. Many constants are deferred to appendices; a compact recipe in the main text would help practitioners.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first linear-rate variance-reduction treatment for general (nonseparable) composite saddle-point problems that:
- permits operator-level splits that are not gradients,
- handles non-uniform sampling in this setting,
- leverages a proximal-point acceleration with a simple analysis,
- and extends to monotone inclusions without cocoercivity.
Compared to prior PD finite-sum methods (e.g., SPDC), the scope is broader (non-separable M/K decompositions) and the analysis is distinct. The monotone-operator extension is especially bold and valuable.

Significance:
High. Saddle-point and monotone operator formulations are ubiquitous in ML (robust optimization, structured losses, non-separable regularizers, variational inequalities). Bringing VR to this setting with solid complexity guarantees, sampling strategies, and an acceleration path is likely to influence both theory and practice. The factored-split idea with O(n+d) storage for SAGA on bilinear problems is practically relevant.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean and novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to composite saddle points via forward–backward.
- Monotone operator framing that relaxes cocoercivity and gradient-structure assumptions.
- Sound linear-rate theory with non-uniform sampling and mini-batching; clear complexity comparisons.
- Practical acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst with simple tuning.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory; non-uniform sampling matters; acceleration helps ill-conditioned cases.

Cons / questions:
- Strong convexity–concavity and knowledge of λ, γ, L, L̄ are assumed; can the methods be made adaptive (as in SAGA variants) or robust to misspecification?
- Computing non-uniform probabilities: for bilinear K, the row/column norms are cheap; how about more general K/Bi? Any guidance when only unbiased oracle access is available?
- Resampling requirement in SAGA for non-uniform sampling may complicate implementations. How sensitive is performance to skipping it (as done in experiments)?
- Storage for SAGA with individual splits is O(nd) and often prohibitive; emphasize guidance on when to prefer SVRG vs SAGA and factored vs individual splits.
- Experimental baselines could include SPDC, stochastic Chambolle–Pock, and extragradient-based VR (if available), and more datasets.

Minor:
- Clarify the “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” claim for acceleration—this holds under the complexity model presented; a caveat about prox costs and constants would improve precision.
- A small practitioner section summarizing recommended settings (sampling, m, τ) would increase impact.

Overall recommendation: Accept (strong). The paper introduces a substantial and timely advance—variance reduction for non-separable saddle-point/monotone operator problems—with clean theory, insightful algorithmic design (operator-level splits, non-uniform sampling), and practical acceleration. Minor limitations in experiments and clarity do not detract from the novelty and potential impact.

Confidence: High.
