Summary
The paper proposes stochastic variance reduction methods for composite convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex-concave and M is possibly nonsmooth but proximal-friendly and strongly convex-concave. The central ideas are:
- Extend SVRG and SAGA from finite-sum convex minimization to saddle-point problems and, more generally, to finding zeros of strongly monotone operators A+B where A has an accessible resolvent and B=∑_i B_i is Lipschitz and monotone (the Bi need not be monotone nor gradients of functions).
- Provide linear convergence analyses that rely only on monotonicity/Lipschitz properties (no co-coercivity), with explicit step sizes in weighted norms that account for possibly very different strong convexity-concavity constants in x and y.
- Show that for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, there are two natural decompositions (individual entries or factored row/column splits) and that non-uniform sampling (by leverage-like probabilities proportional to diagonal entries of KK^T or K^T K, or elementwise K^2) leads to improved complexity via a tuned smoothness constant L̄(π).
- Accelerate SVRG via a proximal-point/catalyst scheme that adds quadratic regularization, yielding an outer linear rate (1−1/(1+τ)) with an optimized τ that balances the cost of the “backward” oracle and forward evaluations; the resulting complexity improves over batch accelerated FB and unaccelerated VR.
- Provide experiments on supervised learning with non-separable loss/regularizer and on a separable benchmark, demonstrating that SAGA/SVRG with non-uniform sampling, and especially accelerated SVRG, outperform batch FB/accelerated FB; uniform sampling can be inferior.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization, primal-dual/forward–backward splitting and accelerated variants (e.g., Chambolle-Pock), stochastic FB with noise, and proximal-point/catalyst acceleration. It contrasts with primal-dual coordinate methods like SPDC and APCG that require separability, and with stochastic operator splitting frameworks that rely on co-coercivity of forward operators (e.g., SMART), which does not hold for generic saddle-point problems. The paper’s monotone operator view (via Rockafellar) generalizes the scope beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.

Quality
- Theory: The analyses for SVRG and SAGA establish global linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity of M and Lipschitz monotonicity of B, using carefully chosen step sizes σ in a weighted norm. The use of L̄(π) to tie sampling to structure is well-motivated and yields clear complexity expressions; the bilinear case bounds (using ‖K‖_F, ‖K‖_op, ‖K‖_max) are insightful and show the advantage of non-uniform sampling and factored splits. The catalyst-style acceleration is adapted cleanly: solving a proximal-point subproblem inexactly with SVRG delivers an accelerated overall rate, without requiring Nesterov’s acceleration machinery because the proximal-point operator is a contraction when M is strongly convex-concave.
- Assumptions and constants: The paper is explicit about the need for strong convexity-concavity (λ,γ) and Lipschitz bounds (L, L̄). Proofs are deferred to the appendix; from the presentation, the claims are consistent with monotone operator theory. One potential point of confusion: the forward–backward linear rate and stepsize are stated in terms of L^2; clarifying the exact definition of L and why L^2 appears (due to the weighted norm/forward–backward averaging constant) would help avoid misinterpretation by readers used to 1/L stepsizes.
- Empirics: The experiments are relevant (non-separable loss/regularizer; AUC-like loss; group/fused penalties) and support the claims: non-uniform sampling helps; accelerated SVRG helps more for ill-conditioned regimes; primal methods on separable losses remain strong when applicable. That said, the empirical section is relatively small (two datasets), focuses on “distance to optimum,” and lacks wall-clock plots and variance bands. Practical choices (e.g., resampling requirement for SAGA with non-uniform sampling, minibatch sizes m, τ selection) are mentioned but could be fleshed out more.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with clear notation and explicit algorithms. The weighted norm Ω and the role of λ,γ are explained and important in practice. The split designs (factored vs elementwise) and their consequences for storage and L̄(π) are nicely discussed. Some suggestions:
- Define all constants where used (e.g., μ in Theorem 2 appears; make the relation to λ,γ explicit).
- Make the dependence on L vs L̄(π) in step sizes and rates more transparent in the main text.
- Summarize the main complexity table in the main paper (it is central to the message).
- Briefly state the key lemmas behind linear convergence in the monotone operator setting to help readers reconstruct the proof sketch.

Originality
The extension of SAGA/SVRG with linear convergence to general saddle-point problems and monotone operators without co-coercivity is, to my knowledge, novel at the time and distinct from SPDC/APCD-style methods that exploit separability, and from operator-splitting VR methods that assume co-coercivity. The sampling analysis and factored decomposition for bilinear forms are also fresh and practically relevant. The catalyst acceleration adapted to this setting (with a simpler analysis owing to proximal-point contraction) is a nice contribution.

Significance
Saddle-point formulations are pervasive (primal-dual optimization, robust optimization, structured losses/regularizers), and many important cases are non-separable, where prior VR methods for finite sums do not apply. Bringing linear-time variance reduction to this class with practical sampling and acceleration is likely to influence both theory and practice. The monotone operator generalization broadens applicability further (e.g., variational inequalities). I expect these ideas to be adopted and extended.

Pros
- Solid, general theory with linear rates under natural assumptions; does not rely on co-coercivity.
- Careful treatment of sampling and structure (factored splits) leading to improved bounds.
- Practical acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst; always competitive with batch accelerated FB.
- Clear algorithms; connections to monotone operator theory.
- Empirical evidence on relevant non-separable problems.

Cons
- Limited experimental breadth; no wall-clock time and limited comparisons (e.g., to SPDC in separable settings).
- Reliance on known λ,γ,L; no adaptive tuning in the main algorithms.
- Some constants/notations (μ, L vs L̄, stepsize with L^2) could be clarified.
- Non-uniform sampling requires computing/maintaining probabilities; overhead and robustness are not quantified.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires resampling in theory; experiments use a heuristic mixture—clarify practical impact.

Questions for authors
1) Please clarify the definition of μ in Theorem 2 and its relation to λ,γ and Ω. Why does the FB stepsize/rate involve L^2 rather than L?
2) How are τ and minibatch size m chosen in practice? Is there an adaptive scheme that tracks λ,γ,L̄ online?
3) What is the overhead to compute non-uniform sampling distributions (e.g., row/column norms) and maintain them if K changes? Can you report wall-clock results including this overhead?
4) For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, what is the empirical effect of omitting the resampling step? Can you quantify the gap to the theoretically justified version?
5) Could you include comparisons to SPDC/APCG on separable instances to position performance when separability holds?

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper makes a clear and significant theoretical and algorithmic contribution, with a compelling monotone-operator perspective and practically relevant sampling/acceleration. Expanding and clarifying the empirical and practical aspects would further strengthen the work, but the current submission already merits acceptance.

Confidence
High.
