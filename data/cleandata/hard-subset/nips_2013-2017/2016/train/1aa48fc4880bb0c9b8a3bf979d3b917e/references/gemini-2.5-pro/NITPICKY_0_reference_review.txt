**Summary:**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, from the standard convex minimization setting to composite saddle-point problems. The key contributions are: (1) a novel convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, which is grounded in the more general theory of monotone operators and does not require co-coercivity; (2) an exploration of problem structure, such as different ways to decompose the operator and the importance of non-uniform sampling; and (3) the application of the Catalyst framework to accelerate the proposed SVRG variant. The authors provide theoretical convergence rates that demonstrate improvements over batch and standard stochastic methods, and validate their findings with experiments on binary classification tasks featuring non-separable objectives.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

The paper addresses a relevant and important problem. As many machine learning problems can be formulated as saddle-point problems (e.g., via duality, robust optimization), extending high-performance variance-reduced stochastic methods to this domain is a valuable contribution. The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical analysis. By framing the problem in the context of finding zeros of monotone operators, the authors provide a general analysis that correctly identifies why existing proof techniques for convex minimization (which rely on co-coercivity) are insufficient. This is a significant technical insight. The resulting linear convergence guarantees for SVRG and SAGA in this new domain are a solid theoretical result. The proposed accelerated variant, based on Catalyst, is shown to be highly effective in experiments, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

However, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws in clarity, presentation, and methodological justification, which collectively detract from its overall quality.

**Quality:**
*   The claim in the introduction that the saddle-point formulation "does need splits into convex-concave terms (as opposed to convex minimization)" is a critical assertion but is never justified or explained. Why does this property, not required for SVRG in convex minimization, become necessary here? This omission leaves a significant gap in the reader's understanding of the problem's technical demands.
*   The experimental validation is based on only two datasets. While the chosen problems are appropriate for showcasing the method's strengths (non-separability), a more comprehensive empirical study would be needed to make a strong case for the practical superiority of the proposed algorithms.
*   The analysis of SAGA (Theorem 2) results in a step-size `σ` and a convergence rate that depend on `|I|^2`. This quadratic dependence on the number of components is highly unusual and potentially problematic for large `|I|`. Standard SAGA analyses result in a linear dependence. This warrants further explanation and justification.

**Clarity:**
*   The paper's presentation lacks the polish expected for a top-tier conference. There is a glaring formatting artifact on page 1 ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems..."). The reference list is riddled with formatting errors, such as inconsistent styles and extraneous commas (e.g., "Adv. NIPS,, ,"). This suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
*   The definition of the proximal operator in Eq. (2) is non-standard. While it may be technically correct given the defined norm `Ω`, it differs from the more common definition `arg min {f(u) + 1/(2σ) ||u-x||^2}`. This deviation should be explicitly noted and justified to avoid confusion.
*   The term `L` is introduced as a Lipschitz constant but is subsequently referred to as the "condition number of the problem." This is imprecise; `L` is a scaled Lipschitz constant, which is related to but not synonymous with the traditional condition number (ratio of smoothness to strong convexity). Such terminological inaccuracies should be avoided.
*   In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), several update steps are marked as "(optional)". This is highly ambiguous. Are these steps necessary for the theoretical guarantees to hold, particularly for non-uniform sampling as hinted in the text? If so, they are not optional. The algorithm's description must be precise about which steps are required under which conditions.

**Originality and Significance:**
*   The core theoretical contribution—the analysis for non-co-coercive monotone operators—is novel and significant. It correctly generalizes variance reduction techniques beyond the confines of convex optimization.
*   However, other claimed contributions feel incremental. The application of the Catalyst framework is a known acceleration meta-scheme, and its application here, while effective, follows a relatively standard template. The authors' note that the analysis is "simpler" is a nice observation, but does not represent a fundamental breakthrough. Similarly, the finding that non-uniform sampling is beneficial is a well-established principle in the stochastic optimization literature, and its confirmation in this new setting is expected.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA on a broad class of composite saddle-point problems.
2.  The theoretical approach, based on general monotone operator theory without co-coercivity, is elegant, powerful, and a genuine technical contribution.
3.  The proposed accelerated SVRG method shows strong empirical performance, outperforming relevant baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The paper is marred by numerous presentation issues, including formatting artifacts and a poorly formatted bibliography, suggesting a rushed submission.
2.  Several key technical points are unclear or unjustified, such as the need for convex-concave splits, the non-standard proximal operator definition, and the "optional" steps in Algorithm 2.
3.  Some claims of contribution (e.g., regarding acceleration and non-uniform sampling) appear to be incremental extensions of existing, well-known ideas.
4.  The experimental section is limited, and the unusual `|I|^2` dependency in the SAGA analysis is a point of concern that is not addressed.

**Recommendation:**
While the core theoretical idea of this paper is strong and addresses an important problem, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The significant number of minor issues in clarity, precision, and presentation collectively undermine the work's credibility and impact. A paper at NIPS should not only present novel ideas but also communicate them with rigor and clarity. In its current state, this manuscript does not meet that standard. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to thoroughly revise the paper to address the issues raised, as a more polished and carefully justified version would likely be a strong candidate for acceptance.
