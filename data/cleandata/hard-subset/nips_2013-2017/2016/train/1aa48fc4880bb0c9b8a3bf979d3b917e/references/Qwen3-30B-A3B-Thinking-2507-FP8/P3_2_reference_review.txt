

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is convex-concave and M is non-smooth but simple (with easy proximal operators). The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, demonstrating linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity assumptions. They extend their analysis to monotone operators and show how to accelerate the methods using the "catalyst" framework. The paper is well-motivated by the prevalence of saddle-point problems in machine learning through duality, and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation on non-separable learning problems.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by correctly identifying that saddle-point problems should be analyzed as finding zeros of monotone operators, which differs fundamentally from convex minimization. The convergence analysis is rigorous and properly accounts for the key differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization problems.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The paper provides compelling examples of machine learning problems where non-separable losses or regularizers make existing methods inapplicable, but the proposed approach works. The examples of AUC maximization (non-separable loss) and grouped norms (non-separable regularizer) are particularly convincing.

3. **Algorithmic Insight**: The paper correctly identifies the importance of different types of splits (factored vs. individual) and non-uniform sampling, which is crucial for practical implementation. The analysis of complexity bounds for different sampling strategies is thorough.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where accelerated SVRG outperforms existing methods. The comparison with batch methods and primal methods on separable problems is well-executed.

## Weaknesses
1. **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption**: The analysis assumes strong convexity-concavity throughout, which limits the applicability of the methods. While the authors note they plan to explore adaptivity to these constants in future work, this limitation should be more prominently acknowledged in the current paper.

2. **Comparison with Alternative Methods**: The paper compares with batch methods but doesn't sufficiently compare with other stochastic methods specifically designed for saddle-point problems (e.g., stochastic extra-gradient methods). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's claims about superiority.

3. **Monotone Operator Extension**: The extension to monotone operators is mentioned but not deeply explored. While the paper states this is a broader class of problems, it doesn't provide a concrete example of how this extension leads to new practical applications beyond saddle-point problems.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that factored splits require O(n+d) storage, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how this is achieved in practice. A brief explanation of the implementation strategy would be helpful.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong theoretical contribution to optimization for machine learning. The paper makes a clear and novel contribution by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, with a rigorous analysis and practical validation. The results advance the state of the art in optimization for machine learning, particularly for problems with non-separable losses or regularizers.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experiments support the claims. The limitations identified (primarily the strong convexity-concavity assumption) are common in this area of research and the authors have acknowledged them as future work.

## Recommendation
**Accept**. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a problem of broad interest in machine learning. The results are well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the paper is clearly written. The limitations are acknowledged and are common in this area of research.

## Specific Comments
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the strong convexity-concavity assumption and its implications for practical applications.
- Consider adding a comparison with stochastic extra-gradient methods to strengthen the experimental section.
- The monotone operator extension is mentioned but not deeply explored; provide a brief example of a problem beyond saddle-point that benefits from this extension.
- Clarify the implementation details for factored splits, particularly how the O(n+d) storage requirement is achieved in practice.

This paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a problem of broad interest in machine learning. The results advance the state of the art in optimization for machine learning, and the paper is of high quality in terms of both theoretical and experimental contributions.
