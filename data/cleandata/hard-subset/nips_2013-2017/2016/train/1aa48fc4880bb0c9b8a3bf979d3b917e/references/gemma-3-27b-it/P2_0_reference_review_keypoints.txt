- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and provides the first formal convergence analysis for this setting.
- Demonstrates the necessity of splitting by functions (not just convex terms) and shows benefits of non-uniform sampling for efficiency.
- Introduces a catalyst-style acceleration adapted to saddle-point SVR methods.
- Frames the algorithms within monotone operator theory, broadening applicability.
- Provides rigorous convergence proofs and clear theoretical guarantees.
- Identifies differences and unique challenges compared to convex minimization.
- Offers practical relevance to machine-learning problems (regularized learning, robust optimization).
- Empirical results support theoretical claims and show practical gains.
- Gives detailed, practitioner-focused analysis of splitting strategies (function- vs derivative-based, factored splits).
- Highlights usefulness of non-uniform sampling strategies in practice.
- Well-written and well-organized with clear notation and explicit assumptions.
- Main weakness: relies on relatively strong assumptions (strong convexity-concavity, Lipschitz continuity).
- Experimental scope is limited; broader datasets and more diverse comparisons (including primal methods) would strengthen claims.
- Theoretical constants depend on problem parameters that are hard to estimate in practice; impact not fully explored.
- Provides limited guidance on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity.
- Quality: technically sound with rigorous proofs and supported claims.
- Originality and significance: novel, important extension likely to impact ML optimization.
- Recommendation: strong accept — significant contribution despite minor weaknesses.
