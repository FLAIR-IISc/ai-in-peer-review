This paper tackles the problem of solving saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction techniques, extending the successes of SVRG and SAGA to this broader class of problems. The motivation is strong – saddle-point formulations naturally arise in many machine learning applications (regularized learning, robust optimization, etc.) where existing methods may struggle. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these extended algorithms, highlighting connections to monotone operator theory and demonstrating applicability beyond strict saddle-point problems. The paper also explores different splitting strategies for the saddle-point formulation and demonstrates the benefit of non-uniform sampling. Finally, they present experimental results showcasing the performance of their algorithms, particularly in comparison to batch and existing stochastic methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:** Saddle-point problems are indeed ubiquitous in machine learning, and efficient solvers are highly desirable. Addressing this problem space is valuable.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting is a significant contribution.  The connection to monotone operator theory is insightful and expands the scope of the results.
* **Algorithmic Extensions:** The straightforward extensions of SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point case are well-presented.
* **Non-Uniform Sampling:** The exploration of non-uniform sampling strategies and demonstration of their benefits (both theoretically and empirically) is important.  This is a key insight.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while relatively limited in scope, provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical improvements over existing methods.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.
* **Catalyst Acceleration:** The extension of the catalyst framework provides a further performance boost, a valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumptions (A)-(C) regarding the functions K and M, while standard, might be limiting in some applications. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial. Specifically, how robust are the results to *approximate* strong convexity-concavity?
* **Practicality of Non-Uniform Sampling:** While theoretically sound, implementing non-uniform sampling can be challenging in practice, especially for very large-scale problems. The paper briefly mentions this, but a more in-depth discussion of practical considerations and potential approximations would be helpful. The need for knowledge of the Frobenius norm of K is also a potential bottleneck.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to two datasets.  Expanding the empirical evaluation to a wider range of problems and datasets would strengthen the claims.  The comparison to primal methods is good but could be more extensive.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The effect of different hyperparameters (step sizes, mini-batch size, regularization strength) on the performance of the algorithms is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Connection to Duality:**  The paper discusses saddle-point problems as arising from duality, but doesn’t fully leverage this connection. Exploring how the choice of splitting strategies impacts the primal-dual gap could be insightful.
* **Limited Discussion of Implementation Details:** More details about the implementation (e.g., software libraries used, specific choices for proximal operator computations) would enhance reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning.
* The theoretical contributions are significant and well-supported.
* The algorithmic extensions are straightforward and effective.
* The experimental results provide evidence of practical improvements.
* The connection to monotone operator theory broadens the applicability of the results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The assumptions might be limiting in some applications.
* The experimental evaluation is relatively limited in scope.
* Practical considerations of non-uniform sampling are not fully addressed.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and implementation details.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions to the field of stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems. The authors have successfully extended existing variance reduction techniques to a more general setting and provided a rigorous analysis of their convergence properties. While there are some weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The authors should address the following points:

1. **Robustness to Assumptions:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to violations of assumptions (A)-(C).
2. **Practical Implementation:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges and potential approximations for implementing non-uniform sampling.
3. **Expanded Experiments:**  Consider adding experiments with more diverse datasets and a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity.
4. **Implementation Details:** Include more details about the implementation to enhance reproducibility.
5. **Duality Discussion:** Explore the connection between splitting strategies and the primal-dual gap.
