Summary
The paper studies composite saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) where M is strongly convex-concave with an easy proximal operator and K is smooth. The key idea is to bring variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting by viewing the problem as finding zeros of a strongly monotone plus Lipschitz operator and randomizing over decompositions of the smooth operator B=(∂_x K, -∂_y K). The paper (i) derives linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point/monotone operator setting, (ii) emphasizes that one can split B either into function components or into partial-derivative “factored” components (no potential needed), enabling efficient storage and non-uniform sampling, and (iii) introduces a catalyst-style acceleration via regularized proximal-point subproblems, yielding improved complexities. The analysis extends beyond saddle-point problems to finding zeros of monotone operators A+B (A strongly monotone resolvent-tractable, B monotone and split into Lipschitz pieces). Experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers show clear gains over batch/primal-dual baselines and the importance of non-uniform sampling.

Relation to prior work
- Batch primal-dual/forward-backward: builds on proximal splitting (Rockafellar; Chambolle-Pock) and shows linear rates for strongly convex-concave settings, including an extrapolated variant.
- Stochastic primal-dual methods: contrasts with stochastic FB with O(1/t) rates; differs from SPDC and related methods that need separability and/or bilinear structure.
- Variance reduction: extends SVRG/SAGA from smooth convex minimization (Johnson & Zhang; Defazio et al.) to monotone inclusions without co-coercivity, a regime not covered by SMART and related cocoercivity-based analyses.
- Acceleration: applies the catalyst idea (Lin et al.) to the monotone/saddle setting via proximal-point, simplifying analysis relative to Nesterov acceleration.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Framing SVRG/SAGA for monotone inclusions (with only monotonicity of the sum B, not cocoercivity or per-component monotonicity) is elegant and widens applicability substantially (e.g., variational inequalities).
- Flexible decompositions: Allowing splits at the operator level (factored row/column updates) is well-motivated for bilinear K and leads to meaningful memory/runtime trade-offs. The paper makes concrete how to choose non-uniform sampling distributions and quantifies their benefits via L̄(π).
- Theory: Clear linear convergence results for SVRG (epoch-wise contraction) and SAGA (online), including mini-batching, non-uniform sampling, and storage-efficient factored variants. The constants are connected to problem-dependent norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) with useful comparisons.
- Acceleration: The catalyst-style scheme is natural for strongly monotone problems (prox-point), and the resulting complexity L̄√(T(B)·max_i T(B_i)) is insightful. The discussion about when acceleration strictly improves over batch (condition on L̄√(max T(B_i)/T(B))) is helpful.
- Empirics: Non-separable loss and regularizer with efficient prox demonstrate the intended scope; comparisons show practical gains of VR and of non-uniform sampling.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption strength and constants: The analysis relies on strong convex-concavity via M (hence strong monotonicity of A). While standard, it limits scope; more discussion on stability/ergodic rates without strong convex-concavity (beyond O(1/t)) would help. The step-size and complexity constants (e.g., σ=1/(L^2+3 L̄^2/m)) are conservative due to working with Lipschitz monotone operators rather than cocoercive ones; empirical guidance for tuning would be valuable.
- Notation and clarity: In Theorem 2, the rate involves µ (µ^2) but µ is not clearly defined earlier in the saddle-point notation (λ, γ are). If µ denotes the strong monotonicity parameter in Ω, please define it and relate it to λ, γ explicitly. Also, the claim in the contributions “does need splits into convex-concave terms” seems to contradict the later point that Bi need not be gradients; likely “does not need” is intended.
- Comparisons: Experiments omit some relevant recent baselines applicable in the strongly convex-concave bilinear case, e.g., Mirror-Prox/extra-gradient variants and SPDC (when applicable). Even if separability is required for SPDC, showing that the proposed methods cover the non-separable case (where SPDC cannot run) and matching/approaching SPDC on separable instances would strengthen the case. Similarly, a stochastic extragradient baseline (deterministic and stochastic) would be informative.
- Catalyst practicality: The acceleration section largely “forgets” the cost of prox M and focuses on T(B), T(B_i). In problems where prox M dominates, the practical benefit may be limited. Also, setting τ requires L̄ and cost-ratio knowledge; some adaptive strategy or sensitivity analysis would be useful.
- Limited empirical breadth: Only two datasets, one with specialized non-separable proxes. Additional tasks (e.g., robust optimization or a variational inequality toy) and reporting wall-clock time, memory (especially for SAGA factored vs individual), and sensitivity to the sampling distribution would improve the empirical story.

Quality
The technical development is sound and carefully argues linear rates under appropriate assumptions, with clear ties to operator theory. Some constants are conservative but justified by the generality (no cocoercivity). Minor notational inconsistencies should be fixed. The proofs are referenced to appendices; the core ideas are explained adequately in the main text.

Clarity
Overall well-written but dense. The discussion of norms and constants for bilinear K is helpful. Clarify: (i) µ vs λ,γ; (ii) the precise epoch length choice for SVRG and how it scales; (iii) resampling requirement for non-uniform SAGA and the practical workaround (mixture sampling) used in experiments.

Originality
The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems and, more generally, to monotone inclusions without cocoercivity, appears novel and fills a gap relative to SPDC/PDHG and SMART. The operator-level splitting and tailored non-uniform sampling for bilinear problems are also thoughtful contributions.

Significance
High. Variance reduction for saddle-point/monotone problems is increasingly important (GANs, robust optimization, game-theoretic learning). The methods are likely to be adopted, and the analysis could spur further work (e.g., adaptive step-sizes, beyond strong convex-concavity, composite structures).

Questions and suggestions to authors
- Please clarify the definition of µ in Theorem 2 and relate it to λ, γ and Ω.
- Can you provide a brief comparison to stochastic extragradient (deterministic/stochastic Mirror-Prox) in theory and in at least one experiment?
- For catalyst, can you include a small ablation on τ selection (including an adaptive heuristic) and report the impact when Tprox(M) dominates?
- For non-uniform SAGA without resampling, can you discuss convergence guarantees (even if weaker) or empirical stability?
- Minor: fix the “does need splits” phrasing; add a short table summarizing memory costs for SAGA individual vs factored.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes substantial theoretical and practical contributions by bringing variance reduction to a broad class of saddle-point/monotone problems, with strong analysis and promising experiments. Addressing the clarity issues and adding a comparison to extragradient methods would further strengthen it.

Confidence: High.
