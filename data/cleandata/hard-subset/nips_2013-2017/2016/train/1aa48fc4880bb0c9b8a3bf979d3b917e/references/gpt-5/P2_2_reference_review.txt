Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex-concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The core contribution is to extend variance-reduced methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting, prove linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity, and show how to accelerate them via a proximal-point (Catalyst-like) wrapper. A unifying monotone operator view underlies the analysis, allowing extension beyond saddle points to strongly monotone inclusions/variational inequalities. The paper further discusses two kinds of finite-sum decompositions (by functions and by partial derivatives/factored), non-uniform sampling, and gives practical complexity bounds in terms of Lipschitz constants (L) and a sampling-dependent smoothness quantity L̄(π).

This builds on and contrasts with: variance reduction for finite-sum minimization (SAG/SAGA/SVRG), primal-dual and forward-backward methods for saddle problems (Arrow-Hurwicz, Chambolle-Pock, accelerated variants), stochastic forward-backward with noise, and stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods for separable problems (SPDC/related). It is distinct in (i) handling non-separable saddle problems; (ii) variance reduction in a general convex-concave (and more generally monotone operator) setting without relying on co-coercivity; and (iii) analyzing non-uniform sampling and catalyst-style acceleration in this context.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper states clear assumptions (strong (λ,γ)-convex-concavity for M, Lipschitz gradients of K, finite-sum decomposability of B). It proves linear convergence for both SVRG and SAGA with explicit step sizes involving L and L̄(π), and gives iteration complexities up to log factors. The monotone operator analysis is a robust choice: avoiding co-coercivity widens applicability (saddle points and VIs) while still yielding linear rates via strong monotonicity. The acceleration via proximal regularization is well-motivated and yields the familiar L̄√(T(B) max T(Bi)) complexity trade-off. The non-uniform sampling bounds (in terms of ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max) are sensible and show concrete advantages for factored vs. element-wise splits.
- Correctness concerns: The paper notes that forward-backward without variance reduction may not converge in general, but linearly converges under strong convexity-concavity. This is consistent with monotone operator theory when A is strongly monotone and B is Lipschitz. However, step-size expressions like σ=1/L^2 in the weighted norm are nonstandard; a brief derivation or intuition would help avoid confusion (e.g., how scaling by Ω induces L^2 vs. L). For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, the need to “resample” stored gradients is acknowledged; it would be helpful to precisely state which option is required for the stated theorem and which is heuristic in experiments.
- Experiments: The experiments validate key claims: (i) uniform sampling underperforms non-uniform; (ii) variance-reduced methods substantially outperform batch and stochastic FB; (iii) acceleration helps ill-conditioned problems; and (iv) when separable, primal SAGA can be faster, but is inapplicable in non-separable cases. That said, the empirical section is relatively limited (two datasets, a few methods) and lacks comparisons to stochastic extragradient/mirror-prox baselines, which are widely used for saddle/VIs. Also, more detail on the proximal operators used (for the AUC surrogate and non-separable regularizer) and wall-clock vs. iteration trade-offs would strengthen the practical story.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The use of the weighted norm Ω and its dual to normalize λ and γ is clear and helpful. Algorithms are specified, constants are defined, and the factored vs. individual decompositions are carefully explained. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Unify and motivate step-size expressions (σ in terms of L vs. L^2), and explain how the weighting leads to those forms.
- Summarize the main theorems’ conditions and rates in the main text (not only in appendices) and include the promised complexity table.
- In Section 4, very explicitly separate the provable variant of SAGA (with resampling under non-uniform sampling) from the practical variant used in experiments.

Originality:
The extension of SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems and, more broadly, to (strongly) monotone inclusions without relying on co-coercivity is, to my knowledge at the time, novel and timely. The analysis with non-uniform sampling and the “factored” decomposition tailored to saddle structure is also original and insightful. The catalyst-style acceleration adapted to this setting is a useful and simple-to-implement addition.

Significance:
This work addresses an important gap: many practical problems admit efficient proximal operators yet are non-separable, making standard finite-sum acceleration inapplicable in primal or dual forms. Bringing linear-time variance reduction to saddle problems (and VIs) is of broad interest and likely to be adopted. The non-uniform sampling and factored storage insights are practically valuable. The acceleration result further strengthens the impact. I expect researchers and practitioners working on large-scale structured prediction, robust optimization, and convex-concave games to benefit.

Strengths:
- Solid and general theory with linear rates under standard strong convexity-concavity.
- Extension to general monotone operators broadens scope.
- Careful treatment of sampling (non-uniform) and decomposition (factored vs. individual).
- Practical acceleration with a clear complexity trade-off.
- Empirical results consistent with theory and demonstrating benefits on non-separable setups.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Limited empirical comparison set; missing stochastic extragradient/mirror-prox baselines common for saddle/VIs.
- Practical impact of resampling requirement for SAGA with non-uniform sampling needs clearer discussion; what is the performance gap without resampling?
- Some constants (L vs. L^2) and step-size choices could be better motivated in the main text.
- Computing/estimating L̄(π) and optimal π may be nontrivial; more guidance would be useful.
- Acceleration parameter τ selection relies on norms like ‖K‖F; do the methods remain competitive with rough estimates?

Suggestions:
- Add a comparison to stochastic mirror-prox/extragradient methods and SPDC (where applicable).
- Clarify the step-size dependencies and provide practical defaults when L and L̄ are unknown.
- Report wall-clock times and memory usage, especially to highlight the factored SAGA storage advantage.
- Expand on robustness to mis-specified λ, γ and π.

Overall assessment:
High-quality theoretical contribution with clear novelty and likely impact. Despite relatively modest empirical breadth, the work is rigorous, well-motivated, and addresses a meaningful need. I recommend acceptance.
