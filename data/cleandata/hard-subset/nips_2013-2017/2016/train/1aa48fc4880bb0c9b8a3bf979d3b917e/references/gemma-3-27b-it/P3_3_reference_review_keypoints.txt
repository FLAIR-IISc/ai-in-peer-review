- Overall assessment: Strong, well-executed contribution extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with first convergence analysis; recommend accept with minor revisions.
- Problem addressed: Extends SVRG/SAGA to min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) saddle-point problems common in ML, filling a theoretical gap.
- Theoretical contribution: First convergence proofs for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting using monotone operator theory.
- Generalization: Connection to monotone operators broadens applicability beyond standard saddle-point formulations.
- Acceleration: Successfully extends the Catalyst acceleration framework to the proposed algorithms.
- Sampling insights: Analysis of function-wise vs derivative-wise splitting and non-uniform sampling is valuable, with promising schemes for bilinear problems.
- Practical relevance: Algorithms perform well empirically, especially on ill-conditioned problems; experiments are comprehensive.
- Novelty and originality: High—novel extension and analysis of variance-reduced methods to saddle-point / monotone operator contexts.
- Quality and rigor: Proofs appear sound and detailed, supporting theoretical claims.
- Clarity issues: Dense notation and occasional lack of intuitive explanations for A, B, K, M and norms (op, F, max) hinder readability.
- Proof readability: Proofs are lengthy and could be condensed or moved to the appendix for improved flow.
- Limitations discussion: Paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations like parameter sensitivity and structural applicability.
- Adaptive step-size: Adaptivity to strong convexity/concavity constants is only briefly mentioned and deserves more emphasis as future work.
- Acceptance pros: First convergence analysis, strong theoretical foundation, practical effectiveness, sampling insights, and Catalyst extension.
- Acceptance cons: Dense presentation, long proofs, and limited discussion of limitations.
- Recommended revisions: Simplify notation, streamline/move proofs to appendix, expand limitations discussion, and highlight future work on adaptive step-sizes.
