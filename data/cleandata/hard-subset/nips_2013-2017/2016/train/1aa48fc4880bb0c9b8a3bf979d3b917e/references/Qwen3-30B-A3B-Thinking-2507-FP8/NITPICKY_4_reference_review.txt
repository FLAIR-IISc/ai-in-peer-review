

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper proposes stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, extending existing convex optimization techniques to non-separable settings. The authors establish convergence guarantees for these algorithms, analyze their computational complexity, and demonstrate their superiority over batch and stochastic methods on machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. The paper makes several contributions: first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, identification of two distinct splitting approaches, and an accelerated variant using the catalyst framework.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by extending stochastic variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems, which have significant applications in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction). The theoretical analysis is generally sound, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The paper also provides a thorough discussion of the relationship between saddle-point problems and monotone operators, extending the applicability of the proposed methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Definition Error**: The paper defines "strongly (λ, γ)-convex-concave" as "the function (x, y) 7→ M(x, y)− λ/2 ‖x‖^2 + γ/2 ‖y‖^2 is convex-concave." This is technically incorrect. For strong convexity in x, we require M(x,y) - (λ/2)||x||^2 to be convex in x. For strong concavity in y, we require M(x,y) + (γ/2)||y||^2 to be concave in y (or equivalently, -M(x,y) + (γ/2)||y||^2 convex in y). The sign on γ is wrong - it should be subtracted for concavity, not added. This error undermines the theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the condition number L. In the main text, it states L = ||K||_op/√(λγ), but in the bilinear saddle-point section, it writes L = ||K||_op/√γλ (which is mathematically equivalent but stylistically inconsistent). More importantly, in the convergence rate claims, it uses L^2 in some places and L in others, creating confusion about the actual rate.

3. **Theoretical Inconsistency in Theorem 1**: The theorem states "E [ Ω(xv − x∗, yv − y∗)^2 ] ≤ (3/4)^v Ω(x0 − x∗, y0 − y∗)^2" - this is technically incorrect. The left side is a random variable (the expectation of a squared norm), while the right side is a constant. It should be "E [ Ω(xv − x∗, yv − y∗)^2 ] ≤ (3/4)^v Ω(x0 − x∗, y0 − y∗)^2" (without the square on the right side).

4. **Confusing Splitting Definition**: The claim that "the saddle-point formulation (a) allows two different notions of splits, in terms of functions, or in terms of partial derivatives" is misleading. Standard saddle-point problems involve splitting the objective function, not the partial derivatives. The paper describes two ways of splitting the gradient operator B, which is unusual and confusing.

5. **Minor Formatting Inconsistencies**: 
   - "th Conference" should be "The Conference"
   - Inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "M(x, y) +M(x, y)" vs "M(x, y) + M(x, y)")
   - Inconsistent capitalization in references ("Adv. NIPS" vs "Adv. NIPS,")
   - Section 6 is referenced as "Section 6" before it's defined, creating confusion

6. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper claims "SAGA/SVRG with non-uniform sampling always has convergence bounds superior to SAGA/SVRG with uniform sampling" without providing the mathematical justification for this claim in the main text.

7. **Experimental Details**: The paper uses "sido" and "rcv1" datasets without providing basic information about them (e.g., what they are, why they were chosen, or their standard usage in machine learning).

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a novel and potentially valuable extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, the technical errors in the definition of strong convexity-concavity and the theoretical inconsistencies in the convergence analysis are significant enough to undermine the paper's credibility. The minor formatting inconsistencies and theoretical ambiguities further detract from the paper's quality.

The paper's main contribution - extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems - is important and timely. However, the fundamental errors in the mathematical definitions and the inconsistent theoretical presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors should carefully revise the definition of strong convexity-concavity, correct the notation inconsistencies, and clarify the theoretical claims before resubmission.

I recommend rejection due to these critical technical errors that compromise the paper's theoretical foundation, despite the paper's potential significance. The errors are not merely typographical but represent fundamental misunderstandings of the mathematical concepts being presented.
