This paper presents stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) extended to saddle-point problems, offering convergence analysis and demonstrating benefits through theoretical results and experiments. The core idea—adapting existing techniques to a non-separable setting—is well-motivated, given the prevalence of saddle-point formulations in machine learning, especially in duality and robust optimization. The paper establishes a clear connection to monotone operator theory, further broadening the applicability of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, building upon existing results for convex optimization while appropriately addressing the differences in the saddle-point setting. The extension to monotone operators adds further generality. The proof sketches in the appendix provide sufficient detail.
* **Novelty:** Adapting SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a novel contribution. The analysis demonstrating linear convergence and highlighting the importance of splitting strategies is valuable. The introduction of the “catalyst” acceleration framework for saddle-point problems is also a significant addition.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail. The connection between the saddle-point problem and the monotone operator framework is clearly explained. The examples in Section 2.1 are helpful in illustrating the applicability of the proposed methods.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a crucial challenge in machine learning – solving large-scale saddle-point problems efficiently. The theoretical results and experimental validation demonstrate the potential for improved performance compared to existing methods, particularly in scenarios with ill-conditioned problems or non-separable regularizers. The extension to monotone operators significantly broadens the scope of application.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:** While experiments are presented, they focus on a relatively limited set of datasets and problem settings. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse scenarios and comparing against a wider range of state-of-the-art algorithms would strengthen the empirical validation. The paper mentions testing losses in Appendix F but does not include them in the main paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithms likely depends on the choice of parameters (e.g., step size, regularization strength). A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning strategies and their impact on performance would be beneficial. The description of parameter tuning choices in Appendix F is brief.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The paper focuses on theoretical analysis and algorithmic development. A more thorough discussion of practical implementation details, such as efficient data structures and parallelization strategies, could enhance the impact of the work.
* **Incremental Improvement over Existing Methods:** While the proposed methods demonstrate improvements, particularly with acceleration, the gains are often incremental rather than revolutionary. A stronger emphasis on the specific scenarios where the proposed algorithms provide the most significant advantages could be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound extension of existing stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the methods in practice, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.
* The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the work.
* The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning with potentially far-reaching implications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The improvements over existing methods are often incremental.
* The discussion of practical implementation details is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. While the experimental evaluation could be more extensive and the practical considerations more thoroughly discussed, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.  The clarity of presentation and the significance of the results make it a worthy addition to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**
