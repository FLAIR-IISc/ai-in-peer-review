

This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, extending techniques from convex minimization to a broader class of problems. The authors consider the problem min_x∈R^d max_y∈R^n K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth and convex-concave, and M is "simple" but potentially non-smooth (with easy proximal operator). They provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context, showing linear convergence under appropriate assumptions. The paper also extends these methods to monotone operators and provides an accelerated version using the "catalyst" framework.

The main contributions are: (1) extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with rigorous convergence analysis; (2) demonstrating the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency; (3) showing how to accelerate these methods using the catalyst framework; and (4) extending the approach to monotone operators.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a clear and rigorous theoretical analysis, with theorems establishing convergence rates for the proposed algorithms. The proofs are detailed in the appendix.
- The authors carefully distinguish their approach from existing work, showing how their methods differ from previous stochastic methods for saddle-point problems.
- The experimental results on real machine learning problems (including non-separable losses and regularizers) demonstrate clear practical advantages of their methods.
- The paper connects to a broader class of problems (monotone operators) and shows how their approach extends beyond saddle-point problems.
- The algorithms are clearly presented with pseudocode, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality:
The paper is original in extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, a problem class where these techniques had not been previously applied. The authors demonstrate how their approach differs from existing work, particularly in the use of non-uniform sampling and the extension to monotone operators. The paper's approach to saddle-point problems is distinct from previous work on stochastic methods for saddle-point problems, which typically did not provide linear convergence rates.

Significance:
Saddle-point problems are fundamental in machine learning, arising in many contexts including robust optimization, dual formulations of learning problems, and unsupervised learning. The proposed methods provide significant speedups over existing batch and stochastic methods, particularly for large-scale problems. The paper demonstrates this through both theoretical analysis and empirical results on real machine learning problems. The acceleration technique using the catalyst framework is particularly significant as it provides a formal advantage for variance reduction methods.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs of convergence rates. The authors carefully consider the assumptions required for their analysis and provide a clear comparison to existing methods. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed methods outperform existing approaches.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written and well-organized. The key contributions are clearly stated, and the algorithms are presented with pseudocode. The notation is consistent throughout, and the paper provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
The paper assumes strong convexity-concavity constants are known, which may be a limitation in practice (though the authors note this is a direction for future work). The experimental section, while supportive, could provide more detailed comparisons with specialized methods for specific problem types.

Pro and Con Acceptance:
Pro: First rigorous convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems; demonstrated theoretical and empirical advantages; clear presentation of algorithms and experimental results; extension to monotone operators broadens applicability.

Con: Requires knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants (a common limitation in accelerated methods); some aspects of the experimental section could be more detailed.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems with a rigorous theoretical analysis and practical implementation. The results advance the state of the art in optimization for machine learning, particularly for problems where the primal or dual formulations are not separable. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with applications to machine learning. The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS paper: it is of high quality, clear, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance.
