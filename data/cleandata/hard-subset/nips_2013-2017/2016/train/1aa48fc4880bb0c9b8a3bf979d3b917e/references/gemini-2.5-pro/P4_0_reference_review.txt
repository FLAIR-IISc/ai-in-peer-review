### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods for solving composite decomposable saddle-point problems of the form `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`. The key assumption is that the gradient operator of the smooth, convex-concave function K can be expressed as a finite sum of component operators. This structure is common in machine learning but is often not exploitable when the objective is non-separable, precluding the direct application of standard variance-reduction techniques to the primal or dual formulations.

The primary contribution is the extension of the SVRG and SAGA algorithms, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization [2, 3], to this saddle-point setting. While the algorithmic adaptation is presented as straightforward, the core of the work lies in providing the first rigorous linear convergence analysis for these methods in this context. The analysis is framed more generally in terms of finding zeros of monotone operators, a framework that includes saddle-point problems as a special case but is broader. This approach allows the authors to prove convergence using only monotonicity, without relying on the co-coercivity property often used in convex minimization analyses, which does not hold for general saddle-point problems.

The paper also introduces two key practical and theoretical improvements: (1) the use of non-uniform sampling, which is shown to yield superior theoretical bounds and empirical performance, and (2) an acceleration scheme based on the "Catalyst" framework [7], which further improves the convergence rate and results in an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods under certain conditions. The work is positioned as a significant improvement over existing batch algorithms [19, 20] and standard stochastic methods [22] for this problem class, offering the fast linear convergence of batch methods with the low per-iteration cost of stochastic methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a novel and important contribution by being the first to successfully apply and analyze variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA for general composite saddle-point problems. This opens the door for efficiently solving a wide range of machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization, robust optimization), which were previously intractable for this class of fast, linearly convergent algorithms. The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate a clear state-of-the-art advance for this problem setting.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides rigorous convergence theorems for SVRG, SAGA, and an accelerated variant. The analysis via monotone operators is elegant and general, correctly identifying that the co-coercivity assumption from the convex case is not needed, which is a crucial insight for the saddle-point setting. The complexity analysis, summarized in Table 1, is comprehensive and clearly articulates the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods over existing ones, particularly highlighting the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, states the assumptions, and systematically presents the proposed algorithms and their theoretical guarantees. The connection to prior work is well-established, and the broader context of monotone operators is explained effectively in Section 6, adding depth to the contribution without sacrificing clarity. The experimental section is focused and provides compelling evidence that supports the theoretical claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Problem Constants:** The proposed algorithms and their theoretical analyses require knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity parameters (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). In practice, these constants can be difficult to estimate accurately. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion and suggest adaptivity as future work, but it remains a practical hurdle for the current methods.

2.  **Gap between SAGA Theory and Practice:** The convergence theory for SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step to maintain an unbiased gradient estimate, which adds computational overhead. The experiments, following common practice, use a heuristic mixture of sampling schemes without this step. While reasonable, this creates a small disconnect between the proven theoretical result and the algorithm implemented in the experiments.

3.  **Rates for Convex Minimization:** The authors note that their general analysis, based only on monotonicity, yields suboptimal rates when applied directly to the special case of convex minimization, as it does not exploit the stronger co-coercivity property. While they correctly argue that convex problems can be recast as saddle-point problems to recover optimal rates, this highlights that the analysis is not tight for all sub-classes of problems covered by the monotone operator framework.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** Provides the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of non-separable saddle-point problems, a significant theoretical and practical advance.
*   **Pro:** The analysis is novel, technically strong, and appropriately general, correctly using the monotone operator framework without restrictive assumptions like co-coercivity.
*   **Pro:** Introduces and analyzes acceleration and non-uniform sampling, leading to state-of-the-art complexity bounds.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written and the empirical results convincingly validate the theoretical findings.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The algorithms require knowledge of problem parameters (e.g., strong convexity, Lipschitz constants) that may not be available in practice.
*   **Con:** The algorithmic adaptation of SVRG/SAGA is relatively direct; the main novelty is in the analysis, which could be seen as an incremental (though important) theoretical step.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It addresses a significant gap in the literature with a high-quality, novel, and impactful contribution.
