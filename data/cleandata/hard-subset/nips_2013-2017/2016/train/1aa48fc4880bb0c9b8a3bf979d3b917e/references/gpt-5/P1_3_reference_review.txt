Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum composite saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex-concave with a proximal operator, and K is smooth but not necessarily separable. The key idea is to extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to convex-concave saddle-point problems and, more generally, to finding zeros of monotone operators. The authors (i) provide linear convergence analyses of SVRG and SAGA in this setting without requiring each component to be a gradient of a scalar function; (ii) show how to exploit two natural splits of the operator B=(∂xK, −∂yK), including a “factored” split tailored to rows/columns in bilinear problems; (iii) develop non-uniform sampling schemes that improve constants; and (iv) adapt the Catalyst/ proximal-point acceleration to obtain improved complexities. They also show that their analysis extends to strongly monotone inclusions A+B, with A maximal monotone (prox available) and B a sum of Lipschitz operators (monotone in aggregate), going beyond standard co-coercive settings used in proximal-SVRG/SAGA for minimization. Experiments on classification with non-separable loss and regularizer confirm the practical benefits, especially with non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

This work sits at the intersection of variance reduction (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) and primal-dual/monotone operator methods (forward-backward splitting, proximal point, Chambolle-Pock PDHG). Related stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC, AdaSPDC) require separability; the proposed methods handle non-separable losses/regularizers. The connection to monotone variational inequalities relates to classical extra-gradient/Mirror-Prox methods.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: First linear-convergence analyses of SVRG/SAGA for saddle-point problems under strong convexity-concavity, without assuming component gradients of scalar functions; extension to general monotone operators beyond co-coercive settings.
- Clear algorithmic contributions: Practical SVRG/SAGA variants, mini-batching, factored vs individual splits, and concrete non-uniform sampling schemes with interpretable constants in the bilinear case (using ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max).
- Acceleration: A clean proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper with simple analysis yields improved complexities and a method that can dominate accelerated batch methods.
- Theory-to-practice: Detailed complexity expressions accounting for prox and operator costs, and experiments showing gains from non-uniform sampling and acceleration, especially in ill-conditioned regimes.
- Scope: The monotone operator viewpoint broadens applicability (e.g., variational inequalities).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption strength: Linear rates require strong convexity-concavity. While standard for variance reduction, adaptivity to unknown strong convexity (as achieved in SAGA for minimization) is deferred to future work.
- Step-size and constants: Step sizes depend on L and L̄, which can be nontrivial to estimate; the analysis uses an L^2-type constant in rates, which is somewhat conservative compared to 1/L-type bounds in smooth minimization. Practical guidance for estimating/tuning these constants would help.
- SAGA storage and resampling: For non-uniform sampling, theory requires resampling of stored components; experiments omit it (as in prior work). A discussion/ablation of the practical impact would strengthen the case. Storage is O(nd) for individual splits; while factored storage reduces this to O(n+d), guidance on when each is preferable would be useful.
- Experimental scope: Results are on two datasets and a specific non-separable setup. Comparisons to strong primal-dual baselines beyond FB/accelerated FB (e.g., Chambolle-Pock PDHG with optimal tuning, Mirror-Prox/extra-gradient, stochastic Mirror-Prox variants) are missing. More tasks (e.g., robust optimization, structured losses) and ablations (uniform vs non-uniform, factored vs individual splits, effect of mini-batch size m) would increase persuasiveness.
- Acceleration for SAGA: Proven only for SVRG; an outline or discussion of extending the proof to SAGA would be valuable.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and careful. The monotone operator analysis that avoids co-coercivity is a key contribution and appears correct. Algorithm statements and rates are explicit; complexities account for prox/operator costs. Some proofs are in the appendix; including brief proof sketches in the main text (esp. why linear convergence follows from strong monotonicity with the chosen step sizes) would aid readability. The experiments substantiate the main claims, though broader baselines would help.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized. The Ω/Ω* norm scaling with λ,γ is useful and clearly motivates conditioning. Suggestions:
- Clarify the definition of L vs L̄ and consistently distinguish L^2 from L 2 typographically.
- Summarize key assumptions (A)-(C) and their consequences (existence/uniqueness, prox availability) in a compact table.
- Provide more intuition for the factored split and storage benefits with a small schematic.
- State more explicitly when resampling is required in SAGA and what the “optional” steps imply for theory vs practice.

Originality
High. Extending SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle-point problems with linear rates and to general monotone operators without co-coercivity is novel. The factored split and non-uniform sampling tailored to saddle-point structure are also original and practically important. The acceleration via a proximal-point view is a neat twist on Catalyst in this context.

Significance
High. Many ML problems naturally fit the non-separable saddle-point template (robust optimization, AUC surrogates, structured regularizers). Providing variance-reduced, accelerated methods with solid theory and practical sampling schemes should influence both research and practice. The monotone operator framing broadens the impact beyond ML.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide practical heuristics for estimating L̄ and choosing σ without over-conservatism?
- How sensitive are the methods to mis-specification of λ,γ in Ω? Any empirical guidelines?
- Could you compare against PDHG (Chambolle-Pock) and Mirror-Prox with optimal step sizes on your tasks?
- What is the empirical impact of the SAGA resampling step under non-uniform sampling?
- Any progress or obstacles toward extending the acceleration proof to SAGA?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel, well-founded extension of variance reduction to saddle points/monotone operators
- Strong, explicit theory with linear convergence and acceleration
- Practical algorithmic design (non-uniform sampling, factored splits) and meaningful experiments
- Broad applicability and potential impact

Cons:
- Strong convexity-concavity required; no adaptivity yet
- Experimental comparisons could be broader (PDHG/Mirror-Prox baselines)
- Practical guidance on constants and resampling could be expanded
- Acceleration proof only for SVRG

Minor comments
- Please add citations/discussion of extra-gradient/Mirror-Prox (Nemirovski) and Korpelevich’s method in related work and empirical comparisons.
- Some notation (L 2 vs L^2) is inconsistent; unify.
- Briefly justify the choice of mini-batch sizes m in bilinear settings and their effect on prox amortization.

Overall recommendation: Strong accept. The paper makes a substantial theoretical and practical contribution to stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems.
