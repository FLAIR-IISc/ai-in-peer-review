This paper presents stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, extending prior work primarily focused on convex minimization. The core idea is to adapt these algorithms, which are effective for finite-sum separable problems, to the saddle-point setting, arising frequently in machine learning duality. The authors provide convergence analysis, demonstrate the importance of splitting strategies, and show that the saddle-point formulation allows for efficient non-uniform sampling. They also present an acceleration technique leveraging a catalyst framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting. The extension of the analysis to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the results. The theoretical results are well-motivated and supported by appropriate assumptions.
*   **Novelty:** Adapting SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a novel contribution. The analysis demonstrating the need for specific splitting strategies within the saddle-point formulation is also insightful.  The extension to monotone operators further enhances novelty.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is reasonably consistent, and the concepts are explained clearly.  The connection to existing work in convex optimization and variational inequalities is well-established.
*   **Significance:** Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., SVMs, robust optimization). Providing efficient algorithms for these problems is therefore highly significant. The experimental results further support the practical value of the proposed methods.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper covers a considerable amount of ground, including detailed analysis, comparisons to existing methods, and an experimental evaluation. The inclusion of several theorems and a detailed overview of related work strengthens the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Assumptions:** The assumptions (A)-(C) regarding the smoothness and convexity/concavity of the functions K and M, while standard, can be restrictive in some applications.  The paper doesn't fully address how sensitive the algorithm's performance is to violations of these assumptions.
*   **Practical Considerations of Non-Uniform Sampling:** While the theoretical benefits of non-uniform sampling are demonstrated, the paper doesn’t discuss the practical challenges of implementing it effectively. Specifically, computing the required sampling probabilities (e.g., based on matrix norms) can be computationally expensive.
*   **Experimental Setup:** While the experiments provide evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, the description of the datasets and experimental setup could be more detailed. Comparing to other state-of-the-art algorithms tailored to saddle-point problems (beyond batch and stochastic gradient methods adapted to the primal) would strengthen the results.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** Experiments focus primarily on binary classification and ROC curve surrogates. Extending the experiments to other types of saddle-point problems would demonstrate broader applicability.
*   **Catalyst Acceleration:** The Catalyst extension while promising, is presented briefly. A more detailed analysis and exploration of the optimal tuning parameters for this acceleration would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of optimization.
*   The analysis is rigorous and provides theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the methods.
*   The work addresses an important problem with significant implications for machine learning.
*   The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees may be restrictive in some applications.
*   The practical challenges of implementing non-uniform sampling are not fully addressed.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The presentation of the acceleration step is brief.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The technical analysis is rigorous, the results are novel and significant, and the paper is well-written and organized. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding the assumptions, practical implementation of non-uniform sampling, and expanded experimental evaluation.
