This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, for solving strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are common in machine learning, particularly for models with non-separable loss functions or regularizers, which cannot be directly addressed by standard variance-reduced methods that require a finite-sum structure in the primal or dual objective.

The authors extend the SVRG and SAGA algorithms to the saddle-point setting, where the gradient operator is decomposed into a sum of component operators. The main contribution is a novel convergence analysis that establishes linear convergence rates for these methods. This analysis is framed more generally in the context of finding zeros of monotone operators and importantly does not rely on co-coercivity, a property often used in the analysis of variance reduction for convex minimization but which does not hold for saddle-point problems. The paper further explores different ways to decompose the operator, highlighting the benefits of "factored" splits and non-uniform sampling schemes, which are shown to yield superior theoretical bounds and empirical performance. Finally, an accelerated variant based on the Catalyst framework is proposed for SVRG, leading to a method that is theoretically guaranteed to be faster than accelerated full-gradient methods. The theoretical findings are supported by experiments on classification tasks with non-separable objectives, demonstrating significant practical speed-ups over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is the core contribution and appears to be sound. Proving linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA in this more general setting without relying on co-coercivity is a non-trivial achievement. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs (referenced in the appendix) and complemented by a clear complexity analysis summarized effectively in Table 1. The work is a complete research contribution, covering the proposal of algorithms, a novel theoretical analysis, an extension to acceleration, and strong empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, situates the work within the existing literature, and methodically builds up its contributions. The problem formulation and assumptions are stated precisely. The distinction between different operator splits and sampling schemes is well-explained. The high-level intuition behind the algorithms and the analysis is conveyed effectively. The experimental section, while concise, clearly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed methods.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. To my knowledge, it is the first to provide a rigorous analysis of SVRG- and SAGA-type methods for the general class of decomposable saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence. While the algorithms are natural extensions of their well-known counterparts, the analysis framework based on monotone operators is novel in this context and provides new insights. The exploration of factored splits and the successful application of Catalyst for acceleration further add to the originality of the contribution. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior art in stochastic saddle-point optimization, which has largely focused on sublinear rates, and from variance reduction for convex minimization, which has relied on more restrictive assumptions.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact. Saddle-point formulations are a powerful tool for modeling complex machine learning problems (e.g., with structured penalties, robust optimization, AUC maximization). By making the state-of-the-art variance reduction techniques applicable to this broader class of problems, this work significantly expands the toolkit of practitioners and researchers. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods are substantial, not just theoretical artifacts. The general analysis based on monotone operators could also serve as a foundation for extending variance reduction to other related problems, such as variational inequalities.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few points that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Algorithmic vs. Analytical Novelty:** The paper's main novelty lies in its analysis. The algorithms themselves are direct analogues of SVRG and SAGA. This is not a major weakness, as the non-trivial analysis and the new application domain are sufficient contributions, but it is worth noting that the algorithmic contribution is modest.

2.  **Clarity on "Factored" Splits:** The description of the "factored" split in Section 2(C) could be more explicit. The notation `B_jk(x, y) = (p_j B_k^x(x, y), q_k B_j^y(x, y))` and the role of the sequences `p` and `q` might be confusing for some readers. It would be helpful to define `B_k^x` and `B_j^y` more formally and clarify that `p` and `q` are probability vectors over the indices of the respective partial derivative splits.

3.  **Theory vs. Practice in SAGA:** The paper notes that for non-uniform sampling in SAGA, the theory requires resampling, but the experiments use a heuristic without resampling. While this is a common practice, a brief discussion on the potential gap between theory and practice, and why the heuristic is expected to work well, would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Incomplete Extension of Acceleration:** The acceleration scheme is developed and proven only for SVRG. The paper claims "the same scheme should work for SAGA," but this is left as a conjecture. While plausible, providing at least a sketch of the argument or acknowledging this as a direction for future work would make the claim more complete.

5.  **Dependence on Problem Parameters:** The algorithms' step sizes depend on constants like `L`, `L̄`, `λ`, and `γ`, which may not be known in practice. The conclusion briefly mentions adaptivity as future work. A short discussion in the main text about the practical implications of this dependence (e.g., sensitivity to estimates) would be beneficial.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and open problem: extending fast variance-reduced methods to general saddle-point problems.
*   Provides the first linear convergence rate analysis for SVRG/SAGA in this setting, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The analysis technique is novel for this problem class and more general than previous analyses for convex minimization.
*   The work is thorough, considering different sampling schemes, operator splits, and an accelerated variant.
*   Strong empirical results on relevant machine learning problems confirm the theoretical advantages.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and is likely to be highly influential.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core algorithms are straightforward extensions of existing methods.
*   Minor clarity issues and small gaps between theory and practice (as noted above) could be improved.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are compelling. This is a strong paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the optimization toolkit for machine learning. The theoretical novelty and practical significance far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
