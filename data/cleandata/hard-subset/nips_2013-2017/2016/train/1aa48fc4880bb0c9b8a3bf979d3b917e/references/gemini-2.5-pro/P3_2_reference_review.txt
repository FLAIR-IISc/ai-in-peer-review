### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents the first stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods for finite-sum saddle-point problems, achieving linear convergence rates. The authors extend the well-known SVRG and SAGA algorithms, originally developed for convex minimization, to the more general convex-concave saddle-point setting: `min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y)`. Here, `K` is a smooth finite sum and `M` is a "simple" strongly convex-concave function.

The main contributions are threefold:
1.  **Algorithmic Extension:** The paper proposes SVRG and SAGA variants for saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic modifications appear straightforward, the analysis is highly non-trivial.
2.  **Novel Convergence Analysis:** A key contribution is the convergence proof, which leverages the interpretation of saddle-point optimization as finding the zero of a monotone operator. This framework is more general and notably does not rely on the co-coercivity assumption used in standard SVRG/SAGA analyses, an assumption that does not hold for saddle-point problems. This analysis establishes linear convergence for the proposed methods.
3.  **Practical and Theoretical Enhancements:** The authors analyze different ways to split the saddle-point operator, introducing "factored" splits that are memory-efficient for SAGA. They demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for achieving the best theoretical rates and superior empirical performance. Finally, they show how to accelerate SVRG using a "Catalyst"-like framework, yielding an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.

### Strengths
**Significance and Impact:** This work addresses a critical gap in the large-scale optimization literature. While variance reduction has revolutionized stochastic convex minimization, its application to saddle-point problems—which are fundamental to areas like robust optimization, game theory, and modern machine learning (e.g., AUC maximization, training GANs)—was an open and important problem. By providing the first linearly-convergent stochastic algorithms for this general class of problems, the paper makes a significant and timely contribution. The results are likely to be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

**Technical Quality and Originality:** The paper is technically excellent. The main novelty and technical depth lie in the convergence analysis. Shifting the perspective from convex optimization to monotone operators is a crucial insight that allows the authors to bypass the limitations of existing proof techniques. This not only provides a sound theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms but also elegantly shows that they apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities, without modification. This is a substantial theoretical advance over prior art. The complexity analysis, summarized in Table 1, is thorough and clearly demonstrates the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, and the problem setting and assumptions are precisely stated. The authors do an excellent job of connecting their work to related literature on convex optimization (SVRG, SAGA), saddle-point algorithms, and monotone operators. The inclusion of concrete machine learning examples in Section 2.1 effectively grounds the abstract formulation in practical applications. The experimental section provides compelling evidence supporting the theoretical claims, showing significant speedups over relevant baselines.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement
The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for further improvement rather than significant weaknesses.

1.  **Practicality of Non-uniform Sampling:** The theoretical gains from non-uniform sampling are clear. However, these sampling schemes require pre-computing quantities like row and column norms of the data matrix `K` (e.g., `(KK^T)_jj` and `(K^T K)_kk`). This pre-computation can be expensive for large, dense matrices. A brief discussion on this computational overhead, and perhaps scenarios where it is negligible (e.g., for sparse data or when these quantities are already needed for other purposes), would add practical depth to the analysis.

2.  **SAGA with Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper mentions that for the theoretical analysis of SAGA with non-uniform sampling to hold, a resampling step is required, but that the experiments follow prior work in using a mixture of sampling schemes without resampling. This is a common practice, but a sentence explaining why this heuristic is effective or what potential issues it avoids (e.g., the complexity of the resampling step) would be helpful for readers aiming to implement the algorithm.

3.  **Acceleration for SAGA:** The paper develops an acceleration scheme for SVRG based on the Catalyst framework and notes that "the same scheme should work for SAGA." While this is a reasonable claim, providing a brief argument or citing relevant work that supports this extension would strengthen the paper's claims about acceleration.

4.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experiments are well-designed, especially the inclusion of a non-separable problem to highlight the unique contribution of the work. For the separable case on `rcv1`, the legends "fba-primal" and "saga-primal" could be slightly more descriptive (e.g., "Accelerated GD (primal)" and "SAGA (primal)") to immediately clarify for the reader that these are standard convex optimization methods applied to the primal formulation.

### Conclusion and Recommendation
This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. It successfully bridges the gap between variance-reduced stochastic methods and saddle-point problems, supported by a novel and powerful theoretical analysis based on monotone operators. The work is significant, technically sound, and clearly presented. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **High Significance:** Solves a long-standing and important problem at the intersection of optimization and machine learning.
*   **Novelty:** Provides the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for general finite-sum saddle-point problems. The proof technique is elegant and novel.
*   **Technical Soundness:** Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear complexity bounds and strong empirical validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   None of significance.

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS. It represents a clear advance in the state of the art and will be of high interest to the community.
