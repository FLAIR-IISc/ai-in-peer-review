

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, which are ubiquitous in machine learning through duality. The authors consider problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y) where K is smooth and convex-concave, and M is potentially non-smooth but has an easily computable proximal operator. The paper builds on previous NIPS work on variance reduction methods (SAG [1], SVRG [2], SAGA [3]) and extends them to the saddle-point setting, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context. The authors also extend the "catalyst" acceleration framework [7] to saddle-point problems, showing theoretical and empirical improvements over batch and stochastic methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide convergence proofs for both SVRG and SAGA variants applied to saddle-point problems, carefully addressing differences from convex minimization (particularly regarding the interpretation as finding zeros of monotone operators). The analysis properly accounts for the structure of saddle-point problems, including different split types (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) and the importance of non-uniform sampling. The experimental results in Section 7 support the theoretical claims, showing that non-uniform sampling and accelerated variants significantly outperform batch methods and uniform sampling approaches. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., requiring knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants) and discusses future work on adaptivity. The proofs in the appendices are thorough and well-constructed.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation, assumptions, and algorithms are presented with precision. The authors carefully define all notation (e.g., the weighted Euclidean norm Ω(x,y)) and provide helpful examples in Section 2.1 (supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, etc.). The experimental section clearly presents results, with well-labeled figures showing the distance to optimizers across iterations. The paper is self-contained and provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The connection to related work is thorough, with appropriate citations to NIPS proceedings and other relevant literature.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant and novel contribution by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. Previous work focused exclusively on convex minimization, and the authors convincingly demonstrate that the saddle-point setting requires different analysis (e.g., using monotonicity rather than strong convexity). The paper's key novel insights include: (1) the need for different split structures in saddle-point problems, (2) the critical importance of non-uniform sampling, and (3) the extension of the catalyst framework to saddle-point problems. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) further broadens the applicability of the methods beyond saddle-point problems, which is a valuable theoretical contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on separable saddle-point problems [11, 12] and convex minimization [1, 2, 3].

**Significance:** The work addresses a meaningful gap in the optimization literature for machine learning. Saddle-point problems arise naturally in many machine learning contexts (duality, robust optimization, non-separable losses), and the proposed algorithms can handle problems that previous methods cannot (non-separable losses/regularizers). The theoretical improvements (linear convergence rates with better complexity bounds than batch methods) are significant, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The empirical results demonstrate substantial practical improvements over existing methods, with accelerated SVRG providing the best performance. The extension to monotone operators also broadens the applicability of these techniques beyond machine learning.

**Strengths:**
- First convergence analysis for variance reduction methods on saddle-point problems
- Clear theoretical and empirical demonstration of the importance of non-uniform sampling
- Extension of the catalyst framework to saddle-point problems, providing accelerated algorithms
- Comprehensive analysis covering both theoretical complexity and practical implementation
- Well-organized presentation with helpful examples and clear notation

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants (though the authors acknowledge this limitation and plan to address adaptivity in future work)
- Experimental section could include more diverse problem instances beyond the specific non-separable loss examples
- The connection to specific real-world applications could be strengthened with more detailed case studies

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1. Novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, addressing a significant gap in the literature
2. Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence proofs and complexity bounds
3. Strong empirical validation showing practical improvements over existing methods
4. Extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the work
5. Well-written, clear presentation with appropriate context and related work

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1. Limited to strongly convex-concave problems (though this is acknowledged as a limitation)
2. Some aspects of the experimental setup could be expanded for broader validation

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the optimization literature with clear theoretical advances and practical benefits for machine learning applications. The work is well-executed, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in the field. The paper is significantly different from previous NIPS work on variance reduction methods, which focused exclusively on convex minimization. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the extension to monotone operators adds further value. I recommend acceptance.
