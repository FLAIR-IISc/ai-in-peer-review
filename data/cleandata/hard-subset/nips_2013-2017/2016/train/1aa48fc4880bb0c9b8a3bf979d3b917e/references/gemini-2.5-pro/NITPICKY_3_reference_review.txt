### Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction (SVRG) and SAGA algorithms, originally developed for convex finite-sum minimization, to the more general setting of saddle-point problems. The authors propose stochastic variance-reduced versions of the forward-backward algorithm for composite, strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The key contributions include: (1) providing the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point setting, leveraging a connection to monotone operators, which makes the analysis applicable to an even broader class of problems; (2) investigating different ways to decompose the problem structure (e.g., "factored" splits) and highlighting the theoretical and practical importance of non-uniform sampling; and (3) showing that these methods can be accelerated using a "Catalyst"-like framework, leading to an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods. Experiments on a binary classification task with non-separable losses and regularizers demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature. While variance reduction techniques are a cornerstone of modern large-scale optimization, their application to general saddle-point problems was not well-established. This work provides the first linearly-convergent stochastic algorithms for this problem class, making it possible to efficiently solve problems with non-separable losses or regularizers that do not fit the standard finite-sum minimization template. This is a significant step forward.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis is a major strength. By framing the problem in the context of finding zeros of monotone operators and using only monotonicity properties, the authors provide a general and powerful analytical framework. This approach elegantly sidesteps the need for co-coercivity, which is required in some related analyses but does not hold for saddle-point problems, thereby broadening the applicability of the results to problems like variational inequalities.

3.  **Practical Insights:** The paper provides valuable practical insights. The discussion on "factored" splits and the rigorous demonstration of the benefits of non-uniform sampling are crucial for achieving good performance. The experimental results, though brief, convincingly show that the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG) outperform standard batch and stochastic baselines in a practical machine learning setting.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

Despite the strong core contributions, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical theoretical omissions to a general lack of polish, which collectively undermine its quality and readiness for publication at a top-tier conference like NIPS.

1.  **Critical Flaws in Theoretical Statements:**
    *   **Theorem 2 (SAGA convergence):** The convergence rate provided for SAGA is `E [ Ω(xt − x∗, yt − y∗)² ] ≲ ( 1− (max{ 3|I|²m , 1 + L²µ² + 3 L̄²mµ² })⁻¹)ᵗ ...`. The parameter `µ` is not defined anywhere in the main body of the paper. This is a critical omission that makes the central result for the SAGA algorithm unverifiable and incomprehensible. Without a definition for `µ`, this theorem is meaningless.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   **Algorithm 2 (SAGA):** The description of the SAGA update is confusing. Several steps are marked as "(optional)". If the step `Replace G← G− ∑{gik − hk} and gik ← hk` is optional, the algorithm is fundamentally incorrect, as the full gradient estimate `G` would become stale. This is not an option but the core of the algorithm. This ambiguity makes the algorithm description deeply flawed.
    *   **Section 5 (Acceleration):** The description of the Catalyst acceleration is hand-wavy and confusing. The claim that the "analysis is simpler" is not well-supported by the text. The connection between the inner SVRG loop and the outer proximal-point iteration is not clearly explained. For instance, the statement "every log(1 + τ) epochs, we change the values of (x̄, ȳ)" is unconventional and lacks a clear justification. The convergence rate in Theorem 3 appears to be for the outer loop, but it is presented in terms of `v` epochs of the inner algorithm, conflating the two. The overall complexity analysis feels rushed and incomplete.
    *   **Notation and Definitions:** Several notations are clumsy or potentially incorrect. For example, in Section 2.2, the matrix notation for the Forward-Backward update is applied in a way that is hard to follow, even if the final expanded expression is correct. In Assumption (C), the definition of the "factored" split `Bjk(x, y) = (pjBxk (x, y), qkB y j (x, y))` is poorly defined, as the terms `Bxk` and `Byj` are not introduced.

3.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper exhibits a lack of careful preparation, which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.
    *   A leftover header from a previous submission template is present on the first page: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain."
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted. Many entries are incomplete, containing extraneous commas (e.g., "Adv. NIPS,, ,") and lacking essential information like years or consistent formatting.
    *   There are multiple typos, such as "which can de done serially" in Section 2.2.
    *   The experimental setup in Section 7 is too brief. Key details about the loss function formulation (e.g., how predictions `Kx` map to the loss arguments) are delegated to the appendix, hindering reproducibility.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

While the core ideas presented in this paper are novel, important, and theoretically sound, the manuscript in its current form falls far short of the quality standards for NIPS. The presence of a critical error in a main theorem (undefined `µ` in Theorem 2), combined with severe clarity issues in the algorithm descriptions and the acceleration section, and a general lack of polish evidenced by numerous formatting errors, makes the paper difficult to trust and fully comprehend.

The work has the potential to be an excellent contribution, but it requires a major revision to fix the theoretical omissions, clarify the confusing sections, and correct the multitude of presentation flaws. As it stands, I cannot recommend acceptance.

**Recommendation: Reject**
