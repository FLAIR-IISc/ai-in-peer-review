## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the setting of saddle-point problems, a common formulation in machine learning arising from duality. The authors provide convergence analysis for these extended algorithms, demonstrating linear convergence under certain assumptions. They highlight the importance of splitting the saddle-point problem in specific ways and show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency.  Furthermore, they present an acceleration technique based on the "catalyst" framework, improving upon existing batch algorithms.  A key contribution is framing the analysis around monotone operators, broadening the applicability of their results beyond purely saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Empirical validation on benchmark datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant extension. While variance reduction methods are well-established for convex optimization, their adaptation to the non-convex/concave nature of saddle-point problems is non-trivial and addresses a gap in the literature.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a convergence analysis for the proposed algorithms, a crucial aspect for establishing their validity and understanding their behavior.  The connection to monotone operator theory is elegant and expands the scope of the results.
* **Practical Relevance:** Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., supervised learning with non-separable losses/regularizers, robust optimization). Providing efficient algorithms for these problems is highly valuable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims and demonstrate the performance of the algorithms on realistic datasets. The comparison to existing methods, including primal counterparts, is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the assumptions are explicitly stated.
* **Acceleration Technique:** The extension of the catalyst framework offers a practical improvement in convergence speed.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on strong convexity-concavity assumptions (A) and Lipschitz continuity (B, C).  While common in optimization literature, these assumptions may be restrictive in some practical scenarios.  The paper acknowledges the need for adaptive techniques to address these assumptions in future work, but it remains a limitation.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The performance of the algorithms is sensitive to parameter choices, such as the step size and sampling probabilities. The paper provides guidelines for setting these parameters, but a more systematic analysis of their impact would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Notation:** The introduction of different norms (Ω, Ω*) and the numerous indices (i, j, k) can make the notation somewhat dense and challenging to follow in some sections.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are focused on a specific set of datasets and problem settings. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of applications would strengthen the claims of generality.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While complexity analysis is provided, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with different splits (individual vs. factored) would be useful. For instance, the memory requirements for storing gradients in SAGA with different splits are not explicitly addressed.



**Assessment based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  High. The paper presents technically sound algorithms with a solid theoretical foundation. The analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written, but the notation could be simplified in some sections.
* **Originality:** High. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, along with the convergence analysis and acceleration technique, represent a novel contribution.
* **Significance:** High. The work addresses an important problem in machine learning and provides practical algorithms with potential for widespread use.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Provides a valuable extension of existing variance reduction methods to a broader class of problems.
* Includes a comprehensive convergence analysis and empirical validation.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on strong assumptions that may limit the applicability of the algorithms.
* The complexity of notation could hinder understanding.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization.  The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the potential impact of the work is substantial. While the assumptions and notation could be improved, these are minor weaknesses that do not detract from the overall value of the paper.  The authors have clearly addressed a challenging problem and provided a valuable solution.
