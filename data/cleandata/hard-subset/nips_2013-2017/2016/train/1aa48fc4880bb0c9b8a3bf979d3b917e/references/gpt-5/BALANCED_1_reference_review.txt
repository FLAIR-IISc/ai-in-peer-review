Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave. The key assumption is that the gradient operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK) admits a finite-sum decomposition B = ∑_i B_i, not necessarily as gradients of functions. The authors extend variance-reduced methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to this general saddle-point setting, derive linear convergence under strong convex–concavity, and analyze both “individual” and “factored” splits (e.g., selecting matrix elements vs. rows/columns in bilinear problems). They develop non-uniform sampling schemes and show they are critical for favorable L̄(π) constants. They further propose an acceleration via a catalyst-style regularization, interpreted as inexact proximal point, yielding improved complexity bounds. Finally, they observe that their analysis carries over to strongly monotone operator inclusions A + B = 0, with A maximal strongly monotone and B a monotone Lipschitz sum of components B_i, thus covering some variational inequalities. Experiments on two datasets (one with genuinely non-separable loss and regularizer) compare batch FB/accelerated FB, stochastic FB, SAGA, SVRG, and accelerated SVRG, highlighting the benefits of variance reduction and non-uniform sampling.

This work connects to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite sums in convex minimization; to primal–dual methods such as Chambolle–Pock and accelerated variants; to SPDC and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods that require separability; and to stochastic forward–backward for monotone inclusions (Rosasco–Villa–Vũ). Unlike prior variance-reduced primal–dual methods focused on separable sums, the present algorithms and analysis handle general (non-separable) composite saddle-point problems and monotone inclusions without cocoercivity.

Strengths
- Technical contribution and generality: Provides what appears to be the first linear-convergence analysis of SVRG/SAGA-type methods for non-separable saddle-point problems and for a broad class of monotone inclusions (A strongly monotone, B monotone). The operator-splitting view with weighted norms (λ,γ) is clean and flexible.
- Non-uniform sampling and split design: Careful treatment of how to split B and choose sampling probabilities, with explicit L̄(π) constants for bilinear K and factored vs. individual splits. This yields actionable guidance and provable improvements over uniform sampling.
- Acceleration: The catalyst-style regularization, interpreted via proximal-point, yields improved complexity bounds and a practically simple schedule (update anchor every O(log(1+τ)) epochs). The analysis is simpler than Nesterov-based acceleration and applies beyond bilinear K in batch form.
- Practical relevance: Many machine learning problems with non-separable losses/regularizers (AUC surrogates, structured penalties) are within scope. The experiments demonstrate that (accelerated) VR methods outperform batch and stochastic FB on the non-separable case; the role of non-uniform sampling is also evident.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Novelty vs. straightforward extension: Algorithmically, the updates are natural adaptations of SVRG/SAGA to the saddle-point FB framework. The main novelty lies in the analysis (monotone operator view) and the careful sampling/splitting; the paper should better emphasize what fails if one naïvely transfers convex minimization proofs and why the monotone operator route is essential here.
- Assumptions and constants: Convergence requires strong convex–concavity (or strong monotonicity of A), and stepsizes depend on L and L̄(π). In practice these constants are hard to know; the paper suggests substitutes but does not provide adaptive tuning strategies. This may limit robustness.
- Memory/compute trade-offs: SAGA with individual splits requires O(nd) storage of operator values; factored splits reduce to O(n+d) but depend on problem structure. A more thorough treatment of memory and wall-clock costs (including prox costs) is desirable, especially given claims of superiority to accelerated batch methods.
- Empirical scope: Experiments are limited (two datasets) and baselines miss strong primal–dual methods tailored to separable structures (e.g., SPDC) and stochastic PDHG variants. While those may be out-of-scope in the non-separable setting, including them when applicable (and stressing when they cannot be applied) would strengthen the case. Reporting wall-clock time, ablations on mini-batch size m, and sensitivity to non-uniform sampling would also help.
- Theoretical gaps and claims: Acceleration is proved for SVRG; SAGA acceleration is only conjectured. The statement that the accelerated incremental method is “always superior to accelerated batch” should be tempered (indeed, experiments show uniform sampling SAGA can underperform accelerated batch). The monotone inclusion extension is appealing but not validated empirically.
- Clarity/organization: Some notation and constants (L vs. L̄, dependence on λ,γ) are dense; a self-contained main-text sketch of one key proof (e.g., Theorem 1) would aid readability. Minor typos and missing details about the resampling option for non-uniform SAGA should be addressed.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical results are sound and nontrivial, with careful attention to sampling and splitting. Proofs are in appendices; tighter discussion in the main text would improve confidence. Experiments support claims but are limited in breadth.
- Clarity: Generally well-written, with helpful operator viewpoint and algorithm boxes. Could improve exposition around constants, parameter choices, and practical implementations.
- Originality: Moderate-to-strong. While the algorithms resemble known VR methods, extending linear convergence to non-separable saddle-point problems and to general monotone inclusions without cocoercivity is a meaningful advance.
- Significance: High potential. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous, and enabling variance reduction beyond separable cases is impactful. The non-uniform sampling insights are useful in practice.

Pros for acceptance
- First linear-convergence VR analysis for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems.
- Broad monotone operator framework; applicability to variational inequalities.
- Practical, provably beneficial non-uniform sampling and split strategies.
- Acceleration via a simple proximal-point perspective with improved complexity.
- Empirical gains on non-separable tasks.

Cons against acceptance
- Algorithmic novelty is incremental; main novelty is analytical framework.
- Experiments are limited and lack some relevant baselines and wall-clock reporting.
- Dependence on hard-to-know constants; limited guidance on adaptive tuning.
- Storage burden for SAGA with individual splits; practical implications underexplored.
- Some claims (superiority to accelerated batch, SAGA acceleration) are stronger than evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add wall-clock and memory usage comparisons; report prox costs explicitly.
- Include stronger baselines where applicable (SPDC/PDHG variants), and clarify when they cannot be used due to non-separability.
- Provide an adaptive stepsize/parameter selection strategy or a heuristic that does not require L or L̄.
- Expand experiments to a variational inequality or game-theoretic example to substantiate the monotone operator scope.
- Temper claims about universal superiority; provide more ablations on sampling (uniform vs non-uniform) and mini-batch size.
- Improve clarity around the computation/estimation of L̄(π) and the resampling requirement in non-uniform SAGA.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning). The paper makes a solid and timely theoretical contribution with practical implications for a broad class of saddle-point problems. Strengthening empirical validation and tempering some claims would make it even more compelling.
