

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. The authors consider problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is convex-concave and smooth, and M is non-smooth but "simple" (with an easily computable proximal operator). The work builds on prior NIPS contributions like SAGA [3], SVRG [2], and proximal methods [10], while addressing the unique challenges of saddle-point problems.

The paper makes three main contributions: (1) providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems, (2) demonstrating that saddle-point formulations allow two different notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) and that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, and (3) showing how to accelerate these algorithms using the "catalyst" framework [7].

**Quality:** The technical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The paper provides complete convergence proofs for both SVRG and SAGA algorithms under appropriate assumptions, with careful handling of the saddle-point structure. The authors appropriately distinguish their analysis from convex minimization cases, particularly through their use of monotone operator theory. The experimental section effectively supports the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements over batch and stochastic methods, with clear demonstrations of the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the need to know strong convexity-concavity constants) rather than overstating results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and states the contributions. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including precise algorithm pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3) and comprehensive theoretical proofs in the appendices. The discussion of different splitting strategies (factored vs. individual) is particularly clear and well-structured. The experimental section is well-presented with appropriate visualizations showing the performance gains of the proposed methods.

**Originality:** This work is highly original in its extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, a gap that the authors properly identify in the literature. The key insight that non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems (whereas uniform sampling may suffice for convex minimization) represents a significant technical contribution. The extension to monotone operators and variational inequalities further broadens the scope of the work beyond saddle-point problems, demonstrating a deeper theoretical understanding. The paper appropriately cites relevant prior work on saddle-point problems [4, 8, 11, 12] and convex optimization [1-3, 7, 24], clearly differentiating their contributions.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of significant importance in machine learning, as saddle-point problems arise naturally in many contexts including duality for supervised learning, robust optimization, and unsupervised learning. The proposed methods provide substantial theoretical and empirical improvements over existing batch and stochastic algorithms. The results are particularly valuable for non-separable losses or regularizers, which cannot be efficiently handled by existing methods. The extension to monotone operators and variational inequalities further increases the applicability of the work to a broader range of optimization problems.

**Strengths:**
- First convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point problems
- Clear theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs
- Practical insights on non-uniform sampling
- Effective experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability
- Well-structured and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- The acceleration framework is primarily demonstrated for bilinear problems, with less discussion for general non-linear K
- The paper focuses on strongly-convex-concave problems, leaving open the extension to non-strongly convex cases

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. It successfully extends stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, providing both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms that significantly outperform existing approaches. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors have carefully situated their work within the broader context of optimization and machine learning, and their experimental results robustly support their claims. The paper would be of interest to both the optimization and machine learning communities, and it represents a significant advancement in the field of stochastic optimization.

The paper meets all four criteria for acceptance at NIPS: it is of high quality, clear, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance.
