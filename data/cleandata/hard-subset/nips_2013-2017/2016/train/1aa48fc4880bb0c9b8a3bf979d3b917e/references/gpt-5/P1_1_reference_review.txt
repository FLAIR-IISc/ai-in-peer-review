Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) where M is proximable and strongly convex–concave (with possibly different scalings λ, γ) and K is smooth. The key idea is to treat the smooth part via its associated monotone operator B(x,y)=(∂xK, −∂yK), which is assumed decomposable as a finite sum of Lipschitz components Bi, and to import variance-reduction ideas (SVRG, SAGA) from empirical risk minimization to this operator setting. The authors give linear convergence analyses for SVRG and SAGA variants applied to saddle-point problems, with step sizes depending on the operator Lipschitz constant L and a sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π). They emphasize that their decomposition can be done at the operator level (not necessarily as gradients of scalar functions) and introduce “factored” splits aligned with the saddle-point structure, enabling non-uniform sampling across rows/columns for bilinear problems.

They also propose an acceleration scheme via a catalyst/proximal-point wrapper: solve a sequence of more strongly monotone subproblems (adding quadratic regularization around a moving center) with the inner SVRG, yielding an overall complexity of order L̄√(T(B) max_i T(Bi)) up to logs, which improves on accelerated batch methods in their bounds. Beyond saddle points, the analysis extends to finding zeros of A+B where A is maximally and strongly monotone (with known resolvent) and B is a sum of Lipschitz operators whose sum is monotone; co-coercivity of Bi is not assumed.

The work is positioned against batch primal–dual splitting (forward–backward, Chambolle–Pock) and stochastic primal–dual methods for separable problems (e.g., SPDC). The authors argue that existing linearly convergent stochastic methods rely on separability of the loss/regularizer, while their operator-based variance reduction covers non-separable cases and even non-integrable splits. Experiments on two classification tasks (one with non-separable loss and regularizer) show SAGA and accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperform batch primal–dual, and that acceleration helps more on ill-conditioned problems.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean operator-theoretic extension of SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems, removing the “sum-of-functions” requirement and allowing non-integrable splits of B. This is practically relevant for non-separable losses/regularizers and theoretically interesting.
- Technical contribution: linear convergence proofs for SVRG and SAGA under strong convexity–concavity with step sizes depending on L and L̄(π), including careful treatment of non-uniform sampling and mini-batches. The analysis relies only on monotonicity of the aggregate operator, not co-coercivity.
- Acceleration via a catalyst/proximal-point perspective tailored to monotone operators; simpler than Nesterov-based analyses and yielding improved complexity bounds compared to batch accelerated primal–dual.
- Practical guidance on sampling: for bilinear problems, the paper derives informative L̄(π) bounds for element-wise vs factored splits and shows that non-uniform sampling can substantially tighten constants.
- Experiments demonstrate gains on non-separable problems where primal ERM methods are inapplicable, and confirm the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions and constants: Linear rates require strong convexity–concavity (λ, γ) and knowledge of L and L̄(π). In practice, these are rarely known tightly; the paper does not provide adaptive step-size or backtracking strategies (beyond a comment in the conclusion). Empirical sensitivity to misspecification is not discussed.
- Comparison baselines: While batch FB/accelerated FB and primal baselines are included, comparisons to extragradient/mirror-prox variants (deterministic and stochastic) and to modern primal–dual coordinate methods beyond [11,12] (e.g., SPDC variants, randomized PDHG) would strengthen the empirical picture, even if those require separability, by showing robustness in cases where separability is partial.
- SAGA storage and resampling: The theory for non-uniform SAGA requires “resampling” (refreshing m stored operator values after the primal-dual update), but experiments omit this step as in [25]. It would be useful to quantify the empirical impact (or provide a justification) and to more explicitly detail memory costs for factored vs individual splits on large-scale problems.
- Clarity/organization: The paper is dense. Some key definitions and choices (e.g., the precise role of Ω and Ω*, and how the block scalings λ, γ affect step-size conditioning) could be more prominently summarized. The forward–backward baseline is described as “known not to converge in general” but then analyzed with linear rates in the strongly convex–concave case; clarifying the conditions and relation to extragradient/PDHG would aid readers.
- Experiments: Only two datasets, and the non-separable proximal operators are delegated to the appendix. A broader suite (e.g., robust optimization or structured prediction tasks) and ablations (mini-batch size m; factored vs individual splits; uniform vs non-uniform sampling) would make the empirical case more compelling.

Quality
Overall technically sound with nontrivial proofs (as sketched) and careful constants. The operator-splitting perspective and monotonicity-based analysis appear correct and of independent interest. Some practical aspects (step-size selection, unknown constants) would benefit from more discussion/experiments. The acceleration analysis is convincing and yields interpretable trade-offs.

Clarity
Well organized, with assumptions and algorithms clearly stated. However, the manuscript is compact; improving exposition on: (i) intuition for L̄(π) and sampling choices; (ii) differences among splits (function vs operator vs factored) with a small running example; (iii) a short “how to implement” section with pseudocode for factored SAGA storage would help reproducibility.

Originality
High. While variance reduction in ERM is well known, extending SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle points with non-integrable operator splits and only monotonicity of the aggregate operator is novel. The catalyst-style acceleration in the monotone-operator setting is also an elegant contribution. Related work is broadly cited; consider adding explicit discussion of mirror-prox/extragradient and stochastic PDHG.

Significance
High. Many important ML problems (AUC surrogates, robust optimization, structured regularizers) fall into this non-separable saddle-point setting. Providing the first linear-convergence, variance-reduced methods with favorable sampling is impactful, and the monotone-operator extension broadens applicability beyond ML. Practitioners can use the non-uniform sampling guidelines immediately.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and general extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points and monotone operators.
- Solid linear convergence theory with insightful sampling-dependent constants.
- Practical acceleration scheme with improved complexity bounds.
- Empirical gains on relevant non-separable problems.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing comparisons to some relevant stochastic saddle-point baselines.
- Reliance on known condition constants; no adaptive step-size guidance.
- Some clarity gaps and implementation details (SAGA resampling, storage) could be improved.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments comparing to (stochastic) extragradient/mirror-prox/PDHG if feasible.
- Provide an adaptive step-size heuristic (e.g., estimating L̄(π) from mini-batch statistics) and report robustness.
- Include ablations on sampling strategies and mini-batch size, and demonstrate the memory/time benefits of factored SAGA on larger bilinear problems.
- Expand the discussion of when to prefer factored vs individual splits, possibly with a small synthetic study tying performance to ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and ‖K‖max.
