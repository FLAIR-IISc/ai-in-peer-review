## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the setting of saddle-point problems, a common formulation in machine learning arising from duality and regularization. The authors provide convergence analysis for these algorithms in this new context, highlighting differences from their convex minimization counterparts. They demonstrate the importance of splitting strategies tailored to saddle-point problems and show how acceleration techniques (a "catalyst" framework) can improve performance.  The paper also generalizes the analysis beyond saddle-points to monotone operators. Experimental results on both separable and non-separable problems validate the theoretical findings and demonstrate performance gains over existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying SVR methods to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. While SVR methods are well-established for convex optimization, their extension to the saddle-point setting, along with a rigorous analysis, is genuinely new.  The analysis extending beyond saddle-points to monotone operators is a further strengthening of the work.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a detailed convergence analysis, leveraging the properties of monotone operators.  The theoretical results, including the discussion of different splitting strategies and the impact of non-uniform sampling, are well-developed. The bound on the acceleration framework is particularly noteworthy.
* **Practical Relevance:**  Saddle-point problems are prevalent in machine learning (as the authors convincingly demonstrate with examples). The proposed algorithms offer a potentially efficient solution for problems where existing techniques struggle due to non-separability.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed and illustrate the benefits of the proposed methods, especially the accelerated version and the importance of non-uniform sampling.  The comparison to both batch methods and SAGA applied to the primal problem is informative.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and contributions. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are (mostly) accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Assumptions:** The assumptions on the functions *K* and *M* (strong convexity-concavity, Lipschitz continuity, etc.) are relatively strong.  While standard in optimization analysis, it would be valuable to discuss the robustness of the algorithms to deviations from these assumptions.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of SVRG/SAGA often relies on careful tuning of step sizes and other parameters. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of practical parameter selection strategies.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are comprehensive for the chosen datasets, it would strengthen the paper to include results on a wider range of problems, potentially including larger-scale datasets or problems with different characteristics.
* **Catalyst Framework Details:** The description of the acceleration framework in section 5, while theoretically sound, could be made more concrete with a clearer algorithmic description. A pseudocode implementation would be helpful.
* **Comparison to Other Saddle-Point Methods:** The paper focuses on extending SVR methods. A more thorough comparison to existing saddle-point solvers (e.g., those based on proximal point algorithms or operator splitting) would further position the work within the broader literature.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. The paper presents a complete and technically sound contribution.
* **Clarity:**  Good. The paper is generally well-written, although some sections (e.g., the discussion of splitting strategies) could benefit from further clarification.
* **Originality:**  High. Extending SVR methods to saddle-point problems is a novel idea, and the associated analysis is a significant contribution.
* **Significance:**  High. The work addresses a relevant problem in machine learning and provides a potentially valuable tool for solving it efficiently.  The generalization to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of machine learning by providing efficient solvers for a wide range of problems.
* The analysis extends beyond the originally stated problem to monotone operators.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions on the problem functions are relatively strong.
* The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing saddle-point solvers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. I **strongly recommend acceptance**.  Addressing the suggestions regarding parameter sensitivity and comparison to other methods could further strengthen the paper.
