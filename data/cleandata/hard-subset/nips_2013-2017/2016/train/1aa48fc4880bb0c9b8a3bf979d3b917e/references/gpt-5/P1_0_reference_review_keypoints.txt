- Studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with smooth K and strongly convex–concave M having an easy prox.
- Extends SVRG and SAGA to composite saddle-point/composite monotone-operator settings, providing first linear convergence analyses for these incremental methods.
- Analysis covers finding zeros of A+B where A is strongly monotone with known resolvent and B is a sum of Lipschitz operators (not necessarily monotone or gradients).
- Distinguishes two splits of B: function-based component sums and operator/factored (e.g., row/column) splits, affecting storage and sampling.
- Identifies weighted-norm smoothness constants induced by strong convexity–concavity (λ,γ) and introduces L̄(π) tied to sampling distributions.
- Shows non-uniform sampling tailored to structure (e.g., row/column norms for bilinear K) yields improved complexity bounds.
- Adapts catalyst/proximal-point acceleration to saddle points, yielding accelerated SVRG with better complexity than accelerated batch forward–backward.
- Argues proximal-point contraction suffices for acceleration without requiring Nesterov’s sequence.
- Situates work against variance reduction in minimization, batch/accelerated primal–dual methods, stochastic FB with sublinear rates, and coordinate/primal–dual methods.
- Theory: careful, clean monotone-operator/forward–backward analysis giving linear rates and well-motivated sampling treatment; concrete bounds for bilinear K using ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max.
- Assumptions: standard but restrictive strong convex–concave regime; requires known λ,γ,L (or bounds).
- Experiments: support theoretical hierarchy (non-uniform > uniform; accelerated > others) but are limited in scope and measure passes over data rather than wall-clock or memory.
- Clarity: generally well-organized with clear definitions and pseudocode; some notation/step-size consistency issues and brief treatment of sampling/resampling overhead.
- Suggest clarifying why FB step-size is 1/L2 under chosen norms and quantifying practical overhead of computing non-uniform probabilities and resampling.
- Originality: novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle points and sums of Lipschitz operators; new factored splits and tailored non-uniform sampling; notable proximal-point/catalyst acceleration adaptation.
- Significance: broadens applicability to non-separable losses/regularizers and variational inequalities, removing separability requirement from many incremental methods.
- Strength: first linear-convergence SAGA/SVRG analysis for non-separable saddle points and a minimal-assumption monotone-operator framework.
- Strength: careful sampling treatment with memory-efficient factored SAGA and concrete bilinear bounds.
- Strength: practical acceleration approach with provable gains and simple implementation; empirical validation consistent with theory.
- Weakness: limited to strong convex–concave cases; no analysis for merely convex–concave (µ=0) sublinear rates.
- Weakness: computing leverage-like non-uniform weights (KK^T diagonal, K^T K diagonal) may be expensive for large sparse K; need discussion of approximations.
- Weakness: theory requires SAGA resampling under non-uniform sampling but experiments mix schemes without resampling—need clarification and ablation.
- Weakness: experiments should include wall-clock time, memory usage, scaling with n,d, sensitivity to mini-batch and step-sizes, and stronger stochastic primal–dual baselines.
- Weakness: acceleration tuning (τ) depends on L̄ and oracle-time ratios that may be unknown—provide practical heuristics and robustness study.
- Questions: can the analysis extend to µ=0 convex–concave for sublinear rates (e.g., Mirror-Prox with variance reduction)?
- Questions: how to construct effective non-uniform sampling probabilities for general non-bilinear K without expensive precomputation?
- Questions: can the monotone-operator extension be demonstrated on a concrete non-saddle example (e.g., variational inequality) in experiments?
- Pros: strong, original theory with practical algorithms; clear benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration; broader applicability via monotone-operator view.
- Cons: limited experimental breadth and system metrics; reliance on strong convex–concave structure and known constants; practical overheads of non-uniform sampling/resampling not fully addressed.
- Recommendation: lean to accept—solid novel theoretical contribution with practical algorithms; requires expanded experiments and practical guidance on sampling/tuning.
