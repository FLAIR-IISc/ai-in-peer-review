- Extends SVRG and SAGA to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems by framing optimization as zeros of a monotone operator A+B and applying forward–backward variance-reduced schemes.
- Allows flexible decompositions B = ∑_i B_i that need not be gradients, enabling factored splits (e.g., row/column sampling for bilinear K) and non-uniform sampling minimizing a tailored smoothness L̄(π).
- Proves linear convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA under strong convex–concavity, with improved complexity over batch forward–backward, especially using non-uniform sampling and minibatching.
- Shows SAGA can be implemented with O(n+d) memory in factored bilinear cases and details step-size/epoch choices tied to L and L̄(π).
- Accelerates SVRG via a proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper, yielding improved complexity bounds and leveraging linear convergence of the proximal-point step.
- Extends analysis beyond saddle points to general maximal strongly monotone A and monotone B = ∑B_i where each B_i is Lipschitz (not necessarily monotone/co-coercive).
- Empirical results on limited datasets support theory: non-uniform sampling and acceleration help, and primal SAGA is faster only when separability allows it.
- Experiments are limited (two datasets), lack broader wall-clock timing and comparisons to mirror-prox/PDHG, weakening practical claims.
- Technical soundness: algorithms and proofs are coherent and appropriately use strong monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity; appendices contain proofs.
- Caveats: requires strong convex–concavity and knowledge of λ, γ, L, L̄, with potentially conservative constants.
- Practical overheads not fully quantified: computing/maintaining non-uniform sampling probabilities and per-epoch full B(x̃,ỹ) costs are under-discussed.
- Theoretical/practical mismatch: non-uniform SAGA needs resampling for theory, but experiments use a pragmatic mixture sampling without resampling.
- Clarity: paper is generally well written; normalization via Ω(·) is helpful, but some notation could be streamlined and a table of assumptions per theorem would help.
- Originality: first to give linear-rate variance-reduced methods for non-separable saddle-point problems without co-coercivity, and introduces insightful factored decompositions and tailored sampling.
- Significance (theory): provides a solid framework bringing variance reduction to saddle-point and monotone operator settings, likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Significance (practice): promising for non-separable losses/regularizers with actionable sampling guidance for bilinear problems, but needs broader benchmarks.
- Pros: novel linear-convergent VR methods for non-separable saddle points, practical non-uniform/factored splits, proximal-point acceleration, and extension to monotone inclusions.
- Cons: strong-convexity assumption and known constants required, thin experiments, theoretical sampling restrictions vs practical variants, and unquantified sampling/epoch overhead.
- Suggestions: add comparisons to stochastic mirror-prox/PDHG, report wall-clock timings and minibatch sensitivity, include more non-bilinear K examples, and discuss costs/adaptive strategies for unknown parameters.
- Verdict: Strong paper with solid theory and promising practical impact; recommend acceptance contingent on strengthening empirical section and clarifying practical parameter choices.
