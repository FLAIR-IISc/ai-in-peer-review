- Studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems splitting operator B as a sum of Lipschitz components and applies SVRG/SAGA variance reduction at the operator level.  
- Allows non-integrable (operator-level) splits and factored splits for bilinear problems enabling non-uniform sampling across rows/columns.  
- Provides linear convergence analyses for SVRG and SAGA with step sizes depending on Lipschitz constant L and sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π).  
- Proposes catalyst/proximal-point acceleration around a moving center with inner SVRG, yielding improved complexity bounds over accelerated batch methods.  
- Extends analysis to finding zeros of A+B where A is strongly monotone with known resolvent and B is a sum of Lipschitz monotone operators (no co-coercivity needed).  
- Conceptual novelty: operator-theoretic extension of SVRG/SAGA removing sum-of-functions requirement, relevant for non-separable losses/regularizers.  
- Technical contribution: careful linear convergence proofs for SVRG/SAGA under strong convexity–concavity including non-uniform sampling and mini-batches.  
- Acceleration contribution: catalyst-style wrapper for monotone operators that is simpler than Nesterov-based analyses and gives better bounds.  
- Practical guidance: derivation of L̄(π) bounds for element-wise vs factored splits and demonstration that non-uniform sampling tightens constants for bilinear problems.  
- Experiments show SAGA and accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperform batch primal–dual on non-separable problems and that acceleration helps ill-conditioned settings.  
- Limitation: linear rates require knowledge of strong convexity/concavity and L, L̄(π), with no adaptive step-size or backtracking strategy provided.  
- Empirical sensitivity to mis-specified constants is not studied or discussed.  
- Baseline comparisons are limited; missing comparisons to extragradient/mirror-prox variants and additional stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods.  
- SAGA theory requires resampling of stored operator values for non-uniform sampling, but experiments omit this; empirical impact and memory costs are not quantified.  
- Presentation is dense; some definitions (e.g., Ω, Ω*, effect of λ, γ on conditioning) and relations to extragradient/PDHG could be clearer.  
- Experiments are narrow (two datasets) and relegate non-separable proximal operators to the appendix; broader tasks and ablations are lacking.  
- Overall technical quality is strong with correct monotonicity-based analysis and careful constants.  
- Manuscript clarity is good but compact; additional intuition, running examples, and implementation pseudocode would aid reproducibility.  
- Originality is high: first to extend variance reduction to saddle points with non-integrable operator splits and to apply catalyst-style acceleration in this setting.  
- Significance is high: broad applicability to ML problems with non-separable structure and immediate practical sampling guidance.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel generalization of SVRG/SAGA, solid linear theory, practical acceleration, and empirical gains on non-separable problems.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical evaluation, dependence on known constants without adaptive guidance, and missing implementation/clarity details.  
- Suggested improvements: add comparisons to extragradient/mirror-prox/PDHG, provide an adaptive step-size heuristic and robustness study, include ablations on sampling and mini-batch size, and quantify memory/time trade-offs for factored vs individual splits.
