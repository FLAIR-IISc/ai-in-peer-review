This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods for solving saddle-point problems, extending existing algorithms like SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The authors provide convergence analysis, highlighting differences from the convex minimization case, and explore different splitting strategies. They also propose an acceleration technique inspired by the catalyst framework. The paper addresses a relevant problem in machine learning where saddle-point formulations arise naturally, particularly when dealing with non-separable losses or regularizers.  Existing work on stochastic optimization has primarily focused on convex minimization, leaving a gap in efficient algorithms for saddle-point problems. Recent related work includes extensions of proximal algorithms (e.g., [22, 30]) but often under stronger assumptions than those considered here.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, building upon the monotone operator framework and demonstrating linear convergence.  The connection to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a novel contribution, particularly with the tailored convergence analysis and investigation of splitting strategies.  The acceleration technique also adds to the novelty.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the assumptions are explicitly stated.  The examples in Section 2.1 effectively illustrate the relevance of the problem.
* **Significance:** The proposed algorithms address a practically important problem, especially in the context of machine learning models with complex regularizers or non-separable losses.  The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the methods, particularly in ill-conditioned settings. The theoretical analysis, extending beyond saddle-point problems to monotone operators, is a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Detail:** While the experimental results are promising, the paper lacks comprehensive details about the experimental setup. The descriptions of the datasets and parameter tuning are limited. It would be helpful to see a comparison with existing saddle-point solvers, even if they are batch methods.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The complexity analysis, while present, is quite involved and relies on several parameters. A more streamlined presentation of the key complexity results would improve readability. The relationship between the various norms used (e.g., ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) and their impact on performance isn’t always clearly articulated.
* **Non-Uniform Sampling Implementation:** The discussion of resampling in SAGA with non-uniform sampling is somewhat unclear. The practical implications of choosing to resample or not are not fully explored.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of strong convexity-concavity (A) might be restrictive in some applications.  The authors acknowledge the need for adaptive methods to estimate these parameters in future work, but this limitation should be explicitly mentioned as a direction for improvement.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to solving a practically relevant problem.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and extends the applicability of the algorithms beyond saddle-point problems.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact research and applications in machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The experimental section could be more comprehensive and provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods.
* The complexity analysis is somewhat cumbersome and could benefit from simplification.
* The assumptions made about strong convexity-concavity may limit the applicability of the methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The authors have successfully extended existing variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems and provided a solid theoretical foundation for their work. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh the concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the comments regarding experimental details and complexity analysis. Specifically, the authors should provide more comprehensive experimental results, simplify the presentation of the complexity analysis, and clarify the implementation details of non-uniform sampling in SAGA.
