This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) adapted to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating performance on several machine learning tasks. The core idea of extending these algorithms to non-strongly convex settings, and the analysis framed around monotone operators, are potentially valuable. However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling novelty, overstatement of contributions, and insufficient experimental validation compared to established baselines.

The authors correctly identify the applicability of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point formulations arising from duality in convex optimization. However, the extension itself is largely straightforward, as acknowledged (“the algorithmic extension is straightforward”). The primary contribution, therefore, lies in the convergence analysis. While framing the problem through monotone operators is interesting, it doesn't fundamentally alter the established convergence properties of SVRG/SAGA; rather, it shows they *extend* to this setting. The claim of going “beyond saddle-point problems” to variational inequalities feels like a consequence of the broader operator analysis, not a new algorithmic contribution.

Several points detract from the paper’s strength. The extensive discussion of splits in B(x, y) – function-wise vs. partial derivative-wise, and “factored” vs. non-factored – feels largely theoretical and doesn’t translate into substantial practical gains demonstrated in the experiments. The emphasis on non-uniform sampling being “key” is supported, but feels incremental, building on known benefits of variance reduction techniques. The accelerated algorithm, while potentially useful, lacks a convincing demonstration of superior performance over simpler, well-tuned accelerated batch methods across all tested scenarios. The theoretical benefits are discussed but often contingent on specific conditions (e.g., the relationship between L̄ and T(B)) that are not consistently met in the experiments.

The experimental section is a significant weakness. While the authors compare against several algorithms, the parameter tuning details are sparse. The comparison with primal SAGA (in the separable case) is valuable, but the experiments lack a systematic evaluation against optimized implementations of accelerated forward-backward methods (specifically, tailored for saddle-point problems, not just general convex optimization). The presentation of results focuses primarily on distance to the optimizer, which is less intuitive than reporting actual objective function values or, crucially, testing performance. The discussion surrounding the impact of regularization strength (λ/λ0 = 1 vs. 0.1) is also a missed opportunity for a more nuanced analysis. The fact that uniform sampling does poorly suggests the importance of the setup, and this is not well-investigated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Extends established variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems.
*   Provides a convergence analysis, potentially broadening the applicability of SVRG/SAGA.
*   Identifies non-uniform sampling as crucial for efficient performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithmic extension is relatively straightforward.
*   The theoretical contributions are incremental and build heavily on existing work.
*   The experimental validation is insufficient; lacks robust comparisons against optimized baselines and clear performance metrics.
*   Overstates the novelty and significance of the results.
*   The extensive discussion of splits does not translate into practical benefit.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a solid, but not exceptional, contribution. It presents a reasonable extension of existing algorithms to a relevant problem setting, but lacks the novelty and rigorous validation required for a top-tier NIPS publication. The authors would significantly strengthen their case by (1) providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to highly optimized baselines with thorough parameter tuning, (2) clarifying the practical benefits of the different splitting strategies, and (3) moderating the claims of novelty and significance. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. A revised version addressing these concerns might warrant reconsideration.
