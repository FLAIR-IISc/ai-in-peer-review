

# Peer Review: Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

## Summary

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, a class of problems that arise naturally in machine learning through duality. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, demonstrating linear convergence under appropriate assumptions. They identify key differences between convex minimization and saddle-point problems in terms of convergence analysis, highlighting the importance of non-uniform sampling and the distinction between two different notions of splits (in terms of functions vs. partial derivatives). The paper also extends these methods to monotone operators and proposes an accelerated version using the "catalyst" framework.

The work is well-motivated by the prevalence of saddle-point problems in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, non-separable regularizers, structured output prediction) where standard stochastic optimization techniques for separable convex problems do not apply. The authors provide detailed theoretical analysis, with proofs in the appendices, and demonstrate through experiments that their methods outperform existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting. The authors correctly recognize that the analysis differs substantially from convex minimization, using the interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators rather than relying on convexity properties. This theoretical insight is well-executed and rigorously proven.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The paper convincingly demonstrates the practical importance of their approach through well-chosen examples (non-separable losses and regularizers) where existing methods fail. The experiments on real-world datasets show clear advantages of their methods over existing approaches, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The authors thoroughly analyze different sampling schemes (uniform vs. non-uniform) and demonstrate their theoretical and practical superiority. The extension to factored splits (where the matrix K is split into rows/columns rather than individual elements) is particularly insightful and leads to significant computational advantages.

4. **Connection to Monotone Operators**: The extension to monotone operators is a valuable theoretical contribution that broadens the applicability of their methods beyond saddle-point problems, including to variational inequality problems. This generalization is handled with care and demonstrates a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematics.

5. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The experiments are well-designed and directly support the theoretical claims, with clear presentation of results showing the advantages of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Strong Assumptions**: The paper relies on strong convexity-concavity assumptions (assumption A), which are restrictive for many practical problems. While the authors mention future work on adaptivity, this limitation should be more thoroughly discussed in the current paper. The connection to problems where these assumptions don't hold (e.g., weakly convex-concave saddle-point problems) could be explored.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope**: While the experiments are well-chosen, they focus primarily on binary classification with specific non-separable losses. More diverse examples (e.g., matrix factorization, robust optimization with different uncertainty sets) would strengthen the practical claim. The comparison with other stochastic saddle-point methods (e.g., those in [11, 12]) could be more comprehensive.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that the proximal operators for the non-separable regularizers can be computed efficiently (e.g., O(n log n) via isotonic regression), but could provide more concrete implementation details that would help practitioners.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references some related work on saddle-point problems, but could more thoroughly compare with recent advances in stochastic methods for saddle-point problems, such as those in [22, 30]. The claim that their methods are "the only ones that can be applied" to non-separable problems might be overstated.

5. **Complexity Analysis**: The complexity analysis for the factored splits could be more detailed, particularly for the relationship between the matrix dimensions and the computational complexity.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is timely and addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the practical experiments support the theoretical claims. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for the advantages of their approach.

While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, it is a common starting point in the literature for such analyses. The authors correctly identify this as a limitation and plan to address it in future work. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field, advancing the state of the art in optimization for saddle-point problems.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems with rigorous theoretical analysis and compelling experimental validation. It is well-written, addresses an important gap in the literature, and has clear practical implications. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Key Arguments for Acceptance

- First convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point problems
- Novel insights into different split structures and importance of non-uniform sampling
- Theoretical extension to monotone operators broadens applicability
- Clear experimental validation showing superiority over existing methods
- Well-motivated by practical machine learning problems with non-separable components

## Key Arguments for Improvement

- More thorough discussion of the strong convexity-concavity assumption
- Broader experimental validation across different problem types
- More detailed comparison with recent stochastic saddle-point methods

The paper is a valuable contribution to the field and would be of interest to a wide audience at NIPS.
