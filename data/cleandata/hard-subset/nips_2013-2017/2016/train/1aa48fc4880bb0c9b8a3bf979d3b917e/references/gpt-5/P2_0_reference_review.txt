Summary
The paper proposes stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG and SAGA) for solving composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly convex–concave. The core ideas are:
- Extend SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point and, more generally, monotone operator setting by viewing the problem as finding a zero of A+B with A strongly monotone (prox available) and B a sum of Lipschitz operators (no cocoercivity assumed).
- Provide linear convergence analyses under strong convexity–concavity using only monotonicity (not cocoercivity), with step sizes depending on an L constant and a sampling-dependent smoothness L̄.
- Exploit two types of decompositions of B: “individual” (sum over entries) and “factored” (row/column), and show the importance of non-uniform sampling for tighter L̄ and faster convergence.
- Use a simple “catalyst”-style proximal-point outer loop to accelerate SVRG in this setting, yielding improved complexities that dominate batch accelerated methods.
- Provide complexity bounds specialized to bilinear problems, expressed via ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max, and discuss storage/time trade-offs.
- Demonstrate empirical gains on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers, showing that non-uniform SAGA/SVRG (and especially accelerated SVRG) outperform batch and stochastic FB baselines; also contrast with primal methods when separability holds.

Relation to prior work is thorough: SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite sums; primal–dual/FB methods including Chambolle–Pock; stochastic FB (O(1/t)); separable stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods; and monotone operator literature. The paper argues novelty by handling non-separable saddle-point problems with variance reduction and by analyzing beyond cocoercive settings.

Quality
- Theory: The linear convergence results for SVRG/SAGA under strong convexity–concavity are well motivated. The use of the monotone operator lens is clean and helps decouple the analysis from saddle-point specifics. The role of L versus L̄(π) is clearly articulated, and the non-uniform sampling bounds are plausible and practically important. The catalyst-style acceleration is justified by proximal-point contraction (1+τ)−1 and leads to improved complexity when the cost ratio favors it. Assumptions (prox of M; Lipschitz gradient of K; strong convex-concavity) are standard and clearly stated. One potential notational gap: Theorem 2 uses μ in denominators; it would help to define μ explicitly (e.g., the strong monotonicity parameter tied to λ,γ) as it currently appears without definition.
- Algorithms: The pseudocode is clear, with minibatching, storage distinctions for factored vs individual splits, and practical notes (e.g., resampling for non-uniform SAGA). The computational cost model T(B), T(Bi), Tprox(M) supports the complexity claims.
- Experiments: The experiments support the main claims (benefit of variance reduction and non-uniform sampling; advantage of acceleration on ill-conditioned problems). However, the empirical section is relatively limited (two datasets) and omits some competitive baselines common for saddle-point/VI problems (e.g., extragradient/mirror-prox variants, accelerated primal–dual methods beyond the presented FB acceleration). Hyperparameter selection (using λ,γ,L,L̄) and sensitivity are not discussed in depth; the authors acknowledge adaptivity as future work. The practice/theory gap for SAGA with non-uniform sampling (resampling not used) is noted but could be elaborated.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The weighted norm Ω and its dual are consistently used to normalize constants. The two splitting schemes and sampling distributions are explained clearly, with helpful norm inequalities to compare methods. Suggestions:
- Define μ where it first appears in rates and ensure consistent notation between λ,γ,μ across sections.
- Briefly contrast with extragradient/mirror-prox (Nemirovski) to position the chosen FB-based updates and clarify why variance reduction is developed on FB rather than extragradient.
- Summarize key assumptions and resulting convergence guarantees in a compact table (assumptions, step-size, rate), to aid reproducibility.

Originality
The extension of variance-reduced incremental methods to general convex–concave saddle-point problems with linear convergence guarantees, without relying on cocoercivity of the component operators, is novel and valuable. The “factored” decomposition and non-uniform sampling analysis tailored to the saddle-point structure are insightful. The catalyst-style acceleration adapted to the proximal-point perspective in this setting is a nice, simple twist. The monotone operator extension broadens the scope beyond saddle points.

Significance
The work addresses an important gap: fast incremental methods for non-separable saddle-point problems, which arise in robust optimization, structured prediction losses, and non-separable regularization. The theoretical rates and complexity bounds indicate tangible gains over batch methods; experiments corroborate the benefit. The monotone operator framing and acceleration may influence subsequent algorithm design beyond the immediate applications.

Pros
- Clear, principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points with linear convergence.
- Monotone operator analysis that avoids cocoercivity; broad applicability.
- Careful treatment of non-uniform sampling and problem-structure-aware splits.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst.
- Practical relevance to non-separable losses/regularizers; empirical support.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited in breadth and baselines; missing comparisons to extragradient/mirror-prox variants.
- Dependence on known λ,γ,L,L̄; no adaptive step-size scheme; limited discussion of sensitivity.
- Memory burden for individual splits (O(nd)) can be high; more practical guidance on when factored vs individual is preferable would help.
- Minor notation clarity issues (μ), and some theory–practice gaps (SAGA resampling).

Minor comments
- Add a citation and brief discussion of Mirror-Prox/extragradient methods and how batch accelerated primal–dual methods compare in the strongly convex–concave regime.
- Clarify the definition of μ in Theorem 2 and ensure consistent symbols for strong convexity–concavity parameters.
- Provide more details on how L̄ is estimated in practice and how non-uniform probabilities are computed efficiently for large K.

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is technically solid, original, and significant. It provides the first rigorous and practically relevant variance-reduced methods for general saddle-point problems, with a clean analysis, sensible algorithmic design choices, and demonstrated gains. With minor clarifications and broader empirical comparisons, it will be a valuable contribution to NeurIPS.
