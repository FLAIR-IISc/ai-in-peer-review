### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the popular variance reduction techniques, SAGA and SVRG, from the standard setting of finite-sum convex minimization to the more general class of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are prevalent in machine learning, arising from duality, robust optimization, and structured prediction tasks, especially when losses or regularizers are non-separable, precluding the direct application of standard stochastic methods on the primal or dual alone.

The main contributions are:
1.  The authors propose SVRG-SP and SAGA-SP, direct adaptations of SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point optimization.
2.  They provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point setting. A key feature of the analysis is that it relies only on the monotonicity of the associated gradient operator, rather than the stronger co-coercivity property used in many analyses for convex minimization. This makes the proof technique more general and applicable to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities.
3.  The paper highlights unique structural aspects of saddle-point problems, such as the possibility of "factored" gradient splits and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling, which is shown to yield superior theoretical bounds and empirical performance.
4.  An accelerated version of SVRG-SP is developed using the Catalyst framework, achieving a faster convergence rate that can provably outperform accelerated batch methods.

The work is situated in the context of foundational variance-reduction methods (SAGA, SVRG) and classical saddle-point algorithms (Forward-Backward, Accelerated Forward-Backward). It significantly advances the state of the art by providing the first linearly-convergent *stochastic* methods for this general problem class.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound and represents a significant contribution. Deriving linear convergence rates for SVRG/SAGA in this setting is non-trivial, and the choice to use a more general monotone operator framework is elegant and powerful. This approach correctly identifies that co-coercivity is not required, thus broadening the applicability of the analysis beyond saddle-point problems. The claims are well-supported by theoretical derivations, and the derived complexity bounds in Table 1 clearly demonstrate the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods over existing batch and standard stochastic algorithms. The experiments, though limited in scope, successfully validate the main theoretical claims, such as the superiority of non-uniform sampling and the effectiveness of the acceleration scheme.

#### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the problem setting and assumptions are formally defined. The authors do an excellent job of connecting their work to the relevant literature in both variance reduction and saddle-point optimization. The algorithms are presented clearly. The inclusion of Table 1 is particularly helpful, as it provides a concise and comprehensive comparison of the complexities of all considered algorithms, making the paper's contribution easy to appreciate. The distinction between different types of splits ("individual" vs. "factored") is a nice, subtle point that is well explained.

#### Originality

This work is highly original. While the proposed algorithms are direct analogues of SVRG and SAGA, their application, analysis, and the surrounding insights for saddle-point problems are novel. To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish linear convergence for variance-reduced stochastic methods on general, non-separable, strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The use of a monotone operator-based analysis, which circumvents the need for co-coercivity, is a key and original theoretical contribution that distinguishes this work from previous analyses of SVRG/SAGA and related methods (e.g., [30]) which were restricted to the convex minimization setting. The introduction of factored splits and the rigorous analysis of non-uniform sampling in this context are also novel contributions.

#### Significance

The significance of this paper is high. Saddle-point formulations are a natural and powerful tool for many machine learning problems, including AUC maximization, robust learning, and models with complex, non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso). By developing efficient, linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this class, the paper makes a large set of models more tractable at scale. Researchers and practitioners working on these problems are likely to use and build upon these ideas. The theoretical framework, based on monotone operators, is also of independent interest and could inspire further work on extending other optimization techniques beyond the standard convex setting.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

Despite the paper's strengths, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the paper's claims on a specific non-separable problem. However, the introduction motivates the method with a wide range of applications (robust optimization, matrix factorization, etc.). The empirical validation would be more compelling if it included an experiment from one of these other domains to showcase the versatility of the proposed algorithms.

2.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The theoretical rates and algorithm step-sizes depend on constants like the Lipschitz constants (L, L_i) and strong-convexity parameters (λ, γ). In practice, these are often difficult to compute or estimate. A brief discussion on the practical selection of these parameters or the robustness of the methods to their mis-specification would be valuable. For example, were the theoretical step-sizes used directly in the experiments, or were they tuned?

3.  **SAGA with Non-uniform Sampling:** The description of the SAGA update (Algorithm 2) and the subsequent discussion on non-uniform sampling could be slightly clearer. The text states that for the bound to hold, one must resample indices for the gradient update step, but the experiments use a heuristic mixture of sampling schemes without resampling. It would be helpful to explicitly state the theoretically-backed version of the algorithm and then discuss the practical variant used in experiments, perhaps with a brief justification for why the heuristic is expected to work well.

4.  **Clarity on "Factored" Splits:** The paper introduces the interesting idea of "factored" splits. The main benefit mentioned is reduced storage for SAGA (O(n+d) instead of O(nd)). It would be beneficial to elaborate if this type of split offers any other advantages, for instance, in terms of the smoothness constants (L̄) or computational cost per iteration.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
*   First work to provide linearly-convergent stochastic algorithms (SVRG/SAGA) for general strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems.
*   Novel and general proof technique based on monotone operator theory, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   Thorough analysis, including acceleration and a detailed study of non-uniform sampling, which is shown to be crucial for performance.
*   High potential impact, enabling efficient optimization for a broad class of ML problems with non-separable objectives.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Cons:**
*   The algorithmic novelty is modest; the main contribution is in the analysis and application domain.
*   The experimental evaluation, while supportive, is limited to a single type of problem.
*   Practical guidance on setting key hyperparameters is limited.

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the optimization toolbox for machine learning. It elegantly bridges the gap between variance reduction methods and saddle-point optimization, backed by a strong and general theoretical analysis. The work is original, significant, and of high quality. I strongly recommend acceptance.
