### **Summary**

This paper proposes an extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG) and online variance reduction (SAGA) algorithms, originally developed for convex minimization, to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating linear convergence for these methods on strongly-convex-concave objectives. A key aspect of their analysis is its foundation in the theory of monotone operators, which allows the results to generalize beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The paper also investigates the importance of problem structure, such as "factored" splits and non-uniform sampling, and presents an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework. The work positions itself as the first to provide linearly convergent incremental methods for this problem class, improving upon existing batch or standard stochastic algorithms.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Application of Variance Reduction:** The paper tackles an important and challenging problem: efficiently solving large-scale saddle-point problems. Extending the powerful variance reduction paradigm of SVRG and SAGA to this setting is a natural and valuable contribution, particularly for machine learning applications involving non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization) where existing primal or dual methods are not applicable.

2.  **General Theoretical Framework:** The analysis is grounded in the theory of monotone operators. This is an elegant approach that not only provides a unified proof for the saddle-point setting but also demonstrates the broader applicability of these variance reduction techniques to other problems like variational inequalities.

3.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper correctly identifies and analyzes the practical importance of non-uniform sampling schemes. The theoretical bounds and experimental results convincingly show that non-uniform sampling can lead to significant performance gains, a crucial insight for practitioners.

4.  **Acceleration:** The inclusion of an accelerated variant using a Catalyst-like framework demonstrates a path to achieving optimal rates, further strengthening the theoretical contribution and practical potential of the proposed methods.

### **Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction of this work, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to potentially significant technical inaccuracies. In my view, the density of these issues severely undermines the quality and reliability of the paper.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a general lack of precision and careful presentation.
    *   **Typos and Inconsistent Notation:** There are numerous distracting errors. For instance, the strong convexity-concavity definition in Assumption (A) should likely use `λ/2` and `γ/2`, not `λ2` and `γ2`. Equation (3) uses non-standard notation like `K>K` instead of `K^T K` and `6` instead of `≤`. The footer on the first page contains a formatting error ("th Conference"). Citations are not styled consistently (e.g., "SAG [1]" vs. "framework of [7]").
    *   **Unclear Definitions:** The definition of the proximal operator in Eq. (2) is highly non-standard. It combines the step-size parameter `σ` with the strong-convexity constants `λ` and `γ` in a manner that is not explained or justified, making it difficult to relate to standard proximal algorithms. The description of the "factored" split in Section 2 is convoluted, with indices that appear to be mismatched, rendering the concept difficult to parse.
    *   **Poorly Presented Algorithms:** The algorithms are not specified with sufficient care. In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), the input is listed as `(Ki)i`, which contradicts the text's claim that function `Ki` need not exist. More critically, the core update step for the average gradient `G` is placed within an "(optional)" block, which is incorrect as this step is fundamental to SAGA's mechanism.

2.  **Potential Technical Issues:**
    *   **SAGA Convergence Rate:** Theorem 2 presents a convergence rate for SAGA that depends on `max{3|I|^2/m, ...}`. The quadratic dependency on the number of components, `|I|^2`, is highly suspect. Standard SAGA analyses for convex minimization exhibit a linear dependency on `|I|`. If this `|I|^2` term is not a typo, it would imply that the algorithm's performance degrades drastically as the sum is split into more components, defeating the purpose of the method. This requires immediate and thorough clarification, as it could represent a major flaw in the analysis.
    *   **Description of Acceleration:** The description of the acceleration method in Section 5 appears to be a misrepresentation of the Catalyst framework [7]. The authors claim their "analysis is simpler, without the need for Nesterov acceleration machinery". However, Catalyst's acceleration is fundamentally achieved by applying a Nesterov-like extrapolation to the sequence of solutions generated by the inner solver. The paper seems to only describe the outer loop as the proximal point algorithm, omitting the crucial momentum step. This gives a misleading impression of the mechanism and its novelty.

3.  **Evaluation and Scope:**
    *   The experimental section is difficult to fully interpret. Acronyms for baseline methods like `fb-acc` and `fba-primal` are not defined. The plots are small and hard to read.
    *   The results on the `rcv1` dataset (right plot, Figure 7) show that `saga-primal` is significantly faster than the proposed saddle-point methods. While the authors correctly note that their method applies to non-separable cases where `saga-primal` does not, this result significantly narrows the practical impact of the work. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit acknowledgment that for a broad class of standard separable problems, existing primal methods remain superior.

### **Recommendation**

**Reject.**

While the paper addresses an interesting and important problem, the execution is unfortunately not at the level required for publication at NIPS. The sheer volume of notational errors, unclear definitions, and confusing presentation details makes the paper difficult to read and trust. More importantly, the potential technical errors—specifically the `|I|^2` dependency in the SAGA rate and the misleading description of the acceleration framework—are critical concerns that question the soundness of the core theoretical contributions.

The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to correct the numerous flaws, clarify the technical exposition, and provide a more careful and transparent evaluation. In its current form, it does not meet the standards of quality and clarity expected of a NIPS paper.
