Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with a tractable proximal operator and K is smooth. The key ideas are:
- View the problem as finding a zero of a sum of monotone operators A+B, with A the (set-valued) subdifferential/prox of M and B the smooth monotone operator induced by K. This allows forward–backward splitting with variance-reduced estimators of B.
- Allow general “splits” B=∑_i B_i where each B_i is Lipschitz, not necessarily a gradient of a function. This includes factored splits tailored to bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x (e.g., row/column sketches).
- Develop non-uniform sampling schemes with a tailored smoothness constant L̄(π) and show linear convergence for both SVRG and SAGA; provide minibatching results and storage-efficient factored versions; and show an acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst scheme that improves iteration complexity and is simpler than Nesterov-style acceleration in this setting.
- Extend the analysis beyond saddle-points to general monotone inclusions, requiring only monotonicity of the sum operator (no cocoercivity of each B_i).
- Experiments on two datasets (non-separable loss/regularizer and a separable benchmark) show that SAGA/SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperform batch forward–backward and its acceleration, with stronger gains on ill-conditioned problems; accelerated SVRG is best.

Relation to prior work is well covered: SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization; primal-dual and coordinate methods that rely on separability; stochastic forward–backward with O(1/t); and SMART-type variance-reduced operator methods that require cocoercivity. The paper argues convincingly that variance reduction was missing for non-separable saddle-point problems with linear rates.

Quality
- Theory: The variance-reduction constructions for SVRG/SAGA are natural generalizations to the monotone operator setting and the proofs (in appendices) rely only on monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity of the forward operator and strong monotonicity from M via the weighted Ω-norm. The convergence results are linear with explicit dependencies on L and L̄(π) and minibatch size m. The acceleration via adding a quadratic regularizer and using one inexact proximal-point step per outer iteration is clean and leverages the linear convergence of the proximal point method.
- Correctness and assumptions: Assumptions (A–C) are standard for strongly convex–concave composite problems. The allowance that B_i need not be gradients is a strength and important for factored/row–column updates. The need to know or bound L and L̄(π) is explicit. A few notational issues should be fixed (see clarity).
- Sampling and complexity: The definition of L̄(π) and the derived non-uniform distributions (proportional to row/column norm squares in bilinear case) match intuition and yield strictly better bounds than uniform. The complexity discussion distinguishes oracle times T(B), T(B_i) and prox costs. The factored SAGA storage reduction to O(n+d) is practically important.
- Experiments: While limited in scope (two datasets), they target the non-separable regime where existing separability-based methods cannot be used. Results corroborate the theory: non-uniform sampling matters; accelerated SVRG is most robust on ill-conditioned instances.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well written and self-contained. Suggestions:
- Clarify the appearance of µ in Theorem 2 (it seems to denote the strong monotonicity parameter in the Ω-norm; elsewhere λ,γ induce µ=1 after scaling). Ensure consistent notation and correct factors (e.g., “3|I|/2m” vs “3|I|2m”).
- The forward–backward baseline: clearly state when FB converges (your σ=1/L^2 condition) vs extragradient/mirror-prox that are guaranteed under Lipschitzness. This helps readers reconcile with the statement that FB “is known not to converge in general.”
- Explicitly discuss how to estimate L and L̄(π) in practice, and whether backtracking/adaptive steps are possible.

Originality
- First linear convergence guarantees for variance-reduced stochastic methods on general (non-separable) saddle-point problems with only monotonicity assumptions on the sum operator.
- The factored split and storage-efficient SAGA variant for bilinear K are practically novel.
- The catalyst-style acceleration recast via proximal-point is a clean and, in this context, simpler adaptation than in convex minimization.
- The extension to general monotone inclusions without cocoercivity assumptions differentiates from SMART and other related operator-splitting VR works.

Significance
- Saddle-point formulations that are not separable are common (non-separable regularizers; ranking/AUC losses; robust optimization), and existing linearly convergent incremental methods typically do not apply. The proposed methods fill an important gap and are likely to be adopted.
- The non-uniform sampling guidance and factored updates are impactful for large bilinear problems.
- The monotone operator viewpoint broadens applicability beyond ML.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicalities of L/L̄: For non-bilinear K, computing L and L̄(π) may be nontrivial; guidance on safe upper bounds or adaptive tuning would improve usability.
- Baselines: A comparison to extragradient/mirror-prox (and their accelerated variants) would strengthen the empirical section, even if those are O(1/t), to quantify gains. Also, some primal-dual proximal splitting methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock with strong convexity) can be competitive; a brief discussion would help.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires resampling of the memory table for the theory; the experiments avoid this using a mixture strategy. Please either provide a theoretical justification for the mixed scheme or report the impact of strict resampling in practice.
- Scope of experiments: Only two datasets, and both with bilinear K. Demonstrating one non-bilinear smooth K (e.g., generalized linear saddle problems) or a simple variational inequality would underline generality.
- Minor: The FB step size of 1/L^2 is unusual; a short intuition in the main text would help. Discuss when accelerated batch may still dominate if non-uniform sampling cannot be exploited.

Pros
- Strong and original theory: linear convergence for VR methods on non-separable saddle points and monotone inclusions.
- Careful sampling analysis; factored storage for SAGA.
- Simple and effective catalyst-like acceleration specialized to saddle points.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory; practical insights on sampling.

Cons
- Limited experimental breadth and missing comparisons to extragradient-type baselines.
- Some notational/clarity issues and practical guidance (L, L̄ estimation) are missing.
- SAGA non-uniform resampling gap between theory and practice.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems in a meaningful way. With minor clarifications and expanded discussion/experiments on baselines and parameter selection, it would make a strong NeurIPS paper. I recommend acceptance.
