Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is simple via a proximal operator, possibly non-smooth, and strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave. The authors extend stochastic variance reduction methods—SVRG and SAGA—to this saddle-point and, more generally, monotone operator setting. Key ideas:
- Reformulate saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a (strongly) monotone operator A+B, where A is the subdifferential of M and B is the gradient operator of K. This enables a forward-backward (FB) framework without relying on co-coercivity, unlike standard convex minimization analyses.
- Provide linear convergence guarantees for SVRG and SAGA with mini-batching and non-uniform sampling, under strong convex-concavity. They introduce two splitting schemes for B: (i) sums of component operators Bi (not necessarily gradients of individual functions), and (ii) a “factored” split aligned with x/y blocks, useful for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x.
- Analyze sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform) and smoothness constants L and L̄(π) tailored to the split; show that non-uniform sampling improves rates and complexity bounds.
- Propose an acceleration via a proximal regularization (“catalyst” style) that yields improved overall complexity without requiring Nesterov’s machinery; provide convergence guarantees for the accelerated SVRG variant.
- Extend the analysis to general monotone inclusions (A+B) with A maximal strongly monotone and B monotone Lipschitz, subsuming saddle-point problems and touching on variational inequalities.
- Empirically compare batch FB and its acceleration, stochastic FB, SAGA, SVRG, and accelerated SVRG on binary classification tasks with non-separable losses/regularizers, and on a separable setup to contrast with primal SAGA.

Relative to prior work: The paper builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA (Le Roux et al.; Johnson & Zhang; Defazio et al.) and primal-dual/mirror-prox/FB methods (Chambolle-Pock; Rockafellar; Chen & Rockafellar). It differs from SPDC (Zhang & Xiao) and related coordinate primal-dual methods by addressing non-separable K and M and by providing variance-reduced FB schemes on the operator level. It also connects to monotone operator splitting beyond cocoercive settings (Raguet et al.; Bauschke & Combettes) and adopts catalyst-style acceleration (Lin et al.).

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The analysis is careful and leverages strong monotonicity with forward-backward splitting to establish linear rates for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting. The treatment of non-uniform sampling and mini-batches is explicit via L̄(π). The acceleration argument via proximal-point regularization is well-motivated and yields a clean complexity trade-off in τ. The extension to general monotone operators is conceptually solid and broadens applicability.
- Assumptions and step sizes: Convergence requires known strong convex-concavity parameters (λ,γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄), and step sizes depend on these. While standard, computing or tightly estimating L̄(π) in practice may be non-trivial, and the paper acknowledges substituting L̄ for L^2 when needed, at the cost of weaker bounds.
- Completeness: Proofs and algorithmic details are deferred to appendices but appear complete from the main text. The bilinear case is analyzed in depth, including complexity bounds under several sampling schemes.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments demonstrate (i) value of non-uniform sampling, (ii) benefits of variance reduction over batch and stochastic FB, and (iii) further gains with acceleration on ill-conditioned problems. However, comparisons omit some relevant baselines (e.g., SPDC or stochastic extragradient variants), and runtime vs. iteration comparisons are not fully disentangled from proximal/sampling overheads.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with assumptions and algorithms stated clearly and pseudocode provided. The use of weighted norms Ω and dual Ω* to balance λ and γ is appropriate but could benefit from a brief intuition and a small guide to parameter scaling in practice.
- The notion of “factored” vs. “individual” splits is useful; highlighting when each is preferable and summarizing storage/time trade-offs in the main text (rather than only appendices) would improve readability.
- Sampling schemes and L̄(π) bounds are key; a concise table in the main text comparing the constants and their practical sampling costs for bilinear K would help practitioners.

Originality
- Extending SVRG/SAGA with a unified monotone operator-based analysis to convex-concave saddle-point problems (with non-separable components) is novel and non-trivial. Allowing Bi that are not gradients of component functions and showing linear convergence without cocoercivity are significant theoretical advances.
- The catalyst-style acceleration tailored to the operator view is a useful and relatively simple addition, with an analysis that avoids extra acceleration constructs.

Significance
- Saddle-point formulations and variational inequalities are central in ML and optimization (e.g., robust optimization, structured losses, non-separable regularizers). Providing linearly convergent, variance-reduced methods that exploit finite-sum structure is impactful.
- The complexity improvements under non-uniform sampling and factored splits are practically relevant for large bilinear problems (e.g., matrix-data settings).
- The extension to monotone inclusions suggests broader adoption beyond ML.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and general theoretical contributions: first linear convergence analysis of SVRG/SAGA for saddle points in a monotone operator framework without cocoercivity.
- Practical algorithmic insights: non-uniform sampling, factored splits, mini-batching, and catalyst acceleration with concrete complexity improvements.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory, showing substantive gains over batch and stochastic FB, and further gains with acceleration.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental scope is limited: only two datasets; lacks comparisons to strong baselines such as SPDC or stochastic extragradient/mirror-prox variants; no wall-clock analyses of non-uniform sampling and storage overhead.
- Practical guidance on estimating L and L̄(π) and choosing τ, m, and sampling distributions could be expanded; some step-size rules depend on constants that may be hard to obtain.
- Acceleration is only analyzed for SVRG; SAGA acceleration is left as a conjecture.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to SPDC and stochastic extragradient/mirror-prox methods, especially on ill-conditioned problems, and report wall-clock times accounting for proximal and sampling overhead.
- Provide practical procedures to estimate or upper bound L and L̄(π) and to set τ and mini-batch size m adaptively.
- Summarize storage/time trade-offs of individual vs. factored splits in the main text; discuss the cost of implementing non-uniform sampling (e.g., alias tables) and maintaining required statistics.
- Consider citing classic extragradient/mirror-prox work (e.g., Nemirovski’s Mirror-Prox, stochastic extragradient) to situate the FB approach among alternative saddle-point solvers.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, clear, original, and likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners dealing with non-separable saddle-point problems. The main limitations are empirical coverage and practical guidance on parameter selection.
