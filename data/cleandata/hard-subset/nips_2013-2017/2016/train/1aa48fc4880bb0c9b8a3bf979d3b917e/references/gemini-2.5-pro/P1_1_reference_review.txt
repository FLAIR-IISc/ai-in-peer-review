### Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques, specifically SVRG and SAGA, from the standard convex minimization setting to the more general class of composite saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `M` is a strongly convex-concave "simple" function (with an accessible proximal operator) and `K` is a smooth convex-concave function whose gradient operator `B` can be decomposed into a finite sum.

The key contributions are:
1.  The proposal of SVRG and SAGA-style algorithms for this saddle-point setting. While the algorithms themselves are natural extensions of their predecessors, this is the first work to provide a rigorous analysis.
2.  A novel convergence analysis that establishes linear convergence rates. Crucially, the analysis is performed through the lens of monotone operator theory and relies only on monotonicity, not on the stronger co-coercivity assumption used in many analyses for convex minimization. This makes the framework applicable to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.
3.  An exploration of different decomposition ("split") strategies for the operator `B`, highlighting the importance and theoretical benefit of non-uniform sampling schemes.
4.  The application of a "Catalyst-like" acceleration scheme to the proposed SVRG method, yielding an accelerated algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods under certain conditions.
5.  Experimental validation on binary classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers, demonstrating the practical superiority of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

This work builds upon the foundational papers on SVRG [2], SAGA [3], and Catalyst acceleration [7], successfully generalizing their core ideas. It significantly advances the state of the art for stochastic saddle-point optimization, moving beyond the sublinear `O(1/t)` rates of previous stochastic methods [22] for this problem class.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and increasingly important problem. Saddle-point optimization is central to areas like robust optimization, game theory, and modern machine learning (e.g., training Generative Adversarial Networks). Providing the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this general setting is a major contribution that will likely be influential. The ability to handle non-separable objectives, as convincingly motivated by the AUC maximization and structured regularization examples, fills a critical gap in the optimization toolkit for machine learning.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The theoretical analysis is the main highlight. The choice to frame the proof in the context of monotone operators is insightful and elegant. It not only provides a correct analysis for saddle-point problems (where standard co-coercivity arguments fail) but also generalizes the results to a wider family of problems. The theoretical results are comprehensive, covering convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA, the benefits of non-uniform sampling, and an accelerated variant. The claims are well-supported by the theory and the empirical results.

*   **Originality:** While the algorithmic templates are inspired by SVRG/SAGA, the originality lies in their novel application and, more importantly, the novel analysis required for the saddle-point/monotone operator setting. The key insight that variance reduction can be analyzed using only monotonicity is a significant conceptual advance that distinguishes this work from prior analyses of SVR methods. The careful treatment of different sampling schemes and "factored" splits for saddle-point problems is also a novel contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem setup and assumptions are stated precisely, and the relationship to prior work is thoroughly discussed. The authors do an excellent job of guiding the reader from the specific problem of saddle-point optimization to the more general framework of monotone operators. Table 1 provides a clear and useful summary of the complexity results, effectively positioning the new algorithms against existing ones.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Dependence on Problem Constants:** The proposed algorithms and their analyses depend on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). In practice, these can be difficult to estimate. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest adaptivity as future work, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion on practical strategies for setting these parameters or the potential robustness of the methods to their mis-specification.

*   **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are well-executed and effectively demonstrate the paper's claims on a relevant non-separable classification problem. However, given the broad motivation including robust optimization and unsupervised learning, showing results on at least one other problem class would have further strengthened the paper's claims of generality. This is a minor point, as the current experiments are already convincing for the chosen application.

*   **Clarity of SAGA Update:** In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), the text mentions an optional resampling step for non-uniform sampling, referencing [25]. For clarity, it would be helpful to explicitly state in the algorithm's pseudocode which sampling distribution is used for the main update step versus the optional gradient-table update step, as this is a subtle but important detail for the theoretical guarantees.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to stochastic optimization for machine learning. It provides the first linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems. The analysis is novel, powerful, and more general than the problem setting initially suggests. The work is of high quality, significant, and clearly presented. It advances the state of the art and is likely to be a foundational paper for future research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
