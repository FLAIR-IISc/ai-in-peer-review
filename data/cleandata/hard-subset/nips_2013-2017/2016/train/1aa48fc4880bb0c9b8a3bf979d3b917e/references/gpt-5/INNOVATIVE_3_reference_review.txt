Summary
The paper proposes stochastic variance-reduction methods (SVRG- and SAGA-style) for general convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex–concave with a tractable proximal operator and K is smooth. The key idea is to extend variance-reduced forward–backward splitting to this setting by working on the monotone operator formulation B(x,y) = (∂_x K, −∂_y K) and applying variance reduction to B when it decomposes as a finite sum B = ∑_i B_i. The authors give linear convergence guarantees with rates that depend on a weighted norm reflecting the different scalings of x and y, and they develop non-uniform sampling schemes that substantially improve the constants. They show two natural ways to split B (element-wise and “factored” row/column splits in bilinear cases) and analyze the cost/variance trade-offs. They also present an acceleration scheme via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper, yielding improved complexity. Beyond saddle points, the analysis extends to solving zeros of strongly monotone operators A+B where only the resolvent of A is available and B is a sum of Lipschitz operators (no co-coercivity needed).

Experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers and on a separable benchmark indicate that (i) non-uniform sampling is important; (ii) SAGA and especially accelerated SVRG improve over batch and stochastic FB baselines; and (iii) when primal separability is available, specialized primal variance-reduction remains faster, as expected, but the proposed methods dominate when separability is absent.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization, but those do not apply to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems.
- Goes beyond primal-dual coordinate methods for separable saddle-point problems (e.g., SPDC, APCD), and beyond Chambolle–Pock-type primal–dual algorithms by exploiting finite-sum structure with variance reduction.
- Distinct from prior stochastic FB analyses that yield sublinear convergence; here the authors prove linear rates under strong convex–concavity.
- The monotone operator extension differs from existing works that rely on co-coercivity of each component; here only monotonicity of the sum is used, making the scope broader (variational inequalities, general saddle points).

Strengths
- Novelty/vision: A clear, general extension of variance-reduced methods to non-separable saddle-point problems and, more broadly, to monotone inclusions without co-coercivity. This is a substantial conceptual step with potential to influence several subareas (robust optimization, structured prediction, VI).
- Theory: Linear convergence with explicit constants; careful treatment of weighted norms for x/y scaling; non-uniform sampling analysis that leverages problem structure (Frobenius vs operator norm bounds) and yields meaningful complexity improvements; clean proximal-point/catalyst acceleration adapted to the saddle-point/monotone operator viewpoint.
- Practical aspects: Two decomposition strategies (individual and factored) with concrete storage/computation trade-offs; guidance on mini-batching; clarity on when variance reduction beats batch acceleration; experiments demonstrating real gains on non-separable problems.
- Scope: The monotone operator framing is elegant and widens applicability beyond ML saddle points.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical evaluation is limited: two datasets, modest ablation. The paper would benefit from:
  - Larger-scale bilinear experiments with different aspect ratios and condition numbers.
  - A variational inequality or game-theoretic example to showcase the monotone operator generality.
  - More thorough study of uniform vs non-uniform sampling, resampling vs reuse in SAGA, and mini-batch scaling.
- Practical guidance: Non-uniform sampling requires access to row/column norms or elementwise squared magnitudes; discuss costs for sparse/streaming settings and how to maintain/update them. For SAGA, O(nd) storage under individual splits is often infeasible; the factored variant helps, but clearer guidance on when each is preferred would be useful.
- Parameter dependence: Algorithms require knowledge of strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). Some discussion or heuristics for adaptive step-size/parameter tuning would improve practicality.
- Clarity/notation: The paper is dense. A few places could be clarified:
  - The role and computation of L vs L̄, and the units in step-size formulas with L^2.
  - The necessity (or not) of splitting into convex–concave terms: a sentence says “does need splits into convex-concave terms,” which seems to contradict the earlier point that Bi need not be gradients of scalar functions—this likely needs rephrasing (“does not need”).
  - In SAGA with non-uniform sampling, the need to resample after the iterate update (and the practical workaround) deserves more explicit guidance in the main text.
- Comparisons: Include comparisons to accelerated PDHG variants (e.g., with over-relaxation/line search) in the batch setting, and to SPDC on separable problems, to further contextualize the gains.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the results are plausible and well-motivated. The appendices reportedly contain the proofs, and the use of monotonicity (rather than co-coercivity) is both careful and important. Some constants and step-size choices could be highlighted more clearly in the main text. The experimental section supports the claims but is not comprehensive.

Clarity
Well-organized but dense. Expository improvements in the main algorithm boxes (explicit parameter choices and their dependencies), a clearer discussion of storage/computation trade-offs, and a short “practical recipe” section would help practitioners.

Originality
High. To my knowledge, this is the first variance-reduced treatment with linear rates for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and a monotone operator extension without co-coercivity assumptions, plus a simple and effective catalyst-style acceleration in this setting.

Significance
High. Many ML problems are naturally saddle-point/VI formulations without separability, and the proposed methods can become the default stochastic workhorses in those settings. The non-uniform sampling insights and factored decompositions are practically relevant. The monotone operator view can spur follow-up work in broader areas.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, broadly applicable conceptual contribution.
- Solid theory with linear rates and meaningful constants.
- Practical algorithmic design (sampling, decomposition, acceleration) with demonstrated gains.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and ablations.
- Reliance on known constants and some implementation overhead (sampling stats, storage).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper advances the state of the art with a novel and impactful direction; minor empirical and presentation gaps should be fixable.

Confidence
High.
