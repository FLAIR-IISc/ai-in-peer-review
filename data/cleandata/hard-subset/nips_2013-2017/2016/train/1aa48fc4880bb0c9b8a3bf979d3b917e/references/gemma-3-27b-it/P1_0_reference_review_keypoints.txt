- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with convergence analysis and links to monotone operator theory.  
- Demonstrates need for specific operator splitting, non-uniform sampling, and proposes a catalyst-based acceleration.  
- Shows applicability beyond saddle-points (variational inequalities) and empirical gains on separable and non-separable problems.  

- Novel contribution: first dedicated extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point settings with theoretical insights.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides linear convergence results under appropriate assumptions and insightful monotonicity-based analysis.  
- Practical relevance: addresses common ML saddle-point formulations and shows empirical effectiveness, especially for the accelerated variant.  
- Comprehensive study of sampling strategies and a detailed analysis of the bilinear saddle-point case.  
- Clear and well-organized presentation, with helpful comparisons to convex minimization.  

- Relies on strong convexity-concavity and Lipschitz assumptions that may be restrictive in practice; lacks robustness analysis under weaker conditions.  
- Accelerated algorithm implementation and selection/trade-offs for parameter τ are under-explained and depend on estimating smoothness constants.  
- Experimental comparisons are limited; missing baselines like ADMM and other proximal solvers and sensitivity analyses (e.g., to regularization strength).  
- Experimental scope is narrow and should include a broader set of saddle-point applications.  
- Catalyst implementation details and τ adaptation remain unclear and need further clarification.  

- Technically sound and claims are well-supported by theory and experiments.  
- Generally clear, though sections on acceleration need more detail.  
- Highly original and significant; results likely useful to researchers and practitioners.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel, significant extension with strong theory and empirical support addressing a gap in the literature.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, incomplete experimental comparisons, and unclear accelerated-version implementation.  

- Overall recommendation: strong accept.
