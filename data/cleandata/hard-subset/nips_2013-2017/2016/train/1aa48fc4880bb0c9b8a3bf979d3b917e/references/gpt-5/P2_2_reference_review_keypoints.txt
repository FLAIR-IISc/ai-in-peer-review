- Problem and contribution: Extends SVRG and SAGA to finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K+M, proving linear convergence and providing a Catalyst-like proximal acceleration.  
- Unified analysis: Uses a monotone operator framework to generalize results to strongly monotone inclusions/variational inequalities beyond saddle points.  
- Decompositions and sampling: Studies two finite-sum decompositions (by function and by partial derivatives/factored) and analyzes non-uniform sampling with practical smoothness quantity L̄(π).  
- Theoretical guarantees: Proves linear rates for SVRG and SAGA under strong convexity-concavity with explicit step sizes and iteration complexity bounds in terms of L and L̄(π).  
- Acceleration result: Shows proximal regularization yields improved complexity trade-offs (familiar L̄√(T(B) max T(Bi)) behavior).  
- Non-uniform sampling bounds: Provides sensible bounds using norms (‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max) and demonstrates advantages for factored vs. element-wise splits.  
- Novelty: Novel extension of variance-reduction methods to non-separable convex-concave and monotone settings without relying on co-coercivity.  
- Practical impact: Addresses an important gap for non-separable problems with efficient proximal operators, likely useful in structured prediction and robust optimization.  
- Strengths: Solid, general theory; extension to monotone operators; careful sampling/decomposition treatment; simple, implementable acceleration; empirical results consistent with theory.  
- Correctness concerns: Some nonstandard step-size forms (e.g., σ=1/L^2 in weighted norm) need clearer derivation/intuition to avoid confusion.  
- SAGA detail gap: The resampling requirement under non-uniform sampling should be explicitly stated for the provable variant versus the heuristic used in experiments.  
- Experiments: Empirically validates key claims (benefit of variance reduction, non-uniform sampling, acceleration), but is limited to two datasets and a few methods.  
- Missing baselines: Lacks comparisons to stochastic extragradient/mirror-prox and SPDC-type methods commonly used for saddle/VIs.  
- Practical guidance lacking: More detail needed on proximal operators used, wall-clock vs iteration costs, memory usage, and how to compute/estimate L̄(π) and optimal π.  
- Robustness questions: Need clarity on sensitivity to mis-specified λ, γ, π and competitiveness when using rough norm estimates for acceleration parameters.  
- Suggestions: Add extragradient/mirror-prox comparisons, clarify step-size motivations and practical defaults, report wall-clock/memory and factored SAGA benefits, and separate provable vs practical SAGA variants in the text.  
- Overall assessment: High-quality, novel theoretical contribution with likely impact despite modest empirical breadth; recommend acceptance.
