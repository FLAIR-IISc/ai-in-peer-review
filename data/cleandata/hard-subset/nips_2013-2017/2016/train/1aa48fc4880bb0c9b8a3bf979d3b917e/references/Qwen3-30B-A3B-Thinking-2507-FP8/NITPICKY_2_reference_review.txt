

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper proposes stochastic variance reduction algorithms (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, extending the successful techniques from convex minimization to this broader class of problems. The authors make several contributions: (1) they provide the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems; (2) they identify key differences between saddle-point and convex minimization problems, particularly regarding splits and sampling strategies; and (3) they demonstrate how to accelerate these algorithms using the catalyst framework.

## Strengths
The paper is well-motivated, with clear examples of machine learning problems where saddle-point formulations naturally arise but where existing variance-reduced methods cannot be applied. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the experiments show significant improvements over existing methods for non-separable problems. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is particularly noteworthy, as it broadens the applicability of the techniques beyond saddle-point problems.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Inconsistencies (Major)
1. **Critical notation error in Theorem 1**: The paper states "E [ Ω(xv − x∗, yv − y∗)² ] 6 (3/4)vΩ(x0 − x∗, y0 − y∗)²" - this is mathematically incorrect. It should be "E [ ... ] 6 (3/4)^v Ω(...)²" (with the exponent v, not multiplied by v). This is a serious error that undermines the theoretical contribution.

2. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses "strongly (λ, γ)-convex-concave" in assumption (A) but later refers to "strongly-convex-concave" without the λ, γ parameters, creating confusion about the exact conditions required.

3. **Typographical errors**: 
   - "gradient descents" should be "gradient descent" (Section 2.2)
   - "11+τ" should be "1/(1+τ)" in the convergence rate discussion (Section 5)
   - "µ2" appears to be a typo for "μ²" in Theorem 2 (Section 4)

4. **Missing elements**: The paper references "Table 1" and "Appendix D.1" but neither is included in the submission. This is unacceptable for a scientific paper.

### Theoretical Concerns (Moderate)
1. The claim that they provide "the first convergence analysis for these algorithms for saddle-point problems" is questionable. While they present novel analysis for these specific algorithms, there is existing work on stochastic methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., [22] cited in the paper).

2. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is described but lacks concrete examples beyond saddle-point problems. The claim that the analysis "extends beyond saddle-point problems, e.g., to variational inequalities" needs more specific justification.

3. In Section 5, the authors state "the solution of Eq. (4) is one iteration of the proximal-point algorithm, which is known to converge linearly [27] with rate (1 + τ−1)−1 = (1 − 11+τ)". The rate expression is incorrect - it should be (1 + τ)⁻¹, not (1 - 1/(1+τ)).

### Experimental Concerns (Minor)
The experiments show promising results, but the paper lacks statistical significance tests to confirm the observed improvements are not due to chance. The claim that "SAGA and accelerated SVRG improve significantly over existing methods" would be stronger with p-values or confidence intervals.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on saddle-point problems and variance reduction techniques. However, the critical notation error in Theorem 1 fundamentally undermines the theoretical contribution, as the convergence rate bound is incorrectly stated. The other technical inconsistencies and missing elements (tables, appendices) are also serious issues for a scientific paper.

While the main ideas are sound and the problem is well-motivated, these technical flaws prevent the paper from meeting NIPS' standards for a high-quality scientific contribution. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to correct the mathematical errors and provide the missing elements.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains critical mathematical errors that invalidate the main theoretical claim, and the missing elements (tables, appendices) make it impossible to evaluate the full contribution. These issues cannot be fixed with minor revisions but require substantial reworking of the theoretical analysis.
