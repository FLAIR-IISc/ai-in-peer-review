## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of optimization, specifically addressing the challenging problem of solving saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods. The authors successfully extend SVRG and SAGA to this setting, provide the *first* convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context, and demonstrate their practical effectiveness. The connection to monotone operator theory is a particularly strong point, broadening the applicability of the results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of solving saddle-point problems arising frequently in machine learning (e.g., supervised learning with non-separable regularizers, robust optimization). These problems are of the form min<sub>x</sub> max<sub>y</sub> K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth and convex-concave, and M is strongly convex-concave (potentially non-smooth).  The authors observe that while SVRG and SAGA have been successful for separable convex minimization, their extension to saddle-point problems is non-trivial and lacks theoretical guarantees. 

They address this gap by adapting these algorithms to the saddle-point setting, carefully analyzing their convergence properties.  Crucially, they leverage the connection between saddle-point problems and monotone operators to provide a broader theoretical foundation. This work builds on the existing literature on SVRG [Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013], SAGA [Defazio et al., NIPS 2014], and forward-backward algorithms [Chen & Rockafellar, 2014].  It also connects to work on stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems [Zhu et al., 2016], though the authors demonstrate a broader applicability through the monotone operator framework. A significant contribution is extending the "catalyst" framework [Lin et al., NIPS 2015] to accelerate the proposed algorithms.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  Providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting is a significant contribution. The extension to monotone operators further broadens the applicability of the work.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The proofs appear solid and are presented with sufficient detail (although streamlining some could be beneficial, see below). The connection to monotone operator theory is insightful and allows for a more general analysis.
* **Practical Relevance:** The algorithms are tailored to problems common in machine learning, and the experimental results demonstrate their practical effectiveness, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.
* **Insights into Sampling:**  The analysis of different splitting strategies (function-wise vs. derivative-wise) and the importance of non-uniform sampling are valuable contributions.  The specific non-uniform sampling schemes proposed for bilinear problems are particularly promising.
* **Acceleration Framework:** The successful extension of the catalyst framework to this setting provides a further performance boost.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental section demonstrates the benefits of the proposed algorithms compared to existing methods across different scenarios.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Notation & Presentation:** The paper suffers from somewhat dense notation and occasionally lacks clarity in its presentation.  For example, the definitions of A, B, K, and M could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The various norms used for bilinear problems (op, F, max) and their interplay could also be explained more clearly.
* **Proof Length & Readability:**  While the proofs are theoretically sound, they are quite lengthy and can be difficult to follow.  Condensing or moving some details to the appendix could improve readability.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed algorithms, such as sensitivity to parameter tuning or applicability to specific problem structures.
* **Adaptive Step-Size:** The paper briefly mentions exploring adaptivity to strong convexity/concavity constants in the conclusion, which is an important direction for future work and could be highlighted more prominently.



**4.  Detailed Evaluation (Addressing NIPS Criteria):**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The completeness of the work is strong; it provides both theoretical foundations and empirical validation.
* **Clarity:** While technically strong, the clarity could be improved through more intuitive explanations of notation and a more streamlined presentation of the proofs.
* **Originality:** The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, the convergence analysis, and the connection to monotone operators are all novel contributions.
* **Significance:** The results are significant, as they provide efficient algorithms for a class of problems that are prevalent in machine learning. The practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments, particularly for ill-conditioned problems, are compelling. The extension to monotone operators is a valuable generalization.




**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* First convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting.
* Strong theoretical foundation based on monotone operator theory.
* Demonstrated practical effectiveness through comprehensive experiments.
* Insightful analysis of sampling strategies.
* Successful extension of the catalyst framework.

**Con:**

* Dense notation and occasionally unclear presentation.
* Lengthy proofs that could benefit from streamlining.
* Limited discussion of limitations.




**6.  Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to improve clarity and readability. Specifically:

* **Simplify Notation:** Review and simplify the notation used throughout the paper, providing more intuitive explanations for key variables and operators.
* **Streamline Proofs:**  Condense the proofs where possible, and move detailed derivations to the appendix.
* **Discuss Limitations:**  Include a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed algorithms.
* **Highlight Future Work:**  Expand the discussion of future work, particularly regarding adaptive step-size selection.

This is a valuable contribution that will be of significant interest to the NIPS community. The authors have made a compelling case for the effectiveness of their approach, and I am confident that addressing the minor suggestions outlined above will further strengthen the paper.
