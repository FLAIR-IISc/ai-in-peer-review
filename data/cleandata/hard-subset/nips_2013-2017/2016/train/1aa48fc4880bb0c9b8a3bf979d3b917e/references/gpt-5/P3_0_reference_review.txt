Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is “simple” via a proximal operator and strongly convex-concave, and K is smooth. The core contributions are (i) extensions of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity, (ii) importance sampling and two forms of decompositions (function-wise and operator/gradient-wise “factored” splits) tailored to saddle structures, (iii) an acceleration via a Catalyst-like proximal-point regularization that yields improved complexities, and (iv) an analysis that extends beyond saddle points to strongly monotone inclusions with a single resolvent and a sum of Lipschitz operators.

Relative to prior work: batch primal-dual/forward-backward methods (Arrow-Hurwicz, Chambolle-Pock) yield O(1/t) or linear rates under strong convexity; stochastic forward-backward methods without variance reduction give O(1/t). Variance-reduced methods (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) are well-established for strongly convex finite sums in the purely primal setting, and SPDC-like primal-dual coordinate methods address separable ERM. Stochastic (Mirror-)Extragradient methods obtain O(1/t) without variance reduction. This paper appears to be among the first to bring variance reduction with linear rates to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and to monotone inclusions without cocoercivity.

Technical soundness and methodology
- Algorithmic framework: The paper adapts forward-backward splitting to the weighted norm Ω and considers stochastic variance reduction estimators B(x̃,ỹ)+ (1/πi)[Bi(x,y)-Bi(x̃,ỹ)] for SVRG, or stored gradients for SAGA. The factored split B=(∑k Bxk, ∑j Byj) is well-motivated for bilinear K and leads to storage/runtime benefits versus element-wise splits.
- Convergence for FB: With σ≈1/L^2 (L defined in the Ω/Ω* norms), the authors show linear convergence for the batch FB under strong convexity-concavity; this is consistent with non-expansiveness of the gradient step composed with a strongly monotone resolvent in the chosen scaling.
- SVRG/SAGA analyses: Linear convergence rates are proved with explicit iteration complexities depending on L and L̄(π), where L̄ captures the effect of sampling distribution. Theorems give clean dependencies on mini-batch size m and show how increasing m trades proximal cost against oracle cost. The requirement to resample when using non-uniform sampling is noted (as in non-uniform SAGA for convex minimization).
- Importance sampling: The paper develops problem-adapted L̄(π) bounds for bilinear K under both element-wise and factored splits, with practical distributions (row/column norm-based and entrywise).
- Acceleration: The Catalyst-like scheme solves a sequence of proximal-point regularized saddle problems and leverages the linear contraction of the proximal-point map, avoiding Nesterov machinery. The resulting complexity scales like L̄√(T(B) maxi T(Bi)) up to log factors, improving over batch/vanilla schemes when the full gradient is relatively cheap versus components.
- Monotone operators: Extending the analysis from saddle points to strongly monotone inclusions A+B with a single resolvent for A and Lipschitz B (not necessarily cocoercive) is a meaningful broadening beyond standard convex minimization. The contrast with prior work requiring cocoercivity is well stated.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Variance reduction for general saddle-point problems (non-separable K/M) with linear rates, plus a clean monotone operator viewpoint.
- Careful treatment of sampling and decomposition: The distinction between functional and operator splits and the non-uniform sampling analysis are insightful and practically relevant for bilinear K.
- Acceleration: The proximal-point/Catalyst adaptation is elegant and simpler than classical acceleration in this setting; the trade-off in τ is transparent.
- Theory-to-practice bridge: The paper connects constants to matrix norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) and provides interpretable complexities.
- Experiments: Show consistent gains for (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling on non-separable problems; demonstrate that naive uniform sampling can be inferior to good batch baselines.

Weaknesses and questions
- Experimental scope: Only two datasets with limited ablations. Missing comparisons to stochastic Mirror-Prox/extragradient baselines (deterministic and stochastic), which are widely used for saddle-point/VI problems. For separable ERM, SPDC or modern PDHG variants would be relevant baselines.
- Practicalities of constants: L and especially L̄(π) can be expensive or impractical to compute for nonlinear K. The paper sometimes suggests replacing L^2 by L̄^2 when L^2 is “too expensive”; more guidance on estimating these constants adaptively would increase usability.
- Step-size clarity: Notation alternates between L2 and L^2; the precise dependencies (and the weighted norm role) deserve clearer exposition in the main text. The batch FB choice σ=1/L^2 may be counterintuitive to readers familiar with 1/L; a short intuition in the main paper would help.
- Assumptions on M: The methods require easy prox for M and knowledge of strong convexity-concavity parameters (λ,γ). Discussion on adaptivity to unknown parameters (e.g., backtracking, restarts) would be valuable.
- Nonlinear K: Acceleration analysis is fully presented for SVRG; for batch acceleration beyond bilinear K the paper alludes to extensions but details are deferred. More explicit statements would help.
- Monotone extension: While compelling, no experiments on non-saddle monotone inclusions/VI are provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written but dense. Key notation (Ω, Ω*, L, L̄(π)) is introduced clearly, though some typographical issues remain (e.g., “does need splits” likely “does not need splits”; spacing in L 2; occasional µ appears without a prior definition). Including a brief algorithmic pseudocode in the main text (kept compact) and a paragraph on parameter selection heuristics would improve readability.

Originality
High. Bridging variance reduction to non-separable saddle points and to general monotone inclusions without cocoercivity is novel (to my knowledge at the time of writing). The factored decomposition and sampling analysis for bilinear forms are also original and practically impactful.

Significance
High. Many modern ML problems (robust optimization, structured losses, non-separable regularizers) fit the authors’ template. The methods can materially reduce runtime relative to batch and plain stochastic methods, and the monotone operator view opens doors beyond ML.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Add baselines: Include stochastic Mirror-Prox and a strong PDHG/Chambolle-Pock implementation with tuned step sizes; for separable cases, include SPDC.
- Report wall-clock time and memory, not only “passes.” Especially compare SAGA storage in element-wise vs factored variants.
- Provide guidance on estimating L̄(π) or using adaptive step-size schemes when constants are unknown.
- Clarify the role of resampling in non-uniform SAGA and report empirical impact of omitting it.
- Expand on nonlinear K: state clearly the conditions under which the accelerated batch method remains valid beyond bilinear K, or delineate open cases.
- Release code and proximal operator implementations referenced in the appendices to enhance reproducibility.

Pros
- Strong, general theory with linear rates; clear benefits of importance sampling and factorization
- Practical acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst
- Meaningful extension to monotone inclusions beyond saddle points
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory on non-separable tasks

Cons
- Limited experimental breadth and missing key baselines
- Dependence on hard-to-estimate constants; limited guidance on parameter tuning
- Some clarity/notation issues; minor typos

Recommendation: Weak accept to accept. The theoretical contribution and the principled, practically relevant algorithmic advances outweigh the experimental limitations. With added baselines and clarity improvements, this would be a very strong NeurIPS paper. Confidence: high.
