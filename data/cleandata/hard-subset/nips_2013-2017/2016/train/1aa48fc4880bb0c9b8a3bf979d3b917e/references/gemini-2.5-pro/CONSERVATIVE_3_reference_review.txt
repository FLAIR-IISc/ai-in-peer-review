**Summary:**
This paper proposes extending the well-known stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods, SVRG and SAGA, to solve composite saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is a smooth convex-concave function with a finite-sum structure, and `M` is a "simple" (i.e., has an efficient proximal operator) strongly convex-concave function. The authors adapt SVRG and SAGA to this setting by applying them to the gradient operator associated with `K`. They provide a theoretical analysis establishing linear convergence, which is grounded in the theory of monotone operators. Additionally, they explore the benefits of non-uniform sampling and demonstrate how the "Catalyst" framework can be used to accelerate their SVRG variant. The methods are evaluated on binary classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers, showing empirical improvements over batch and standard stochastic algorithms.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA from convex minimization to the saddle-point setting is a logical and technically sound contribution. The paper is well-structured, the assumptions are clearly stated (A-C), and the proposed algorithms are direct, sensible adaptations of their well-established counterparts.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first linear convergence guarantees for SVRG and SAGA in this general saddle-point setting. Framing the analysis within the context of monotone operators is appropriate and allows for a general proof that does not rely on co-coercivity, which is a key property in many analyses for convex minimization but does not hold here. This distinction is important and correctly identified by the authors. The resulting complexity bounds (Table 1) clearly articulate the theoretical advantages over existing batch and stochastic methods.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. It provides a good overview of related work on saddle-point optimization and variance reduction techniques. The distinction between different types of "splits" (individual vs. factored) and the role of non-uniform sampling are well explained.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Algorithmic Originality:** The primary weakness of this work is its incremental nature. The core algorithms are direct applications of SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point gradient operator. Similarly, the acceleration technique is a straightforward application of the existing Catalyst framework. While applying established methods to a new problem class is a valid contribution, the lack of significant algorithmic novelty makes the work feel more like a careful case study than a fundamental advance. The novelty lies almost entirely in tailoring the analysis to this setting.
2.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The empirical results, while promising, are not extensive enough to provide conclusive evidence of broad superiority.
    *   The experiments focus on a custom-designed non-separable problem. While this demonstrates a setting where the proposed methods are uniquely applicable among variance-reduced methods, it does not show how they perform against a wider range of saddle-point solvers. On the `rcv1` dataset where a separable formulation exists, the comparison shows that a primal SAGA implementation remains superior. This suggests the primary benefit is confined to the specific (and arguably niche) case where the objective is non-separable in the primal/dual but the saddle-point kernel `K` has a sum structure. The paper could be strengthened by better motivating the prevalence and importance of this specific problem structure in machine learning.
    *   The baselines are somewhat limited. The main comparisons are against batch forward-backward (FB) methods and a standard stochastic FB method. While the proposed methods outperform these, it is a well-known result from the convex minimization literature that variance-reduced methods are superior to both batch and standard SGD on finite-sum problems. More compelling baselines might include stochastic coordinate-based methods for saddle-point problems [11, 12] where applicable, even if they require separability.
3.  **Analysis Claims:** The paper states that its convergence analysis "differs significantly" from the convex minimization setup. This claim feels overstated. The analysis relies on showing a contraction in the expected distance to the solution, a standard technique in the analysis of SVRG/SAGA. While adapting this to the monotone operator framework without co-coercivity is a non-trivial technical step, the overall proof structure does not seem to represent a radical departure from the established analytical machinery for variance-reduced methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important class of problems and provides the first theoretically-backed, linearly-convergent stochastic variance-reduced methods for them.
*   The work is technically solid, well-written, and connects several important lines of research (saddle-point optimization, variance reduction, acceleration).
*   The demonstrated practical speed-ups over standard batch and stochastic methods are significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The algorithmic contribution is highly incremental, applying existing algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst) to a new domain with minimal modification.
*   The experimental validation is not comprehensive enough to fully support the claims of superiority, particularly regarding the scope of problems where these methods offer a true advantage over primal-based approaches.
*   The claimed novelty of the analysis seems somewhat exaggerated.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a solid, well-executed piece of work that extends popular variance-reduction techniques to the saddle-point setting. The theoretical analysis is sound and the empirical results are positive. However, the contribution is ultimately incremental, applying well-known algorithmic templates to a new problem class. The primary value lies in the careful analysis and in demonstrating that these extensions work as expected. While the work is of good quality, its limited novelty and the narrow scope of its demonstrated empirical advantage make it a borderline case for a highly competitive conference like NIPS. A more thorough experimental comparison and a more compelling argument for the widespread importance of the specific problem structure addressed would be needed to make a stronger case for acceptance.
