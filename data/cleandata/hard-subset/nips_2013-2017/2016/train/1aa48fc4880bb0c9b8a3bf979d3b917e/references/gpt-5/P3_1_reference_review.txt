Summary
The paper studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) where M is proximable and strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave and K is smooth. The key idea is to view the problem via monotone operators: A = (∂xM, −∂yM) (maximal strongly monotone) and B = (∂xK, −∂yK) (monotone, Lipschitz), and to extend variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this forward-backward setting. The operator B is assumed decomposable B = ∑i Bi, with two relevant decompositions in bilinear cases (element-wise and factored row/column splits). The authors define a sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π) and show linear convergence for SVRG (epochal) and SAGA (online) with non-uniform sampling and mini-batching. They analyze complexity in terms of the cost of full B, a single Bi, and the prox of M, and give explicit and often sharp bounds for bilinear problems using Frobenius and row/column norm-based probabilities. They further “catalyze” SVRG via a proximal-point regularization, obtaining a simpler acceleration than in convex minimization and an overall complexity ~ L̄√(T(B) max_i T(Bi)). Finally, they remark that the analysis only uses monotonicity (not co-coercivity), so it extends to broader monotone inclusion problems. Experiments on two datasets with non-separable loss/regularizer (and one separable case for comparison) show that non-uniform sampling and acceleration significantly improve over batch forward-backward methods.

Relation to prior work
This work connects variance reduction (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) to primal-dual/monotone operator methods, bridging a gap: existing stochastic primal-dual/coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC, SPDHG/Chambolle-Pock variants) typically rely on separability; deterministic extragradient/mirror-prox methods achieve sublinear rates or linear rates under strong monotonicity but are not incremental. The authors explicitly avoid the co-coercivity assumption that underpins many convex minimization analyses, which is the core technical novelty. The Catalyst-style acceleration is adapted here using a proximal-point view, simplifying the outer-loop analysis compared to convex minimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: clean operator-splitting view that enables SVRG/SAGA without co-coercivity, extending to general monotone inclusions.
- Theory: linear convergence guarantees with explicit constants for both SVRG and SAGA; careful treatment of λ,γ scaling via weighted norms; non-uniform sampling analysis with informative L̄(π) bounds (especially for bilinear factored splits).
- Practical insights: storage/computation trade-offs for factored vs. individual splits; mini-batching to amortize prox cost; importance of non-uniform sampling.
- Acceleration: proximal-point-based “catalyst” variant with clear complexity trade-offs and simplified analysis.
- Applicability: covers non-separable losses/regularizers with available prox, a regime not handled by prior variance-reduced primal methods.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Forward-backward convergence claims: The paper states FB is linearly convergent for strongly convex-concave problems with σ=1/L^2 and rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t. This is non-standard in the general monotone-Lipschitz (non-co-coercive) setting, where FB can fail and extragradient/Tseng’s FBF are typically used. Please clarify the exact assumptions under which your FB result holds (e.g., strong monotonicity of A, mere Lipschitz monotonicity of B), how the Ω/Ω* metric is used, and sketch the key argument in the main text.
- Notation/step sizes: “L2” appears to denote L^2 while accelerated FB uses σ=1/(2L). This is confusing and units feel inconsistent across sections and algorithms. Unify notation and explicitly state whether “L2” means L^2 everywhere.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling: The theorem requires a post-update resampling step; the experiments omit it and use a mixture heuristic. Please add an ablation quantifying the impact of resampling vs. the heuristic, and discuss any theory-practice gap.
- Experimental scope: Only two datasets, and results are reported in data passes. Since per-iteration costs differ (prox, storage updates), wall-clock comparisons and variance/error bars would strengthen the case. Including extra-gradient/mirror-prox baselines (deterministic and stochastic) would better position the contribution. Report the one-time cost to compute non-uniform probabilities (row/column norms) and its effect.
- Practical assumptions: The approach assumes known λ,γ and L/L̄ and efficient prox for potentially complex non-separable M. Please discuss sensitivity to misspecification and whether backtracking/adaptive tuning can be used.
- Monotone operator extension: This is promising; a brief comparison to Tseng’s FBF, mirror-prox, and SMART (Davis, 2016) in terms of assumptions and constants would help readers understand when your scheme is preferable.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled extension of variance reduction to saddle-point/monotone operators without co-coercivity.
- Strong theoretical results (linear rates), insightful sampling analysis, and a simple yet effective acceleration.
- Practical relevance to non-separable losses/regularizers common in ML.

Cons for acceptance
- Some theoretical claims (FB linear convergence) need clearer exposition and conditions.
- Limited empirical evaluation (datasets, baselines, wall-clock) and a minor mismatch between theory and practice (SAGA resampling).
- Heavy reliance on known constants and efficient prox; robustness not empirically assessed.

Questions for authors
- Precisely state the conditions under which your FB rate holds and contrast with known counterexamples for FB on saddle problems.
- How sensitive are your methods to misestimation of L, L̄, λ, γ? Can you incorporate adaptive step-size/backtracking?
- What is the empirical effect of the SAGA resampling step with non-uniform sampling?
- Could an extragradient step be combined with your variance-reduced estimators to relax assumptions or improve constants?

Overall evaluation
High-quality and original work with significant potential impact. With clarifications on FB convergence, improved notation, and a strengthened experimental section (including wall-clock and additional baselines), this would be an excellent NeurIPS paper.
