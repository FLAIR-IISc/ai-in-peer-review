### Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**
This paper proposes extensions of the SVRG and SAGA algorithms, originally developed for convex finite-sum minimization, to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The key motivation is to tackle machine learning problems involving non-separable loss functions or regularizers, which cannot be directly addressed by applying standard variance-reduction techniques to the primal or dual formulations alone. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these new algorithms, demonstrating linear convergence rates. The analysis is framed within the more general theory of monotone operators, which allows the results to apply beyond saddle-point problems. The paper also highlights the importance of non-uniform sampling for practical performance and introduces an accelerated version of their SVRG algorithm by adapting the "Catalyst" framework, which yields theoretically superior convergence bounds.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to the saddle-point setting is a significant and timely contribution. Many important problems in machine learning, such as AUC maximization, robust optimization, and structured prediction, naturally lead to saddle-point formulations with non-separable objectives. This work provides the first linearly-convergent incremental algorithms for this general class of problems, representing a clear advance over existing batch or O(1/t) stochastic methods.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing linear convergence for both the SVRG and SAGA variants. The use of monotone operator theory for the analysis is elegant and general. Furthermore, the successful application of the Catalyst framework for acceleration is a strong result, leading to an algorithm with state-of-the-art theoretical complexity.

3.  **Practical Insights:** The paper correctly identifies and demonstrates the crucial role of non-uniform sampling. The experimental results clearly show that uniform sampling can be inferior to accelerated batch methods, making non-uniform sampling a practical necessity rather than just a theoretical curiosity. The discussion of different "split" strategies (individual vs. factored) is also insightful.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the strong contributions, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws in presentation, methodological clarity, and experimental rigor. These issues, in aggregate, detract from the overall quality of the manuscript and suggest a lack of polish.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Formatting and Professionalism:** The footnote on the first page ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") appears to be a leftover from a template and is unprofessional. The citation style is inconsistent throughout the introduction (e.g., "SAG [1], SVRG [2]" vs. "framework of [7]"). The use of `y>Kx` should be `y^T Kx` for clarity. The notation is occasionally overloaded; for instance, `K` is used for both the coupling function and the design matrix, and later for an index set in `I = J x K`.
    *   **Algorithm Description:** In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), several steps are marked as "(optional)". The text clarifies this relates to resampling for non-uniform sampling, but this makes the step conditional, not optional. The description is ambiguous and could be integrated more clearly into the algorithm's logic.
    *   **Dense Definitions:** The definitions in Section 2, particularly for the Lipschitz constant `L` in assumption (B), are presented in a very dense manner that hinders readability. The assertion that `L` "represents the condition number" is not universally defined and could benefit from more careful phrasing.

2.  **Methodological and Technical Details:**
    *   **Parameter Dependence:** A significant practical limitation, which is entirely unaddressed, is the algorithms' dependence on the smoothness constants `L` and `L̄`. The step-sizes are critically dependent on these values, which are often unknown in practice. The paper provides no discussion on how these might be estimated or adapted to, which is a crucial aspect for real-world usability.
    *   **SAGA Storage Cost:** The paper mentions that SAGA with individual splits requires `O(dn)` storage. For modern datasets where `d` and `n` can be large (e.g., the `rcv1` dataset used in the experiments), this cost is prohibitive. This major practical drawback is downplayed and deserves more prominent discussion as it severely limits the applicability of that variant.
    *   **Monotone Operator Extension:** Section 6, which presents the generalization to monotone operators, is disappointingly brief. It makes the strong claim that the analysis extends but provides almost no detail. The statement that applying the paper's analysis directly to convex minimization would yield "slower rates" because it does not use co-coercivity is puzzling. If a problem satisfies co-coercivity, a proper analysis should leverage it. The suggestion to recast convex problems as saddle-point problems feels like an ad-hoc fix rather than a unified theoretical treatment. This section feels underdeveloped and seems aimed more at broadening the paper's scope than providing a complete contribution.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Choice of Metric:** The primary metric reported in the main paper is the squared distance to the optimizer. While theoretically motivated, this metric is of limited interest to the machine learning community. Plots showing convergence of test error, test AUC, or the primal-dual gap would be far more compelling and should have been included in the main text, not relegated to the appendix.
    *   **Baselines:** The set of baselines is somewhat limited. While comparison to forward-backward variants is reasonable, including other relevant methods for structured problems, such as variants of ADMM, could have provided a more comprehensive picture of the state of the art.
    *   **Reproducibility:** The description of the loss and regularizer used in the experiments is very brief. While references are provided, the paper would be more self-contained and reproducible if it included the explicit mathematical forms of the objectives and a clearer explanation of why their proximal operators are efficient to compute.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important, unsolved problem: applying variance reduction to general saddle-point formulations.
*   The core ideas are novel, and the theoretical results (linear convergence and acceleration) are strong and represent a significant advance.
*   The work opens up the use of SVR-type methods for a much wider class of machine learning models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript lacks the polish and attention to detail expected for a top-tier publication. Numerous minor presentational flaws accumulate to create a poor impression.
*   Key practical aspects, such as the tuning of step-sizes, are ignored. Major limitations, like the storage cost of SAGA, are not given sufficient weight.
*   The experimental validation is incomplete, focusing on a theoretical metric over practical performance measures and using a limited set of baselines.
*   Important theoretical sections, particularly the extension to monotone operators, are rushed and lack sufficient detail to be fully convincing.

**Recommendation:**
Reject.

While the core contribution of this paper is strong and addresses a significant problem, the execution is flawed. The manuscript is marred by a lack of attention to detail in its presentation, a failure to address key practical considerations, and an experimental section that is not as thorough as it should be. The paper feels premature and would benefit significantly from another round of careful revision to address these numerous, albeit individually minor, shortcomings. In its current state, it does not meet the high standards of quality and clarity I would expect from a NIPS paper.
