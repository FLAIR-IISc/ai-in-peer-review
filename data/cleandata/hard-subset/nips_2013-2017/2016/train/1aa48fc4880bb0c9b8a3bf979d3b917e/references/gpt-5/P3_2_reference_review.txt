Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is simple/strongly convex-concave (prox-available) and K is smooth. The main contributions are: (i) extensions of SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point/monotone operator setting with linear convergence under strong convex-concavity; (ii) importance sampling and two kinds of decompositions (function-based and operator/partial-derivative-based “factored” splits), with corresponding complexity bounds; (iii) an acceleration via a catalyst-style proximal regularization, yielding improved oracle complexity; and (iv) an extension of the analysis beyond saddle points to general monotone inclusions A+B with A maximal strongly monotone and B a sum of Lipschitz operators with monotone sum.

The work is positioned against SAG/SVRG/SAGA for strongly convex finite sums, which assume separability of the objective; against batch primal-dual methods (Chambolle-Pock, forward-backward, Nesterov acceleration); and against stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (SPDC) that require separability. The proposed algorithms do not require each component to be the gradient of a scalar function and can exploit operator factorizations (e.g., row/column sampling in bilinear problems). The acceleration builds on the proximal point interpretation (Rockafellar), akin to Catalyst but simpler due to the monotone operator setting.

Technical quality and correctness
- Algorithms: The stochastic FB update uses a variance-reduced estimate of B with either a snapshot (SVRG) or table (SAGA), then applies a prox step for M with a scaling matching the weighted norm induced by λ and γ. The factored split and non-uniform sampling are carefully integrated.

- Convergence: The analysis leverages the monotone operator view rather than co-coercivity, which is important because B may not be co-coercive in saddle-point problems. Linear convergence is shown under strong convex-concavity; the rates depend on L (Lipschitz constant of B) and on a sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π). The extension to general monotone inclusions A+B (A resolvent-known, B sum of Lipschitz operators with monotone sum) is significant and technically nontrivial; the paper correctly highlights differences from prior co-coercive analyses.

- Acceleration: The catalyst-style scheme regularizes the problem around a moving center (x̄,ȳ), solves each proximal subproblem approximately by SVRG, and updates the center periodically. The resulting complexity T(B)(1+τ) + L̄^2(1+τ)^{-1} max_i T(B_i) (up to logs) has the characteristic multiplicative improvement L̄ sqrt(T(B) max_i T(B_i)) at the optimal τ, and the batch variant follows as a special case.

- Sampling and complexity: The derivation of L̄(π) for different splits in bilinear problems is well motivated. The use of importance probabilities proportional to row/column squared norms (or entrywise squares) is standard and the resulting bounds (e.g., L̄ ≤ ||K||_F/√(λγ) under factored sampling) are reasonable and match known inequalities among operator, Frobenius, and max norms.

Overall the theoretical development is sound, with a few caveats: (1) Forward-backward with a general Lipschitz monotone B does not converge in general; the paper acknowledges this and restricts to strongly convex-concave settings for linear convergence, citing appropriate references. (2) Some complexity claims are asymptotic and hide constants and log factors; the “always superior to accelerated batch” statement for the accelerated SVRG warrants more careful qualification (e.g., dependency on τ, accuracy of inner solves, prox cost). (3) The analysis for non-uniform SAGA requires resampling updated components; the practical variant without resampling is used in experiments but not covered by the bound.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized. The operator-norm scaling with Ω/Ω* is clearly motivated and necessary in practice. The two decomposition regimes (individual vs factored) and their storage/complexity trade-offs are crisply described. That said, several aspects could be clarified:
- The linear convergence of batch FB in the strongly convex-concave setting is unusual to many readers; offering intuition or a short sketch in the main text (not only in the appendix) would help.
- The presentation of Algorithm 2 (SAGA) with the optional resampling step and its necessity for the non-uniform sampling bound should be highlighted more prominently.
- Practical guidance for choosing p and q in factored sampling and for mini-batch size m would help reproducibility.

Originality
The paper’s main novelty is adapting SVRG/SAGA to general saddle-point/monotone inclusions without relying on co-coercivity, allowing operator-level decompositions that are not gradients of functions. The accelerated scheme in the monotone operator setting with a simple proximal point view is also a nice twist. To my knowledge (circa the cited timeframe), linearly convergent VR methods for non-separable saddle-point problems with these general splits and the monotone inclusion analysis were not available; SPDC and related primal-dual methods require separability. The non-uniform sampling analysis tailored to saddle-point factorizations is also original and useful.

Significance and empirical evaluation
This work addresses an important gap: large-scale saddle-point problems with non-separable losses/regularizers and efficient prox. The theoretical results are general and likely to influence subsequent research on VR for variational inequalities and game-theoretic problems.

The experiments demonstrate:
- Gains of non-uniform sampling over uniform.
- Gains of SVRG/SAGA over batch FB/accelerated FB, with larger margins on ill-conditioned problems.
- Applicability to non-separable regularizers/losses with efficient prox.

However, the empirical section is limited:
- Only two datasets and two problem classes; no tests on truly non-bilinear K or on variational inequality tasks.
- No comparison to extragradient/Mirror-Prox style methods (deterministic or stochastic), or to SPDC on separable cases, and no wall-clock time vs. passes trade-offs.
- Memory/time costs for SAGA with individual splits (O(nd) storage) are substantial; the factored variant alleviates this only in specific models.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and general extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points and monotone inclusions.
- Solid linear convergence analysis without co-coercivity; careful treatment of sampling and decomposition structures.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal regularization; clear complexity improvements.
- Practical insights on importance sampling and factored splits; experiments with non-separable prox.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow; missing comparisons to extragradient/Mirror-Prox and SPDC baselines where applicable; no wall-clock results.
- Some claims (e.g., “always superior”) are a bit strong; dependence on constants and prox cost should be qualified.
- SAGA’s storage can be prohibitive except in factored settings; this practical limitation should be emphasized.
- Requires knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants; no adaptive step-size/regularization schemes are provided.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you include comparisons to extragradient/Mirror-Prox variants (deterministic and stochastic), and to SPDC on separable problems?
- Provide wall-clock plots and report memory consumption for SAGA in both splits. How does sparsity affect T(B) and T(B_i)?
- Clarify the linear convergence of batch FB in the main text and discuss why extragradient is not needed here.
- Discuss sampling beyond squared-norms, e.g., leverage-score-based sampling, and whether it can further reduce L̄.
- Comment on adaptivity to unknown λ, γ (e.g., via line-search or doubling), and on robustness when strong convexity is small or only one side is strongly convex.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper offers substantial theoretical and algorithmic advances on an important class of problems, with clear potential impact. Strengthen the empirical section and temper a few claims.
