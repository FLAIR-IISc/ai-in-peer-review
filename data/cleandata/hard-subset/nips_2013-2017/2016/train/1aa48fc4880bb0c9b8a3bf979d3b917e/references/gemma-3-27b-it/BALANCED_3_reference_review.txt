This paper presents stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems, extending SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The motivation stems from the prevalence of saddle-point formulations in machine learning, particularly when dealing with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors provide convergence analysis, identify key properties of the saddle-point formulation (splits, non-uniform sampling), and demonstrate acceleration techniques. The work builds on previous NIPS contributions in stochastic optimization (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) and extends them to a different, but related, problem structure. The connection to monotone operator theory is a valuable generalization.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying stochastic variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems is a valuable extension of existing work. The analysis and adaptations needed for this new setting constitute a clear contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a convergence analysis for the proposed algorithms, which is a crucial aspect for any optimization method. The connection to monotone operator theory strengthens the theoretical foundation.
* **Insights into Saddle-Point Structure:** The authors correctly identify the importance of different types of splits (function-wise vs. derivative-wise) and the necessity of non-uniform sampling for efficiency.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application examples in machine learning (supervised learning with non-separable terms, robust optimization) highlight the potential impact of this work.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms compared to existing methods, particularly in ill-conditioned scenarios. The comparison against primal methods is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from motivation to analysis to experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of strong convexity-concavity (Assumption A) is relatively strong and might not hold in all practical scenarios. Exploring weaker assumptions, or techniques to handle non-strongly convex-concave cases, would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of parameters (step sizes, sampling probabilities) can be crucial for performance. While the paper provides guidance, a more in-depth discussion of parameter sensitivity and practical tuning strategies would be helpful.
* **Mini-batch Size:** The impact of mini-batch size *m* isn’t explored thoroughly enough in the experiments. The analysis suggests a trade-off with the complexity of the proximal operator, but more empirical investigation would be valuable.
* **Catalyst Acceleration:** While the catalyst acceleration is presented, the rationale behind the specific choice of the parameter τ could be explained more clearly. The connection to the condition number and problem structure is not fully intuitive.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other saddle-point solving methods is somewhat limited. Including more state-of-the-art alternatives (beyond the basic forward-backward variants) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation can be dense, particularly in Section 2.2, making it harder to follow the derivation of complexities.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization with clear connections to machine learning applications.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the algorithms.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in practice.
* The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the work.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The strong convexity-concavity assumption might limit the applicability of the methods.
* The discussion of parameter tuning and mini-batch size could be more comprehensive.
* The comparison to alternative saddle-point solvers is somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is a novel and valuable contribution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Relax Assumptions:** Investigate the performance of the algorithms under weaker convexity/concavity assumptions.
* **Parameter Tuning Guide:** Provide a more detailed guide for parameter tuning, including sensitivity analysis.
* **Expand Experiments:**  Explore the impact of different mini-batch sizes and compare against a wider range of saddle-point solvers.
* **Clarify Catalyst Acceleration:**  Provide a more intuitive explanation for the choice of the acceleration parameter τ.
* **Improve Notation:**  Simplify the notation where possible to enhance readability.
