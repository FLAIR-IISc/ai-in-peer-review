- Applies SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and provides first convergence analysis showing linear convergence under suitable assumptions.
- Emphasizes importance of separable problem structure and analyzes different splitting strategies.
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for practical performance.
- Proposes a catalyst-inspired acceleration that outperforms accelerated batch methods.
- Extends results beyond saddle-point problems to monotone operator theory.
- Builds on prior SVRG/SAGA work and situates contribution relative to batch saddle-point methods and monotone operator literature.
- Connects sampling strategies to sketching techniques for insight into sampling design.
- Novel application and theoretical extension of variance-reduction methods to a broader problem class.
- Strong theoretical rigor with convergence guarantees and avoidance of co-coercivity assumptions.
- Highlights practical relevance across supervised learning, robust optimization, and convex relaxations.
- Provides detailed analysis of function- vs. derivative-based splitting and uniform vs. non-uniform sampling.
- Acceleration technique and experiments demonstrate empirical advantages, especially for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling.
- Assumptions (A)–(C) are standard but potentially hard to verify in practice; sensitivity to violations is not explored.
- Notation is dense (multiple norms and splitting conventions), which hampers readability.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and could benefit from more datasets and scenarios.
- Algorithms depend on efficient proximal-oracle computation, and the paper lacks thorough discussion of expensive proximal cases.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, novelty, clear core ideas, and broad practical impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: possibly restrictive assumptions and complex presentation.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant contribution and suitable for NIPS; recommends acceptance (Strong Accept, 8/10).
- Recommendations: analyze sensitivity to assumption violations, simplify notation or add glossary, expand experiments, and discuss proximal operator computational costs.
