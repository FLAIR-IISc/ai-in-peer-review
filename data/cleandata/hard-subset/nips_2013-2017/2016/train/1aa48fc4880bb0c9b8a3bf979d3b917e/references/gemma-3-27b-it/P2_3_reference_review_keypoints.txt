- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and provides linear convergence analysis under certain assumptions.  
- Emphasizes importance of problem splitting choices and non-uniform sampling for algorithmic efficiency.  
- Introduces a catalyst-based acceleration technique that improves convergence over batch methods.  
- Frames analysis using monotone operator theory, broadening applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Empirical results on benchmark datasets support theoretical claims and show practical effectiveness.  
- Novel contribution: adapting variance-reduction methods to saddle-point (non-convex/concave) settings.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides convergence proofs and an elegant connection to monotone operator theory.  
- Practical relevance: addresses common ML formulations (e.g., non-separable losses, robust optimization).  
- Experiments compare favorably to existing methods, including primal counterparts.  
- Presentation is generally clear, with motivations and assumptions explicitly stated.  
- Acceleration via catalyst is a useful practical improvement in convergence speed.  
- Analysis depends on strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz assumptions, which may be restrictive.  
- Algorithm performance is sensitive to parameter choices (step sizes, sampling probabilities).  
- Notation is dense and sometimes hard to follow due to multiple norms and many indices.  
- Experimental scope is limited to specific datasets and settings; broader evaluation would strengthen claims.  
- Computational cost discussion is limited—memory and cost differences for different splits (e.g., SAGA) are not fully addressed.  
- Overall assessment: high quality, original, significant, and well-written work.  
- Primary arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically validated, and impactful.  
- Primary arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions and complex notation.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong Accept.
