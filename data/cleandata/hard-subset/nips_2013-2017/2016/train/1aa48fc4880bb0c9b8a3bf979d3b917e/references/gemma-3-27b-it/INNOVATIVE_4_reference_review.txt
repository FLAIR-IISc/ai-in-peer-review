This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to the challenging setting of saddle-point problems. The core idea – adapting these methods to leverage separable structure in the saddle-point formulation – is novel and potentially impactful, particularly given the prevalence of saddle-point formulations in machine learning (e.g., dual formulations, robust optimization). The authors successfully provide convergence analysis, extending beyond standard convex settings to monotone operators, which demonstrates a broader applicability. The identification of non-uniform sampling as crucial and the acceleration framework based on "catalyst" further strengthens the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a genuinely novel contribution. While variance reduction methods are well-established for convex problems, their adaptation to non-convex/non-strongly-convex saddle-point settings requires non-trivial extension and analysis.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis appears solid, building on the interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators. This allows for a broader theoretical foundation and applicability.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The applicability to problems common in machine learning (robust optimization, structured prediction, etc.) makes the work highly relevant. The examples provided are compelling.
*   **Insights into Sampling:** Demonstrating the importance of non-uniform sampling in the saddle-point context is a valuable insight.
*   **Acceleration Framework:** The extension of the catalyst framework for accelerated convergence is a strong addition, showing a clear path to improved performance.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is reasonably well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Validation is Limited:** While experiments are included, they are somewhat limited in scope. The comparison primarily focuses on a specific set of problems, and the empirical gains aren’t always dramatically pronounced. More comprehensive experiments on a wider variety of saddle-point problems would greatly strengthen the paper.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The practical performance seems sensitive to the choice of regularization parameters (λ). The discussion of how to select these parameters in a principled way could be more detailed.
*   **Implementation Details:** The paper would benefit from more details regarding the implementation, particularly regarding the factored splits and the practical aspects of non-uniform sampling. The reliance on Appendix F for some experimental details is a minor inconvenience.
*    **Overly Strong Claims:** Phrases like "always superior to accelerated batch algorithms" (in the contributions section) could be toned down. The experimental evidence doesn’t definitively support *always* being superior, and the theoretical guarantee likely holds under specific conditions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The work tackles a fundamentally important problem with a genuinely novel approach.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and extends the applicability of SVRG/SAGA to a broader class of problems.
*   The insights into sampling strategies and acceleration techniques are valuable contributions.
*   The paper has the potential to influence future research in optimization for machine learning.
*   The paper is well-written and clear, despite a few minor organizational issues.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is not as compelling as it could be.
*   The parameter sensitivity and implementation details could be further explored.
*   Some of the claims are potentially overstated.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the experimental validation could be more extensive, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of this work are substantial. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution, and the accompanying analysis and insights are valuable. The minor weaknesses are outweighed by the strengths, and I believe this paper will be of great interest to the NIPS community. I value bold approaches and significant conceptual advances, and this paper delivers on both fronts. The potential for this work to spur further research in optimization for machine learning is high.
