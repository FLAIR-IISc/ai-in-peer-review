

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which arise naturally in machine learning through duality (e.g., supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers). The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, demonstrating that they can achieve linear convergence rates with appropriate sampling strategies. The key insight is that saddle-point problems require different treatment than convex minimization due to their inherent structure (convex-concave nature), which affects the sampling strategies and convergence analysis. The authors also extend their framework to monotone operators and accelerate their methods using the "catalyst" framework. This work builds on prior stochastic optimization methods (SAGA, SVRG [1, 2, 3]) but extends them to a fundamentally different class of problems with significant theoretical and practical implications.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper provides the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems, which differs significantly from convex minimization. The authors correctly identify that saddle-point problems require different handling due to their convex-concave structure, and they properly leverage monotone operator theory for their analysis (Section 6). This is a significant theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature.

2. **Clear Distinction from Prior Work**: The paper effectively distinguishes saddle-point problems from convex minimization, particularly regarding the need for different split structures (functions vs. partial derivatives) and the importance of non-uniform sampling. This is a crucial insight that previous works on stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems (e.g., [11, 12]) did not fully address.

3. **Strong Practical Motivation**: The authors provide compelling examples of machine learning problems where non-separable losses or regularizers make existing stochastic methods inapplicable (Section 2.1), demonstrating the practical necessity of their approach.

4. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation**: The experiments on non-separable learning problems (Section 7) clearly show that the proposed methods outperform batch algorithms and uniform sampling variants, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The comparison with primal methods on separable problems is particularly convincing.

5. **Extension to Monotone Operators**: The extension of the framework to monotone operators (Section 6) is well-executed and demonstrates the broader applicability of the authors' approach beyond saddle-point problems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Practical Implementation Details**: While the paper theoretically justifies non-uniform sampling, it doesn't provide practical guidance for choosing sampling probabilities πi for general saddle-point problems beyond the bilinear case. The dependence on the Lipschitz constants and strong convexity parameters (λ, γ) may be difficult to estimate in practice.

2. **Comparison with Recent Saddle-Point Methods**: The paper could better position itself against other recent work on stochastic saddle-point optimization (e.g., [22, 29]), particularly regarding their assumptions and convergence guarantees. The connection to the Arrow-Hurwicz method (Section 2.2) is mentioned but could be more thoroughly contextualized.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experimental section focuses on a single non-separable example (ROC curve surrogate loss). While the experiments are well-designed for their specific example, additional experiments on other non-separable problems (e.g., robust optimization, structured output prediction) would strengthen the claims.

4. **Accelerated Method Justification**: The acceleration via the catalyst framework is theoretically sound, but the paper doesn't clearly explain why their approach (which avoids Nesterov acceleration machinery) is superior to other acceleration techniques for saddle-point problems.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The extension of variance-reduced methods to saddle-point problems is both novel and well-motivated, with clear theoretical justification and empirical validation. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and provides valuable insights for both theory and practice.

The main weakness is the lack of detailed guidance for practical implementation (sampling probabilities), which could limit the immediate applicability of the method. However, this is a common issue in theoretical papers and could be addressed in future work. The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and represent a meaningful advance over prior work.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the field of stochastic optimization with well-motivated theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The distinction between saddle-point problems and convex minimization is crucial, and the authors successfully extend variance reduction techniques to this important class of problems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 2.4, provide more concrete guidance for choosing sampling probabilities πi in non-bilinear saddle-point problems, perhaps with a brief discussion of how to estimate L̄(π) in practice.

2. In Section 2.2, more thoroughly compare with recent saddle-point optimization methods (e.g., [29]) to better position the paper's contributions.

3. In Section 7, consider adding a second non-separable problem to strengthen the empirical claims.

4. In Section 5, briefly explain why the authors' acceleration approach avoids the need for Nesterov machinery, as this is a key advantage over other methods.

The paper is already strong, but these minor improvements would make it even more compelling. The theoretical framework and experimental results are sufficiently robust to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
