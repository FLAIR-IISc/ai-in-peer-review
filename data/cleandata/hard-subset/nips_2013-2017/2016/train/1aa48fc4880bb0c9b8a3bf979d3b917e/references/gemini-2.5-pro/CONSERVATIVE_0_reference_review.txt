This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, specifically extensions of SVRG and SAGA, for solving strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The primary motivation is to handle machine learning problems with non-separable loss functions or regularizers, where standard SVR techniques cannot be applied directly to the primal or dual formulations. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing linear convergence for these methods, leveraging the framework of monotone operators. They investigate different sampling strategies, highlighting the importance of non-uniform sampling for performance. Furthermore, they introduce an accelerated variant using the Catalyst framework, which theoretically and empirically outperforms accelerated batch algorithms. The experimental results on binary classification tasks with non-separable objectives demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods, particularly the accelerated version.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper is technically solid. It builds upon the well-established and successful frameworks of SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst acceleration. The extension to the saddle-point setting is methodologically sound, and the provided convergence analysis, grounded in the theory of monotone operators, is rigorous and appropriate for this problem class.

2.  **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The work tackles an important gap in the optimization literature for machine learning. While SVR methods are state-of-the-art for finite-sum convex problems, their application to problems that are only naturally separable in a saddle-point formulation (e.g., with non-separable regularizers like total variation or certain structured-output losses) has not been rigorously established. This paper provides the first linearly-convergent SVR algorithms for this setting.

3.  **Thorough Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a complete theoretical picture, including convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA, an analysis of the impact of non-uniform sampling, and a formal acceleration scheme. The resulting complexity bounds clearly demonstrate the theoretical advantage over existing batch and standard stochastic algorithms for this problem class.

4.  **Clear Empirical Validation of Key Claims:** The experiments effectively support the main theoretical claims. In particular, they clearly show that (a) non-uniform sampling is critical for SAGA/SVRG to outperform batch methods, (b) the proposed accelerated SVRG method achieves the fastest convergence, especially on more ill-conditioned problems, and (c) for problems where primal methods are applicable, they remain strong competitors, which correctly positions the contribution of this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The core algorithmic ideas are direct extensions of existing methods. As the authors state, the "algorithmic extension is straightforward." The SVRG and SAGA updates for the saddle-point operator are the natural analogues of their original counterparts. Similarly, the application of the Catalyst framework follows directly from its original presentation. The main contribution is therefore analytical rather than algorithmic.

2.  **Overstated Claims on Analysis Novelty:** The paper claims the analysis "differs significantly from the associated convex minimization set-up." While the use of monotone operator theory is the correct framework, the core proof techniques for variance-reduced methods typically rely on constructing a potential function that decreases in expectation at each step. It is not sufficiently clear how the analysis here fundamentally departs from an adaptation of that style of proof to the monotone operator setting, which itself is a standard generalization.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Breadth:** While the experiments are well-executed, they are confined to a specific type of AUC-like loss and a clustering-inducing regularizer on two classification datasets. The introduction motivates the work with a broader set of applications, including robust optimization and convex relaxations for unsupervised learning. The empirical validation would be much more compelling if it demonstrated the effectiveness of the proposed methods on at least one other distinct problem type to better support the claims of general applicability.

4.  **Practicality of Non-Accelerated Variants:** The experiments show that without acceleration, the performance gain of SVRG/SAGA over the accelerated batch method can be modest (e.g., Figure 1, left plot). This suggests that the primary practical benefit comes from the combination of variance reduction and acceleration. While theoretically important, the stand-alone SVRG/SAGA extensions may not be a compelling replacement for existing strong batch methods in all practical scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous analysis of SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems, filling an important theoretical gap.
*   The work is methodologically sound and builds on a strong foundation of established optimization techniques.
*   It addresses a practical need for efficient optimization of models with non-separable components.
*   The accelerated method is shown to be state-of-the-art for this problem class, which is a significant contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The algorithmic contribution is incremental, consisting of a straightforward application of existing ideas to a new problem setting.
*   The experimental validation, though positive, is narrow and does not fully substantiate the broad applicability of the methods.
*   The practical benefit of the non-accelerated variants over strong, established baselines appears limited in some cases.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a methodologically sound and rigorous extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. Its main value lies in the thorough theoretical analysis and the demonstration that an accelerated variant can achieve state-of-the-art performance. The primary weakness is the limited novelty of the algorithms themselves and the narrow scope of the experimental validation. Nevertheless, the paper makes a solid and useful contribution to the optimization toolbox for machine learning and is a good fit for NIPS.
