This paper presents a significant and timely contribution by extending stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The core idea is to leverage the finite-sum structure of the smooth part of the saddle-point objective, `K(x, y)`, to achieve linear convergence rates, mirroring the success of these techniques in the standard convex minimization setting. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in this more general context, introduce an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework, and demonstrate the practical benefits of non-uniform sampling.

### Strengths:

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the optimization literature. While variance reduction for finite-sum minimization is a well-established field, its application to saddle-point problems was underdeveloped. Given the prevalence of saddle-point formulations in machine learning (e.g., duality, robust optimization, generative adversarial networks), developing fast, linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems is of great importance. This work provides a foundational theoretical and algorithmic contribution that future research will likely build upon.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The analysis is the main strength of the paper. Extending the proofs of SVRG/SAGA to the saddle-point setting is non-trivial. The key insight of framing the problem in the more general context of finding zeros of a sum of monotone operators is elegant and powerful. This approach not only facilitates the convergence proof by relying only on monotonicity (and not co-coercivity, which fails for saddle-point operators) but also immediately broadens the applicability of the proposed methods to variational inequalities. The careful derivation of convergence rates and computational complexities, summarized effectively in Table 1, clearly delineates the theoretical advantages over existing batch and stochastic methods.

3.  **Comprehensive and Well-Executed Work:** The paper is thorough in its treatment of the subject. It considers both SVRG and SAGA, discusses practical aspects like factored vs. individual splits and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling, and provides an acceleration mechanism. The inclusion of the "Catalyst" style acceleration is a valuable addition, showing that the proposed methods can be systematically improved to achieve near-optimal rates.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined with precise assumptions (A-C). The connections to machine learning applications are well-motivated in Section 2.1, particularly for non-separable losses and regularizers, which are cases where these new methods are uniquely applicable among fast gradient techniques. The logical progression from batch to stochastic to variance-reduced and finally to accelerated methods makes the paper's narrative compelling.

5.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively support the theoretical claims. The choice of non-separable regularizers and losses (clustering-inducing penalty and AUC surrogate) is excellent, as it showcases a domain where standard primal SAGA/SVRG cannot be applied. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, significantly outperform batch and standard stochastic algorithms, especially in ill-conditioned settings. The comparison with uniform sampling for SAGA also validates the theoretical importance of the sampling scheme.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:

1.  **Gap between SAGA Theory and Practice:** The paper notes that the theoretical guarantee for SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step, but the experiments use a mixture of uniform and non-uniform sampling without resampling, following prior work. While this is a common practice, it creates a slight disconnect between the proven theory and the implemented algorithm. It would be beneficial to briefly comment on the potential reasons why this heuristic works well or acknowledge it as a minor theoretical gap.

2.  **Knowledge of Problem Parameters:** The proposed algorithms and their analyses rely on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). In many practical applications, these parameters are unknown or difficult to estimate. While the authors rightly point to adaptive methods as a direction for future work in the conclusion, this remains a practical limitation of the current methods.

3.  **Acceleration for SAGA:** The acceleration framework is formally developed and proven only for SVRG. The paper states that "the same scheme should work for SAGA," but this is not formally established. While plausible, a more detailed discussion or even a brief proof sketch in the appendix would strengthen this claim.

### Detailed Comments and Questions:

*   **Factored Splits for SAGA:** The discussion on SAGA for factored splits is brief. Given the significant practical implications for storage (O(n+d) vs. O(nd)), it would be valuable to elaborate slightly more on how the table of stored gradients `gi` and their aggregate `G` are managed in this case. Is `G` maintained as two separate vectors, one for the x-component and one for the y-component?

*   **Monotone Operator Analysis:** In Section 6, the authors astutely observe that their analysis, when applied to standard convex minimization, would yield slower rates than specialized analyses that leverage co-coercivity. This is an important point of honesty. Could the authors briefly elaborate on precisely where the analysis is looser? Is it that the lack of co-coercivity forces a more conservative step-size `σ` that is too small for the purely convex case?

### Conclusion:

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to optimization for machine learning. It successfully bridges the gap between variance-reduction techniques and saddle-point optimization, providing the first linear convergence guarantees for SVRG and SAGA in this setting. The theoretical analysis is novel and general, the algorithmic contributions are practical and well-motivated, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and highly significant. It is a strong candidate for acceptance and will likely be influential.
