- Problem and contributions: Extends SVRG and SAGA to finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems with linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity; introduces importance sampling and two decomposition types; proposes a Catalyst-like acceleration; and extends analysis to strongly monotone inclusions with one resolvent and Lipschitz operators.  
- Relation to prior work: Places work relative to batch primal-dual methods, stochastic forward-backward, variance-reduced primal methods, and stochastic extragradient methods, arguing novelty in bringing variance reduction with linear rates to non-separable saddle problems and non-cocoercive monotone inclusions.  
- Algorithmic framework: Adapts forward-backward splitting in a weighted norm and uses SVRG/SAGA-style estimators; motivates factored operator splits for bilinear K to reduce storage/runtime versus element-wise splits.  
- Convergence for batch FB: Shows linear convergence for batch forward-backward with step σ ≈ 1/L^2 in the chosen scaling, consistent with non-expansiveness plus strong monotonicity.  
- SVRG/SAGA analyses: Proves linear rates with explicit complexities depending on L and a sampling-dependent L̄(π), quantifies mini-batch trade-offs, and notes resampling requirements for non-uniform sampling.  
- Importance sampling: Develops practical sampling distributions and L̄(π) bounds for bilinear K under element-wise and factored splits (row/column and entrywise strategies).  
- Acceleration: Uses a proximal-point/Catalyst scheme to accelerate convergence by solving a sequence of regularized problems, improving complexity when full-gradient cost is cheaper than component costs.  
- Monotone operator extension: Broadens analysis to strongly monotone inclusions A+B with A having a resolvent and B Lipschitz (not requiring cocoercivity), extending beyond standard convex minimization.  
- Conceptual novelty: Introduces variance reduction to general non-separable saddle-point problems and a monotone-operator viewpoint, which is novel and impactful.  
- Sampling and decomposition treatment: Carefully distinguishes functional vs operator splits and provides non-uniform sampling analysis that is practically relevant for bilinear forms.  
- Acceleration elegance: The proximal-point/Catalyst adaptation is simpler than classical acceleration and provides transparent trade-offs.  
- Theory-to-practice link: Connects theoretical constants to matrix norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) for interpretable complexities.  
- Experiments: Show consistent empirical gains for accelerated SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling on non-separable problems, and highlight cases where uniform sampling underperforms.  
- Experimental limitations: Only two datasets with limited ablations; missing comparisons to stochastic Mirror-Prox/extragradient, SPDC, and modern PDHG baselines.  
- Practicality of constants: L and especially L̄(π) can be expensive or impractical to compute for nonlinear K; paper lacks adaptive estimation guidance.  
- Step-size and notation clarity: Notation inconsistencies (L2 vs L^2), weighted-norm role, and counterintuitive σ=1/L^2 choice need clearer exposition and intuition in the main text.  
- Assumptions on M and parameters: Requires easy prox for M and known strong-convexity/concavity parameters; lacks discussion of adaptivity to unknown parameters (backtracking/restarts).  
- Nonlinear K and acceleration: Acceleration is fully detailed mainly for SVRG and bilinear K; extensions and conditions for nonlinear K are insufficiently explicit.  
- Monotone experiments missing: No experiments are provided for non-saddle monotone inclusions/variational inequalities despite theoretical claims.  
- Clarity and presentation: Generally well-written but dense, some typos and notation issues; suggests adding a compact main-text pseudocode and parameter-selection heuristics.  
- Reproducibility suggestions: Recommend adding stochastic Mirror-Prox/PDHG/SPDC baselines, reporting wall-clock and memory, clarifying resampling role, expanding nonlinear-K statements, and releasing code and prox implementations.  
- Pros summary: Strong general theory with linear rates, effective importance sampling and factorization, practical acceleration, and relevant monotone-operator extension.  
- Cons summary: Limited experimental breadth and baselines, reliance on hard-to-estimate constants and limited tuning guidance, and some clarity/notation issues.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept to accept; theoretical and algorithmic advances outweigh experimental limitations, with high reviewer confidence.
