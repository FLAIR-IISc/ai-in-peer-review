This paper presents an extension of stochastic variance reduction gradient methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of composite decomposable saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is a smooth convex-concave function whose gradient operator can be expressed as a sum of components, and `M` is a strongly convex-concave function with a simple proximal operator. This formulation is motivated by machine learning applications involving non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation), which cannot be directly handled by existing fast incremental methods that require separability in the primal or dual objectives.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The adaptation of SVRG and SAGA algorithms to the saddle-point structure.
2.  A novel convergence analysis that establishes linear convergence rates for these algorithms. Crucially, the analysis is framed in the more general context of finding zeros of monotone operators and does not rely on co-coercivity, a property assumed in some prior work on variance reduction for monotone inclusions but which does not hold for the saddle-point operator.
3.  An investigation of different decomposition ("split") strategies and sampling distributions, highlighting that non-uniform sampling is key to achieving the best theoretical rates and practical performance.
4.  An accelerated version of the SVRG algorithm based on the Catalyst framework, which yields a theoretically superior algorithm to accelerated batch methods.

The paper positions these contributions against existing batch algorithms (Forward-Backward and its accelerated variants) and standard stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems, which exhibit sublinear convergence. The work directly extends the line of research on variance reduction for finite sums, pioneered by SAG, SVRG, and SAGA, into the important domain of saddle-point optimization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The primary theoretical contribution—the convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems—is a significant achievement. The choice to analyze the problem through the lens of monotone operators is insightful, providing a general proof framework that correctly handles the problem structure without restrictive assumptions like co-coercivity. The derived convergence rates are clearly presented and compared, demonstrating a theoretical advantage over existing methods. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, with proofs provided in the appendix.
*   **Originality:** This work appears to be the first to establish linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this general class of composite saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic templates are extensions of existing methods, the application and, more importantly, the analysis in this new domain are highly original. The generalization of the proof technique beyond the co-coercive setting distinguishes this work from prior attempts to extend variance reduction to monotone operators and substantially broadens the applicability of these powerful methods.
*   **Significance:** The results are of high significance to the machine learning and optimization communities. The paper provides practitioners with efficient, state-of-the-art algorithms for a class of problems (e.g., with non-separable objectives) that is of growing importance but previously lacked dedicated fast incremental solvers. The Catalyst-based acceleration further solidifies the advantage of these stochastic methods. The framework is general and likely to inspire further research into variance reduction for other structured optimization problems beyond standard convex minimization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It begins with a strong motivation, clearly defines the problem setting and assumptions, and presents the algorithms in an accessible manner. Table 1 provides an excellent and comprehensive summary of the computational complexities of all discussed algorithms, making the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods easy to appreciate. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented clearly, effectively demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** The analysis relies on the strong convexity-concavity of the function `M`. This assumption is necessary for the linear convergence results but may not hold in all applications. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion as an area for future work, the current paper's applicability is restricted to this setting.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The proposed algorithms depend on several parameters, including step sizes and smoothness constants (L, L̄). The theoretical rates depend on knowledge of these constants, which can be difficult to estimate in practice. A discussion of the practical challenges of parameter tuning or potential adaptive strategies would strengthen the paper's practical impact.
*   **Theory-Practice Gap in SAGA Experiments:** The paper notes that the theoretical analysis for SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step to be valid. However, the experiments employ a heuristic mixture of sampling schemes without resampling. While this is a common practical approach, it creates a minor disconnect between the theory and the empirical validation. Including an experiment with the theoretically justified resampling scheme would be beneficial for completeness.

In summary, the paper makes a novel and significant contribution by successfully extending variance-reduced stochastic methods to a broad class of saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is a highlight, being both rigorous and general. The work is well-motivated by practical machine learning problems and the empirical results convincingly support the theoretical claims. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the contribution.
