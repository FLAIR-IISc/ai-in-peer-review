Summary
The paper proposes stochastic variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for general convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), with M proximable and strongly convex–concave, and K smooth. The key idea is to treat the smooth part via a finite-sum decomposition of the saddle operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK), not necessarily as gradients of component functions Ki, thereby enabling variance reduction without separability of the original loss or regularizer. The authors analyze linear convergence (under strong convex–concavity) using only monotonicity (not co-coercivity), extend the analysis to general monotone inclusions A + ∑ Bi (with A maximal strongly monotone and only B summed monotone), develop non-uniform sampling with tight problem-dependent constants L̄(π), introduce “factored” splits tailored to bilinear problems that reduce storage, and propose a simple proximal-point/catalyst-style acceleration whose complexity interpolates between batch and stochastic passes and, in their bounds, dominates accelerated batch methods on ill-conditioned problems. Experiments on supervised learning with non-separable losses/regularizers demonstrate the practical advantages of non-uniform sampling and of the accelerated SVRG variant, especially in ill-conditioned regimes.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization, PDHG/Chambolle–Pock for saddle points, and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods for separable ERM (e.g., SPDC/AdaSPDC). Unlike those, it: (i) handles non-separable losses or regularizers while retaining linear rates via variance reduction; (ii) allows splitting at the operator level (Bi need not be gradients), which broadens applicability; and (iii) provides an analysis rooted only in monotonicity rather than co-coercivity, enabling an extension to general monotone inclusions/variational inequalities. The catalyst-style acceleration is adapted to the saddle setting via a proximal-point view, avoiding Nesterov machinery. The connection to SMART and other stochastic operator-splitting works is discussed; authors argue those require co-coercivity (worth clarifying precisely).

Strengths
- Novel conceptual advance: variance reduction for saddle problems without separability by splitting B, plus a clean monotone-operator analysis. This is a meaningful new direction beyond “minimization-as-sum.”
- Generality: covers non-separable losses/regularizers with available prox; extends beyond saddle points to monotone inclusions/variational inequalities.
- Thoughtful algorithmic design: factored vs individual splits; non-uniform sampling with tight L̄(π) bounds for bilinear problems; mini-batching to trade off prox cost; memory-efficient SAGA for factored splits.
- Theory: linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA under strong convex–concavity with explicit complexities; O(1/t) for stochastic FB; principled acceleration via proximal-point iterations with a clear complexity tradeoff in τ.
- Practical insights: non-uniform sampling is consistently beneficial; accelerated SVRG shines in ill-conditioned regimes; clear guidance on when storage-efficient factored SAGA is preferable.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical scope: Only two datasets and primarily bilinear settings; no wall-clock time, ablations on m, or sensitivity to step sizes. Comparisons against strong stochastic primal–dual baselines (SPDC/AdaSPDC/ASVRG-PD for separable cases) are absent; even if out-of-scope for non-separable objectives, these would contextualize performance on separable instances (rcv1).
- Assumptions/tuning: Strong convex–concavity and knowledge of λ, γ, L, L̄ are required; adaptivity is deferred. In practice, computing/estimating L̄(π) and setting π may be nontrivial beyond bilinear K; more guidance would help.
- Storage: SAGA requires O(nd) memory for individual splits; the factored O(n+d) alternative is compelling for bilinear K but unclear for general non-bilinear K—some discussion of broader storage-light strategies would be useful.
- Theory/practice gap: The non-uniform SAGA analysis requires resampling after the iterate update, but experiments use a heuristic mixture without resampling; a brief empirical justification or robustness study would strengthen the claims.
- Claims vs SMART/other operator methods: Please sharpen the comparison—precisely which assumptions (co-coercivity vs monotonicity) differ, and where their stepsizes would diverge in your setting.

Quality
The technical development appears sound, with appropriately scoped assumptions and clear complexity statements. The acceleration via proximal-point is elegant. Some proofs are deferred (not included here), but the arguments are plausible and consistent with operator splitting theory.

Clarity
The paper is dense but reasonably well organized. The operator viewpoint, norms with λ,γ scaling, and sampling constants are well motivated. Algorithm boxes help. Suggestions:
- Consolidate the bilinear case (individual vs factored) into a compact table early to guide the reader.
- Clarify how to choose π and estimate L̄(π) in non-bilinear settings.
- Temper absolute claims like “always superior to accelerated batch” with the specific regime assumptions.

Originality
High. The operator-level variance reduction for saddle points (without separability), monotone-operator analysis eschewing co-coercivity, and the factored split with non-uniform sampling are novel and impactful.

Significance
High. Many important ML problems involve non-separable losses/regularizers and/or constraints; bringing variance reduction with linear rates and acceleration to this regime is valuable. The monotone-inclusion extension opens doors beyond ML.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, substantive novelty; broad applicability; strong, clean theory; practically relevant insights; acceleration that is simple and effective.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth and missing comparisons to some strong baselines; reliance on hard-to-know constants; some implementation details deferred.

Recommendation
Accept (8/10). The conceptual contribution is strong and opens a new direction; the weaknesses are addressable and do not undermine the main advance.
