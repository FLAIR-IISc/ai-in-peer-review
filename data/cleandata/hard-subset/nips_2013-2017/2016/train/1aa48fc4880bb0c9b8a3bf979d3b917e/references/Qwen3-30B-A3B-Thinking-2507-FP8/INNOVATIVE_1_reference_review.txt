

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper introduces the first stochastic variance reduction algorithms (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, extending techniques previously developed for convex minimization. The authors demonstrate how these methods can be adapted to saddle-point problems, which are ubiquitous in machine learning applications like robust optimization, non-separable supervised learning, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) providing the first convergence analysis for variance reduction methods on saddle-point problems, (2) showing that saddle-point formulations allow for two distinct types of splits (function-based or derivative-based), (3) demonstrating the critical importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and (4) extending these algorithms with acceleration via the "catalyst" framework.

The paper is particularly notable for its novel theoretical insights. Unlike convex minimization, saddle-point problems require a fundamentally different analysis approach, which the authors address by leveraging monotone operator theory. Their analysis shows that the same variance reduction techniques that work for convex minimization can be extended to saddle-point problems with similar theoretical guarantees, but with important differences in the convergence rates and sampling strategies. The authors also demonstrate that their framework extends beyond saddle-point problems to general monotone operator problems, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

The paper's experimental results on non-separable learning problems with structured regularizers and non-separable losses confirm the theoretical predictions. The authors show that their non-uniform sampling variants of SAGA and SVRG significantly outperform both batch methods and uniform sampling variants, with the accelerated versions providing particularly strong gains on ill-conditioned problems. The comparison with primal methods on separable problems further demonstrates the value of their approach for non-separable cases where primal methods cannot be applied.

This work represents a clear and significant step forward in the theory and practice of optimization for machine learning. While there are existing methods for saddle-point problems (such as batch forward-backward algorithms), the paper establishes the first variance-reduced methods that achieve linear convergence rates for these problems. The extension to monotone operators is particularly impressive, showing that the framework is not limited to saddle-point problems but applies to a broader class of optimization problems.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the claims made. The authors appropriately discuss limitations (such as the need for strong convexity-concavity constants) and future work.

The main potential weakness is that the paper assumes strong convexity-concavity, which is a common assumption but may limit applicability to some problems. However, the authors acknowledge this as a limitation and note that they plan to explore adaptivity in future work, which is a natural direction. The paper also doesn't explore the practical estimation of the strong convexity-concavity constants, but this is a minor issue given the paper's primary focus on establishing the theoretical framework.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be highly compelling. It addresses a significant gap in the literature by extending a well-established class of optimization methods (variance reduction) to a new domain (saddle-point problems) with non-trivial theoretical challenges. The paper demonstrates that the same techniques that revolutionized convex optimization can be successfully applied to saddle-point problems, which opens up new possibilities for large-scale machine learning applications.

The significance of this work is substantial. Saddle-point problems arise in many important machine learning applications, and the proposed methods provide a significant speedup over existing approaches. The paper's extension to monotone operators further broadens its applicability. The combination of theoretical novelty and practical significance makes this a strong candidate for NIPS.

In summary, the paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field by extending variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems with a rigorous theoretical foundation and practical validation. The novelty of the approach, combined with its theoretical soundness and practical significance, makes it a valuable contribution to the optimization literature in machine learning.

**Strengths:**
- First to extend variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems
- Novel theoretical insights regarding convergence analysis for saddle-point problems
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages through experiments
- Extends framework to monotone operators, broadening applicability
- Well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail

**Weaknesses:**
- Assumes strong convexity-concavity constants (though acknowledged as limitation)
- Limited discussion on practical estimation of these constants
- Could potentially include more extensive experimental comparisons

**Recommendation: Accept** - This paper presents a significant theoretical advancement with clear practical implications. The novelty is substantial, and the paper successfully demonstrates the value of extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. The minor limitations do not detract from the overall contribution, which is a clear step forward in optimization for machine learning.
