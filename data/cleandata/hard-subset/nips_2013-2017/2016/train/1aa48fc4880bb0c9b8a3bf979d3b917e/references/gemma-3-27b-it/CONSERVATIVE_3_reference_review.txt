This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis and empirical validation. While the extensions themselves appear straightforward, the authors claim novelty in the convergence analysis adapted to this setting and in demonstrating the importance of non-uniform sampling. The work builds on a solid foundation of existing optimization techniques, aiming to address a common problem in machine learning where duality leads to saddle-point formulations.

However, despite the technical correctness and clear presentation, I find the paper’s contributions incremental and the level of novelty overstated. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems feels natural and almost inevitable, given the existing literature. The convergence analysis, while rigorously presented, largely mirrors the analyses for convex minimization, adapting them to the saddle-point context. The claim of extending beyond saddle-point problems to monotone operators is interesting, but feels like a consequence of the analysis rather than a central contribution of the work itself. The emphasis on non-uniform sampling, while empirically supported, isn’t entirely surprising – similar observations have been made in the context of stochastic optimization. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears correct and well-supported. The authors provide clear assumptions and demonstrate linear convergence.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the extensions of the algorithms are explained intuitively.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on sido and rcv1 datasets provide evidence that the proposed algorithms outperform baseline methods, especially in ill-conditioned settings. The comparison to both primal and dual formulations is insightful.
* **Connection to Monotone Operators:** The extension to monotone operators is a welcome broadening of the scope, indicating potential applicability beyond the initial saddle-point focus.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is not particularly groundbreaking. The theoretical contributions are primarily adaptations of existing results.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claims regarding the significance of the convergence analysis and the importance of non-uniform sampling feel slightly inflated. These aspects are already well-established in related literature.
* **Dependence on Strong Convexity-Concavity:** The analysis relies heavily on strong convexity-concavity assumptions. While common in optimization, it limits the applicability of the algorithms to a specific class of problems. A discussion of robustness to weaker conditions, or at least acknowledging this limitation more prominently, would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-executed, the limited number of datasets and algorithms tested leaves room for further investigation. A comparison to more recent and sophisticated optimization methods would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the practical challenges of implementing these algorithms, such as tuning parameters or handling large-scale datasets efficiently.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-written extension of established optimization techniques to a relevant problem setting.
* The empirical validation supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
* The connection to monotone operators broadens the potential applicability of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The level of novelty is limited, and the claims of significant contributions feel overstated.
* The reliance on strong convexity-concavity assumptions restricts the practical applicability of the algorithms.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the work is technically correct and clearly presented, it lacks the substantial novelty and impact expected of a NIPS publication. The contributions feel incremental, and the claims of significance are not fully justified. The paper would be more suitable for a venue with a lower bar for novelty, such as a specialized workshop or a journal focused on optimization methods. The authors should consider revising the paper to temper the claims of novelty and focus on the specific technical details of the saddle-point adaptation, rather than positioning it as a significant breakthrough. Furthermore, exploring the algorithms’ performance under weaker convexity assumptions or on a wider range of datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.
