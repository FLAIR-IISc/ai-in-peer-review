This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning duality. The core idea—adapting these algorithms to handle the min-max structure—is conceptually straightforward but, crucially, accompanied by the *first* convergence analysis for this setting. This analytical contribution is the paper’s greatest strength. The authors further demonstrate the importance of considering separable structure within the saddle-point formulation, highlighting different notions of ‘splits’ and the benefits of non-uniform sampling. The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of their work considerably.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is novel. The theoretical analysis establishing convergence is a key contribution, filling a gap in the literature. The extension to monotone operators is a valuable generalization.
*   **Significance:** Saddle-point problems are fundamental in machine learning (regularized learning, robust optimization, etc.). Efficient algorithms for these problems have broad applicability.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical derivations appear sound (though detailed verification would require significant effort). The discussion of different ‘splits’ is particularly helpful for understanding the nuances of the approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis, based on interpreting the saddle-point problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator, is a clever approach, providing a robust foundation for the convergence guarantees.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The experimental results, although limited in scope, support the theoretical findings, demonstrating performance gains, particularly with accelerated methods and non-uniform sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited. While they demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, a more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets and problem settings would strengthen the claims. The comparison with primal methods on separable problems is interesting but could be expanded.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on strong convexity-concavity assumptions (A), which may not hold in all practical scenarios. While acknowledging this limitation, exploring the robustness of the algorithms to weaker assumptions would be valuable.
*   **Catalyst Acceleration:** The acceleration technique (Section 5) feels somewhat tacked on. The benefits are stated but not deeply explored or compared to other acceleration strategies.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't provide detailed guidance on hyperparameter selection (step sizes, sampling probabilities), which could be crucial for practical implementation.
* **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** Comparison is mostly to the standard Forward-Backward methods. A comparison against more recent and sophisticated saddle-point solvers would be valuable.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Significant theoretical contribution, filling a gap in the literature.
*   Novel application of SVRG/SAGA to a practically relevant problem.
*   Potential for broad impact on machine learning algorithms.
*   Sound methodology and analysis.

**Con:**

*   Limited experimental validation.
*   Strong assumptions that may limit applicability.
*   Acceleration technique lacks in-depth exploration.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited and the assumptions are strong, the theoretical novelty and potential impact outweigh these concerns. The paper offers a valuable new tool for tackling saddle-point problems, and the extension to monotone operators broadens its applicability. I believe this work meets the NIPS criteria for acceptance. Despite the minor flaws, the boldness of the approach and the strong theoretical foundation justify accepting the paper, even with the caveats mentioned. I rate it as a **Strong Accept**.
