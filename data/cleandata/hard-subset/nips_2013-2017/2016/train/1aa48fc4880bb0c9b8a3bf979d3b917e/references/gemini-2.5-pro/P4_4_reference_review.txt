### Summary

This paper extends the variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization problems, to the setting of composite decomposable saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `M` is a simple, strongly convex-concave function, and `K` is a smooth convex-concave function whose gradient operator can be decomposed into a sum of many components. This structure is common in machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization), which cannot be directly addressed by applying standard SVRG/SAGA to the primal or dual formulations alone.

The main contributions are:
1.  The proposal of SVRG and SAGA algorithms for this saddle-point setting.
2.  The first linear convergence analysis for these methods in this context. The analysis is framed within the theory of monotone operators, which is more general than the co-coercivity arguments used for convex minimization and applies to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.
3.  An exploration of problem-specific aspects like different ways to split the gradient operator ("factored" vs. "individual") and the theoretical and practical benefits of non-uniform sampling.
4.  An acceleration scheme based on the "Catalyst" framework, which further improves the convergence rate and yields an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods under certain conditions.

The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, deriving complexity bounds for each proposed algorithm and comparing them to existing batch and stochastic methods. Experiments on binary classification tasks with non-separable objectives validate the theoretical findings, showing significant practical speed-ups for the proposed variance-reduced methods, especially the accelerated variant.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is of high technical quality. The claims of linear convergence for the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants are supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, with proofs provided in the appendix. A key strength is the use of monotone operator theory for the convergence proof. This approach is more general than the analyses for standard SVRG/SAGA, which often rely on co-coercivity—a property not held by the gradient operators in saddle-point problems. By relying only on monotonicity, the authors provide a sound analysis and also demonstrate that their algorithms and proofs extend beyond saddle-point problems to a wider class of monotone inclusion problems. The experimental results are consistent with the theoretical claims, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the methods. The assumptions (strong convexity-concavity of `M`, Lipschitz continuity of `K`) are standard for proving linear convergence in this domain.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It begins with a strong motivation, effectively explaining why extending variance reduction to the saddle-point formulation is necessary for certain classes of machine learning problems. The problem setup, assumptions, and algorithms are presented formally and precisely. The distinction between different splitting schemes and the importance of non-uniform sampling are well-explained. Table 1 provides an excellent and concise summary of the theoretical complexity results, allowing for easy comparison between the different methods. The connection to monotone operators is also clearly articulated in Section 6, providing valuable context and highlighting the generality of the analysis. The experimental section is clear and supports the paper's main claims.

**Originality:**
The paper's originality is significant. While the proposed algorithms are direct adaptations of SVRG and SAGA, their application to and analysis for the general composite saddle-point problem is novel. Previous work on variance reduction has focused almost exclusively on convex minimization. This paper is the first to provide a rigorous linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point setting. The analytical technique, based on monotone operators rather than co-coercivity, represents a novel theoretical contribution that distinguishes this work from prior analyses of SVRG/SAGA and from other work on stochastic methods for monotone inclusions (e.g., [30]), which assumed co-coercivity. The introduction and analysis of an accelerated variant using the Catalyst framework is also a novel and valuable addition in this context.

**Significance:**
The contributions of this paper are highly significant. It bridges a gap in the literature by making powerful variance-reduction techniques applicable to a broader class of optimization problems that are important in machine learning but do not fit the standard finite-sum minimization template. Problems involving structured-output prediction, AUC maximization, or robust optimization often have non-separable components that are naturally handled by a saddle-point formulation. By providing efficient, linearly convergent algorithms for this setting, the paper offers a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers. The theoretical framework based on monotone operators is also of broader significance, as it provides a unified perspective and potentially opens the door for analyzing other stochastic operator splitting methods. The demonstrated practical performance improvements over established batch and stochastic methods underscore the impact of this work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Intuition on the Analysis:** While the paper correctly states that the analysis differs significantly from the convex minimization case, the main text provides limited intuition on the specific technical challenges encountered and how they are overcome. A short, high-level sketch of a key proof step or a discussion of why standard analysis techniques fail would enhance the paper's self-containedness and impact for readers not delving into the appendix.
2.  **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** The analysis relies on the strong convexity-concavity of the function `M`. While this is necessary for linear convergence, many practical problems are not strongly convex. The authors mention this as future work in the conclusion, but a brief discussion in the main text about the potential behavior of the algorithms without this assumption, or possible routes to extend the analysis (e.g., to achieve sublinear rates), would be beneficial.
3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are well-executed but could be strengthened by including comparisons to other relevant classes of algorithms. For instance, for problems with non-separable regularizers, methods based on ADMM or other splitting schemes are often used. Comparing against such methods would provide a more complete picture of the performance landscape.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important class of problems (saddle-point problems with decomposable structure) for which existing variance-reduction methods are not applicable.
*   **Pro:** It provides the first rigorous linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in this general setting, representing a strong and novel theoretical contribution.
*   **Pro:** The analysis via monotone operator theory is elegant, general, and technically sound, potentially influencing future work on stochastic algorithms for related problems.
*   **Pro:** The proposed methods, particularly the accelerated variant, are shown to achieve significant empirical speed-ups over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The algorithmic templates are direct adaptations from prior work, meaning the novelty lies primarily in the analysis and application rather than in the algorithm design itself.
*   **Con:** The reliance on strong convexity-concavity may limit the direct applicability of the linear convergence results in some practical scenarios.

In summary, the paper's strengths, particularly its novelty in extending variance reduction to a new and important problem class with a sound and general theoretical analysis, substantially outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a high-quality contribution that advances the field of large-scale optimization.
