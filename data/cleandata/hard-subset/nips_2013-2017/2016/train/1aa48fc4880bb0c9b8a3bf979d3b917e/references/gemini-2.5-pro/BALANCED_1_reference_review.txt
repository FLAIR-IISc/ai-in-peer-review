This paper extends the variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for minimizing finite sums of convex functions, to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `M` is a strongly convex-concave function with a simple proximal operator, and `K` is a smooth convex-concave function whose gradient operator `B(x,y) = (d_x K, -d_y K)` can be decomposed into a sum of `B_i`. This work provides the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in this general saddle-point setting.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it presents and analyzes SVRG and SAGA variants for saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence rates that improve upon existing batch and stochastic methods. Second, it highlights the importance of non-uniform sampling schemes for achieving the best theoretical rates and practical performance, drawing parallels to the convex minimization setting. Third, it shows how these methods can be accelerated using the "Catalyst" framework, leading to an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods. Finally, the analysis is elegantly unified under the more general framework of finding zeros of monotone operators, demonstrating that the proposed methods apply beyond saddle-point problems and clarifying that the analysis does not rely on co-coercivity, a restrictive assumption made in prior work on variance reduction for monotone operators that excluded the saddle-point case.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The extension of variance reduction methods to general saddle-point problems is a significant and timely contribution. Many problems in machine learning, such as AUC maximization, robust optimization, and training models with non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation), are naturally cast as saddle-point problems but previously could not leverage the state-of-the-art performance of SVRG/SAGA. This paper fills an important gap in the literature and provides powerful new tools for practitioners and researchers working on these problems. The theoretical results, particularly the complexity bounds summarized in Table 1, clearly demonstrate a formal advantage over existing approaches.

2.  **Technical Quality and Thoroughness:** The paper is technically sound and comprehensive. It provides rigorous convergence proofs for both SVRG and SAGA variants. The analysis is built upon the general and powerful theory of monotone operators, which is the correct mathematical lens for this problem class. This approach not only yields a clean analysis but also broadens the applicability of the results to problems like variational inequalities. The inclusion of an acceleration scheme and a detailed analysis of different sampling strategies further strengthens the paper's contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem setup, assumptions, and algorithms are clearly defined. The summary of convergence rates in Table 1 is particularly effective at contextualizing the contributions and comparing them with prior art. The paper does an excellent job of relating the new algorithms to their predecessors in convex optimization (SVRG, SAGA) and to existing algorithms for saddle-point problems (Forward-Backward, stochastic methods).

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively support the theoretical claims. The results show that the proposed methods (SAGA and accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) substantially outperform standard batch, accelerated batch, and stochastic algorithms, especially on ill-conditioned problems. The choice of experiments on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers convincingly demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed framework in scenarios where standard primal SVRG/SAGA cannot be applied.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Known Problem Parameters:** The analysis and algorithms rely on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity parameters (`λ`, `γ`) and the Lipschitz constant (`L`). In practice, these constants can be difficult to estimate accurately, and the performance of the algorithms is sensitive to their step-sizes. While this is a common feature of many first-order methods, a brief discussion of this limitation and potential remedies (e.g., adaptive step-sizes or line search procedures) would make the work more complete from a practical standpoint.

2.  **Assumption of Strong Convexity-Concavity:** The linear convergence result is predicated on the strong convexity-concavity of the function `M`. While this is a standard assumption for proving linear rates, many practical saddle-point problems are only convex-concave. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a limitation of the current paper.

3.  **Subtleties in SAGA with Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper correctly notes that for the theoretical bound of SAGA with non-uniform sampling to hold, one must resample indices for the gradient update step. However, the experiments use a practical heuristic (a mixture of uniform and non-uniform sampling without resampling). This creates a minor disconnect between theory and practice. While this heuristic is sensible and has been used elsewhere, a short justification in the experimental section for why this is a reasonable and effective choice would be beneficial for the reader.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   It would be helpful to add a brief discussion on the practical challenges of setting the step-sizes, which depend on problem constants that may not be readily available. A pointer to future work on adaptive variants would be valuable.
*   In Section 7 (Experiments), briefly clarifying the choice of the SAGA sampling strategy and why it deviates from the one in the theoretical analysis would improve the paper's self-containedness. For instance, explaining that resampling can add computational overhead and that mixed sampling is a common and effective practical alternative would suffice.
*   The claim that the "algorithmic extension is straightforward" in the introduction, while technically true, slightly understates the novelty. The key insight is that the variance reduction estimator works for the structured *saddle-point gradient operator*, which is non-trivial. Reframing this slightly to emphasize the novelty of the *analysis* and its departure from the co-coercive case would be more impactful.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It rigorously extends powerful variance reduction techniques to the broad and important class of saddle-point problems, supported by strong theory and compelling experiments. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the work. The paper is well-written, technically solid, and will likely be influential. It is a clear accept for NeurIPS.
