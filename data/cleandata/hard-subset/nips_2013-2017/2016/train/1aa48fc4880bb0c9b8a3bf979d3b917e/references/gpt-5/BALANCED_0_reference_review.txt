Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M admits a proximal operator and imparts strong convexity-concavity (λ,γ). The central idea is to exploit decomposability of the smooth part via B(x,y)=(∂xK,−∂yK), which can be written as a sum of Lipschitz components Bi. Building on variance-reduction for finite-sum minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), the paper proposes straightforward but nontrivial extensions of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, including:
- variance-reduced forward-backward iterations with a carefully scaled norm Ω and step sizes depending on L and a sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π);
- two splitting schemes (element-wise and factored row/column splits for bilinear K), along with theoretically justified non-uniform sampling;
- a simple proximal-point/catalyst-style acceleration tailored to saddle-point settings;
- an analysis via monotone operator theory that generalizes beyond saddle points to finding zeros of A+B, where A is maximal strongly monotone and B is a sum of Lipschitz operators with monotone aggregate.

The paper relates to NIPS work on finite-sum minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), batch primal-dual and accelerated methods (Chambolle–Pock; Nesterov), stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods for separable SP problems (SPDC, APCG variants), and stochastic forward-backward/mirror-prox. Unlike SPDC/APCG, the proposed methods handle nonseparable M and K and do not require each Bi to have a resolvent. Compared to stochastic FB/mirror-prox, the variance-reduced schemes achieve linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: First linear-convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA-type methods on general (nonseparable) saddle-point problems, with a clean monotone-operator proof strategy that also covers variational inequalities. The distinction between function and operator splits (and the fact that Bi need not be gradients) is insightful.
- Sampling design: The analysis explicitly handles non-uniform sampling and shows improved L̄(π)-dependent rates. The factored split for bilinear K with row/column probabilities is practically appealing and lowers storage.
- Acceleration: A proximal-point/catalyst scheme specialized to saddle points yields improved complexities without requiring Nesterov-style estimate sequences; the resulting trade-off in τ is transparent and gives clear guidance on when acceleration helps.
- Practical considerations: Discussion of storage/time for factored vs individual splits, mini-batching to amortize prox cost, and initialization tricks for bilinear problems.
- Experiments: On two datasets, the methods (particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) improve over batch forward-backward and stochastic FB; the non-uniform sampling effect is visible.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope: Experiments are limited (two datasets, one class of nonseparable losses/regularizers and one separable case). Comparisons omit strong baselines for saddle points like stochastic mirror-prox and SPDC/APCG-type methods in regimes where they apply. No ablation on resampling vs reuse for SAGA (which is discussed theoretically), mini-batch size m, or the cost of non-uniform sampling.
- Assumption and tuning burden: Linear rates require known strong convexity-concavity constants and L, L̄(π). In practice these are often unknown; there is little guidance on estimating/tuning them robustly, and sensitivity is not evaluated.
- Memory/time overheads: For individual splits SAGA requires O(nd) storage, which can be prohibitive; empirical memory/runtime trade-offs are not quantified. The cost of computing/maintaining non-uniform probabilities (and alias tables) and L̄(π) is not discussed in detail.
- Claims vs evidence: Statements such as “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” depend on cost models (Tprox, T(B), T(Bi)) and problem geometry; the experiments do not fully substantiate the breadth of this claim.
- Clarity/minor issues: The paper is dense; some notation could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of µ vs λ,γ; clear definitions of L vs L̄). A few typos/phrases are confusing (e.g., “does need splits into convex-concave terms” appears inconsistent with the operator-splitting perspective). Table 1, central to complexity claims, is referenced but not visible in the excerpt.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The algorithms are natural extensions; the novelty lies in analysis and in handling operator splits and non-uniform sampling. Theorems and rates appear plausible and consistent with known special cases; however, proofs are in the appendix and a brief sketch in the main text would help. Practical tuning issues are underexplored experimentally.
- Clarity: Generally well written but quite dense. Algorithms are specified; however, clearer guidance on parameters, sampling implementation, and when to prefer factored vs individual splits would improve readability.
- Originality: Good. Extending SVRG/SAGA to general saddle-point/monotone-operator settings (without co-coercivity or separability) and combining with a proximal-point accelerator is a meaningful contribution circa the existing literature.
- Significance: Potentially high. Saddle-point formulations are pervasive, and variance-reduced methods with non-uniform sampling and acceleration could become standard tools. The impact would be strengthened by broader experimental validation and comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and general theoretical contribution; first linear rates for variance-reduced methods on nonseparable saddle points via monotone operators.
- Clear practical insights on splits and non-uniform sampling; simple and implementable acceleration scheme.
- Demonstrated empirical gains in representative tasks; aligns with NeurIPS interest in optimization for ML.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing comparisons to some strong baselines (e.g., stochastic mirror-prox, SPDC/APCG where applicable).
- Practical tuning and robustness (unknown constants, sampling overhead) not addressed experimentally.
- Some overstatements and clarity issues; reliance on strong convexity-concavity.

Constructive suggestions
- Expand experiments: include bilinear games with known condition numbers, AUC optimization, robust optimization, and TV/overlapping-group regularizers; report wall-clock time, memory, and sensitivity to λ,γ scaling and mini-batch m. Compare against stochastic mirror-prox and SPDC/APCG where separability holds.
- Provide practical tuning guidance: heuristics to estimate λ,γ,L,L̄(π); adaptive step-size variants; ablations for resampling in SAGA and for non-uniform vs uniform sampling, including implementation details (alias sampling).
- Clarify theory: add brief proof sketches in the main text; state conditions under which accelerated SVRG is guaranteed to beat accelerated batch in terms of T(B), T(Bi), and L̄; temper “always” claims.
- Improve exposition: unify notation, correct minor typos, and include the full complexity table in the main paper.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical advances and the framing via monotone operators are compelling and broadly relevant, and the empirical results, while limited, are encouraging. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying practical aspects would make the paper more impactful.

Confidence: Medium.
