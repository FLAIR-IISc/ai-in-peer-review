## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the setting of saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning due to duality and regularization. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these algorithms tailored to saddle-point problems, demonstrating linear convergence under suitable assumptions. They highlight key differences from the convex minimization setup, including the importance of splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling.  Furthermore, they propose an acceleration technique based on a catalyst framework, showing improved performance over accelerated batch methods.  The work also generalizes its results to monotone operator problems, broadening its scope.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVR methods to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution, as existing accelerated methods often don't readily apply due to the non-separable nature of these problems.  The analysis specifically for the saddle-point setting, rather than relying on convex minimization results, is a strong point.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a robust theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates and conditions for linear convergence. The connection to monotone operator theory provides a broader perspective and justifies the algorithm's applicability beyond saddle-point problems.
* **Practical Relevance:** The focus on problems frequently encountered in machine learning (regularized linear models, robust optimization) makes this work highly relevant. The experimental section, while relatively limited, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* **Insights into Saddle-Point Optimization:** The paper offers valuable insights into the specific challenges and opportunities of applying stochastic methods to saddle-point problems, such as the need for tailored splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling.
* **Acceleration Framework:** The proposed catalyst-inspired acceleration technique offers a practical improvement, and the analysis clearly shows its benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope, focusing on a specific set of problems.  A broader evaluation on diverse datasets and problem structures would strengthen the claims.  More detailed comparisons with other relevant algorithms, beyond those presented, would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumptions (strongly convex-concave, Lipschitz continuity) are standard but potentially restrictive.  The impact of relaxing these assumptions could be explored, even if only theoretically.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the algorithms themselves are conceptually straightforward extensions of SVRG/SAGA, the optimal implementation (especially the non-uniform sampling and acceleration) can be complex and require careful tuning.
* **Lack of Adaptive Step Size:** While the paper provides theoretical guarantees with fixed step sizes, an adaptive step size strategy could potentially improve performance in practice.




**Evaluation based on NIPS criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is thorough and the proofs appear rigorous. It's a complete piece of work within its defined scope.  The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though some sections (e.g., the discussion of different splitting strategies) could benefit from further clarification.  The notation is standard and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly. **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems is novel. The insights into splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling, and the generalization to monotone operators, contribute to the originality of the work.  The authors cite relevant work, though perhaps a more extensive survey of related approaches in saddle-point optimization could be included. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:** The results are significant, addressing a practical and challenging problem in machine learning. The proposed methods have the potential to be widely used and built upon. The acceleration framework is a valuable contribution. **(Strong)**

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
* Presents novel theoretical results with strong guarantees.
* Offers practical improvements over existing methods.
* Demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges in saddle-point optimization.
* Generalization to monotone operators broadens the impact.

**Con:**

* Limited experimental scope.
* Strong assumptions.
* Implementation complexity.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems is a valuable addition to the toolkit for solving machine learning problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. While the experimental scope is somewhat limited and the assumptions are strong, these are not fatal flaws. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets and problem structures.  A more thorough discussion of potential relaxations of the assumptions would also be beneficial.
