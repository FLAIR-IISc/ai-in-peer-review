### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the highly successful stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods, originally designed for finite-sum convex minimization problems, to the setting of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. Saddle-point formulations are common in machine learning, particularly for problems involving non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., structured output SVMs, robust optimization) where standard SVRG/SAGA on the primal or dual alone are not applicable.

The authors' main contributions are:
1.  Proposing straightforward algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems by treating the gradient operator as a sum of individual operators.
2.  Providing the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point setting. A key aspect of the analysis is that it relies only on the monotonicity of the saddle-point operator, not co-coercivity, which is a property used in convex minimization proofs but does not hold for saddle-point problems. This makes the analysis novel and applicable to a broader class of monotone inclusion problems.
3.  Investigating saddle-point-specific structures, such as "factored" splits of the operator and the critical role of non-uniform sampling for achieving better theoretical rates and practical performance.
4.  Demonstrating that these variance-reduced methods can be accelerated using the "Catalyst" framework, leading to an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods.

The theoretical results are supported by experiments on binary classification tasks with non-separable regularizers and losses, showing significant practical gains over batch and standard stochastic algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the literature. While SVRG and SAGA have become standard tools for finite-sum optimization, their application to the general saddle-point setting was not established. This work provides a principled and effective extension, opening up these powerful techniques to a wider range of machine learning problems that were previously inaccessible to them. The connection to and analysis via monotone operators is a strong theoretical contribution.

2.  **Novel and Sound Theoretical Analysis:** The core theoretical contribution—proving linear convergence using only monotonicity—is a significant achievement. This is not a trivial extension of the existing SVRG/SAGA proofs. By avoiding the co-coercivity assumption, the authors have developed a more general analytical tool that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, as correctly pointed out in Section 6. The derived complexity bounds in Table 1 clearly show the theoretical advantage over existing methods.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Insights:** The paper successfully connects theory to practice. The identification of non-uniform sampling as a key component for efficiency is a crucial practical insight, and it is well-supported by both theory (improved bounds for L̄(π)) and experiments. The discussion on different splitting strategies ("factored" vs. "individual") is also a valuable, problem-specific contribution.

4.  **Completeness and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. It systematically introduces the problem, reviews existing work, presents the new algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated SVRG), provides convergence theorems, and validates them experimentally. The inclusion of the "Catalyst" acceleration framework makes the work very comprehensive. The experimental setup, using a genuinely non-separable loss and regularizer, is well-chosen and convincingly demonstrates the method's utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Gap Between SAGA Theory and Practice:** The paper notes that for non-uniform sampling, the SAGA theory requires resampling, but the experiments use a heuristic mixture of uniform and non-uniform sampling without resampling, following prior work. While this is a common practice, it creates a small disconnect between the presented theory and the algorithm that was empirically evaluated. A short discussion on why this heuristic is expected to work well or its potential pitfalls would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Reliance on Known Constants:** The proposed algorithms and their theoretical analyses depend on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄), which are often not known or are difficult to estimate in practice. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion as a direction for future work, it remains a practical hurdle for the immediate adoption of these methods.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental comparison is good but could be slightly broader. The main competitors are batch and basic stochastic methods. While the paper focuses on the non-separable setting where methods like primal-dual coordinate descent [12] might not directly apply, a more detailed discussion of the relationship to these methods and why they are not applicable would be beneficial. Including a comparison to a stochastic primal-dual method on the separable `rcv1` dataset (in addition to the primal-only solvers) could provide a more complete picture of the landscape.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem: efficiently solving saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure.
*   Provides the first linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in this setting, a significant and non-trivial theoretical contribution.
*   The analysis technique (relying only on monotonicity) is novel and has broader implications for monotone operator splitting.
*   The paper is comprehensive, covering SVRG, SAGA, acceleration, and practical aspects like sampling strategies.
*   Empirical results clearly demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed methods on relevant machine learning problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical SAGA implementation for non-uniform sampling deviates slightly from the one that is theoretically analyzed.
*   The methods require knowledge of problem-dependent constants, which can be a limitation in practice.
*   The experimental comparison, while solid, could be expanded to include a wider range of modern stochastic primal-dual algorithms.

### Final Recommendation

This is a very strong paper that makes a significant contribution to both the theory and practice of large-scale optimization for machine learning. The extension of variance-reduction techniques to saddle-point problems is a natural, timely, and important step forward. The theoretical analysis is novel and sound, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. This paper is a clear advance in the state of the art and will likely be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
