Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex–concave and prox-friendly, and K is smooth. The central idea is to extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) beyond separable convex minimization to general composite saddle-point problems by working on the monotone operator associated with the saddle-point optimality condition, B(x,y)=(∂xK,−∂yK), and using prox steps for M. The authors develop two families of splits: (i) arbitrary decompositions B=∑i Bi, where Bi need not be gradients of functions; and (ii) “factored” splits adapted to rows/columns in bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, enabling non-uniform sampling. They provide linear convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA with mini-batches, explicit step sizes, and complexity bounds that depend on problem-dependent smoothness constants L and L̄(π) (the latter reflects the sampling scheme). They further propose an acceleration scheme inspired by the Catalyst/proximal-point framework, yielding improved complexities that compare favorably to accelerated batch forward–backward methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems. Finally, the analysis is framed for general monotone operators A+B, extending beyond saddle-point problems and not relying on co-coercivity.

Related prior work includes SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization; Chambolle–Pock and other primal–dual/forward–backward methods for saddle-point problems; stochastic forward–backward with noise; and variance-reduced primal–dual methods for separable structures (e.g., SPDC). The paper distinguishes itself by handling non-separable M with prox, allowing Bi that are not gradients, emphasizing non-uniform sampling tailored to saddle-point structure, and providing linear rates without co-coercivity assumptions.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Casting the problem as finding zeros of a (sum of) monotone operators clarifies why SVRG/SAGA-style control variates extend and where they differ from convex minimization (no co-coercivity).
- Generality: Handles non-separable losses/regularizers as long as M is prox-friendly; Bi need not be gradients; extends to variational inequalities.
- Non-uniform sampling and factored splits: Careful treatment of sampling probabilities with problem-dependent constants (‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max) and memory-time tradeoffs; practical importance in bilinear problems.
- Acceleration: A simple proximal-point/Catalyst-style wrapper with clear rate expressions that interplay with oracle costs T(B), T(Bi). The acceleration story is compelling for ill-conditioned problems.
- Theory: Clear linear convergence guarantees with explicit iteration complexities and mini-batch effects; bounds match intuitions from convex minimization but are tailored to saddle-point structure.
- Experiments: Demonstrate benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration; include a non-separable setup where primal methods are inapplicable.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Experimental scope and baselines: Only two datasets; comparisons omit strong contemporaneous baselines for saddle-point VR such as SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, cited) and accelerated primal–dual variants. Reported metrics are mainly “passes over data”; wall-clock time and memory (notably SAGA with individual splits) are critical here.
- Assumptions and applicability: Strong convex–concave (M) is required; many important saddle-point formulations are only convex–concave. It would help to discuss extensions (e.g., regularization or restart schemes) and practical behavior when strong convexity is small or absent.
- Clarity and notation: The paper is dense and occasionally confusing:
  - The symbol L^2 is used where it appears to mean L squared; consider using L^2 consistently or an unambiguous symbol. Similarly for L̄(π).
  - The constant µ appears in Theorem 2 without prior definition (is µ=min{λ,γ}?); please define precisely.
  - The number of inner iterations in SVRG “log 4×(L^2+3 L̄^2/m)” is unusual; clarify the origin and whether this is a natural constant or a proof artifact.
  - More intuition would help for the factored vs individual splits and how their storage/complexity scales concretely.
- Practicalities of sampling: Non-uniform sampling requires computing and maintaining pj, qk or πjk. Clarify one-time precomputation cost and how to handle changing scalings (λ,γ) or adaptive normalization.
- Prox costs and mini-batching: The bounds discuss amortizing an expensive prox via larger mini-batches m; ablations varying m and reporting wall-clock and total prox calls would be informative. The chosen non-separable AUC surrogate and OSCAR-like regularizer have prox routines with O(n log n); more discussion on their implementation and numerical stability would be useful.
- Tightness of rates: The linear rates rely on L and L̄(π); the gap between these and actual Lipschitz constants in practice (especially with non-uniform sampling) could be discussed, and whether adaptive step size schemes (as in SAGA variants) can be ported.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the monotone operator perspective and proofs (as described) are appropriate and appear novel for this setting. Some constants and step-size expressions feel conservative. Minor clarity issues (undefined µ, notation).

Clarity
Well-organized but dense. The main text could benefit from: (i) a compact table summarizing assumptions and constants; (ii) clearer algorithm pseudocode (consistent notation, define all variables, especially in SAGA resampling option); (iii) moving a short proof sketch for at least one theorem to the main text.

Originality
Good. Extending VR methods to general saddle-point problems without co-coercivity and with non-uniform sampling/factored splits is new and non-trivial. The acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst in this context is also a nice contribution.

Significance
Potentially impactful: variance-reduced methods for non-separable saddle-point problems are broadly useful. The general monotone operator extension increases reach. Empirical evidence is promising but could be stronger with broader comparisons and metrics.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and monotone operator settings with linear convergence.
- Practical sampling schemes and factored splits with clear complexity benefits.
- Simple and effective acceleration scheme.
- Applicable to important ML problems with non-separable structures.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison to SPDC/other strong baselines.
- Clarity/notation issues; some parameters/constants are under-explained.
- Reliance on strong convex–concave assumption; limited discussion of the non-strong case.
- Memory concerns for SAGA with individual splits not fully addressed experimentally.

Recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on addressing clarity and experimental breadth. If only one or two slots are available, I would ask for stronger empirical comparisons (SPDC, accelerated CP variants; wall-clock; memory) and clearer exposition; otherwise, the theoretical contributions justify acceptance.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add baselines: SPDC; Chambolle–Pock with overrelaxation; (where applicable) primal VR methods and Mirror-Prox variants.
- Report wall-clock time, memory usage, and sensitivity to mini-batch size m and sampling distributions; include ablation of factored vs individual splits.
- Clarify µ in Theorem 2, unify L/L̄ notation, and explain the inner-loop iteration counts; provide a brief proof sketch in the main text.
- Discuss practical computation of non-uniform probabilities and one-time costs.
- Expand on handling weakly convex–concave problems (e.g., adding small regularization, restarts).
- Provide guidance for estimating L and L̄(π) or using backtracking/adaptive step sizes.
