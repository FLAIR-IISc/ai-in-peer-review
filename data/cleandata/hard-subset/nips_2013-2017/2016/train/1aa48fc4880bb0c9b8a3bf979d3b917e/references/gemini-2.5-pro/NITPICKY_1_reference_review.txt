### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods SVRG and SAGA to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `M` is strongly convex-concave with a simple proximal operator, and `K` is smooth and decomposable into a sum of components. The authors propose direct algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The main contributions are theoretical: they provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods on saddle-point problems, framed more generally through the lens of monotone operators. The analysis highlights the importance of non-uniform sampling strategies for achieving better convergence bounds. Furthermore, the paper shows how these methods can be accelerated using a simple adaptation of the "Catalyst" framework, leading to an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods. The theoretical improvements are supported by experiments on machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically solid in its core theoretical contributions. The derivation of linear convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA in this new domain is a valuable result. The insight to use the monotone operator framework for the analysis is elegant and allows for a more general treatment that extends beyond saddle-point problems. The application of the Catalyst framework for acceleration is also a nice, non-trivial extension.

However, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws and a lack of careful presentation, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Clarity of Notation and Definitions:** The presentation is often imprecise.
    *   In Assumption (B), the constant `L` is defined as a Lipschitz constant but is later referred to as the "condition number". This is non-standard terminology and can be misleading. A condition number is typically a ratio of constants (e.g., Lipschitz/strong convexity).
    *   The definition of `L̄(π)^2` in Section 2.4 uses the phrase "such that `Ω(x− x′, y − y′)^2 <= 1`", which is a slightly awkward way to define a supremum over a unit ball.
    *   In Section 7, the parameter `γ` is stated to be `≈ n`, which is insufficiently precise for a reproducible experiment. The procedure for finding the optimizer `(x*, y*)` to compute the "distance to optimizers" is not described at all.

2.  **Algorithmic Descriptions:** The algorithm boxes contain details that are confusing or impractical.
    *   In Algorithm 1 (SVRG), the number of inner loop iterations is given as `log 4 * (L^2 + 3L̄^2/m)`. This exact, non-integer value is clearly an artifact of the proof and is not a practical specification for an algorithm. It suggests a lack of attention to the transition from theory to practice.
    *   In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), the resampling step for non-uniform sampling is marked as "(optional)". However, the main text states it is *required* for the theoretical bound to hold. This is a direct contradiction that makes the algorithm description ambiguous.

3.  **Analysis Nuances:** The paper makes several strong claims that are either not fully substantiated or contain concerning caveats.
    *   The primary weakness is a statement in Section 6: "applying our results directly to convex minimization, we would get slower rates". This is a critical admission. If the general monotone operator analysis is not tight enough to recover the known optimal rates for the key special case of convex minimization, it calls into question the quality and tightness of the entire analytical framework. It suggests the analysis relies on some cancellation specific to the saddle-point structure, undermining the claim of generality.
    *   The acceleration section (Section 5) is cursory. The claim that "the same scheme should work for SAGA" is pure speculation and should not be included without proof.
    *   The distinction between SVRG and SAGA in Section 4 glosses over important practical differences, such as SVRG's epoch overhead versus SAGA's storage requirement and dependence on `|I|` in the step-size.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally readable, but the aforementioned notational imprecision and ambiguous algorithmic descriptions detract from its clarity. The organization is logical, but several key details are relegated to the appendix without sufficient summary in the main text (e.g., the complexity of the proximal operators used in the experiments). The very first page contains a formatting error, with "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems..." appearing as a floating footnote, which is unprofessional.

**Originality:**

The core algorithmic ideas are direct adaptations of SVRG and SAGA, which the authors rightly describe as "straightforward". The originality lies in the analysis. This is the first work to provide a rigorous linear convergence proof for these methods on saddle-point problems. The use of the monotone operator framework is a novel analytical approach in this context, distinguishing it from prior SVRG/SAGA analyses that often rely on properties like co-coercivity which do not hold here.

**Significance:**

The work is significant. Saddle-point problems are common in machine learning, particularly with the rise of non-separable regularizers, robust optimization, and GANs. Providing efficient, linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this class of problems is an important contribution that other researchers and practitioners are likely to build upon. The theoretical demonstration of the superiority of non-uniform sampling and acceleration is valuable. However, the significance is tempered by the concerns about the analysis's tightness and the paper's overall lack of polish.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and increasingly relevant class of optimization problems in machine learning.
*   Provides the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems, filling a notable gap in the literature.
*   The theoretical analysis via monotone operators is novel and more general than previous analyses of variance-reduced methods.
*   The proposed accelerated method is shown to be theoretically superior to existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper is marred by numerous minor errors, including notational imprecision, formatting mistakes, and impractical algorithmic descriptions, suggesting a lack of careful preparation.
*   The analysis, while novel, is admitted to be suboptimal for the important special case of convex minimization, which is a major red flag regarding its tightness and generality.
*   Key claims are not fully substantiated (e.g., acceleration for SAGA) or are ambiguously presented (e.g., the necessity of resampling in Algorithm 2).
*   The experimental section lacks sufficient detail for full reproducibility and could benefit from stronger baselines.

### Decision

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents novel theoretical ideas, the execution is flawed. The accumulation of many small issues in presentation and clarity, combined with the major concern about the sub-optimality of the analysis for the convex case, prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The core contribution is valuable, but the paper requires a thorough revision to fix the ambiguities, strengthen the claims, and present the work with the level of precision expected at NIPS. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
