Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is strongly convex–concave with an easy proximal operator. The authors extend two popular variance-reduced methods from convex minimization—SVRG and SAGA—to this saddle-point/composite setting. They provide the first linear convergence analyses for these incremental methods in saddle-point problems and, more broadly, for finding zeros of (strongly) monotone operators A+B when A is strongly monotone with a known resolvent and B is a sum of Lipschitz operators (not necessarily each monotone nor gradients of functions). They emphasize two types of splits of B: (i) function-based (sum over components) and (ii) operator/partial-derivative “factored” splits (e.g., rows/columns for bilinear K), which influences storage and sampling.

A key contribution is identifying appropriate smoothness constants under weighted norms induced by the strong convexity–concavity parameters (λ,γ), and showing that non-uniform sampling tailored to the structure (e.g., row/column norms for bilinear K) is crucial for improved bounds. They also adapt the catalyst/proximal-point acceleration framework to the saddle-point setting, yielding accelerated SVRG with complexity that improves over accelerated batch forward–backward, and argue the approach extends cleanly because the proximal-point step for saddle points contracts without requiring Nesterov’s sequence.

The paper situates the work against: (i) variance reduction in convex minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), (ii) batch forward–backward and accelerated primal–dual algorithms (Chambolle–Pock) for saddle points, and (iii) stochastic forward–backward with sublinear rates. It contrasts with primal–dual coordinate methods that require separability and with prior variance-reduced analyses that rely on co-coercivity (not available here). Experiments on binary classification with non-separable loss/regularizer and on a separable case illustrate gains from SAGA/SVRG (notably with non-uniform sampling) and further gains from acceleration.

Quality
- Theory: The algorithms and analyses are carefully developed. The forward–backward perspective and the monotone operator view yield clean linear rates under strong convexity–concavity. The treatment of sampling distributions and the new smoothness constant L̄(π) is well-motivated, and the derived complexities for bilinear K (using ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max) clarify when non-uniform sampling and factored splits help. The catalyst-style acceleration is adapted convincingly to saddle points with a simpler argument via proximal-point contraction.
- Assumptions: Strong convex–concave structure via M and Lipschitzness of K are standard but restrictive; the work explicitly focuses on this regime. The need for known λ,γ,L (or upper bounds) is noted.
- Experiments: Empirical results support the theoretical hierarchy (non-uniform > uniform; accelerated SVRG > others, especially when ill-conditioned). However, the experimental section is relatively small in scope and relies on “passes over data” rather than comprehensive wall-clock/memory metrics.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. Definitions (weighted norms, L, L̄(π)) are clear; pseudocode is provided; assumptions (A–C) are explicit. The discussion of splits (individual vs factored) and their impact on storage and complexity is useful. Some places could be clarified:
- Step-size expressions use L2 and L̄2; it would help to keep dimensions consistent and remind the reader why the FB step size is 1/L2 under the chosen norms.
- The practical overhead of computing non-uniform probabilities and of resampling in SAGA is only briefly mentioned; a short paragraph quantifying this would improve readability.

Originality
The extension and analysis of SVRG/SAGA to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and to sums of Lipschitz operators B without requiring each to be a gradient or monotone is original and timely. The identification of factored splits and tailored non-uniform sampling for bilinear K is novel and practically relevant. The acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst scheme for the saddle-point setting, with a simpler argument than in convex minimization, is a noteworthy contribution.

Significance
Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous (regularized ERM, robust optimization, convex relaxations). Existing linearly convergent incremental methods largely rely on separability; this work removes that limitation and covers important non-separable losses/regularizers. The monotone operator perspective broadens applicability (e.g., to variational inequalities). The results are likely to be adopted and extended by the community.

Strengths
- First linear-convergence analysis of SAGA/SVRG-type methods for non-separable saddle-point problems.
- Clear monotone-operator framework; minimal assumptions (no co-coercivity).
- Careful treatment of sampling; concrete, improved bounds for bilinear problems with non-uniform sampling and factored splits; memory-efficient SAGA with factored storage.
- Acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst with provable gains and simple implementation.
- Empirical validation consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: Requires strong convex–concave structure; extensions to merely convex–concave (e.g., via regularization/restarts) are not analyzed. A brief discussion of convergence without strong monotonicity (e.g., sublinear with averaging) would broaden impact.
- Practicalities of non-uniform sampling: Computing row/column leverage-like weights ((KK>)jj, (K>K)kk) may be expensive or memory-bound for very large, sparse K. Discuss approximate schemes (e.g., sampling from cached diagonal approximations) and their impact on L̄(π).
- SAGA resampling: The theory requires resampling stored operator values under non-uniform sampling; experiments mix sampling schemes without resampling. Please clarify the bias/variance trade-offs and provide a small ablation on resampling vs no-resampling.
- Experimental breadth: Add wall-clock time, memory usage (especially SAGA storage for individual vs factored splits), scaling with n,d, and sensitivity to mini-batch m and step sizes. Comparing against strong stochastic primal–dual baselines (e.g., stochastic Chambolle–Pock variants, SPDC when applicable) would strengthen the case.
- Acceleration parameters: The choice of τ depends on L̄ and oracle-time ratios T(Bi)/T(B), which may be unknown. Provide a practical tuning heuristic and show robustness.
- Minor: Specify mini-batch sizes used; clarify when proxM cost dominates and how m alleviates it; mention how the weighted scaling (λ,γ) should be chosen in practice.

Questions for the authors
- Can your analysis be adapted to the merely convex–concave case (µ=0) to obtain sublinear rates (e.g., via Mirror-Prox variants with variance reduction)?
- For general non-bilinear K, how do you recommend constructing effective non-uniform sampling probabilities without prohibitive precomputation?
- In the monotone operator extension, can you provide a concrete non-saddle example (e.g., a variational inequality) in experiments?

Pros
- Strong, original theory with practical algorithmic implications
- Clear benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration
- Broader applicability via monotone operator viewpoint

Cons
- Experimental section limited; missing some baselines and system-level metrics
- Dependence on strong convex–concave structure and known constants
- Practical overheads of non-uniform sampling/resampling not fully addressed

Recommendation
I lean to accept. The paper makes a solid and novel theoretical contribution with practical algorithms, and the empirical results, while limited, support the claims. With expanded experiments and additional practical guidance on sampling/tuning, this work would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS. Confidence: high.
