### Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the composite decomposable saddle-point problem, `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is a smooth convex-concave function whose gradient operator can be expressed as a finite sum of component operators, and `M` is a strongly convex-concave, possibly non-smooth function with an efficient proximal operator. This formulation is relevant to many machine learning tasks, including supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers and robust optimization.

The authors extend the well-known stochastic variance reduction techniques, SVRG and SAGA, from the domain of strongly-convex finite-sum minimization to this saddle-point setting. While the algorithmic adaptations are presented as straightforward, the core contribution lies in providing the first convergence analysis that establishes linear convergence for these methods on saddle-point problems. This analysis is notably different from prior work on SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization. Instead of relying on the co-coercivity property of gradients, which does not hold for saddle-point problems, the authors base their proof on the more general property of monotonicity. This allows the framework to be extended to finding zeros of monotone operators, a broader class of problems that includes variational inequalities.

Furthermore, the paper investigates practical aspects such as different ways to split the gradient operator (including "factored" splits) and demonstrates theoretically and empirically the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal performance. Finally, the authors show how to accelerate the proposed SVRG variant using the "catalyst" framework, resulting in an algorithm with a convergence rate that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the main strength of the work. The authors correctly identify that standard analysis techniques for SVRG/SAGA, which often rely on co-coercivity, are not applicable here. Their alternative analysis, based solely on the monotonicity of the saddle-point operator, is sound, non-trivial, and appropriately general. The provided theorems for SVRG, SAGA, and the accelerated variant clearly state the assumptions and deliver explicit linear convergence rates. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, testing the proposed methods on relevant machine learning tasks with non-separable objectives and comparing them against appropriate baselines (batch, accelerated batch, stochastic). The results convincingly support the theoretical claims, showing significant speed-ups, especially for ill-conditioned problems.
*   **Weaknesses:** The proposed algorithms require knowledge of several problem-dependent constants (strong convexity-concavity parameters, Lipschitz constants) to set the step sizes. This is a common requirement for this class of algorithms, but it can be a practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this and suggest adaptivity as an area for future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation for studying saddle-point problems is clearly articulated with concrete machine learning examples in Section 2.1. The problem setup, assumptions, and notations are introduced precisely. The review of existing batch and stochastic algorithms provides excellent context for the paper's contributions. The algorithms are presented clearly using pseudo-code, and the complexity results are neatly summarized in Table 1, which greatly aids comparison and understanding. The distinction between different splitting schemes and sampling strategies is explained well.
*   **Weaknesses:** Some of the key details, particularly the full algorithm and proof for the accelerated method (Section 5), are deferred to the appendix. While this is necessary due to space constraints, it makes the main paper slightly less self-contained regarding one of its significant contributions.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original and significant contributions.

*   **Strengths:** To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to successfully extend SVRG and SAGA to general composite saddle-point problems and prove their linear convergence. While the algorithmic templates are borrowed from prior work, their application and analysis in this new, more challenging setting are highly novel. The key originality lies in the proof technique, which bypasses the restrictive co-coercivity assumption and uses only monotonicity. This generalization of the analysis framework to monotone operators is a valuable theoretical contribution that distinguishes this work from related literature (e.g., [30]), which was limited to the co-coercive case. The application of the catalyst framework and its simplified analysis in this context is also a novel aspect.
*   **Weaknesses:** As noted, the high-level algorithmic ideas (variance reduction, acceleration via smoothing) are not new in themselves. The novelty is in the synthesis, application to a new problem class, and the accompanying theoretical justification.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are likely to have a substantial impact on the field.

*   **Strengths:** Saddle-point problems are foundational in optimization and are increasingly prevalent in modern machine learning (e.g., adversarial training, AUC maximization, robust optimization). This paper provides the first class of efficient, linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a general form of these problems. This could unlock the ability to solve larger-scale instances of such problems than were previously feasible with batch or standard stochastic methods. The theoretical framework is elegant and may inspire further research into variance-reduced methods for other problems structured as monotone inclusions. The practical insights on non-uniform sampling are immediately useful for practitioners.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper provides the first linearly convergent stochastic variance-reduced algorithms for a broad and important class of composite saddle-point problems.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is a key contribution; it is rigorous, novel, and more general than previous analyses of variance reduction methods, as it does not rely on co-coercivity.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed methods demonstrate strong empirical performance, outperforming existing batch and stochastic algorithms, and address problems with practical applications in machine learning.
5.  **Generality:** The theoretical framework based on monotone operators broadens the applicability of the analysis beyond saddle-point problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  **Inherited Algorithmic Structure:** The core algorithms are direct adaptations of SVRG and SAGA, with the primary novelty residing in the analysis rather than algorithmic design.
2.  **Dependence on Problem Constants:** The practical implementation of the algorithms requires knowledge of problem parameters, which may be difficult to obtain.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novel and sound theoretical contributions to a significant problem class, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It represents a solid advancement in large-scale optimization for machine learning.
