

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper proposes extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which arise naturally in machine learning through duality. The authors claim to provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling, and show how their methods can be accelerated using the "catalyst" framework.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with convergence analysis, (2) showing that saddle-point problems allow different splits (functions or partial derivatives) with non-uniform sampling being crucial, and (3) demonstrating acceleration via the catalyst framework. The authors claim their method is "the only one that can be applied" for non-separable losses with efficient proximal operators.

While the paper is technically sound and the convergence proofs appear rigorous, I have significant concerns about the novelty and significance of the contribution. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems appears to be a straightforward application of existing techniques rather than a fundamental advance. The authors themselves acknowledge this in the introduction: "While the algorithmic extension is straightforward, it comes with challenges and opportunities." However, they fail to convincingly demonstrate why this straightforward extension is novel or valuable.

The theoretical analysis relies on monotonicity properties of saddle-point problems, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this analysis from previous work on convex optimization. The connection to monotone operators is interesting, but it's not clear how this extends beyond what has been done in the literature on monotone operator theory. The paper would have benefited from a more thorough comparison with existing work on saddle-point problems and monotone operators.

The experimental section is limited in scope, focusing primarily on bilinear saddle-point problems with non-separable regularizers. The claim that their method is "the only one that can be applied" for non-separable losses is overstated. The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with other methods that might handle non-separable problems, such as dual decomposition approaches or other proximal methods. The experiments on the rcv1 dataset, which has separable losses, are particularly problematic as they compare to primal methods that are not applicable to non-separable problems.

I'm also concerned about the practical significance of the claimed improvements. The paper states that "accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is the most efficient method," but the experiments don't convincingly demonstrate this across a broad range of problems. The results for the "ill-conditioned" problem (λ/λ0 = 0.10) show a modest improvement over existing methods, but the paper doesn't establish whether this improvement is statistically significant or meaningful in practical terms.

The paper's claim that "these incremental algorithms can be easily accelerated using a simple extension of the 'catalyst' framework" seems like a minor modification rather than a substantial contribution. The acceleration technique appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing methods rather than a novel insight.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it:
1) Provided a more thorough comparison with existing methods for handling non-separable problems
2) Demonstrated the approach on a broader range of problems beyond the specific examples provided
3) Included a more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., how to choose sampling probabilities for different problem structures)
4) Better articulated the theoretical novelty and how it differs from straightforward extensions of existing methods

While the work is technically sound and the experiments are reasonable, the contribution doesn't seem substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The paper represents a careful application of existing techniques to a new problem domain, but it doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The novelty is marginal, and the significance is limited to a narrow class of problems.

In comparison to recent NIPS papers on stochastic optimization (e.g., the SAGA and SVRG papers themselves), this work lacks the fundamental theoretical insights or substantial empirical validation needed for acceptance. The paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of the limitations of their approach and a clearer articulation of how it differs from straightforward extensions of existing methods.

Given the high standards of NIPS and the need for significant contributions to the field, I recommend rejection. While the work is competent, it doesn't meet the bar for novelty and significance required for publication at this venue. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims about novelty and provide more comprehensive validation to make a compelling case for acceptance.
