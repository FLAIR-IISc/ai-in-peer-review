This paper addresses the important and timely problem of developing efficient, linearly-convergent stochastic optimization algorithms for composite saddle-point problems. The authors extend the popular variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the min-max setting. The core problem is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `M` is a strongly convex-concave function with a simple proximal operator, and `K` is a smooth convex-concave function whose gradient operator can be decomposed into a sum of components, `B = sum_i B_i`.

The main contributions are:
1.  The proposal of SVRG and SAGA algorithms for this saddle-point structure.
2.  The first linear convergence analysis for these methods in this setting. A key aspect is that the analysis is based on the more general framework of monotone operators, which correctly identifies monotonicity as the sufficient property, rather than the more restrictive co-coercivity used in many convex minimization analyses.
3.  The identification of novel structural aspects unique to the saddle-point setting, such as "factored" splits of the operator and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal theoretical rates.
4.  An extension of the "Catalyst" acceleration framework, providing an accelerated SVRG variant that provably improves upon accelerated batch methods under certain conditions.

This work builds upon the foundational variance reduction literature (SVRG, SAGA) and connects it to the literature on saddle-point optimization and monotone operator splitting methods. It significantly advances the state-of-the-art by moving beyond the sub-linear rates of existing stochastic saddle-point methods for this problem class.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in modern machine learning, arising from duality, robust optimization, AUC maximization, and generative adversarial networks (GANs). Standard stochastic gradient methods converge at a slow O(1/t) rate for strongly-convex-concave problems, while batch methods do not scale to large datasets. This paper provides the first class of stochastic algorithms that achieve linear convergence, effectively bridging this gap. This is a major theoretical and practical breakthrough.

2.  **Theoretical Quality and Depth:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful. Grounding the proof in the theory of monotone operators is particularly elegant and powerful. It not only provides a clean proof but also demonstrates that the algorithms are applicable to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The authors correctly identify that the gradient operator for saddle-point problems is monotone but not co-coercive, explaining why a naive transfer of proofs from convex minimization would fail and highlighting the novelty of their analysis.

3.  **Originality and Novel Insights:** While the algorithmic templates are direct extensions of SVRG/SAGA, the paper provides crucial new insights specific to the saddle-point structure. The distinction between splitting functions vs. splitting partial derivatives ("factored splits") is a subtle but important contribution, with practical implications for storage in SAGA. Furthermore, the detailed analysis of non-uniform sampling (Table 1), showing its provable advantage over uniform sampling and batch methods, is a key takeaway that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.

4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Assumptions are stated clearly, the algorithms are presented in an easy-to-follow manner, and the theoretical results are summarized effectively in Table 1, allowing for a clear comparison of complexities. The experiments, while focused on a specific application, convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods, especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** The analysis relies on the strong convexity-concavity of the function `M` (i.e., `λ > 0, γ > 0`). This is a standard assumption for proving linear convergence but is a notable limitation. Many important saddle-point problems, such as those arising from standard GAN formulations or linearly constrained convex problems, are merely convex-concave. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but the current applicability is restricted to problems with inherent strong-convexity or those that have been explicitly regularized.

2.  **Practicality of Parameters:** The algorithms, particularly the step-size `σ` and epoch length in SVRG, depend on knowledge of the Lipschitz constants (`L`, `L̄`) and strong convexity-concavity parameters (`λ`, `γ`). In practice, these constants are often unknown and difficult to estimate. A discussion of the practical challenges of parameter tuning, or potential avenues for developing adaptive variants, would strengthen the paper's practical impact.

3.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are well-executed and support the theoretical claims. However, the application space for saddle-point problems is vast. Including an experiment on a problem more directly related to robust optimization or a simplified GAN-like objective could have showcased the versatility of the proposed methods even more compellingly. Additionally, the comparison in the separable case (rcv1) shows SAGA on the primal is faster. While the paper's methods are more general, it would be useful to briefly comment on the performance gap and when a user might still prefer a primal-only approach if available.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. It successfully extends variance-reduced stochastic methods to the important class of saddle-point problems, providing the first linear convergence guarantees for such methods. The analysis is deep, the insights are novel, and the practical implications are significant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly relevant and important problem in modern machine learning.
*   Provides the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this problem class, a significant theoretical leap over existing O(1/t) methods.
*   The analysis via monotone operators is elegant, general, and technically sound.
*   Uncovers novel structural properties and algorithmic choices (factored splits, non-uniform sampling) specific to saddle-point problems.
*   The paper is clear, well-structured, and the empirical results convincingly support the theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core algorithms are direct analogues of existing methods; the primary novelty is in the analysis and application domain.
*   The strong convex-concavity assumption limits the immediate applicability to a subclass of saddle-point problems.

Overall, the strengths overwhelmingly outweigh the weaknesses. This paper represents a complete and impactful piece of work that will likely inspire considerable follow-up research. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
