- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with the first convergence analysis, highlighting differences from convex minimization and importance of monotonicity.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency and adds acceleration via the "catalyst" framework.  
- Connects the methods to monotone operator theory, broadening applicability beyond saddle-point problems.  

Strengths:
- Provides novel and rigorous theoretical analysis for variance-reduced methods in the saddle-point setting.  
- Demonstrates practical significance with examples where non-separable losses/regularizers arise and strong experimental gains, especially for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Offers comprehensive coverage: problem setup, algorithms, proofs, extensions, and experiments.  
- Presents clear pseudocode and well-motivated theoretical results with logical organization.  

Weaknesses:
- Analysis assumes strong convexity–concavity, limiting applicability to non-strongly convex-concave problems common in practice.  
- Insufficient comparison to other recent stochastic saddle-point methods in the literature.  
- Theoretical analysis is complex and could use more intuitive explanations, particularly the monotone-operator discussion.  
- Lacks practical details on how non-uniform sampling probabilities were computed in experiments beyond theoretical bounds.  

Evaluation:
- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous analysis and supporting experiments.  
- Clarity: Well-written and organized, though some theory could be made more accessible.  
- Originality: Novel application and analysis of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems and linkage to monotone operators.  
- Significance: Addresses ubiquitous ML problems and offers accelerated, practical algorithms for non-separable cases.  

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify literature connections and provide more intuitive theoretical explanations.  

Key arguments for acceptance:
- First rigorous analysis of SVRG/SAGA for saddle-point problems.  
- Compelling experimental evidence of practical advantages.  
- Broadens applicability via monotone operator connections.  
- Provides a theoretically grounded approach for non-separable problems.  

Key arguments for rejection:
- None significant; only common limitations that are acknowledged.
