

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning through duality. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, demonstrating linear convergence rates under appropriate assumptions. They show that these algorithms can be accelerated using the "catalyst" framework, leading to improved performance over existing batch or stochastic methods. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a convergence analysis that differs significantly from convex minimization, (2) demonstration that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, and (3) extension of the framework to monotone operators beyond saddle-point problems. The authors validate their theoretical claims with experiments on machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

The work builds on seminal papers in stochastic optimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) but extends them to a fundamentally different problem class. The paper is well-organized, with clear connections to previous work in convex optimization and saddle-point problems.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed proofs for their main theorems (Theorems 1-3) in the appendix, which is essential for a theoretical contribution. The convergence analysis properly accounts for the unique properties of saddle-point problems, particularly the distinction between convex-concave functions and convex minimization. The authors carefully analyze the impact of different sampling schemes and splits (factored vs. individual), providing tight bounds for bilinear problems.

The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate comparison to existing methods (batch forward-backward, accelerated batch methods, and primal methods for separable problems). The authors demonstrate significant empirical improvements with their non-uniform sampling schemes, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. They also acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need to know strong convexity-concavity constants), which demonstrates intellectual honesty.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear, with well-motivated assumptions (A)-(C) that are necessary for their analysis. The notation is consistent throughout, and the authors make a clear distinction between the saddle-point problem and convex minimization. The algorithms are presented with pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3), making them easy to understand and implement.

The connection to related work is well-explained, with appropriate citations to seminal papers in convex optimization and saddle-point problems. The experiments are presented clearly with informative plots showing the squared distance to optimizers as a function of data passes. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

## Originality

This paper makes significant original contributions. While stochastic variance reduction methods for convex minimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) are well-established, extending them to saddle-point problems is novel. The authors show that the analysis differs substantially from convex minimization, particularly in how they interpret saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators.

The demonstration that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency (both in theory and practice) is a key insight that differs from previous work on convex minimization. The extension to monotone operators beyond saddle-point problems is particularly noteworthy, as it broadens the applicability of their framework.

The paper is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on variance reduction (e.g., SAGA, SVRG) and addresses a fundamentally different problem class. The authors properly acknowledge related work on saddle-point problems (e.g., [11, 12]) while demonstrating how their approach is superior.

## Significance

Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning through duality, particularly in problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors demonstrate that their algorithms are the only ones that can be applied to such problems from the class of large-scale linearly convergent algorithms. This has significant practical implications for applications like robust optimization, structured prediction, and non-separable regularizers.

The theoretical improvements (linear convergence rates) and practical advantages (empirical results showing significant speedups) advance the state of the art in stochastic optimization. The acceleration via the catalyst framework provides a practical improvement over existing batch methods, which is particularly valuable for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper's significance is further enhanced by its applicability to variational inequality problems, which are common in game theory and other domains.

## Strengths

1. First rigorous convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction methods on saddle-point problems
2. Demonstrates the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency
3. Provides a clean extension to monotone operators beyond saddle-point problems
4. Theoretical and empirical results are well-supported
5. Clear connections to previous work while highlighting key differences
6. Well-organized and exceptionally well-written

## Weaknesses

1. The need to know strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) is a limitation that might be addressed in future work (though the authors acknowledge this)
2. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is somewhat brief compared to the main contribution
3. The experiments, while well-designed, could include more diverse problem types beyond the binary classification examples

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and novel, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has clear implications for machine learning practitioners.

The paper is of high quality, clear in its presentation, original in its approach, and significant in its impact. It represents a substantial contribution to the field that would be valuable to the NIPS community.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how your algorithms might be adapted to problems with different strong convexity/concavity properties (e.g., when λ or γ are not known).

2. In Section 6, while the extension to monotone operators is important, it could be slightly expanded to better highlight how this broadens the applicability of your framework.

3. The experiments could be strengthened by including results on a wider range of problem types beyond binary classification.

4. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the relationship between your analysis and the co-coercivity condition used in convex minimization (which you mention in Section 6).

5. Consider adding a brief comparison to the recent work by Davis (2016) on monotone operators, as it appears related to your extension in Section 6.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The results are well-supported, the writing is clear, and the significance is evident. I strongly recommend acceptance.
