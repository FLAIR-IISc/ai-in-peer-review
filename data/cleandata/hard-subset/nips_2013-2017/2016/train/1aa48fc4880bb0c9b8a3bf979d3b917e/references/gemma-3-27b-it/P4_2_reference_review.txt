This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods—specifically, SVRG and SAGA—for solving saddle-point problems, a class of problems frequently arising in machine learning through duality. The authors extend these algorithms from their standard convex minimization setting and provide the first convergence analysis for their application to saddle-point problems. A key contribution is framing saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, allowing them to establish linear convergence under monotonicity conditions that generalize beyond traditional saddle-point settings. They also highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling and demonstrate that the saddle-point formulation necessitates splits different from those used in convex minimization. Finally, they show that these incremental algorithms can be accelerated using a catalyst-like framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVR/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. While variance reduction methods exist for saddle-point problems, this work provides a rigorous convergence analysis tailored to this setting.
* **Generality:** Framing the problem within the theory of monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed algorithms beyond strict saddle-point problems, including variational inequalities.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears robust, leveraging monotonicity properties to achieve linear convergence, a desirable property.
* **Practical Relevance:** The identified importance of non-uniform sampling and the accelerated framework offer practical advantages for real-world applications, as demonstrated by the experiments.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully situates its contributions within the existing literature on convex optimization, saddle-point problems, and stochastic optimization techniques.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide a good empirical evaluation, showcasing the benefits of the proposed methods and demonstrating performance improvements over alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on strong convexity-concavity assumptions (A). The sensitivity of the results to deviations from these assumptions isn't thoroughly investigated.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The optimal choice of step sizes and other hyperparameters, while discussed, could benefit from further guidance. The practical implications of tuning these parameters for various problem instances aren’t fully explored.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The paper uses a lot of notation which sometimes makes it hard to follow the analysis.
* **Minor Technical Details:** While generally well-written, some proofs or technical details could be made more accessible with added explanation. For example, the explanation of how catalyst framework accelerates the convergence is not very detailed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of widely used optimization algorithms to a practically relevant problem setting.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and extends the applicability of the algorithms beyond the traditional saddle-point framework.
* The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods.
* The work addresses a gap in the existing literature and has the potential to impact research in areas such as machine learning and convex optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong convexity-concavity assumptions may limit the applicability of the algorithms in certain scenarios.
* The paper could benefit from further discussion of parameter tuning and its impact on performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are compelling, and the work is well-motivated and clearly presented. The assumptions, while strong, are not uncommon in the field, and the benefits of the proposed methods appear substantial. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide more practical guidance on parameter tuning. The authors have successfully extended established algorithms to a new and important problem setting, and the work is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the NIPS community.
