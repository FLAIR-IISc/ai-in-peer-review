

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper proposes extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems. The authors claim their work provides the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context, with applications to non-separable losses and regularizers in machine learning.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) a convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in saddle-point problems, (2) identification of two different notions of splits for these problems, and (3) an acceleration framework using the "catalyst" approach. The theoretical analysis appears methodologically sound, building on monotone operator theory and extending existing convex minimization techniques. The paper correctly identifies that saddle-point problems require different treatment than convex minimization, particularly regarding the interpretation of the problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator.

The paper presents two main algorithmic contributions: SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems, with convergence guarantees. The experimental section compares these methods against batch forward-backward algorithms, stochastic forward-backward, and primal methods on classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers. The results show that non-uniform sampling versions of SAGA and accelerated SVRG outperform other methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

While the technical content is well-structured and the analysis appears rigorous, I have significant concerns about the novelty and significance of this work. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is relatively straightforward, as the authors themselves acknowledge ("the algorithmic extension is straightforward"). This is not a fundamentally new algorithmic approach but rather a direct application of established techniques to a different problem class.

The paper's claim of "first convergence analysis" for these algorithms in saddle-point problems is questionable. The authors cite several works on saddle-point problems (e.g., [4, 8, 19, 20]) but fail to adequately address how their analysis differs from existing work. For instance, the convergence rate for the batch forward-backward method is cited from [19], but the paper doesn't clearly establish why their analysis for stochastic variants represents a significant advancement.

More critically, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing methods specifically designed for saddle-point problems. The authors reference works on separable saddle-point problems (e.g., [11, 12]), but these methods are not directly comparable to the non-separable case they address. The paper should have compared against specialized saddle-point solvers like the primal-dual hybrid gradient method (PDHG) [20], which is widely used in this context and has established convergence properties.

The experimental results, while showing some improvement over batch methods, do not convincingly demonstrate that these methods are superior to existing specialized approaches for saddle-point problems. The paper compares against "batch forward-backward" but not against methods like PDHG, which are standard for saddle-point problems. The right-hand plot on the rcv1 dataset compares against a primal method ("fba-primal"), but this is not a fair comparison since the problem is separable and primal methods are directly applicable.

The paper also fails to adequately address the practical challenges of implementing these methods for saddle-point problems. For example, the paper mentions that "non-uniform sampling is key to an efficient algorithm," but doesn't provide guidance on how to compute these sampling probabilities in practice for general problems.

While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that these methods provide a significant practical advantage over existing approaches. The claimed "significant acceleration" in Table 1 is based on theoretical complexity bounds, but these don't necessarily translate to practical speedups, especially given the additional bookkeeping required for variance reduction methods.

The paper's main strength is its methodological soundness and clear presentation of the theoretical analysis. However, the significance of the work is limited by the straightforward nature of the extension from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, and the lack of meaningful comparison to existing saddle-point solvers.

In summary, while the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of stochastic variance reduction in a new context, it doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these methods offer practical advantages over existing approaches for saddle-point problems. The novelty is marginal, and the significance is limited. For NIPS, we expect work that provides both methodological novelty and clear practical significance, which this paper doesn't fully deliver.

Pro: Methodologically sound analysis, clear presentation, identifies a useful application context (non-separable losses/regularizers).

Con: Overstates novelty, lacks meaningful comparison to specialized saddle-point methods, minimal practical significance demonstrated, straightforward extension from established techniques.

I recommend rejection. The work is of sufficient technical quality but lacks the novelty and significance required for NIPS, especially given the substantial body of existing literature on saddle-point problems. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the comparison to existing methods and demonstrate clear practical advantages to justify the extension.
