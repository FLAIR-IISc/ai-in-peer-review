

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning through duality (e.g., in supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers). The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, demonstrating that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. They also show how to accelerate these methods using the "catalyst" framework, and extend their analysis to monotone operators. The paper builds on NIPS work like SVRG [2] and SAGA [3], but extends them to a fundamentally different problem structure.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting. The analysis is rigorous and demonstrates how the saddle-point structure differs from convex minimization, particularly regarding the use of monotonicity properties.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The authors identify important machine learning applications where their methods are particularly useful (e.g., non-separable losses/regularizers in supervised learning, robust optimization, and unsupervised learning relaxations). This addresses a gap in the literature where existing fast gradient methods cannot be applied to non-separable problems.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper thoroughly analyzes different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform), different splits (function-based vs. derivative-based), and provides clear complexity comparisons in Table 1. The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability beyond saddle-point problems.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on real-world datasets (sido and rcv1) clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic algorithms, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

## Weaknesses
1. **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption**: The paper assumes strong convexity-concavity (assumption A), which limits applicability to problems where these properties hold. While common in saddle-point problems, this is a restrictive assumption for broader applicability. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide guidance for relaxing it.

2. **Lack of Adaptive Parameter Estimation**: The methods require knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) and the Lipschitz constant L. The paper does not address how to adaptively estimate these parameters, which is a significant practical concern. Recent work on convex minimization (e.g., [3]) has addressed this, but the paper does not extend these adaptive techniques to the saddle-point setting.

3. **Limited Comparison with Saddle-Point Specific Methods**: While the paper compares with batch and stochastic gradient methods, it does not sufficiently contrast with specialized saddle-point algorithms (e.g., [11, 12]), which might provide a more meaningful benchmark for the proposed methods.

4. **Theoretical Clarity**: Some parts of the theoretical analysis (e.g., the extension to monotone operators in Section 6) could benefit from more intuitive explanations to make the results more accessible to a broader audience.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. Address the strong convexity-concavity assumption by discussing potential extensions to weaker conditions or providing adaptive estimation techniques for the parameters λ, γ, and L, similar to recent work in convex optimization.

2. Include a more detailed comparison with saddle-point specific algorithms (e.g., [11, 12]) to better position the contribution within the existing literature.

3. Add a short discussion on how to handle cases where the strong convexity-concavity constants are unknown, perhaps by referencing adaptive techniques from convex optimization.

4. Provide more intuition for the monotone operator extension in Section 6, possibly with a simple example, to enhance accessibility.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a thorough theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The convergence proofs are rigorous and the complexity analysis is comprehensive. However, the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a notable limitation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some theoretical sections could be made more accessible. The problem setup and contributions are well-motivated.

**Originality**: The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is novel, and the analysis provides important theoretical insights that differentiate this work from previous approaches. The connection to monotone operators further broadens the contribution.

**Significance**: The work addresses an important gap in the literature and provides practical benefits for large-scale machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. The acceleration via the catalyst framework is particularly significant for ill-conditioned problems.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and the paper's contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS. With minor revisions addressing the adaptive parameter estimation and improving the comparison with specialized saddle-point methods, the paper would be even stronger. The work advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning and provides valuable tools for practitioners dealing with saddle-point problems.
