- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with rigorous convergence analysis.
- Generalizes analysis beyond saddle-points to monotone operators.
- Highlights importance of problem-specific splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling.
- Introduces an acceleration ("catalyst") framework that improves performance.
- Empirical results on separable and non-separable problems validate theoretical claims and show gains over existing methods.
- Novel contribution: first substantial extension of SVR methods to saddle-point settings.
- Theoretical rigor: detailed convergence proofs leveraging monotone operator properties.
- Practical relevance: addresses common ML formulations where existing methods struggle.
- Experiments are comprehensive for chosen datasets and compare to batch methods and primal SAGA.
- Paper is generally well-written, well-organized, and mathematically accessible.
- Assumptions (strong convexity-concavity, Lipschitz continuity) are relatively strong and could limit applicability.
- Algorithm performance may be sensitive to step-size and parameter tuning; more guidance is needed.
- Experiments are somewhat narrow; broader and larger-scale benchmarks would strengthen claims.
- Description of the catalyst acceleration is theoretical and would benefit from clearer algorithmic detail or pseudocode.
- Lacks thorough comparison to other saddle-point solvers (e.g., proximal point, operator splitting).
- Quality: strong theoretical and empirical contribution.
- Clarity: good overall, though some sections (e.g., splitting strategies) need more clarification.
- Originality: high, with a novel methodological extension.
- Significance: high, with potential broad impact in ML optimization.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically validated, and broadly applicable (monotone operators).
- Arguments against acceptance: strong assumptions and insufficient comparison to alternative saddle-point methods.
- Overall: strong paper recommended for acceptance; addressing parameter sensitivity and broader comparisons would further improve it.
