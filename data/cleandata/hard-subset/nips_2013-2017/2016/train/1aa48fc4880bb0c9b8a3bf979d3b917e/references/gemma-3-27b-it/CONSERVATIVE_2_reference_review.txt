This paper investigates stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems, extending algorithms like SVRG and SAGA to this non-separable setting. The authors present convergence analysis and demonstrate theoretical advantages, particularly through an accelerated variant. While the extension of SVRG/SAGA is conceptually straightforward, the analysis correctly identifies the crucial differences stemming from the saddle-point structure.

The paper's primary strength lies in the theoretical analysis, which establishes convergence guarantees for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. The connection to monotone operator theory provides a broader perspective and potential for generalization. The exploration of different splitting strategies (function-based vs. derivative-based) is insightful, and the demonstration of the importance of non-uniform sampling is a valuable contribution.  The accelerated algorithm based on the “catalyst” framework is a welcome addition and appears to offer genuine performance improvements.

However, I have significant reservations regarding the novelty and practical impact. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, while theoretically interesting, feels incremental.  The core algorithms themselves aren't fundamentally new; rather, the authors adapt existing techniques to a different problem class.  The extensive theoretical analysis, while rigorous, feels disproportionately weighty given the relatively modest algorithmic innovation. Furthermore, the gains demonstrated in Section 7, while positive, are not overwhelming. The comparison to accelerated batch methods is favorable, but the improvements seem heavily dependent on specific parameter settings and problem structures.  

My main concern is the lack of compelling evidence that these algorithms outperform readily available alternatives *in practice*, beyond the specific experimental setup.  The authors claim superiority, but the experiments are limited in scope. The comparison to primal methods (SAGA applied to the primal problem) is important but conducted on a single, separable example.  A more thorough empirical evaluation, including a wider range of datasets and problem sizes, is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits. Specifically, the performance gain of the accelerated SVRG is not clearly established.

The paper also relies heavily on assumptions regarding function smoothness and strong convexity-concavity. While standard in optimization, these assumptions limit the applicability of the results. The authors acknowledge the need for adaptive step-size selection in future work, but this remains an open challenge. The discussion of monotone operators in Section 6 feels somewhat disconnected from the main thrust of the paper and could be streamlined.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis of SVRG/SAGA for saddle-point problems.
*   Extension to monotone operator theory offers a broader perspective.
*   Identifies the importance of non-uniform sampling.
*   Demonstrates potential for acceleration through the catalyst framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental algorithmic contribution – the core idea is adapting existing methods.
*   Limited empirical evaluation – results are not broadly convincing.
*   Heavy reliance on strong convexity-concavity assumptions.
*   Theoretical complexity overshadows the algorithmic novelty.
*   Section 6 feels somewhat tangential.



I believe this paper is a solid, technically sound contribution, but falls short of the NIPS bar for originality and significance. The results, while correct, do not represent a substantial advance in the field.  I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a more theoretically focused venue after conducting a more extensive empirical validation. Specifically, the authors should include experiments demonstrating clear advantages on a more diverse set of datasets and compared to a wider range of existing optimization algorithms applicable to saddle-point problems.  Further work on adaptive step-size selection would also strengthen the paper.
