- Extends SVRG/SAGA variance-reduced methods to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems via monotone-operator splitting A+B.
- Treats A as the proximal/subdifferential of M and B as the smooth monotone operator from K, enabling forward–backward splitting with VR estimators.
- Allows general splits B = ∑_i B_i where B_i need not be gradients, enabling factored/row–column updates for bilinear K.
- Develops non-uniform sampling with a tailored smoothness constant L̄(π) and proves linear convergence for SVRG and SAGA.
- Provides minibatching, storage-efficient factored SAGA, and a catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration that improves iteration complexity.
- Extends analysis beyond saddle points to general monotone inclusions, requiring only monotonicity of the sum operator (no cocoercivity).
- Experiments on two datasets (non-separable and separable benchmarks) show non-uniform SAGA/SVRG outperform batch FB, with accelerated SVRG best on ill-conditioned problems.
- Prior work coverage is thorough, distinguishing this work from SAG/SVRG/SAGA for minimization, separability-based primal–dual/coordinate methods, and cocoercivity-requiring SMART methods.
- Theory relies only on monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity of B, with strong monotonicity from M via a weighted Ω-norm and gives explicit dependence on L, L̄(π), and minibatch size.
- Correctness: assumptions A–C are standard; allowing B_i to be non-gradients is a practical strength; L and L̄(π) must be known or bounded.
- Sampling/complexity: non-uniform distributions (e.g., proportional to row/column norm squares) yield strictly better bounds; complexity distinguishes oracle and prox costs.
- Factored SAGA reduces storage to O(n+d), which is practically important for bilinear problems.
- Experiments are targeted to the non-separable regime and corroborate theory, but are limited in scope (only two datasets, both bilinear).
- Clarity issues: inconsistent notation for µ and parameters (e.g., λ, γ scaling), occasional factor typos, and a need to clarify FB convergence conditions vs extragradient/mirror‑prox.
- Practical guidance lacking on estimating L and L̄(π) and on possible backtracking/adaptive step sizes.
- SAGA non-uniform sampling theory requires resampling memory, but experiments use a mixture strategy without theoretical justification; this gap should be addressed.
- Missing empirical baselines: comparisons to extragradient/mirror‑prox and Chambolle–Pock (with strong convexity) would strengthen empirical claims.
- Scope suggestion: include one non-bilinear smooth K experiment (e.g., generalized linear saddle problems) to demonstrate generality.
- Minor questions: intuition for the FB step-size 1/L^2 in the main text and discussion of when accelerated batch methods might still dominate.
- Originality: first linear-rate VR guarantees for non-separable saddle points under only monotonicity; novel factored split and storage-efficient SAGA; clean proximal-point acceleration.
- Significance: fills an important gap for non-separable saddle-point problems common in ML and robust optimization; non-uniform sampling and factored updates are impactful.
- Pros: strong original theory, careful sampling analysis, practical storage reductions, simple effective acceleration, empirical results consistent with theory.
- Cons: limited experimental breadth, missing comparisons to extragradient baselines, notation/clarity issues, and practical guidance for L/L̄ estimation; theory/practice gap for SAGA resampling.
- Overall recommendation: solid, timely contribution deserving acceptance after minor clarifications and expanded discussion/experiments.
