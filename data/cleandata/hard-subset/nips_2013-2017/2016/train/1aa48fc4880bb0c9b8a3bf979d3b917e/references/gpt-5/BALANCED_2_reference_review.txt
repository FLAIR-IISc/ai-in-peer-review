Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is simple/strongly convex-concave and K is smooth. The key setting is when the smooth operator B=(∂xK, −∂yK) decomposes as a finite sum of Lipschitz components Bi, enabling stochastic updates. The paper extends SVRG and SAGA from convex minimization to this saddle-point setting, providing (i) linear convergence analyses that do not rely on co-coercivity, (ii) importance sampling schemes and two natural decompositions (“individual” vs “factored”), (iii) a catalyst-style acceleration that amounts to regularized proximal-point outer loops with SVRG inner loops, and (iv) an extension to solving zeros of maximal strongly monotone operators plus a Lipschitz sum (variational inequalities).

The authors compare to prior work on variance reduction for finite-sum minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), on primal-dual methods for separable saddle-point problems (e.g., Chambolle–Pock and stochastic/coordinate variants), on stochastic forward–backward for monotone operators (Rosasco–Villa–Vũ), and on VR for co-coercive operators. The novelty here is to obtain linear rates for saddle points/monotone inclusions without co-coercivity, to exploit non-uniform sampling with explicit constants, and to give a simple acceleration via proximal-point regularization. Experiments on classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers show gains over batch and stochastic baselines.

Strengths
- Technical contribution and scope: Provides first (to my knowledge) linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA-like methods on strongly convex–concave composite saddle-point problems and, more broadly, monotone inclusions A+B with A strongly monotone and B a Lipschitz finite sum, without requiring co-coercivity of the Bi. The monotone operator perspective is clean and general.
- Algorithmic design: Two practical decompositions (individual and factored) and importance sampling with explicit constants L̄(π); discussion of storage and cost T(B), T(Bi), Tprox(M).
- Acceleration: Catalyst-like outer regularization is natural in the monotone setting (proximal-point), yields simple analysis and improved complexities; the guidance for choosing τ is appreciated.
- Non-uniform sampling theory: Clear derivations of L̄(π) for bilinear problems and evidence that non-uniform sampling substantially improves bounds and practice.
- Empirical evidence: Demonstrates that uniform sampling can underperform batch methods, while (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling provide consistent gains, especially on ill-conditioned problems.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and scope: All linear-rate theory requires strong convexity–concavity (strong monotonicity). The paper does not provide practical strategies for the common merely convex–concave case (beyond referencing O(1/t) stochastic FB), nor adaptive schemes to unknown strong convexity (acknowledged in the conclusion).
- Baselines and experiments: The empirical section is relatively limited (two datasets) and omits strong primal-dual/extragradient baselines that are standard for saddle-point problems, e.g., Chambolle–Pock (with optimal step-size tuning) and accelerated variants, and extragradient/Mirror-Prox methods. Reporting only “passes over data” and squared distance to an optimizer (how is the ground-truth solution obtained?) leaves open wall-clock, memory, and robustness comparisons. An ablation of individual vs. factored splits, and the effect of resampling for non-uniform SAGA, would strengthen the case.
- Practicality of parameters: The algorithms require L and L̄(π) (or safe upper bounds) and the strong convexity–concavity constants (λ, γ) to set step sizes and epoch lengths. Guidance for estimating these in practice is limited. The analysis also requires a resampling step for non-uniform SAGA; the experiments use a heuristic mixture—this mismatch should be discussed more candidly.
- Choice of forward–backward vs. extragradient: For saddle-point problems, extragradient methods are often preferred to forward–backward-type primal–dual gradient for stability. The paper asserts linear convergence for the FB iteration in strongly convex–concave settings; a more detailed comparison to extragradient (both theoretically and empirically) would clarify trade-offs.
- Storage and scalability: SAGA with individual splits can require O(nd) memory; factored splits mitigate this, but the choice depends on structure. A clearer discussion of when each is viable, and memory vs. speed trade-offs, would be helpful.
- Presentation and references: Some references are incomplete (missing years/venues), and parts of the text have minor typographical issues. The exposition is dense; a self-contained proof sketch for a core theorem (beyond appendices) would aid readability.

Quality
The technical development appears sound under the stated assumptions, with careful complexity accounting and explicit constants. The extension to monotone operators is well-motivated and broadens impact. However, empirical validation is modest and lacks some key baselines and metrics (time, memory).

Clarity
Generally clear but notation-heavy. The distinction between individual and factored splits and their storage/compute implications is useful. The need for resampling in non-uniform SAGA and how the experiments deviate should be more prominently explained. Complete and consistent references are needed.

Originality
Good. Bringing variance reduction to composite saddle-point/monotone inclusion problems without co-coercivity, with non-uniform sampling and a catalyst-style acceleration, is novel relative to standard finite-sum minimization and separable primal-dual methods.

Significance
Potentially high for convex–concave saddle-point optimization and variational inequalities, especially for non-separable ML models. The acceleration and sampling guidance are practically relevant. Wider empirical validation would increase confidence in impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to Chambolle–Pock (and its acceleration) and extragradient/Mirror-Prox with tuned step sizes, and report wall-clock time and memory.
- Provide guidelines or heuristics for estimating L, L̄(π), and strong convexity–concavity, and sensitivity analyses.
- Include ablations on factored vs individual splits, batch size m, and resampling strategies for non-uniform SAGA.
- Clarify how “distance to optimizer” is computed, and complement with primal–dual gap curves.
- Consider adaptive schemes that reduce dependence on known (λ, γ), e.g., restart/regularization schedules.
- Improve reference formatting and fix typos.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a solid and novel theoretical advance with practical algorithmic ideas; despite a somewhat limited empirical section and reliance on strong convexity–concavity, it meets NeurIPS standards in quality and potential impact. Strengthening experiments and practical guidance would make it even more compelling. Pros: novel theory beyond co-coercivity, clear sampling/acceleration story, generality to monotone operators. Cons: strong assumptions, limited baselines/metrics, parameter practicality.
