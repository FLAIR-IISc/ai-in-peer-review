Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The key idea is to exploit a finite-sum-like structure in K via a decomposition of the gradient operator B = (∂xK, −∂yK) as B = Σ_i B_i. Importantly, the B_i need not be gradients of functions, which allows “factored” splits aligned with the saddle structure (e.g., row/column splits for bilinear K).

Building on SVRG and SAGA (NIPS’12–’14), the authors propose variance-reduced forward–backward methods for saddle-point problems that achieve linear convergence under strong convex–concavity. They give complexity bounds that depend on L (Lipschitz constant of B under a weighted norm) and on a sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π). They emphasize non-uniform sampling schemes tailored to bilinear K that lead to provably improved L̄(π). They also adapt the Catalyst (NIPS’15) proximal-point acceleration: solve a sequence of more strongly monotone subproblems with SVRG, yielding improved iteration complexity, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. Finally, they show the analysis extends beyond saddle problems to finding zeros of strongly monotone operators A + Σ_i B_i, without requiring cocoercivity of the B_i, thus subsuming convex minimization as a special case.

Compared to prior work: SAG/SVRG/SAGA target finite-sum convex minimization; primal–dual and PDHG/Chambolle–Pock-type methods address saddle problems but are batch; stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao; Zhu & Storkey) exploit separability and coordinate updates. This paper appears to be the first to give linear-rate variance-reduced methods for general (not necessarily separable) composite saddle-point problems, and to articulate factored decompositions and non-uniform sampling within that setting. The Catalyst adaptation to saddle-point problems is also new and simpler here due to proximal-point structure.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Clear extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with a careful operator-splitting viewpoint. The convergence analysis uses monotonicity rather than cocoercivity, broadening applicability to general convex–concave problems and variational inequalities.
- Sampling and decomposition: The distinction between individual and factored splits and the role of non-uniform sampling are well-motivated and lead to sharper L̄(π) bounds for bilinear K.
- Acceleration: The Catalyst-like outer loop is adapted convincingly, with explicit complexity trade-offs and a simple rule for τ. The conclusion that variance-reduced methods can outperform accelerated batch methods under realistic regimes is supported theoretically and empirically.
- Practical aspects: Mini-batching, storage trade-offs (SAGA O(n+d) memory for factored bilinear splits), and initialization details are discussed. Experiments on non-separable loss/regularizer settings demonstrate improvements over batch and plain stochastic FB, and show the importance of non-uniform sampling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumptions and scope: Linear rates require strong convex–concavity (known λ, γ). Many saddle-point problems lack strong convexity in both variables; while the proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper can induce strong monotonicity, the practical cost/accuracy trade-off for the inner solves is not fully explored experimentally. Clarify applicability when only one side is strongly convex or when M is merely convex–concave.
- Competing methods: The comparison set omits classical extragradient/Mirror-Prox variants (which, under strong monotonicity, can achieve linear rates) and modern primal–dual accelerated methods beyond the bilinear case. A discussion or empirical comparison to (stochastic) extragradient variants would strengthen the case.
- Step-size and constants: The algorithms require L and L̄(π). Guidance for estimating these in practice, or adaptive strategies, would improve applicability. The paper notes the need for resampling in non-uniform SAGA for the theory, but experiments use mixtures without resampling; discussing the theoretical gap would be useful.
- Experimental coverage: Only two datasets and one non-separable setting are shown. Ablations isolating the effects of sampling (uniform vs non-uniform), decomposition (individual vs factored), and mini-batch size m would substantiate the practical claims. Including problems with non-bilinear K would test generality.
- Clarity and organization: Some derivations (e.g., the exact rate for batch FB and the choice σ=1/L^2) would benefit from more intuition. The roles of L, L̄(π), and norms ‖K‖op/‖K‖F/‖K‖max could be summarized more systematically. A concise table with storage and per-iteration costs for each variant would help.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The methods are well-defined; the analysis is technically sound given the stated assumptions. The monotone operator perspective is appropriate and extends the scope. Some implementation/theory gaps (e.g., SAGA resampling) should be clarified. Proof details are deferred to the appendix; key steps are plausible.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with precise assumptions (A–C), algorithms, and theorems. Clarity can be improved for step-size guidance, practical parameter choices, and a more self-contained presentation of the acceleration scheme.
- Originality: High. Extending variance reduction to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems with linear rates and non-uniform sampling/factored splits appears new. The monotone-operator extension without cocoercivity is a notable generalization.
- Significance: High. Many ML problems admit saddle formulations without separability. The results provide practically relevant algorithms with favorable complexity, and the acceleration is impactful on ill-conditioned problems.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a brief comparison or discussion of (stochastic) extragradient/Mirror-Prox methods and how the proposed methods differ in assumptions and rates.
- Provide practical heuristics for estimating L and L̄(π), and a short note on adaptive step sizes.
- Expand experiments: ablations on sampling and decomposition choices; at least one non-bilinear K example; include a stochastic extragradient baseline if feasible.
- Clarify the necessity and impact of resampling in non-uniform SAGA and reconcile with the experimental protocol.
- Soften statements such as “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” by specifying parameter regimes.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and technically solid extension of SVRG/SAGA to general saddle-point problems with linear rates.
- Careful treatment of sampling and decomposition yielding improved complexities.
- Effective and simple acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst.
- Broader applicability via monotone operator framework.

Cons:
- Dependence on strong convex–concavity and knowledge of constants; limited guidance on adaptivity.
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow and lacks some key baselines/ablations.
- Some theoretical–practical gaps (non-uniform SAGA resampling) and limited discussion of alternative first-order saddle-point methods.

Overall, the paper makes a substantial and timely contribution to optimization for saddle-point problems and variational inequalities, with clear theoretical advances and promising empirical results.
