## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the setting of saddle-point problems, arising frequently in machine learning via duality. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context, highlighting differences from the convex minimization case, and establish connections to monotone operator theory.  Key contributions include demonstrating the necessity of splitting the saddle-point problem in specific ways, the importance of non-uniform sampling, and an acceleration technique built on the "catalyst" framework. The work also highlights applicability beyond saddle-points, to variational inequalities. Empirical validation on both separable and non-separable problems demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. While these algorithms are well-studied for convex optimization, their adaptation and analysis for saddle-point problems are novel.  The connection to monotone operator theory expands the scope of the work.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, demonstrating linear convergence under appropriate assumptions. The analysis leveraging monotonicity is particularly insightful and provides broader applicability. The exploration of different splitting strategies and their impact on convergence is valuable.
* **Practical Relevance:** Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (regularized learning, robust optimization, etc.), making the work directly relevant to the community.  The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, especially the accelerated variant.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough exploration of different sampling strategies, their theoretical properties, and their impact on performance.  The analysis of the bilinear saddle-point case is particularly detailed.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the necessary background and motivation. The discussion of the differences between convex optimization and saddle-point settings is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumptions regarding strong convexity-concavity (Assumption A) and Lipschitz continuity (Assumption B) are standard but can be restrictive in practice. While acknowledged, the paper doesn't delve into the robustness of the algorithms under weaker conditions or discuss adaptive parameter tuning strategies to mitigate the impact of inaccurate estimates of these parameters.
* **Complexity of Accelerated Version:**  While the acceleration scheme is promising, the discussion of its implementation and the trade-off in selecting the acceleration parameter `τ` could be more detailed. The reliance on knowing/estimating smoothness constants impacts practical usability.
* **Experimental Setup:**  The experimental section, while demonstrating improvements, could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with alternative saddle-point solvers (e.g., ADMM, other proximal algorithms). Further investigation into the sensitivity of performance to different regularization strengths (as briefly mentioned) would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on a specific set of problems. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of saddle-point applications would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Catalyst implementation details:** The implementation details of the accelerated version (Algorithm 3) and how the parameter `τ` is adapted were somewhat unclear and could benefit from further clarification.




**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. It represents a complete and valuable contribution.

**Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear and well-organized, although some sections (e.g., the accelerated algorithm) could benefit from further clarification. 

**Originality:** The work is highly original in extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems and providing a dedicated theoretical analysis.

**Significance:** The results are important and address a challenging problem in machine learning. The proposed algorithms are likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners working with saddle-point formulations. The connection to monotone operator theory broadens the impact of the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  Novel and significant extension of popular optimization algorithms to a critical class of problems.
*  Strong theoretical guarantees and insightful analysis.
*  Demonstrated empirical effectiveness.
*  Addresses a gap in the literature regarding efficient solvers for saddle-point problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Assumptions regarding strong convexity-concavity and Lipschitz continuity might limit practical applicability.
*  Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*  The accelerated version's implementation and parameter tuning could be clarified further.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong accept** paper. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of optimization, providing novel algorithms and a rigorous analysis for solving saddle-point problems. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall value of the work.  I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
