### Summary

This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods for solving composite decomposable saddle-point problems. The authors extend the SVRG and SAGA algorithms, originally developed for strongly-convex finite-sum minimization, to the saddle-point setting of `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`. The key assumption is that the smooth convex-concave function `K` has a gradient operator that can be decomposed into a sum of many components, `B = Σ B_i`. This structure is common in machine learning problems, particularly those with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation), which cannot be efficiently handled by existing variance-reduced methods that require separability in the primal or dual objective.

The main contributions of the paper are:
1.  The first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting, demonstrating linear convergence. The analysis is novel as it relies only on the monotonicity of the saddle-point gradient operator, in contrast to standard analyses for convex minimization which leverage co-coercivity—a property not generally held by saddle-point operators. This generalization extends the applicability of these methods to the broader class of finding zeros of monotone operators.
2.  An exploration of problem structures unique to the saddle-point formulation, such as "factored" splits of the operator and the critical role of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal theoretical rates.
3.  An acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework, which further improves the convergence rate and yields an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods.

The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical comparison of their proposed methods against existing batch and stochastic algorithms, showing significant improvements in complexity. These theoretical gains are validated empirically on binary classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims of linear convergence for the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants are supported by theorems with proofs referenced in the appendix. The core of the analysis, which uses the monotone operator framework, is a strong theoretical contribution that correctly identifies the essential properties needed for convergence in this more general setting. The paper is a complete piece of work, presenting new algorithms, a rigorous theoretical analysis, an acceleration method, and empirical validation. The authors are careful in their presentation, clearly laying out assumptions and providing a detailed complexity analysis in Table 1 that compares various norms and sampling schemes, highlighting the strengths of their proposed methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and a concise summary of contributions. The problem formulation and assumptions are formally stated in Section 2, which also provides relevant machine learning examples and a review of existing algorithms, effectively situating the work within the literature. The proposed algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and the accelerated version) are presented sequentially and clearly described. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is a valuable addition that elegantly contextualizes the theoretical analysis. The experimental section is clear, and the results presented in the figures effectively support the paper's claims. The level of detail is sufficient for an expert to understand the methods and their analysis.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the algorithmic templates for SVRG and SAGA are familiar, their application and analysis in the context of general saddle-point problems are novel. Prior work on variance reduction has focused almost exclusively on the convex minimization setting. The key originality lies in the convergence analysis. By eschewing the co-coercivity assumption and relying solely on monotonicity, the authors provide a new analytical framework for variance reduction that is broader than existing ones. This allows them to establish the first linear convergence rates for stochastic/incremental methods on this class of saddle-point problems, a significant departure from the sublinear O(1/t) rates of previous stochastic approaches. The discussion on different splitting strategies (e.g., "factored" splits) and the emphasis on non-uniform sampling are also novel contributions specific to the saddle-point structure. Related work is cited appropriately.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a strong impact. Saddle-point formulations are fundamental to many areas of machine learning, including robust optimization, structured prediction, and training generative adversarial networks, among others. In particular, the ability to efficiently handle problems with non-separable components in either the loss or regularizer opens the door to applying powerful variance-reduction techniques to a wider range of practical models. The presented algorithms advance the state of the art by providing a faster alternative to both batch methods (which are slow on large datasets) and standard stochastic methods (which have slow convergence). Practitioners and researchers working on problems that fit this composite saddle-point structure are likely to adopt these algorithms. Furthermore, the theoretical connection to monotone operators is of independent interest and may spur further research into variance reduction for a broader class of variational inequalities and related problems.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Novelty:** Proposes the first linearly convergent, stochastic variance-reduced methods for a general class of composite saddle-point problems. The convergence analysis based on monotonicity is a key theoretical innovation.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a broad and important class of problems in machine learning where existing variance-reduced methods are not applicable. The proposed methods offer significant theoretical and practical speedups over existing alternatives.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong, with rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   The practical utility of the methods relies on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity parameters (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants, which may not always be readily available. The authors acknowledge the former as an area for future work.
*   The algorithmic structures are direct extensions of SVRG/SAGA, with the primary novelty residing in the analysis. However, given the challenge and importance of this extension, this is a minor criticism.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a substantial and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
