- Studies composite convex-concave saddle problems min_x max_y K+M with proximable strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave M and smooth decomposable K, via monotone-operator splitting A=(∂xM,−∂yM), B=(∂xK,−∂yK).  
- Extends variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA) to the forward-backward setting without relying on co-coercivity.  
- Assumes B decomposes as ∑i Bi and defines a sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π); proves linear convergence for SVRG (epochal) and SAGA (online) with non-uniform sampling and mini-batching.  
- Provides complexity bounds in terms of full B cost, single Bi cost, and prox of M; gives explicit sharp bounds for bilinear problems using Frobenius and row/column norms.  
- Proposes a proximal-point “catalyst” acceleration for SVRG with simplified analysis and complexity ~ L̄√(T(B) max_i T(Bi)).  
- Notes analysis uses only monotonicity (not co-coercivity), extending to broader monotone inclusion problems.  
- Experiments on two datasets with non-separable loss/regularizer (plus one separable case) show benefits from non-uniform sampling and acceleration over batch FB.  
- Connects variance reduction to primal-dual/monotone operator methods, filling a gap left by separability-dependent stochastic primal-dual methods.  
- Highlights novelty of avoiding co-coercivity assumption, a core technical contribution.  
- Theoretical strengths: linear rates with explicit constants, weighted-norm handling of λ,γ, and informative non-uniform sampling analysis.  
- Practical insights: trade-offs for factored vs. individual splits, mini-batching to amortize prox cost, and importance of non-uniform sampling.  
- Acceleration strength: simple proximal-point-based catalyst variant with clear complexity trade-offs.  
- Applicability: handles non-separable losses/regularizers with available prox, a regime not covered by prior variance-reduced primal methods.  
- Raises concern: claim that forward-backward (FB) is linearly convergent under given assumptions is non-standard; needs clarification of exact assumptions and metric (Ω/Ω*) used.  
- Notation issues: inconsistent use of “L2” vs L^2 and σ values (e.g., σ=1/(2L) vs σ=1/L^2) creates confusion; request to unify notation.  
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a post-update resampling in theory but experiments omit it—request ablation quantifying impact and discussing the theory-practice gap.  
- Experimental limitations: only two datasets, no wall-clock timings, missing error bars, and lack of extra-gradient/mirror-prox baselines; request reporting cost to compute non-uniform probabilities.  
- Practical assumptions concern: method assumes known λ,γ and L/L̄ and efficient prox for complex M—ask for sensitivity analysis to misspecification and adaptive/backtracking approaches.  
- Monotone operator extension: ask for brief comparison to Tseng’s FBF, mirror-prox, and SMART (Davis 2016) regarding assumptions and constants.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel variance-reduction extension to monotone operators without co-coercivity, strong theory, insightful sampling, and simple acceleration.  
- Cons for acceptance: unclear FB convergence claims, limited empirical evaluation and baselines, and reliance on known constants and efficient prox without robustness checks.  
- Key questions for authors: precisely state FB rate conditions and contrast with counterexamples; sensitivity to misestimation of L, L̄, λ, γ and possibility of adaptive tuning; empirical effect of SAGA resampling with non-uniform sampling; whether extragradient steps can combine with variance-reduced estimators.  
- Overall evaluation: high-quality, original work with significant potential impact pending clarifications on FB convergence, improved notation, and strengthened experimental validation.
