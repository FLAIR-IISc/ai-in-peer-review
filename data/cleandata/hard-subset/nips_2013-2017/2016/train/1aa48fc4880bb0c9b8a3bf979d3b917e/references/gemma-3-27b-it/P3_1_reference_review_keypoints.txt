- Paper extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, offering convergence analysis and practical algorithms.
- Frames saddle-point problems as zeros of monotone operators, broadening theoretical applicability.
- Investigates different splitting strategies (function-based vs derivative-based) and emphasizes non-uniform sampling.
- Builds on prior work including forward-backward splitting and the catalyst framework for acceleration.
- Novelty: first rigorous extension of SVR methods to the saddle-point setting with meaningful theoretical contributions.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear assumptions (A, B, C) and derived convergence rates.
- Practical relevance: highlights importance of non-uniform sampling and splitting choices for algorithm design.
- Experiments on real datasets (sido, rcv1) validate methods, with accelerated SVRG performing well in some cases.
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, easing comprehension of technical results.
- Generalization to monotone operators is a significant conceptual contribution.
- Assumption of strong convexity-concavity is strong and may limit practical applicability; behavior under weaker assumptions is not analyzed.
- Mini-batch size m significantly affects performance, but guidance on selecting m is limited.
- Accelerated SVRG is not consistently superior in experiments, especially with uniform sampling; conditions for its advantage are unclear.
- Notation can be dense and may hinder accessibility for less-expert readers.
- Request to discuss computational cost of proximal operators for motivating examples (grouped norms, total variation).
- Suggest expanding the explanation of how the catalyst framework is adapted to the saddle-point setting.
- Recommend reporting confidence intervals or standard deviations in experiments for robustness.
- Ask for translation of convergence rates into function/gradient evaluation counts to assess practical cost.
- For acceptance: novelty, rigorous analysis, strong experiments, and broad applicability via monotone operator link.
- Against acceptance: strong assumptions, inconsistent acceleration gains, and complex notation.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address weaker-assumption behavior, mini-batch guidance, acceleration conditions, and notation simplification.
