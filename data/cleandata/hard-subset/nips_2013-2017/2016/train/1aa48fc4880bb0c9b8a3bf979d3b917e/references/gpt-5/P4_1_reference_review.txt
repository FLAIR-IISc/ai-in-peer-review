Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The main contributions are:
- Extending variance-reduced methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, with linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity.
- Analyses that rely on monotonicity of the associated operator B(x,y)=(∂xK, −∂yK), allowing extensions beyond saddle points to finding zeros of sums of monotone operators A+B (A strongly monotone with known resolvent, Bi Lipschitz, ∑Bi monotone).
- Two notions of decompositions for B (sum of components, and a factored row/column split for bilinear K), and the utility of non-uniform sampling schemes. Complexity bounds are given in terms of problem-dependent norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) and sampling distributions.
- An acceleration via a proximal-point (catalyst) scheme that wraps SVRG and yields improved complexity, with a simple analysis in the monotone operator view.
- Empirical evaluations on binary classification with non-separable loss and regularizer and on a separable case, showing superiority of (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA over batch methods and the importance of non-uniform sampling.

This work relates to stochastic variance reduction for finite sums (SAG, SVRG, SAGA), primal-dual and saddle-point methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock, Arrow–Hurwicz), and monotone operator theory (Rockafellar). Prior stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods typically exploit separability; the present paper targets non-separable settings. The monotone operator literature (e.g., Mirror-Prox/extragradient methods by Nemirovski and Juditsky for VIs) is also relevant comparators; the paper emphasizes forward–backward-type updates and proximal point acceleration rather than extragradient, and does not rely on cocoercivity.

Evaluation

Quality (technical soundness)
- The algorithms are natural and well-specified variants of SVRG/SAGA where the variance-reduced direction is applied through a proximal forward–backward step on the composite monotone inclusion A+B. Step sizes and epoch lengths are chosen from Lipschitz constants L and sampling-dependent L̄(π). Linear rates are established for strongly convex–concave problems.
- The extension to general monotone operators is clearly stated: A needs to be strongly monotone with accessible resolvent; Bi need only be Lipschitz and sum to a monotone B. Avoiding cocoercivity is significant and broadens applicability.
- The acceleration via a catalyst/proximal-point outer loop is technically clean; the rate follows from linear convergence of proximal point with parameter τ and the inner SVRG complexity scaling as O(T(B) + L̄^2/(1+τ)^2 max T(Bi)). The choice of τ balances inner and outer costs.
- Sampling and complexity analyses for bilinear K are careful, including factored vs individual splits and non-uniform distributions proportional to row/column leverage-like quantities. The bounds interpolate between ‖K‖op and ‖K‖F and demonstrate formal advantages over batch FB and uniform sampling.
- Assumptions are standard for linear rates (strong convexity–concavity; availability of prox for M). The requirement to know or upper bound L and L̄ is explicit; the paper notes practical replacements if L is unknown at some cost.
- One subtlety: forward–backward on general monotone inclusions is known to require cocoercivity for global convergence; the paper restricts to strongly convex–concave K+M and uses a weighted-norm Lipschitz constant L of B to justify linear convergence with σ=1/L^2. This distinction is acknowledged, with proofs deferred to the appendix. Providing an explicit comparison to extragradient/Mirror-Prox (which do not require cocoercivity and are standard for saddle points) would strengthen the positioning.
- The SAGA analysis for non-uniform sampling requires an extra resampling step; experiments use a practical mixture without resampling. Clarifying theoretical guarantees for the implemented variant would be useful.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written overall, with precise assumptions, algorithm pseudocode, and complexity expressions. The weighted norm Ω and dual Ω* are defined and used consistently.
- Suggestions: (i) explicitly define µ (the strong monotonicity parameter) early and relate it to (λ,γ) to avoid mixing notations (L^2 vs L); (ii) give more intuition for why σ=1/L^2 arises in FB here; (iii) include a brief complexity table for the accelerated batch variant beyond bilinear K; (iv) discuss implementation details and cost of non-uniform sampling (precomputing and updating (KK^T)jj and (K^TK)kk).

Originality
- To the best of knowledge at the time, this provides the first linear-convergence variance-reduced methods tailored to non-separable composite saddle-point problems, and a monotone-operator analysis that does not rely on cocoercivity. The factored split and non-uniform sampling design for bilinear operators is a useful contribution. The catalyst acceleration adapted to the saddle-point/monotone context with a simplified analysis is also novel.

Significance
- Many modern ML problems naturally fit the composite saddle point form with non-separable loss/regularizers; bringing the benefits of variance reduction to this setting is valuable. The theoretical improvements (rates and complexities) and the empirical gains suggest practical impact. The monotone operator extension broadens the scope to VIs and related problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and monotone operators with linear rates.
- Well-motivated sampling schemes with concrete complexity gains; factored storage reduces memory.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point wrapping, improving over batch accelerations.
- Solid empirical evidence, including ablations on sampling and comparison to primal methods when applicable.

Cons and limitations
- Empirical evaluation is limited in breadth; more datasets and tasks (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction, TV-based imaging) would bolster claims.
- No direct comparison to extragradient/Mirror-Prox (deterministic or stochastic) baselines; positioning versus these classics is underdeveloped.
- Practical guidance on selecting/estimating L and L̄ and on step-size tuning is limited; adaptivity would increase robustness.
- SAGA storage for individual splits is O(nd), which can be prohibitive; discussion of when factored splits are viable and their effect on variance would help.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors provide comparisons to (stochastic) Mirror-Prox or PDHG with over-relaxation, especially under strong convexity–concavity where linear rates may be expected?
- How sensitive are the methods to misspecification of L and L̄? Are backtracking or adaptive step-size variants feasible?
- For non-uniform SAGA without post-update resampling, is there a theoretical guarantee for the mixed sampling heuristic used in experiments?
- Could the factored split be combined with sketching/preconditioning to further reduce L̄?
- Please clarify the cost model for non-uniform sampling: preprocessing time, storage of probabilities, and dynamic updates if K changes or if reweighting is used.

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent notation for strong convexity–concavity (λ,γ vs µ) across sections. 
- Add citations to Nemirovski’s Mirror-Prox/extragradient methods for saddle-point/VIs as related baselines. 
- A brief discussion of memory–time trade-offs for SAGA (individual vs factored) would aid practitioners.
