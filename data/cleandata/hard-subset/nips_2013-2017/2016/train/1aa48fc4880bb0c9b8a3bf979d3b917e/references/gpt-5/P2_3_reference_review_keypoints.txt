- Studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K+M where K is smooth and M is strongly convex-concave with a computable prox.
- Extends SVRG/SAGA to saddle points by treating B=(∂xK,−∂yK) as a sum of Lipschitz forward operators (not necessarily gradients).
- Uses forward-backward iterations (prox for M, forward for K) with variance-reduced stochastic estimates of B.
- Designs non-uniform sampling and “factored” splits tailored to bilinear K to reduce variance and storage.
- Analyzes problem as finding a zero of A+B using monotone operator theory, with A strongly monotone and B monotone and Lipschitz.
- Proves linear convergence for SVRG and SAGA under strong convexity-concavity and derives complexities in L and sampling-dependent L̄(π).
- Shows catalyst-style regularization yields further acceleration.
- Framework extends beyond saddle points to general monotone inclusions.
- Empirical results (binary classification) show consistent improvements over batch and stochastic forward-backward methods.
- In separable cases, comparisons to primal SAGA show specialized primal methods can be faster.
- Novelty: first linear-convergence analysis of SVRG/SAGA for non-separable saddle points and for strongly monotone + Lipschitz monotone operators without cocoercivity.
- Operator-level decomposition (not requiring component functions) and factored split/storage for bilinear K are practically important innovations.
- Analysis avoids cocoercivity assumptions and leverages strong monotonicity appropriately; theory appears technically sound.
- Assumptions (strong convexity-concavity, availability of prox_M) are standard; requirement to know λ,γ,L,L̄ for stepsizes is reasonable but could be relaxed.
- Non-uniform sampling design and L̄(π)-based bounds are carefully derived and yield better constants for bilinear K.
- Algorithms are clearly specified and the factored split achieves O(n+d) memory for bilinear problems.
- Experiments support theoretical claims but are limited to two datasets and lack broader baselines (e.g., SPDC, advanced primal-dual methods).
- Clarity is generally good, but some notation (e.g., μ, L vs L^2) and abrupt constant introductions could be tightened.
- Many proofs are in the appendix; a higher-level proof sketch in the main text would aid intuition.
- Practical guidance for estimating/tuning L, L̄, λ, γ and adaptive stepsizes is limited and should be addressed.
- Pros: solid general theory, novel operator-level extension, practical factored variant, non-uniform sampling, catalyst acceleration, empirical gains.
- Cons: limited experimental breadth, notation/stepsize clarity issues, limited practical heuristics for constants, SAGA resampling caveat.
- Suggestions: clarify μ and notation, give heuristics for non-uniform probabilities beyond bilinear K, discuss overhead of computing distributions, add comparisons to SPDC and tuned primal-dual methods, address robustness to mis-specified constants and possibility of backtracking/adaptive stepsizes, include a main-text contraction sketch.
- Overall: strong, timely contribution that merits acceptance with minor clarifications and broader empirical comparisons.
