Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stochastic variance-reduced methods for convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), with M proximable and strongly convex-concave and K smooth. The authors show that SVRG and SAGA can be extended to this setting, yielding the first linear convergence analyses for variance-reduced methods on general (non-separable) saddle-point problems. The analysis operates through the lens of finding zeros of monotone operators, without relying on co-coercivity, which is critical because the gradient field in saddle problems is monotone but not co-coercive in general. They also emphasize two practically important decompositions of B(x,y)=(∂_x K, −∂_y K): (i) sums over individual components, and (ii) “factored” row/column splits tailored to bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, allowing O(n+d) storage for SAGA in that case. The paper derives non-uniform sampling schemes with improved smoothness constants L̄(π) and provides complexity comparisons vs batch forward-backward (FB) and its acceleration. An acceleration via a proximal-point/Catalyst-like outer loop is proposed; the authors argue that in the saddle/monotone operator setting the analysis is simpler than in convex minimization. They further claim the analysis extends to broader monotone inclusions (A + ∑ B_i = 0) with A maximal strongly monotone and B monotone Lipschitz, thereby covering variational inequalities in addition to saddle-point problems. Experiments on two classification tasks illustrate gains over batch and naïve stochastic methods, and highlight the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

This work is well positioned relative to: SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization [1–3,24]; stochastic primal–dual methods for separable saddle problems [11,12,20]; Catalyst acceleration [7]; and operator-theoretic frameworks for FB/proximal-point [8,19,27]. The paper also distinguishes itself from variance-reduced methods relying on co-coercivity (e.g., [30]) and from separability-dependent primal-dual coordinate methods such as SPDC [12], which do not apply to the non-separable settings targeted here.

Strengths
- Quality: The algorithms are simple, and the theoretical results are clearly stated with linear rates for SVRG/SAGA under strong convexity-concavity. The operator-theoretic analysis that avoids co-coercivity is technically nontrivial and broadly applicable. The sampling analysis for bilinear problems is careful and yields meaningful improvements with non-uniform choices tied to row/column norms.
- Originality: Extending variance reduction to general convex-concave saddle problems (and more general monotone inclusions) with linear convergence appears novel at the time and not a straightforward corollary of convex minimization results. The “factored” decomposition and non-uniform sampling tailored to bilinear K are nice ideas with concrete benefits.
- Significance: Many machine learning problems involve non-separable losses/regularizers or arise naturally via duality, where existing VR methods in the primal/dual are inapplicable. Providing VR with provable linear rates in this broader class is potentially impactful. The acceleration strategy further improves practical relevance.
- Clarity: The main ideas are communicated well; assumptions and constants (L, L̄(π), λ, γ) are explicit, and algorithmic pseudocode is provided. The relationship to FB/accelerated FB is helpful for context.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Quality and empirical evaluation:
  - Experiments are limited (two datasets) and mostly report progress in terms of passes rather than wall-clock time. Since per-iteration costs differ across methods (e.g., storage/lookup for SAGA, proximal costs), wall-clock comparisons would strengthen the empirical case.
  - Comparisons to strong baselines for separable cases (e.g., SPDC [12], Chambolle-Pock variants with diagonal preconditioning [20]) are missing. While the paper’s focus is non-separable settings, including such comparisons on rcv1 would help position the gains relative to the best separable methods.
  - The necessity and practical impact of the “resampling” step in SAGA with non-uniform sampling is discussed but not ablated; more empirical clarity would be useful.
- Practicality and assumptions:
  - The analysis and step sizes require knowledge of strong convexity/concavity constants and smoothness constants (L, L̄(π)), which can be hard to estimate in general. Heuristics or adaptive schemes (akin to [3]) would make the methods more plug-and-play.
  - SAGA storage for the “individual” split can be O(nd) and impractical for large problems. The paper notes the advantage of the factored split, but a clearer guidance on when each split is preferable and how to implement memory-efficiently would be helpful.
  - For general non-bilinear K, computing good non-uniform probabilities and L̄(π) is unclear; more discussion or proxies would aid adoption.
- Clarity:
  - The cost model should more explicitly map theoretical terms (T(B), T(B_i), T_prox) to practical operations for the non-bilinear case.
  - Some proofs and parameter schedules are deferred; a brief intuition in the main text about why the operator-based step sizes guarantee linear convergence without co-coercivity would improve readability.
- Scope:
  - The extension to general monotone operators is promising but only validated empirically on saddle-point problems. A small synthetic variational inequality example would substantiate the claimed generality.
  - The acceleration scheme introduces additional parameters (τ, epoch frequencies). A short sensitivity study or guideline for tuning would be valuable.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of variance reduction to a broader and practically important class (non-separable saddle-point problems) with new analysis tools.
- Linear convergence guarantees under standard strong convexity-concavity; acceleration via a proximal-point outer loop.
- Non-uniform sampling and “factored” decompositions yield tangible improvements in both theory and practice.
- Potentially influential operator-theoretic perspective extending beyond saddle points.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is relatively modest in breadth and lacks wall-clock and stronger baselines when separability holds.
- Practical guidance for tuning/estimating constants and for handling memory/storage trade-offs is limited.
- Claims of generality to monotone inclusions are not empirically demonstrated beyond saddle-point instances.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution with straightforward, implementable algorithms and nontrivial analysis that broadens where variance reduction can be used. Strengthening the empirical section (wall-clock, additional baselines, and ablations) and adding practical guidance on parameter estimation would further improve the paper.

Questions for the authors
- How do you choose or estimate λ, γ, L, and L̄(π) in practice for non-bilinear K? Can you provide an adaptive variant that avoids knowing these constants?
- Can you report wall-clock results and memory usage, and compare to SPDC/Chambolle-Pock on the separable task?
- For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, how sensitive is performance to the resampling step you recommend theoretically?
- Can you include a small experiment on a variational inequality to illustrate the monotone operator generality?
