

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SAGA, SVRG) to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this setting. The authors demonstrate that these methods can be readily extended to saddle-point problems, but the convergence analysis differs significantly from convex minimization due to the nature of saddle-point problems. The work builds upon established stochastic optimization literature [1-3] but addresses a different class of problems where the objective function is not separable in the usual sense (as a sum of individual loss terms).

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a rigorous convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting using monotonicity properties rather than strong convexity; (2) the insight that saddle-point problems allow for different notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) and that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency; and (3) an acceleration framework using the "catalyst" approach [7] that yields improved complexity bounds.

Strengths:
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful development of convergence guarantees under appropriate assumptions. The connection to monotone operator theory in Section 6 significantly broadens the applicability of the results.
- The paper provides comprehensive complexity analysis, showing that non-uniform sampling always yields better bounds than uniform sampling, which is confirmed in experiments.
- The experimental section demonstrates clear performance improvements over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems (middle vs. left plots in Figure 7), and shows that the proposed methods can handle non-separable losses and regularizers where existing approaches fail.
- The paper correctly identifies limitations (e.g., needing to know strong convexity-concavity constants) and suggests future work on adaptivity.

Weaknesses:
- The comparison with specialized saddle-point algorithms (e.g., [11, 12]) is somewhat limited. While the paper acknowledges these methods, it could better clarify how the proposed approach compares to them.
- The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is valuable, but the paper could more explicitly discuss how this relates to existing work on monotone operators (e.g., [29]).
- The experimental validation is limited to two datasets (sido and rcv1), and more comprehensive testing across diverse problem instances would strengthen the claims.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper provides the first convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction methods in the saddle-point setting, addressing a gap in the literature.
- The theoretical results are sound and significantly advance the state of the art in optimization for machine learning.
- The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the results beyond saddle-point problems.
- The experimental results clearly demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is conceptually straightforward, making the novelty somewhat incremental.
- The paper does not fully address how the proposed methods compare to specialized saddle-point algorithms that may already exist for specific problem classes.
- The experimental section could be strengthened with more extensive testing across diverse problem instances.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature by providing the first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for saddle-point problems. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the practical advantages are clearly demonstrated. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning through duality, making this work broadly applicable. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly valuable, as it shows the results apply to a much broader class of problems beyond saddle-point formulations.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The convergence analysis correctly identifies the differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization, and the theoretical results are carefully developed. The experiments provide strong empirical support for the claims.

This work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded and practically effective approach to solving saddle-point problems at scale. The results are likely to be valuable for researchers working on machine learning problems that can be formulated as saddle-point problems, including supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and makes a substantive contribution to the field.
