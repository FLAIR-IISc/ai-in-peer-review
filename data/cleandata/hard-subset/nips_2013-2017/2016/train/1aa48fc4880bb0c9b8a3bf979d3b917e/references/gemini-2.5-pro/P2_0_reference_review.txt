### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the highly successful variance reduction techniques, SVRG and SAGA, from the standard convex minimization setting to the more general class of composite saddle-point problems. The authors tackle problems of the form `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is a smooth function that can be decomposed into a sum of components, and `M` is a "simple" (proximable) but potentially non-smooth strongly convex-concave function.

The main contributions are:
1.  The first linear convergence rate analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting. The analysis is notably based on the more general framework of monotone operators, which does not require the co-coercivity assumption used in many convex optimization analyses and thus applies to a broader class of problems.
2.  An exploration of practical and theoretical aspects unique to the saddle-point structure, such as different ways to decompose the problem ("factored" splits) and a demonstration that non-uniform sampling is crucial for achieving state-of-the-art performance.
3.  The application of the "Catalyst" acceleration framework to their SVRG variant, resulting in an accelerated algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods in certain regimes.
4.  Experimental validation on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers, demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Review

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It bridges an important gap by extending powerful variance reduction methods to the saddle-point setting, which is of growing importance in areas like robust optimization, GANs, and duality-based learning. The theoretical analysis is novel and strong, and the practical insights are well-supported by experiments.

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is the core strength. Instead of a simple adaptation of existing proofs, the authors correctly identify that the standard analysis for SVRG/SAGA in convex minimization relies on co-coercivity, a property not held by the gradient operator in saddle-point problems. Their decision to use the more general framework of monotone operators is elegant and provides a sound foundation for their results. The derived convergence rates are rigorous and clearly show how variance reduction leads to improvements over batch methods, mirroring the results in the standard convex setting. The claims are well-supported by the theorems and the experimental results align with the theoretical predictions. For instance, the experiments clearly show the superiority of non-uniform sampling and the benefit of acceleration for ill-conditioned problems, as suggested by the complexity analysis in Table 1.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is stated clearly in Section 2, with all assumptions laid out explicitly. The authors provide a helpful review of existing batch and stochastic methods, which effectively situates their contribution. The algorithms are presented in a clean, easy-to-follow format. A particularly helpful feature is Table 1, which provides a concise summary of the running-time complexities of all discussed algorithms, making it easy to compare their theoretical performance. The motivation for using saddle-point formulations for problems with non-separable losses/regularizers is also very compelling and clearly articulated.

A minor point for improvement would be to clarify the implementation of non-uniform SAGA (Algorithm 2). The text mentions that for the theoretical bound to hold, resampling is necessary, but the experiments use a heuristic mixture of sampling schemes. A more explicit discussion of this discrepancy and the practical implications would be beneficial for reproducibility.

**Originality:**
The paper's originality lies not in the algorithmic templates—which are direct extensions of SVRG and SAGA—but in their application and analysis in a new domain. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a rigorous linear convergence analysis for these variance reduction methods for general composite saddle-point problems. The connection to monotone operators is a key theoretical innovation that distinguishes this work from prior analyses. Furthermore, the detailed study of sampling schemes and the adaptation of Catalyst acceleration in this context are novel contributions. The paper does an excellent job of citing related work in both variance reduction and saddle-point optimization, clearly delineating how its contributions are distinct.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is high. Saddle-point problems are becoming increasingly prevalent in machine learning, and this paper provides a new set of powerful, practical tools for solving them. The ability to handle non-separable but proximable functions (both losses and regularizers) is a major advantage, as shown in the experiments on AUC optimization and clustering-based regularization. Researchers and practitioners working on structured prediction, robust optimization, or any problem naturally formulated via duality will likely find these algorithms useful. The theoretical framework based on monotone operators may also inspire further research into applying variance reduction to an even broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The accelerated SVRG variant provides a state-of-the-art algorithm that will likely become a go-to method for large-scale saddle-point optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** Provides the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in the general saddle-point setting, using a novel proof technique based on monotone operators.
*   **High practical relevance:** Addresses a class of problems of growing importance in machine learning and provides efficient, ready-to-use algorithms.
*   **Comprehensive work:** The paper covers two key algorithms (SVRG, SAGA), an acceleration scheme, a thorough theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical validation.
*   **Clarity and readability:** The paper is very well-written, making a technically dense topic accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The algorithmic novelty is limited, as the methods are direct analogues of existing ones. However, this is heavily outweighed by the novelty and importance of the analysis and application domain.
*   The experimental evaluation could be broader (e.g., testing on other applications mentioned, like robust optimization), but the chosen experiments effectively demonstrate the core claims of the paper.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It is a complete and impactful piece of work that advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning.
