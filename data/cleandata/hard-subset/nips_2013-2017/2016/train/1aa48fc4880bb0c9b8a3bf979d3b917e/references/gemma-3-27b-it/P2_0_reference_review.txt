## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the setting of saddle-point problems. It provides the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context, highlighting key differences from the convex minimization setup. The authors demonstrate the necessity of splitting the problem in terms of functions (rather than just convex terms) and the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency. They also present an acceleration technique based on the "catalyst" framework.  The paper rigorously analyzes the algorithms, positioning them within a broader framework of monotone operator methods, and provides experimental validation on machine learning tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution.  While SVR methods are well-established for convex optimization, their adaptation to saddle-point problems is not trivial and hasn’t been formally analyzed before.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is comprehensive and provides clear guarantees.  The connection to monotone operator theory is insightful and broadens the applicability of the results.  The analysis correctly identifies and addresses the unique challenges posed by the saddle-point structure.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The paper explicitly targets machine learning problems where saddle-point formulations naturally arise (e.g., regularized learning, robust optimization). The experiments support the theoretical findings and demonstrate practical gains.
* **Detailed Analysis of Splits:** The careful discussion of different splitting strategies (function-based vs. derivative-based, factored splits) is valuable for practitioners. The insights regarding the benefits of non-uniform sampling are particularly useful.
* **Catalyst Acceleration:** The extension of the catalyst framework to this setting is a strong addition, further improving performance.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the assumptions are explicitly stated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Assumptions:** While the assumptions are clearly stated, they are relatively strong (strong convexity-concavity, Lipschitz continuity).  Relaxing these assumptions, or providing guidance on how to adapt the algorithms when they are not fully met, would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate effectiveness, they are limited in scope.  Exploring a wider range of datasets and problem settings would strengthen the claims. The comparison to primal methods is somewhat limited, focusing on cases where the primal can be efficiently solved.  A more comprehensive comparison across a diverse range of problem types would be helpful.
* **Constant Factors:**  The theoretical analysis yields convergence rates with constants that depend on problem parameters (e.g., Lipschitz constants, condition numbers).  In practice, these constants can be difficult to estimate, and their impact on performance is not fully explored.  A discussion of practical methods for estimating these parameters would be useful.
* **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:**  The paper provides limited guidance on how to tune the hyperparameters (step sizes, regularization parameters).  A more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs appear rigorous, and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. It is a complete piece of work, although further extensions (relaxing assumptions, more extensive experiments) are possible. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations and assumptions of their work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. While the technical details are dense, the authors do a good job of explaining the key concepts and motivations.
* **Originality:** The extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems is original and significant. The analysis of different splitting strategies and the acceleration technique are also novel contributions.  The authors adequately reference related work.
* **Significance:** The results are important because saddle-point problems arise frequently in machine learning. The proposed algorithms offer a potentially significant improvement over existing methods in these settings. The work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretical foundation for applying SVR methods to a broader class of problems.




**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Strong theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs.
*   Novel and important extension of SVR methods.
*   Practical relevance to a wide range of machine learning problems.
*   Clear writing and organization.
*   Positive experimental results.

**Con:**

*   Relatively strong assumptions.
*   Limited experimental scope.
*   Complexity of estimating theoretical constants.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions.  The extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems is a valuable addition to the optimization literature. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS. The paper’s contribution is substantial, the analysis is rigorous, and the results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications.  I suggest the authors address the minor weaknesses identified above in a future revision.
