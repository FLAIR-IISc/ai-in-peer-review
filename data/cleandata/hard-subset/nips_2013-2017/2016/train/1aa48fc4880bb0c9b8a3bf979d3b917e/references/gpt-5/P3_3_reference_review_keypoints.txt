- Problem studied: composite saddle-point min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with prox-friendly strongly convex-concave M and smooth K; cast as solving A+B monotone inclusion and apply SVRG/SAGA.  
- Main contributions: linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point/monotone settings, operator-level decomposition (function or factored partial-derivative splits), and catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration.  
- Generality: analysis extends to zeros of monotone operators A+B where A is strongly monotone with tractable resolvent and B is split into Lipschitz pieces.  
- Experimental claims: binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers shows gains over batch/primal-dual baselines and benefits of non-uniform sampling.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on proximal splitting (Rockafellar, Chambolle–Pock), contrasts with stochastic FB (O(1/t)) and SPDC, and extends variance reduction beyond cocoercivity-covered regimes.  
- Conceptual strength: novel framing of SVRG/SAGA for monotone inclusions without requiring cocoercivity, broadening applicability (e.g., variational inequalities).  
- Flexible decompositions: operator-level (factored) splits enable memory/runtime trade-offs for bilinear K and allow effective non-uniform sampling quantified via L̄(π).  
- Theoretical strength: clear linear rates for SVRG (epoch-wise) and SAGA (online), including mini-batching, non-uniform sampling, and storage-efficient factored variants linked to problem norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max).  
- Acceleration insight: proximal-point/catalyst scheme is natural for strong monotonicity and yields complexity L̄√(T(B)·max_i T(B_i)) with a condition for when acceleration helps over batch methods.  
- Empirics positives: demonstrates practical use of non-separable prox and shows empirical advantages of variance reduction and non-uniform sampling.  
- Key weakness — assumptions: relies on strong convex-concavity (strong monotonicity), limiting scope; step-size and complexity constants are conservative and need empirical tuning guidance.  
- Notation/clarity issues: µ in Theorem 2 is undefined relative to λ,γ; wording typo “does need splits” likely should be “does not”; epoch length and resampling requirements need clearer statement.  
- Missing comparisons: experiments omit relevant baselines like Mirror-Prox/extra-gradient and SPDC (on separable instances); stochastic extragradient baselines suggested.  
- Catalyst practicality concern: analysis downplays cost of prox M and requires knowledge of L̄ and cost ratios for τ selection; adaptive strategies or sensitivity analysis needed.  
- Limited empirical breadth: only two datasets used; additional tasks, wall-clock/time and memory measurements (especially SAGA factored vs individual), and sampling-sensitivity studies are needed.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound and carefully argued with conservative but justified constants; minor notational inconsistencies and appendix-referenced proofs should be fixed/clarified.  
- Originality: novel extension of variance-reduced methods to monotone inclusions without cocoercivity and operator-level splitting are meaningful new contributions.  
- Significance: high — relevant to GANs, robust optimization, and game-theoretic learning; likely to spur further work.  
- Suggestions to authors: define µ and relate to λ,γ; compare to stochastic extragradient methods empirically/theoretically; provide τ ablation/adaptive heuristics and prox-cost analysis; discuss non-uniform SAGA without resampling; add memory-cost table.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
