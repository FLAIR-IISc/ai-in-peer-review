## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper presents extensions of popular stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to the challenging setting of saddle-point problems, which arise naturally in many machine learning applications. The authors provide convergence analysis for these adapted algorithms, highlighting differences from their convex minimization counterparts. They identify key aspects of saddle-point formulations – the existence of different split structures and the importance of non-uniform sampling – and demonstrate acceleration techniques leveraging a “catalyst” framework.  Finally, they support their theoretical results with empirical validation.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly upon the seminal papers on SVRG [Johnson & Zhang, 2013] and SAGA [Defazio et al., 2014]. It extends these methods beyond their traditional domain of convex minimization. The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing batch algorithms for saddle-point problems [Chen & Rockafellar, 2014; Chambolle & Pock, 2011] and stochastic approaches [Rosasco et al., 2014], demonstrating theoretical and practical improvements.  The connection to monotone operator theory [Rockafellar, 1997] is also well-established and provides a broader perspective on the applicability of their methods. The paper explicitly references and differentiates from work on related areas like proximal algorithms and sketching techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant extension. The theoretical analysis for this non-trivial case is a primary contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is well-developed, moving beyond co-coercivity assumptions and grounding the results in the properties of monotone operators. The analysis clearly articulates the impact of different splitting strategies and sampling schemes.
* **Practical Insights:** Identifying the importance of non-uniform sampling and demonstrating its efficacy in experiments is crucial. The accelerated catalyst framework provides a practical improvement.
* **Broad Applicability:** The extension to monotone operators broadens the scope of the work beyond saddle-point problems, potentially impacting areas like variational inequalities.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental section provides evidence supporting the theoretical results, showcasing the performance benefits of the proposed algorithms compared to alternatives, including those optimized for the convex setting. The analysis of different splitting schemes (factored vs. individual) is valuable.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly define their assumptions and notation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Assumptions:**  While theoretically sound, the assumptions (A)-(C) regarding strong convexity-concavity and Lipschitz continuity may be restrictive in practice. The dependence on knowing λ and γ could be a limiting factor.  While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion on potential adaptive methods for estimating these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Catalyst Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of the “catalyst” parameter (τ) depends on the specific problem instance.  A more detailed discussion on how to choose this parameter adaptively, beyond the given formula, would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate performance gains, they are limited to a few datasets and problem settings. Expanding the experiments to a wider range of applications (e.g., robust optimization, matrix factorization) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Notation Heavy:** The paper uses a lot of notation. While mathematically precise, it can be somewhat daunting for readers less familiar with the field. A more concise presentation of some derivations might improve readability.
* **Convergence Rate Focus:** The paper primarily focuses on convergence *rates*. A discussion of the practical implications of these rates, such as wall-clock time or resource usage, could add further value.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution by extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, a crucial setting in modern machine learning.
* The rigorous analysis and clear exposition make a valuable contribution to the literature.
* The empirical validation supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates practical advantages.
* The work opens up new avenues for research in stochastic optimization for non-convex problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The restrictive assumptions might limit the applicability of the methods in certain scenarios.
*  The experiments, while insightful, could benefit from broader coverage of problem settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It is a well-written, technically sound, and original contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths far outweigh them. The paper addresses a challenging problem with a rigorous theoretical framework and provides empirical evidence supporting its claims. The work has the potential to significantly impact research and practice in machine learning, particularly in areas involving saddle-point formulations.

**Confidence:** 9/10
