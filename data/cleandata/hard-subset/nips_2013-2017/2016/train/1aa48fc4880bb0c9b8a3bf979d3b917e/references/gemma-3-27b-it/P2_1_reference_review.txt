## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the challenging setting of saddle-point problems. It provides the first convergence analysis for these algorithms applied to saddle-points, highlighting differences from the convex minimization context. The authors demonstrate that the saddle-point formulation enables non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and show how to accelerate the algorithms using a catalyst-like framework.  The work also offers a broader perspective by connecting saddle-point problems to monotone operator theory, extending the applicability of the algorithms beyond standard saddle-point scenarios.  The paper is accompanied by experiments on binary classification problems, showcasing the benefits of the proposed techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. While SVR methods are well-established for convex optimization, their adaptation to non-convex/non-strongly convex saddle-point problems is non-trivial. The analysis and convergence guarantees are novel.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The convergence analysis is rigorous, leveraging the interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators. The theoretical results provide clear guidance on algorithm parameter tuning and expected performance. The extension to monotone operators is a particularly valuable theoretical contribution, broadening the scope beyond explicitly saddle-point problems.
* **Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a class of problems (saddle-point formulations arising in machine learning, e.g., regularization, robust optimization) that are increasingly common. The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements over existing methods, especially when combined with acceleration. The exploration of different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform) is valuable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The authors clearly define their assumptions and provide sufficient detail to understand the proposed algorithms and their analysis. The connection to existing work (e.g., proximal algorithms, SVRG/SAGA) is well-explained.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly impact the development of efficient optimization algorithms for a wide range of machine learning tasks. The insights gained regarding the importance of non-uniform sampling and the benefits of the accelerated framework are particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Assumptions:**  The assumptions (A)-(C), while standard for this type of analysis, can be restrictive. Specifically, requiring Lipschitz continuity of the gradients and the ability to compute the proximal operator of M might not hold for all practical problems. It would be helpful to discuss the implications of relaxing these assumptions.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the accelerated algorithm relies on careful tuning of the catalyst parameter (τ). While the paper provides a guideline for selecting τ, it might be sensitive to the specific problem instance. A discussion on robustness to parameter choices would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to binary classification problems. While these provide a good demonstration, exploring other applications (e.g., robust optimization, matrix factorization) would strengthen the conclusions and demonstrate broader applicability.
* **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The comparison to existing algorithms could be more comprehensive. While the paper compares to batch and stochastic methods, exploring other saddle-point solvers or recent advancements in related areas might provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving a challenging optimization problem (saddle-point problems).
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of SVR methods in this setting.
* The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements over existing methods.
* The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
* The work addresses a significant and growing area of research in machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions are somewhat restrictive and could limit the applicability of the algorithms.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The parameter sensitivity of the accelerated algorithm warrants further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the practical relevance of the results outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper meets all four evaluation criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance) at a high level.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with the suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses related to assumption sensitivity, experimental scope, and a more thorough comparison to related work.  Specifically, a discussion of the robustness of the algorithm to deviations from the assumptions, some additional experiments on different problem classes, and a more detailed comparison to existing saddle-point solvers would further strengthen the paper.
