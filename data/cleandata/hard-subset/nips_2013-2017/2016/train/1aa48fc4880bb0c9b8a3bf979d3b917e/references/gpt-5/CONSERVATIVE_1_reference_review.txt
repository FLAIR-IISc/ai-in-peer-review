Summary
The paper studies finite-sum composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly convex–concave. The authors adapt SVRG and SAGA to this setting, allowing splits at the operator level B=(∂xK, −∂yK)=∑_i B_i without requiring each B_i to be a gradient of a scalar function. They analyze linear convergence under strong convex–concavity, introduce non-uniform sampling schemes (including “factored” row/column splits for bilinear K), and give complexity bounds in terms of a sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π). They also provide an acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper, and argue the analysis extends to finding zeros of strongly monotone operators (beyond classical co-coercive settings). Experiments on two datasets with non-separable losses/regularizers suggest improvements over batch forward–backward and PDHG-like methods; accelerated SVRG and SAGA with non-uniform sampling perform best.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization and on primal–dual/forward–backward schemes (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock) for saddle-point problems. It is related to SPDC and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods, but those typically hinge on separability; here the focus is on non-separable losses/regularizers and operator-level splits. The extension to monotone operators connects to proximal point and monotone inclusion literature, but departs from analyses that rely on co-coercivity.

Quality
- Theory: The paper gives linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA variants in the strongly convex–concave case with explicit step sizes depending on L and L̄. The operator-level analysis is technically interesting and, to my knowledge, nontrivial. However, several concerns remain:
  - The step-size/rate constants (e.g., dependence on L^2 in FB, bounds via L̄) appear conservative; practical guidance is limited, and the analysis requires knowledge of λ, γ, L, L̄.
  - Non-uniform SAGA requires “resampling” to maintain unbiasedness in the analysis, but the experiments use a heuristic mixture without the required resampling; the theory–practice gap should be addressed empirically.
  - The “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” claim for the accelerated wrapper is too strong; it depends on oracle costs and constants and is not supported by experiments across diverse settings.
- Experiments: The empirical section is modest (two datasets), reported in passes rather than wall-clock, and lacks several relevant baselines and ablations. Notably absent are:
  - SPDC/SDCA-style baselines (on the separable dataset), and tuned PDHG variants with linesearch/preconditioning.
  - Ablations on factorized vs element-wise splits, uniform vs non-uniform sampling for both SAGA and SVRG, mini-batch effects, and sensitivity to τ in the accelerated method.
  - Wall-clock vs dataset size and memory footprint (storage is O(n+d) for factored, but O(nd) for element-wise SAGA).
  - Non-bilinear K (the theory covers general smooth K, but experiments appear bilinear).
These omissions make it hard to assess practical significance and robustness.

Clarity
The paper is dense but overall readable. The operator/sampling notation is heavy; a small worked example early on (e.g., bilinear with factored vs individual splits) would help. There are a few typos/ambiguities (e.g., “does need splits into convex–concave terms” likely means “does not need…”). Hyperparameter choices and proximal implementations should be described more transparently.

Originality
Adapting variance reduction to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and to operator-level splits with a monotone operator analysis is novel for the 2016 timeframe. The acceleration via a proximal-point wrapper in the saddle-point context is a useful perspective. While the algorithms are natural extensions, the analysis and non-uniform sampling treatment are original.

Significance
The problem setting is important—non-separable losses/regularizers are common, and exploiting finite-sum structure in saddle-point formulations is practical. If borne out empirically, the proposed methods could be impactful. However, the current experimental evidence is too limited to convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong established baselines across regimes, and the reliance on hard-to-know constants may limit applicability.

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points with operator-level splits.
- Monotone operator viewpoint broadens applicability beyond saddle points.
- Non-uniform sampling analysis tailored to bilinear problems (row/column sampling).
- Acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper is theoretically appealing.
- Some empirical gains over batch and stochastic FB/PDHG on non-separable problems.

Cons
- Limited and incomplete empirical validation; missing key baselines, wall-clock results, and ablations.
- Theory depends on strong convex–concavity and known constants; step sizes may be conservative.
- The practical benefit of non-uniform sampling and acceleration is not systematically demonstrated.
- Memory/storage and implementation overhead not thoroughly discussed.
- Some overstatements (e.g., “always superior”) not justified.

Questions/requests for authors
1. Include comparisons to SPDC/SDCA on the separable dataset and a tuned PDHG baseline with linesearch/preconditioning.
2. Report wall-clock time and memory, and scaling with n,d. Provide code.
3. Show ablations: factorized vs element-wise splits; uniform vs non-uniform for both SAGA/SVRG; mini-batch size; with/without resampling in SAGA; sensitivity to τ.
4. Demonstrate a non-bilinear K case to substantiate generality.
5. Clarify how L̄ and sampling probabilities are computed efficiently in practice; discuss robustness to mis-specification of λ, γ, L.
6. Tone down the “always superior” claim and precisely state the regimes where acceleration helps.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are solid and likely of interest, but the empirical evaluation is not yet sufficient to support the strong practical claims, and several key comparisons/ablations are missing. Strengthening the experiments and clarifying practical guidance could make this a strong contribution.
