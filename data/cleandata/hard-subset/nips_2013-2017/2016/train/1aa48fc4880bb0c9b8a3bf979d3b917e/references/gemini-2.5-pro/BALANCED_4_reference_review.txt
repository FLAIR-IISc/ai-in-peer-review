### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the variance-reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the broader class of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The problem formulation `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)` is general and captures many machine learning tasks that cannot be addressed by standard finite-sum methods, particularly those involving non-separable loss functions (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso).

The authors make several key contributions. First, they propose straightforward algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point setting. Second, and more significantly, they provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in this context. The analysis is novel as it relies on the interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding the zeros of a monotone operator, rather than using co-coercivity properties that hold for gradient descent in convex minimization but not for saddle-point problems. Third, the paper investigates different ways to decompose the saddle-point operator, including "factored" splits, and demonstrates theoretically and empirically the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for achieving state-of-the-art performance. Finally, they show that these new methods can be further accelerated using a catalyst-style framework, yielding an algorithm with theoretical complexity superior to accelerated batch methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the literature. While variance-reduction methods have revolutionized large-scale convex optimization, their application has been limited to separable objectives. This work significantly broadens the reach of these powerful techniques to a wide array of important, non-separable problems in machine learning, such as structured prediction, robust optimization, and learning with complex regularizers. The results are likely to be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

2.  **Strong and Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The primary contribution is not just the algorithm, but the novel convergence analysis. Proving linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting is non-trivial. The authors' choice to frame the analysis in the more general language of monotone operators is elegant and powerful. It correctly identifies monotonicity as the key property, unlike co-coercivity which is typically used for analyzing SVRG/SAGA in convex minimization but does not apply here. This theoretical framing not only provides a sound proof for the saddle-point case but also immediately generalizes the results to a wider class of variational inequalities.

3.  **Thorough and Careful Analysis:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical treatment. It analyzes SVRG, SAGA, and an accelerated version, comparing their complexities under different assumptions and sampling schemes in a clear summary (Table 1). The discussion on different splitting strategies (e.g., factored splits) and the importance of non-uniform sampling provides valuable practical insights beyond the main convergence results.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, presents the algorithms and theorems logically, and provides empirical validation that supports the theoretical claims. The distinction between different problem constants (L, L̄) and norms (operator, Frobenius) and their impact on complexity is handled clearly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on Known Constants:** The proposed algorithms and their theoretical rates depend on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity parameters (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). While this is a common feature of this line of research, it can be a practical limitation as these constants are often unknown or difficult to estimate. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the practical challenges this poses, and perhaps by mentioning potential remedies, such as line search or adaptive step-size strategies, even if they are left for future work. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but a bit more discussion in the main body would be helpful.

2.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic baselines on the chosen tasks. However, the motivation for the work rests on its applicability to a wide range of non-separable problems (AUC maximization, robust optimization, clustering, etc.). The experiments focus on a specific combination of a non-separable AUC-like loss and a clustering-inducing regularizer. While this is a good case study, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on at least one other distinct problem domain would make the empirical validation more compelling and better reflect the claimed generality.

3.  **Algorithmic Novelty:** It is worth noting that the algorithmic templates for SVRG and SAGA are direct adaptations from their convex optimization counterparts. The main novelty lies in the analysis and the application to a new problem class. This is a minor point, as the adaptation and its analysis are the core scientific contributions, but it is useful to frame the work's originality accurately.

### Overall Evaluation and Justification

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It successfully bridges the gap between the highly successful variance-reduction techniques and the important class of saddle-point problems.

*   **Quality:** The technical quality is very high. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, novel, and based on a well-chosen mathematical framework (monotone operators) that provides deep insight into why these methods work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is written with outstanding clarity. The arguments are easy to follow, and the results are presented in a comprehensive and accessible manner.
*   **Originality:** While the algorithms are adaptations, the analysis is highly original and the application to this problem class is new. This is the first work to establish linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA on general saddle-point problems.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. It makes a powerful class of algorithms applicable to a much broader range of machine learning problems, likely stimulating much follow-up research and practical application.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first linearly-convergent stochastic algorithms for a general and important class of saddle-point problems.
*   The novel analysis based on monotone operator theory is a strong theoretical contribution in its own right and provides a more general understanding than existing analyses for convex minimization.
*   Thorough theoretical and empirical validation, including an accelerated variant and practical insights on non-uniform sampling.
*   The work is well-presented and addresses a clear need in the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility is somewhat limited by the need to know problem constants, a common issue not resolved here.
*   The algorithmic templates themselves are not entirely new, but rather adaptations.

The arguments for acceptance decisively outweigh the arguments against. This paper is a strong contribution that combines theoretical depth with practical relevance. I recommend it for acceptance.
