- Proposes SVRG and SAGA extensions for composite convex–concave saddle-point problems via A+B monotone-operator formulation.  
- Treats A as strongly monotone (prox available) and B as a sum of Lipschitz operators without assuming cocoercivity.  
- Provides linear convergence analyses under strong convexity–concavity using monotonicity only, with step-sizes based on L and a sampling-dependent L̄.  
- Introduces and analyzes two decompositions of B—“individual” and “factored”—and emphasizes non-uniform sampling for tighter L̄ and faster convergence.  
- Adapts a simple catalyst-style proximal-point outer loop to accelerate SVRG, yielding improved complexities that dominate batch accelerated methods in some regimes.  
- Derives complexity bounds specialized to bilinear problems using ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max, and discusses storage/time trade-offs.  
- Empirically shows non-uniform SAGA/SVRG and accelerated SVRG outperform batch and stochastic FB baselines on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers.  
- Thoroughly relates to prior work (SAG/SVRG/SAGA, primal–dual/FB methods, stochastic FB, separable coordinate methods, monotone operator literature).  
- Claims novelty in handling non-separable saddle-point problems with variance reduction and analysis beyond cocoercive settings.  
- Theoretical contributions are well motivated; monotone-operator lens cleanly decouples analysis from saddle-point specifics.  
- Clearly articulates the role of L vs L̄(π) and provides plausible, practically important non-uniform sampling bounds.  
- Catalyst-style acceleration is justified via proximal-point contraction and improves complexity when the computational cost ratio favors it.  
- Standard and clearly stated assumptions (prox of M, Lipschitz K gradient, strong convex–concavity); notation gap: μ used in Theorem 2 without explicit definition.  
- Pseudocode is clear with minibatching and storage distinctions; computational cost model supports complexity claims.  
- Experiments support main claims but are limited in breadth (two datasets) and omit some competitive baselines like extragradient/mirror-prox and other accelerated primal–dual methods.  
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (adaptivity to λ,γ,L,L̄) are not thoroughly discussed; adaptivity left as future work.  
- Notes a practice–theory gap for SAGA with non-uniform sampling (resampling not used in practice) that could be elaborated.  
- Paper is generally well written and organized; weighted norm usage is consistent and helpful.  
- Suggests clarifying μ and consistently using λ, γ, μ, briefly contrasting with extragradient/mirror-prox, and adding a compact assumptions/guarantees table.  
- Originality: first rigorous variance-reduced incremental methods for general saddle-point problems without cocoercivity; factored decomposition and tailored non-uniform sampling are insightful.  
- Significance: addresses an important gap for non-separable saddle-point problems with theoretical and empirical gains likely to influence future algorithm design.  
- Pros: principled SVRG/SAGA extension with linear convergence, broad monotone-operator applicability, careful non-uniform sampling, simple acceleration, and practical relevance.  
- Cons: limited experimental breadth and baselines, reliance on known problem constants (λ,γ,L,L̄) without adaptive schemes, potentially high memory for individual splits, minor notation/theory–practice gaps.  
- Minor suggestions: cite/discuss Mirror-Prox/extragradient, define μ in Theorem 2, and explain how L̄ is estimated and non-uniform probabilities computed for large K.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — technically solid, original, significant, with minor clarifications and broader empirical comparisons suggested.
