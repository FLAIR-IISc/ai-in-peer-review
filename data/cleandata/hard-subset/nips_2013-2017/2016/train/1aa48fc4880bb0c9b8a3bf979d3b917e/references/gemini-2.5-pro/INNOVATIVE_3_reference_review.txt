### Summary

This paper introduces and analyzes stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and stochastic average gradient (SAGA) methods for composite saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic extensions themselves are natural analogues of their well-known convex minimization counterparts, the primary contribution lies in providing the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point setting. The authors correctly identify that the standard analysis for convex minimization does not apply here. Instead, they leverage the more general and powerful framework of monotone operators, which not only yields the desired convergence results but also shows that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper further investigates practical aspects like different ways to decompose the problem ("factored splits") and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling. Finally, it demonstrates how to achieve acceleration by integrating the SVRG algorithm into the "Catalyst" framework, resulting in a method that is theoretically superior to accelerated full-batch algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is the main highlight. Shifting the perspective from convex-concave functions to the more abstract setting of monotone operators is an elegant and powerful choice. It simplifies the analysis while simultaneously broadening the applicability of the results. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the theorems provide explicit convergence rates that recover the expected speed-ups from variance reduction. The experiments, while focused on a specific application (AUC maximization with a non-separable regularizer), are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, over existing batch and stochastic baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts by clearly motivating the need for specialized saddle-point solvers, especially for machine learning problems with non-separable components. It provides a concise review of existing batch and stochastic methods, setting the stage for the new contributions. The descriptions of the proposed algorithms are clear, and the main theoretical results are presented upfront and summarized effectively in Table 1. The connection to monotone operators in Section 6 is a crucial part of the story and is explained lucidly.

**Originality:** This is the paper's greatest strength. While SVRG and SAGA are established techniques, their application and, more importantly, their rigorous analysis for the general saddle-point problem are entirely novel. To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish linear convergence for incremental methods in this setting. The insight that the analysis should be grounded in monotonicity rather than co-coercivity (which holds for convex minimization but not for saddle-point problems) is a significant conceptual contribution. This work moves beyond a simple, incremental extension of an existing algorithm and opens up a new avenue for research in large-scale saddle-point optimization. The discussion on factored splits and the formal integration of the Catalyst framework are also novel in this context.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous in machine learning, arising from duality, robust optimization, and certain unsupervised learning relaxations. Many modern ML models involve complex, non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso) or loss functions (e.g., structured prediction, AUC maximization), for which standard primal SVR methods are inapplicable. This paper provides a principled and efficient way to optimize such models, directly addressing a critical gap in the large-scale optimization toolkit. The proposed methods are likely to be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners. The general analysis based on monotone operators also provides a template for analyzing other incremental algorithms in settings beyond standard convex optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the submission.
1.  The core algorithmic structures of SVRG and SAGA are not new in themselves, being direct adaptations from the convex case. The novelty is in the new context and analysis.
2.  The experimental validation could be broader. While the chosen problem is a compelling use case, demonstrating the methods on other applications mentioned, such as robust optimization or matrix factorization, would have further strengthened the paper's impact.
3.  The acceleration framework is fully developed only for SVRG. While the authors state it "should work for SAGA," a formal analysis is not provided. This is a minor point of incompleteness.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Novelty and Impact:** The paper is the first to provide linearly-convergent stochastic variance-reduced methods for the general class of saddle-point problems. This is a significant breakthrough that extends the reach of these powerful optimizers to a much wider range of important machine learning problems.
*   **Elegant Theoretical Contribution:** The use of monotone operator theory for the convergence proof is a major strength. It is a bold and insightful choice that is more general and powerful than a direct, likely cumbersome, extension of existing proofs. This establishes a strong theoretical foundation for future work.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper delivers not just theory but practical algorithms that demonstrably outperform existing methods. The accelerated SVRG variant, in particular, offers state-of-the-art performance. The insights on non-uniform sampling are also practically valuable.

### Arguments for Rejection
*   The algorithmic templates are direct translations from prior work. One could argue the novelty is primarily analytical rather than algorithmic. However, in my view, the new analysis in this challenging setting is a more significant contribution than a novel but incrementally better algorithm for an old problem.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to large-scale optimization for machine learning. It addresses an important, open problem with a novel and elegant theoretical approach, resulting in practical algorithms with state-of-the-art performance. The work is characterized by the kind of bold conceptual leap that pushes the field forward. The minor weaknesses are far outweighed by the significance and originality of the contributions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
