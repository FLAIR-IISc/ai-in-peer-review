Summary
The paper proposes variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG- and SAGA-style) for solving strongly convex–concave composite saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is proximable and strongly convex–concave. The authors (i) extend variance reduction to saddle-point settings via a forward–backward splitting view and a monotone operator formulation, (ii) allow decompositions at the operator level (Bi) not necessarily derived from individual functions Ki, (iii) analyze non-uniform sampling and “factored” splits (row/column sampling in bilinear cases), and (iv) present an acceleration scheme inspired by the catalyst/proximal point framework. They provide linear convergence proofs with constants depending on L and an importance-sampling dependent L̄(π), discuss mini-batches, and show an extension to general monotone operators. Experiments on two datasets suggest accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms batch and basic stochastic baselines; uniform SAGA can underperform accelerated batch.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization and on primal–dual/forward–backward methods for saddle points (e.g., Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock). SPDC and related primal–dual coordinate methods address separable saddle-point structures; here the emphasis is on non-separable M and operator-level splits. The monotone operator perspective is classical; the novelty is applying variance reduction without co-coercivity to saddle-point/variational inequality settings.

Strengths
- Technical: Clear monotone operator framing of variance-reduced forward–backward for convex–concave problems; linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity. The allowance for operator-level splits Bi (not necessarily gradients) is useful for bilinear K and sketching-like structures.
- Sampling: Non-uniform sampling analysis with explicit L̄(π) bounds for several schemes (individual vs factored; uniform vs importance) is informative and aligns with empirical observations that non-uniform helps.
- Acceleration: The proximal-point/catalyst outer loop yields an improved complexity bound and is conceptually simple in the saddle-point setting.
- Scope: Extension to general monotone operators broadens applicability beyond saddle points.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical validation is limited. Only two datasets are considered, with a narrow set of models, and the evaluation focuses mainly on distance-to-solution. Strong claims such as “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” are not convincingly supported; the paper itself notes cases where uniform sampling SAGA is worse than accelerated batch. Comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., tuned Chambolle–Pock variants, SPDC, stochastic extragradient methods) are missing.
- Mismatch between theory and practice: The SAGA non-uniform sampling theorem requires a resampling step after the update, but experiments follow a heuristic “mixture” without resampling. The impact of this divergence from the theory is not quantified.
- Assumption dependence and implementability: The methods require knowledge of strong convexity–concavity parameters and of L and L̄(π). Although the authors acknowledge this and mention adaptivity as future work, the practical selection of these quantities and of sampling probabilities (especially beyond the bilinear case) is not addressed. Precomputing probabilities like pj∝(KK^T)_jj and qk∝(K^T K)_kk may be non-trivial in large-scale/sparse contexts; overheads are not accounted for in the complexity discussion.
- Clarity of some claims/details: 
  - The statement “(b) does need splits into convex-concave terms” seems contradictory; presumably they mean “does not need splits into convex-concave terms.”
  - The complexity discussions occasionally “forget the complexity of the proximal operator” or rely on T(B) and T(Bi) abstractions; while useful analytically, this makes it hard to assess end-to-end runtime advantages in typical ML settings with sparsity and caching.
  - The experimental metric “distance to optimizers” implies access to (x*,y*) or a high-accuracy reference; how this is computed should be clarified. Primal–dual gaps would be a more standard and reproducible diagnostic; only limited mention is made.
- Breadth of the monotone operator extension is theoretical only; no empirical evidence supports benefits in broader VI settings.

Quality
The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound within the stated assumptions. The sampling and acceleration analyses are plausible, and the operator-level splits are well-motivated. However, empirical evidence is too narrow to substantiate the stronger practical claims, and some implementation-critical aspects (parameter selection, overheads) are under-discussed.

Clarity
Overall readable but dense. Key algorithms are specified, but some key points are deferred to appendices and practical guidance is limited. Clarify the noted wording issue, detail how (x*,y*) or gaps are computed, and better explain how to set/estimate L, L̄, and sampling probabilities in practice.

Originality
Moderately strong. Extending variance reduction to general convex–concave saddle points (beyond separable cases) and to monotone operators without co-coercivity is a meaningful step. The acceleration via a proximal-point outer loop is natural but valuable in this context.

Significance
Potentially significant for practitioners dealing with non-separable losses/regularizers in primal–dual formulations. That said, the lack of comprehensive comparisons and practical guidance reduces immediate impact.

Questions for the authors
- How do you estimate or adapt L, L̄(π), and strong convexity–concavity constants in practice? Can you provide an adaptive step-size/schedule that preserves linear convergence?
- What is the overhead of computing non-uniform sampling distributions in large sparse problems? Do the empirical gains persist when this cost is included?
- Can you report primal–dual gaps and wall-clock time, and add comparisons to tuned Chambolle–Pock and SPDC (where applicable), and to stochastic extragradient variants?
- In SAGA experiments, what is the effect of omitting the resampling step required by the theorem?
- How are distances to optimizers computed on real data?

Pros
- Solid theoretical contribution extending SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point and monotone operator settings with linear rates.
- Careful analysis of non-uniform sampling and factored splits.
- Simple and principled acceleration via proximal-point outer loop.
- Practical relevance for non-separable structures.

Cons
- Limited and somewhat misaligned empirical validation; strong practical claims not fully substantiated.
- Reliance on known problem constants; limited guidance for adaptive tuning.
- Missing comparisons to state-of-the-art primal–dual baselines.
- Some clarity issues and unaccounted overheads.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theory is strong and potentially impactful, but the experimental evidence and practical guidance fall short of the paper’s claims. Strengthening the empirical section with broader baselines, wall-clock comparisons, adaptive parameterization, and clearer reproducibility would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
