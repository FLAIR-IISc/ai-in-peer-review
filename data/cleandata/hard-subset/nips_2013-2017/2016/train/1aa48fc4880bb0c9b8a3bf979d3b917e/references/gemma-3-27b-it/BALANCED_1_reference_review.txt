This paper presents stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) extended to saddle-point problems, a common formulation in machine learning arising from duality. The authors provide convergence analysis, highlighting differences from the convex minimization setup, and demonstrate that the approach extends to monotone operators. They identify key aspects like the importance of splitting schemes and non-uniform sampling. The paper also includes an accelerated version of the algorithm and experimental validation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Relevance:** Applying SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a valuable contribution. Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous, and efficient solvers are crucial. Extending these methods beyond convex minimization is significant.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis appears solid, correctly identifying the differences and nuances compared to the standard convex case. The connection to monotone operator theory is a nice generalization.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper investigates different splitting schemes (functions vs. partial derivatives, factored splits) and emphasizes the importance of non-uniform sampling.
* **Practical Acceleration:** The incorporation of the catalyst framework is a strong point, potentially leading to substantial speedups.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide empirical support for the theoretical results and demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed algorithms, including comparisons to established methods.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumptions (A)-(C) are relatively strong. While standard, it would be valuable to discuss the robustness of the algorithms under weaker assumptions. Specifically, the Lipschitz continuity of gradients (B) might be a limitation in some applications.
* **Complexity of Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions the importance of proper parameter tuning (e.g., τ in the acceleration step), but the practical guidance on how to choose these parameters could be improved. The connection between regularization strength (λ) and the problem-specific parameters is somewhat hand-wavy.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are helpful, they are limited to a few datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a broader range of problem instances (including cases where the primal problem is easier to solve) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Connection to Prior Work:** While related work is cited, the paper could more explicitly position the results within the broader context of saddle-point solver research. For example, discussing the relationship to operator splitting methods (e.g., ADMM) or other accelerated proximal algorithms could be beneficial.
* **Catalyst Framework Details:** The explanation of the catalyst acceleration is somewhat terse. Providing more intuitive explanations and potentially illustrating the algorithm with a simplified example would be helpful.
* **Notational Burden:** The abundance of symbols (λ, γ, σ, τ, L, L̄, etc.) can be overwhelming, making it hard to follow the details. A more concise notation or a dedicated table of notations could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with significant implications for machine learning.
* The theoretical contributions are substantial, providing a rigorous analysis of SVRG/SAGA for saddle-point problems.
* The experimental results validate the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
* The extension to monotone operators is a valuable generalization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions, while standard, might be limiting in some applications.
* The complexity of parameter tuning and the lack of detailed guidance could hinder practical adoption.
* The experimental scope is relatively narrow.
* The connection to related work could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, albeit with the recommendation of minor revisions to address the points raised above.  Specifically, clarifying parameter tuning guidance, expanding the experimental section, and strengthening the connection to related work would elevate the paper further.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
