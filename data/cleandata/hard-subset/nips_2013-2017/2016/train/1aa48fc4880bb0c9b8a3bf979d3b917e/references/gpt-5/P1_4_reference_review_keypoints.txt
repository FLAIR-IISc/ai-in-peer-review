- Studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K+M and extends SVRG/SAGA via a monotone-operator + proximal M formulation.  
- Provides linear convergence analyses for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point settings using monotonicity (not cocoercivity).  
- Supports two finite-sum decompositions including memory-efficient “factored” splits for bilinear K.  
- Introduces explicit non-uniform sampling with L̄(π) constants and sampling-dependent complexity bounds.  
- Proposes a catalyst-like proximal-point acceleration that improves complexities and empirically outperforms accelerated batch methods on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Analysis extends to general strongly monotone inclusions A+B with resolvent access for A and Lipschitz Bi, covering variational inequalities.  
- Advances prior work by not requiring separability or gradient/monotone Bi, relying instead on strong monotonicity and Lipschitzness.  
- Technical novelty: first linear-rate VR guarantees for general composite saddle-point problems and monotone-operator framing.  
- Clear modeling: weighted norm Ω, Lipschitz constants, and explicit sampling-dependent smoothness are carefully defined.  
- Practical insights: factored vs individual splits, SAGA storage reduction, mini-batching tradeoffs, and sampling tied to matrix norms.  
- Empirically, non-uniform SVRG/SAGA outperform batch FB and accelerated FB on non-separable problems; accelerated SVRG best in ill-conditioned settings.  
- Experiments are limited (two datasets) and lack comparisons to stronger baselines like Chambolle–Pock with linesearch, extragradient/optimistic methods, SPDC, etc.  
- Theory–practice gap: SAGA’s non-uniform sampling analysis requires a resampling step omitted in experiments; empirical impact should be reported.  
- Methods require known λ, γ, L, L̄ and lack adaptive step-size or unknown-strong-convexity handling; practical estimation guidance is missing.  
- Acceleration claims need more empirical evidence and clearer conditions for dominance in non-bilinear K (explicit dependence on L̄, T(B), T(Bi)).  
- Some clarity issues: unfamiliar constants/rates presented without main-text derivations, inconsistent L vs L̄ in step-sizes, and overloaded notation K.  
- Overall analysis is technically sound, well-scoped, and precise; proofs deferred but statements are clear.  
- Paper is original and significant: broadens applicability of VR methods to saddle-point and monotone-inclusion problems with practical accelerations.  
- Suggested experiments and clarifications: resampling study, proximal-point complexity for non-strong problems, cost of maintaining non-uniform probabilities, and comparisons to extragradient/optimistic methods.  
- Pros: first linear-rate VR for composite saddle-points, monotone-operator approach, analyzable non-uniform sampling and factored splits, simple acceleration, clear complexity-vs-cost tradeoffs.  
- Cons: limited experiments/baselines, reliance on known parameters/no adaptivity, theory–practice mismatch for SAGA resampling, and some exposition/notation issues.  
- Recommendation: Accept, subject to broader empirical comparisons and clarifications on technical/implementation points.
