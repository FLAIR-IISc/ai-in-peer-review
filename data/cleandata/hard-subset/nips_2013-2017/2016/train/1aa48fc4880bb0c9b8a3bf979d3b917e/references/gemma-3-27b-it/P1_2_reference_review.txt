## Peer Review: Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

**Summary:**

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the saddle-point problem setting, a common formulation in many machine learning applications like regularized learning and robust optimization. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context, highlight the importance of splitting structures inherent in saddle-point problems, and demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. They also present an acceleration technique based on the “catalyst” framework, improving upon accelerated batch methods. The paper offers a thorough theoretical analysis and experimental validation, demonstrating practical improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. While these methods are well-established for convex optimization, their application to saddle-point problems and the accompanying theoretical analysis are novel.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis, based on interpreting the saddle-point problem as finding the zeros of a monotone operator, is strong and general, extending beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The clear characterization of the convergence rate and its dependence on problem parameters (condition number, sampling schemes) is valuable.
* **Practical Relevance:** The analysis of splitting structures (function-based vs. derivative-based, uniform vs. non-uniform sampling) provides insightful guidance for practical implementation. The experiments on real datasets demonstrate the advantages of the proposed algorithms, particularly with non-uniform sampling and the acceleration technique.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing batch and stochastic algorithms, including a thorough discussion of complexity bounds and trade-offs. The inclusion of analysis for bilinear saddle-point problems is particularly helpful, as it provides concrete examples and benchmarks.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the arguments are presented in a logical manner. The inclusion of an appendix with detailed proofs and experimental setup enhances readability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithms appears sensitive to the choice of regularization parameters (λ and γ), as highlighted in the experiments. While the paper discusses the importance of these parameters, more guidance on their selection in practical scenarios would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Analysis:** While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, it is also quite complex. Readers unfamiliar with monotone operator theory or specialized optimization techniques may find it challenging to follow.  Simplifying some of the derivations or providing more intuitive explanations could improve accessibility.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate the benefits of the proposed methods on specific datasets, exploring a wider range of problems and datasets could further strengthen the results.  Specifically, testing on large-scale problems with different characteristics of K and M would be valuable.
* **Adaptive Step-Size:** The paper mentions plans for future work on adaptivity to strong convexity-concavity constants.  Addressing this limitation in the current work would further improve the practical applicability of the algorithms.
* **Limited Discussion of Memory Requirements:** The analysis focuses primarily on computational complexity.  A more detailed discussion of the memory requirements, especially for SAGA with factored splits, would be useful for evaluating its scalability to very large problems.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems, filling a significant gap in the literature.
* **Practical Improvements:** The proposed algorithms demonstrate practical improvements over existing methods on real datasets, particularly with non-uniform sampling and acceleration.
* **Relevance to NIPS Audience:** The topic is highly relevant to the NIPS community, addressing a fundamental optimization problem with applications in various machine learning domains.
* **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides a thorough analysis of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Complexity of the Theoretical Analysis:** The technical depth of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* **Parameter Sensitivity and Limited Adaptivity:** The sensitivity to regularization parameters and the lack of adaptive step-size control could hinder the practical adoption of the algorithms.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions.  The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a valuable addition to the optimization toolkit, and the accompanying analysis provides important insights into the behavior of these algorithms in this context.  The paper addresses a relevant and important problem, and the results are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental validation.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should consider:

1.  Providing more guidance on the selection of regularization parameters (λ and γ).
2.  Simplifying the presentation of the theoretical analysis to improve accessibility.
3.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and problem characteristics.
4. Briefly discussing memory requirements, especially for SAGA.



I believe that addressing these minor points will further enhance the quality and impact of this excellent work.
