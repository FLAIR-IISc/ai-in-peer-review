### Summary

This paper presents the first extension of stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods to the general class of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors propose direct algorithmic adaptations of SVRG and SAGA for this setting and provide a novel convergence analysis that establishes linear convergence rates, a significant improvement over the sublinear rates of standard stochastic gradient methods for this problem class. Key contributions include: (1) a new convergence analysis based on the more general framework of monotone operators, which does not rely on the co-coercivity property used in analyses for standard convex minimization; (2) an investigation of different sampling schemes, highlighting the theoretical and practical importance of non-uniform sampling; and (3) an acceleration of the proposed methods using a simple "catalyst"-like wrapper, which leads to state-of-the-art complexity bounds. The authors validate their theoretical findings with experiments on machine learning problems involving non-separable losses and regularizers, demonstrating substantial improvements over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Review

This is an excellent and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It addresses a clear and important gap: the lack of fast, scalable, linearly-convergent algorithms for a broad class of saddle-point problems that are prevalent in modern machine learning.

**Quality: High**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis (proofs are detailed in the appendix). The central theoretical contribution—the analysis via monotone operators—is particularly strong. This approach is more general and correctly identifies why the standard analysis for SVRG/SAGA in the convex setting does not apply here (i.e., the gradient operator for saddle-point problems is monotone but not necessarily co-coercive). This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's underlying mathematical structure.

The experimental evaluation is convincing. The authors wisely choose to test their methods on problems with non-separable losses (AUC surrogate) and regularizers (clustering-inducing penalty), which are precisely the cases where this work is most needed and existing primal-only methods like standard SAGA would fail. The results in Figure 1 clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods (SAGA/SVRG with non-uniform sampling and especially accelerated SVRG) over batch methods and standard stochastic approaches. The authors are also honest about the limitations, noting that knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity constants is required, and they position this as a direction for future work.

**Clarity: High**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The structure flows logically from the problem definition, to existing batch and stochastic methods, to the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants, followed by acceleration and the theoretical generalization to monotone operators.

The use of Table 1 to summarize the complexity of all competing algorithms is highly effective and allows the reader to quickly grasp the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods. The distinction between different splits ("factored" vs. "individual") and the discussion of sampling probabilities are presented with sufficient detail. The core algorithms are presented in clear pseudocode. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand the contributions and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality: High**

While the algorithmic templates for SVRG and SAGA are directly inspired by prior work in convex minimization, the originality of this paper is substantial and lies in several areas:
1.  **Problem Domain:** This is the first work to successfully apply variance reduction techniques to general composite saddle-point problems and prove linear convergence. Previous work focused on the simpler convex minimization case or specific separable saddle-point structures.
2.  **Analysis Technique:** The convergence analysis is novel. By forgoing the co-coercivity assumption and relying only on monotonicity, the authors develop a proof technique that is both more general and necessary for this problem class. This is a significant theoretical departure from the original SVRG/SAGA papers.
3.  **Practical Insights:** The analysis and empirical validation of non-uniform sampling for this class of problems are novel and provide crucial practical guidance for achieving the best performance.
4.  **Acceleration:** The application of the catalyst framework is not just a straightforward extension. The authors note that the analysis is simpler in this setting, as the inner loop solver is applied to a proximal point update, which is inherently convergent. This provides a clean and effective acceleration scheme.

The paper does an excellent job of citing relevant prior work on variance reduction (SVRG, SAGA), saddle-point optimization (Chambolle-Pock), and monotone operators, clearly delineating its own novel contributions.

**Significance: High**

The results of this paper are highly significant and likely to have a lasting impact. Saddle-point formulations are becoming increasingly central to machine learning, appearing in areas like robust optimization, generative adversarial networks (GANs), and dual formulations of complex models. The lack of scalable, linearly convergent solvers has been a major bottleneck. This work provides a powerful and practical set of tools to fill this void.

Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale optimization will likely adopt and build upon these ideas. The theoretical generalization to monotone operators is also very important, as it extends the applicability of these powerful variance-reduction techniques beyond machine learning to areas like game theory and variational inequalities. The paper demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in optimization for a crucial class of problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   First paper to provide linearly-convergent stochastic algorithms (SVRG/SAGA) for general strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems.
*   Presents a novel and more general convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   Demonstrates the crucial importance of non-uniform sampling, providing strong theoretical and empirical justification.
*   Includes a simple and effective acceleration technique that achieves state-of-the-art complexity.
*   The work is highly significant, addressing a clear need in the ML community for scalable saddle-point solvers.
*   The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and rigorously executed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   A minor weakness is the dependence on knowing the problem's strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ), which can be difficult to set in practice. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation.

Overall, this is a top-tier paper that combines novel theory, practical algorithms, and convincing experiments to solve an important problem. It is a clear and strong contribution to the NIPS community.
