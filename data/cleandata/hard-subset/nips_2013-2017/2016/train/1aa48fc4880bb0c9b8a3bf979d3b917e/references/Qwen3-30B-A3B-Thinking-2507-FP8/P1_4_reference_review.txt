

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. The authors consider problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth and convex-concave and M is non-smooth but "simple" (with easy proximal operator). They extend SVRG and SAGA to this setting, analyze different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform), and show how to accelerate these algorithms using the "catalyst" framework. The work is well-motivated by machine learning examples where non-separable losses or regularizers make existing methods inapplicable, and provides rigorous theoretical guarantees and compelling experimental results.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide clear convergence guarantees for their algorithms (Theorems 1 and 2) and carefully analyze the impact of different sampling strategies. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly strong, showing that their analysis extends beyond saddle-point problems to a broader class of problems. The experimental results (Section 7) are well-designed and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., needing strong convexity-concavity constants) and provide a clear path for future work.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and states the contributions. Technical sections are logically structured with precise definitions and assumptions. The connection to previous work is well-explained, and the paper is self-contained for readers familiar with convex optimization. The experimental setup is clearly described, and results are presented in an accessible manner (with clear figures showing distance to optimizers). A minor suggestion would be to add a brief summary table comparing algorithm complexities at the end of Section 2.

## Originality
This work represents a significant original contribution. While there are methods for separable saddle-point problems, this is the first to extend variance reduction techniques from convex minimization to the general saddle-point setting. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work (e.g., in Sections 2.2 and 2.3), and the analysis using monotone operators (Section 6) is a novel contribution that extends the applicability of their methods. The examples from machine learning (Section 2.1) where their methods are particularly useful (non-separable losses, robust optimization) further demonstrate the novelty of their approach.

## Significance
The results are highly significant for machine learning optimization. Many important machine learning problems (non-separable losses, robust optimization, convex relaxations of unsupervised learning) can be formulated as saddle-point problems where existing methods fail. The authors provide the first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for these problems, which is a major advance. The experimental results show clear improvements over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The extension to monotone operators also broadens the applicability to a wider class of problems. This work is likely to be widely adopted by researchers working on machine learning problems that can be formulated as saddle-point problems.

## Strengths
1. First convergence analysis for variance reduction methods in saddle-point problems
2. Rigorous theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
3. Demonstrated significant experimental improvements over existing methods
4. Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems
5. Well-motivated machine learning examples where the method is particularly useful

## Weaknesses
1. The paper assumes knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants, which might be a practical limitation (though the authors acknowledge this as future work)
2. The complexity analysis for factored splits could be more detailed for specific applications
3. The experimental scope is limited to two datasets; more diverse experiments would strengthen the claims

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to optimization for machine learning, extending powerful variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are compelling, and the work is clearly original and addresses an important gap in the literature. While there are some limitations (like the need for strong convexity-concavity constants), these are acknowledged and provide a clear path for future work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the state of the art in optimization methods with clear applications to machine learning problems.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- First convergence analysis for variance reduction in saddle-point problems
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
- Significant experimental improvements over existing methods
- Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability
- Well-motivated examples from machine learning

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Assumes knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants (acknowledged as future work)
- Limited experimental scope (only two datasets)
- Complexity analysis for factored splits could be more detailed

**Final Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is of high quality, original, and significant, making a clear contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS.
