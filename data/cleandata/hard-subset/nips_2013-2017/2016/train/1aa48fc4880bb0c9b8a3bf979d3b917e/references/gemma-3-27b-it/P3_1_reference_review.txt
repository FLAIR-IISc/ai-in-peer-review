## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, specifically extending stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods like SVRG and SAGA to the challenging setting of saddle-point problems. The work is technically sound, well-written, and offers valuable theoretical and practical insights. While the theoretical framework builds upon established results, its application to saddle-point problems and the subsequent analysis, particularly the connection to monotone operators and the exploration of different splitting strategies, are novel and impactful. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of solving saddle-point problems of the form min<sub>x</sub> max<sub>y</sub> K(x, y) + M(x, y), which frequently arise in machine learning (e.g., dual formulations of supervised learning, robust optimization).  Existing SVR methods (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) are well-established for finite-sum convex minimization, but their extension to saddle-point problems hasn’t been rigorously analyzed.  This work fills that gap by providing convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting, highlighting key differences compared to the convex case. Crucially, the authors leverage the interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, broadening the applicability of their analysis. They also investigate the impact of different splitting strategies (function-based vs. derivative-based) and the importance of non-uniform sampling.

The paper correctly positions itself relative to existing literature, including forward-backward splitting methods (both batch and stochastic) and existing algorithms for separable saddle-point problems.  It builds on the catalyst framework of Nesterov et al. (2016) to achieve acceleration.  The comparison to related work is thorough and insightful.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to the non-convex setting of saddle-point problems, and the accompanying convergence analysis, are genuinely novel.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-presented. The connection to monotone operator theory provides a broader perspective and strengthens the results. The authors clearly state their assumptions (A, B, C) and derive convergence rates accordingly.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The identification of the importance of non-uniform sampling and the exploration of different splitting strategies have direct implications for practical algorithm design.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:**  The experimental evaluation on real-world datasets (sido, rcv1) demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly the accelerated SVRG variant.  The comparisons to baseline methods (batch and stochastic) are well-executed and support the theoretical findings.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the technical details.
* **Extension to Monotone Operators:** The generalization of the framework to monotone operators is a significant conceptual leap, broadening the scope and impact of the work.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of strong convexity-concavity (A) is relatively strong and may not hold in all practical scenarios. While acknowledged, a discussion of the algorithm’s behavior under weaker assumptions (e.g., convexity-concavity) would be valuable.
* **Mini-Batch Size Tuning:** The choice of mini-batch size *m* impacts performance significantly. The paper provides some guidance but a more detailed exploration of the trade-offs involved in selecting *m* would be helpful.
* **Practical Acceleration:** While the theory demonstrates potential acceleration, the experiments show that accelerated SVRG is not *always* superior to the standard version, particularly with uniform sampling. A deeper investigation of the conditions under which acceleration is most effective would strengthen the practical contribution.
* **Complexity of Notation:**  The notation, while generally clear, can be dense and potentially intimidating for readers unfamiliar with the field. Some simplification might improve accessibility.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.1:**  The examples provided are excellent for motivating the problem.  Could the authors briefly discuss the computational cost of evaluating the proximal operator for the specific examples mentioned (e.g., grouped norms, total variation)?
* **Section 5:**  The discussion of the “catalyst” framework is concise. A slightly more detailed explanation of how the algorithm is adapted to the saddle-point setting would be beneficial.
* **Section 7:** The experimental setup is clear. It would be helpful to report confidence intervals or standard deviations alongside the reported distances to optimizers.
* **Theorem 1 & 2:**  The convergence rates are expressed in terms of the number of epochs/iterations. Could the authors provide an estimate of the number of function/gradient evaluations required to achieve a given level of accuracy?



**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant theoretical contribution extending SVR methods to saddle-point problems.
* Rigorous analysis and clear presentation of results.
* Strong experimental validation demonstrating practical effectiveness.
* Broad applicability through the connection to monotone operators.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning optimization.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Relatively strong assumptions regarding strong convexity-concavity.
* Performance of accelerated SVRG is not consistently superior in experiments.
* Notation can be dense and complex.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest that the authors:

1.  Discuss the behavior of the algorithms under weaker convexity-concavity assumptions.
2.  Provide more detailed guidance on the selection of the mini-batch size *m*.
3.  Investigate the conditions under which accelerated SVRG consistently outperforms the standard version.
4.  Consider simplifying the notation where possible to improve accessibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a rigorous and insightful approach, and the results are likely to have a significant impact on future research in optimization for machine learning.
