Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) where K is smooth convex-concave and M is strongly convex-concave with a computable proximal operator. The key idea is to extend variance-reduced finite-sum methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems by (i) viewing the gradient operator B(x,y)=(∂xK, −∂yK) as a sum of Lipschitz forward operators Bi that need not be gradients of functions, (ii) using a forward-backward (prox for M, “forward” for K) iteration with variance-reduced stochastic estimates of B, and (iii) designing non-uniform sampling and “factored” splits tailored to bilinear problems to reduce variance and storage. The analysis relies on monotone operator theory: the problem is to find a zero of A+B where A is the (strongly) monotone subdifferential of M and B is monotone and Lipschitz. The authors prove linear convergence for SVRG and SAGA under strong convexity-concavity, derive complexities in terms of L (global Lipschitz constant) and L̄(π) (sampling-dependent smoothness), and show that a catalyst-style regularization yields further acceleration. They also show the framework extends to general monotone inclusions beyond saddle points. Experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers illustrate consistent improvements over batch and stochastic FB; on a separable case they compare with primal SAGA.

This sits among extensive work on finite-sum variance reduction for convex minimization (SAG/SAGA/SVRG, proximal variants) and stochastic primal-dual/coordinate methods for separable saddle-point problems (e.g., Chambolle-Pock variants, SPDC). The novelty is handling non-separable M and allowing decompositions at the operator level (not necessarily gradient of a component function), with linear rates under strong convexity-concavity and explicit non-uniform sampling. The extension to general monotone operators contrasts with prior variance-reduction analyses that typically rely on cocoercivity.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis appears technically sound. Leveraging strong monotonicity of A and Lipschitz B to establish contraction with variance-reduced estimators is appropriate and avoids cocoercivity assumptions that fail for saddle-point problems. The linear rates for SVRG/SAGA and the catalyst acceleration are plausible and align with intuition from convex minimization, with adaptations (weighted norms via λ, γ; L̄(π) entering complexities; epoch schedules). The operator-splitting view justifies the generalization beyond saddle points.
- Assumptions: Strong convexity-concavity and availability of prox_M are standard for linear convergence in composite settings. The requirement to know λ, γ, and L/L̄ for step sizes is reasonable but could be relaxed in practice (the authors note adaptivity as future work).
- Sampling: The non-uniform sampling design and bounds via L̄(π) are carefully derived, with concrete choices for bilinear K using row/column or entrywise norms; these yield provably better constants than uniform sampling.
- Algorithms: The SVRG/SAGA variants are clearly specified. The factored split/storage trick for bilinear problems (O(n+d) memory) is practical.
- Experiments: Results support the claims: non-uniform SAGA and especially accelerated SVRG outperform batch and stochastic FB, with larger gains on ill-conditioned problems. However, the experimental scope is modest (two datasets), and baselines could be broader (e.g., comparison to SPDC or modern primal-dual with line search where applicable). The separable case comparison to primal SAGA is appropriate and shows that when separability exists, specialized primal methods can be faster.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable given the technical breadth. The motivation for operator-level splits and non-uniform sampling is clear, and the bilinear case is used effectively to instantiate constants and complexities. Some notation could be tightened: e.g., Theorem 2 introduces μ without prior definition in the main text; L vs. L^2 usage could be standardized; occasionally σ/L scaling interleaves with λ, γ in ways that could confuse non-experts. Many proofs are deferred to appendices (appropriate for space), but a higher-level proof sketch in the main text for at least one theorem would aid intuition. Practical guidance for step-size selection when L/L̄ are unknown would also help.

Originality
High. To my knowledge (for the NIPS 2016 timeframe), this is the first linear convergence analysis of SVRG/SAGA-type methods for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and, more broadly, for finding zeros of strongly monotone plus Lipschitz monotone operators without cocoercivity. The operator decomposition that does not require component functions and the factored split/storage for bilinear K are novel and practically important. The catalyst-acceleration adaptation to the saddle-point/operator setting is also a valuable contribution with a simpler analysis than in convex minimization.

Significance
High. Many ML problems (robust optimization, structured prediction, total variation/grouped norms, dual formulations) are naturally saddle points and often non-separable; enabling variance-reduced, linearly convergent methods in this regime is impactful. The non-uniform sampling guidance and factored variant are likely to be adopted in practice. The monotone operator view opens the door to applications in variational inequalities and game-theoretic equilibria. While the requirement of strong convexity-concavity limits scope, the authors’ acceleration and regularization approach partially mitigates this.

Pros
- Solid and general theory with linear rates under standard assumptions.
- First extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle points with operator-level splits.
- Non-uniform sampling analysis and practical factored variant for bilinear problems.
- Catalyst-style acceleration with clear complexity trade-offs.
- Empirical gains align with theory.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited in breadth; stronger baselines (e.g., SPDC on separable cases, advanced primal-dual methods) would strengthen the case.
- Some notation/step-size constants are introduced abruptly; clarity can be improved.
- Practical guidance for estimating L, L̄, λ, γ and for tuning in absence of exact constants is limited.
- SAGA non-uniform sampling requires resampling to validate the bound; ablations on this choice would be informative.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the appearance of μ in Theorem 2 and unify notation for Lipschitz/strong monotonicity constants across sections.
- Provide practical heuristics for non-uniform probabilities beyond bilinear K (e.g., approximate Li or block norms).
- Discuss overhead to compute/maintain non-uniform sampling distributions and L̄ in large-scale settings.
- Include comparisons to SPDC on separable problems and to a tuned Chambolle–Pock/mirror-prox variant if possible.
- Comment on robustness to mis-specified λ, γ, L and whether backtracking or adaptive step sizes work in practice.
- Briefly sketch the main contraction argument in the body to aid readers who cannot consult the appendix.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong, timely, and well-justified contribution that advances variance-reduced methods to an important and previously underserved class of problems. With minor clarifications and, ideally, slightly broader empirical comparisons, it merits acceptance.
