

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context. The authors consider the saddle-point problem min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth and convex-concave, and M is non-smooth but "simple" with an easy proximal operator. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point problems, (2) analysis of different splitting strategies (including factored splits) and the importance of non-uniform sampling, and (3) acceleration via the "catalyst" framework. The work builds on prior work on SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) for convex minimization, while extending to saddle-point problems and monotone operators.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis for extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, which is a significant gap in the literature. The analysis differs from convex minimization due to the nature of saddle-point problems, and the authors effectively leverage monotonicity properties rather than relying on convexity.

2. **Novel Insights:** The paper makes important observations about the saddle-point structure, particularly that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency (both theoretically and empirically), and that there are two natural ways to split the problem (by functions or by partial derivatives). The factored splitting approach for bilinear problems is particularly insightful.

3. **Practical Impact:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The comparison with both batch methods and existing stochastic methods (like forward-backward) shows significant improvements in convergence rates.

4. **Theoretical Extension:** The extension to monotone operators is a valuable theoretical contribution that broadens the applicability of the results beyond saddle-point problems, making the work relevant to variational inequalities and other related areas.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** The paper assumes strong convexity-concavity throughout, which is a limitation for some applications. While the authors mention future work on adaptivity, the current paper doesn't address how to handle problems where these constants are unknown or difficult to estimate.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments focus on specific types of problems (binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers), and while they're well-designed, it would be helpful to see more diverse applications to better demonstrate the generality of the approach.

3. **Comparison with Recent Methods:** The paper doesn't thoroughly compare with other recent methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., some primal-dual methods with different acceleration techniques), which would strengthen the significance claim.

4. **Technical Accessibility:** The extension to monotone operators, while theoretically valuable, might be challenging for readers without background in monotone operator theory. Some additional intuition or simpler examples would help make this contribution more accessible.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Fills a significant gap in the literature by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with novel insights about sampling strategies
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages through well-designed experiments
- Makes a valuable extension to monotone operators, broadening the applicability
- Well-written and clearly organized, with thorough comparison to related work

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The strong convexity-concavity assumption limits the applicability to some problems
- Limited experimental scope compared to the broad theoretical claims
- Could provide better comparison with recent saddle-point optimization methods

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this context. The theoretical insights about splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling are particularly valuable. The experiments support the theoretical claims and demonstrate clear practical advantages. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, the paper is well-motivated, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The extension to monotone operators is a particularly strong theoretical component that broadens the impact of the work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the extension to monotone operators significantly broadens the applicability of the results. To improve the paper, I would suggest:

1. Adding a brief discussion of how to handle cases where strong convexity-concavity constants are unknown (even if just as a future work direction)
2. Expanding the experimental section to include a more diverse set of problem instances
3. Providing a more thorough comparison with recent primal-dual methods for saddle-point problems

These suggestions would strengthen an already strong paper, but they don't detract from the significant contribution that the paper makes. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS, addresses an important gap in the literature, and advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning.
