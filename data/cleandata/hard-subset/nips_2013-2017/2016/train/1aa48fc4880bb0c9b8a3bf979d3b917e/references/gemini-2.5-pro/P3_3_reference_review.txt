This paper presents a significant contribution by extending the popular stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to solve composite decomposable saddle-point problems. The core problem is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is a smooth convex-concave function with a finite-sum structure, and `M` is a strongly convex-concave function with a simple proximal operator. The authors provide the first linear convergence guarantees for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. A key aspect of their analysis is the use of the monotone operator framework, which is more general and avoids the co-coercivity assumption typically used in SVR analyses for convex minimization—an assumption that does not hold for saddle-point problems. The paper also explores the benefits of non-uniform sampling, different problem decomposition schemes, and proposes an accelerated variant using the Catalyst framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** This work addresses a critical and timely problem. Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous in modern machine learning, arising in areas like robust optimization, AUC maximization, and generative adversarial networks. While SVR methods have revolutionized large-scale convex minimization, their application to the more general saddle-point setting was an open problem. This paper provides the first linearly convergent, incremental algorithms for this general class of problems, a major theoretical and practical advancement over existing batch methods (which are slow) and standard stochastic methods (which are not linearly convergent).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a standout feature. The main technical hurdle is that the gradient operator for a saddle-point problem is monotone but not co-coercive, rendering standard SVR analyses inapplicable. The authors elegantly overcome this by providing a direct analysis based only on monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity. This approach is powerful, correct, and extends the applicability of their methods beyond saddle-point problems to a broader class of variational inequalities and monotone inclusion problems.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It clearly articulates its contributions, carefully lays out the assumptions, and provides a concise but comprehensive overview of related batch and stochastic algorithms. Table 1 is particularly effective at summarizing the theoretical complexity improvements of the proposed methods over existing ones. The algorithms are presented clearly, and the logical flow from standard SVRG/SAGA to the accelerated version is easy to follow.

4.  **Generality and Practical Relevance:** The proposed framework is quite general. It allows for non-separable composite terms `M(x, y)` and flexible decompositions of the smooth term `K(x, y)`, including "factored" splits for bilinear problems. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of their methods, especially the importance of non-uniform sampling and the effectiveness of the accelerated SVRG, on machine learning tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers. The experiments clearly show a significant performance gain over relevant baselines.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification

1.  **SAGA Convergence Rate:** The convergence rate presented for SAGA in Theorem 2 seems to depend on `|I|^2` (via the step-size `σ`), where `|I|` is the number of components in the sum. This is in contrast to the typical `|I|` dependency for SAGA in convex minimization. A quadratic dependency would make the algorithm impractical for problems with a very large number of components (e.g., one per data point). Could the authors clarify if this is a fundamental property of SAGA for monotone operators or an artifact of the current analysis? If the latter, it would be valuable to discuss potential paths to a tighter analysis.

2.  **Comparison with Stochastic Primal-Dual Methods:** The paper positions itself against batch and standard stochastic methods. However, there is a rich literature on stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, 2014; Zhu & Storkey, 2015) that also achieve linear convergence for saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure. These methods are typically restricted to the bilinear case `K(x,y) = y^T Kx` with separable `M(x,y) = f(x) - g(y)`, but this covers a large fraction of the motivating examples. A more direct comparison, both theoretical and empirical, with these specialized primal-dual algorithms would help to delineate more precisely the settings where the proposed general-purpose SVR methods are superior (e.g., for non-bilinear `K` or non-separable `M`).

3.  **Practicality of Acceleration:** While the Catalyst-based acceleration is theoretically powerful and leads to the best complexity, two-loop algorithms can be challenging to tune in practice. The outer loop introduces a new parameter `τ`, and the inner SVRG loop requires setting the number of iterations per epoch correctly. A brief discussion on the practical aspects of tuning the accelerated algorithm would enhance the paper's value for practitioners.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The experimental results are promising, but some details are missing that would aid reproducibility. For example, what was the epoch length (number of inner iterations) used for SVRG? How were the step-sizes `σ` chosen in practice (i.e., were the theoretical constants `L` and `L̄` computed or tuned)? Providing these details, perhaps in the appendix, would strengthen the experimental section.

### Summary

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to large-scale optimization for machine learning. It successfully bridges the gap between variance-reduction techniques and the important class of saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is novel and strong, the writing is clear, and the empirical results validate the proposed methods. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for clarification and strengthening the comparison to related work; they do not detract from the core importance of the contribution. The paper is of high quality and is certain to be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
