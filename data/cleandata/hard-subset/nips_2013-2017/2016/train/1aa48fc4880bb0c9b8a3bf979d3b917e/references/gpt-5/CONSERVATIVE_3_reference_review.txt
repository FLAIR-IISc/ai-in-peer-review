Summary
The paper studies stochastic variance-reduced methods for convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex-concave and M is strongly convex-concave with an efficient proximal operator. The main contributions are:
- Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems (and more generally, to zeros of A+B with A strongly monotone and B monotone), with linear convergence under strong convex-concavity. The analysis is framed in monotone operator theory and does not rely on co-coercivity.
- Handling decompositions of the smooth operator B into summands Bi beyond functional splits, including “factored” splits tailored to bilinear problems, and advocating non-uniform sampling with improved constants. Complexity bounds depend on L and a sampling-dependent L̄(π).
- An acceleration via a catalyst/proximal-point outer loop, yielding improved complexity L̄·sqrt(T(B)·max_i T(Bi)) up to logs, and claimed to dominate accelerated batch methods.
- Empirical evaluation on two binary classification datasets with non-separable losses/regularizers and one separable benchmark, comparing batch FB, accelerated FB, stochastic FB, SAGA/SVRG (uniform and non-uniform), and accelerated SVRG.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization and on primal–dual/forward–backward schemes (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock) for saddle points. It contrasts with stochastic FB for monotone inclusions (sublinear rates) and proximal variance reduction for convex minimization. The extension to monotone operators is positioned against co-coercive settings (e.g., SMART), and the acceleration builds on the Catalyst framework.

Strengths
- Timely problem: variance-reduced methods for saddle points and non-separable structures are of broad interest.
- Theory: linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA in the strongly convex-concave composite setting under general decompositions Bi, with a clean monotone-operator proof strategy. The sampling-dependent constants and discussion of factored vs individual splits are insightful, especially for bilinear K.
- Acceleration: a proximal-point flavored catalyst outer loop is elegant in the saddle-point/monotone setting and the complexity calculus is clear.
- Practical considerations: non-uniform sampling, mini-batching, and memory/storage trade-offs are discussed; pseudocode is provided.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited. Only two datasets, moderate scale, and specialized non-separable losses/regularizers are shown. No wall-clock time, memory, or implementation overheads are reported, despite the algorithms’ reliance on stored operator values and non-uniform sampling. Claims like “always superior to accelerated batch” are not convincingly demonstrated empirically.
- Baselines: Missing or thin comparisons to strong and relevant saddle-point methods, e.g., Chambolle–Pock with optimized step sizes/over-relaxation and diagonal preconditioning, stochastic mirror-prox, and primal-dual variance-reduced methods (e.g., SPDC) on bilinear ERM. For non-separable settings, a tuned PDHG baseline would be informative.
- Assumptions and constants: Step sizes depend on µ, L, and L̄(π), which are nontrivial to estimate in practice, especially for non-bilinear K. The empirical section acknowledges deviating from the resampling step required for the non-uniform SAGA analysis; more discussion on robustness to mis-specified constants and sampling is needed.
- Storage and practicality: The individual split requires O(nd) memory for SAGA; factored storage reduces to O(n+d) but is specific to bilinear K. The paper should better quantify the cost of computing/maintaining non-uniform probabilities (row/column norms) and the overhead of maintaining G in SAGA.
- Novelty claims: The “first convergence analysis” claim should be carefully delimited. There is related work on variance reduction for saddle-point/dual formulations (e.g., SPDC/ASDCA-type methods) and stochastic primal-dual methods. The paper cites SMART and others, but a more precise positioning versus primal-dual variance-reduced methods for ERM would help.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound under the stated assumptions, and the monotone-operator perspective is technically clean. However, some expressions are unusual (e.g., FB with σ=1/L^2 rather than the more standard 1/L form; the notation may embed scaling in the norm). Theorems provide linear rates with explicit dependence on L and L̄. The experimental methodology is not yet sufficient to validate the practical superiority or robustness of the proposed methods.

Clarity
Overall well written, but notation is heavy and some definitions (e.g., exact normalization leading to L^2 in FB) could be clarified earlier. The distinction between individual and factored splits and their memory/runtime implications should be summarized more prominently. The acceleration section is terse and relies on appendix details; a brief main-text derivation of the outer-loop choice of τ and its practical selection would help.

Originality
Moderate. Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point/monotone operator settings without co-coercivity and handling general Bi decompositions is a meaningful step. The catalyst-style acceleration in this context is natural but not conceptually new. The sampling analyses for bilinear problems are useful.

Significance
Potentially significant for practitioners handling non-separable losses/regularizers and large bilinear problems. That said, broader impact depends on demonstrating practical gains over established primal–dual methods and on alleviating the need for hard-to-estimate constants.

Questions for authors
- Can you report wall-clock, memory usage, and sensitivity to µ/L/L̄ mis-specification? How do you estimate these in practice?
- How does your method compare to tuned PDHG (Chambolle–Pock) with diagonal preconditioning and to stochastic mirror-prox on your tasks?
- For the non-uniform SAGA variant, you mention not performing the required resampling. How does this affect convergence empirically? Any theory for the mixed sampling you used?
- Can you demonstrate acceleration gains beyond bilinear K and provide an ablation showing the effect of τ and the frequency of outer updates?
- For large sparse data, what is the overhead of maintaining non-uniform probabilities and stored gradients? Any strategies to reduce it?

Pros
- Solid theoretical contribution under a general monotone-operator view
- Careful treatment of sampling and factored splits for bilinear problems
- Simple and plausible acceleration scheme
- Clear algorithms and bounds

Cons
- Limited empirical evidence; missing strong baselines and wall-clock analyses
- Practical deployment details (step-size constants, storage, sampling overhead) underexplored
- Some over-claims relative to experimental support

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject (score: 5). The theory is interesting and likely correct, but I am not convinced by the current empirical validation and comparative analysis. Strengthening experiments with stronger baselines, wall-clock/memory metrics, and demonstrations on larger and more diverse problems would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
High (4/5).
