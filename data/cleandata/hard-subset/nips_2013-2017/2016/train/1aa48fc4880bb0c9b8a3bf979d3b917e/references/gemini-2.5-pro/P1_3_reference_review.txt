### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the popular variance-reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the broader class of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. The key motivation is to tackle machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization), which cannot be directly addressed by standard SVRG/SAGA but often have natural saddle-point formulations.

The authors propose straightforward algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA to this setting by applying the variance-reduced updates to the saddle-point gradient operator. The main contributions are theoretical:
1.  They provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods on saddle-point problems.
2.  The analysis is framed more generally in the context of finding zeros of monotone operators, demonstrating that the results hold beyond saddle-point problems and, crucially, do not require the co-coercivity assumption used in prior work on SVRG for monotone operators, which made them inapplicable to this setting.
3.  They introduce and analyze different ways to decompose the problem ("factored splits") and demonstrate the theoretical and empirical necessity of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal rates.
4.  They show how to apply the "Catalyst" acceleration framework to their SVRG variant, resulting in an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.

Experiments on binary classification tasks with non-separable AUC-based loss and clustering-inducing regularizers validate the theory, showing that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, significantly outperform existing batch and standard stochastic algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the core strength of the paper. Proving linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting is a significant result. Grounding the analysis in monotone operator theory (and correctly identifying the lack of co-coercivity as the key challenge) is elegant and generalizes the contribution beyond the immediate application. The theoretical rates presented in Table 1 are clear, comprehensive, and effectively showcase the advantages of the proposed methods, especially with non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The claims are well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix and the experimental results.
*   **Weaknesses:** The work relies on knowledge of several problem-dependent constants (strong-convexity/concavity λ and γ, Lipschitz constants L and L̄). While common in this line of work, it is a practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this and suggest adaptivity as future work, which is an honest assessment.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. Section 2 does an excellent job of setting up the notation, assumptions, and key examples. The algorithms are presented clearly. The inclusion of Table 1 is extremely helpful, providing a concise summary of the complexity of all competing methods and making the paper's contribution easy to grasp. The experimental section is clear and the results are well-presented.
*   **Weaknesses:** The presentation of the acceleration in Section 5 is slightly rushed. The details of the algorithm (Algorithm 3) are relegated to the appendix, which makes the main text slightly less self-contained. A more detailed sketch or explanation of how the proximal-point updates integrate with the SVRG epochs in the main body would have been beneficial. In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), the block for updating the stored gradients is marked "(optional)", which is confusing as this update is the core of the SAGA method. This likely refers to the choice of resampling or re-using indices, but the wording should be clarified to avoid ambiguity.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.
*   **Strengths:** While the algorithms are extensions of existing SVRG/SAGA ideas, their application and analysis in the saddle-point context are novel and non-trivial. This is the first work to establish linear convergence for variance-reduced methods on this problem class. The analysis via monotone operators without co-coercivity is a significant theoretical novelty that distinguishes it from previous attempts to generalize these methods (e.g., [30]). The exploration of "factored splits" and the rigorous demonstration of the benefits of non-uniform sampling are also original contributions in this context.
*   **Weaknesses:** The core algorithmic ideas (variance reduction, catalyst acceleration) are borrowed from prior work. The contribution is thus more in the successful transfer, adaptation, and analysis of these ideas to a new, important domain, rather than the invention of a fundamentally new algorithmic template. This is a minor point, as the contribution is still very strong.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the NIPS community.
*   **Strengths:** This work bridges a gap between the highly successful variance-reduction literature and the important class of saddle-point optimization problems. It makes state-of-the-art optimization techniques applicable to a wider range of machine learning models, particularly in structured prediction, robust optimization, and learning with complex, non-decomposable objectives. The provided algorithms, especially the accelerated SVRG variant, demonstrably advance the state of the art. The clear theoretical results and strong empirical performance make it highly likely that researchers and practitioners will use and build upon these ideas.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance:** Solves a well-motivated and important problem: applying fast variance-reduced methods to non-separable problems via their saddle-point formulation.
2.  **Strong, Novel Theory:** Provides the first linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in this setting. The analysis based on monotone operators is a key theoretical innovation.
3.  **Comprehensive Work:** The paper covers two algorithms (SVRG, SAGA), an acceleration scheme, insightful analysis of sampling strategies, and strong empirical validation.
4.  **Excellent Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a technically deep contribution accessible and easy to understand. Table 1 is a model of clarity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Dependence on Unknown Constants:** The practical application of the methods requires knowing or estimating multiple problem parameters, a limitation the authors note but do not solve.
2.  **Algorithmic Novelty is Incremental:** The algorithms themselves are direct adaptations of SVRG/SAGA. The primary novelty is in the analysis. (This is a weak argument against, as the analysis itself is a major contribution).

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It is technically strong, clearly written, and addresses an important gap. The theoretical analysis is novel and insightful, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
