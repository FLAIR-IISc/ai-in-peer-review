

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context. The authors demonstrate that these methods can be applied to saddle-point problems with non-separable losses or regularizers, which are common in machine learning. The paper also shows how to accelerate these algorithms using the "catalyst" framework. The main contribution is the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods in the saddle-point setting, which differs significantly from convex minimization.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by extending variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The connection to monotone operators in Section 6 is a nice theoretical extension.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the significant contribution, the paper contains numerous minor technical inconsistencies that undermine its technical rigor:

1. **Notation errors:** The paper consistently uses "λ2" and "γ2" instead of "λ/2" and "γ/2" in equations (2) and throughout the paper. This is a fundamental notational error that could lead to confusion about the proximal operator definition.

2. **Proximal operator definition:** In assumption (A), the proximal operator is defined as "arg min x∈Rd max y∈Rn σM(x, y) + λ2 ‖x− x ′‖2 − γ2 ‖y − y ′‖2." This is problematic because the minimization is over x while maximizing over y, which is not standard for proximal operators. The authors should clarify why this definition is valid for saddle-point problems.

3. **Inconsistent use of L:** The paper defines L as "supΩ(x−x′,y−y′)=1 Ω∗(B(x, y)−B(x′, y′))" in assumption (B), but then uses L in the accelerated algorithm without clarifying the relationship. In Theorem 1, they write "L 2" instead of "L^2" (missing superscript).

4. **Vague explanations:** The paper claims that "factored splits" replace |I| with max{|J|, |K|} in Theorem 2, but provides no justification for why it's the maximum rather than the sum. This is a critical detail for understanding the computational complexity.

5. **Formatting issues:** The abstract contains a typo ("th Conference" instead of "The Conference"), and references have inconsistent punctuation (e.g., "Adv. NIPS," vs. "Adv. NIPS,,"). The paper also inconsistently uses "SAGA/SVRG" and "SAGA or SVRG."

6. **Experimental section:** The dataset "sido" is not a standard dataset name, and the paper doesn't explain why it's called "sido." The experimental comparison for rcv1 is confusing because it claims the problem is separable yet still compares to primal methods.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents a novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and formatting errors indicate a lack of careful proofreading and attention to detail. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these flaws are significant enough to warrant rejection, especially since they could indicate potential issues with the technical content that might not be caught in a less meticulous review. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these minor but important issues before consideration for publication.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**
- Pro: Novel extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems, solid theoretical analysis, good experimental results
- Con: Multiple notation errors, inconsistent use of L, unclear proximal operator definition, vague explanations, formatting inconsistencies

The paper has significant potential but currently fails to meet the high technical standards expected at NIPS due to these minor but pervasive errors. I recommend rejection.
