This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods—specifically SVRG and SAGA—for solving saddle-point problems, a common formulation in machine learning arising from duality. The authors provide convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, extending previous work focused on convex minimization. They also highlight the importance of considering different splitting strategies for the saddle-point formulation and demonstrate the benefits of non-uniform sampling. A significant contribution is showing how the algorithms can be accelerated using a catalyst-like framework, and extending the analysis to the broader class of monotone operators.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the relevance of saddle-point problems in machine learning. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to this setting appears straightforward, but the theoretical analysis – particularly the convergence guarantees – is a substantial contribution. The connection to monotone operator theory is insightful and broadens the applicability of the presented work.  The experimental validation, while relatively limited in scope, provides empirical support for the theoretical findings, particularly the advantage of non-uniform sampling and the acceleration method.  The authors adequately reference related work, including foundational papers in optimization and recent advances in stochastic gradient methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems and providing convergence analysis is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis, particularly the extension to monotone operators, is strong and adds significant theoretical value.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The focus on saddle-point problems is highly relevant to many machine learning applications, and the proposed methods offer potential performance improvements.
*   **Insights into Splitting:** The discussion of different splitting strategies for saddle-point problems is insightful.
*   **Acceleration Framework:** The catalyst-like acceleration is a valuable addition and improves practical performance.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex theoretical concepts relatively accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions (e.g., strong convexity-concavity, Lipschitz continuity). The robustness of the algorithms to deviations from these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to a few datasets and problem settings. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with other relevant algorithms (e.g., those specifically designed for saddle-point problems), would strengthen the empirical validation.  The comparison to primal methods is useful, but could be expanded.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of SVRG/SAGA can be sensitive to hyperparameters (e.g., step size, sampling probabilities). The paper does not provide much guidance on how to tune these parameters in practice, especially in different application domains.
*   **Practical Implementation:** While the authors mention a potential for adaptive step size selection in future work, a discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., memory requirements, parallelization) would be beneficial.
*   **Notation**: There are a few instances where the notation could be clarified for better readability. Specifically, the dual norm definition could benefit from a more explicit explanation.
*   **Catalyst implementation detail**: In Section 5, the text states that the acceleration technique extends to batch setups. However, it's unclear how the algorithm in that section would be adapted for batch setups, and details would enhance clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and provide valuable insights into the convergence properties of SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting.
*   The experimental results, while limited, support the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The work extends existing algorithms to a broader class of problems (monotone operators).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may limit the practical applicability of the algorithms.
*   The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to fully demonstrate the benefits of the proposed methods.
*   The lack of guidance on hyperparameter tuning could make it difficult for practitioners to apply the algorithms effectively.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution with significant theoretical and practical potential. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, and the assumptions are fairly strong, the novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the potential impact on the field warrant acceptance.  

**Recommendation:**  Accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Expand the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and problem settings.
*   Investigate the robustness of the algorithms to deviations from the strong convexity-concavity assumption.
*   Provide guidance on hyperparameter tuning.
*   Discuss practical implementation challenges and potential optimizations.
*   Clarify the notation and provide more detailed explanations where needed.
*   Elaborate the catalyst acceleration technique in the context of batch updates.
