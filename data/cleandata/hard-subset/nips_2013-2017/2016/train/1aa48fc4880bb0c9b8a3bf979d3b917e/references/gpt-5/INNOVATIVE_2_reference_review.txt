Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The central idea is to bring variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG/SAGA) to saddle-point and, more generally, monotone operator settings. The authors (i) adapt SVRG and SAGA to estimate the monotone operator B(x,y)=(∂_x K, −∂_y K) using unbiased variance-reduced estimators; (ii) allow “operator-level” decompositions B=∑_i B_i without requiring each B_i to be a gradient of a convex–concave function, and propose a factored split aligned with rows/columns in bilinear problems; (iii) analyze non-uniform sampling and mini-batching via a tailored smoothness constant L̄(π); (iv) derive linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity with explicit iteration and runtime complexities; (v) provide a Catalyst-style acceleration for the saddle-point setting via a proximal-point envelope, yielding improved complexity; and (vi) extend the analysis to general maximal strongly monotone A and Lipschitz summands B_i with monotone sum B, thus covering variational inequalities and convex minimization as special cases. Experiments on non-separable regularizers/losses and a separable baseline show that (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling improves over batch primal–dual schemes, particularly on ill-conditioned problems.

In relation to existing work, this goes beyond applying SAG/SVRG to primal/dual finite-sum problems that require separability, and beyond stochastic FB methods with O(1/t) rates. The paper discusses Arrow–Hurwicz/Chambolle–Pock-type batch methods; it would be useful to also reference Nemirovski’s Mirror-Prox/Korpelevich’s extragradient, which are standard for saddle-point/VI and yield linear rates under strong monotonicity, to better position the contributions. To my knowledge, allowing B_i that are not gradients while retaining variance reduction and linear convergence is a significant conceptual step.

Strengths:
- Novelty and scope: A clean and general operator-centric view that imports SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and monotone operator problems, not restricted to separable finite sums or to B_i with proximal access. The “factored” split and non-uniform sampling analysis are well-motivated and practically relevant for bilinear problems.
- Theory: Clear linear convergence guarantees under strong convexity–concavity, with explicit constants in a weighted norm, mini-batching, and sampling. The Catalyst-style acceleration via proximal-point is elegant and simpler than Nesterov-style extrapolation in this setting. Extending the analysis to general monotone inclusions is valuable.
- Practicality: Attention to implementation (storage trade-offs for SAGA, mini-batches to amortize prox costs, choice of sampling distributions aligned with row/column norms), and experiments demonstrating gains, especially for ill-conditioned regimes.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Algorithms are spelled out; the role of L vs L̄(π), λ, γ, and sampling probabilities is explicit. Appendices reportedly contain proofs/derivations and proximal details.

Weaknesses / points for improvement:
- Positioning versus extragradient/Mirror-Prox: The paper focuses on FB and its acceleration; a discussion/empirical comparison to extragradient or Mirror-Prox (batch and stochastic) would strengthen context. Under strong monotonicity, Mirror-Prox can be linearly convergent; clarifying how the proposed VR methods compare in constants and oracle costs would help.
- Experimental scope: The study is limited to two datasets; adding larger-scale bilinear cases (e.g., CRF training, matrix games), more ablations (uniform vs non-uniform sampling, effect of m, memory–time trade-off for factored vs element-wise splits), and comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., PDHG with line-search, Mirror-Prox) would bolster significance.
- Parameter knowledge and adaptivity: Methods require λ, γ, and L/L̄; sensitivity and adaptive strategies (e.g., backtracking, online estimation) would increase practical robustness. The authors note future work on adaptivity; some empirical guidance here would still be useful.
- Memory for SAGA: For individual splits the O(nd) storage can be prohibitive. While factored splits reduce this, a clearer prescription of when each variant is preferable and whether compressed or lazy storage tricks apply would help practitioners.
- Minor clarity issues: In Section 2, “does need splits into convex-concave terms (as opposed to convex minimization)” appears to be a typo; I believe you mean “does not need.” Also, guidance for choosing π beyond the bilinear case (e.g., for general smooth K) would be appreciated.

Quality:
The analysis appears technically careful and leverages monotone operator theory appropriately. The unbiasedness of the VR estimators in the operator setting, linear rates under strong monotonicity, and the Catalyst-based acceleration are convincing. Some proofs are deferred; based on the presentation, I did not spot fundamental gaps, though I encourage adding a brief comparison in constants to batch extragradient.

Clarity:
Generally well written for a dense topic. The weighted norms and constants are defined clearly; algorithms are explicit. A brief diagram/table summarizing the three families (batch FB/accelerated FB; stochastic FB; VR SVRG/SAGA + accelerated) with assumptions and complexities (partly already in Table 1) would aid readability.

Originality:
High. The operator-level VR framework for saddle-point/monotone inclusions and the factored split with non-uniform sampling are, to my knowledge, novel at the time and open a promising direction.

Significance:
High. Many modern ML problems are naturally saddle-point or VI with non-separable structure. Providing linearly convergent, variance-reduced methods with practical sampling/storage strategies is impactful. The monotone operator extension broadens the audience beyond ML.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually bold and broad extension of variance reduction to saddle-point/monotone operators
- Solid linear-rate theory with practical sampling/mini-batching guidance
- Effective acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst
- Empirical gains on non-separable problems; clear path to practice (factored splits)

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical comparisons and datasets; missing baselines (Mirror-Prox/extragradient)
- Dependence on known constants; limited discussion of adaptivity/sensitivity
- SAGA memory may be prohibitive for some splits; more practical guidance warranted

Recommendation:
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and timely conceptual advance with solid theory and promising practice. The identified experimental and contextual gaps are addressable and do not detract from the novelty and potential impact of the contributions.
