

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, presenting convergence analysis and experimental validation. The authors claim to provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, with extensions to monotone operators and acceleration via the "catalyst" framework. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, as saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning, yet variance reduction techniques have not been systematically developed for this setting.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting extension of well-established convex optimization techniques to saddle-point problems, which is a meaningful contribution. The authors provide clear examples of machine learning problems where their methods are particularly applicable, such as non-separable losses and regularizers. The experimental validation on real-world datasets demonstrates practical advantages of their methods over existing approaches.

## Weaknesses
The paper contains several substantial technical issues that undermine its credibility:

1. **Incorrect step size in Forward-Backward algorithm (Section 2.2):** The paper claims σ = 1/L² for linear convergence, but standard convex optimization theory dictates that for strongly convex problems, the step size should be σ ≤ 1/L (not 1/L²). The convergence rate (1-1/(1+L²))^t also appears incorrect for L being the condition number.

2. **Undefined parameter μ in Theorem 2:** The convergence rate in Theorem 2 contains the term μ without any definition. This parameter appears to be critical to the analysis but is never defined in the paper, making the result impossible to verify.

3. **Suspicious complexity expression in Theorem 1:** The computational complexity is stated as proportional to [T(B) + (mL² + L̄²)max_i T(B_i) + (1 + L² + L̄²/m)Tprox(M)] log(1/ε). The term (1 + L² + L̄²/m) is unusual and likely incorrect; standard variance reduction complexity would have a term like (1 + L²/m) or similar.

4. **Incorrect SVRG step size (Section 3):** The paper uses σ = [L² + 3L̄²/m]⁻¹ for SVRG, but standard SVRG uses σ = 1/L. This appears to be a significant error in the algorithm description.

5. **Unjustified simplification in Section 6:** The authors claim "we assume for simplicity that λ = γ = μ" without justification. This is a major simplification that could significantly affect the generality of their results, yet they provide no discussion of how this impacts the practical applicability of their methods.

6. **Missing definition of λ0 in experiments (Section 7):** The paper refers to "λ/λ0 = 0.10" without defining λ0, making it impossible to reproduce the experimental setup.

7. **Formatting inconsistencies:** The paper consistently cites "Adv. NIPS" instead of the proper conference name "Adv. Neural Inf. Process. Syst." or "NeurIPS." The paper references "NIPS 2016" in the header, but the content suggests a more recent conference.

8. **Inconsistent notation:** The paper uses "K(x,y) = y>Kx" but later refers to "K(x,y) = y>Kx for a matrix K ∈ Rn×d," creating confusion about whether K is a matrix or a function.

These issues are not merely minor typographical errors but substantive technical flaws that affect the paper's core contributions. The incorrect step sizes, undefined parameters, and suspicious complexity expressions undermine the authors' claims about the algorithms' convergence rates and computational complexity. The paper's claim to provide "the first convergence analysis" for these algorithms is weakened by the presence of these errors.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important problem and contains potentially valuable ideas, the technical errors and omissions are too substantial to justify acceptance. A paper claiming to provide new theoretical results must be technically precise, and these errors make the paper's claims difficult to verify. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the paper to correct these issues before it could be considered for publication.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly improved with a careful reworking of the theoretical results, precise definitions of all parameters, and correction of the technical errors. As it stands, the paper's contributions are overshadowed by these substantial technical flaws.
