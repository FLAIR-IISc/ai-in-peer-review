This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating improvements over existing batch and stochastic algorithms. The core idea – adapting these methods to exploit separable structure in the saddle-point formulation – is a natural and potentially valuable contribution. However, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a sometimes overly assertive tone, diminishing the overall impact.

**Summary:** The authors correctly identify the relevance of saddle-point problems in machine learning (especially in duality) and the potential benefits of extending SVRG/SAGA to this setting. They provide convergence analysis under fairly general assumptions, explore different splitting strategies, and introduce an accelerated variant. Experimental results on binary classification tasks demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed algorithms, particularly the accelerated version.

**Strengths:**
* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems *is* novel, particularly the convergence analysis. The exploration of different splitting strategies (function-wise vs. derivative-wise) is insightful.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors attempt a formal convergence analysis, connecting the problem to monotone operator theory.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to machine learning problems (regularized linear learning, non-separable losses/regularizers) is well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the proposed algorithms. The comparison against existing methods is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Excessive Detail & Redundancy:** The paper is excessively verbose. Sections 2 and 2.1 repeat information unnecessarily. The lengthy discussion of norms for bilinear problems (Section 2.4) is overly detailed and distracts from the core contributions.
* **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation is inconsistent. For example, ‘I’ is used to represent both the index set for the sum in the saddle-point problem and a specific index. This creates confusion. The definition of L (condition number) lacks clarity, and the explanation of L̄(π) feels convoluted.
* **Overly Assertive Claims:** The authors frequently make strong claims without sufficient justification. Phrases like "always superior" (referring to the accelerated algorithm) are unsubstantiated and potentially misleading. The claims of a “formal advantage for variance reduction” are not compellingly demonstrated.
* **Minor Technical Errors/Imprecisions:** Several minor technical inaccuracies exist. The statement regarding the convergence of the forward-backward algorithm in general is inaccurate – it doesn’t *always* diverge. The explanation of the catalyst acceleration lacks sufficient detail to be easily reproducible.
* **Experimental Limitations:** The experiments are limited in scope. Only two datasets are used, and the parameter tuning details are not fully specified. The comparisons are not comprehensive. The presented results are focused on distance to optimizer, but testing accuracy is missing, making it difficult to assess the practical impact.
* **Lack of Clear Algorithmic Presentation:** Algorithm 2 (SAGA) utilizes vague terms like "optional resampling", which weakens the clarity of the procedure.
* **Missing citations:** The connection to existing work on stochastic saddle point methods (e.g., [11, 12]) are not fully articulated.

**Specific Concerns:**

* **Section 2.4 (Sampling Probabilities):** The discussion of sampling probabilities and their relation to matrix norms feels overly complex and doesn’t provide clear guidance for practical implementation.
* **Theorem 1 & 2:** The stated convergence rates are complex and difficult to interpret. A more intuitive explanation of the dependence on parameters would be beneficial.
* **Section 5 (Acceleration):** The explanation of the catalyst-based acceleration is too brief. The choice of τ is not adequately justified.
* **Section 6 (Extension to Monotone Operators):** While the connection to monotone operators is interesting, it feels tacked on and lacks a clear motivation within the context of the paper.
* **Algorithm 3:** A proper pseudocode showing how (x̄, ȳ) are updated inside the loop would be more clear.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The core idea of extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is novel and potentially impactful.
* The theoretical analysis, despite its complexity, provides some guarantees on the convergence of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental results, though limited, suggest that the algorithms can perform well in practice.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is overly verbose and contains numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.
* The overly assertive tone and lack of nuanced discussion detract from the credibility of the work.
* The experimental validation is insufficient to support the strong claims made by the authors.
* The technical details are often unclear or incomplete, hindering reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the idea, I recommend **rejection**. While the authors present a technically interesting extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, the numerous flaws, inconsistencies, and overly assertive claims overshadow the potential benefits. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication. A significant revision addressing the issues outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The current version, while not entirely without merit, does not meet the high standards of NIPS.
