

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper proposes stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, extending the convex optimization techniques to the saddle-point setting. The authors claim to provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context, with extensions to monotone operators and acceleration via the catalyst framework. The paper is well-motivated by machine learning examples where non-separable losses or regularizers require saddle-point formulations.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, which is non-trivial due to the different structure of these problems compared to convex minimization. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The paper provides valuable insights into the importance of non-uniform sampling and the different ways to split the problem structure.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

### Formatting and Technical Errors
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism and readability. Key examples include:
- The abstract incorrectly states "th Conference" instead of "The Conference"
- Equation (1) has a missing space before the "+" sign: "K(x, y) +M(x, y)"
- In assumption (A), the notation "λ2" and "γ2" should be "λ/2" and "γ/2"
- The norm definition "Ω(x, y)2 = λ‖x‖2 + γ‖y‖2" should be "Ω(x, y)^2 = λ‖x‖^2 + γ‖y‖^2"
- The supremum definition "L = supΩ(x−x′,y−y′)=1 Ω∗(B(x, y)−B(x′, y′))" is poorly formatted with missing spaces

### Technical Inconsistencies
Several technical inconsistencies raise concerns about the paper's rigor:
- The paper defines "M is strongly (λ, γ)-convex-concave" but the expression M(x, y)− λ2 ‖x‖ 2 + γ2 ‖y‖ 2 should use λ/2 and γ/2
- The bilinear saddle-point notation is inconsistent: "K(x, y) = y>Kx" uses K both as a matrix and a function
- Theorem 1 states "E [ Ω(xv − x∗, yv − y∗)2 ] 6 (3/4)vΩ(x0 − x∗, y0 − y∗)2" which implies the error decreases by 3/4 per epoch, but this is not standard notation for linear convergence
- Theorem 3's convergence rate "E [ Ω(xv − x∗, yv − y∗)2 ] 6 ( 1− 1τ+1 )v Ω(x0 − x∗, y0 − y∗)2" is identical to the non-accelerated SVRG rate, suggesting a possible error in the acceleration claim

### Missing Context and Citations
The paper makes strong claims about being "the first convergence analysis" but fails to adequately cite related work on stochastic methods for saddle-point problems. Key references to monotone operator theory (e.g., Rockafellar's foundational work) are missing, and the paper doesn't properly distinguish its contribution from existing work on stochastic methods for variational inequalities.

### Theoretical Gaps
The paper claims to extend to monotone operators but doesn't properly address the case where the problem isn't strongly convex-concave, which is common in practice. The acceleration argument in Section 5 seems to assume strong convexity-concavity without discussing how to handle the more general case.

### Experimental Details
The experimental section is brief and lacks sufficient detail about the datasets, implementation specifics, and comparison with state-of-the-art methods. The claim that "accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is the most efficient method" is not sufficiently supported by the limited experimental results presented.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a novel extension of stochastic optimization techniques, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, technical errors, and theoretical gaps significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before it can be considered for publication.

The paper's contribution is interesting but the execution is flawed, with multiple technical errors that would require correction before the paper could be considered sound. The theoretical claims need to be more carefully verified and presented with precise mathematical notation.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but critical technical and formatting errors that indicate a lack of attention to detail. These errors are not merely stylistic but impact the paper's technical credibility and would require significant revision before publication. While the core idea is valuable, the current version does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.

Pro: Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, well-motivated with machine learning examples, reasonable experimental validation.
Con: Numerous formatting and technical errors, incomplete theoretical justification, missing key citations, inconsistent notation.
