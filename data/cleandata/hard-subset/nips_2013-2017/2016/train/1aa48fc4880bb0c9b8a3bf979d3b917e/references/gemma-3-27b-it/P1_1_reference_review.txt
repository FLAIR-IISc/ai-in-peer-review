## Peer Review: Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

**Summary:**

This paper presents an extension of Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to tackle saddle-point problems arising in machine learning. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, highlighting differences from the convex minimization case. They demonstrate the importance of splitting the problem appropriately (functionally vs. partial derivative-wise) and the benefit of non-uniform sampling. Furthermore, they introduce an acceleration technique based on the “catalyst” framework, improving performance. The work connects saddle-point problems to the broader class of monotone operator problems, offering a more general theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution, filling a gap in the existing literature. Existing algorithms often struggle with non-separable losses or regularizers common in machine learning, and this work provides a practical solution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis presented is a core strength. Demonstrating linear convergence using monotonicity properties extends the applicability of these algorithms beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.
* **Practical Relevance:** The identified importance of non-uniform sampling and the catalyst-based acceleration are highly relevant for practical applications, as shown by the experimental results.  The analysis of splitting strategies (functionally vs. partial derivative-wise) is insightful and provides guidance for algorithm design.
* **Broad Applicability:**  Framing the problem within the context of monotone operators broadens the scope and impact of the work.  The authors clearly illustrate applications in supervised learning (with non-separable components), robust optimization, and unsupervised learning.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, although limited, provide convincing evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, especially the accelerated SVRG, compared to existing methods, including batch and primal approaches. The analysis of different sampling strategies is particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on strong convexity-concavity assumptions (A), and Lipschitz continuity (B,C). While common in optimization, relaxing these assumptions could broaden the applicability of the methods. The authors acknowledge this and mention plans for future work to address adaptivity to these constants.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The non-uniform sampling strategies, while theoretically beneficial, might be more complex to implement than uniform sampling, potentially hindering practical adoption.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus on a relatively limited set of problems (sido and rcv1).  Exploring a wider range of datasets and problem settings would strengthen the empirical validation. More detailed comparisons to alternative saddle-point solvers beyond batch methods would be beneficial.
* **Notation & Readability:**  While generally clear, the paper occasionally suffers from dense notation, making it difficult to follow the theoretical derivations without careful attention.  Some sections, particularly those related to the acceleration framework, could benefit from more intuitive explanations.




**Detailed Comments:**

* **Introduction:** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing methods.  The contributions are well-defined.
* **Section 2:** The composite decomposable saddle-point problem setup is well-explained.  The discussion of the different splitting strategies (functional vs. partial derivative-wise) is insightful. The examples provided are relevant and illustrate the applicability of the methods.
* **Section 3 & 4:** The algorithms SVRG and SAGA are presented in a clear and concise manner. The theoretical analysis, although dense, appears to be sound (given the reviewer's expertise).
* **Section 5:** The acceleration framework builds logically upon the SVRG/SAGA algorithms. The explanation of the catalyst-like approach is clear.
* **Section 6:** The extension to monotone operators is a valuable generalization and adds significant theoretical depth.
* **Section 7:** The experimental results provide compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The comparison with batch methods, primal methods, and uniform vs. non-uniform sampling is informative.
* **Overall:** The paper is well-structured and logically organized. The authors are careful to discuss both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The work addresses a significant problem in machine learning – solving saddle-point problems efficiently.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting.
* The proposed algorithms demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods in the experimental evaluation.
* The extension to monotone operators broadens the scope and applicability of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumptions required for the convergence analysis limit the applicability of the methods.
* The complexity of implementation of non-uniform sampling might hinder practical adoption.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field of optimization and machine learning.  The novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the work are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve readability (clarifying some notation) and potentially expanding the experimental evaluation.

**Confidence:** 8/10
