Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly convex-concave (with possibly different scalings for x and y). The key algorithmic contributions are variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG- and SAGA-type) tailored to saddle-point problems, with the following components:
- Extension of variance reduction (SVRG, SAGA) to the forward–backward splitting for saddle-point problems, using a decomposition B = ∑i Bi of the gradient operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK). The split may be by functions or by partial derivatives (“factored” splits), and need not correspond to gradients of individual Ki.
- Linear convergence analyses under strong convex-concavity, based solely on monotonicity (via viewing the problem as finding a zero of a strongly monotone plus Lipschitz operator), rather than on co-coercivity as in convex minimization. This extends to a broader class of monotone inclusion problems beyond saddle points.
- Importance sampling schemes for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, with non-uniform probabilities based on row/column norms or element-wise magnitudes, yielding improved smoothness constants L̄(π) and better iteration complexity than uniform sampling.
- An acceleration scheme via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper: solve a sequence of more strongly monotone subproblems approximately with SVRG, updating the center periodically, leading to improved dependence on L̄ and per-oracle costs.

The paper situates these contributions relative to variance-reduced methods for convex minimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) and primal–dual/coordinate methods designed for separable saddle-point problems (e.g., SPDC; Zhang & Xiao 2015; adaptive SPDC). Unlike those, the proposed methods explicitly handle non-separable losses/regularizers with proximal access to M and allow general operator splits of B. Batch baselines (FB and an accelerated FB akin to Chambolle–Pock/Nesterov-style extrapolation) are reviewed and compared. The analysis also connects to classical monotone operator methods (Rockafellar; Bauschke–Combettes).

Quality (technical soundness and empirical support)
- Theory: The algorithms are clearly stated, with step-size choices and mini-batching. Linear convergence for SVRG and SAGA is established under strong convex-concavity, with rates in terms of L (Lipschitz constant of B under a weighted norm) and L̄(π) (sampling-adapted smoothness). The monotone-operator proof strategy is appropriate for saddle-point problems and cleanly avoids co-coercivity assumptions that do not hold here. The acceleration via proximal point is well motivated; the complexity trade-off in τ is explicit and recovers the expected (1−1/(1+τ))^v contraction for the outer loop. The bilinear case is worked out with concrete norm inequalities and sampling choices.
- Correctness/readability of claims: Rates (e.g., FB rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t) and step-sizes are somewhat nonstandard in form, but consistent with the normalization that embeds strong convexity/concavity into the proximal operator; the paper indicates appendices with proofs. The distinction between L and L̄ and their roles in complexity is carefully tracked. The storage/runtime trade-offs for SAGA vs SVRG and factored vs individual splits are discussed.
- Experiments: The empirical section demonstrates the upside of variance reduction and importance sampling, and shows the benefits of acceleration on ill-conditioned problems. However, the experimental scope is limited (two datasets), and comparisons to closely related primal–dual VR methods (e.g., SPDC) are not provided due to differing applicability. Wall-clock time, sensitivity to λ,γ, mini-batch size, and cost of computing non-uniform sampling statistics are not extensively analyzed.

Clarity
Overall the paper is well organized, with assumptions (A–C), algorithm boxes, and a clear separation between batch/stochastic/accelerated variants. The bilinear case is used effectively to instantiate constants and probabilities. Some notation is dense (weighted norms, dual norms, L vs L̄, multiple index sets), and a concise summary of constants and their relationships in the main text (not just appendices) would aid readability. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
Extending SAGA/SVRG with linear rates to general (non-separable) convex–concave saddle-point problems and analyzing them via strong monotonicity without co-coercivity is, to my knowledge at the time, novel. The allowance for operator-level splits that need not correspond to gradients of component functions, and the systematic treatment of importance sampling in the saddle-point setting, are also new. The catalyst-like acceleration applied to this setting is natural; the simplified analysis via proximal point is a useful contribution.

Significance
The work addresses a common gap: many ML problems yield non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient proximal operators but lack a separable finite-sum structure on the primal or dual objectives. Providing variance-reduced, linearly convergent methods for such saddle-point formulations meaningfully advances the algorithmic toolkit. The monotone inclusion perspective broadens potential impact (e.g., variational inequalities). The practical importance sampling guidance for bilinear problems is useful.

Pros
- First linear-convergence VR algorithms for non-separable composite saddle-point problems.
- Clean monotone-operator analysis; extends beyond saddle points.
- Practical importance sampling strategies with improved bounds; clear treatment for bilinear K.
- Acceleration via a proximal-point wrapper with clear complexity trade-offs.
- Solid theoretical development complemented by empirical evidence.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is limited in breadth; no wall-clock plots or larger-scale benchmarks.
- Comparison to closely related primal–dual VR methods (e.g., SPDC) is mostly conceptual; a more explicit theoretical/empirical contrast would strengthen the case.
- Storage and preprocessing costs (e.g., SAGA with individual splits, computation of non-uniform probabilities) may be high; limited discussion on mitigating these in very large-scale settings.
- Assumes known strong convexity/concavity parameters; adaptivity is deferred to future work.
- Some constants/rates (e.g., FB with σ=1/L^2) are unconventional; brief intuition in the main text would help.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide explicit complexity and/or experiments comparing to SPDC or related primal–dual VR methods on problems where they apply (e.g., separable M)?
- How sensitive is performance to inaccuracies in λ and γ, and can your methods be made adaptive?
- What is the overhead to maintain non-uniform sampling probabilities in streaming/online settings?
- For non-bilinear K, do you have empirical evidence that the acceleration carries over?

Suggestions
- Add a small table summarizing L, L̄(π), their bounds under different sampling schemes, and per-iteration costs.
- Include wall-clock time plots and a larger dataset to demonstrate scalability.
- Clarify in the main text the intuition behind step-size/rate choices for FB and the normalized strong monotonicity.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of variance reduction to general saddle-point problems with meaningful theoretical and practical contributions, and it is relevant to the NeurIPS community. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying a few technical points would further improve the work.
