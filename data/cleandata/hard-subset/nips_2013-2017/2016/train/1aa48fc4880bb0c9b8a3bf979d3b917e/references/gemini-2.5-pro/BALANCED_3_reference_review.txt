### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of composite decomposable saddle-point problems of the form `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`. Here, `K` is a smooth convex-concave function that can be written as a sum of components, and `M` is a strongly convex-concave (but possibly non-smooth) function for which a proximal operator is available. This formulation is particularly relevant for machine learning problems with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso), where standard SVR methods cannot be directly applied to the primal or dual formulations.

The main contributions are:
1.  The first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in this general saddle-point setting. The analysis is framed more broadly through the lens of finding zeros of monotone operators, a perspective that is more general than the standard co-coercivity arguments used for convex minimization.
2.  An investigation of different splitting strategies for the saddle-point gradient operator (e.g., "factored" splits) and a demonstration, both theoretically and empirically, of the significant benefits of non-uniform sampling.
3.  The development of an accelerated SVRG variant for saddle-point problems, inspired by the "Catalyst" framework, which provably achieves a faster convergence rate.

The work builds upon the foundational SVR literature (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) and connects it to the theory of monotone operators and primal-dual optimization algorithms. It provides a new class of efficient, linearly convergent algorithms for problems that were previously only solvable with slower batch or non-variance-reduced stochastic methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the optimization literature for machine learning. While variance reduction for finite-sum convex minimization is a mature area, its extension to the more general and challenging saddle-point setting is a novel and significant contribution. This work makes SVR techniques applicable to a wider range of modern machine learning problems involving non-separable objectives, which is a major practical advance.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful. The key insight is to use only the monotonicity of the saddle-point operator, rather than the stronger co-coercivity property that holds for gradients in convex minimization. This allows for a unified analysis that covers saddle-point problems and other variational inequalities, showcasing a deeper understanding of why these algorithms work. The derivations of the linear convergence rates and the complexity analysis in Table 1 are clear and compelling.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins by clearly defining the problem, motivating it with relevant machine learning examples, and contextualizing it by reviewing existing batch and stochastic methods. The progression from the basic SVRG/SAGA extensions to the accelerated variant is logical. Table 1 provides an excellent and concise summary of the theoretical complexity improvements offered by the proposed methods.

4.  **Thoroughness and Practical Relevance:** The authors consider several important practical aspects, such as mini-batching, different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform), and different ways to decompose the operator ("factored" splits). The inclusion of an accelerated algorithm further strengthens the paper's practical impact. The experiments on problems with genuinely non-separable loss and regularization terms effectively demonstrate the real-world utility and superiority of the proposed methods over existing approaches.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Generality of Analysis vs. Rate for Convex Case:** Section 6 makes the excellent point that the analysis extends to the more general setting of monotone operators. However, it also notes that applying this general analysis back to the special case of convex minimization would yield slower rates than what is known from specialized analyses (which leverage co-coercivity). This is an important subtlety. It would be beneficial to add a sentence explicitly explaining *why* this is the case—namely, that the analysis, by being more general, does not exploit the stronger structural property of co-coercivity that allows for more aggressive step-sizes in the purely convex setting.

2.  **Acceleration for SAGA:** In Section 5, the Catalyst-style acceleration is developed and proven for SVRG. The paper then states, "the same scheme should work for SAGA," without further justification. While plausible, this is a speculative claim. I would suggest either providing a brief proof sketch or rephrasing this to be more cautious (e.g., "We conjecture that a similar acceleration scheme could be developed for SAGA, which we leave as an interesting direction for future work.").

3.  **Experimental Details:** The experiments are well-chosen and demonstrate the paper's claims. However, a plot of the primal-dual gap over time would be a very natural and informative addition for evaluating saddle-point algorithms, complementing the current plots of distance to the optimizer. While the appendix may contain this, its inclusion in the main text would strengthen the experimental section.

4.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The theoretical results and algorithms rely on knowledge of problem-dependent constants (e.g., strong convexity-concavity `λ`, `γ` and Lipschitz constants `L`, `L̄`). This is a common requirement in the literature but can be a practical hurdle. While the conclusion mentions future work on adaptive methods, a brief discussion in the main text about the sensitivity to these parameters or practical heuristics for setting them would make the work more accessible to practitioners.

### **Decision**

**Accept.**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the optimization toolkit for machine learning. It elegantly extends powerful variance reduction techniques to the important class of saddle-point problems, supported by a rigorous and general theoretical analysis and convincing empirical evidence. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and opens up several new avenues for research and applications. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's overall excellence. This work is a perfect fit for NeurIPS and is likely to be highly influential.
