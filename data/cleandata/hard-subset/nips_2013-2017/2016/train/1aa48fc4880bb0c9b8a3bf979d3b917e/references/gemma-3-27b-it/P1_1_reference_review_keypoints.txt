- Highlights importance of correct splitting (functional vs. partial-derivative) and benefits of non-uniform sampling.  
- Introduces catalyst-like acceleration improving practical performance.  
- Frames saddle-point problems as monotone operator problems, broadening theoretical impact.  

- Novel contribution filling a gap: SVRG/SAGA for non-separable saddle-point problems.  
- Strong theoretical rigor: linear convergence proofs using monotonicity, applicable to variational inequalities.  
- Practical relevance: non-uniform sampling and acceleration show empirical gains.  
- Broad applicability: examples include supervised, robust, and unsupervised learning.  
- Experiments support claims, especially for accelerated SVRG and sampling strategies.  

- Analysis depends on strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz assumptions, limiting applicability.  
- Non-uniform sampling may be complex to implement in practice.  
- Experimental scope limited (datasets: sido, rcv1) and lacks broader solver comparisons.  
- Dense notation and some hard-to-follow sections, especially on acceleration, hurt readability.  

- Introduction and problem setup are well-motivated and clearly presented.  
- SVRG/SAGA algorithm descriptions are clear; theoretical analysis is dense but sound.  
- Acceleration section builds logically on prior algorithms; could use more intuition.  
- Monotone operator extension adds significant theoretical depth.  
- Experimental section compares batch vs. primal methods and sampling strategies effectively.
