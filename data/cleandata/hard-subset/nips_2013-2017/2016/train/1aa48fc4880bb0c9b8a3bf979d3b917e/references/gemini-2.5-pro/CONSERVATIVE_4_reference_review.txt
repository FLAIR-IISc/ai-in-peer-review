This paper proposes the extension of stochastic variance reduction gradient (SVRG) and stochastic average gradient (SAGA) methods, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The key assumption is that the smooth part of the saddle-point objective has a gradient operator that can be decomposed into a sum of components. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these methods, establishing linear convergence rates for strongly-convex-concave problems. The analysis is cast within the more general framework of finding zeros of monotone operators, which allows the results to apply beyond saddle-point problems. The paper also demonstrates how to combine these methods with the "Catalyst" acceleration framework to achieve theoretically superior convergence rates. The proposed methods are evaluated empirically on binary classification problems with non-separable regularizers and losses.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound for the most part, and the theoretical contributions are clear. The generalization of the SVRG/SAGA analysis to the monotone operator setting is a solid piece of theoretical work, and the derivation of linear convergence rates is well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix.

However, there are weaknesses that detract from the overall quality and rigor of the work.

1.  A significant concern is the disconnect between the theory and the experimental implementation of the SAGA algorithm with non-uniform sampling. The paper notes in Section 4 that for the theoretical bound to hold with non-uniform sampling, a resampling step is required after the iterate update. This is known to add significant computational overhead. The authors then state that in the experiments, they follow prior work and use a heuristic mixture of sampling schemes *without* resampling. While this may be a pragmatic choice, it creates a gap between what is proven and what is evaluated. A paper that emphasizes its theoretical contributions should either provide a proof for the heuristic used or evaluate the algorithm that is actually analyzed. Without this, the empirical validation of the non-uniform SAGA method is not rigorously supported by the provided theory.

2.  The analysis framework, while general, comes with a noted trade-off. In Section 6, the authors mention that their analysis, when applied to the standard convex minimization case, would yield slower rates than existing, specialized analyses because it does not use co-coercivity. This raises the question of whether the derived rates for the saddle-point problem are tight, or if they could be improved with a more specialized analysis. While generality is a strength, the potential looseness of the bounds should be more clearly acknowledged and discussed.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear, and the motivation for studying saddle-point problems with this decomposable structure is well-argued. The algorithms are presented in a straightforward manner. The distinction between different types of "splits" (e.g., factored vs. individual) is a useful contribution to understanding the practical application of these methods. The summary of complexity results in Table 1 is particularly helpful for comparing the different algorithms.

**Originality**

The originality of this work lies primarily in the theoretical analysis rather than in the algorithms themselves. As the authors state, the algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA are "straightforward." Similarly, the acceleration scheme is a direct application of the existing Catalyst framework. The main novel contribution is the adaptation of the convergence analysis of variance-reduced methods to the saddle-point (or, more generally, monotone operator) setting, particularly in a way that does not rely on co-coercivity.

While this theoretical extension is valuable, the overall novelty feels incremental. The work combines established methods (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst) and applies them to a problem setting where they were not previously analyzed. This is a valid and useful contribution, but it may not represent a significant conceptual leap. The paper's framing of the "straightforward" algorithmic extension as a primary contribution seems to overstate the novelty.

**Significance**

The paper addresses an important and practical class of problems. As machine learning models incorporate more complex, non-separable regularizers (e.g., for structured sparsity) and losses (e.g., ranking-based measures), methods that can efficiently handle them are needed. Moving from the primal/dual convex minimization view to a saddle-point formulation is a powerful technique, and this paper provides a path to applying state-of-the-art stochastic optimization methods in this context.

However, the practical significance is somewhat undermined by the experimental results.
1.  The experiments are conducted on only two datasets, with the key non-separable case demonstrated on just one (`sido`). A more extensive empirical evaluation on a wider range of non-separable problems would be needed to make a convincing case for the method's broad utility.
2.  The baseline SAGA with uniform sampling performs poorly, failing to consistently outperform the accelerated batch method. This suggests that the practical success of the proposed methods is heavily dependent on the non-uniform sampling scheme (whose practical implementation lacks theoretical guarantees) and the Catalyst acceleration wrapper.
3.  The set of baseline algorithms could be stronger. While comparing against forward-backward methods is necessary, the literature also contains stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, 2014; Zhu & Storkey, 2015, which are cited) that can be applied to related problems. A comparison against such methods would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses the important problem of optimizing non-separable objectives via a saddle-point formulation.
*   Provides the first linear convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods (SVRG/SAGA) in the general saddle-point setting.
*   The analysis using monotone operator theory is elegant and provides a general framework.
*   The accelerated SVRG method shows strong empirical performance and has solid theoretical backing.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The algorithmic novelty is limited; the paper mainly applies existing algorithms (SVRG/SAGA/Catalyst) to a new domain.
*   There is a critical disconnect between the theory for non-uniform SAGA (which requires an expensive resampling step) and the heuristic implemented in the experiments.
*   The experimental validation is not extensive enough to fully demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods over a wider range of problems and strong baselines.
*   The performance of the basic methods with uniform sampling is not compelling, suggesting the practical benefits rely heavily on complex additions like non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

In conclusion, this paper presents a solid theoretical analysis for an important class of optimization problems. However, its claims of practical superiority rest on an experimental evaluation that is not fully rigorous (due to the theory-practice gap in SAGA) and is somewhat limited in scope. The work is a valuable theoretical contribution but would be a more compelling submission for NIPS with a more thorough and rigorous empirical validation.
