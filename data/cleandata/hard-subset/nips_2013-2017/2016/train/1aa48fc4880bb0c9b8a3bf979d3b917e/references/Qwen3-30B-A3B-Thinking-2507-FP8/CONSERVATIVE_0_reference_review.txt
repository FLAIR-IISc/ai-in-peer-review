

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper proposes extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems. The authors claim to provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, which they argue differ significantly from convex minimization settings. The paper follows a standard structure, presenting assumptions, algorithms, theoretical guarantees, and limited experiments.

The paper's main contribution is the extension of existing stochastic optimization methods to saddle-point problems, with the authors noting that the algorithmic extension is "straightforward." The theoretical analysis appears technically sound, leveraging monotone operator theory to establish linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity assumptions. The paper also claims to provide the first analysis of these methods for saddle-point problems, which differs from convex minimization.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and properly contextualizes the problem within monotone operator theory.
- The paper correctly identifies the need for different sampling strategies (non-uniform vs. uniform) for saddle-point problems.
- The connection between saddle-point problems and variational inequalities is well-explained.
- The experimental section, while limited, does show the proposed methods outperforming some baselines for specific non-separable problems.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its lack of sufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The authors claim their methods "improve significantly over existing methods" but provide only limited experiments on two datasets with specific non-separable regularizers and losses. Crucially, they don't demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform standard approaches when the problem is separable (where existing methods like SAGA would be applicable).

The restrictive assumption of strong convexity-concavity is problematic. The paper acknowledges this by noting "we currently need to know the strong convexity-concavity constants," which is a significant limitation in practice. Many real-world saddle-point problems don't satisfy these assumptions, and the paper doesn't address how to handle such cases. This is particularly concerning given the paper's claim that their methods are "always superior to accelerated batch algorithms" - a claim that likely only holds under the restrictive assumptions.

The experimental section is insufficiently detailed. The paper mentions "more details and experiments with primal-dual gaps and testing losses" in the appendix, but the main paper lacks these crucial results. The comparison with "fba-primal" (Nesterov acceleration in the primal) is misleading because this method is only applicable to separable problems, which the authors acknowledge are already solvable with existing methods. The claim that "primal methods... are inferior" is therefore not supported by the experiments, as they're comparing apples to oranges.

The paper also fails to adequately address the relationship between this work and existing approaches to saddle-point problems. For example, the authors cite works on separable saddle-point problems (e.g., [11, 12]) but don't clearly explain why their approach is necessary when the problem is separable, where established methods would be applicable.

**Originality:**
The core idea of extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is not particularly novel - the extension is described as "straightforward" by the authors themselves. The theoretical analysis is technically sound but doesn't represent a significant theoretical leap. The paper would be stronger if it could demonstrate that the saddle-point problem is fundamentally different from convex minimization in a way that makes these extensions non-trivial.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is limited by the restrictive assumptions and lack of empirical validation. The paper claims that their methods are "the only ones that can be applied from the class of large-scale linearly convergent algorithms" for non-separable losses or regularizers, but this is an overstatement. Existing methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., proximal gradient methods) already exist and can handle non-separable problems, though they may not be as efficient.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound extension of existing stochastic optimization methods to saddle-point problems, but the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of superiority over existing approaches. The restrictive assumptions (strong convexity-concavity) significantly limit the practical applicability of the proposed methods, and the experimental comparison is not comprehensive enough to validate the claims.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it:
1. Provided more comprehensive experiments comparing against all relevant baselines, including methods designed specifically for saddle-point problems.
2. Addressed the limitations of the strong convexity-concavity assumption and demonstrated how the approach could be adapted to more general cases.
3. Clearly articulated why saddle-point problems with the specific properties they consider are more prevalent or important than problems solvable by existing methods.

As it stands, the paper makes a modest contribution to a well-studied area with limited empirical validation and restrictive assumptions. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the lack of thorough experimental validation against established baselines makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed methods. The paper would be more appropriate for a more specialized optimization conference than NeurIPS, where the focus is on significant advances with clear practical impact.

**Recommendation:** Rejected. The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, and the assumptions limit its practical relevance. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches.
