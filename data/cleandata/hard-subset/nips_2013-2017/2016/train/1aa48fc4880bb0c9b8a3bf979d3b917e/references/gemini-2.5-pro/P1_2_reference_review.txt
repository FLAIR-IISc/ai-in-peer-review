### Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the broader class of composite saddle-point problems of the form `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`. The key motivation is that many machine learning problems, especially those with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation), naturally fit this saddle-point structure but cannot be directly addressed by existing variance-reduction techniques that require a separable objective.

The authors make several key contributions:
1.  They propose SVRG and SAGA variants for saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic structure is a natural extension of prior work [2, 3], this is the first paper to provide a rigorous convergence analysis for them in this setting.
2.  The analysis is based on the more general framework of monotone operators, which not only covers saddle-point problems but also extends to variational inequalities, highlighting the fundamental nature of the approach.
3.  They demonstrate that the saddle-point formulation allows for novel "factored" splits of the gradient operator and show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for achieving superior theoretical bounds and practical performance.
4.  They successfully integrate their SVRG variant with the "Catalyst" acceleration framework [7], yielding an accelerated algorithm that is provably faster than accelerated batch methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.
5.  Experimental results on binary classification tasks with non-separable objectives validate the theoretical findings, showing significant practical speedups over batch and standard stochastic methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the literature. While variance reduction methods have revolutionized large-scale convex optimization, their application was limited to separable objectives. This work significantly broadens their reach to a wide and practical class of problems involving non-separable losses and regularizers, which are common in areas like structured prediction, robust optimization, and ranking. This makes a powerful class of algorithms available to a new set of problems.

2.  **Strong and General Theoretical Contribution:** The technical quality of the paper is very high. The core theoretical contribution—analyzing the algorithms through the lens of monotone operators—is both elegant and powerful. This approach is more general than one based purely on convex-concave structure and unifies the analysis of these algorithms for both convex minimization and saddle-point problems. The authors are also careful to point out that their analysis does not use co-coercivity, which makes it applicable to the saddle-point setting where this property does not hold (unlike in standard convex minimization). This demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying optimization theory.

3.  **Novelty and Originality:** This is the first work to provide linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in the general saddle-point setting. The insights regarding different splitting strategies (e.g., "factored" splits) and the theoretical and empirical validation of non-uniform sampling's importance are novel and valuable contributions. Furthermore, the successful application of Catalyst acceleration is a non-trivial extension that results in a state-of-the-art algorithm.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a clear motivation, systematically introduces the problem setting, reviews existing methods, and then presents its own contributions in a logical progression from basic SVRG/SAGA to the accelerated version. Table 1 provides an excellent and concise summary of the complexity improvements. The experimental section is well-designed, using real-world tasks that genuinely require the proposed methods (i.e., non-separable objectives), which strongly supports the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The proposed algorithms, especially the final accelerated version, depend on multiple parameters: the strong convexity/concavity constants (λ, γ), Lipschitz constants (L, L̄), and the Catalyst parameter (τ). The paper does not provide guidance on how to set these in practice when they are not known, which is often the case. While the authors mention future work on adaptivity, a short discussion on practical estimation or heuristics would have strengthened the paper's immediate applicability for practitioners.

2.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The core algorithmic templates for SVRG and SAGA are direct analogues of their counterparts in convex minimization. The novelty lies primarily in the analysis and application context, not in a fundamentally new algorithmic design. This is a minor weakness, however, as the main contribution is demonstrating that these templates are effective in this new domain and rigorously proving why.

3.  **Dependence on Strong Convexity-Concavity:** The analysis relies entirely on the strong convexity-concavity assumption (Assumption A) to achieve linear convergence. While the authors acknowledge this and suggest future work on the non-strongly convex case, this assumption can be restrictive for some problems. It would be beneficial to briefly comment on what kind of convergence rates (e.g., sublinear) might be expected without this assumption.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Pro:** Solves a significant and practical problem by extending powerful optimization techniques (SVRG/SAGA) to non-separable objectives common in machine learning.
*   **Pro:** Provides the first rigorous linear convergence analysis for these methods on saddle-point problems, based on a general and elegant monotone operator framework.
*   **Pro:** Introduces and analyzes the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, leading to a new state-of-the-art algorithm for this problem class.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically sound, very clearly written, and supported by convincing experiments that directly test the claims on appropriate problem instances.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**
*   **Con:** The algorithmic structures are straightforward extensions of existing methods.
*   **Con:** The practical implementation requires knowledge of several problem-dependent constants, and no guidance is provided for tuning them.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It bridges the gap between the highly successful variance-reduction techniques and the important class of saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is novel, general, and rigorous, and the empirical results clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods. The weaknesses identified are minor compared to the paper's substantial strengths. This work is highly likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
