Summary
The paper studies stochastic variance reduction for convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), with M strongly convex-concave and prox-friendly, and K smooth. It adapts SVRG and SAGA to this setting, proves linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity, and provides explicit iteration and runtime complexities. A key technical angle is viewing the problem as finding zeros of a sum of a strongly monotone operator and Lipschitz (not necessarily co-coercive) operators; this allows the analysis to extend beyond saddle points to certain monotone inclusions/variational inequalities. The paper further develops importance sampling schemes and “factored” splits tailored to the saddle structure (notably for bilinear K), provides complexity comparisons to batch and stochastic baselines, and applies a proximal-point (“catalyst”-style) acceleration that provably improves complexities. Experiments on two datasets (non-separable and separable cases) show accelerated SVRG/SAGA outperforming batch methods and stochastic FB, and highlight the benefit of non-uniform sampling.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic extensions of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points are natural, but the analysis is nontrivial because co-coercivity is unavailable. The authors build on the monotone operator view, derive step sizes and linear rates for strongly convex-concave problems, and give careful complexity bounds in terms of L and the sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π). The acceleration via a proximal-point outer loop is well-justified and simpler than in minimization-only settings. The treatment of non-uniform sampling and factored/individual splits is thoughtful and leads to sharper bounds for bilinear problems.
- Assumptions: Prox access to M and strong convexity-concavity are standard in this literature and clearly stated. The Lipschitz constants for components Bi and the aggregate L may be hard to know in practice; the paper discusses substituting L̄ for L where needed, with the expected trade-offs.
- Proof coverage: Core theorems are in the appendix with reasonable sketches in the main text. I did notice a notational gap: in Theorem 2, the symbol µ appears but is not clearly defined earlier in the main text (likely the strong monotonicity parameter induced by λ,γ via the Ω-norm). Clarifying this would aid readability.
- Experiments: The empirical section validates the theory qualitatively (non-uniform sampling helps; acceleration matters on ill-conditioned problems). However, the experimental coverage is somewhat limited: two datasets, no comparison to extragradient/Mirror-Prox variants, and limited exploration of sensitivity to step sizes or sampling distributions. The non-separable example and proximal operators are relevant and credible.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with clear statement of assumptions, algorithms, and main results. The Ω/Ω* norming makes constants interpretable. The discussion of different splits and their storage/runtime consequences is helpful.
- Areas to improve:
  - Define µ explicitly where it first appears (or stick consistently to λ,γ with Ω).
  - Summarize the key proof ideas for the monotone-operator extension in the main text (currently mostly pointer to Appendix D/E).
  - Provide more guidance on estimating L̄(π) in practice and the impact of mis-specification on stability.
  - The accelerated batch baseline considered is FB with extrapolation; adding Mirror-Prox/PDHG with standard accelerations would better situate the contributions.

Originality
- Novelty is strong for the time: linear-convergence SVRG/SAGA-style methods for general (non-separable) convex-concave saddle-point problems, with an analysis that does not rely on co-coercivity and that extends to monotone inclusions. Prior stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., SPDC, APCG-like variants) require separability; stochastic FB yields O(1/t) rates. The factored splits and non-uniform sampling tailored to bilinear K, and the proximal-point acceleration adapted to this setting, are also new elements. Related work is cited appropriately.

Significance
- The contributions are significant. Many ML applications naturally appear as saddle-point problems, often with non-separable losses/regularizers but prox-friendly structure. Bringing variance reduction with linear rates to this class is impactful. The monotone operator perspective broadens applicability beyond ML. The complexity refinements with importance sampling and factored storage are practical and likely to influence subsequent work. The acceleration scheme gives a clean path to improved complexities in both stochastic and batch settings.

Pros
- First linear-rate variance-reduced methods for general convex-concave saddle problems; clean monotone-operator analysis.
- Practical and theoretically-justified non-uniform sampling and factored splits; storage/runtime trade-offs clearly analyzed.
- Proximal-point-based acceleration with clear complexity improvements and simple implementation on top of SVRG.
- Solid theoretical development with explicit complexities; empirical results align with theory.

Cons
- Experimental section is modest: limited datasets, baselines, and ablations (e.g., no Mirror-Prox, no evaluation on non-bilinear K, limited sensitivity analysis).
- Step-size constants depend on L and L̄(π) which may be hard to estimate; guidance for practical tuning is limited.
- SAGA storage can be prohibitive for individual splits; while factored storage mitigates this for bilinear K, general K may lack this structure.
- Minor clarity issues (undefined µ in main text, dense notation).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the definition of µ in Theorem 2, and connect it explicitly to λ,γ and the Ω-norm.
- Could you include Mirror-Prox/PDHG baselines, and perhaps an extragradient variant with variance reduction if feasible?
- Provide practical heuristics for step-size selection when L and L̄ are unknown; how robust are the methods to overestimation?
- Any empirical results on monotone inclusions beyond saddle points, to demonstrate the generality?
- Discuss when factored splits exist beyond the bilinear case; any guidance for constructing good splits in general?

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes a technically solid and original contribution with clear significance for optimization in ML. With minor clarifications and stronger experimental baselines, it would be even more compelling.
