This paper tackles the challenging problem of accelerating optimization for saddle-point problems, extending variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The core idea—adapting these algorithms to leverage separability in the saddle-point formulation—is highly novel and represents a significant contribution. While the analysis is complex, the authors successfully provide convergence guarantees and demonstrate a connection to monotone operator theory, broadening the applicability of their results. The exploration of different splitting strategies (function-wise vs. derivative-wise) and the emphasis on non-uniform sampling are insightful and contribute to a deeper understanding of the problem. Furthermore, the acceleration framework built upon the “catalyst” approach is a strong addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a genuinely new contribution. The analysis revealing the differences from convex minimization is particularly valuable.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis, especially the connection to monotone operators, is impressive and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Saddle-point problems arise frequently in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction). Efficient solvers are therefore highly desirable.
*   **Insights into Splitting Strategies:** The identification of different splitting options and the impact of non-uniform sampling are crucial for practical implementation.
*   **Acceleration Framework:** The adaptation of the catalyst framework is a significant enhancement, demonstrating potential for substantial performance gains.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The extension to monotone operators elevates the work beyond just saddle-point problems, broadening its impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Analysis:** The analysis, while rigorous, is dense and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions (A)-(C), while standard for this type of analysis, may not hold in all practical scenarios. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions could be further explored.
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms, they are limited to a few datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problem instances would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Implementation Details:** More details on the implementation of the algorithms, particularly concerning the selection of sampling probabilities and step sizes, would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters (e.g. λ, γ) requires careful tuning, and the paper could benefit from a discussion of strategies for selecting appropriate values.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution to the field of optimization.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees for the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental results, while limited in scope, are encouraging and support the theoretical findings.
*   The work has the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications.
*   The connection to monotone operator theory broadens the applicability and theoretical interest.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the analysis may limit its accessibility.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The assumptions made in the analysis may not always be satisfied in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving saddle-point problems. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work far outweigh them. The potential impact of this research is significant, and it represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. I am willing to overlook the limitations in experimental scope and analysis complexity, given the novelty and potential importance of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept**. This paper meets the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. It presents a novel, theoretically sound, and potentially significant contribution to the field. Minor revisions addressing the comments on experimental scope and analysis clarity would further strengthen the paper.
