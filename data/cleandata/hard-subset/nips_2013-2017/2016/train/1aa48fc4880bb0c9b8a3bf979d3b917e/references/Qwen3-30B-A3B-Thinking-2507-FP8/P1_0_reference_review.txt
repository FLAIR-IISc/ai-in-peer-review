

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are prevalent in machine learning but have not received the same algorithmic attention as convex minimization problems. The authors consider problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth and convex-concave, and M is potentially non-smooth but "simple" (with easy proximal operator). They provide a convergence analysis for these algorithms, showing linear convergence rates under appropriate assumptions. A key insight is that saddle-point problems allow for two different notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) and that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. The authors also extend their approach using the "catalyst" framework to obtain accelerated algorithms, and they generalize their analysis to monotone operators, which broadens the applicability beyond saddle-point problems.

This work builds on the SVRG and SAGA methods for convex optimization (Johnson & Zhang, 2013; Defazio et al., 2014) but extends them to a more general class of problems. The paper also relates to the literature on saddle-point problems (e.g., Arrow-Hurwicz methods) and monotone operator theory (Rockafellar, 1976).

## Quality Assessment

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous convergence analysis for saddle-point problems. The authors carefully establish the necessary assumptions (A)-(C) and provide a complete theoretical framework. The convergence proofs for SVRG and SAGA are sound and extend the analysis to monotone operators, which is a significant contribution. The complexity analysis is thorough, with careful consideration of different sampling strategies and their impact on performance.

The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate comparisons to existing methods (batch forward-backward, stochastic variants, and primal methods). The experiments on non-separable losses and regularizers (where existing methods fail) effectively demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the need for knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants, and discuss future work on adaptivity.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and the contributions. The technical sections are logically presented, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The notation is consistent and well-chosen, though the dual norm definitions might be slightly confusing for readers unfamiliar with saddle-point problems. The authors provide clear algorithm descriptions and theorems with concise proofs in the appendices.

The paper could be slightly improved by adding a brief discussion of how the algorithm parameters (like the step size σ) should be set in practice, beyond the theoretical bounds. A small example illustrating the difference between the factored and individual splits would also help clarify this important distinction.

## Originality Assessment

This paper makes several significant original contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA for saddle-point problems, which is non-trivial due to the different convergence behavior compared to convex minimization. Second, the authors identify that saddle-point problems allow for two different types of splits (function-based and derivative-based) and show that non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency. Third, the extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities and other monotone operator problems.

While there is related work on stochastic methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., [22]), this paper provides a more comprehensive analysis and practical algorithms that outperform existing methods. The connection to the "catalyst" framework for acceleration is also novel in this context.

## Significance Assessment

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, as saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (through duality), but most stochastic optimization methods have been developed for convex minimization. The proposed algorithms are the first to provide linear convergence rates for stochastic variance reduction in saddle-point problems, with significant practical advantages demonstrated in experiments.

The significance is further enhanced by the authors' ability to handle non-separable losses and regularizers, which are common in applications like robust optimization and structured prediction. The experiments show clear advantages over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The extension to monotone operators also opens up new possibilities for applying these techniques to a broader range of problems.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- First rigorous convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems
- Clear demonstration of practical advantages through experiments
- Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability
- Careful theoretical analysis with appropriate complexity bounds
- Addressing an important gap in the literature

**Weaknesses:**
- The need for knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- Some technical details in the convergence proofs might be challenging for non-specialists
- The paper could provide more intuition about why non-uniform sampling is so effective

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical results are novel and the experimental validation is thorough. The paper demonstrates clear significance by providing practical algorithms for a class of problems where previous methods were limited.

I recommend acceptance. This work would be valuable to the NIPS community and would likely influence future research on optimization methods for saddle-point problems and monotone operators.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief example illustrating the difference between factored and individual splits to clarify this important technical distinction.
2. In Section 7, provide a more detailed discussion of how the parameters (like σ) should be set in practice beyond the theoretical bounds.
3. Consider adding a sentence explaining why non-uniform sampling is so effective for saddle-point problems compared to convex minimization.

The paper is well-written and makes a substantial contribution to the field, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
