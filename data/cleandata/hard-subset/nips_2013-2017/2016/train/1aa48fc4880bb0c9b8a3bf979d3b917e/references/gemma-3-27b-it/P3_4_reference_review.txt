## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of optimization, specifically extending stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques to saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the extensions of existing algorithms (SVRG, SAGA) are natural and well-motivated, and the experimental validation demonstrates practical benefits. While the notation is dense and the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations in places, its overall quality is high and warrants acceptance to NIPS.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of solving saddle-point problems, which arise frequently in machine learning (e.g., duality in supervised learning, robust optimization).  It observes that many algorithms designed for separable convex optimization don't directly translate to saddle-point formulations. The core idea is to adapt SVRG and SAGA – successful techniques for finite-sum minimization – to the saddle-point setting.  A key contribution is providing a convergence analysis specifically tailored to saddle-point problems, interpreting them through the lens of monotone operators, which allows for broader applicability. The authors also highlight the importance of specific splitting strategies (function-wise vs. partial derivative-wise) and non-uniform sampling for efficient algorithms.

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on both convex optimization (SVRG, SAGA, accelerated methods) and saddle-point problems (forward-backward algorithms, primal-dual methods). It distinguishes its work by addressing a previously unanalyzed regime of *stochastic* saddle-point problems with separable structure, a scenario common in large-scale machine learning. The connection to monotone operator theory is a clever move, expanding the scope of the results beyond purely saddle-point settings.  The discussion of related work is fairly comprehensive, though mentioning recent work on stochastic primal-dual methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., some of the more recent developments building on Chambolle-Pock) might be useful.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with rigorous convergence analysis is a genuine contribution. The generalization to monotone operators is a significant bonus.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis, particularly the connection to monotone operator theory, appears sound and provides a strong foundation for the proposed algorithms.  The detailed analysis of convergence rates under different sampling strategies is also commendable.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed (saddle-point problems in ML) is highly relevant, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms, particularly with accelerated versions and non-uniform sampling. The examples provided in section 2.1 are compelling and clearly motivate the work.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply adapting algorithms. It investigates different splitting strategies for the gradient and explores the crucial role of sampling probabilities.
* **Well-Organized:** The paper follows a logical structure, building from problem formulation to algorithm design, analysis, and experiments.
* **Detailed Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section provides a thorough comparison against various baseline algorithms and highlights the performance gains achieved by the proposed methods.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Notation & Readability:** The paper is quite dense in notation. While mathematically precise, it can be challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the literature.  More intuitive explanations alongside the equations would be helpful.  The different norms used (Ω, Ω*, etc.) are not always clearly motivated.
* **Lack of Intuition for Splitting Strategies:** While the paper correctly identifies the importance of different splitting strategies, the intuition behind *why* certain splits lead to better performance could be more clearly explained.  A more visual explanation of the "factored" split for the bilinear case might be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the experiments are strong, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations, such as tuning parameters (e.g., step sizes) and dealing with numerical instability.
* **Catalyst Acceleration:** While the acceleration technique described in Section 5 is promising, its presentation feels a bit rushed. A more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the catalyst approach and its adaptation to the saddle-point setting would be helpful.



**4. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical contribution:  Extends a powerful class of optimization algorithms to a challenging and important problem setting.
* Rigorous analysis and strong theoretical guarantees.
* Compelling experimental validation demonstrating practical benefits.
* Relevance to a wide range of machine learning applications.
* Well-written and organized (despite the density of notation).

**Against Acceptance:**

* High notational density and challenging readability for some readers.
*  Limited intuition provided for some of the key design choices.



**5. Final Recommendation:**

**Accept.**  Despite the weaknesses regarding readability and intuitive explanation, the paper's strengths – particularly its theoretical novelty, rigor, and experimental validation – significantly outweigh its drawbacks. This is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. I recommend the authors carefully consider suggestions for improving readability and clarifying the intuition behind their choices in a final revision.
