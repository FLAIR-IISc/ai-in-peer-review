This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, for solving large-scale saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure. The core idea is to extend these powerful optimization techniques from the standard convex minimization setting to the more general convex-concave saddle-point setting. The authors make several key contributions: 1) they provide the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in this context, highlighting that the analysis is distinct from the convex case due to the lack of co-coercivity; 2) they explore different ways to decompose the problem structure (e.g., "factored" splits) and demonstrate the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal rates; 3) they show that these methods can be accelerated using a Catalyst-like framework, leading to an algorithm that is theoretically and empirically superior to previous methods; and 4) they generalize their analysis to the broader setting of finding zeros of monotone operators, unifying saddle-point problems and convex minimization under a single theoretical framework.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are significant and appear to be sound. The authors correctly identify that a naive extension of existing proofs for SVRG/SAGA is insufficient, as the saddle-point operator is merely monotone and not co-coercive. The analysis under the more general monotone operator framework in Section 6 is a strong indicator of the paper's technical depth and correctness. The theoretical complexity results, summarized effectively in Table 1, clearly position the proposed methods against existing batch and stochastic algorithms, demonstrating a clear advantage for the proposed variance-reduced approaches, particularly with acceleration and non-uniform sampling. The experimental evaluation is also strong. The authors test their methods on relevant machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers (AUC maximization and a clustering-inducing regularizer), which is the primary motivation for this work. The experiments convincingly validate the theoretical claims, showing that accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling significantly outperforms other methods, especially on ill-conditioned problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and explicitly lists the paper's contributions. Section 2 provides the necessary background, sets up the problem formulation, and reviews related work in a structured manner. The algorithms are presented clearly and concisely. A major strength in terms of clarity is Table 1, which provides a superb summary of the theoretical complexities of all considered algorithms, allowing the reader to quickly grasp the key theoretical takeaways. The flow from the basic algorithms (SVRG, SAGA) to acceleration and finally to the general monotone operator framework is logical and compelling. The writing is precise and easy to follow for a reader familiar with optimization theory.

**Originality:** The paper's originality is high. While the base algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst) are not new, their application, and more importantly, their rigorous analysis for the general class of finite-sum saddle-point problems, is a novel and substantial contribution. The authors appear to be the first to establish linear convergence for these methods in this setting. The insights provided are also original to this problem class, such as the discussion of "factored" splits for bilinear problems and the theoretical and empirical demonstration that non-uniform sampling is not just a minor improvement but is key to outperforming accelerated batch methods. The generalization to monotone operators without the assumption of co-coercivity (unlike prior work such as Davis, 2016) is a significant theoretical novelty that broadens the applicability of the results beyond the immediate scope of saddle-point optimization.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. Saddle-point problems are increasingly prevalent and important in modern machine learning, with applications in robust optimization, adversarial training (e.g., GANs), and duality-based formulations. However, the development of fast, scalable stochastic algorithms for this setting has lagged behind the progress seen in standard convex minimization. This paper directly addresses this critical gap by providing the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms that can effectively leverage finite-sum structure. The demonstrated practical performance improvements are substantial. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale optimization problems that can be formulated as saddle-point problems are very likely to use and build upon these ideas. The theoretical framework based on monotone operators also provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   **First analysis:** Provides the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Strong practical impact:** Addresses a highly relevant problem in machine learning and provides algorithms with state-of-the-art empirical performance.
*   **High quality:** The paper is technically sound, well-written, and thoroughly evaluated.
*   **Novel insights:** The importance of non-uniform sampling and the generalization to monotone operators are valuable and novel contributions.
*   **Excellent presentation:** The paper is very clear and well-organized, making a complex topic accessible.

**Con:**
*   The reliance on known strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) is a practical limitation, though a common one in the analysis of such algorithms. The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work. (This is a very minor point and does not detract significantly from the paper's contribution).

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It is theoretically sound, practically relevant, and clearly presented. I strongly recommend acceptance.
