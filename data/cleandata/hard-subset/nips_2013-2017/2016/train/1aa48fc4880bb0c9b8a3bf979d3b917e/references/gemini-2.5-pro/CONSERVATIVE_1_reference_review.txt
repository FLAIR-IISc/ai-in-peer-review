**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods for composite saddle-point problems. The authors extend the well-known SVRG and SAGA algorithms, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the min-max setting. The core contributions include providing the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point context, which is framed more generally through the lens of monotone operators. The paper also investigates practical considerations such as different ways to decompose the problem structure ("splits") and the importance of non-uniform sampling for achieving better theoretical rates and practical performance. Finally, an accelerated version based on the Catalyst framework is presented, which is shown to achieve a faster convergence rate. Experimental results on binary classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers demonstrate the proposed methods' empirical advantage over standard batch and stochastic baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper builds upon a solid foundation by extending two of the most successful and well-understood variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to a new domain. This is a logical and methodologically sound step. The adaptation of these algorithms to the saddle-point structure is clear and well-motivated.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The primary contribution is the convergence analysis. The authors provide proofs of linear convergence for their SVRG and SAGA variants under strong convex-concavity assumptions. Grounding the analysis in the more general framework of monotone operators is a definite strength, as it clarifies that the results are not merely an artifact of the saddle-point formulation but rely on more fundamental properties. This provides a valuable theoretical perspective.

3.  **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature. While variance reduction for finite-sum minimization is a mature area, its application to saddle-point problems, particularly those arising from non-separable losses or regularizers in machine learning, is less explored. The examples provided (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization) are relevant and highlight cases where traditional primal or dual formulations are not amenable to existing stochastic methods.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The proposed algorithms are direct adaptations of SVRG and SAGA. The authors themselves state that "the algorithmic extension is straightforward." While the theoretical analysis is new for this setting, the core algorithmic ideas are not. The contribution lies more in the porting and analysis of existing techniques rather than the development of a fundamentally new approach.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation and Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is the most significant weakness of this work. While the results show an improvement over basic batch and stochastic forward-backward methods, this is largely expected, as variance reduction is known to outperform these baselines in the standard minimization setting. The validation is not comprehensive enough to establish the proposed methods as state-of-the-art for this problem class.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The paper fails to compare against relevant, modern stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems. For instance, stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, 2014 [12]; Zhu & Storkey, 2015 [11]), even if designed for separable cases, would serve as a crucial benchmark on the `rcv1` dataset. The absence of any comparison to other dedicated stochastic primal-dual solvers weakens the empirical claims.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are conducted on only two datasets. The benefits of the proposed methods should be demonstrated on a wider variety of problems that fit the saddle-point formulation, such as the robust optimization or matrix factorization examples mentioned in the introduction. As it stands, the evidence feels anecdotal.
    *   **Uniform vs. Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper correctly highlights the theoretical advantage of non-uniform sampling. However, the plots show that uniform sampling (`saga (unif)`) performs poorly, sometimes worse than the accelerated batch method. This suggests a high sensitivity to the sampling scheme, which could be a practical drawback. This trade-off deserves more discussion.

3.  **Strong Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the assumption of strong convexity-concavity. While this is a common starting point for proving linear convergence, it restricts the applicability of the theoretical results. Many practical saddle-point problems (e.g., arising from constrained optimization or GANs) do not satisfy this condition. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a key limitation of the current paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper provides the first rigorous linear convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA-type methods for an important class of saddle-point problems.
*   The theoretical framing using monotone operators is elegant and provides a general tool for analysis that could be built upon by future work.
*   The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a clear need for efficient solvers for non-separable optimization problems in machine learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The algorithmic contribution is incremental, adapting existing methods to a new setting without introducing significant new ideas.
*   The experimental evaluation is not thorough. The lack of comparison against stronger, more relevant stochastic saddle-point baselines makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed methods.
*   The reliance on strong convexity-concavity limits the immediate impact of the work, as many interesting problems do not meet this requirement.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a solid, technically sound contribution by extending established variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems and providing a rigorous convergence analysis. The theoretical work is well-executed. However, the paper's overall impact is diminished by its limited algorithmic novelty and, more critically, by an experimental evaluation that is not comprehensive enough. The failure to compare against modern stochastic primal-dual methods is a significant omission that leaves the practical advantages of the proposed approach unproven against relevant competitors. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the paper in its current form does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of superiority in practice. It is a competent piece of work but falls short of the high bar for originality and significance expected at NIPS.
