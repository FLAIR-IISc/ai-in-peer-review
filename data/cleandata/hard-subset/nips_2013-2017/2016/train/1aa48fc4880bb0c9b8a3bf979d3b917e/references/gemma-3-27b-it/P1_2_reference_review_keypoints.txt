- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and provides the first convergence analysis for this setting.  
- Interprets saddle-point problems as zeros of a monotone operator, yielding results that generalize to variational inequalities.  
- Emphasizes the importance of problem splitting (function vs derivative-based) and non-uniform sampling for efficiency.  
- Proposes a catalyst-based acceleration technique that improves over accelerated batch methods.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical characterization of convergence rates and dependence on condition number and sampling schemes.  
- Empirical results on real datasets show practical improvements, especially with non-uniform sampling and acceleration.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, with consistent notation and a detailed appendix for proofs and experimental setup.  
- Algorithm performance is sensitive to regularization parameters (λ and γ); more guidance on selection is needed.  
- Theoretical analysis is complex and may be inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with monotone operator theory.  
- Experimental evaluation is somewhat limited; broader, larger-scale tests and varied K/M characteristics are desirable.  
- Lacks adaptivity to strong convexity-concavity constants and adaptive step-size schemes in the current work.  
- Memory requirements—particularly for SAGA with factored splits—are not discussed in sufficient detail.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, practical improvements, and high relevance to the NIPS community.  
- Arguments against acceptance: technical complexity and parameter sensitivity could limit accessibility and adoption.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; ask for parameter guidance, simpler exposition, expanded experiments, and a brief memory discussion.
