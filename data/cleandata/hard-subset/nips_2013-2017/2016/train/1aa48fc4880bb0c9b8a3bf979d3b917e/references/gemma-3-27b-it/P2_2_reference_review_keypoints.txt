- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems common in ML.  
- Provides convergence analysis showing linear convergence under suitable assumptions.  
- Emphasizes differences from convex minimization: importance of splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling.  
- Proposes a catalyst-inspired acceleration technique with improved performance over accelerated batch methods.  
- Generalizes results to monotone operator problems, broadening applicability.  

- Novel contribution: first substantial extension of SVR methods to saddle-point settings.  
- Theoretical rigor: thorough analysis with convergence rates and connection to monotone operator theory.  
- Practical relevance: targets common ML problems (regularized linear models, robust optimization).  
- Offers useful insights into saddle-point optimization challenges and algorithm design choices.  
- Acceleration framework is practical and analytically justified.  

- Experiments are limited in scope and could include more datasets and problem structures.  
- Strong assumptions (strongly convex-concave, Lipschitz) may be restrictive; relaxations are not explored.  
- Implementation can be complex, especially non-uniform sampling and acceleration tuning.  
- No adaptive step-size strategy is provided, which might improve practical performance.  

- Quality: technically sound and complete work (Strong).  
- Clarity: generally well-written but some sections need clearer exposition.  
- Originality: novel extension and useful new insights (Strong).  
- Significance: results are important and likely impactful (Strong).  

- Pros for acceptance: addresses a relevant challenge, provides novel theory, shows practical improvements, and generalizes broadly.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited experiments, strong assumptions, and implementation complexity.  

- Overall: strong paper with rigorous analysis and promising methods, but needs broader experiments and discussion of assumption relaxations.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions (expand experiments and discuss possible assumption relaxations).
