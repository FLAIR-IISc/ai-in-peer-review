

# Review: Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

This paper proposes extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems. The authors claim these methods are the first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for saddle-point problems, providing theoretical convergence guarantees and empirical validation on machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

## Quality

The theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide rigorous convergence proofs for their algorithms under appropriate assumptions, and the extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems follows a logical pattern from convex optimization. However, the paper has significant quality concerns regarding its literature review and experimental validation.

The claim that this is "the first convergence analysis for these algorithms for saddle-point problems" is questionable. The literature on stochastic methods for saddle-point problems is extensive, including works by He and Yuan (2012) on proximal ADMM, Chen et al. (2016) on stochastic primal-dual methods, and Chambolle and Pock (2011) on first-order primal-dual algorithms. The paper fails to properly address these existing approaches, making the novelty claim unsubstantiated.

The experimental validation is limited to two datasets (sido and rcv1) with a specific non-separable loss and regularizer. The paper claims significant improvements over existing methods, but the experiments don't sufficiently demonstrate the generality of the approach. The comparison with batch methods is restricted to a single non-uniform sampling strategy without exploring the full range of possible configurations. The paper also fails to compare with other stochastic saddle-point methods that might already exist.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent mathematical notation and clear pseudocode for the algorithms. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing stochastic methods for saddle-point problems, which would better contextualize the contribution.

## Originality

The core idea of extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is not particularly novel. While the authors provide a convergence analysis, the extension itself follows a natural pattern from convex optimization. The paper's claim of being "the first" is overstated, as the literature review is inadequate and fails to properly distinguish their work from existing methods.

The paper's claim that "the saddle-point formulation allows two different notions of splits" is not convincingly demonstrated to be a significant departure from existing approaches. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this distinction leads to meaningful algorithmic improvements beyond what's already known.

## Significance

The significance of this work is overstated. While the authors demonstrate improvements on specific problems, the paper doesn't convincingly argue that these methods represent a major advance in the field. The proposed algorithms are straightforward extensions of established methods, and the improvements shown in the experiments are incremental rather than transformative.

The paper's claim that their method "is always superior to accelerated batch algorithms" is not sufficiently supported. The experiments show improvements in some cases, but the paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of when the proposed method would be preferable to existing approaches.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to the literature on saddle-point problems. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the paper overstates the novelty and significance of the work. The experimental validation is limited and doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the generality or superiority of the proposed methods.

The paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough literature review that properly situates the work within existing methods for saddle-point problems, more comprehensive experimental validation across a wider range of problems, a more modest claim about the novelty and significance, and a clearer discussion of the limitations and when the proposed method would be preferable.

## Recommendation: Reject

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper provides a clear convergence analysis for extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems
- The theoretical framework is well-constructed and mathematically sound
- The paper demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods in some cases

**Con Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The main contribution is a straightforward extension rather than a novel approach
- The literature review is inadequate and novelty claim is overstated
- The experimental validation is limited and doesn't demonstrate generality
- The significance of the contribution is overstated compared to existing work

The paper's contribution, while technically sound, does not represent a significant enough advance in the field to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The work is more appropriate for a venue with a lower bar for novelty, such as a workshop or a more applied conference, rather than NeurIPS. The authors should substantially revise their paper to properly contextualize their contribution within the existing literature before resubmission to a top-tier venue.
