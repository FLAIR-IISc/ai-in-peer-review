- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and provides the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in that setting.  
- Proves linear convergence rates under appropriate assumptions for stochastic variance reduction on saddle-point problems.  
- Shows acceleration via the catalyst framework, yielding improved performance over existing batch and stochastic methods.  
- Identifies that the analysis for saddle-point problems differs substantially from convex minimization and frames saddle-points as zeros of monotone operators.  
- Demonstrates both theoretically and empirically that non-uniform sampling is crucial for algorithmic efficiency.  
- Extends the framework beyond saddle-point problems to general monotone operators.  
- Technical analysis is rigorous with detailed proofs for the main theorems provided in the appendix.  
- Convergence analysis properly accounts for unique saddle-point properties, distinguishing convex-concave from convex minimization.  
- Provides tight bounds for bilinear problems and careful analysis of sampling schemes and factored vs. individual splits.  
- Experiments compare appropriately against batch forward-backward, accelerated batch, and primal methods, showing significant empirical improvements.  
- Experimental design is solid and demonstrates strong empirical gains for ill-conditioned problems with non-uniform sampling.  
- Acknowledges limitations such as requiring knowledge of strong convexity/concavity constants (λ, γ).  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and uses consistent notation with clear problem formulation and assumptions.  
- Algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1–3), facilitating implementation and reproducibility.  
- Connects clearly to related work with appropriate citations and distinguishes itself from prior variance-reduction papers.  
- Original contribution is significant: novel extension of variance reduction to a fundamentally different problem class.  
- Practical significance is high due to applicability to ML problems with non-separable losses/regularizers and variational inequality problems.  
- Strengths: first rigorous convergence analysis for saddle-point variance reduction, importance of non-uniform sampling, clean monotone-operator extension, strong theoretical and empirical support, and excellent exposition.  
- Weaknesses: reliance on knowing strong convexity/concavity constants, Section 6 on monotone operators is brief, and experiments could cover more diverse problem types beyond binary classification.  
- Recommendation: accept — the paper is high quality, original, significant, and well-supported.  
- Specific suggestions: discuss adapting algorithms when λ or γ are unknown, expand Section 6 on monotone operators, include more diverse experiments, relate analysis to co-coercivity in convex minimization, and compare to Davis (2016) on monotone operators.
