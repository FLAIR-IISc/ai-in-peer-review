This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to the challenging setting of saddle-point problems. The authors correctly identify the increasing relevance of these problems in machine learning, particularly where separability assumptions common in standard stochastic gradient descent don't hold. The theoretical contributions – convergence analysis tailored to saddle-point structure and extension to monotone operator theory – are substantial. The demonstration that non-uniform sampling is crucial, both theoretically and empirically, is a valuable insight. The accelerated variant leveraging the “catalyst” framework is a nice addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is genuinely novel. Existing work largely focuses on batch methods or separable scenarios. Bridging this gap is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Depth:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous and thoughtfully adapts concepts from monotone operator theory, providing a broader scope than just saddle-point problems. The connection to variational inequalities is interesting.
* **Practical Relevance:** The focus on saddle-point problems arising from duality in machine learning (regularized learning, robust optimization) positions the work in a highly relevant context.
* **Non-uniform Sampling Insight:** Identifying and demonstrating the importance of non-uniform sampling is a key finding.
* **Acceleration:** The incorporation of the catalyst framework provides a further performance boost.
* **Thorough Experiments:** The experiments, while focused on a specific setup, demonstrate the benefits of the proposed algorithms, especially in ill-conditioned problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumptions on the functions K and M, while standard, might be restrictive in some applications. Exploring the robustness of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Practicality of Non-Uniform Sampling:** While theoretically justified, implementing effective non-uniform sampling schemes can be challenging, particularly in high dimensions. The paper provides some guidance (e.g., for bilinear problems), but a more detailed discussion of practical considerations would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope. While the provided results are compelling, expanding to additional datasets and problem types would strengthen the empirical validation. More comparison with appropriate baselines, like ADMM or other specialized saddle-point solvers, would also be valuable.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks specific details regarding implementation. Providing code or more detailed pseudocode would significantly enhance reproducibility and adoption.
* **Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The sensitivity of the algorithms to hyperparameters (step sizes, regularization parameters) isn’t fully explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The convergence analysis is a major contribution, extending existing theory to a more general setting.
* The empirical results, while limited, support the theoretical findings.
* The insight regarding non-uniform sampling is valuable and potentially impactful.
* The potential for acceleration using the catalyst framework is a bonus.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions on the problem structure might be limiting.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* Practical implementation challenges related to non-uniform sampling aren't fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the approach, the depth of the theoretical analysis, and the practical relevance of the problem make it a valuable contribution to the field. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper represents a substantial advance and warrants acceptance to NIPS, despite the incomplete validation. The bold approach and potentially high impact of this work outweigh the methodological limitations. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more detailed guidance on implementing non-uniform sampling.



**Score:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
