### Summary
This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance reduction gradient (SVRG) and stochastic average gradient (SAGA) methods for composite saddle-point problems. The core idea is to extend these well-established variance reduction techniques, originally designed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the min-max setting. The authors present the algorithmic extensions, which are relatively straightforward, and provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point context. The analysis is framed more broadly through the lens of monotone operators, showing the applicability of the methods beyond convex-concave problems. Furthermore, the paper incorporates the "Catalyst" acceleration framework to develop an accelerated version of SVRG for this problem class. The empirical evaluation on two datasets demonstrates that the proposed methods, particularly the accelerated variant with non-uniform sampling, can outperform existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel Theoretical Analysis:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel convergence analysis. It provides the first linear convergence guarantees for SVRG and SAGA in the context of strongly-convex-concave saddle-point problems. Extending the analysis to the more general framework of monotone operators is a significant theoretical result that broadens the applicability of these variance reduction ideas and clarifies their underlying mechanics, showing that co-coercivity (required for standard SVRG/SAGA analysis) is not necessary.

2.  **Systematic Approach:** The paper systematically develops the stochastic methods from their batch counterparts (Forward-Backward), analyzes their complexities, and incorporates an acceleration scheme. The treatment of different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform) and problem structures ("factored splits") is thorough and adds value.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem setup, assumptions, and algorithms are clearly defined. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the connections to prior work in both convex minimization and saddle-point optimization are well-articulated.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** As the authors acknowledge, the algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA are "straightforward." The core novelty lies in the analysis. While a new analysis is valuable, the lack of significant algorithmic innovation tempers the overall contribution. The proposed methods are essentially direct applications of existing algorithmic templates to a different, albeit important, problem class.

2.  **Motivation and Positioning:** The paper motivates the need for these new methods by focusing on problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. It claims that for such problems, existing fast gradient techniques cannot be applied to the primal or dual forms. This is only partially true; while stochastic methods like the original SAGA/SVRG require separability, accelerated *batch* proximal gradient methods can handle non-separable objectives and are also linearly convergent. The paper's own baselines (fb-acc) and tables confirm this. The key question is therefore not whether these problems *can* be solved, but whether the proposed saddle-point stochastic approach offers a compelling advantage over applying a robust, accelerated batch method to the primal or dual. The paper's case rests on this empirical advantage.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental section, while providing some support for the claims, is not extensive enough to be fully convincing.
    *   **Limited Datasets:** The evaluation is performed on only two datasets. Given the wide applicability claimed, a more comprehensive study across a broader range of problems (e.g., with different dimensions, sparsity patterns, and condition numbers) would be necessary to firmly establish the superiority of the proposed methods.
    *   **Baseline Comparison:** While the comparison to accelerated forward-backward is appropriate, the performance difference is not always dramatic. For instance, in the `sido` experiment with λ/λ₀=1.0, the non-accelerated SAGA and SVRG variants offer only a modest improvement over the accelerated batch method (`fb-acc`). The clear winner is the accelerated SVRG (`svrg-acc`), but this relies on combining the proposed method with another existing framework (Catalyst). It is unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the variance reduction versus the acceleration scheme, which could also be applied to the batch method.
    *   **Practical Metrics:** The primary evaluation metric is the distance to the optimizer, which is a standard theoretical measure. However, for a machine learning audience, it is crucial to also see performance in terms of test error or other relevant application-specific metrics in the main paper to assess the practical impact of faster convergence. These are relegated to the appendix.

4.  **Assumptions and Practicality:** The analysis and algorithms rely on knowledge of several problem parameters, including strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). While common in theoretical analyses, estimating these constants in practice can be challenging, potentially hindering the straightforward application of these methods. The paper does not discuss this practical limitation.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   The paper provides the first rigorous linear convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems, a theoretically significant contribution.
*   The generalization of the analysis to monotone operators connects variance reduction techniques to a broader class of problems in optimization.
*   The proposed accelerated method shows promising empirical performance, advancing the state-of-the-art for this problem class.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   The algorithmic contribution is incremental, consisting of a direct application of existing methods to a new domain.
*   The empirical validation is limited to two datasets and does not conclusively demonstrate a universal advantage over well-established accelerated batch methods, which are often simpler to implement and tune.
*   The motivation for requiring a specialized stochastic saddle-point solver over applying standard batch solvers to the primal/dual objective could be strengthened with more compelling evidence.

### Conclusion
This is a technically sound paper with a solid theoretical contribution. The analysis extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems and the more general class of monotone operators is valuable. However, the work is more of a theoretical extension than a practical breakthrough. The algorithmic novelty is low, and the experimental evidence is not comprehensive enough to make a strong case for the practical necessity of these methods over existing, robust accelerated batch solvers. While the results are promising, the paper would be significantly stronger with a more extensive empirical evaluation that demonstrates a clear and consistent advantage across a wider variety of problems. Based on its current form, it sits on the borderline, but its theoretical rigor makes it a worthy contribution.
