Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly convex-concave. The key ideas are:
- Treat saddle-point optimization as finding zeros of a monotone operator A+B (A from M via the resolvent/prox, B from ∇K), and design forward–backward schemes that use variance-reduced stochastic estimates of B.
- Allow flexible decompositions B=∑_i B_i without requiring each B_i to be a gradient of a scalar function, enabling “factored” splits aligned with the saddle structure (e.g., row/column sampling for bilinear K), and develop non-uniform sampling schemes that minimize a tailored smoothness constant L̄(π).
- Prove linear convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA under strong convex–concavity, with complexities that improve over batch forward–backward, especially with non-uniform sampling and minibatching; show that SAGA can be implemented with O(n+d) memory in factored bilinear cases.
- Accelerate SVRG using a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper, leading to improved complexity L̄√(T(B) max_i T(B_i)) up to logs; analysis leverages linear convergence of proximal-point rather than Nesterov machinery.
- Provide an extension of the analysis beyond saddle points to general (maximal) strongly monotone operators A and monotone B=∑B_i (each B_i Lipschitz, not necessarily monotone or co-coercive).
- Experiments on non-separable losses/regularizers and a separable baseline show that (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling outperform batch methods and uniform SAGA; primal SAGA is faster only when separability allows it.

Relation to prior work: Variance-reduction methods (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) were established for finite-sum convex minimization; stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods exist for separable saddle-point problems. Stochastic forward–backward for monotone inclusions gives O(1/t) rates. This paper provides, to my knowledge, the first linear rates for variance-reduced stochastic methods on non-separable saddle-point problems, and a clean operator-theoretic analysis that avoids co-coercivity assumptions.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithms are natural extensions of SVRG/SAGA to operator-splitting with a proximal backward step and a variance-reduced forward step. The use of strong monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity to obtain linear convergence is appropriate for saddle-point settings; the step-size choices and epoch lengths are explicitly tied to L and L̄(π). The acceleration via proximal-point is well-justified and avoids the pitfalls of applying Nesterov acceleration to saddle-point problems. The extension to monotone operators is carefully scoped (A strongly monotone and resolvent-known; B monotone as a sum of Lipschitz components). Proofs are in the appendices; the arguments sketched in the main text are coherent.
- Empirical support: Experiments are limited (two datasets, one with non-separable loss/reg and one separable) but consistent with theory: non-uniform sampling helps; uniform SAGA can underperform batch; acceleration helps ill-conditioned problems. Results are reported in passes; some wall-clock comparisons would strengthen claims about practical efficiency, but the proximal costs are discussed.
- Caveats: The strong convex–concave requirement and knowledge of λ,γ (and L, L̄) are needed; constants may be conservative. Non-uniform sampling requires computing/maintaining row/column norms for bilinear K; the cost is not discussed in detail. For non-bilinear K, the best sampling and bounds are less explicit. The SAGA non-uniform variant requires resampling for the theory; experiments deviate (mixture sampling without resampling), which is reasonable in practice but should be discussed as such.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The normalization via Ω(·) with λ,γ is thoughtful and clarifies condition numbers. The distinction between individual versus factored splits is clear and useful. Algorithm boxes are precise.
- Some notation (e.g., rates involving L vs L^2) could be streamlined; a concise table of assumptions per theorem would help. A short practical guide: how to choose between SVRG/SAGA, minibatch sizes, and factored vs individual splits, would benefit practitioners.

Originality
- Conceptual novelty: Extending variance reduction to composite saddle-point problems with linear rates under strong convex–concavity, without requiring co-coercivity or separability, is new. The “factored” decomposition and the tailored non-uniform sampling analysis for bilinear K are insightful.
- Relation to concurrent work: Prior stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods target separable structures; stochastic FB methods give O(1/t). The operator-splitting perspective broadens applicability (variational inequalities, general monotone inclusions) beyond convex minimization and beyond earlier “SMART”-type approaches relying on co-coercivity.

Significance
- Theoretical impact: Provides a solid, general framework to bring variance reduction into saddle-point and monotone operator settings, with clean linear rates and acceleration. Likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., beyond strong monotonicity, adaptive parameter tuning, extragradient variants).
- Practical impact: For non-separable losses/regularizers, these are among the few linearly convergent large-scale methods; the sampling guidance is actionable for bilinear problems. The empirical evidence is promising but would benefit from broader benchmarks and timing.

Pros
- First linear-convergent VR methods for non-separable saddle-point problems with clear operator-theoretic analysis.
- Practical non-uniform sampling and factored splits that reduce variance and storage.
- Proximal-point-based acceleration that is conceptually simple and effective for ill-conditioned problems.
- Extension to general monotone inclusions broadens relevance.

Cons
- Assumes strong convex–concavity and known constants; step sizes may be conservative.
- Experimental section is relatively thin (few datasets, limited wall-clock analysis, missing comparisons to mirror-prox/PDHG baselines).
- Non-uniform SAGA requires resampling for theory; practical variant deviates.
- Overhead of computing sampling probabilities and full B(x̃,ỹ) each epoch not fully quantified.

Suggestions
- Add comparisons to stochastic mirror-prox and PDHG (with/without acceleration) on the same tasks.
- Report wall-clock time and sensitivity to minibatch size m; include more non-bilinear K examples.
- Discuss costs of computing/maintaining non-uniform sampling probabilities and how to handle unknown L, λ, γ (e.g., backtracking or adaptive strategies).
- Clarify the scope of the acceleration beyond bilinear K and provide guidance for choosing τ in general.

Verdict: Strong paper with solid theory and promising practical implications. I lean toward acceptance, contingent mainly on strengthening the empirical section and clarifying practical parameter choices. Confidence: high.
