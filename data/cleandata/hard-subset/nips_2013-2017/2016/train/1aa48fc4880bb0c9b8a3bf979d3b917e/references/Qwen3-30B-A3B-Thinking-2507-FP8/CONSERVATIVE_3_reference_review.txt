

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper proposes extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, claiming to provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this setting. The authors argue that while SVRG and SAGA have been successfully applied to convex minimization problems, their extension to saddle-point problems is non-trivial and requires a different theoretical framework, particularly using monotone operator theory.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) a convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems, (2) a demonstration that non-uniform sampling is key to efficiency, and (3) an acceleration technique using the "catalyst" framework. The authors support their claims with theoretical complexity bounds and experiments on two datasets with non-separable losses and regularizers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to algorithms to experiments.
- The theoretical analysis extends the monotone operator framework to saddle-point problems, which is a technically sound approach.
- The experimental results show some improvement over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.
- The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature for efficient stochastic methods for non-separable saddle-point problems.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its overstatement of novelty and insufficient validation. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is not as novel as claimed. The authors cite existing work on saddle-point problems (e.g., [4, 8, 19]), yet they present their extension as a major theoretical advance without sufficient justification. The key insight that saddle-point problems can be framed as finding zeros of monotone operators is well-established in the literature (see, e.g., Rockafellar's work [8] and the textbook by Bauschke and Combettes [8]).

The theoretical contribution is particularly weak. The convergence analysis appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing convex optimization proofs to the saddle-point setting, with the main difference being the use of monotonicity properties instead of strong convexity. The paper claims "this differs significantly from the associated convex minimization set-up," but the proof techniques are nearly identical to those used in convex minimization, suggesting the extension is less novel than claimed.

The experimental validation is limited and does not sufficiently support the paper's claims. The authors only test on two datasets with a single non-separable loss function, and the comparison is against a small set of baseline methods. The "significant improvement" shown in Figure 7 is marginal for the main dataset (sido) and disappears when comparing to primal methods on the separable dataset (rcv1). The paper does not provide statistical significance tests for these results.

Most critically, the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed methods are substantially better than existing approaches for practical machine learning problems. The theoretical complexity bounds claim improvements, but the experiments do not convincingly show that these translate to practical advantages. For example, the paper states that "accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is the most efficient method," but Figure 7 shows only modest improvements over existing methods, with the most significant gains occurring on an ill-conditioned problem (λ/λ₀ = 0.10), which is not the standard setting.

The paper also makes an unsubstantiated claim that "our new fast algorithms are the only ones that can be applied from the class of large-scale linearly convergent algorithms" for non-separable losses. This is not true - there are other methods for saddle-point problems, such as the primal-dual hybrid gradient (PDHG) method [20], which has been shown to be effective for non-separable problems. The paper fails to compare against these established methods.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing methods to saddle-point problems, the novelty is overstated, the theoretical contribution is marginal, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of significant improvement over existing methods. The paper makes a straightforward extension without sufficient evidence that it provides meaningful advantages for practical machine learning applications.

The authors would benefit from:
1. More rigorous comparison against existing saddle-point methods (not just batch algorithms)
2. More extensive experiments on a wider range of datasets and problem types
3. A clearer articulation of the limitations of their approach
4. A more modest claim about novelty (e.g., "we provide a convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in this setting, which has not been previously documented")

The paper's main contribution is an incremental extension of existing methods to a new problem domain, but it does not provide compelling evidence that this extension is substantially better than existing approaches for practical machine learning problems. Given the high bar for NIPS acceptance, the paper falls short of demonstrating a meaningful advance in the state of the art.
