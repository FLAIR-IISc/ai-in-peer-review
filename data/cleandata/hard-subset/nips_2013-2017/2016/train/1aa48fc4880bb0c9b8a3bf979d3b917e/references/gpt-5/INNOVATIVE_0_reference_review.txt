Summary
The paper tackles composite convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M admits an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The key idea is to extend variance-reduced finite-sum methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems and, more broadly, to monotone operator inclusions. The authors:

- Develop SVRG- and SAGA-style algorithms adapted to saddle-point structure, including “factored” splits that operate at the level of partial derivatives/operators Bi, without requiring each Bi to be a gradient of a scalar Ki.
- Provide the first linear convergence analyses for these variance-reduced methods in the saddle-point setting under strong convex-concavity, using a monotone operator viewpoint that does not rely on co-coercivity (unlike standard convex minimization analyses).
- Analyze non-uniform sampling schemes and show they are crucial both theoretically and practically; derive problem-dependent smoothness constants L̄(π) and associated complexity bounds.
- Propose a simple acceleration via a proximal-point (“catalyst-like”) outer loop, yielding improved oracle complexity and outperforming accelerated batch methods in their bounds.
- Extend the analysis to general strongly monotone operator sums A + ∑ Bi, with access to the resolvent of A, hence covering saddle-point problems and variational inequalities.
- Empirically validate on binary classification tasks with non-separable loss/regularizer, showing gains of SVRG/SAGA (especially accelerated SVRG) over batch and stochastic baselines.

Relation to prior work is appropriately discussed: the paper situates itself relative to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for minimization, primal-dual methods for separable saddle points, stochastic FB/Mirror-Prox-type methods with O(1/t) rates, and recent non-uniform sampling in VR methods.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: Extending variance reduction to non-separable saddle-point problems and to monotone operators (without co-coercivity) is a significant conceptual step that opens a new direction beyond convex minimization.
- Operator-level splits: Allowing Bi to be generic Lipschitz operators (not necessarily gradients of Ki) and introducing “factored” splits is an elegant, saddle-point-aware design that also reduces memory for SAGA in bilinear cases.
- Solid theory: Clear linear rates under strong convex-concavity; clean dependence on L and L̄(π); principled non-uniform sampling; acceleration via a proximal-point outer loop with transparent complexity trade-offs.
- Practical insights: Discussion of mini-batching to amortize proximal costs, memory/runtime trade-offs between SVRG and SAGA, and concrete sampling choices for bilinear K (rows/columns vs. entries).
- Experiments: Showcasing non-separable losses/regularizers where standard VR methods on primal/dual do not apply; accelerated SVRG’s advantage grows with ill-conditioning, matching the theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumptions and constants: The algorithms require knowledge of strong convexity-concavity parameters (λ, γ) and Lipschitz bounds (L, L̄). While common in theory, these can be hard to estimate. An adaptive step-size/linesearch or automatic estimation would strengthen practicality; the authors mention adaptivity as future work.
- Experimental scope: The empirical section is relatively limited (two datasets), and focuses mostly on bilinear K. Additional experiments on non-bilinear smooth K and on other monotone-operator/VI instances would better demonstrate generality. A comparison to stochastic extragradient/Mirror-Prox variants for strongly monotone operators would be informative.
- SAGA resampling: The non-uniform SAGA analysis requires a resampling step that is not used in experiments (they follow a practical heuristic). A short ablation quantifying the gap between the theoretically sound variant and the heuristic would aid clarity.
- Memory for SAGA: For individual splits SAGA needs O(nd) storage; practically this limits use to factored splits or SVRG. A discussion of memory-light surrogates (e.g., sketching, low-rank caches, block storage) would be useful.
- Acceleration: The acceleration proof is provided for SVRG; a statement or sketch for SAGA (even deferred) would complete the picture. Also, clarifying when the accelerated method strictly improves over accelerated batch in practice (beyond bounds) would help practitioners.
- Clarity: The paper is dense. A brief intuition for L̄(π) and its tightness, and a small table mapping assumptions/parameters to step sizes per algorithm would improve readability. Minor notation consistency (L vs L̄, norms) could be tightened.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful complexity analyses and clear assumptions. Proofs leverage monotone operator theory appropriately. Some practical deviations (SAGA resampling) and modest empirical breadth are minor weaknesses.

Clarity
Well organized but compact. The appendices appear to contain necessary details. A few additional explanations, as noted above, could make it more accessible.

Originality
High. The operator-splitting perspective enabling VR for saddle points and general monotone inclusions, plus the factored splits and non-uniform sampling analysis, represent meaningful advances beyond prior VR work.

Significance
High. The work broadens the applicability of variance-reduction to an important class of problems (non-separable saddle points, VIs), with both theoretical and practical relevance. It is likely to influence subsequent research and practice.

Pros
- First linear-rate VR methods for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems.
- Extension to monotone operators without co-coercivity.
- Non-uniform sampling and factored splits with concrete complexity benefits.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point outer loop.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theoretical predictions.

Cons
- Requires knowledge of problem constants; no adaptive scheme provided.
- Limited experimental scope and missing comparisons to some stochastic extragradient baselines.
- SAGA’s non-uniform variant deviates from the analyzed version in practice.
- Storage burden for SAGA under individual splits.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The conceptual novelty and breadth outweigh the relatively minor empirical limitations. The paper opens a clear and impactful new direction.

Confidence
High.
