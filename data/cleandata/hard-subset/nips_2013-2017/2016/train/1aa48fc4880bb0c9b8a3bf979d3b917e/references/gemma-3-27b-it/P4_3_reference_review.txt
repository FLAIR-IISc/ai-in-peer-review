This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for solving saddle-point problems, extending existing work primarily focused on finite-sum convex optimization. The core idea is to adapt these algorithms to the saddle-point setting, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating that the techniques extend to monotone operator problems. A significant contribution lies in identifying two distinct splitting strategies for the saddle-point problem and showing the importance of non-uniform sampling. The paper also presents an accelerated variant using a catalyst framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a novel contribution, bridging the gap between optimization techniques developed for convex minimization and a broader class of problems arising in machine learning (e.g., regularized learning, robust optimization). The extension to monotone operators further expands the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides convergence analysis for the proposed algorithms, differing significantly from the convex minimization setup. The use of monotone operator theory lends a broader theoretical foundation. The analysis seems rigorous and includes clear convergence rates.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The identified split structures (function-based vs. derivative-based) and the importance of non-uniform sampling are potentially crucial for practical implementation and performance.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates the performance improvements of the proposed methods compared to batch algorithms and uniform sampling variants.  The inclusion of comparisons with primal methods on separable problems provides valuable context.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. Equations and algorithm descriptions are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The convergence analysis relies on strong convexity-concavity assumptions (Assumption A), which may not hold in all practical scenarios. While acknowledged, the sensitivity of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions could be further explored.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions the importance of choosing appropriate step sizes and sampling probabilities, but provides limited guidance on how to tune these parameters in practice. The theoretical justification for the suggested parameters (e.g., in the accelerated version) is presented, but practical considerations might warrant further discussion.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The discussion of the complexity bounds, while thorough, is somewhat dense. A more intuitive explanation of the trade-offs between different algorithmic choices (e.g., batch size, splitting strategy) would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate clear advantages, they are limited to specific datasets and problem settings. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problems would strengthen the claims of practical relevance. The comparison to more recent or state-of-the-art algorithms in saddle-point optimization would also be valuable.
*   **Repetitive Discussion of Existing Methods**: The paper spends a significant amount of space reiterating existing work, particularly in Section 2.2 and 2.3.  While providing context, this section feels somewhat verbose and could be streamlined.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving saddle-point problems, a crucial component in many machine learning applications.
*   The convergence analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
*   The extension to monotone operator problems broadens the applicability of the work.
*   The identification of key algorithmic choices (splitting strategy, sampling) is practically useful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong convexity-concavity assumptions may limit the applicability of the algorithms.
*   The lack of clear guidance on parameter tuning could hinder practical adoption.
*   The complexity analysis, while thorough, could be more accessible.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the theoretical analysis, and the positive experimental results all support acceptance. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
