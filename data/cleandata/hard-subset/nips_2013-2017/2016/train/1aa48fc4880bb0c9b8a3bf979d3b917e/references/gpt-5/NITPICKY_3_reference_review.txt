Summary
The paper studies stochastic variance-reduced methods for composite convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), with M strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave and prox-friendly, and K smooth. It extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point settings (and further to strongly monotone inclusions), allows two kinds of decompositions of the smooth part B=(∂_xK,−∂_yK): arbitrary finite sums and “factored” splits aligned with x/y blocks, and advocates non-uniform sampling. The analysis establishes linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA in a weighted norm Ω, with rates expressed via a Lipschitz constant L and a sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π), and provides complexity bounds for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x under various sampling schemes. A Catalyst-style proximal regularization yields an accelerated scheme with improved oracle complexity. Experiments on AUC-type losses and non-separable regularizers show advantages over batch and uniform-sampling variants.

Relation to prior work
Builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum minimization and primal-dual/FB-type methods (Chambolle–Pock, Nesterov) for saddle-point problems. It contrasts with stochastic FB (sublinear) and separable-coordinate primal-dual methods. The monotone operator view connects to proximal point methods and monotone inclusion splitting; the paper claims to avoid co-coercivity assumptions often needed in convex minimization analyses.

Strengths
- Novelty: Extending variance reduction (SVRG/SAGA) to general composite convex-concave saddle points with provable linear convergence is timely and nontrivial. The monotone operator perspective is elegant and potentially broadly applicable.
- Technical ideas: Careful use of weighted norms and non-uniform sampling tailored to bilinear structure; clear articulation of factored vs. individual splits; Catalyst-style acceleration adapted to the saddle-point context.
- Theory: Linear rates for both SVRG and SAGA, with explicit dependence on L and L̄(π); sampling strategies with Frobenius/row/column-norm-based bounds; extension to monotone inclusions.
- Practical relevance: Targets non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient prox, a gap not covered by standard variance-reduced minimization methods; non-uniform sampling empirically helps.
- Experiments: Nontrivial non-separable losses and regularizers; acceleration helps on ill-conditioned problems.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical/notation inconsistencies:
- Condition number vs. Lipschitz constant: L is repeatedly called “the condition number,” but it is the Lipschitz constant (in the Ω/Ω* operator norm). If μ denotes strong monotonicity (implied by M), the condition number is L/μ. This imprecision pervades rate expressions.
- Undefined μ in Theorem 2: The bound uses μ (e.g., 1+L^2/μ^2), but μ is never defined in Section 2. Either μ=1 by normalization in Ω, or it denotes strong monotonicity; this must be clarified consistently.
- Repeated ambiguous “L2”/“L̄2”: Many places write “L2” when “L^2” is intended; same for “γ2”, “λ2”. In Eq. (2), “λ2‖x−x′‖^2” and “γ2‖y−y′‖^2” appear to mean (λ/2)‖x−x′‖^2 and (γ/2)‖y−y′‖^2, but the notation is ambiguous. This is not just cosmetic; it affects step-size dimensions and readers’ ability to reproduce results.
- FB step-sizes and rates: Section 2.2 states FB converges linearly for strongly convex-concave problems with σ=1/L^2 and rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t. This is unconventional; standard FB/forward-backward-splitting rates depend on μ and L (often requiring cocoercivity or using contractive resolvents with σ∈(0,1/L)). The paper should state precisely the assumptions (e.g., strong monotonicity of A, monotonicity and Lipschitzness of B) and cite exact results from monotone operator theory to justify σ and the rate. As written, dimensions are opaque.
- Algorithm 1 inner-loop length: “for k=1 to log 4×(L2+3L̄2/m)” is unclear (logarithm of what? missing parentheses? why a logarithm for inner iterations?). The dependency on L^2+3L̄^2/m should be specified without ambiguous “log 4×”.
- Statement “(b) does need splits into convex-concave terms” in Section 1 appears to be a typo; the paper later emphasizes no need for each Bi to be a gradient of a convex-concave Ki.
- In Algorithm 2, the optional resampling step is required by the theory for non-uniform sampling, but is disabled in experiments. This mismatch should be discussed more carefully (variance/bias effects).
- The symbols and matrices (1/λ 0; 0 1/γ) in several updates are poorly typeset and may confuse implementation.

Theoretical gaps/assumptions:
- Strong convexity-concavity constants: Algorithms require λ,γ (or μ) to set steps and epoch lengths; there is no discussion of how to estimate/tune them in practice when unknown.
- Complexity claims “always superior to accelerated batch” (Section 5) are too strong; the advantage depends on relative oracle costs T(B) vs. T(Bi) and L̄. Please qualify the claim and provide regimes where batch may be competitive.
- Non-uniform sampling: Computing pj,qk or πjk from KK^T and K^TK diagonals can be nontrivial for very large/sparse K; clarify preprocessing costs and whether they are included in complexity.

Experiments:
- Limited datasets (two) and no ablation on factored vs. individual splits, mini-batch size m, and the impact of non-uniform vs. uniform sampling on convergence constants. The catalyst parameter τ and schedule are not detailed; “adaptive” updates are mentioned but not specified.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to stochastic mirror-prox or recent stochastic primal-dual variants beyond [11,12]. Even if sublinear, they can show early-iteration behavior.
- Reproducibility: No code or sufficient hyperparameter details to reproduce plots; prox solvers for the non-separable regularizer/loss are cited but not described enough for implementation.

Formatting/references:
- Numerous reference entries are incomplete (years/pages missing), inconsistent, or malformed (“Adv. NIPS,, ,” appears multiple times). The manuscript contains stray text (“th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...”) and several typos.

Quality
The core ideas and many proofs (as claimed) are sound and promising, but the presentation of constants, step-sizes, and rates is confusing, and some assumptions are not clearly delineated. Experimental evidence is positive but limited.

Clarity
Significant notation/formatting issues hinder readability and reproducibility. The paper needs careful editing and clearer statement of assumptions and parameter choices.

Originality
High. Extending variance reduction to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and monotone inclusions with linear rates is novel at the time.

Significance
Potentially high, given the ubiquity of saddle-point formulations with non-separable terms. However, broader empirical validation and clearer guidance for practitioners are needed.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define μ (strong monotonicity) used in Theorem 2 and reconcile with λ,γ and the Ω-norm scaling. Please restate all rates in terms of L and μ.
- Justify σ=1/L^2 and the rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t with a clear theorem and assumptions. Are you assuming cocoercivity of B, or only Lipschitz monotone?
- Clarify the inner-loop length in SVRG (Algorithm 1) and provide a clean, dimensionally consistent schedule.
- Detail the catalyst parameters (τ, frequency of updating (x̄,ȳ)) used in experiments.
- Provide ablations comparing factored vs. individual splits, and uniform vs. non-uniform sampling.
- Discuss cost of computing sampling probabilities and memory footprint for SAGA in the individual split.

Pros
- Novel, principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and monotone operator settings.
- Solid theoretical development with linear rates and insightful sampling strategies.
- Meaningful application scenarios; promising empirical results.

Cons
- Numerous clarity/notation/formatting errors; ambiguous constants and step-sizes.
- Limited experimental scope and missing ablations; some theory–practice mismatches.
- Overstated claims about superiority over batch methods without fully articulated regimes.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The contribution is substantial, but the accumulation of technical clarity issues, notation inconsistencies, and limited empirical validation makes the paper hard to verify and reproduce in its current form. With careful revision to fix the notation, solidify assumptions/rates, and expand experiments/ablations, this would be a strong submission.
