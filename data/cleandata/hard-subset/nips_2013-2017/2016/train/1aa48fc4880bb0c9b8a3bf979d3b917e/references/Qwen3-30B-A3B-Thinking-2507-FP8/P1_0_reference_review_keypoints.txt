- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems of form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with K smooth convex-concave and M simple/prox-friendly.  
- Provides convergence analysis showing linear rates under suitable assumptions.  
- Identifies two split notions (function-based and derivative-based) specific to saddle-point problems.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for algorithmic efficiency.  
- Uses the "catalyst" framework to obtain accelerated variants.  
- Extends analysis to monotone operators, broadening applicability beyond saddle-point problems.  
- Builds on prior SVRG/SAGA literature while filling a gap for saddle-point stochastic variance reduction.  
- Technical quality is strong with rigorous proofs, clear assumptions (A)-(C), and thorough complexity analysis.  
- Complexity analysis carefully considers different sampling strategies and their performance impact.  
- Experiments are well-designed and demonstrate practical benefits, especially for non-separable losses/regularizers and ill-conditioned problems.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations, notably requiring knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written with consistent notation; dual-norm definitions may confuse some readers.  
- Would benefit from more practical guidance on parameter selection (e.g., step size σ) beyond theoretical bounds.  
- Would benefit from a small example illustrating factored vs. individual splits.  
- Original contributions: first SVRG/SAGA convergence analysis for saddle-point problems, identification of split types, non-uniform sampling insight, catalyst-based acceleration, and monotone-operator extension.  
- Significance: addresses an important literature gap, provides first linear-rate stochastic variance reduction for saddle-point problems, and handles common non-separable settings.  
- Strengths: novel theoretical results, clear experimental validation, broadened applicability, careful analysis, and practical relevance.  
- Weaknesses: need for strong convexity-concavity constants, some proofs may be hard for non-specialists, and limited intuition on why non-uniform sampling is so effective.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — strong, novel, technically sound, and practically valuable.  
- Specific suggestions: add an example for split types, provide practical parameter-setting advice, and briefly explain why non-uniform sampling helps for saddle-point problems.
