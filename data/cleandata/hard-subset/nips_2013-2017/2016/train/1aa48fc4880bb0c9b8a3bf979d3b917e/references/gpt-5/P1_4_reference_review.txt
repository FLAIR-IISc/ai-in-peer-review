Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. It extends stochastic variance-reduced methods SVRG and SAGA to this setting by working with the monotone operator formulation B(x,y)=(∂xK, −∂yK) and a proximal step for M. The contributions are: (i) linear convergence analyses for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting using only monotonicity (not cocoercivity), (ii) support for two kinds of finite-sum decompositions, including “factored” splits that respect the saddle structure and allow memory-efficient SAGA for bilinear problems, (iii) importance of non-uniform sampling with explicit L̄(π) constants and complexity bounds, and (iv) an acceleration scheme inspired by catalyst/proximal-point that yields improved complexities and, for bilinear problems, is claimed to dominate accelerated batch methods. The analysis is shown to extend to general strongly monotone inclusions A+B with access to the resolvent of A and Lipschitz forward operators Bi (not necessarily monotone or gradients), thus covering variational inequalities.

The paper relates to and generalizes: batch forward–backward and accelerated primal–dual methods (Chambolle–Pock, Arrow–Hurwicz), stochastic FB with O(1/t) rates, variance reduction for convex minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), primal–dual coordinate methods for separable ERM (SPDC, APCG variants), and monotone operator splitting (proximal point). The distinction from prior variance-reduced primal–dual work is that here K and M need not be separable and Bi need not be gradients nor monotone; the analysis relies on strong monotonicity and Lipschitzness rather than cocoercivity.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: First linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in general composite saddle-point problems beyond separable ERM; monotone-operator framing that accommodates non-gradient, non-monotone Bi and factored splits.
- Clear modeling and assumptions: The (λ,γ)-weighted norm Ω and Lipschitz constants are carefully defined; the sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π) is explicit, enabling principled non-uniform sampling.
- Acceleration: A simple and principled proximal-point (catalyst-like) wrapper with clean complexity T(B)(1+τ)+L̄^2(1+τ)^{-1}max T(Bi), optimized by 1+τ≈L̄√(max T(Bi)/T(B)), avoids Nesterov extrapolation machinery and applies beyond bilinear K in batch.
- Practical insights: Factored vs individual splits for bilinear K, storage complexity of SAGA (O(n+d) vs O(nd)), mini-batching to offset large prox costs, and non-uniform sampling choices tied to matrix norms (‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max).
- Empirical support: On non-separable problems, SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling outperform batch FB and its accelerated variant; accelerated SVRG is best on ill-conditioned settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Experimental scope: Only two datasets and baselines limited primarily to FB and accelerated FB. Comparisons to strong saddle-point baselines (e.g., Chambolle–Pock with linesearch, extra-gradient/mirror-prox variants, SPDC on separable cases, and recent optimistic/extragradient methods) would strengthen the case.
- Theory–practice mismatch: The SAGA analysis for non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step after updates; the experiments omit it (following pragmatic practice). Please report the empirical impact of adhering to the theoretically justified resampling.
- Dependence on parameters: Methods rely on known λ, γ, L and L̄; there is no adaptive step-size/unknown-strong-convexity handling (acknowledged as future work). Practical guidance for estimating these constants would be helpful.
- Acceleration claims: While the proximal-point acceleration is elegant, empirical evidence against accelerated batch methods is limited; also, the exact conditions under which the accelerated SVRG dominates (in terms of L̄, T(B), T(Bi)) should be discussed more concretely for non-bilinear K.
- Clarity: Some constants and rates (e.g., FB rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t with σ=1/L^2) are unfamiliar; a brief derivation or pointer in the main text (not only appendix) would aid readers. The switch between L and L̄ in step-size formulas could be clarified. Notation uses K for both function and matrix.

Quality
The analysis appears technically sound and is carefully scoped: strong convex–concave structure with accessible prox for M; Lipschitz B; variance reduction via SVRG/SAGA with explicit sampling. The extension to monotone operators (A+B) is well-motivated and distinguishes the work from cocoercivity-based analyses (e.g., SMART). Proofs are deferred but the algorithmic statements are precise. Experiments support key claims but could be broader.

Clarity
Well organized and readable despite technical density. Assumptions are explicit, algorithms are clearly stated, and complexity comparisons are reasoned via matrix norms. Some polishing (rates/step-sizes in main text; consistent notation; a short algorithmic schematic for the accelerated wrapper) would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. Applying VR methods to general saddle-point problems with linear convergence and without separability or cocoercivity assumptions, along with the factored split idea and explicit non-uniform sampling analysis, is a notable contribution. The acceleration via proximal-point within this setting is also a clean technical contribution.

Significance
Significant. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous (robust optimization, structured losses, non-separable regularizers). Bringing SAGA/SVRG with linear rates and acceleration to this setting broadens their applicability and is likely to influence subsequent work on stochastic primal–dual methods and monotone inclusions.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide an empirical study of the resampling step for non-uniform SAGA, and discuss practical workarounds?
- For convex–concave problems without strong convexity/concavity, can your proximal-point wrapper recover near-optimal complexity in terms of condition numbers after adding τ? Please clarify global complexity and dependence on ε.
- What is the overhead of computing/maintaining non-uniform sampling probabilities in large-scale settings? Any heuristics for approximate probabilities?
- Could you compare to extra-gradient or optimistic gradient methods (deterministic and stochastic) that are known to stabilize saddle-point optimization?
- Provide guidance on choosing mini-batch size m in practice to balance prox costs vs oracle calls.

Pros
- First linear-rate VR methods for general composite saddle-point problems.
- Monotone-operator analysis not requiring cocoercivity or separability.
- Practical, analyzable non-uniform sampling and factored splits.
- Simple and effective proximal-point acceleration.
- Clear complexity bounds tied to matrix norms and operation costs.

Cons
- Limited experimental comparisons and datasets.
- Reliance on known condition parameters; no adaptivity.
- Theory–practice gap regarding SAGA resampling.
- Some clarity issues around constants and rates.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes substantive theoretical and algorithmic advances on an important class of problems, and presents practical methods with supportive experiments. Expanding empirical comparisons and clarifying a few technical points would further strengthen the work.
