Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends SVRG and SAGA from convex minimization to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The key design choices are: (i) viewing the problem through monotone operators (A+B) with A = ∂M and B = ∇K reshaped as (∂xK, −∂yK), (ii) allowing B to be split into Lipschitz components Bi that need not be gradients, with a “factored” split tailored to bilinear K, (iii) non-uniform sampling, and (iv) a catalyst-style acceleration that regularizes around (x̄,ȳ) and updates it periodically. The paper proves linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA (and improved complexities under non-uniform sampling), and extends the analysis to general monotone operators. Experiments on non-separable losses/regularizers show gains over batch and stochastic baselines; accelerated SVRG performs best on ill-conditioned instances.

This sits at the intersection of: variance reduction for finite sums (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), primal-dual/forward-backward splitting for saddle points (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock), and monotone operator methods. Related stochastic methods for saddle points and variational inequalities include Mirror-Prox/extragradient (Nemirovski–Juditsky), PDHG and randomized coordinate variants, and recent variance-reduction for monotone operators (e.g., SMART). The paper’s angle—variance reduction without cocoercivity and with factored sampling—appears distinct from much of that prior work.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of SVRG/SAGA to composite saddle points, with careful use of weighted norms (λ,γ) and prox of M.
- Monotone operator framing that removes the need for cocoercivity; the results extend beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.
- Non-uniform sampling analysis, including factored row/column schemes for bilinear K, with sharper L̄ bounds than uniform.
- Acceleration via a simple proximal-point (catalyst-like) outer loop; complexity improves to ~L̄ sqrt(T(B) max_i T(Bi)) in regimes of interest.
- Practical relevance: supports non-separable losses/regularizers with available prox (AUC surrogate, OSCAR-like penalties).
- Storage/complexity discussion for factored vs individual splits; clear intuition on when SAGA/SVRG beat batch/accelerated batch.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/clarity:
- Notation inconsistencies and likely typographical corruption make it hard to validate constants: many “λ2”, “γ2”, “L2”, “1/L2” appear where 1/2 factors or squares seem intended. For example, M “strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave” should be M−(λ/2)||x||^2+(γ/2)||y||^2 convex–concave, and prox uses λ/2, γ/2, but the slashes are missing throughout. Similarly, FB uses σ = 1/L2 whereas the accelerated variant uses σ = 1/(2L); this L vs L^2 inconsistency is serious.
- In Theorem 2 (SAGA), the contraction uses µ, but µ is never defined in Section 4. µ only appears later in Section 6 (“assume λ=γ=µ”), which is not part of the SAGA setup. This renders the bound ambiguous.
- Algorithm 2 step size: σ = [max{3|I|/(2m) − 1, L^2 + 3 L̄^2/m}]^−1 appears garbled (spacing, missing parentheses); please restate precisely.
- Batch FB rate claims (e.g., linear with rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t) and choice σ=1/L^2 need a clean derivation in the stated norm; as written, they conflict with the accelerated case and with standard FB step sizes tied to Lipschitz constants without cocoercivity.
- Table 1 is referenced many times but not present; key comparisons and complexities cannot be verified.

Methodology/positioning:
- The “first convergence analysis” claim should be carefully positioned relative to Mirror-Prox/extragradient (Nemirovski–Juditsky) for smooth monotone VIs (sublinear and linear rates under strong monotonicity), stochastic variants, and recent variance-reduced methods for monotone operators (e.g., SMART). Some are cited ([29]/SMART), but Mirror-Prox is not explicitly cited and not used as a baseline.
- For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, the bound requires resampling; experiments use a heuristic mixture “without the resampling step.” This undermines the theory–practice alignment; an ablation is needed to show the impact.
- The acceleration section selects τ using L̄ and cost ratios T(B), T(Bi); in practice, how are these estimated? No sensitivity analysis is provided.

Experiments:
- Only two datasets; no wall-clock times, no memory discussion, no variance/error bars. Reporting “passes over data” ignores differences in Tprox(M) vs T(Bi), which are central to your complexity claims.
- Baselines lack stochastic Mirror-Prox/extragradient. PDHG (Chambolle–Pock) is included (fb-acc), but stochastic/extragrad variants would be natural comparators.
- “Distance to optimizer” requires knowledge of (x*,y*); how is it computed? If via duality gap minimization, please state; otherwise the metric could be misleading.

Minor issues (but numerous)
- Text/formatting: “does need splits” likely “does not need”; “de done serially”; “th Conference on Neural …” header fragment; multiple missing slashes “1/2” throughout; random spaces in symbols (“L 2”, “L̄ 2”); inconsistent capitalization of norms; missing Eq. references (e.g., to Table 1).
- Probability definitions: non-uniform π for factored/individual splits assume access to row/column norms or entries; precomputation/storage costs and applicability to non-bilinear K not discussed.
- Algorithms: clarify mini-batch m usage, step-size tuning in practice, and memory footprint for stored operators in SAGA under factored splits.
- Claims like “SAGA/SVRG with uniform sampling always superior to batch FB” rely on norm inequalities that bound L̄; these are worst-case upper bounds and do not guarantee empirical superiority (the text later partially acknowledges this).

Pros
- Novel and useful extension of variance reduction to composite saddle points and more general monotone operators.
- Solid non-uniform sampling theory tailored to bilinear problems.
- Simple acceleration scheme with intuitive interpretation.
- Potentially high impact for non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient prox.

Cons
- Significant notation/formatting issues obscure correctness and reproducibility (undefined constants, inconsistent step sizes).
- Missing key table, incomplete baselines, and limited experimental rigor.
- Theory–practice gap (resampling requirement vs implementation).
- Positioning relative to extragradient/Mirror-Prox and recent VR methods for monotone operators could be stronger.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The core ideas are promising and likely significant, but the paper in its current form has enough technical/notation inconsistencies and experimental/positioning gaps that I cannot confidently assess correctness or impact. I strongly encourage a revision that (i) fixes notation and constants, (ii) reinstates the missing table and clarifies step sizes and µ, (iii) adds Mirror-Prox/extragradient baselines and wall-clock/memory metrics, and (iv) aligns experiments with the theoretical sampling assumptions. My confidence in the assessment is high regarding clarity issues and moderate regarding the precise rates/constants due to the formatting corruption.
