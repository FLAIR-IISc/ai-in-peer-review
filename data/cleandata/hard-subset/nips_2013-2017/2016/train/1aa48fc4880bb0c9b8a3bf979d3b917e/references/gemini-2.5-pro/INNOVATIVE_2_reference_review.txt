This paper proposes the extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to the general class of composite saddle-point problems. The key motivation is that many machine learning problems, such as those with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation), are not amenable to standard SVR methods in their primal or dual formulations, but naturally exhibit a finite-sum structure in a saddle-point representation. The authors adapt the SVRG and SAGA algorithms to this setting and provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods. A notable aspect of their analysis is the use of a monotone operator framework, which generalizes their results beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Furthermore, the paper investigates the impact of non-uniform sampling and demonstrates how a catalyst-style acceleration scheme can be applied to achieve even faster convergence rates, theoretically outperforming accelerated batch methods.

This work builds directly upon the foundational papers on SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014) but carves out a significant new application domain for these powerful techniques. While prior work has focused on finite-sum convex minimization, this paper tackles a more general and challenging problem structure that has become increasingly relevant in modern machine learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and the importance of the problem it addresses. Extending variance-reduced methods to the saddle-point setting is a significant and timely contribution. It provides a principled way to accelerate a broad class of optimization problems that could not previously benefit from these state-of-the-art techniques. The insight that separability can exist in the saddle-point formulation even when absent in the primal/dual is key and opens a new direction for algorithmic development. The generalization of the analysis to monotone operators further broadens the impact of this work beyond the immediate machine learning context.
*   **Bold and General Approach:** While the authors state the algorithmic extension is "straightforward," the main intellectual contribution is in recognizing this possibility and, more importantly, providing a rigorous convergence analysis. The choice to frame the analysis in the language of monotone operators is particularly insightful. This avoids the need for co-coercivity assumptions that limit other SVR analyses to the convex minimization setting and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's underlying mathematical structure. This generality is a hallmark of a foundational paper.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms, establishing linear convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA in the strongly-convex-concave setting. The complexity analysis (Table 1) is clear and effectively demonstrates the theoretical advantages over batch and standard stochastic methods, particularly when using non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The application of the Catalyst framework is also well-motivated and analyzed.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly lays out the problem setting, assumptions, algorithms, and theoretical results. The connections to prior work are well-articulated, and the motivation is compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Algorithmic Design:** From a purely mechanical perspective, the proposed algorithms are direct adaptations of SVRG/SAGA to the forward-backward algorithm for saddle-point problems. The novelty is therefore not in the design of a fundamentally new update rule but in the application, analysis, and framing of existing ideas in a new context. For a reviewer focused on purely algorithmic innovation, this might be seen as a minor weakness, but in my view, the conceptual leap is the more important contribution.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on a relevant non-separable problem, showing clear superiority over baseline methods. However, the validation is limited to two datasets. While sufficient to support the paper's claims, a broader experimental evaluation, perhaps including problems from game theory or other variational inequalities, could have further strengthened the case for the method's generality.
*   **Dependence on Problem Parameters:** The proposed algorithms and their analysis rely on knowledge of the strong convexity-concavity constants and Lipschitz constants. While this is common for this class of algorithms, it can be a practical limitation. The authors rightly point to adaptivity as an important direction for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** This is the first work to provide linearly convergent, variance-reduced algorithms for the general saddle-point setting. This is a significant advance that opens up a new and important class of problems to these powerful optimization techniques.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The analysis is rigorous, insightful, and general, relying on the sophisticated framework of monotone operators. This is a substantial theoretical contribution.
3.  **Addresses a Real Need:** The paper clearly motivates the work with concrete examples from machine learning (e.g., non-separable losses/regularizers) where existing fast methods fail.
4.  **Establishes a New State of the Art:** The proposed accelerated SVRG method is shown, both in theory and practice, to outperform existing batch, accelerated batch, and stochastic methods for this problem class.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  The core update rule is a combination of existing components (SVR + Forward-Backward).
2.  The experimental validation, while positive, is not exhaustive.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It introduces a bold and significant new direction by bridging the world of variance reduction with the rich domain of saddle-point optimization and monotone inclusions. The paper's primary contribution—identifying this connection and providing the first rigorous analysis—far outweighs the fact that the algorithm itself is a combination of known components. The theoretical work is elegant and general, and the practical implications for machine learning are substantial. This is exactly the type of novel, high-impact work that should be presented at NIPS.
