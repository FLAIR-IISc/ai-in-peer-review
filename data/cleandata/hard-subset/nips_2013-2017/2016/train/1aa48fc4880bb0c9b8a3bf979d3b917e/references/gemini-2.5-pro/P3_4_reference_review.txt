### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods to the setting of composite, decomposable saddle-point problems. The core problem is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is a smooth convex-concave function that can be decomposed into a sum of components, and `M` is a strongly convex-concave (potentially non-smooth) function with a simple proximal operator. The primary motivation is that many machine learning problems, particularly those with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or regularizers (e.g., total variation), naturally fit this saddle-point structure even when their primal or dual formulations are not additively separable, precluding the direct application of existing variance-reduced methods.

The authors' main contributions are:
1.  The introduction of SVRG and SAGA algorithms for this saddle-point setting.
2.  A novel convergence analysis that establishes linear convergence. Crucially, this analysis is based on the interpretation of the problem as finding the zero of a monotone operator and relies only on monotonicity, unlike standard analyses for convex minimization which often use co-coercivity—a property not held by saddle-point gradient operators.
3.  A detailed analysis of different decomposition strategies ("splits") and the theoretical and empirical demonstration that non-uniform sampling is key to achieving the best performance.
4.  An accelerated version of the SVRG algorithm using a Catalyst-like framework, which yields a theoretically superior algorithm.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the optimization literature for machine learning. While variance reduction is a cornerstone of modern large-scale convex optimization, its application has been largely limited to additively separable objectives. This work significantly broadens the scope of these powerful techniques to a new class of problems, including important non-separable models that were previously tackled with slower batch or sublinearly convergent stochastic algorithms. The examples of AUC maximization and complex regularizers are highly relevant.

2.  **Novel and Sound Theoretical Contribution:** While the algorithmic extensions of SVRG/SAGA may seem straightforward, the convergence analysis is not. The authors correctly identify that the standard proof techniques from the convex minimization setting do not apply. Their choice to frame the problem in the more general context of monotone operators and provide a proof that relies only on monotonicity is the paper's key theoretical insight. This not only makes the analysis applicable to saddle-point problems but also to a wider class of variational inequalities, strengthening the contribution. The resulting convergence rates clearly demonstrate the advantage over existing methods.

3.  **Thorough and Comprehensive Work:** The paper is very complete. It considers both SVRG and SAGA, thoughtfully discusses the trade-offs (e.g., memory vs. epochs), and investigates different sampling schemes. The inclusion of an accelerated variant via the Catalyst framework is a valuable addition, leading to the state-of-the-art complexity for this problem class. The comparison of complexities in Table 1 is particularly clear and effective at summarizing the theoretical improvements.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly support the theoretical findings. On a realistic task with non-separable components (AUC-like loss and clustering regularizer), the proposed SAGA and accelerated SVRG methods with non-uniform sampling significantly outperform batch, accelerated batch, and standard stochastic methods. The performance gap is especially pronounced on the more ill-conditioned problem, as predicted by theory. The comparison to primal SAGA on a separable problem is a good sanity check that validates the framework.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The proposed methods, particularly the accelerated version, rely on knowledge of problem-dependent constants like `L` (Lipschitz constant), `L̄` (sampled Lipschitz constant), and strong convexity/concavity parameters (`λ`, `γ`). While this is common in theoretical analyses, a brief discussion on the practical challenges of setting these parameters or potential adaptive strategies would be beneficial for practitioners. For instance, the Catalyst framework introduces `τ`, and while an optimal value is suggested, its practical tuning can be non-trivial.

2.  **Clarity on Storage for Factored SAGA:** The paper notes that for factored splits, SAGA only requires O(n+d) storage, which is a significant practical advantage. However, the update rule and complexity in Theorem 2 depend on `|I|`, which for a factored split would be `|J| x |K|`. The text clarifies that `|I|` is replaced by `max{|J|, |K|}`. This is a crucial detail that is slightly buried. It would be helpful to make this point more prominent and perhaps explicitly write out the factored SAGA algorithm or update rule to make the low-memory implementation crystal clear.

3.  **Positioning vs. Stochastic Primal-Dual Methods:** The paper cites stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, 2014). While these methods target separable saddle-point problems and are thus distinct, a slightly more explicit discussion in the main text on why the problem structures are different and why those methods are not applicable here would further strengthen the paper's positioning and highlight its novelty.

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution. It successfully bridges the gap between the powerful variance-reduction paradigm and the broad class of saddle-point problems with non-separable structure. The combination of a novel and general theoretical analysis, a comprehensive set of algorithms, and strong empirical evidence makes this a standout submission. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to small points of clarification that do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
-   Solves a well-motivated and important problem in machine learning optimization.
-   Provides the first linearly-convergent stochastic algorithms for this general class of problems.
-   The theoretical analysis based on monotonicity is novel, elegant, and more general than the problem setting itself.
-   The work is comprehensive, covering SVRG, SAGA, non-uniform sampling, and acceleration.
-   Experimental results are clear and convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
-   None. The paper is of high quality across all criteria.
