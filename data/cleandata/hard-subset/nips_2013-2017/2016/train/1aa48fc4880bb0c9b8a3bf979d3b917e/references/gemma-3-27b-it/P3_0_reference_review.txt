## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) techniques – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the realm of saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning arising from duality and various regularized learning scenarios. The authors provide convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, highlighting differences from the convex minimization case. They demonstrate the importance of splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling, and introduce an acceleration technique building upon the “catalyst” framework.  The work is well-motivated, addressing a gap in the theoretical understanding of applying accelerated stochastic methods to saddle-point formulations frequently encountered in practical applications.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. While SVR techniques are well-established for convex optimization, their application to saddle-point problems necessitates careful analysis due to the differing structure.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The convergence analysis is thorough and provides valuable insights. Framing the problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator allows for a broader applicability beyond just saddle-point problems, encompassing variational inequalities.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The paper clearly articulates the relevance of saddle-point formulations in machine learning, citing examples like robust optimization, non-separable regularizers, and structured prediction.
* **Detailed Analysis of Splits:** The discussion of different splitting strategies (function-wise vs. derivative-wise, factored splits) is insightful and highlights the importance of problem structure for efficiency.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, and provide a compelling comparison to existing methods. The analysis of different sampling strategies is well presented.
* **Extension to Monotone Operators:** The extension beyond saddle-point problems to the broader class of monotone operators enhances the paper’s significance and potential impact.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex theoretical aspects relatively accessible.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Assumptions:**  The assumptions (A)-(C), while standard for this type of analysis, are potentially restrictive in some practical scenarios. Relaxing these assumptions could broaden the applicability of the algorithms.  The need for strong convexity-concavity is a notable limitation.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The optimal choice of parameters (step-sizes, regularization parameters) is not fully explored. While the theory provides guidance, practical guidelines for parameter selection would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments focus primarily on binary classification problems.  Demonstrating the performance on a wider range of problems, particularly those with different structures and data characteristics, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Catalyst Acceleration Details:**  While the acceleration technique is mentioned and a bound provided, the details of its implementation and impact relative to the base SVRG algorithm could be more comprehensively explained.  The scaling with problem dimensions could also be made clearer.
* **Comparison to Dual Ascent:** The paper focuses on primal-based SVR extensions. A discussion of how these methods compare to dual ascent methods, especially in the context of saddle-point problems, would be beneficial.




**Detailed Comments & Specific Points:**

* **Assumption (A):**  The strong convexity-concavity assumption is a significant restriction.  Can the results be extended to weaker conditions, such as convexity-concavity?
* **Splitting Strategies:** The discussion of factored splits is excellent.  Are there practical guidelines for choosing between function-wise and derivative-wise splits?  How does the choice depend on the structure of the K matrix?
* **Non-Uniform Sampling:** The analysis of non-uniform sampling is crucial.  How sensitive are the results to the accuracy of the probability estimates?
* **Acceleration:** The catalyst acceleration framework is promising. Is the overhead of the additional computations involved in the acceleration scheme justified by the improved convergence rate in practice?
* **Experiment Section:** While the results are convincing, the presentation could be improved by including confidence intervals or statistical significance tests.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The connection to related work in the field of stochastic dual coordinate ascent (SDCA) could be further explored.




**Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant theoretical gap in applying accelerated stochastic methods to saddle-point problems.
* Provides rigorous convergence analysis with clear insights.
* Demonstrates practical effectiveness through empirical validation.
* Offers extensions to monotone operators, broadening the applicability of the work.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Con:**

* Strong assumptions limit the applicability of the algorithms.
* Limited scope of experiments.
* Parameter tuning and practical implementation details could be more comprehensively addressed.
* Lack of comparison to related methods like dual ascent.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. While some limitations exist, the novelty, rigor, and relevance of the work make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should consider expanding the experimental section and providing more detailed guidance on parameter tuning and the implementation of the acceleration scheme.  Also, a discussion of the connection to dual ascent methods would be valuable.



**Confidence:** 8/10
