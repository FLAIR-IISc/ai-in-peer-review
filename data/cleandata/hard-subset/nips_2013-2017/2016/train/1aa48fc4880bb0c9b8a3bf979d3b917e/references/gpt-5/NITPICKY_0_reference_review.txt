Summary
The paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), with M prox-friendly and strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave and K smooth. The key idea is to extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems and, more generally, to zeros of monotone operators A+B where A is strongly monotone with available resolvent and B is a sum of Lipschitz single-valued components. The authors:
- Formulate SVRG and SAGA variants that estimate B via variance reduction, allow non-uniform sampling and mini-batches, and handle “factored” splits that need not be gradients of component functions.
- Prove linear convergence rates under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz assumptions, with constants that reveal the benefit of non-uniform sampling (Frobenius-norm vs operator-norm regimes).
- Propose a Catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration wrapper and claim improved complexity bounds.
- Provide empirical results on two datasets with non-separable elements, comparing to batch and stochastic baselines.

Strengths
- The extension of variance-reduction to saddle-point problems (and monotone inclusions) without requiring each component to be a gradient of a function is interesting and useful, and the “factored” storage for bilinear problems is a practical design.
- The emphasis on non-uniform sampling and the derived constants L̄(π) for several splits clarify when variance reduction can beat batch methods.
- The proximal-point (Catalyst-like) acceleration in the saddle/monotone setting is natural and, if rigorously justified, can be impactful.
- Experiments suggest the accelerated SVRG variant can outperform batch primal–dual and naive stochastic methods, especially in ill-conditioned regimes.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity:
- Forward–backward (FB) for saddle problems: The paper claims linear convergence of a basic FB update for strongly convex–concave problems at σ=1/L^2 with rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t. Classical results indicate that FB with a single forward step on a general monotone Lipschitz B is not convergent unless B is cocoercive (or an extragradient step is used). Chambolle–Pock/PDHG and Mirror-Prox address the instability via extrapolation/extragradient. The stated FB linear rate appears nonstandard and potentially incorrect; a precise statement of assumptions (e.g., cocoercivity vs mere Lipschitzness) and a self-contained proof are needed.
- SAGA theorem (Theorem 2) introduces μ in denominators without having defined it in the main text prior to Sec. 6. If μ = min{λ,γ} or the strong monotonicity parameter, this must be stated clearly. The current presentation is confusing.
- Algorithmic pseudocode contains several issues:
  - Algorithm 1 loops “for k=1 to log 4 × (L^2 + 3L̄^2/m)”: the iteration budget cannot be a logarithm of a constant; parentheses and the intent (number of inner iterations per epoch) are unclear.
  - Step sizes are written with inconsistent spacing and possible typos (e.g., L 2 vs L^2). In Algorithm 2, σ = [max{3|I|2m − 1, L 2 + 3 L̄ 2 m}]^−1 is not parsable; presumably 3|I|/(2m)−1 and L^2 + 3 L̄^2/m are intended.
  - The (optional) “resampling” step for non-uniform SAGA is underspecified. The theorem requires it, but experiments use a mixture without resampling. Please formalize what is proven vs what is used.
- Acceleration: The “accelerated FB” with a Nesterov-like extrapolation for bilinear K is presented as yielding a rate (1−1/(1+2L))^t. This needs precise assumptions and a direct connection to existing PDHG/CP variants; otherwise it risks being misleading. For the Catalyst wrapper, complexity statements omit constants/log factors and depend critically on how accurately each proximal subproblem is solved; the exact inner accuracy schedule and its impact on outer rate should be formalized.
- Monotone operator extension: While attractive, the analysis in the general A+B setting must contend with the lack of cocoercivity of B. Please clarify the precise assumptions under which linear convergence holds (strong monotonicity of A, Lipschitz of B, and which step-size regime), and how they differ from prior co-coercive analyses.

Claims and prior work:
- “First convergence analysis” for variance reduction in saddle/monotone settings should be carefully scoped. SPDC, AdaSPDC, and related stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (Zhang & Xiao 2015; Zhu & Storkey 2015) provide linear rates in separable ERM saddle formulations; Mirror-Prox and PDHG offer alternative baselines. The paper mentions [11,12] but does not compare empirically to SPDC or to stochastic Mirror-Prox, which seems necessary to substantiate significance.
- Some strong claims (e.g., “accelerated SVRG … always superior to accelerated batch algorithms”) are too broad without caveats and could be softened.

Empirical evaluation:
- Only two datasets; no comparison to SPDC/Mirror-Prox; no wall-clock results; no ablations on mini-batch m or storage variants; no sensitivity to step-sizes or to inaccuracies in L and L̄. The “distance to optimizers” metric requires knowing the optimum—how is this computed?
- The non-separable regularizer is described as ∑_{i≠j}|x_i−x_j| with O(n log n) prox via isotonic regression; this matches OSCAR-like sorted-l1 penalties, not the all-pairs fused penalty in general. The cited OSCAR reference suggests a mismatch—please correct the formulation or the reference and justify the prox complexity.

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typographical and notation issues (inconsistent spacing, missing parentheses, L vs L̄, undefined μ, K reused as both matrix and index set name, etc.).
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues), and several are arXiv technical reports where archival counterparts exist. Please update to consistent NeurIPS style and add missing related work (SPDC, Mirror-Prox variants, PDHG acceleration literature).
- Minor but distracting: header text “th Conference on…” remains; equation numbering and punctuation are inconsistent.

Quality: The core ideas are promising, but the current draft has several technical ambiguities (FB convergence, step-size formulas, acceleration claims) and many small errors that undermine confidence. The theory may be correct with proper assumptions, but it needs clearer, rigorous statements and cleaned-up proofs/pseudocode.

Clarity: Moderate to poor. The paper needs careful proofreading, consistent notation, and precise algorithmic descriptions.

Originality: Extending SVRG/SAGA to general saddle/monotone settings with non-potential “factored” splits and non-uniform sampling is novel relative to separable ERM-focused works. The Catalyst viewpoint for saddle problems is a sensible adaptation.

Significance: Potentially significant if the theory is made watertight and comparisons broadened, as non-separable saddle problems are common. As written, empirical support and positioning versus strong baselines are insufficient.

Pros
- Novel and useful extension of variance reduction to general saddle/monotone settings.
- Clear identification of advantageous sampling and splitting strategies; memory-efficient factored SAGA for bilinear K.
- Practical acceleration via proximal-point wrapping.

Cons
- Questionable/unclear claims about FB convergence and accelerated variants; lack of extragradient discussion.
- Sloppy and sometimes incorrect pseudocode/notation; undefined symbols.
- Limited experiments; missing comparisons to key baselines; questionable regularizer/prox description.
- Overly strong claims; incomplete references.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The contribution is promising, but the paper needs substantial revisions to fix technical clarity/ correctness, broaden comparisons, and polish presentation. Constructive suggestions:
- Provide precise assumptions and self-contained proofs for FB convergence and all theorems; define μ clearly.
- Fix algorithms, step-sizes, and inner/outer iteration counts; align acceleration with established PDHG/Mirror-Prox literature.
- Correct the regularizer description and prox complexity; expand experiments (add SPDC, Mirror-Prox; wall-clock; ablations).
- Clean up notation, remove typos, and complete references.
