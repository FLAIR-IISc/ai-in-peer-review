Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is “simple” (prox-friendly) and strongly convex-concave, and K is smooth, possibly decomposed into many components. The authors propose SVRG- and SAGA-type variance-reduced forward-backward schemes that operate on the monotone operator B=(∂_x K, -∂_y K), allowing decompositions into arbitrary Lipschitz components Bi (not necessarily gradients of functions), and a “factored” split tailored to saddle-point structure. They analyze linear convergence in a weighted norm and emphasize the importance of non-uniform sampling. They further wrap SVRG in a Catalyst-like proximal-point outer loop to obtain accelerated complexities, and claim the analysis extends beyond saddle points to general monotone operator inclusions A+B=0 with prox access to A and Lipschitz B.

This sits alongside SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization, SPDC/PDHG-type primal-dual methods for ERM with separable structure, and variational inequality methods (extragradient/forward-backward). The claimed novelty is linear-rate variance-reduced methods for general (non-separable) composite saddle problems and a monotone-operator analysis not relying on co-coercivity.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: clean extension of SVRG/SAGA to operator-splitting for saddle points without requiring each Bi to be a gradient of a function; allows factored decompositions.
- Monotone operator perspective is elegant; suggests applicability to variational inequalities.
- Non-uniform sampling analysis with meaningful L̄(π) bounds for bilinear problems (row/column norm-based probabilities); aligns with practical speedups.
- Acceleration via a proximal-point (“Catalyst”) outer loop is natural and practically useful; claimed complexity improvements are plausible.
- Experiments indicate benefits of (accelerated) VR over batch FB/PDHG on ill-conditioned cases.

Weaknesses and issues (technical and presentation)
- Inconsistent and sometimes incorrect step-size and rate expressions:
  - Forward-backward: paper states σ=1/L^2 with rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t; later “accelerated” FB uses σ=1/(2L) with rate (1−1/(1+2L))^t. Standard results for strongly monotone + Lipschitz operators yield rates depending on condition number L/μ, not only L or L^2. The disappearance of strong convexity/concavity (μ) from the batch FB rate is suspicious.
  - Throughout, L and L^2 are interchanged oddly in complexities and stepsizes; please unify and correct.
- Undefined constants/notation:
  - Theorem 2 includes μ in the denominator (e.g., 1+L^2/μ^2), but μ is not defined in Section 4; only later in §6 is μ introduced (by setting λ=γ=μ). Define μ (strong monotonicity) early and use it consistently.
  - In multiple places “λ2” and “γ2” likely mean λ/2 and γ/2 (e.g., in the prox definition (2) and strong convexity-concavity). The current typography is ambiguous and can be misread as λ squared. Use 1/2 λ||·||^2.
  - The weighted norm Ω and dual Ω* are defined, but sometimes Ω(x−x′,y−y′)^2≤1 is used while L is a Lipschitz constant; clarify whether L is Lipschitz under Ω→Ω* or squared variants (causing the L vs L^2 confusion).
- Algorithmic details lacking/ambiguous:
  - Algorithm 2 step-size: “max{ 3|I|2m − 1, L 2 + 3 L̄2 m }” has missing parentheses/spaces; likely max{3|I|/(2m)−1, L^2+3 L̄^2/m}. Similar spacing inconsistencies elsewhere.
  - SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires resampling of stored components; the theorem assumes this, but experiments use a mixture without resampling “following [25]”. Please clarify theoretical guarantees for the implemented variant or report the theoretically supported variant.
  - Complexity expressions depend on T(B) and max_i T(B_i). For bilinear K, T(B)=O(nnz(K)) not necessarily nd; sparsity should be discussed. Also, cost to precompute pj,qk or π_jk (requires row/col norms) should be accounted for.
  - Mini-batch sizes: analysis allows general m, but recommended m for different splits and practical defaults are unclear. For “individual” splits, you recommend m=n+d to match factored updates; quantify memory/time trade-offs clearly.
- Claims vs prior work:
  - The paper claims “first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for saddle-point problems.” However, SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, 2015) and related stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods achieve linear rates for ERM in saddle form under separability/strong convexity. The novelty here is non-separable composite structure and non-gradient Bi—please qualify the claim accordingly throughout.
  - PDHG/Chambolle-Pock with variance reduction variants have emerged; please discuss how your methods differ in assumptions and rates (e.g., extragradient vs plain FB, need for strong concavity, co-coercivity).
- Acceleration section:
  - The rate for the proximal-point outer loop is stated as (1−1/(1+τ))^v. Please relate τ to the added strong monotonicity σ τ (units/dimensions), and connect to standard proximal-point contraction 1/(1+στ/μ). The choice “update (x̄, ȳ) every log(1+τ) epochs” is ad hoc; provide a principled schedule or bound with exact constants.
  - The complexity bound L̄ sqrt(T(B) max_i T(B_i)) omits log factors and prox costs; state precise conditions under which it improves over non-accelerated SVRG.
- Monotone operator extension:
  - Nice idea, but the paper explicitly does not use co-coercivity; consequently step sizes can be conservative. Please clarify practical regimes where the monotone-operator variant remains stable, and how it compares to extragradient (mirror-prox) which is standard for monotone VIs.
- Experiments:
  - Only two datasets; include wall-clock time with hardware, not just passes. Report mini-batch sizes, step sizes, τ, epoch lengths, and non-uniform sampling schemes used. Provide standard deviations over runs.
  - The non-separable proximal claims: the AUC surrogate and clustering regularizer proximals are non-trivial; are they exact or approximate (OSCAR reference [31] suggests approximations)? If approximate, discuss impact on convergence guarantees.
  - Compare against extragradient/mirror-prox baselines and modern primal-dual variance-reduced methods where applicable. Provide primal-dual gap plots in the main paper or a concise summary.
- Numerous typos/formatting:
  - “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…” stray line.
  - Many spacing issues around fractions/indices; e.g., “can de done serially”.
  - Table 1 is referenced multiple times but not present; critical for understanding complexities.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually interesting extension of VR methods to general composite saddle problems via operator decomposition.
- Non-uniform sampling analysis tailored to saddle structure; practical relevance.
- Acceleration via proximal-point is compelling and easy to implement.
- Potentially broad impact on variational inequalities beyond ML.

Cons for acceptance
- Notational inconsistencies, undefined constants, and step-size/rate irregularities undermine rigor and reproducibility.
- Experimental section is too limited and lacks key details; no wall-clock results, few baselines.
- Overstated novelty relative to SPDC/PD methods; related work discussion incomplete.
- Table/appendices are essential but missing here; hard to verify proofs and constants.

Questions for authors
1) Please define μ and reconcile all occurrences of L vs L^2 in stepsizes/rates. What is the correct dependence on μ, λ, γ?
2) Provide the exact SAGA stepsize and clarify the resampling requirement under non-uniform sampling.
3) For acceleration, specify τ, epoch schedule, and a precise bound including prox costs; when does it strictly improve?
4) Are the proximal operators for the non-separable examples exact? If approximate, how does this affect the theory?
5) Can you include wall-clock results and extragradient baselines?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper’s ideas are promising and could be impactful, but the current version has too many technical/notation issues, incomplete experimental evidence, and some overstated claims. With a careful rewrite, consistent theory (correct constants), stronger empirical section, and tighter positioning vs prior work, this could become a strong contribution.

Confidence: high.
