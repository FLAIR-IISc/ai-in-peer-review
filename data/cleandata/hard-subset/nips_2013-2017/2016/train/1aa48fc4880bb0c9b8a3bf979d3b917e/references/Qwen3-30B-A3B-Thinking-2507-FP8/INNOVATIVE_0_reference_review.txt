

# Review for "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper introduces the first stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for saddle-point problems, a significant extension of existing optimization techniques that have previously been limited to convex minimization. The authors recognize a critical gap in the literature: while variance reduction methods have transformed convex optimization, they have not been applied to saddle-point problems, which are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, supervised learning with non-separable losses, convex relaxations of unsupervised learning).

The main novelty lies in demonstrating that saddle-point problems offer unique opportunities for variance reduction that differ fundamentally from convex minimization. The authors show that saddle-point formulations allow for two distinct types of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives), that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, and that their algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities and monotone operators. This is not merely an extension of existing methods but a conceptual shift in how we approach optimization for problems with saddle structures.

The theoretical contributions are substantial. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context, showing that their approach differs significantly from convex minimization (which relies on convexity properties). By interpreting saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, they develop a novel analysis framework that avoids the need for co-coercivity assumptions used in prior work. The extension to monotone operators is particularly impressive, as it broadens the applicability of their methods beyond saddle-point problems while maintaining the same theoretical guarantees.

The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of their approach. The authors carefully choose examples where non-separable losses or regularizers make existing methods inapplicable, clearly showing that their algorithms are the only ones that can be applied from the class of large-scale linearly convergent algorithms. The results for ill-conditioned problems (where accelerated SVRG shows the strongest gains) are particularly compelling, as they match the theoretical expectations. The comparison to primal methods on separable problems also effectively demonstrates the broader applicability of their approach.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with excellent examples that make the technical contributions accessible. The authors carefully explain how their work differs from existing methods and provide a thorough discussion of related work.

I see two minor weaknesses: (1) The paper assumes strong convexity-concavity, which might limit applicability, but the authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work (which they plan to address). (2) The experiments are limited to two datasets, but they are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the key theoretical points.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values. It's not an incremental improvement to existing methods but opens up an entirely new direction for optimization in machine learning. The authors have identified a genuine gap in the literature and filled it with a conceptually clean solution that is well-supported by both theory and experiments.

**Strengths**:
- First application of stochastic variance reduction to saddle-point problems
- Novel convergence analysis specific to saddle-point problems
- Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability
- Clear demonstration of practical advantages over existing methods
- Addresses a genuine gap in the literature

**Weaknesses**:
- Assumes strong convexity-concavity (acknowledged as future work)
- Limited to two datasets (but well-chosen and sufficient for demonstration)

This paper represents a significant advancement in optimization for machine learning. It introduces a novel concept that is likely to influence future research in the field, and the authors have provided strong theoretical and experimental support for their claims. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
