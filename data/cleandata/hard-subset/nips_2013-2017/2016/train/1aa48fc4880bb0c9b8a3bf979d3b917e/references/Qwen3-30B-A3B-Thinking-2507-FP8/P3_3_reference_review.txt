

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

## Summary
This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, demonstrating linear convergence rates under strong convexity-concavity assumptions. They show that saddle-point formulations allow unique splits (functions or partial derivatives), highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling, and extend the algorithms with acceleration via the "catalyst" framework. The paper demonstrates theoretical and empirical advantages over existing batch and stochastic methods, with applications to machine learning problems featuring non-separable losses or regularizers.

The work builds on seminal papers on variance reduction for convex optimization (SVRG: Johnson & Zhang, SAGA: Defazio et al.) and extends them to saddle-point problems, which naturally arise through duality in machine learning. It also relates to broader work on monotone operators (Rockafellar, Zhu & Marcotte) and variational inequalities (Harker & Pang).

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first rigorous convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in saddle-point problems. The analysis correctly adapts to the monotone operator framework (Section 6), which is a significant theoretical advance over previous work that primarily focused on convex minimization.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The paper identifies an important gap in the literature—existing variance reduction methods don't apply to saddle-point problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors convincingly demonstrate the applicability to real machine learning problems (Section 2.1), including non-separable losses for ROC curve optimization and structured regularizers.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper provides detailed analysis of different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform) and their impact on convergence rates, showing how factored splits can significantly improve performance (Section 2.4). The theoretical bounds for bilinear saddle-point problems are particularly well-motivated.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments (Section 7) clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The comparison with batch methods and existing stochastic approaches is thorough and well-designed.

## Weaknesses
1. **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption**: The paper relies on strong convexity-concavity (Assumption A), which limits applicability to problems where this property holds. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 8), they don't adequately address how to handle problems without this property, which is common in practice.

2. **Acceleration Analysis**: The accelerated version (Section 5) is presented as a simple extension of the catalyst framework, but the key insight—how the acceleration differs from existing methods for saddle-point problems—is not sufficiently highlighted. The connection to the accelerated forward-backward method (Section 2.2) is underdeveloped.

3. **Limited Connection to Existing Saddle-Point Methods**: The paper doesn't sufficiently contrast with other saddle-point optimization methods (e.g., Chambolle & Pock's primal-dual methods), which are standard in the literature. This weakens the claim that their methods are "the only ones that can be applied" for non-separable problems.

4. **Technical Depth in Factored Splits**: The analysis of factored splits (Section 4) is less rigorous than for individual splits. The paper states that "factored splits" only make a difference in storage (Section 2), but doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why this approach is superior in practice.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, which are prevalent in machine learning through duality. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses an important gap in the literature.

The main limitations are the strong convexity-concavity assumption and the underdeveloped connection to existing saddle-point optimization methods. However, these are common challenges in this area, and the paper's clear presentation of the theoretical framework provides a solid foundation for future work addressing these limitations.

## Recommendation
**Accept**. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that extends the scope of variance reduction methods to a broader class of problems. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and has clear practical relevance. The experimental results validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, the authors appropriately acknowledge this, and the paper provides a strong foundation for future work addressing this limitation.

The paper advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning by extending variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems, which are fundamental to many learning tasks. The techniques proposed are likely to be widely adopted in the machine learning community, particularly for problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. The paper is a strong contribution to the field and deserves publication at NIPS.
