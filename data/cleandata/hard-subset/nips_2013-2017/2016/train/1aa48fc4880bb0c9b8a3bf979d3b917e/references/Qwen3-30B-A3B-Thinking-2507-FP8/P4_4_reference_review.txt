

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems. The authors address the gap in the literature by providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms applied to saddle-point problems, which differ significantly from the convex minimization setting. The paper builds upon prior work on SAG, SVRG, and SAGA for convex optimization [1,2,3] but extends it to saddle-point problems that arise naturally in machine learning through duality. The authors also extend their framework to monotone operators, broadening the applicability of their methods.

The main contributions include: (1) a convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems, (2) demonstration that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, (3) extension of the catalyst acceleration framework to saddle-point problems, and (4) extension to monotone operators. The paper demonstrates that their algorithms achieve linear convergence rates, which is a significant improvement over existing stochastic methods for saddle-point problems.

**Quality:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with complete convergence proofs for all proposed algorithms (Theorems 1-3), which are supported by detailed appendix proofs. The analysis correctly identifies key differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization, particularly in the convergence rates. The experimental section demonstrates clear empirical improvements over existing methods on non-separable machine learning problems, with well-designed comparisons to batch methods and other stochastic approaches. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the need for knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants (λ and γ), which is a common requirement in similar optimization frameworks. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by both theoretical derivations and empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is precise and well-motivated with relevant examples from machine learning (Section 2.1). The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) are presented in a clear pseudocode format with detailed explanations of parameters. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is logically presented and well-motivated. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, and the results are presented in an easily interpretable manner with appropriate visualizations.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, which has not been done before. The authors demonstrate that saddle-point problems require different treatment than convex minimization, particularly regarding the need for non-uniform sampling and the different convergence properties. The introduction of "factored" splits for saddle-point problems is novel and well-justified. The extension to monotone operators is also original and significantly broadens the applicability of their framework beyond saddle-point problems. This work clearly differs from previous NIPS papers on variance reduction methods [1,2,3] and related work on saddle-point problems [11,12].

**Significance:** Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (through duality, robust optimization, and convex relaxations), yet existing optimization methods do not leverage the natural separability of these problems. This paper provides the first scalable methods with linear convergence rates for these problems, which is a substantial improvement over existing batch or stochastic methods. The practical implications are significant, as demonstrated in the experiments with non-separable losses and regularizers. The extension to monotone operators further increases the significance by applying to a broader class of problems. The paper demonstrates that their methods can significantly outperform existing approaches, particularly for ill-conditioned problems, which is an important practical contribution.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field by extending variance reduction methods to a new class of problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and complete, the experimental validation is thorough, and the extension to monotone operators is a substantial theoretical advance. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it accessible to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses:** The main limitation is that the analysis is restricted to strongly-convex-concave problems, which excludes some practical scenarios. The need to know the strong convexity-concavity constants (λ and γ) is a practical limitation, though the authors note they plan to address this in future work. The comparison with specialized saddle-point algorithms [11,12] is limited, though the paper demonstrates that their methods can be applied to non-separable problems where those methods cannot.

**Pro/con for acceptance:**
- Pro: Novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems with rigorous analysis and empirical validation; significant theoretical contribution extending to monotone operators; well-written and clearly organized.
- Con: Restricted to strongly-convex-concave problems; requires knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants; limited comparison with specialized saddle-point methods.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in this setting. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the methods. While the restriction to strongly-convex-concave problems is a limitation, the paper provides clear direction for future work to address this. The contribution is substantial and well-suited for NIPS.
