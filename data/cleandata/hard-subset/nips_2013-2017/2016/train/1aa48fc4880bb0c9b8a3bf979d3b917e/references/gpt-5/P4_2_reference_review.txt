Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and convex-concave and M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave with an efficient proximal operator. The key idea is to extend stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to general saddle-point problems that may be non-separable, and to analyze them via monotone operator theory. The paper provides:

- Linear-convergence analyses for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting under strong convexity-concavity, using the interpretation of solving a strongly monotone inclusion A+B, where A is the subdifferential of M and B is the (monotone) gradient operator of K. Unlike prior work on variance reduction for monotone operators requiring co-coercivity, the analysis here uses monotonicity and strong monotonicity only.
- Two splitting schemes for B: (i) function-based finite sums; (ii) “factored” splits along x- and y-components that do not require each Bi to be a gradient of a scalar, enabling more flexible sampling/storage for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x.
- Importance sampling schemes and mini-batching with explicit smoothness constants L and L̄(π) induced by the sampling distribution, with concrete bounds for bilinear problems in terms of ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max.
- An acceleration mechanism inspired by the Catalyst framework: solve a sequence of proximal-point regularized saddle problems approximately with SVRG, leading to improved complexity L̄ sqrt(T(B) max_i T(Bi)) up to log factors (for bilinear K), and claiming superiority over batch accelerated methods.
- Experiments on two datasets comparing batch forward–backward (FB), accelerated FB, stochastic FB, SAGA/SVRG (uniform vs non-uniform sampling), and accelerated SVRG. Results show faster convergence for SAGA/SVRG with non-uniform sampling; accelerated SVRG helps in ill-conditioned regimes. For a separable problem, primal SAGA is fastest but inapplicable to non-separable cases where the proposed methods apply.

This relates to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization (Le Roux et al.; Johnson & Zhang; Defazio et al.), primal-dual splitting (Chambolle & Pock) and stochastic FB methods (Rosasco–Villa–Vũ) yielding O(1/t) rates, as well as SPDC and related coordinate methods that rely on separability. The extension to general monotone inclusions complements recent work on variance reduction for co-coercive operators (e.g., SMART) by covering saddle-point problems where co-coercivity fails.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The algorithms are natural adaptations of SVRG/SAGA to monotone inclusions with a backward (prox) step for A and a variance-reduced forward step for B. The linear convergence claims rely on strong monotonicity (from M’s strong convexity-concavity) and Lipschitz continuity of B, with step sizes and epoch lengths specified.
- The sampling analysis carefully introduces L̄(π) and derives improved bounds with non-uniform sampling for bilinear problems, matching known benefits of importance sampling in finite-sum variance-reduction.
- The Catalyst-style acceleration is cast as inexact proximal-point iterations; the argument that one proximal-point outer step yields factor (1+τ)^−1 contraction is standard, and the inner SVRG complexity matches known proximal SVRG results adapted to the saddle-point setting.

Points needing clarification or correction:
- Notation consistency: Theorem 2 uses µ in denominators (e.g., 1+L^2/µ^2) while µ is not clearly defined in the main text; presumably µ relates to strong monotonicity (e.g., min{λ,γ}). Define µ explicitly and keep consistent with Ω(·) norm scaling.
- In Section 2.2, the stated FB step size and rate use L^2 and (1−1/(1+L^2))^t; this differs from common presentations in monotone forward–backward where step sizes scale with 1/L and rates depend on strong monotonicity. A brief derivation or clearer connection to standard results (e.g., via the weighted norm Ω) would improve confidence.
- The cost of computing/maintaining non-uniform sampling probabilities (e.g., row/column norms or element-wise probabilities for K) should be discussed, including preprocessing/storage and impact on overall complexity.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally well organized. The operator-norm scaling via Ω and Ω* is appropriate but could benefit from an early summary table of symbols (λ, γ, µ, L, L̄, T(B), Tprox(M), etc.).
- Clearly state all assumptions per theorem at the point of use; unify symbols for strong monotonicity across sections.
- Algorithm pseudocode is helpful; indicating default mini-batch choices for each setting (bilinear factored vs individual) would aid practitioners.

Originality
- Extending SVRG/SAGA with linear rates to general (non-separable) saddle-point problems and to monotone inclusions without co-coercivity is novel relative to prior variance-reduced methods focused on convex minimization or separable saddle-point settings.
- The “factored” split that does not require each Bi to be a gradient, and the explicit non-uniform sampling analysis for bilinear problems, are original and practically relevant.
- The acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst in this context is a meaningful adaptation with a simpler analysis than Nesterov-type acceleration on the batch side.

Significance
- The results offer linearly convergent, large-scale methods for important classes of non-separable losses/regularizers and robust/structured problems naturally modeled as saddle points, where prior variance-reduced methods do not directly apply.
- The monotone operator view broadens applicability to variational inequalities.
- Empirical results support the theory, particularly the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The experimental scope is somewhat limited (two datasets, mainly bilinear K), but demonstrates practical gains.

Pros for acceptance
- First linear-convergence variance-reduced algorithms for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Solid monotone-operator analysis that relaxes co-coercivity requirements.
- Practical algorithmic contributions: factored splits, importance sampling, mini-batching, and acceleration.
- Empirical improvements over batch/stochastic baselines; clear guidance on when variance reduction and acceleration help.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth: no direct empirical comparison to SPDC/mirror-prox-type methods; few datasets; mostly bilinear K.
- Some notation/clarity issues (µ definition; step-size/rate presentation for FB; complexity constants).
- Practical costs of importance sampling and storage trade-offs (SAGA individual vs factored) could be elaborated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Define µ explicitly and make L vs L̄ roles transparent; add a symbol table.
- Provide a short, self-contained derivation (or intuition) for the stated FB step size and rate under the Ω scaling.
- Include comparisons to extragradient/mirror-prox or SPDC on problems where they apply, even if separability differs, to contextualize performance.
- Report preprocessing costs for non-uniform sampling and memory footprints for SAGA’s storage under each split.
- Add an example with non-bilinear smooth K to illustrate generality.
- Clarify how τ is chosen in practice for acceleration and whether adaptivity is used.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of variance reduction to general saddle-point problems with strong potential impact. With minor clarifications and broader experiments, it meets NeurIPS standards on quality, originality, and significance.
