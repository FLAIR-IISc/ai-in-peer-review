This paper investigates stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems, extending techniques like SVRG and SAGA to this non-separable setting. The motivation is well-articulated: saddle-point formulations arise naturally in duality and are prevalent in machine learning, yet existing accelerated methods often assume separability. The authors provide convergence analysis, identify key characteristics of the saddle-point formulation (different notions of splits, necessity of convex-concave terms, importance of non-uniform sampling), and demonstrate acceleration via a catalyst-like framework.

The paper’s strengths lie in its formalization of existing algorithms for a challenging problem and the theoretical convergence guarantees. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a natural and valuable contribution. The analysis framing the problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator is clever and broadens the applicability of the results beyond purely saddle-point scenarios. The exploration of different splitting strategies and the emphasis on non-uniform sampling are insightful, and the connection to sketches of matrices is relevant for practical applications. The experiments provide some empirical validation, showing gains over baseline methods.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the theoretical analysis, while comprehensive, feels overly focused on mathematical formalism and lacks intuitive explanations. The significance of some theoretical findings is not immediately clear. While the authors establish convergence, the practical implications of the convergence rates, particularly the constants involved, are not thoroughly discussed. The impact of the λ and γ scaling factors is acknowledged as crucial, but a more in-depth analysis of how to choose these parameters effectively would be beneficial. 

Secondly, the experimental section is somewhat limited. The presented results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The comparison to accelerated batch methods feels cursory, and the choice of datasets seems geared towards showcasing the method's strengths rather than providing a robust evaluation across diverse scenarios. The claim of superior performance requires more extensive validation with a wider range of problems and hyperparameter settings. Specifically, the comparison with the primal SAGA on the separable problem is not a fair comparison as the primal problem utilizes the separability. More importantly, the comparison with existing first-order methods such as FISTA is missing.

Thirdly, the discussion of the catalyst-like acceleration, while promising, lacks sufficient detail. The practical implementation and tuning of this acceleration scheme are not clearly explained. The claim of “always superior” performance is a strong one and needs more rigorous justification.

Finally, the reliance on strong convexity-concavity assumptions, although standard, limits the applicability of the methods. The authors acknowledge this and state intent to explore adaptivity in future work, but the current analysis doesn’t address scenarios with weaker convexity assumptions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of established stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems.
*   Solid theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Identification of key properties of saddle-point formulations impacting algorithmic design.
*   Potential for practical impact in various machine learning applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation and potentially biased dataset selection.
*   Theoretical analysis lacks intuitive explanations and discussion of practical implications.
*   The catalyst acceleration scheme requires more detailed justification and implementation guidance.
*   Strong convexity-concavity assumptions limit the scope of applicability.
*   Missing comparison with FISTA.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound contribution to the field of optimization, particularly relevant to machine learning. However, the limitations in experimental validation, lack of practical insights, and strong assumptions prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. The paper is a good, solid piece of work, but doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough. I would rate it as a **weak accept**. Major revisions addressing the experimental validation, clarity of the analysis, and discussion of practical considerations are required before it can be considered a truly impactful contribution to NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to demonstrate broader applicability beyond the carefully chosen datasets and provide more practical guidance on parameter tuning and implementation.
