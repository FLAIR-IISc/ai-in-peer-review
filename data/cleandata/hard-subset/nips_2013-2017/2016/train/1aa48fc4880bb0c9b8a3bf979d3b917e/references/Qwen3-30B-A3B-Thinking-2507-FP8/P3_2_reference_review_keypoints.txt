- Summary: Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems, provides first convergence analysis with linear rates under strong convexity–concavity, extends to monotone operators, and accelerates via catalyst with empirical validation.  
- Theoretical contribution: Correctly frames saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators and provides rigorous convergence analysis distinguishing them from convex minimization.  
- Practical relevance: Addresses non-separable losses/regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, grouped norms) where existing methods fail, showing practical applicability.  
- Algorithmic insight: Identifies importance of factored vs. individual splits and non-uniform sampling; analyzes complexity for different sampling strategies.  
- Experimental validation: Empirically shows benefits, especially on ill-conditioned problems where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch and primal methods.  
- Weakness — strong convexity assumption: Analysis relies on strong convexity–concavity, limiting applicability and needing clearer prominence and discussion.  
- Weakness — baseline comparisons: Lacks sufficient comparison to other stochastic saddle-point methods (e.g., stochastic extra-gradient).  
- Weakness — monotone operator extension: Extension is claimed but not deeply explored or illustrated with concrete new applications beyond saddle-point problems.  
- Weakness — implementation detail gap: Insufficient explanation of how factored splits achieve the stated O(n+d) storage in practice.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-written theoretical contribution with sound analysis and supportive experiments; limitations are acknowledged.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Specific suggestions: Add a deeper discussion of strong convexity implications, compare to stochastic extra-gradient methods, provide a concrete example benefiting from the monotone-operator extension, and clarify factored-split implementation and storage.
