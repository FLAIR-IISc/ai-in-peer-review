This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to saddle-point problems, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating benefits over existing batch algorithms. The core idea – adapting these successful techniques from convex optimization to the saddle-point setting – is promising and potentially impactful. However, the paper suffers from a density of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and overly assertive claims that, collectively, undermine confidence in the presented results.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper correctly identifies the prevalence of saddle-point problems in machine learning, particularly those arising from duality. It leverages the separable structure present in many of these problems, analogous to how SVRG and SAGA exploit separability in convex optimization. The connection to monotone operator theory is a welcome generalization, broadening the applicability of the proposed algorithms. The existing work on batch algorithms for saddle-point problems (Forward-Backward, accelerated variants) is well-summarized. The paper builds upon prior work by providing convergence guarantees tailored to the saddle-point setting, and by exploring non-uniform sampling schemes.

**Strengths:** The theoretical analysis, particularly the convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, is a key strength. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to the saddle-point setting is a logical and potentially fruitful direction. The exploration of different splitting strategies (function-level vs. partial derivative-level) is insightful. The demonstration of acceleration via a catalyst-like framework is a valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:** A significant number of weaknesses permeate the paper.

* **Notational Inconsistencies & Ambiguity:** The notation is occasionally unclear and inconsistent. For instance, the definition of the weighted Euclidean norm Ω and its dual Ω* are presented but not consistently utilized throughout. The definition of L (condition number) feels contrived, and its connection to practical ill-conditioning is not fully elucidated. The various norms of the matrix K (op, F, max) are introduced but their interrelation, while stated (eq. 3), isn't used consistently to simplify the bounds.
* **Overly Assertive Claims:** The authors repeatedly claim superiority without fully justifying it. For example, the statement that the accelerated algorithm is *always* superior to accelerated batch algorithms feels unsupported; it hinges on specific conditions related to L and the cost of proximal operators, which aren’t adequately discussed in the general case. The assertion about the advantage of the factored split for storage is only valid under specific conditions.
* **Lack of Rigor in Experimental Design:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The choice of hyperparameters, particularly the regularization parameter λ, is not fully justified. The comparison to “fba-primal” seems somewhat artificial – it doesn't represent a standard baseline. The justification for using non-uniform sampling consistently (without exploring the sensitivity to this choice) is weak. The claim of improved performance is supported visually but lacks statistical significance analysis.
* **Repetitive Discussion of Complexity:** The discussion of running-time complexities is excessively repetitive and often lacks clarity. The interplay between T(B), T(Bi), and Tprox(M) is not sufficiently emphasized, hindering a practical understanding of the computational trade-offs.
* **Minor Technical Gaps:** The proof sketches mentioned (e.g., in Appendix B.1) are insufficient. The statement about the simplicity of the analysis compared to convex minimization requires further clarification. There is a lack of discussion around the practical challenges of implementing non-uniform sampling, such as maintaining accurate statistics.
* **Redundant Material:** A considerable amount of the introduction and background sections feels verbose and could be condensed.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
* Novel application of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems.
* Sound theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
* Potential for significant performance improvements in specific applications.
* Extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the results.

**Con:**
* Numerous notational inconsistencies and ambiguities.
* Overly assertive claims lacking sufficient justification.
* Weak experimental setup and lack of statistical analysis.
* Repetitive and often unclear discussion of complexity.
* Minor technical gaps and insufficient detail in proofs.
* Overly verbose and redundant presentation.

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the novelty of the approach and the theoretical contributions, the paper’s numerous flaws prevent a positive recommendation. The density of minor issues creates a sense of carelessness and undermines confidence in the results. While the core ideas are valuable, they are obscured by a presentation that lacks precision and rigor.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The paper requires substantial revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the authors must address the notational inconsistencies, provide stronger justification for their claims, strengthen the experimental design, and improve the clarity and conciseness of the presentation. A more careful and critical self-assessment is needed.
