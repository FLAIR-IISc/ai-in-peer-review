### Summary

This paper presents a significant and well-executed extension of stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods to the setting of composite saddle-point problems. The primary motivation is to tackle optimization problems common in machine learning, such as those with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso), which are naturally formulated as saddle-point problems but cannot be directly addressed by existing variance-reduced methods that require separable objectives in their primal or dual forms.

The authors' main contributions are:
1.  The adaptation of SVRG and SAGA algorithms to the saddle-point structure.
2.  A novel convergence analysis framework based on the theory of monotone operators. This is a key insight, as it does not rely on co-coercivity, an assumption that holds for gradient descent in convex minimization but fails for saddle-point problems. This makes the analysis both more general and more appropriate for the problem class.
3.  A thorough investigation of practical and theoretical aspects unique to the saddle-point setting, such as different ways to split the operator ("factored" vs. "individual") and a clear demonstration of the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for achieving state-of-the-art performance.
4.  An acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework, which provably improves the convergence rate and yields the best-performing algorithm in both theory and experiments.

### Review

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs provided in the appendix. The core of the analysis, which frames the problem as finding the zero of a sum of monotone operators, is elegant and powerful. It correctly identifies why a naive extension of existing proofs for convex minimization would fail and provides a sound alternative. The assumptions (A, B, C) are standard for this line of work and are clearly stated.

The experimental evaluation is also strong. The authors don't just test on a standard separable problem where their method is one of many options; they specifically construct a problem (AUC-like loss with a clustering-inducing regularizer) that fits their core motivation—a problem with non-separable components where their method is one of the few applicable linearly convergent algorithms. The results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) over batch and standard stochastic methods. The inclusion of comparisons against primal methods on separable problems (rcv1 dataset) further grounds the results and shows the relative performance trade-offs.

#### Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The problem setup in Section 2 is precise. The progression from batch algorithms to stochastic, SVRG/SAGA, and finally accelerated methods is easy to follow. Table 1 is particularly effective at summarizing the theoretical complexity results and highlighting the improvements offered by the proposed methods.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide more intuition in Section 6 about why the monotone operator framework is necessary and why the co-coercivity assumption, used in analyses of SVRG for convex minimization, does not hold for saddle-point problems. While this is implicitly clear to experts in optimization theory, a brief explanatory sentence or two would make this key theoretical contribution more accessible to a broader machine learning audience.

#### Originality

The originality of this work is high. While the algorithms are extensions of SVRG and SAGA, the contribution is not the algorithmic template but its successful adaptation and, more importantly, the novel analysis required for this new domain. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a linear convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on general composite saddle-point problems.

Previous works on stochastic saddle-point problems typically achieve sublinear O(1/t) rates, while variance-reduction for convex minimization was a major NIPS topic. This paper successfully bridges that gap. The insight to use the monotone operator perspective is a key theoretical innovation that differentiates this work from a simple "application" paper. The analysis of different sampling schemes and factored splits is also a novel contribution specific to the saddle-point structure.

#### Significance

The significance of this paper is substantial. It makes a powerful and popular class of optimization algorithms applicable to a wider range of machine learning problems. As the authors point out, non-separable regularizers and losses are increasingly common in areas like structured prediction, robust optimization, and learning with complex performance measures. This work provides practitioners and researchers in these areas with a theoretically sound and practically efficient tool.

The theoretical framework based on monotone operators may also have a broader impact, potentially inspiring similar analyses for other algorithms or problem structures beyond standard convex minimization. The clear practical gains shown in the experiments will likely encourage the adoption of these methods. This paper advances the state of the art in large-scale optimization and is likely to be frequently cited and built upon.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Addresses a key gap:** Extends the highly successful variance-reduction paradigm from separable convex minimization to the broader and important class of saddle-point problems.
*   **Novel and sound theory:** The convergence analysis via monotone operators is a significant theoretical contribution and is more general than prior analyses for variance-reduced methods.
*   **Practical relevance:** The methods are applicable to important ML problems with non-separable losses/regularizers (e.g., AUC, total variation) where previous fast stochastic methods could not be used.
*   **Comprehensive work:** The paper covers multiple algorithms (SVRG, SAGA), an acceleration scheme, a thorough complexity analysis, and convincing experimental validation.
*   **Strong empirical results:** Experiments clearly show that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, significantly outperform existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The algorithmic templates are direct extensions of existing methods (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst). The primary novelty is in the analysis, which might be perceived by some as an incremental (though technically difficult) step. However, I believe the theoretical barrier overcome here is significant enough to warrant high praise.
*   The paper is theoretically dense, which may limit its immediate accessibility to some practitioners, but this is inherent to the nature of the contribution.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh any minor weaknesses. It is a complete, rigorous, and impactful piece of work that solves an important problem. I strongly recommend acceptance.
