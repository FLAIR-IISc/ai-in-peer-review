This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to saddle-point problems, provides convergence analysis, and explores the impact of different splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling. While the core idea of extending these methods is natural, and the convergence analysis represents a contribution, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that collectively detract from its overall quality.

**Summary:** The authors correctly identify the prevalence of saddle-point problems in machine learning, particularly in scenarios involving duality. They demonstrate how SVRG and SAGA can be adapted to handle these problems and provide theoretical guarantees on their convergence. They also explore the impact of different splitting strategies for the gradient computation (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) and emphasize the importance of non-uniform sampling. The work is positioned within the context of related optimization algorithms, including accelerated forward-backward methods and existing stochastic algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems is a genuine contribution, as such analysis was previously lacking. The connection to monotone operators is also a positive aspect, broadening the applicability of the results.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The identified problem of saddle-point optimization is highly relevant to machine learning.
*   **Exploration of Splitting Strategies:** The discussion of different splitting strategies (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) offers valuable insights.
*   **Emphasis on Non-Uniform Sampling:** The emphasis on the importance of non-uniform sampling is well-justified and supported by the analysis.
*   **Extension to monotone operators:** the paper clearly states that the result extends to a general framework, which is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Detailed and Redundant Presentation:** The paper is excessively long and contains substantial redundancy. The same concepts are reiterated multiple times in different sections, adding to the length without providing new information. For example, the discussion of existing batch algorithms is extremely detailed and could be significantly condensed.
*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. The definition of `L` (Lipschitz constant) shifts subtly between sections, which is confusing. The use of different norms (e.g., ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) without clear justification for their selection in specific contexts is problematic. The meaning of 'I' is also not consistent throughout.
*   **Minor Technical Errors and Imprecisions:** Several minor technical errors and imprecisions are present. For example, the assertion about the complexity of the accelerated SVRG algorithm needing a precise trade-off in τ seems overly strong without further justification. The connection between convex minimization and the saddle-point formulation through duality feels glossed over. A statement is made without backing that the initialization at (0, 0) has zero cost for bilinear saddle-point problems, this is not correct and could mislead the reader.
*   **Experiments Lack Rigor:** The experimental setup, while providing some supporting evidence, lacks sufficient rigor. The comparisons to other algorithms are not comprehensive, and the choice of datasets and parameters seems somewhat arbitrary. The analysis of the results is superficial. More detailed reporting of experimental settings and error bars is needed.
*    **Formatting and Presentation:** The paper suffers from poor formatting. Equations are not consistently numbered. The use of bullet points is excessive and disrupts the flow of the text. The layout overall lacks polish.
*   **Lack of discussion of practical implementation:** It’s not clear how to choose the probabilities (πi) in practice for non-uniform sampling, and the paper does not provide any guidance on this.
*   **Unnecessary complexity:** The introduction of the catalyst framework in Section 5 feels tacked on and adds complexity without a substantial payoff. The conditions for improvement are very specific and might not hold in many practical scenarios.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The theoretical contribution regarding the convergence of SVRG/SAGA for saddle-point problems is valuable.
*   The analysis extends to a more general monotone operator setting.

**Con:**

*   The paper is excessively long, redundant, and lacks clarity.
*   The numerous minor technical flaws and inconsistencies undermine the credibility of the work.
*   The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous.
*   The formatting and presentation are subpar.
*   The theoretical results are buried under a lot of details.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the theoretical contribution, the paper’s numerous flaws make it unsuitable for acceptance in its current form. The excessive length, notational inconsistencies, minor technical errors, and lack of experimental rigor detract significantly from its overall quality. While the authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the underlying theory, the presentation is disorganized and confusing. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, focusing on clarity, conciseness, and accuracy. A more thorough experimental evaluation is also crucial. The paper has potential, but requires substantial work to meet NIPS standards. Specifically, the authors should carefully review the notation, streamline the presentation, address the technical errors, and provide a more comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation. They should also consider focusing on the most important contributions and removing the less essential details.
