

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context. The authors demonstrate how these methods can be adapted to handle saddle-point problems with separable structures, which naturally arise in many machine learning applications through duality. The work builds on the rich literature of variance reduction methods for convex optimization (SAGA, SVRG, etc.) but extends them to a more general setting where the objective function is not necessarily separable in the primal form.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in saddle-point problems. The analysis is fundamentally different from convex minimization due to the nature of saddle-point problems, and the authors successfully leverage monotone operator theory to establish linear convergence rates. This is a clear advancement over existing work.

2. **Practical Relevance:** The paper identifies several important machine learning applications where saddle-point formulations with non-separable components arise (e.g., robust optimization, non-separable regularizers, structured output prediction). The authors provide a compelling case for why their methods are necessary when existing approaches fail.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper thoroughly examines different split structures (factored vs. individual), sampling schemes (uniform vs. non-uniform), and acceleration techniques. The theoretical complexity analysis is detailed and provides clear insights into when each method is most effective.

4. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real machine learning problems (including a non-separable ROC-based loss and a clustering-inducing regularizer) convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods over batch algorithms and uniform sampling variants.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** The convergence analysis relies on the strong convexity-concavity assumption, which might be restrictive for some applications. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide a clear path forward for handling non-strongly convex-concave problems, which would significantly broaden the applicability.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Saddle-Point Methods:** The paper could better position itself against other recent advances in saddle-point optimization, such as those in the context of generative adversarial networks (GANs) or distributed saddle-point problems. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods for saddle-point problems would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Implementation Details:** The experimental section would benefit from more details on how the Lipschitz constants were estimated in practice, as this is often challenging in real-world applications. The authors mention that "L² is too expensive to compute" but don't provide practical alternatives.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point problems
- Theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods
- Clear identification of important machine learning applications
- Strong experimental validation across multiple problem settings

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Strong convexity-concavity assumption may limit applicability
- Could better position against recent saddle-point optimization literature
- Needs more discussion on practical implementation challenges

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. The authors should explore how to extend their methods to problems without strong convexity-concavity assumptions, perhaps by adapting the "catalyst" framework to handle non-strongly convex settings as in recent convex optimization work.

2. Include a more detailed comparison with recent saddle-point optimization methods, particularly those designed for non-smooth or non-convex problems that have appeared in NIPS/ICML in the last two years.

3. Add a subsection discussing practical implementation details, such as how to estimate the Lipschitz constants in real-world applications, and provide guidance on choosing sampling probabilities when theoretical bounds are not available.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the proposed methods might apply to distributed or decentralized optimization settings, which is an active area of research with strong relevance to saddle-point problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems with rigorous convergence guarantees. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical benefits. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, the authors are transparent about this and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work that might address this limitation. The paper meets the high standards of NIPS with its theoretical novelty, clear exposition, and experimental validation. The significance of the work is demonstrated through its application to important machine learning problems that were previously intractable with existing large-scale optimization methods.

I recommend acceptance, as this work advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning and provides a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with saddle-point problems. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be even stronger.
