Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with a proximal operator and K is smooth. The main contributions are:
- Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems by operating on the monotone operator B(x,y)=(∂_xK, −∂_yK) decomposed as B=∑_i B_i, without requiring each B_i to be a gradient (allowing “factored” splits across x- and y-components).
- Linear convergence analyses that rely only on monotonicity and Lipschitzness (not co-coercivity), with nonuniform sampling, mini-batches, and per-iteration complexities; bilinear problems receive refined bounds using ‖K‖_F and row/column norms.
- A catalyst-style acceleration tailored to saddle-point problems by adding a strongly monotone regularizer around (x̄,ȳ), yielding an overall complexity scaling like L̄√(T(B) max_i T(B_i)) (ignoring prox costs), and an extension to maximal monotone inclusions A+B.
- Experiments showing SAGA/SVRG (especially accelerated SVRG) can outperform batch FB/accelerated batch methods; uniform sampling underperforms.

This builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA (Le Roux et al., Johnson & Zhang, Defazio et al.), primal–dual/FB/accelerated methods (Chambolle–Pock; Rockafellar; Beck–Teboulle; Chen–Rockafellar), and stochastic PD/coordinate methods for separable saddle points (Zhu & Storkey; Zhang & Xiao). It contrasts with SMART (Davis) and co-coercive analyses by Zhu & Marcotte, claiming broader applicability to monotone operators. The nonuniform sampling echoes importance-sampling variants (Schmidt et al., Harikandeh et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean operator view that decouples “forward” smooth parts from a single “backward” prox, enabling SAGA/SVRG beyond gradient-sum settings.
- Allowing non-gradient B_i and “factored” splits is practically relevant for bilinear K with row/column sketches and for memory-efficient SAGA state.
- Nonuniform sampling is carefully integrated with complexity constants L̄(π) and sensible choices for bilinear K.
- The catalyst variant is natural in saddle-point/operator terms (prox-point view), avoids Nesterov machinery, and yields improved complexities in regimes where T(B_i)≪T(B).
- Experiments include a genuinely nonseparable regularizer with efficient prox and suggest practical gains of accelerated SVRG in ill-conditioned regimes.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness/notation
- Inconsistent use of L vs L^2 throughout. FB step-size is given as σ=1/L^2 with rate (1−1/(1+L^2))^t, but the accelerated FB uses σ=1/(2L) and rate (1−1/(1+2L))^t. Please define precisely whether L is a Lipschitz constant or its square in each bound; the current mix appears dimensionally inconsistent.
- Theorem 2 includes terms 1 + L^2/μ^2 + 3 L̄^2/(m μ^2), but μ is never defined in the main text (later you write “assume λ=γ=μ”). If μ is the strong monotonicity parameter (min{λ,γ} in your weighted norm), state this clearly and propagate it consistently.
- The SAGA rate bound uses an expression like max{3|I|2m, …}; likely 3|I|/(2m) or (3|I|)/(2m). As written it is ambiguous and likely incorrect.
- The claim “forward–backward algorithm … is known not to converge in general, but is linearly convergent for strongly-convex–concave problems, when σ=1/L^2” needs a precise reference and a clean derivation in your chosen weighted norm. Standard FB for A maximal monotone, B Lipschitz monotone, with A strongly monotone, admits σ<2α/L^2 (α strong monotonicity), or σ<1/L if B is cocoercive, etc. Please align your constants with a standard statement (e.g., Bauschke–Combettes).
- Catalyst section: the rate (1−1/(1+τ))^v is asserted based on prox-point iteration. The precise contraction factor should depend on the strong monotonicity μ and the added τ; show the contraction in Ω-norm explicitly and clarify the log(1+τ) epoch-swap schedule. The statement “always superior to accelerated batch” is too strong without prox costs and constants carefully handled.
- Extension to monotone operators: you claim to require only monotonicity of B (Bi need not be monotone), but Theorem statements and step-sizes rely on Lipschitz constants of Bi and strong monotonicity of A. Spell out exact assumptions (A strongly monotone with resolvent; B monotone and L-Lipschitz) and list step-size restrictions. As written, the monotone operator extension is promising but under-specified.

Practicality/assumptions
- Algorithms assume knowledge of L and L̄(π); in practice these are unknown or expensive. You briefly say to replace L^2 by L̄^2 “if L^2 is too expensive to compute,” but L̄ is also unknown. Provide adaptive step-size or line-search strategies, or empirical guidance beyond theory values.
- Nonuniform sampling: computing p_j ∝ ‖K_j·‖^2 and q_k ∝ ‖K_·k‖^2 is reasonable for sparse K, but the paper should discuss cost for dense K and general nonlinear K. For individual splits, π_jk ∝ K_jk^2 may be expensive; also, the need for large mini-batches (m=n+d) to match factored complexity seems impractical.
- SAGA requires storing and updating g_i. While bilinear with (0,0) avoids an O(|I|) initialization, general K(x,y) will incur this cost; discuss initialization strategies and memory/runtime trade-offs for non-bilinear cases.

Experiments
- Only two datasets and limited baselines. No wall-clock times, only “passes,” despite quite different per-iteration costs across methods. No ablation on factored vs individual splits, mini-batch size m, or the effect of resampling (which your theorem requires but experiments omit). No tests on variational inequalities/the monotone operator setting despite being a main claim.
- Step-size/probability choices: unclear whether theoretical step-sizes are used or tuned. Mixtures of uniform/nonuniform sampling are used but not specified quantitatively; this weakens the link to theory.
- Figures lack axes labels/units here; Table 1 (central to your complexity claims) is referenced but not included in the main text we received.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational/typographical issues hinder readability: e.g., “does need splits into convex-concave terms” presumably “does not need”; frequent ambiguous “L2/L̄2” vs “L^2/ L̄^2”; missing definition of μ; inconsistent spacing (γ −1), and using “sup” where “max” is intended on finite index sets.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages) and should be standardized; add missing classic related work (e.g., Mirror-Prox for saddle-point/variational inequalities).
- Provide precise statements of all assumptions and constants in theorems in the main text; currently, many key details are deferred to appendices and inconsistent in the body.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and useful operator-centric extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point settings with non-gradient splits.
- Nonuniform sampling and factored splits are well-motivated for bilinear problems and likely impactful.
- Acceleration via a prox-point/catalyst perspective is elegant and potentially beneficial.

Cons for acceptance
- Multiple notational inconsistencies and unclear constants (L vs L^2; μ undefined) that cast doubt on the correctness/applicability of the stated rates.
- Important claims (monotone operator extension; “always superior” acceleration) are under-qualified.
- Experimental validation is limited and does not cover key claims (monotone operators), lacks wall-clock comparisons and ablations, and deviates from theoretical requirements (resampling).
- Presentation issues (missing Table 1, incomplete references, typos) and reproducibility concerns (unknown step-sizes).

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The ideas are promising and potentially significant, but the paper needs substantial revisions to fix notation/constants, precisely state and prove results, qualify claims, and strengthen/clarify experiments.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clean up all constants and define μ; reconcile FB step-sizes/rates with standard results.
- Include full, consistent statements of Theorems with assumptions and step-size ranges in the main text.
- Provide adaptive step-size heuristics and discuss practical computation of sampling probabilities.
- Add wall-clock experiments, ablations (factored vs individual, m, resampling), and at least one variational inequality example.
- Fix typos, the “does not need” sentence, include Table 1, and complete references.
