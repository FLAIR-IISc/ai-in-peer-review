- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Develops a novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, more general than standard convex optimization analysis.  
- Identifies two splitting strategies for saddle-point problems: function-wise vs. derivative-wise.  
- Shows the theoretical and empirical importance of non-uniform sampling for these algorithms.  
- Extends the methods to accelerated versions using the "catalyst" framework.  
- Technically sound work with experiments that demonstrate clear practical advantages, especially for non-separable problems.  
- Strength: proven linear convergence for the new stochastic algorithms on saddle-point problems.  
- Strength: insightful distinction between saddle-point and convex minimization analyses and splitting choices.  
- Strength: practical handling of non-separable losses and regularizers relevant to applications like robust optimization and structured prediction.  
- Strength: clear experimental results and reproducible experimental details with thorough comparisons.  
- Strength: extension to variational inequalities broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems.  
- Weakness: notational confusion using K for both design matrix and function could reduce clarity.  
- Weakness: acceleration section lacks guidance on how to adaptively choose intervals for updating the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ).  
- Weakness: theoretical extension from SVRG to SAGA is stated but not fully justified; convergence-rate clarification for SAGA is needed.  
- Overall assessment: well-written, significant contribution, within NIPS scope, and recommended for acceptance.
