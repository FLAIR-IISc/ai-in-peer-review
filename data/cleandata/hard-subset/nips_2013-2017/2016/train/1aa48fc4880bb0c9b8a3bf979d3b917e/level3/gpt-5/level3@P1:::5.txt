Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems with smooth K and proximable, strongly convex–concave M, and proposes stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG and SAGA variants) that achieve linear convergence while exploiting finite-sum structure in K/B. The key novelty is not the algorithmic templates themselves—which mirror the standard SVRG/SAGA updates—but a new convergence analysis that treats saddle-point optimization as finding a zero of a sum of monotone operators A + B. This perspective avoids co-coercivity assumptions used in convex minimization analyses and yields the first linearly convergent, large-scale incremental methods applicable to general convex–concave saddle points and, more broadly, to variational inequalities.

Compared to batch primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock/PDHG) that are linearly convergent under strong convexity-concavity but require full gradients, the proposed methods attain linear rates with per-iteration costs that can scale with mini-batches. Relative to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC and related work), the proposed methods do not require separability of the loss/regularizer and handle more general splits, including “factored” splits adapted to the saddle structure. The paper also extends “catalyst” acceleration to this setting via a proximal-point viewpoint, yielding complexities superior to accelerated batch in theory.

On the applied side, the paper highlights machine learning problems where non-separable losses or regularizers arise (e.g., AUC surrogates, group/TV norms, robust optimization), for which standard finite-sum acceleration in the primal or dual is not applicable, thereby motivating the new algorithms.

Technical contributions
- Monotone-operator analysis: Linear convergence of SVRG/SAGA-like forward–backward schemes for strongly monotone A and Lipschitz B = ∑Bi, without requiring co-coercivity of Bi. This extends to variational inequalities.
- Splitting choices: Two notions of splits are formalized—by functions or by partial derivatives—with a special “factored” split for bilinear K that reduces storage for SAGA.
- Importance of convex–concave consistency in splits: Unlike convex minimization, the decomposition must preserve convex–concave structure.
- Non-uniform sampling: A general L̄(π) constant controls rates. For bilinear K, the paper derives informative bounds tied to ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and ‖K‖max, showing provable gains of non-uniform over uniform sampling.
- Acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst: Adding quadratic regularization around an extrapolated point and approximately solving the subproblem with SVRG results in improved iteration complexity; the analysis is notably simpler than Nesterov-style acceleration.

Quality
The theoretical results are carefully stated with explicit step sizes and complexities, and they reconcile well with known batch/stochastic limits. The monotone-operator framing is technically sound and broadens applicability; the avoidance of co-coercivity is a genuine advance over analyses tailored strictly to convex minimization. The experimental section, while focused, demonstrates the practical benefits on problems with non-separable components and validates the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. I would encourage including a high-level proof sketch in the main text to make the key ideas behind the monotone-operator convergence more accessible (e.g., Lyapunov function, contractive operator argument, role of σ and L̄).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The definitions of L and L̄(π) are clear, and the bilinear case helps ground the theory. A few points would benefit from clarification:
- Beyond bilinear K, how should practitioners choose π? Is π ∝ Li or π ∝ Ω∗-Lipschitz constants recommended? Heuristics or an adaptive scheme would be helpful.
- Step size selection in experiments: were σ and epoch lengths set exactly per theory, or tuned? Please detail any tuning and sensitivity.
- Provide more intuition for why non-uniform sampling has a larger impact here than in convex minimization, ideally by contrasting L̄(π) gaps in the bilinear case.

Originality
While the algorithmic forms are natural extensions of SVRG/SAGA, the analysis via general monotone operators (without co-coercivity) for saddle points and variational inequalities is original and impactful. The factored split, storage-saving SAGA variant, and the proximal-point/catalyst acceleration specialized to saddle points are also novel in this combination.

Significance
The work fills a clear gap: linearly convergent, incremental methods for non-separable convex–concave saddle-point problems common in ML. The theory suggests consistent advantages over batch methods and over uniform sampling; the experiments corroborate this, particularly the superiority of accelerated SVRG on ill-conditioned problems. The monotone-operator perspective may catalyze further advances for stochastic algorithms on variational inequalities and games.

Strengths
- First linear-convergence results for SVRG/SAGA-type methods on general convex–concave saddle points via a unified monotone-operator analysis.
- Clear complexity improvements with interpretable constants; strong case for non-uniform sampling.
- Practical acceleration scheme with simple implementation and theory.
- Relevant ML applications where separability is absent; meaningful experiments.

Weaknesses / questions for authors
- Guidance for π in general settings beyond bilinear K is limited. Please provide a principled recipe (e.g., estimate Li online; hybrid uniform/non-uniform) and discuss computational overhead.
- Experimental details on step size σ, epoch length, and τ for acceleration are sparse. How sensitive are results to these choices? Any automatic tuning?
- Provide ablations: effect of mini-batch size m; storage vs. speed trade-offs for SAGA factored vs. individual splits; comparisons to stochastic PDHG variants and SPDC (where applicable).
- Clarify the requirement that splits respect convex–concave structure; give examples of invalid splits and their failure modes.
- The main novelty is analytical; a short proof sketch would improve accessibility.

Minor comments and typos
- Please correct: line 78 (k -> d) and line 121 (extra y_{t-1} term in second equality).
- Notation: explicitly define µ when switching to the monotone-operator section; ensure consistency of λ, γ in norms and proximal definitions.
- State storage costs for SAGA precisely in the factored case and outline the update operations.

Reproducibility
The algorithms are described with sufficient detail to implement. Please consider releasing code and including all hyperparameters used in experiments.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contribution with broad applicability (saddle points and variational inequalities).
- Clear empirical gains on relevant non-separable ML tasks.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with principled acceleration.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited guidance for non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear cases.
- Experimental section could be broader (more datasets/ablations/competitors) and more detailed on tuning.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper provides a substantial and timely contribution with both theoretical depth and practical relevance, and it is likely to influence subsequent work on stochastic methods for saddle-point and monotone-operator problems.
