- Main contribution: Extends SVRG/SAGA variance-reduction to convex–concave saddle-point problems and general monotone inclusions with linear convergence.  
- Analysis framework: Recasts analysis in the monotone operator framework, avoiding co-coercivity assumptions.  
- Splitting strategies: Introduces and analyzes two splits of the smooth part K (function-based individual vs derivative-based factored) and their effect on storage and complexity.  
- Sampling: Demonstrates the importance of non-uniform sampling, with tighter bounds via L̄(π) and empirical benefits.  
- Acceleration: Incorporates Catalyst-style proximal regularization for acceleration, improving iteration complexity and empirical performance.  
- Theory quality: Convergence analysis is careful, technically correct, and explains why co-coercivity-based step sizes fail for saddle points.  
- Technical clarity: Role of weighted norm Ω, Lipschitz constant L, and sampling-dependent L̄(π) is clearly articulated.  
- Algorithm practicality: SVRG and SAGA variants are natural and implementable; factored split reduces SAGA storage from O(nd) to O(n + d).  
- Complexity results: Bounds improve over batch FB and uniform sampling; dependence on ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and ‖K‖max is informative.  
- Acceleration trade-offs: Acceleration yields the expected L̄√(T(B) max T(Bi)) trade-off.  
- Experiments: Numerical results support main claims (non-uniform sampling matters, acceleration helps, SVRG-accel/SAGA outperform baselines) but are limited in scope.  
- Clarity and presentation: Paper is well written; presentation of splits and practical implications is clear and useful.  
- Suggested additions: Add a concise figure/table contrasting storage/compute of SVRG vs SAGA for both splits and a practical recipe for choosing sampling probabilities.  
- Originality: Novel extension of variance reduction to saddle points via monotone-operator analysis and non-uniform sampling; Catalyst adaptation is noteworthy.  
- Significance: Relevant to many ML problems formulated as non-separable saddle points; methods can change practice when batch methods are too slow.  
- Key weakness — assumptions: Requiring strong convexity–concavity on both sides is restrictive; need discussion or results for one-sided or no strong convexity.  
- Key weakness — comparisons: Experimental baselines should include accelerated Chambolle–Pock, Mirror-Prox, and SPDC, with wall-clock timings and step-size sensitivity.  
- Key weakness — experimental scope: Need more datasets (dense/sparse, varying n/d/conditioning), non-separable regularizers, and ablations (mini-batch m, resampling vs reuse).  
- Practical concerns: Discuss cost/approximations for computing non-uniform sampling probabilities and when factored split is applicable given SAGA’s O(nd) storage.  
- Parameter guidance: Provide heuristics for choosing m and inner iteration counts in SVRG without explicit L or L̄ computations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: First linear-rate variance-reduced methods for general composite saddle-point problems, meaningful Catalyst acceleration, careful sampling treatment, extension to monotone inclusions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Two-sided strong-convexity requirement limits applicability, narrow experimental evaluation, and underexplored practical costs.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong theoretical contribution with practical promise; recommend acceptance pending broader experiments and discussion/partial results on one-sided strong convexity.
