This paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, allowing two natural split types (individual and factored). The analysis departs from the convex minimization playbook by working through monotone operators, yielding linear rates and a clean unification that also covers variational inequalities. The bilinear case is treated in depth with uniform vs. nonuniform sampling, producing complexity bounds that mirror familiar convex-minimization intuitions. Experiments on binary classification indicate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are necessary to outperform batch forward–backward (FB).

Quality: The technical development is careful and largely sound. The monotone-operator framework is appropriate and clarifies why co-coercivity-based analyses do not apply. The complexity bounds are thorough across splits and sampling schemes. However, a key practical bottleneck remains: each incremental step pays O(n + d) for the proximal operator, which can dominate the cost and blunt the theoretical gains. As a result, even accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling may not improve over accelerated batch FB, and factored splits can be worse when n ≫ d. Please expand the discussion of (i) whether factored splits are intrinsically worse than individual splits in theory (beyond storage), (ii) precise regimes where accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB given prox cost, and (iii) whether the n, d dependence in your rates is tight. It would also help to discuss options to mitigate prox cost (e.g., block/coordinate-friendly prox, inexact prox with controlled errors, or problem reformulations).

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized; assumptions and constants are clearly defined. A more explicit guidance section summarizing when to choose individual vs. factored splits and how to set nonuniform sampling would benefit practitioners.

Originality: The extension to saddle points with linear convergence and the Catalyst adaptation are novel and technically non-trivial, with clear distinctions from prior NIPS work on SAG/SVRG/SAGA and primal–dual methods.

Significance: If the proximal bottleneck can be alleviated, the methods could be broadly useful for non-separable losses/regularizers. The experimental evidence is promising but limited; additional benchmarks (e.g., larger bilinear games, robust optimization) would strengthen impact.

Pros:
- Novel variance-reduced and accelerated methods for saddle points with linear rates
- Monotone-operator analysis of independent interest
- Sharp comparisons for bilinear cases; nonuniform sampling insights

Cons:
- O(n + d) proximal bottleneck per iteration
- Factored splits may underperform when n ≫ d
- Limited empirical breadth

Post-rebuttal: The authors addressed the three requested discussion points satisfactorily; I appreciate the clarifications, though my overall assessment remains unchanged.
