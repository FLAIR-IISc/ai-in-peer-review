This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated variants) to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The authors address a gap in the literature by providing algorithms and complexity analysis for these problems, which differ significantly from standard convex minimization. They identify two distinct notions of ‘splits’ (functions vs. partial derivatives) and demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The work builds upon existing literature in stochastic optimization (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) and extends it to the saddle-point setting, also drawing connections to monotone operator theory [8].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, especially with the detailed analysis of different split types, represents a genuine contribution. The connection to monotone operator theory provides a broader theoretical foundation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with clear convergence results presented. The extension of the catalyst framework ([7]) is a valuable addition. The detailed complexity analysis under different sampling strategies is commendable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define the problem setting and assumptions. The distinction between individual and factored splits is well explained.
* **Significance:** The results address a practically relevant problem in machine learning, where saddle-point formulations arise frequently (e.g., in regularized learning with non-separable losses/regularizers – see Section 2.1). The empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.
* **Comparison to Batch Methods:** The clear comparisons to batch forward-backward algorithms and accelerated variants provide a useful benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Bottleneck:** The primary weakness, as highlighted by the authors themselves, is the O(n + d) cost per incremental iteration due to the proximal operator computation. This significantly impacts the overall complexity and potentially negates the benefits of acceleration.
* **Complexity Concerns:** The dependence on both *n* and *d* in the computational cost is a significant limitation, particularly when *n >> d*. The claim that factored splits can *worsen* performance in this case is a critical issue.
* **Unresolved Questions:** The reviewer rightly points out three key areas requiring further discussion: (i) the theoretical comparison between factored and individual splits, (ii) conditions under which accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) the tightness of the *n*, *d* dependence. While the authors provided a rebuttal, the scores remained unchanged, suggesting the concerns weren’t fully addressed.



**Addressing the Reviewer’s Concerns (and further elaboration):**

**(i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits?** The paper acknowledges this possibility when *n >> d*, but a more thorough theoretical justification would be beneficial. The factored split introduces additional storage and potentially more complex calculations within each iteration, which the analysis needs to quantify precisely. A more detailed analysis of the constants hidden within the asymptotic complexity would be helpful.

**(ii) When does accelerated SVRG outperform batch FB?** The paper states that accelerated SVRG is *always* superior to accelerated batch FB. This claim seems strong given the O(n+d) cost. A more nuanced discussion is required, outlining the conditions under which the variance reduction benefits outweigh the proximal operator cost. Specifically, how does the condition number (L) and the dimensions *n* and *d* interact to determine which algorithm is faster?

**(iii) Is the *n*, *d* dependence tight?** The O(n + d) cost of the proximal operator is a major bottleneck. Demonstrating the tightness of this dependence (i.e., proving that it’s impossible to compute the proximal operator faster) would strengthen the paper significantly. Are there specific problem structures where this dependence could be reduced?


**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound extension of SVRG/SAGA.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning.
* Comprehensive complexity analysis.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.

**Con:**

* The O(n + d) cost of the proximal operator is a significant limitation.
* The potential for factored splits to perform worse than individual splits is concerning.
* The reviewer’s questions regarding the performance comparison with batch methods and the tightness of the complexity bounds were not fully resolved.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant achievement. However, the computational bottleneck associated with the proximal operator and the unanswered questions raised by the reviewer prevent a truly enthusiastic recommendation. While the authors’ response to the rebuttal was appreciated, it didn't fully alleviate the concerns. 

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed theoretical justification for why factored splits can be worse than individual splits when *n >> d*.
2.  Offer a more nuanced discussion of the conditions under which accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, considering the trade-off between variance reduction and the proximal operator cost.
3.  Discuss the potential for tighter bounds on the complexity, or at least provide arguments supporting the tightness of the current O(n + d) dependence.
