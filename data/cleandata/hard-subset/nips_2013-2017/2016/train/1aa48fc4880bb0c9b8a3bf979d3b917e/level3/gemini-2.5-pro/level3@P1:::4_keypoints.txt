- First to provide linearly convergent, large-scale stochastic algorithms (SVRG/SAGA variants) for strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems.  
- Novel convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory, appropriate and more general than convex-minimization proofs.  
- Proposes two gradient splitting schemes ("element-wise" and "factored") to handle non-separable objectives.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is critical for optimal theoretical rates and practical performance.  
- Extends the Catalyst acceleration framework to further improve convergence rates.  
- High significance and originality: fills an important gap enabling fast methods for many ML problems with non-separable losses/regularizers.  
- Strong technical quality and rigor: sound theoretical proofs and appropriate use of monotone operator framework.  
- Comprehensive treatment: analyzes both SVRG and SAGA, sampling strategies, split choices, and includes an accelerated variant.  
- Strong empirical validation: experiments on sido and rcv1 show non-uniform sampling helps, proposed methods beat baselines, and accelerated SVRG excels in ill-conditioned settings.  
- Weakness: vague discussion of "more aggressive" adaptive updates in Catalyst — needs concrete heuristics or formal rules for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: acceleration is proven for SVRG but not for SAGA; either a proof or detailed justification for SAGA acceleration is missing.  
- Weakness: notational ambiguity using K for both the smooth function and a data/design matrix in examples — recommend distinct symbols.  
- Overall assessment: excellent, timely, technically sound paper with strong empirical support; minor clarifications needed; strongly recommend acceptance.
