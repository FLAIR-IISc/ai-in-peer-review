- First extension of SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory as a key technical tool, generalizing analysis to variational inequalities beyond saddle points.  
- Identifies two natural split types for saddle-point problems (by functions or partial derivatives), distinct from convex minimization.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is critical for efficiency, supported by theoretical and empirical results.  
- Extends the catalyst acceleration framework to these methods and claims the accelerated method outperforms accelerated batch algorithms.  
- Provides a thorough complexity analysis with clear comparisons across split types, sampling strategies, and problem structures.  
- Experimental results on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers support benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and connects appropriately to prior work.  
- Major practical weakness: each incremental iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, creating a bottleneck that may negate practical advantages.  
- Insufficient discussion of when the proposed methods actually outperform batch algorithms, especially in the common regime n >> d.  
- Analysis of factored splits is incomplete; claimed advantages for large n and d are not convincingly shown when n >> d.  
- Unclear whether the n,d dependence in the complexity bounds is tight; experimental results do not consistently demonstrate the claimed improvements for n >> d.  
- Overall: important theoretical contribution but limited practical significance without addressing proximal-cost bottleneck and clearer trade-off analysis; recommendation: Weak Accept (major revisions).
