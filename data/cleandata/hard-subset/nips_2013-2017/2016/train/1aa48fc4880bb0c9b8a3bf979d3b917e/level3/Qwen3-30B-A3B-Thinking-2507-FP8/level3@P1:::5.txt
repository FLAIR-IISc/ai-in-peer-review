

This paper proposes the first linearly convergent stochastic variance reduction algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending SVRG and SAGA to this class of problems. The authors make a significant contribution by providing a convergence proof using monotone operator theory, which differs substantially from the convex minimization analysis used in prior work. This approach is particularly valuable as it generalizes beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, broadening the applicability of these algorithms.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical novelty. While the algorithmic extensions themselves are straightforward (SVRG/SAGA applied to saddle-point problems), the convergence analysis using monotone operators is a key insight that enables the first linear convergence guarantees for this problem class. The authors demonstrate that these algorithms can handle non-separable losses and regularizers—common in machine learning—where previous methods fail. The paper correctly identifies that saddle-point problems require different treatment than convex minimization, particularly regarding the need for convex-concave splits and the critical role of non-uniform sampling.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many important machine learning problems naturally formulate as saddle-point problems (supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, convex relaxations of unsupervised learning), yet no efficient large-scale algorithms existed for these problems. The authors establish that their algorithms provide both theoretical and practical advantages over existing batch methods. The experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that non-uniform sampling significantly outperforms uniform sampling and batch methods, with accelerated variants showing particularly strong results on ill-conditioned problems.

The paper excels in addressing the quality criterion. The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful attention to the differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization. The authors provide clear definitions of the problem setup, make appropriate assumptions, and prove convergence under these conditions. The experiments are well-designed and effectively support the theoretical claims, with the right comparison points to demonstrate the advantages of their approach.

For clarity, the paper is generally well-written and logically structured, though some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations. The key insight about using monotone operators for the convergence proof is somewhat buried in the technical details, and a more concise presentation of this novel proof technique would be helpful. The authors do provide a useful connection to existing work on monotone operators and variational inequalities, showing how their approach generalizes beyond saddle-point problems.

The originality of the work is strong. While the algorithms themselves are extensions of existing techniques, the application to saddle-point problems with the novel convergence analysis represents a significant advance. The paper carefully distinguishes its contribution from prior work on convex minimization and saddle-point problems, and the references to recent NIPS papers show awareness of related work.

The paper could be strengthened by addressing several points. First, the explanation of the non-uniform sampling strategy beyond bilinear problems is insufficient. The authors mention πjk = K²jk/‖K‖²F for bilinear problems but don't explain how to choose π for general saddle-point problems. Second, the choice of step size σ in experiments lacks justification—readers would benefit from more insight into how σ was selected and why non-uniform sampling is more crucial for saddle-point problems than for convex minimization. Finally, minor typos (e.g., line 78: "k" instead of "d", line 121: extra y_{t-1} term) should be corrected.

The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of this work. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning, yet no efficient large-scale linearly convergent algorithms existed before this work. The authors demonstrate that their approach provides both theoretical guarantees and practical advantages over existing methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Pro: First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems with rigorous convergence proof using monotone operators
Pro: Handles non-separable losses and regularizers that existing methods cannot address
Pro: Demonstrates practical advantages through experiments on real ML problems
Pro: Generalizes to variational inequalities, broadening applicability
Con: Insufficient explanation of non-uniform sampling strategy beyond bilinear problems
Con: Limited justification for step size selection in experiments
Con: Some technical details could be presented more clearly

This paper represents a significant advance in optimization for machine learning, providing both theoretical and practical solutions to a class of problems that had previously lacked efficient algorithms. The use of monotone operator theory for the convergence proof is particularly elegant and broadly applicable. With minor clarifications on the sampling strategy and step size selection, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The work is of high quality, original, and significant, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
