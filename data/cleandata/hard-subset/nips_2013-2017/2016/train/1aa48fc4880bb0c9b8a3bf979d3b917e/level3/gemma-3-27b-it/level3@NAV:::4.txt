## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper tackles the important problem of efficiently solving strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, using stochastic variance reduction techniques. The authors extend SVRG and SAGA to this setting, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale instances. This is a significant contribution, as standard convex minimization analyses do not directly apply to saddle-point formulations. The work is well-motivated, clearly explaining the challenges and opportunities presented by this extension, and highlighting applications in areas like robust optimization and problems with non-separable regularizers.

A key strength lies in the comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors cleverly leverage the framework of monotone operators to prove convergence, demonstrating broader applicability beyond just saddle-point problems, extending to variational inequalities.  They also meticulously consider the nuances of gradient-operator splits, proposing both element-wise and factored approaches tailored to SVRG and SAGA respectively.  The theoretical results are supported by empirical validation, with experiments showing the effectiveness of non-uniform sampling and the benefits of their acceleration scheme. The provided experimental results, including additional details in the appendix, convincingly demonstrate the practical efficacy of the proposed algorithms.

However, the paper isn’t without minor weaknesses. The discussion of acceleration (Section 5) lacks clarity regarding the adaptive update of the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ), leaving the reader unsure about practical implementation details. It would be beneficial to expand on this aspect. Furthermore, the authors state that the convergence proof for SVRG should hold for SAGA, but don’t explicitly provide it; detailing this connection would strengthen the paper. A notational issue arises with the use of ‘K’ for both the smooth function and the design matrix, which could cause confusion. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to a challenging problem setting.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees with a broader applicability due to the monotone operator framework.
*   Clear presentation and convincing experimental validation.
*   Addresses a significant problem with potential impact on various machine learning applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in acceleration scheme implementation details.
*   Missing explicit proof of SAGA convergence mirroring SVRG.
*   Notational ambiguity.



**Overall:** This is a solid and valuable contribution that significantly advances the state-of-the-art in solving saddle-point problems. Despite the minor concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance to NIPS, with a request for the authors to address the clarity issues regarding acceleration and the SAGA proof.
