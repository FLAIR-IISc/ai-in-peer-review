Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure and proposes stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG- and SAGA-style) tailored to this setting. The key modeling assumptions are: (i) M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator, (ii) K is smooth convex–concave, and (iii) the gradient operator B = (∂xK, −∂yK) decomposes into a finite sum of Lipschitz components. The authors present two variance-reduction algorithms (SVRG and SAGA), establish linear convergence rates, and show how to accelerate them via an extension of the Catalyst framework (proximal point with periodic updates of a reference iterate). Importantly, they analyze the methods through the lens of monotone operator splitting, which both simplifies some arguments and broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities and general monotone inclusions. They also emphasize two practical ingredients: factored versus element-wise splits of B and non-uniform sampling, which improves both theory and practice. Numerical experiments on classification tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers illustrate the benefits.

Relative to prior work, this paper bridges two lines: (1) variance-reduced algorithms for finite-sum convex minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) and (2) primal–dual and forward–backward methods for saddle points and monotone inclusions (e.g., Chambolle–Pock; stochastic FB). Earlier stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC, APCG variants) rely on separability and/or co-coercivity; this paper targets non-separable settings and does not require each component to be monotone or co-coercive. The Catalyst-based acceleration adapts universal acceleration ideas from convex minimization to the monotone/saddle framework.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: First comprehensive variance-reduction analysis for general convex–concave saddle-point problems under a finite-sum structure, with clear linear rates under strong convexity–concavity.
- Generality: Extends naturally to maximal monotone operators A and Lipschitz forward operators Bi with B monotone; does not rely on co-coercivity of components.
- Algorithmic design: Clarifies two granularities of decomposition (factored vs element-wise) and motivates non-uniform sampling; includes guidance on mini-batching and storage/compute trade-offs.
- Acceleration: Adapts Catalyst through proximal-point regularization with simple scheduling; leads to improved complexity bounds akin to accelerated variance reduction in minimization.
- Empirics: Demonstrates gains over batch FB/accelerated FB and over uniform sampling; accelerated SVRG is particularly effective on ill-conditioned problems.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Assumption strength: The main rates require strong convexity in x and strong concavity in y (via M). In many important saddle problems only one side is strongly convex (e.g., regularized ERM with smooth loss but only primal strong convexity). Requiring strong convexity on both sides is overly strong; it limits applicability and complicates comparison with methods that assume one-sided strong convexity.
  • Recommendation: Establish linear (or accelerated sublinear) rates under one-sided strong convexity (either primal or dual), leveraging the smoothness of K and the metric induced by Ω. Related analyses exist for primal–dual methods under one-sided strong convexity; adapting them to the variance-reduced setting would significantly broaden impact.
  • Alternatively, show how to “shift” strong convexity only to one side via an additive–subtractive quadratic trick, or use Catalyst/proximal regularization to artificially induce strong monotonicity on one side and control the bias.
- Practical constants and tuning: Step sizes depend on L and L̄(π), which can be difficult to estimate tightly in practice, especially under non-uniform sampling. While the paper notes adaptivity as future work, providing heuristic rules or online estimation (e.g., backtracking, spectral estimates for ‖K‖op, plug-in variance estimates for L̄) would improve usability.
- Scope of experiments: The experiments are informative but limited (two datasets, one non-separable setting emphasized). More thorough evaluations would help validate the claims “always superior to accelerated batch” and the benefits of non-uniform sampling in diverse regimes.
  • Recommendation: (i) add larger-scale and higher-condition-number problems; (ii) ablate factored vs element-wise splits and mini-batch sizes; (iii) evaluate primal–dual gap and wall-clock; (iv) compare against strong baselines such as SPDC/APCG-style methods in settings where separability holds, and Chambolle–Pock with tuned over-relaxation; (v) include at least one non-bilinear K example and a monotone variational inequality to illustrate the generality.
- SAGA resampling detail: The need to resample stored gradients for non-uniform SAGA to retain the stated bounds is a subtle but important implementation detail. A clearer discussion of when resampling is required in practice and its overhead would help practitioners.

Quality
- The theoretical results are well-motivated and appear sound. The monotone-operator viewpoint is clean and avoids reliance on co-coercivity. Acceleration analysis via proximal-point interpretation is elegant and yields transparent complexity trade-offs. Empirical results support the main claims, though a broader suite of tests would strengthen them.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally well-organized. The distinction between L and L̄(π), and the role of non-uniform sampling, are clearly explained. Minor suggestions: summarize storage/compute trade-offs (factored vs element-wise) in a compact figure; provide practical guidance for selecting π and estimating norms; clarify SAGA resampling and its cost.

Originality
- Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and general monotone settings without co-coercivity, and coupling this with a Catalyst-style acceleration, is novel and timely. The decomposition and sampling design for bilinear K with factored splits are also practically meaningful.

Significance
- High. Variance-reduced methods are workhorses for large-scale minimization; bringing them rigorously to saddle-point and monotone problems broadens their impact to robust optimization, structured prediction with non-separable penalties, and variational inequalities.

Pro and con arguments
Pros:
- Solid theory with linear rates and acceleration.
- Broad applicability (non-separable objectives; monotone operators).
- Practical design choices (non-uniform sampling; mini-batching; factored splits).
- Clear improvements over batch/stochastic baselines in experiments.

Cons:
- Requires strong convexity on both primal and dual sides.
- Sensitivity to unknown constants; limited guidance on tuning.
- Experimental scope is relatively narrow; few non-bilinear or VI examples.

Recommendations to authors
- Relax the strong convexity–concavity assumption to require strong convexity on only the primal or only the dual side, and analyze the resulting rates.
- Expand the empirical section with more datasets, broader baselines, and ablations; include a monotone VI example.
- Provide practical recipes for estimating L, L̄(π), and sampling probabilities; discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Elaborate on the memory/runtime trade-offs for SAGA under factored vs element-wise splits and when resampling is needed.

Overall assessment
An important and well-executed contribution that extends variance reduction to a broad and impactful class of problems, with compelling theory and promising empirical results. With a relaxed strong-convexity assumption and expanded experiments, the paper would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
