- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing first linear convergence guarantees in this context.
- Leverages monotone operator theory to generalize stochastic variance reduction beyond convex minimization to saddle-point problems and variational inequalities.
- Identifies two crucial splitting notions (function-based vs. partial-derivative-based) needed for non-separable problems.
- Demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is both theoretically and practically important.
- Shows how to accelerate the proposed methods using the "catalyst" framework.
- Theoretical analysis is sound and provides new insights; convex minimization analyses do not directly apply.
- Connection to monotone operators is a major strength but is presented poorly and appears late.
- Unclear whether assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity; needs clarification for convergence guarantees.
- The constant μ in Theorem 2 is unclear—authors should confirm its identity and relation to Theorem 1.
- Experiments are limited to two datasets (sido and rcv1), providing insufficient empirical validation.
- Missing comparison to accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock-type methods (Zhang & Xiao 2015), which are state-of-the-art for saddle-point problems.
- Unclear whether assumptions (particularly A) hold for common SVM-induced saddle-point problems; needs discussion.
- Minor technical issues: inconsistent use of L (condition number vs. Lipschitz constant) and missing reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
- Distinction between standard stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach is not clearly articulated.
- Originality: novel application of stochastic variance reduction to saddle-point problems with a rigorous monotone operator framework.
- Significance: fills an important gap for scalable, non-separable saddle-point optimization relevant to many ML applications.
- Arguments for acceptance: first SVRG/SAGA convergence on saddle-points, strong monotone-operator-based theory, practical experimental benefits, unified broader framework.
- Arguments against acceptance: weak presentation of key theory, limited experiments, missing comparisons, unclear assumptions, and minor technical inconsistencies.
- Recommendation: weak acceptance conditional on revisions to clarify theory, expand experiments, and add missing comparisons.
