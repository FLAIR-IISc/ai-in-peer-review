This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning applications like regularized supervised learning and robust optimization. The authors extend existing algorithms, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to this setting, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems. The work is motivated by the limitations of applying standard stochastic gradient techniques directly to problems exhibiting separability in the saddle-point formulation rather than the associated minimization/maximization problems.

The core contribution lies in adapting SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point context, acknowledging the differences in analysis compared to convex minimization. Notably, the authors utilize the framework of monotone operators to prove convergence, demonstrating broader applicability beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. They highlight the importance of considering two distinct notions of "splits" – in terms of functions versus partial derivatives – and emphasize that efficient algorithms require non-uniform sampling strategies. Furthermore, they present an acceleration technique based on a Catalyst framework, achieving performance superior to accelerated batch algorithms.  

The paper’s strengths are significant. The extension of stochastic variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems is a valuable contribution, addressing a practical need in many machine learning applications. The convergence analysis, utilizing monotone operator theory, is a rigorous and insightful approach. The identification of the importance of non-uniform sampling is well-justified, both theoretically and empirically. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, showcasing improvements over existing methods, including batch algorithms and SAGA with uniform sampling. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with a clear organization and logical flow. The authors provide a good overview of related work in the field, citing relevant papers from NIPS and other venues.  The inclusion of the extension to monotone operators broadens the impact of the work beyond the specific saddle-point setting.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. A key assumption is strong convexity-concavity, applied to *both* the convex and concave components of the saddle-point problem. This is a relatively strong condition. In many realistic machine learning scenarios, strong convexity might only hold for the convex part (e.g., a regularization term) or the concave part (e.g., a negative log-likelihood). Requiring strong convexity on both sides unnecessarily restricts the applicability of the proposed algorithms.  Relaxing this requirement to only require strong convexity on either the primal or dual problem would significantly broaden the paper’s scope and practical relevance. 

While the experiments provide compelling evidence of the algorithms' performance, they could be expanded. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and problem instances, including scenarios where the strong convexity assumption is relaxed. Investigating the sensitivity of the algorithms to different non-uniform sampling strategies and comparing their performance to other state-of-the-art methods beyond those already presented would also be valuable.  Further analysis of the impact of the mini-batch size *m* on performance, especially concerning the trade-off between the cost of proximal operator computation and variance reduction, would strengthen the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with theoretical guarantees.
*   Rigorous convergence analysis using monotone operator theory.
*   Practical improvements demonstrated through experiments, including acceleration via the Catalyst framework.
*   Clear writing and organization.
*   Broadens applicability beyond saddle-points to monotone operators and variational inequalities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The strong convexity assumption on both primal and dual problems is limiting.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and explore a broader range of scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant and valuable contribution to the field of optimization, specifically addressing the challenges of solving large-scale saddle-point problems. Despite the limitation of the strong convexity assumption, the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors address the strong convexity requirement in future work and expand their experimental evaluation. Addressing these concerns would further strengthen the paper’s impact and broaden its applicability.
