This paper extends SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, proving linear convergence and demonstrating practical gains. The authors recast stochastic forward–backward schemes with variance reduction under a monotone-operator lens, enabling analysis beyond standard convex minimization and covering variational inequalities. A key contribution is handling non-separable losses/regularizers via two operator splits: an element-wise split and a factored split (rows/columns), with rigorous complexity bounds that justify non-uniform sampling. The paper also proposes an acceleration via a catalyst-style regularization of the saddle-point subproblems.

Relation to prior work: While SAG/SVRG/SAGA deliver linear rates for finite-sum convex minimization, they rely on separability and co-coercivity. Primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock) and stochastic coordinate variants (Zhu–Storkey; Zhang–Xiao) typically require separability. The present work appears to be the first to obtain linearly convergent incremental methods for general strongly convex–concave, non-separable saddle points with a monotone-operator analysis, and it connects to Rockafellar’s proximal-point framework and recent catalyst acceleration.

Quality: The theory is careful and technically sound; the monotone-operator analysis is nontrivial and broadens applicability (e.g., to VIs). The linear convergence results and non-uniform sampling analysis are well supported, and the empirical section, including appendices, aligns with the theoretical claims, particularly the superiority of non-uniform over uniform sampling.

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear with helpful complexity comparisons. However: (i) Acceleration (Section 5) leaves unclear how to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in practice to realize the predicted speed-up; a concrete schedule or heuristic would help. (ii) It is not explicit whether the convergence and acceleration guarantees established for SVRG directly extend to SAGA; please clarify the proof changes or limitations. (iii) Notationally, the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K should use distinct symbols to avoid confusion.

Originality: Strong. Extending variance reduction to non-separable saddle points with a monotone-operator analysis is novel, and the factored vs element-wise splits are insightful.

Significance: High. The results are likely to influence work on large-scale saddle-point and VI problems in ML and game theory.

Pros for acceptance:
- First linear-convergence incremental methods for non-separable saddle points
- Solid monotone-operator analysis and practical non-uniform sampling
- Consistent empirical support

Cons for acceptance:
- Acceleration section lacks actionable guidance on updating (x̄, ȳ)
- Unclear extension of acceleration to SAGA and practical resampling/storage trade-offs
- Missing experiments on variational inequalities and more wall-clock/memory reporting

Overall, a strong and timely contribution; addressing the noted clarifications would further strengthen the paper.
