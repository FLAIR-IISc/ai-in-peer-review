This paper proposes an extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. This is an important and growing class of problems in machine learning, covering applications with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation penalties) where standard SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization cannot be applied. The authors provide the first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for this problem class.

The main contributions are:
1.  The extension of SVRG and SAGA algorithms to the saddle-point setting. The analysis departs from the standard proofs for convex minimization and is instead based on the more general theory of monotone operators, which is a significant technical contribution.
2.  The introduction and analysis of two different ways to split the problem structure: "individual" splits and "factored" splits, which are particularly relevant for bilinear problems.
3.  A thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence rates, including the benefits of non-uniform sampling, which is shown to be critical for improving performance.
4.  The integration of an acceleration scheme based on the "Catalyst" framework, leading to an algorithm that has, in theory, the best overall complexity.

The paper is well-written and relates the new algorithms and their analysis clearly to the extensive prior work on variance reduction for convex minimization and batch methods for saddle-point problems.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature. While variance reduction methods have revolutionized large-scale convex minimization, their application to the equally important class of saddle-point problems was an open question. This work provides the first such extension with guarantees of linear convergence, making it a significant contribution. The framework is general enough to cover many machine learning problems that were previously intractable for this class of fast stochastic methods.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and non-trivial. The move to a monotone operator framework for the proof is well-motivated and necessary, as the co-coercivity properties used in standard SVRG/SAGA analyses do not hold for saddle-point problems. The complexity analysis presented in Table 1 is comprehensive and provides clear (asymptotic) comparisons between the proposed methods and existing batch and stochastic algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-organized and clearly written. It successfully builds intuition by drawing parallels to the familiar convex optimization setting while carefully highlighting the key differences and challenges that arise in the saddle-point context. The experiments, while on a specific problem class, effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, confirming the predictions of the theory.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **The Proximal Operator Bottleneck:** My main concern with this work lies in the practical complexity of the proposed algorithms. The key assumption is that the proximal operator of the (potentially non-separable) function `M(x, y)` can be computed efficiently. However, in the proposed SVRG and SAGA algorithms, this full `prox` operator, which involves both `x` (in `R^d`) and `y` (in `R^n`), must be computed at *every single incremental iteration*. For many relevant problems, this operation has a cost of `O(n+d)`. This creates a fundamental bottleneck. While the gradient estimate is cheap (costing `T(Bi)`), the overall per-iteration cost is dominated by `O(n+d)`. This undermines the primary motivation for using incremental methods, which is to avoid `O(nd)` or `O(n+d)` work at each step.
*   **Unclear Advantage Over Batch Methods:** Consequent to the above point, the claimed performance benefits over accelerated batch methods are not entirely convincing. An accelerated forward-backward (batch) method also performs `O(n+d)` work for the proximal step, but only once per pass over the data (which has a total cost of `T(B) + T_prox`). The proposed variance-reduced methods perform this `O(n+d)` work many times within a single effective pass. It is therefore not clear that the reduction in the number of required passes translates to a reduction in total runtime. The complexity comparison in Table 1 seems to omit the cost of the proximal operator, which is a critical component of the total work.
*   **Complexity of Factored Splits:** The analysis of factored splits, while interesting, may also be problematic. For bilinear problems where `n >> d`, the storage requirement for SAGA is `O(n+d)`, but the iteration complexity appears to depend on `max{|J|, |K|}`, which could be `O(n)`. This could make the algorithm prohibitively slow in such common asymmetric settings.

**Questions for the Authors:**

The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide a more detailed discussion of the total computational complexity, explicitly incorporating the cost of the proximal operator. In particular:

1.  Could you clarify the precise conditions on the problem parameters (`n, d`, condition numbers, cost of `T(Bi)`) under which your proposed accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is provably faster than an accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm, when the `O(n+d)` per-iteration cost of the prox is accounted for?
2.  Is the dependency on `n` and `d` in your complexity bounds for factored splits tight? The current results suggest they may perform poorly when `n` is much larger than `d`; is this a fundamental limitation or an artifact of the analysis? A deeper comparison of the trade-offs between individual and factored splits would be valuable.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and technically impressive extension of variance-reduced methods to saddle-point problems. The theoretical contribution is strong. However, the practical significance is questionable due to the expensive, full-dimensional proximal step required at each iteration, which may make the methods slower than accelerated batch algorithms in many realistic scenarios. A more careful and transparent discussion of this crucial issue is needed.
