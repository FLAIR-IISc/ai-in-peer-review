## Review of “Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of minimizing convex-concave saddle-point functions, a common occurrence in machine learning, particularly in scenarios involving duality and regularization. The authors extend existing stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques like SVRG and SAGA to this setting, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale problems of this type. The contribution is significant as standard convex minimization acceleration methods don’t directly translate to saddle-point problems. They further demonstrate acceleration via a catalyst framework and generalize their approach to monotone operators, broadening the applicability of their methods.

The paper is well-structured, clearly outlining the problem, existing approaches, and their proposed solutions. The theoretical analysis, specifically the convergence proofs leveraging monotone operator theory, is a strong point, demonstrating a solid understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. The inclusion of numerical results, showcasing performance gains compared to baseline methods (including batch and stochastic alternatives), strengthens the empirical validation. The connection to related work, including references to foundational papers in optimization and machine learning, is adequate. The extension to monotone operators is a nice generalization, demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

However, a key weakness lies in the strong convexity assumptions imposed on *both* the primal and dual problems. This severely limits the applicability of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios where such strict conditions are rarely met. The authors acknowledge this limitation, and the suggested remedy – requiring strong convexity on only one side – is a crucial improvement that should be addressed in future work.  Additionally, while the numerical experiments are a good start, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and problem settings, particularly those where the strong convexity assumption does not hold.  Exploring the impact of different sampling strategies more thoroughly would also be valuable. The current experiments primarily focus on two datasets and a relatively narrow set of parameters.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems with proven linear convergence.
*   Solid theoretical foundation based on monotone operator theory.
*   Demonstrated acceleration via the catalyst framework.
*   Practical validation through numerical experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Overly strong assumption of strong convexity on both primal and dual problems.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.



**Overall:** I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Addressing the strong convexity assumption is critical. Expanding the numerical evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the community. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the practical utility hinges on relaxing the assumptions and demonstrating broader applicability.
