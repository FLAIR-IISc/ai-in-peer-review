- Extends SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems, enabling variance-reduced methods for non-separable losses/regularizers.  
- Proposes variants for "individual" and "factored" finite-sum structures, analyzes convergence rates, and studies non-uniform sampling.  
- Frames analysis in the general monotone-operator setting, increasing applicability beyond standard co-coercive cases.  
- Accelerates the SVRG variant via the Catalyst framework and presents experiments (binary classification with non-separable components) showing benefits of acceleration and non-uniform sampling.  

- First work to achieve linear convergence of SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum saddle point problems, filling an important literature gap.  
- Strong technical contributions: non-trivial extension, comprehensive complexity analysis, and insightful bilinear-case comparisons.  
- Clear writing and organization, helpful comparisons (e.g., Table 1), and clean algorithmic descriptions including Catalyst acceleration.  

- Major concern: high per-iteration cost—core proximal update prox_M appears to be O(n + d) per incremental step, much larger than standard O(d) SVRG/SAGA steps.  
- Due to that cost, overall runtime improvement over accelerated batch methods is questionable; worst-case complexity comparisons suggest stochastic method can be no better or worse, especially when n and d are large.  

- Request analysis of factored vs. individual splits: clarify runtime trade-offs and regimes (e.g., n >> d) where factored splits help given the O(n + d) proximal cost.  
- Ask for precise conditions (in n, d, condition number, matrix norms) where Accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms accelerated batch forward-backward.  
- Question tightness of the O(n + d) proximal cost: is it inherent to these non-separable problems or specific to examples, and are there applications where proximal cost could be much lower?  

- Overall: technically strong and novel work, but practical superiority claims are weakened by a potentially critical per-iteration computational bottleneck; addressing this would substantially strengthen the paper.
