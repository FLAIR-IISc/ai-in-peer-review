- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems and proves the first linear convergence rates for large-scale incremental methods in this setting.
- Develops a monotone-operator convergence proof that also yields cleaner analyses for forward–backward splitting and extends to variational inequalities.
- Saddle-point formulation enables handling non-separable losses and regularizers via proximal structure, important for many ML tasks.
- Studies non-uniform sampling, shows it is often crucial, and links sampling choices to complexity; adapts catalyst to give an accelerated SVRG that outperforms batch baselines.
- Theory is sound with rates and constants carefully tracked and algorithmic details (factored vs individual splits) tied to complexity.
- Experiments are limited in breadth but well chosen and consistent with claims, highlighting the impact of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.
- Clarity is generally good but main text needs: a description of recommended non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear K, how σ is chosen in practice, intuition for why non-uniform sampling matters more here, and a self-contained proof sketch.
- Originality is high: novel monotone-operator interpretation to obtain linear rates for SVRG/SAGA and a generalization to variational inequalities; simplified FB splitting proofs are of independent interest.
- Significance is strong: linearly convergent incremental methods for saddle points can affect many ML problems with non-separable structure; sampling and acceleration guidance are practical.
- Question for authors: beyond bilinear K, what non-uniform π do you recommend? Provide a concrete rule and complexity-sensitive bounds.
- Question for authors: how is σ set in experiments (estimated L vs L̄, backtracking, tuning)? Any robustness observations?
- Question for authors: why is non-uniform sampling more critical for saddle points than for convex minimization? Provide intuition and, if possible, a small example.
- Pros: novel analysis; broad applicability (variational inequalities); handles non-separable structure; competitive and often superior empirics; clear practical guidance on sampling and acceleration.
- Cons: main novelty is the proof technique; some experimental and presentation details are missing.
- Minor: typos at line 78 (k -> d) and line 121 (extra y_{t-1} in the second equality).
