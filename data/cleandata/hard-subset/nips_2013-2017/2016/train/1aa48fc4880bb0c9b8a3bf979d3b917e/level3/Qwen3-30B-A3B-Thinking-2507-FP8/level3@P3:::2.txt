

# Review: Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors build upon recent advances in variance reduction for convex minimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) but face significant challenges in the saddle-point setting due to the lack of convexity in both primal and dual variables. They address these challenges by leveraging the theory of monotone operators, which enables a unified convergence analysis that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced algorithms in saddle-point problems, demonstrating that these methods converge linearly under strong convexity-concavity assumptions. Second, it identifies two distinct ways to split the problem (by functions or by partial derivatives) and shows that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. Third, it extends the Catalyst framework to accelerate the proposed methods, leading to algorithms that outperform accelerated batch methods. Finally, it generalizes the approach to monotone operators, broadening the applicability beyond saddle-point problems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong connection to previous work in convex optimization. It properly situates its contributions within the NIPS community, referencing relevant prior work on variance reduction (SAGA, SVRG) and saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful attention to the different scaling factors for primal and dual variables, which is crucial for good performance in practice.

The numerical experiments validate the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements over batch methods and uniform-sampling variants. The authors demonstrate the advantage of non-uniform sampling and acceleration on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a challenging non-separable classification problem. The results confirm that the proposed methods outperform existing approaches, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper excels in technical quality and originality. The extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The use of monotone operators for convergence analysis is elegant and provides a more general framework than previous approaches. The Catalyst acceleration is a natural and effective extension.

However, the paper has some limitations. The most significant weakness is the assumption of strong convexity on both the primal and dual problems, which is overly restrictive for many machine learning applications. As noted in the critique, this assumption could be relaxed to require strong convexity on only one side (primal or dual), which would broaden the applicability of the methods. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore it in depth.

Additionally, the numerical experiments, while convincing, could be expanded. The paper includes results on two datasets, but more extensive comparisons with a wider range of baselines and problem types would strengthen the empirical claims. The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of the impact of different sampling schemes and parameter choices.

The significance of this work is substantial. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning through duality, and the proposed methods provide the first linearly convergent algorithms that can handle large-scale problems with separable structure. The acceleration via Catalyst framework provides a practical advantage that could benefit a wide range of applications.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and empirical results. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition and clear pseudocode for the algorithms. The originality is high, as the extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems represents a novel contribution. The significance is strong, as the methods address an important gap in the literature and provide practical advantages for large-scale machine learning problems.

The main weakness is the strong convexity assumption, which limits the applicability of the methods. The paper would be significantly stronger if it addressed this limitation by showing how the methods could be adapted to cases where strong convexity holds on only one side. The authors should also expand their numerical experiments to provide more comprehensive validation.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical advances are sound and the empirical results are convincing. With minor improvements addressing the strong convexity assumption and expanding the experimental section, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems
- Elegant convergence analysis using monotone operators
- Practical acceleration via Catalyst framework
- Strong empirical validation of theoretical claims

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overly strong assumption of strong convexity on both primal and dual
- Limited numerical experiments compared to the scope of the problem
- The extension to monotone operators is theoretically interesting but not fully validated empirically

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions (addressing the strong convexity assumption and expanding numerical experiments). The paper makes a significant contribution to the field and addresses an important gap in the literature on stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems.
