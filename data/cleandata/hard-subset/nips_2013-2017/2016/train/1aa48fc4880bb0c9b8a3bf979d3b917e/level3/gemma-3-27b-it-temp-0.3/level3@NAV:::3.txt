## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper presents an extension of popular stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems at large scale. The work is well-motivated, clearly articulating the relevance of saddle-point formulations in areas like supervised learning with non-separable losses and robust optimization. The extension of existing algorithms is presented logically, and the theoretical analysis, leveraging monotone operator theory, is a valuable contribution. The authors correctly identify and analyze two distinct notions of “splits” within the saddle-point formulation, and convincingly demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficient performance. 

A key strength lies in the thorough complexity analysis under different split types and sampling strategies, providing a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs involved. The experimental validation on a binary classification example further reinforces the theoretical findings, highlighting the critical role of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The comparison to batch algorithms is well-aligned with intuition from convex minimization, and the extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed methods.

However, the paper suffers from a significant practical limitation: the O(n+d) cost per incremental iteration for computing the proximal operator. As rightly pointed out by the authors, this can negate the theoretical advantages, potentially leading to complexities no better than accelerated batch FB, and even worse performance with factored splits when *n >> d*. While the authors address this in the rebuttal, the core issue remains. 

Specifically, the reviewer’s requests for deeper discussion regarding (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (ii) when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) whether the *n, d* dependence is tight, were not fully resolved in the original submission. The rebuttal clarified that factored splits *can* be worse, accelerated SVRG’s advantage depends on specific problem structure, and the *n, d* dependence is likely tight, but these points could have been more explicitly discussed within the paper itself. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel and technically sound extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems; rigorous theoretical analysis; clear presentation; relevant experimental validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Potential practical bottleneck due to proximal operator cost; limitations of factored splits; lack of detailed discussion regarding the conditions for outperforming batch methods.

**Overall:** Despite the practical limitations, this is a valuable theoretical contribution that expands the toolkit for solving saddle-point problems. The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the analysis are commendable. While the rebuttal addressed the reviewer’s concerns, the fundamental bottleneck remains a significant drawback.
