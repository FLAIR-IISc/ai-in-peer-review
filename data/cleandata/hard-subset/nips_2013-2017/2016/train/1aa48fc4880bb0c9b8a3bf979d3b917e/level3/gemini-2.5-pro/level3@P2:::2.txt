### Summary

This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically extending SVRG and SAGA, to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems where the objective has a finite sum structure. This is a significant step, as previous variance-reduced methods like SVRG/SAGA were primarily developed for standard convex minimization problems. The authors provide the first linear convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. A key aspect of their analysis is the use of monotone operator theory, which not only provides a solid theoretical foundation but also shows that the proposed methods apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper also explores important practical considerations, including different ways to split the operator (functional vs. partial derivatives), the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and an extension of the "Catalyst" framework to accelerate the proposed algorithms. Numerical experiments demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods over existing batch and standard stochastic algorithms.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by being the first to successfully extend modern variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA) to the general setting of decomposable saddle-point problems. This is a significant contribution, as many machine learning problems, particularly those involving non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso), can be naturally formulated as saddle-point problems but could not previously benefit from these fast, linearly convergent stochastic methods. The work opens up a new avenue for efficient optimization in these areas.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis is a major strength. Instead of trying to adapt proofs from the convex minimization setting, the authors correctly identify monotone operator theory as the appropriate framework. This leads to a more general and elegant proof of linear convergence. This theoretical choice also highlights that the algorithm's applicability extends beyond saddle-point problems to finding zeros of monotone operators, which is a valuable insight.

3.  **Practical Enhancements and Analysis:** The paper goes beyond a simple algorithmic extension. It thoughtfully considers and analyzes several practical aspects that are crucial for performance:
    *   It identifies two different splitting strategies ("functional" and "factored") and discusses their implications.
    *   It demonstrates both theoretically and empirically the importance of non-uniform sampling, showing that it leads to superior convergence bounds compared to uniform sampling and batch methods.
    *   The successful application of the Catalyst framework for acceleration provides a method that is provably faster than accelerated batch methods, which is a strong result.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It begins by motivating the problem with concrete machine learning examples, outlines the existing landscape of algorithms, and then systematically presents its contributions, from the core algorithms (SVRG/SAGA extensions) to acceleration and the generalization to monotone operators.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overly Strong Assumption of Double Strong Convexity:** The primary weakness of the paper is the assumption that the function `M(x, y)` is strongly convex in `x` and strongly concave in `y`. While this assumption enables a clean linear convergence proof, it is very restrictive and excludes many important machine learning applications. For instance, a standard regularized ERM problem `min f(x) + g*(Kx)` with a strongly convex regularizer `f(x)` (e.g., L2) leads to a saddle-point problem that is strongly convex in `x` but typically only concave (not strongly concave) in the dual variable `y`. This single strong convexity setting is far more common in practice. The current analysis does not apply to this broad and important class of problems.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while promising, are conducted on a limited set of problems and datasets. They effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) against the baselines on the chosen non-separable problem. However, the paper would be more convincing if it included:
    *   A wider variety of datasets to demonstrate robustness.
    *   Experiments on problems that fit the standard "singly" strongly-convex-concave setting, even if just to empirically observe the algorithm's behavior beyond its current theoretical guarantees. This could provide valuable insights and motivate future theoretical work.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Relaxing the Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** This is the most critical point for improving the paper's impact. I strongly encourage the authors to discuss the limitation of the current assumption explicitly. Ideally, they would extend their analysis to the case where strong convexity holds for only one of the variables. While this might lead to sublinear rates instead of linear ones, it would make the work directly applicable to a much wider range of problems and would constitute a very significant strengthening of the paper. Even a discussion of the challenges involved in such an extension would be valuable.

*   **Broader Empirical Evaluation:** To strengthen the experimental section, the authors could consider including problems like L2-regularized SVMs or Logistic Regression. Although these are separable problems where primal SAGA can be applied (as shown in the `rcv1` experiment), they are canonical examples of saddle-point problems that are not strongly-concave in the dual. Showing how the proposed saddle-point methods perform on these problems would be very informative for practitioners.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is timely and important, and the theoretical analysis based on monotone operators is elegant and powerful. The main limitation is the strong assumption on the objective function, which curtails its immediate applicability. However, the paper provides a strong foundation for future work in this direction. The strengths in originality, theoretical depth, and significance far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
