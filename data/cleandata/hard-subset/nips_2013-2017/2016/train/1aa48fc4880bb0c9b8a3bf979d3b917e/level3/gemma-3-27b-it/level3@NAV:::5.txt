## Review of “Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems”

This paper tackles the important problem of developing linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems, a common structure in many machine learning applications. The authors extend SVRG and SAGA to this setting, demonstrating that with careful analysis, these algorithms can achieve linear convergence – a significant advancement as this was previously an open problem. The key contribution lies in reframing saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator, enabling a convergence proof that generalizes beyond standard saddle-point scenarios to variational inequalities. This is a clever and broadly applicable analytical technique.

The paper builds upon established stochastic variance reduction methods, appropriately adapting them for the saddle-point formulation. Importantly, the authors highlight the necessity of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically, and show how to accelerate these methods using a catalyst framework. The results presented in Section 7 are encouraging, showing competitive performance against existing methods, particularly when utilizing non-uniform sampling.

However, while the core ideas are sound and the theoretical contribution is notable, the paper suffers from clarity issues and a lack of detailed explanation in certain areas. The analysis, while correct, feels somewhat terse; providing more details or a proof sketch within the main paper – as suggested – would significantly improve understanding and confidence in the results. Specifically, the definition of π (the non-uniform sampling distribution) beyond the bilinear case requires clarification. What practical considerations dictate its choice in more general scenarios? Additionally, the paper would benefit from explaining the step-size selection (σ) used in the experiments and offering intuition as to *why* non-uniform sampling is demonstrably more crucial for saddle-point problems than it is for convex minimization problems. 

Minor errors (line 78, 121) should be corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental challenge in machine learning optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides a strong, generalized convergence proof using monotone operator theory.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Demonstrates competitive performance and highlights the importance of non-uniform sampling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** Some sections are difficult to follow due to lack of detail.
*   **Completeness:** Proof details are lacking, hindering thorough assessment.
*   **Experiment Details:** Insufficient explanation regarding parameter choices.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a significant contribution. Addressing the clarity and completeness concerns, particularly by providing more detail on the non-uniform sampling and a proof sketch, would elevate it further. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
