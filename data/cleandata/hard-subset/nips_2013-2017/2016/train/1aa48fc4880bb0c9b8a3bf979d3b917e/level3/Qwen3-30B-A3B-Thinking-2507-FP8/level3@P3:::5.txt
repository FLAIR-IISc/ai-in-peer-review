

# Review: Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems that arise frequently in machine learning. The authors recognize that convex minimization analyses do not apply to saddle-point problems, and instead use the monotone operator framework to prove convergence, which also allows the approach to generalize to variational inequality problems. This is a key insight that enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers through saddle-point formulation, addressing a limitation of existing methods.

The paper's main contribution is the novel proof technique using monotone operators to interpret saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators. This approach provides a simpler proof for the forward-backward splitting method and its stochastic variant, which is a significant theoretical advance. The authors also demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, both theoretically and in practice, with their algorithms outperforming prior methods.

The paper is well-organized and mostly clear, with good examples from machine learning (Section 2.1) that illustrate the importance of the approach. The experiments (Section 7) convincingly show that non-uniform sampling SAGA and accelerated SVRG outperform batch methods and uniform sampling variants, with particularly strong results for ill-conditioned problems.

However, the paper has some clarity issues that need addressing. The authors should provide more details on the non-uniform sampling strategy beyond bilinear problems (what exactly is π in general cases?) and explain how the step size σ was chosen in experiments. Additionally, the paper should clarify why non-uniform sampling is more crucial for saddle-point problems than for convex minimization, as this is an important theoretical point.

The paper is technically sound and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors properly cite related work, including the use of monotone operators in previous contexts. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. The paper's main novelty is the proof technique, and including a more detailed proof sketch in the main paper would strengthen the contribution.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- **Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The convergence proof is novel and correct, though some details on sampling and step size selection could be improved.
- **Clarity**: The paper is generally clear, but needs more explanation on sampling probabilities and step size selection. Minor typos (line 78, line 121) should be fixed.
- **Originality**: The use of monotone operators for saddle-point problems is a novel approach that extends beyond previous work on convex minimization. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related contributions.
- **Significance**: This is a significant advance that addresses an important gap in the literature. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning, and having linearly convergent algorithms for them is a major step forward. The extension to variational inequalities broadens the applicability of the approach.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems with theoretical guarantees
- Novel monotone operator framework for convergence proof
- Generalizes to variational inequality problems
- Handles non-separable loss functions and regularizers
- Experimental results confirm theoretical advantages
- Provides simpler proofs for existing methods

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some clarity issues around sampling probabilities and step size selection
- Minor typos in the manuscript
- Could provide more details on the non-uniform sampling strategy beyond bilinear problems

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization in machine learning. The theoretical insight of using monotone operators to analyze saddle-point problems is elegant and broadly applicable. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the paper advances the state of the art in optimization for saddle-point problems. With minor clarifications on sampling strategies and step size selection, this would be a strong paper for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the clarity issues noted in the review.
