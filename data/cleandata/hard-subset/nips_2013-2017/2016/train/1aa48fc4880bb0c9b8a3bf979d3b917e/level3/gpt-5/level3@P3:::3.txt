Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA) and Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave composite saddle point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M has an efficiently computable proximal operator. A central contribution is to handle two natural splitting models for the smooth operator B = (∂xK, −∂yK): (i) “individual” splits (e.g., per-entry of a bilinear K), and (ii) “factored” splits (row/column blocks), leading to different storage and sampling strategies. The analysis departs from classical convex minimization by formulating saddle-point optimization as finding zeros of a strongly monotone operator A + B and proving linear convergence using monotone operator theory, which also extends to variational inequalities.

The paper develops iteration complexity bounds under uniform vs non-uniform sampling, introduces mini-batching, and shows how Catalyst-like regularization yields an accelerated SVRG scheme with improved dependence on the smoothness constant and oracle costs. For bilinear saddle points, the comparisons mirror convex minimization: non-uniform sampling tightens the smoothness constant (L̄) and accelerated SVRG enjoys the best theoretical running time among the incremental options, with clear norm-based bounds (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max). Experiments on binary classification with non-separable loss and regularizer confirm that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are key to beating batch FB; uniform sampling often fails to do so.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for general (non-separable) strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems. The monotone operator framing is technically non-trivial and broadens applicability beyond saddle points (to variational inequalities).
- Thorough complexity analysis: Careful treatment of individual vs factored splits, uniform vs non-uniform sampling, and mini-batching, with clear norm-dependent constants and practical guidance.
- Acceleration: A simple and elegant Catalyst-style scheme analyzed via proximal point, avoiding delicate Nesterov machinery in the SP setting; yields provable improvements.
- Clarity of bilinear case: Comparisons line up with convex minimization intuitions; the role of ‖K‖F vs ‖K‖op and sampling is well articulated.
- Empirical validation: Experiments isolate the value of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, and include comparisons to batch methods and to primal SAGA where applicable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Proximal bottleneck: Each incremental update invokes prox_M, which costs O(n + d) in the stated model. This can dominate the per-iteration cost and erode the practical advantage over accelerated batch FB, especially when computing Bi is O(1) (e.g., individual entry sampling). While mini-batching can amortize some costs, the fundamental prox cost remains.
- When do we beat batch FB? Even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling may not improve over accelerated batch FB if Tprox(M) dominates T(Bi) and T(B). The paper claims superiority “in theory,” but the wall-clock advantage depends critically on the prox cost and problem dimensions.
- Split choice trade-offs: Factored splits may be worse when n >> d (or vice versa), depending on storage, cost per sampled operator, and L̄. The paper could better articulate these regimes and the practical implications.
- Assumptions: Knowledge of strong convexity–concavity parameters is required; adaptivity is left for future work. The analysis assumes exact prox; inexact or coordinate-friendly prox variants are not discussed.

Requests for deeper discussion (addressed in rebuttal; leaving scores unchanged)
(i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits? Please add a clear comparison that jointly accounts for:
- L̄(π): factored non-uniform gives L̄ ≲ ‖K‖F/√(λγ), while individual non-uniform gives L̄ ≲ √max{n,d}‖K‖F/√(λγ) (better for factored).
- Oracle costs: for bilinear K, T(Bi) is O(1) for individual entries but can be O(nnz(row)+nnz(col)) for factored row/column selections; SAGA storage is O(nd) vs O(n+d), respectively.
- Net complexity: show explicit regimes (e.g., n ≫ d with sparse K) where individual splits with mini-batching m ≈ n+d are preferable, and regimes (dense K, memory constraints) where factored splits win.

(ii) Precise conditions when accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB. Using your cost model,
- Accel-SVRG complexity (up to logs): L̄ √(T(B) max_i T(Bi)) plus prox costs per inner step. Batch FB (accelerated) uses O(T(B)+Tprox(M)) per iteration with rate ~(1−1/(1+2L)).
- Please give a simple inequality comparing the two, e.g., Accel-SVRG wins when L̄ √(T(B) max_i T(Bi)) + c Tprox(M) < c′ (T(B) + Tprox(M)), highlighting the role of Tprox(M), L vs L̄, and mini-batch m.

(iii) Tightness in n,d. Are the ‖K‖F- and ‖K‖max-based dependences improvable? It would help to:
- Provide lower-bound examples (e.g., worst-case K with flat spectrum or spiky rows/columns) showing the necessity of the √max{n,d} gaps for individual splits.
- Clarify whether improved sketching/sampling (beyond pj ∝ (KK⊤)jj, qk ∝ (K⊤K)kk) could close any gaps, or whether current bounds are information-theoretically tight.

Quality
The technical development is sound, proofs appear careful, and the monotone operator route is insightful. The experimental setup is appropriate for the claims; results convincingly demonstrate the necessity of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. The main caveat is the practical impact of prox costs; a discussion of inexact prox or coordinate-friendly prox variants would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and readable. Suggestions:
- Make the cost model (T(B), T(Bi), Tprox(M)) and its implications more prominent in the main text.
- Summarize split/sampling trade-offs in a compact table with both iteration and wall-clock perspectives.
- Explicitly state the memory costs for SAGA under each split.
- Expand the “when do we beat batch?” discussion with concrete inequalities as above.

Originality
High. Extending SVRG/SAGA/Catalyst to non-separable SP problems with a monotone-operator analysis is novel and non-trivial. The treatment of two split types and sampling is new in this context.

Significance
High theoretical significance and likely to influence subsequent work on stochastic SP and variational inequalities. Practical significance depends on the availability of cheap prox operators; nevertheless, the framework is valuable and broadly applicable.

Pros for acceptance
- First general, linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for strongly convex–concave finite-sum SP.
- Clean, general analysis via monotone operators; extends to VIs.
- Thorough complexity and sampling analyses; principled acceleration.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory; clear message on non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Cons against acceptance
- Proximal bottleneck can nullify practical gains over accelerated batch FB.
- Limited guidance on when factored vs individual splits are preferable in practice.
- No treatment of inexact/coordinate prox or adaptivity to unknown strong convexity.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a substantial and technically solid contribution; despite practical caveats around prox costs, the theory and methodology are impactful. Post-rebuttal: I am satisfied with the authors’ responses to (i)–(iii), but I leave my scores unchanged.
