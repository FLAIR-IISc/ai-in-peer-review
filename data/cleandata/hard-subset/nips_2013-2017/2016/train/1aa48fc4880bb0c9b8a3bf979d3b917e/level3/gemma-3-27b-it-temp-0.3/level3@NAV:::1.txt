## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction (SVRG/SAGA) techniques. The core idea – extending these methods to a saddle-point setting – is a valuable contribution, particularly given the prevalence of such problems in machine learning, especially when dealing with non-separable functions. The authors correctly identify that standard convex minimization analysis doesn’t directly apply and leverage the theory of monotone operators to establish linear convergence. The introduction of non-uniform sampling and acceleration via a catalyst framework further strengthens the work.

The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and the extension to monotone operators is a notable insight, broadening the applicability of the proposed algorithms beyond traditional saddle-point problems. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the nuances of splitting functions in the saddle-point context, distinguishing between function-level and partial derivative-level splits. The proposed acceleration technique builds logically on existing work and promises improved performance.

However, the presentation suffers from a lack of clarity. The connection to monotone operators feels somewhat tacked on, appearing as an extension rather than a foundational element despite underpinning the convergence analysis. This makes the initial motivation and positioning of the work less compelling. The assumptions (A-C) require further clarification, particularly regarding their applicability to SVM-induced saddle-point problems – a concrete example would be beneficial. A clearer distinction between the authors’ method and standard stochastic primal-dual methods is also needed. Specifically, the authors should highlight the key differences in how the variance reduction is applied in the saddle-point setting.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. Only two datasets are used, limiting the generalizability of the findings. More diverse datasets and a comparison to accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock-type methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao 2015) are crucial. In Theorem 2, the meaning of the constant μ needs to be explicitly stated and its relation to the monotonicity constant from the appendix clarified. The transition from assumptions (A-C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is confusing and should be more clearly articulated. Finally, there are minor inconsistencies in notation (e.g., the use of ‘L’ for both condition number and Lipschitz constant) and a missing reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with theoretical guarantees.
*   Insightful connection to monotone operator theory, expanding the algorithm’s applicability.
*   Potential for significant impact in machine learning applications with non-separable objectives.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak presentation and lack of clarity in motivation and assumptions.
*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Missing comparison to relevant existing methods.



**Overall:** While the theoretical contributions are significant, the paper requires substantial revisions to improve clarity, strengthen the experimental validation, and address the identified weaknesses. I recommend a **weak reject** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing these concerns.
