- Addresses large-scale convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with smooth K and efficiently prox-able, strongly convex–concave M.  
- Extends variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this SP setting and proves linear convergence via monotone-operator analysis.  
- Frames SP as finding a zero of a monotone operator and proves convergence using monotonicity rather than co-coercivity.  
- Clarifies two operator-splitting strategies for variance reduction: by functions vs. by partial derivatives (factored splits), and stresses splits must respect convex–concave structure.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is essential for optimal theoretical and practical complexity.  
- Proposes a simple proximal-point “catalyst” acceleration that improves complexity and can dominate accelerated batch methods in theory.  
- Extends analysis beyond SPs to general monotone operator inclusions and variational inequalities.  
- Experiments on two classification problems demonstrate benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration but are limited in scope.  
- Builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA literature and complements primal–dual and stochastic coordinate methods; connects to classical and modern operator-splitting theory.  
- Analysis is technically clean and sound, yielding linear rates under strong convexity–concavity of M and Lipschitz B with sampling-adapted constants.  
- Main theoretical limitation: requires M to be strongly convex in x and strongly concave in y (both sides), which is restrictive for many applications.  
- Practical concerns: need to know/estimate L, L̄, and strong convexity parameters; adaptive step-size strategies are not provided.  
- Experimental limitations: only two datasets, lacking broader ablations (splits, sampling sensitivity, mini-batches, wall-clock/prox cost comparisons, variational inequality examples).  
- Clarity is generally good with helpful discussion of splits/sampling, but some technical details (e.g., SAGA resampling conditions) and notation consistency need tightening.  
- Originality is high: first linearly convergent variance-reduced incremental methods for general convex–concave SPs without relying on co-coercivity; catalyst acceleration in SP is novel.  
- Significance is high: important for ML problems with non-separable losses/regularizers and for broader variational-inequality/game-theoretic applications.  
- Strengths: first linear-convergence VR methods for general SPs, clean monotone-operator analysis, practical non-uniform sampling strategies, simple effective acceleration, solid complexity comparisons.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: relax to one-sided strong convexity or analyze strong monotonicity of A alone; expand experiments and ablations; provide practical guidance for estimating probabilities/parameters and resampling overhead.  
- Acceptance pros: novel important setting, strong general analysis, practical insights with demonstrable gains.  
- Acceptance cons: restrictive strong convexity–concavity assumption, limited empirical evaluation, underexplored practical aspects (parameter estimation, resampling cost).  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on expanded experiments and discussion/relaxation of the strong convexity–concavity assumption.  
- Reviewer confidence: Medium.
