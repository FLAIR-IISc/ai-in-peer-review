Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is strongly convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator and K is smooth. The main contribution is to extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting, yielding the first large-scale algorithms with linear convergence for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems. The analysis departs from convex minimization arguments and is carried out in a monotone operator framework: the saddle-point problem is cast as finding a zero of A+B, where A is a strongly monotone (set-valued) operator associated with M and B is the monotone sum of Lipschitz forward operators Bi underlying K. This viewpoint also shows the methods apply beyond saddle points, e.g., to variational inequalities.

Algorithmically, the authors: (i) define two natural “splits” for B—by functions or by partial derivatives—emphasizing that splits must respect convex–concave structure; (ii) analyze non-uniform sampling schemes and show their impact on the constants; and (iii) adapt catalyst-style regularization to accelerate SVRG in this setting, yielding improved complexities over accelerated batch methods. They provide rates in terms of problem-dependent constants (L, L̄(π)), mini-batch size, and proximal costs, and give complexity bounds for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T Kx under different sampling choices. Experiments on classification with a non-separable AUC surrogate and a non-separable proximal regularizer, and on rcv1, show that (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling outperform batch forward–backward and uniform sampling, especially under ill-conditioning.

This work connects to prior NIPS advances on finite-sum variance reduction for convex minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) and to primal–dual and PDHG/Chambolle–Pock methods for saddle points; it differs from stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods focused on separable structures. The monotone-operator analysis links to classic operator-splitting and proximal point literature, and provides a clean route to linear rates here.

Quality
- Theory: The assumptions and convergence guarantees are clearly stated; linear convergence is established for strongly convex–concave problems under Lipschitzness and appropriate step sizes. Extending the analysis to general monotone inclusions is elegant and technically meaningful. The catalyst-style acceleration is adapted thoughtfully and the resulting complexity trade-offs are transparent.
- Algorithms: The extensions of SVRG/SAGA are natural but nontrivial in this setting (variance reduction within forward–backward for saddle points; two notions of split; careful handling of non-uniform sampling and storage).
- Empirics: Experiments support the claims about non-uniform sampling and acceleration, albeit on a limited set of datasets. Implementation choices (e.g., resampling for non-uniform SAGA) are discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, but a few points could be clarified:
- Non-uniform sampling beyond the bilinear case: please provide a general recipe for π (e.g., πi ∝ Li or πi ∝ squared local Lipschitz constants with respect to Ω/Ω*), and guidance on estimating Li/L̄(π) in practice when K is not bilinear.
- Step sizes in practice: how were σ, epoch lengths, and mini-batch sizes chosen in experiments? Are λ,γ,L,L̄ known or estimated/adapted online? Any backtracking or robustness to misspecification?
- Why non-uniform sampling is particularly impactful here: include intuition tying L̄(π) improvements to the saddle-point geometry and “factored” splits.
- Include a brief proof sketch in the main text for at least one core theorem to aid readers not diving into the appendix.

Originality
The algorithmic templates build directly on SVRG/SAGA, but the monotone-operator analysis for saddle points and the identification of appropriate splits and sampling schemes are original and important. The extension to general monotone inclusions and the clean catalyst-based acceleration in this context add novelty.

Significance
The results are practically relevant for machine learning problems with non-separable losses/regularizers, where prior variance-reduction methods on the primal/dual do not apply directly. Linear rates under strong convexity–concavity and acceleration beyond batch methods are compelling. Given the ubiquity of saddle-point and variational inequality formulations, the impact is likely to be broad.

Questions for the authors
- Beyond bilinear K, what concrete choices of π do you recommend? Can you provide computable surrogates for Li or L̄(π) and an online estimation procedure?
- How sensitive are the methods to misspecifying λ,γ,L,L̄? Can you adopt adaptive step-size rules (e.g., line search/backtracking) without breaking linear rates?
- In SAGA with non-uniform sampling, the analysis requires resampling after the iterate update. In your experiments you mix sampling heuristics; can you comment on theoretical implications and empirical trade-offs?
- Could you compare against extragradient/Mirror-Prox-style methods (batch and stochastic) and primal–dual algorithms with acceleration, where applicable?

Comments on experiments
- Helpful to add ablations on: effect of uniform vs non-uniform π; different mini-batch sizes; sensitivity to conditioning and strong convexity levels; and the cost of proximal operators relative to Bi evaluations.
- Please report wall-clock times alongside passes, and clarify whether prox computations dominate runtime on your tasks.

Minor issues and typos
- Line 78: “k” should be “d”.
- Line 121: extra y_{t−1} term in the second equality (please check).
- Consider adding brief pseudocode clarifications (e.g., where (x̄,ȳ) enters accelerated SVRG; resampling step in SAGA for non-uniform π).

Pros
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for general convex–concave saddle points.
- Clean, general analysis via monotone operators; extension to variational inequalities.
- Careful treatment of sampling and splits; practical acceleration via catalyst.
- Solid theoretical bounds that match or beat accelerated batch in relevant regimes.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Cons
- Algorithmic novelty is incremental; main novelty is analysis and instantiation to saddle points.
- Practical guidance on π, step sizes, and estimating constants could be strengthened.
- Experimental scope is modest; more baselines (e.g., extragradient variants) would be informative.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper provides a substantive theoretical and practical advance by bringing variance reduction with linear rates to a broad class of saddle-point problems, with a principled and general analysis framework and promising empirical results. Addressing the clarity questions and expanding practical guidance would further strengthen the contribution.
