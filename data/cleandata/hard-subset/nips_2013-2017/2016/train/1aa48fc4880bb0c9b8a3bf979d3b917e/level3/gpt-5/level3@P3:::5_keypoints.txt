- Paper extends SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave composite saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent incremental methods for this class.  
- Key technical contribution: monotone-operator interpretation of saddle points and FB splitting analysis that yields linear rates under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz forward operators, and extends to general monotone inclusions/variational inequalities.  
- Emphasizes two splitting notions (function vs. partial-derivative/factored), necessity of convex–concave splits, and critical role of non-uniform sampling; proposes Catalyst proximal-point acceleration.  
- Fills a gap versus prior work: linear-rate variance-reduced methods for minimization existed, but not for non-separable saddle-point problems; batch primal–dual and stochastic FB methods either sublinear or require separability/co-coercivity.  
- Novel and impactful analysis: replaces cocoercivity with monotonicity to prove linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point settings and generalizes to VIs.  
- Broad applicability: framework handles non-separable losses and regularizers important in practice.  
- Clear algorithmic design with careful handling of factored vs. individual splits and derived practical non-uniform sampling schemes.  
- Acceleration via Catalyst gives improved complexity and empirically better performance, also improving batch FB.  
- Empirics show non-uniform sampling significantly improves convergence and accelerated SVRG is competitive or superior across benchmarks.  
- Main-text lacks a concise proof sketch of the core analysis (Lyapunov construction, variance decomposition under non-uniform sampling, and monotonicity-based step-size/contraction arguments).  
- Parameter selection in experiments is underspecified: unclear how σ was chosen, whether L and L̄(π) were estimated, and how λ, γ were scaled/tuned.  
- Guidance for non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear K is unclear; practitioners need concrete π heuristics/examples for general Bi.  
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step in theory; the paper omits it empirically without discussing potential bias/variance effects.  
- Storage/compute trade-offs for factored vs. individual splits need more elaboration, especially for sparse or streamed K.  
- Numerical roles and selection of λ, γ (metric Ω) should be clarified to help balance primal/dual curvature.  
- Technical question 1: Which π is recommended for non-bilinear splits Bi (πi ∝ Li or based on Bi norms), and are there comparisons?  
- Technical question 2: Were theoretical step-sizes used in experiments (e.g., σ = 1/(L^2 + 3L̄^2/m)) or tuned; how were L and L̄(π) estimated and is sensitivity shown?  
- Technical question 3: Request for intuition on why non-uniform sampling matters more here than in convex minimization (anisotropic primal–dual couplings explanation).  
- Theory is sound and carefully positioned; monotone-operator analysis is a genuine contribution with thoughtful complexity comparisons.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized but would benefit from added proof sketch and implementation details to improve accessibility and reproducibility.  
- Originality: high — first linear-rate incremental algorithms for general saddle points via operator-theoretic analysis and simpler FB proofs.  
- Significance: high — likely to influence theory (monotone inclusions, VIs) and practice (non-separable ML models); acceleration and sampling insights are practically meaningful.  
- Pros for acceptance: first linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for saddle points, strong generalizable analysis, handles non-separable settings, competitive accelerated algorithms.  
- Cons for acceptance: central analysis lacks main-text detail, incomplete practical guidance on sampling and step-size selection, reliance on knowledge of strong convexity/concavity and Lipschitz constants.  
- Suggested improvements: add compact proof sketch; provide practical π recipes for non-bilinear problems; clarify σ, L, L̄(π), λ, γ selection and include sensitivity analyses; discuss SAGA resampling choice used in practice.  
- Minor issues: a few typos (line 78, line 121), and L̄(π) and factored split should be defined more prominently with consistent notation.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept.
