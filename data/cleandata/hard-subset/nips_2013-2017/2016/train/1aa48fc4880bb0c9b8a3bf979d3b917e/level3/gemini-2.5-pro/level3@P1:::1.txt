### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends recent stochastic variance reduction gradient (SVRG) and stochastic average gradient (SAGA) methods to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`. The function `K` is assumed to be smooth with a decomposable gradient structure, while `M` is strongly convex-concave and admits an efficient proximal operator. This problem class is important as it covers many machine learning tasks with non-separable losses or regularizers, which cannot be directly handled by standard SVRG/SAGA applied to a primal or dual formulation.

The key contributions are:
1.  The development of SVRG and SAGA-type algorithms for this saddle-point setting.
2.  A convergence analysis grounded in the theory of monotone operators, which shows that the proposed algorithms converge linearly. This framework is more general and also applies to problems like variational inequalities.
3.  An exploration of different ways to decompose the gradient operator, including factored splits, and the introduction of non-uniform sampling to improve theoretical convergence rates and practical performance.
4.  An acceleration scheme based on the "Catalyst" framework, which improves the complexity and demonstrates a clear theoretical advantage over accelerated batch methods.

The work is positioned relative to batch forward-backward methods for saddle-point problems and standard stochastic gradient methods, which achieve sublinear rates. It aims to be the first to provide large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this general class of composite, decomposable saddle-point problems.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing the first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems. The ability to handle non-separable functions (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization) where traditional primal/dual SVRG/SAGA fail is a major advance. The theoretical results are strong and the derived complexity bounds are state-of-the-art.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The analysis via monotone operators is elegant and powerful. It provides a unified proof for a class of problems broader than just saddle-point formulations. The inclusion and analysis of non-uniform sampling and acceleration are thorough and demonstrate a clear theoretical advantage over baseline methods.
*   **Potential Impact:** The proposed algorithms are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. They offer practitioners a new set of powerful tools for structured prediction, robust optimization, and other areas where non-separable objectives are common.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's presentation could be significantly improved. The connection to monotone operators is fundamental to the entire convergence analysis, yet it is relegated to Section 6 and presented as an "extension." This makes the motivation for the proof techniques in the appendix confusing. This connection should be introduced much earlier, perhaps in Section 2, to provide a clear theoretical foundation for the reader. The transition from the problem assumptions (A)-(C) to the strong monotonicity assumption used in the proofs is also not clearly established.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper is missing a discussion of and comparison to a relevant line of work on stochastic primal-dual methods, particularly those that can be viewed as stochastic versions of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm. For example, the work of Zhang & Xiao ("Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization," JMLR 2015; and a 2014 ICML paper) presents linearly convergent methods for a related class of separable saddle-point problems. While the authors' setting is more general (non-separable `M`), the relationship and key differences should be explicitly discussed and, if possible, compared empirically.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are preliminary and not entirely convincing. Using only two datasets is insufficient to demonstrate the broad practical utility of the methods. A more comprehensive experimental section with a wider variety of datasets (especially those with the non-separable structures that motivate the paper) and a broader set of baselines (including stochastic primal-dual methods) would be necessary to substantiate the claims of practical superiority.
*   **Clarity of Assumptions:** The scope and limitations of the assumptions could be better clarified. Specifically, assumption (A) requires the proximal operator for the joint function `M(x, y)` to be computable. It would be helpful to discuss for which interesting machine learning problems this holds. For instance, does the saddle-point formulation of a standard SVM satisfy this assumption?

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**
*   In Theorem 2, the parameter `μ` is not defined in the main text. I presume this is the strong monotonicity constant of the operator `A` from the appendix. If so, this should be explicitly stated. It is also unclear how the convergence rate compares to that of SVRG in Theorem 1 without a clearer definition of `μ`.
*   Could you please explicitly state whether assumptions (A)-(C) are sufficient to imply the strong monotonicity of the operator `A + B` as required by the proofs? The link is not obvious.
*   The notation `L` is used inconsistently. In Section 2.2, it is stated that `σ = 1/L^2`, where `L` is the Lipschitz constant of `B`, but in the text, it is also referred to as the condition number. Please clarify the definition and usage.
*   A citation is needed for the linear convergence rate of the forward-backward algorithm mentioned in Section 2.2. The reference [19] (Chen & Rockafellar) is appropriate, but it would be good to cite it in the text where the rate is first mentioned.
*   How do your proposed algorithms differ fundamentally from standard stochastic primal-dual methods, beyond the variance reduction component? A brief discussion on this could help position the contribution more clearly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  High originality and significance in extending variance reduction to a general class of saddle-point problems.
2.  Strong and novel theoretical results, including linear convergence, non-uniform sampling analysis, and an effective acceleration scheme.
3.  Addresses a practical need for optimizing problems with non-separable losses and regularizers, which is a limitation of many existing methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The presentation is confusing, particularly the delayed introduction of the monotone operator framework, which is central to the analysis.
2.  The paper omits comparison to a key and relevant line of work on stochastic primal-dual methods.
3.  The experimental evaluation is too limited to fully support the paper's claims of practical effectiveness.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the weaknesses in presentation and experimental validation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and novel. The extension of variance-reduced methods to this general saddle-point setting is a significant step forward. I lean towards acceptance but strongly recommend that the authors thoroughly revise the paper to address the clarity and related work issues outlined above. A reorganization that introduces the monotone operator framework earlier would dramatically improve the paper's readability and impact.
