## Review of Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems

This paper proposes extensions of Stochastic Variance Reduction Gradient (SVRG) and Stochastic Average Gradient (SAGA) algorithms to address saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning arising from duality and non-separable formulations. The authors claim to provide the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems, a significant advancement. The core contribution lies in adapting the analysis – moving beyond the traditional convex minimization framework – and leveraging the concept of monotone operators to prove convergence. This broader framework also extends the applicability of the proposed algorithms to variational inequalities.

The work builds upon recent advances in stochastic optimization, particularly SVRG [2] and SAGA [3], which accelerate stochastic gradient descent for finite-sum problems. The authors identify a crucial gap: these existing methods do not directly apply to saddle-point formulations, where the objective function involves both minimization and maximization variables. By cleverly extending these algorithms to the saddle-point setting, they address a practical need in various machine learning applications, like robust optimization and problems with non-separable regularizers. The paper clearly articulates the challenges and opportunities presented by this extension, including the necessity of convex-concave splitting and the importance of non-uniform sampling. Moreover, the authors demonstrate a simple acceleration scheme based on the “catalyst” framework [7], surpassing the performance of accelerated batch algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems, coupled with the use of monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, represents a novel contribution. The authors correctly identify a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is a key strength. Framing the problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator allows for a more general and robust proof, expanding beyond the constraints of convex minimization.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addressing saddle-point problems is highly relevant to a wide range of machine learning tasks, including those involving non-separable losses or regularizers, as the examples in Section 2.1 demonstrate.
*   **Improved Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms, particularly when coupled with non-uniform sampling and the catalyst acceleration, show promise for improved performance compared to existing methods.
*   **Generalization:** The analysis extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, showcasing the broader applicability of the proposed framework.
*   **Clear Identification of Key Aspects:** The paper clearly highlights the importance of convex-concave splitting and non-uniform sampling for efficient algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While the overall structure is sound, certain sections lack sufficient clarity. The discussion of the two “notions of splits” (functions vs. partial derivatives) could benefit from further elaboration.
*   **Implementation Details:**  The description of non-uniform sampling, beyond the bilinear case, is limited. The paper needs to specify how the probability vector π is constructed for more general problems. This is a crucial point as the performance relies heavily on effective sampling.
*   **Experimental Details:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. Specifically, the selection of the step size σ is not clearly explained. Providing justification and sensitivity analysis for this parameter would strengthen the empirical evaluation. The reasoning behind the improved performance with non-uniform sampling *specifically for saddle-point problems* compared to convex minimization also deserves further discussion.
*   **Proof Details:** While the theoretical results are significant, the proofs are relegated to the appendices. Including key proof sketches or highlighting the core ideas within the main text would enhance the paper's accessibility and impact.  
*   **Minor Errors:** Minor typos exist (line 78, k -> d; line 121, extra y_{t-1} term).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is a valuable contribution, and the use of monotone operator theory provides a powerful analytical framework. While clarity and detailed implementation/experimental explanations could be improved, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The presented work has the potential to influence future research in stochastic saddle-point optimization and improve the performance of machine learning algorithms in various applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded solution to a significant problem in machine learning.
*   The convergence analysis using monotone operators is a key innovation and broadens the applicability of the proposed methods.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the potential for improved performance compared to existing approaches.
*   The work is well-motivated and clearly articulates the contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity in certain sections and the limited detail in the experimental setup might hinder reproducibility and understanding.
*   The omission of key proof details in the main text reduces the accessibility of the theoretical results.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns, providing more detail on the non-uniform sampling strategy, clarifying the experimental setup (specifically the step size selection), and potentially including key proof sketches in the main text. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper's impact and accessibility.
