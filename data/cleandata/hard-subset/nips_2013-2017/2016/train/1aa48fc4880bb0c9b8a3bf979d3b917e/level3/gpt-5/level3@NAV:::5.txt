Summary: The paper extends SVRG and SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems and proves the first linear convergence rates for large-scale incremental methods in this setting. Because convex minimization analyses do not directly apply, the authors develop a convergence proof via a monotone-operator framework, which also yields cleaner proofs for forward–backward splitting (and its stochastic variant) and extends beyond saddle points to variational inequalities. The saddle-point formulation enables handling non-separable losses and regularizers through proximal structure, which is important in many ML applications. The paper further studies non-uniform sampling and shows empirically that it is often crucial; an adaptation of the catalyst scheme gives an accelerated SVRG variant that outperforms batch baselines.

Quality: The theory appears sound and carefully argued around monotone operators, with rates and constants tracked and algorithmic details (e.g., factored vs individual splits) clearly tied to complexity. Experiments are limited in breadth but well chosen and consistent with the claims, highlighting the impact of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Clarity: Generally clear and well structured, but several points need clarification in the main text: describe the non-uniform sampling distribution beyond bilinear problems; explain how σ is selected in practice; and provide intuition for why non-uniform sampling matters more here than in standard convex minimization. Given that the algorithmic extensions are simple, including a more self-contained proof sketch in the main paper would help readers assess the key technical ideas.

Originality: High. Interpreting saddle-point problems as zero-finding for a monotone operator to obtain linear rates for SVRG/SAGA is novel, and the analysis generalizes to variational inequalities. The simplified proofs for FB splitting are also of independent interest.

Significance: Strong. Linearly convergent incremental methods for saddle points can impact a broad range of ML problems with non-separable structure. The non-uniform sampling guidance and acceleration recipe are practical contributions.

Questions for authors:
- Beyond bilinear K, what non-uniform π do you recommend? Provide a concrete rule and complexity-sensitive bounds.
- How is σ set in experiments (estimated L vs L̄, backtracking, tuning)? Any robustness observations?
- Why is non-uniform sampling more critical for saddle points than for convex minimization? Provide intuition and, if possible, a small example.

Pros: Novel analysis; broad applicability (variational inequalities); handles non-separable structure; competitive and often superior empirics; clear practical guidance on sampling and acceleration.

Cons: Main novelty is the proof technique; some experimental and presentation details missing.
