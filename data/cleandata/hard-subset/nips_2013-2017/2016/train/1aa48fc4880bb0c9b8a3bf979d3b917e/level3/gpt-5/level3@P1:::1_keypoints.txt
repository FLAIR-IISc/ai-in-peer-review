- Problem: studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with M proximable and strongly convex–concave and K smooth.
- Main contribution: extends SVRG and SAGA to this saddle-point/monotone-operator setting and proves linear convergence without requiring separability.
- Analysis viewpoint: casts saddle-point problem as zeros of A+B (A strongly monotone/prox-friendly, B smooth monotone split as finite sum).
- Splitting choices: introduces and analyzes two natural splits—by functions (individual) and by partial derivatives (factored).
- Non-uniform sampling: emphasizes its importance for tighter complexity bounds and improved empirical performance.
- Acceleration: adapts the catalyst/proximal-point wrapper to obtain an accelerated variant that dominates accelerated batch primal–dual methods in complexity.
- Extensions: techniques generalize to variational inequalities and more general monotone inclusions.
- Relation to prior work: broadens beyond SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization and beyond batch PD/Chambolle–Pock methods by exploiting finite-sum structure.
- Contrast to separable methods: covers non-separable losses/regularizers, unlike SPDC and other separable-coordinate primal–dual methods.
- Strength: novel monotone-operator-based analysis with explicit convergence guarantees and complexity bounds.
- Strength: careful treatment of individual vs. factored splits and practical guidance via matching intuition from matrix sketching.
- Strength: well-motivated acceleration producing improved complexity for ill-conditioned problems.
- Strength: theoretical framework naturally extends beyond saddle points.
- Empirics: two datasets show linear convergence, benefits of non-uniform sampling, and advantage of the accelerated variant under ill-conditioning.
- Exposition issue: monotone-operator viewpoint introduced late; should be presented earlier to clarify why convex minimization analyses fail.
- Assumptions unclear: need clarification on (A)–(C), including when strong convex–concavity holds for common ML problems (hinge/logistic + ℓ2) and separable vs non-separable splits.
- Notation/definitions: L is inconsistently called a “condition number”; must unify L, L̄(π), spectral/Frobenius relations, and define μ, τ, norms, and prox scalings on first use.
- Theorem 2 ambiguity: μ used in rate but not defined in main text—clarify it is the strong-monotonicity parameter and compare SAGA vs SVRG rates as functions of μ, L, L̄(π), and resampling needs.
- Practical concerns: discuss how to estimate L and L̄(π) for stepsizes or provide robust defaults/backtracking if exact values are unavailable.
- Experimental gaps: limited to two datasets and few baselines—request more datasets (varying n,d, conditioning), comparisons to SPDC and accelerated CP variants, and ablations on sampling, mini-batch size, splits, and SAGA memory/computation trade-offs.
- Minor presentation fixes: unify L notation, add forward–backward citations, highlight SAGA resampling detail, and ensure consistent prox/parameter scaling across sections.
- Overall judgment: theory is sound, original, and significant, but clarity on assumptions/constants and broader empirical validation are needed.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong novel theory (linear convergence), practical sampling/splitting insights, and acceleration that outperforms batch PD under broad conditions.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation and expositional weaknesses around assumptions, constants, and central monotone-operator framing.
- Suggested reference: add discussion of Zhang & Xiao (2015) SPDC to contrast separability assumptions and rates.
