### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of solving large-scale convex-concave saddle-point problems, particularly those where the objective function has a finite-sum structure. This problem class is important in machine learning, encompassing applications like robust optimization and models with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., structured output SVMs, total variation regularization).

The main contribution is the extension of recent and highly successful variance-reduction methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to this saddle-point setting. While the algorithmic modifications are straightforward, the theoretical analysis is novel and constitutes a core part of the work. The authors prove linear convergence for their proposed methods under a strong convexity-concavity assumption. The analysis is framed within the more general theory of monotone operators, demonstrating that the algorithms and their convergence guarantees apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.

Furthermore, the paper shows that these new stochastic methods can be accelerated through an extension of the Catalyst framework, leading to algorithms that are theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods. The authors investigate different sampling strategies, highlighting the practical and theoretical benefits of non-uniform sampling. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on binary classification problems with non-separable objectives.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a timely and important problem. While variance-reduction techniques have revolutionized large-scale convex minimization, their application to saddle-point optimization was not well-established. This work provides the first linearly convergent *stochastic* algorithms for finite-sum saddle-point problems, filling a significant gap in the literature and advancing the state-of-the-art.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis is a key strength; moving from the standard analysis of convex minimization to the framework of monotone operators is elegant and powerful. It not only provides a correct proof for the saddle-point case but also generalizes the applicability of SVRG/SAGA to a wider set of problems, which is a valuable insight. The extension of the Catalyst acceleration framework is also a strong and well-integrated contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem with relevant machine learning examples, situates the work within the context of existing batch and stochastic algorithms, and presents its contributions in a logical progression. The theoretical results are stated clearly, and the summary table of complexities (Table 1) is particularly effective at highlighting the advantages of the proposed methods.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** The primary weakness is the assumption that the function `M` is strongly convex-concave. This requires strong convexity with respect to the primal variable `x` *and* strong concavity with respect to the dual variable `y`. While this assumption enables the linear convergence proof, it is overly restrictive and does not hold for many common machine learning problems. For instance, `L1`-regularization (Lasso) is convex but not strongly convex, and many loss functions used in classification (e.g., hinge loss) are not strongly convex either. This limitation significantly narrows the direct applicability of the proposed methods in their current form.

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experiments effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over baselines on the chosen tasks, the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The experiments are focused on a custom AUC-like loss and a clustering-inducing regularizer. While interesting, this makes it difficult to assess performance on more standard saddle-point problems and to compare against a wider array of specialized solvers.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

-   **Relaxing the Strong Convexity Assumption:** The paper would be significantly more impactful if the analysis were extended to the case where strong convexity is assumed on only one side (i.e., only in the primal *or* the dual variable). This setting is far more common in practice, covering, for example, `L2`-regularized empirical risk minimization with a non-strongly-convex loss. Even a sub-linear convergence rate under this weaker assumption would be a valuable result and would greatly broaden the applicability of this work. The authors mention this as future work, but I encourage them to consider it a high-priority extension.

-   **Expanding Numerical Experiments:** To strengthen the paper's empirical claims, I recommend including more diverse experiments.
    *   It would be beneficial to test the algorithms on problems where the primal and/or dual forms are explicitly known and can be solved by standard SVRG/SAGA. The `rcv1` experiment is a good start, but more such comparisons would better delineate the practical performance trade-offs.
    *   Including experiments on canonical saddle-point problems, such as constrained optimization formulations (e.g., `L1`-regularized problems solved via their saddle-point formulation, even if it requires modifications to handle the non-strong convexity) or applications in robust optimization, would provide a more complete picture of the algorithms' performance.
    *   A more detailed study on the effect of acceleration would be welcome, for example, by showing how the performance gap between the accelerated and non-accelerated versions changes as the problem's condition number `L` varies.

### **Final Assessment**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The extension of variance-reduction methods to the saddle-point setting is an important step forward. The theoretical analysis is solid and insightful. The main criticism is the restrictive strong convexity-concavity assumption, which limits the immediate practical impact. However, the paper lays excellent groundwork for future research in this direction. The strengths of originality, technical quality, and significance far outweigh the weaknesses.
