This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods – specifically extensions of SVRG and SAGA – to tackle large-scale saddle-point problems, common in machine learning. The core contribution lies in providing the first linear convergence guarantees for these algorithms in this setting, a significant step forward given the prevalence of saddle-point formulations in areas like regularized learning with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors also demonstrate the broader applicability of their analysis through the lens of monotone operators, extending the results to variational inequalities.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods when applied to non-separable problems. The connection to related work is strong, referencing key papers in stochastic gradient descent, variance reduction (SAG, SVRG, SAGA), and convex/monotone operator theory. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of optimization and highlight relevant applications in machine learning. The introduction of two distinct notions of “splits” – in terms of functions versus partial derivatives – is a valuable contribution, deepening the understanding of how these algorithms can be adapted to saddle-point problems.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, building upon the foundation of monotone operator theory to establish convergence. The extension beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities demonstrates the generality of the approach. The emphasis on non-uniform sampling, supported by both theoretical results and experimental validation, is crucial for practical efficiency. The acceleration technique, building on the “catalyst” framework, further enhances the algorithm’s performance. The experimental evaluation, while detailed and presented in the appendix, provides compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly in comparison to batch and stochastic alternatives. The clear identification of the benefits of factored splits versus element-wise splits in the context of bilinear problems is insightful.

However, there are a few weaknesses. While the analysis is sound, the reliance on monotone operator theory makes the paper less accessible to readers unfamiliar with this area. The notation, specifically the repeated use of ‘K’ for both a smooth function and a matrix, introduces a potential for confusion, though it is largely mitigated by context. The discussion of adaptive updates for the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in the acceleration section (Section 5) feels incomplete; more detail on practical implementation strategies would be beneficial. The authors mention that similar acceleration techniques *should* extend to SAGA, but a formal proof would strengthen the claim.  A minor point is that the paper assumes strong convexity-concavity; exploring relaxation of this assumption, even briefly, would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with proven linear convergence is a novel and significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous, leveraging monotone operator theory to establish convergence guarantees.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The methods address a common challenge in machine learning – solving non-separable problems – and demonstrate empirical improvements over existing approaches.
*   **Generality:** The extension to variational inequalities expands the applicability of the research.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed algorithms and their theoretical foundations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Accessibility:** The reliance on monotone operator theory might limit accessibility for some readers.
*   **Incomplete Discussion:** The discussion of adaptive updates in the acceleration section is insufficient.
*   **Lack of Proof:** The claim regarding extending the acceleration to SAGA lacks a formal proof.
*   **Notational Ambiguity:** The use of the same symbol 'K' for different concepts could cause confusion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The proposed algorithms are theoretically sound, practically relevant, and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods for solving saddle-point problems. While minor improvements could be made regarding accessibility, completeness, and notation, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above (clarifying the adaptive update strategy and providing a proof for acceleration with SAGA). The paper is of high quality and aligns well with the interests of the NIPS community.
