

**Review**

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors make several important contributions: they provide a convergence analysis using monotone operators (which is more general than standard convex optimization analysis), identify two different ways to split the problem (function-wise vs. derivative-wise), show the importance of non-uniform sampling, and extend the algorithms to be accelerated using the "catalyst" framework.

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant contribution to the literature. The authors correctly identify that the standard convex minimization analysis does not apply to saddle-point problems, and they successfully develop a novel analysis using monotone operators. The theoretical results are well-supported by experiments that demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, particularly for non-separable problems where traditional approaches fail.

The paper's strengths include:
1. Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with proven linear convergence.
2. Insightful distinction between saddle-point problems and convex minimization (e.g., two different split approaches).
3. Demonstrated importance of non-uniform sampling, both theoretically and empirically.
4. Practical algorithms that handle non-separable loss and regularizer functions, which are common in applications like robust optimization and structured prediction.
5. Clear experimental results showing significant improvements over existing methods.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses:
1. The notation uses K for both the design matrix and the function, which could be confusing (e.g., K(x,y) vs. the design matrix K). Using distinct symbols would improve clarity.
2. The acceleration technique in Section 5 requires the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) to be updated at specific intervals, but the paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to adaptively choose these intervals for optimal performance.
3. The paper states that the convergence analysis for SVRG extends to SAGA, but the theoretical justification for this is not fully elaborated. It would be helpful to clarify whether the same convergence rate holds for SAGA.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear structure that guides the reader through the technical content. The experimental section provides sufficient detail to reproduce the results, and the comparisons with existing methods are thorough.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a gap in the literature for large-scale saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning but haven't been addressed by efficient variance-reduction methods before. The algorithms can be applied to a wide range of machine learning problems, including non-separable losses (e.g., AUC optimization) and non-separable regularizers (e.g., grouped norms), where traditional methods fail. The extension to variational inequalities also broadens the applicability of these methods beyond saddle-point problems.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with significant implications for machine learning applications. The authors have carefully related their work to previous NIPS papers (e.g., SAGA [3], SVRG [2], and the catalyst framework [7]) and demonstrated how their approach differs from and improves upon them.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is convincing, and the practical implications are clear. The minor notational issues and the lack of detailed guidance on adaptive reference iterate updates are not enough to detract from the paper's overall quality.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Pro (for acceptance):**
- First linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems
- Rigorous convergence analysis using monotone operators
- Clear experimental evidence of practical advantages
- Broad applicability to non-separable machine learning problems
- Extension to variational inequalities

**Con (against acceptance):**
- Notational confusion with K (design matrix vs. function)
- Limited guidance on adaptive reference iterate updates in acceleration
- Slightly unclear theoretical extension from SVRG to SAGA

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with clear theoretical and practical implications. I believe it deserves acceptance at NIPS 2016.
