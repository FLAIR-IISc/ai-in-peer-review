This paper presents an extension of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The authors provide a convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory, demonstrating linear convergence, and propose an acceleration technique. The work addresses a practically relevant problem and offers valuable theoretical insights, particularly regarding the applicability of these algorithms to a broader class of problems beyond traditional saddle-point formulations.

The core idea is to adapt the existing SVRG and SAGA algorithms to handle the min-max structure of saddle-point problems. This involves careful consideration of the splitting of the objective function and the use of non-uniform sampling for efficiency. The analysis cleverly utilizes the framework of monotone operators, which allows for a more general convergence proof applicable to variational inequalities. The proposed acceleration scheme builds upon the “catalyst” framework, leading to performance improvements over standard batch algorithms.

This work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in stochastic optimization. The authors correctly situate their contribution within the landscape of methods like SAG [1], SVRG [2], and SAGA [3], emphasizing the limitations of these methods when applied to non-separable problems. The connection to monotone operator theory draws upon work in convex analysis [8, 28] and is a key novelty. Furthermore, the discussion of related algorithms, such as the Forward-Backward algorithm [19, 20] and accelerated variants, provides valuable context.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis, based on monotone operator theory, is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The extension beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities is a notable achievement.
*   **Novelty:** Applying stochastic variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems with a convergence guarantee is a significant contribution. The analysis of different splitting strategies and the importance of non-uniform sampling are insightful.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Saddle-point problems arise frequently in machine learning, making this work applicable to a wide range of applications.
*   **Acceleration Technique:** The extension of the catalyst framework offers a practical improvement in performance, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper suffers from occasional lack of clarity. The connection between the saddle-point formulation and the monotone operator framework feels somewhat delayed and abrupt. More explicit motivation for the monotone operator approach early in the paper would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is relatively limited, using only two datasets. While the results are promising, a more extensive evaluation with a wider range of datasets and problem settings would strengthen the claims.  Comparisons to more recent and relevant algorithms, like stochastic Chambolle-Pock type methods [23, 35] would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions and Applicability:** The assumptions (A)-(C) require further clarification. It would be helpful to discuss more explicitly whether these assumptions hold for common machine learning problems, such as SVMs, and if not, under what conditions they might be relaxed. The strength and practical implications of these assumptions deserve more discussion.
*   **Comparison to Primal-Dual Methods:** The distinction between the authors’ approach and standard stochastic primal-dual methods could be clearer. A more detailed comparison of the algorithms and their respective strengths and weaknesses would be beneficial.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The use of 'L' to denote both the condition number and a Lipschitz constant is confusing. A more distinct notation would improve readability.
*   **Missing References**: The Forward-Backward algorithm is mentioned without a citation in the initial discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental results, while limited, are promising and support the theoretical claims.
*   The work provides valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities of applying stochastic variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.
*   Further discussion of the assumptions and their implications is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the proposed algorithms offer a promising approach to solving saddle-point problems. While the presentation and experimental evaluation could be improved, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity issues, expanding the experimental evaluation, and providing a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and comparisons to related methods.
