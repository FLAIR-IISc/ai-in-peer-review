- Paper extends SVRG/SAGA and Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave composite saddle-point problems with prox-friendly M.  
- Key novelty: handles two splitting models for the smooth operator B — individual splits (per-entry) and factored splits (row/column blocks) — with different storage/sampling trade-offs.  
- The analysis reframes saddle-point optimization as finding zeros of a strongly monotone operator A+B and proves linear convergence via monotone operator theory, extending to variational inequalities.  
- Provides iteration complexity bounds for uniform vs non-uniform sampling, introduces mini-batching, and derives Catalyst-like accelerated SVRG with improved smoothness/oracle dependence.  
- In bilinear cases, results align with convex minimization intuition: non-uniform sampling tightens the effective smoothness L̄ and accelerated SVRG attains the best theoretical running time with norm-based bounds (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max).  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are necessary to beat batch forward–backward; uniform sampling often fails.  

Strengths:
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for general non-separable strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Monotone-operator framing is technically novel and broadens applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Thorough complexity analysis covering split types, sampling regimes, and mini-batching with clear norm-dependent constants.  
- Simple, well-analyzed Catalyst-style acceleration avoids delicate SP-specific Nesterov machinery and yields provable gains.  
- Clear bilinear-case comparisons and practical guidance on sampling and norms.  
- Empirical validation isolates benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, and compares to batch and primal methods.

Weaknesses / concerns:
- Proximal bottleneck: each incremental update requires prox_M (cost O(n+d)), which can dominate and erase incremental methods' wall-clock advantage.  
- Ambiguity on when methods beat accelerated batch FB: superiority depends critically on prox cost and problem dimensions despite theoretical claims.  
- Split-choice trade-offs underexplored: factored vs individual may flip depending on n vs d, sparsity, storage, and per-sample cost.  
- Assumptions require known strong convexity–concavity and exact prox; no treatment of adaptivity or inexact/coordinate-friendly prox variants.

Requested deeper discussions (authors addressed in rebuttal):
- (i) Joint comparison of factored vs individual splits needed: L̄ scaling (factored ≲ ‖K‖F/√(λγ) vs individual ≲ √max{n,d}‖K‖F/√(λγ)), oracle costs (T(Bi) and SAGA storage O(nd) vs O(n+d)), and explicit regimes where each split is preferable.  
- (ii) Precise inequality showing when accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB using cost model, e.g., compare L̄ √(T(B) max_i T(Bi)) + c Tprox(M) vs c′(T(B)+Tprox(M)).  
- (iii) Tightness in n,d: provide lower-bound examples (flat spectrum or spiky rows) to justify √max{n,d} gaps and discuss whether improved sketching/sampling can close gaps or if bounds are information-theoretically tight.

Quality:
- Technical development and proofs are sound; monotone-operator route is insightful and experiments are appropriate.  
- Main practical caveat is prox cost; discussing inexact/coordinate prox variants would strengthen the work.

Clarity / presentation suggestions:
- Make the cost model (T(B), T(Bi), Tprox(M)) and its wall-clock implications more prominent.  
- Summarize split/sampling trade-offs in a compact table with iteration and wall-clock perspectives and state SAGA memory costs per split.  
- Expand the “when do we beat batch?” discussion with concrete inequalities.

Originality and significance:
- High originality: novel extension of SVRG/SAGA/Catalyst to non-separable SP via monotone operators.  
- High theoretical significance and likely to influence stochastic SP and VI research; practical impact depends on availability of cheap prox operators.

Pros for acceptance:
- First general linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for strongly convex–concave finite-sum SPs with clean monotone-operator analysis and principled acceleration, backed by experiments.

Cons against acceptance:
- Proximal bottleneck may eliminate practical gains over accelerated batch FB.  
- Insufficient practical guidance on split choice and no treatment of inexact/coordinate prox or adaptivity.

Final recommendation:
- Weak accept: substantial, technically solid contribution with practical caveats; reviewer satisfied by rebuttal responses but keeps scores unchanged.
