Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is proximable and strongly convex–concave and K is smooth but possibly non-separable. It extends variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG/SAGA) to this setting, provides the first linear-convergence analyses for such saddle-point problems, and shows that the analysis naturally fits within the broader framework of finding zeros of strongly monotone operators. Key technical ingredients: (i) use of monotone operator theory rather than cocoercivity, (ii) two notions of splitting B=∑i Bi either at the function level or at the level of partial derivatives (including factored row/column splits for bilinear K), (iii) the necessity and benefits of non-uniform sampling, and (iv) an acceleration scheme via a Catalyst-like proximal-point outer loop tailored to saddle-point problems. The paper also discusses practical storage for SAGA under factored splits and provides experiments on two datasets with non-separable losses/regularizers.

This work is closely related to variance-reduction for finite-sum minimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) and primal–dual splitting methods (Chambolle–Pock and variants). Compared to stochastic primal–dual/coordinate methods such as SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015), which require separability and exploit primal–dual structures of ERM, the present contribution targets non-separable losses/regularizers via saddle-point formulations and allows splits that are not gradients of individual potentials. It is also complementary to stochastic forward–backward and (accelerated) mirror-prox–type methods for variational inequalities; the paper argues improved linear rates via variance reduction when strong monotonicity is present.

Quality
- Theory: The linear convergence proofs for both SVRG and SAGA under strong convex–concavity/strong monotonicity appear technically solid and novel for this setting. The monotone-operator perspective is well-motivated and broadens applicability beyond saddle points (e.g., variational inequalities).
- Algorithms: The extension of SVRG/SAGA is clean, with careful treatment of factored vs individual splits and non-uniform sampling. The Catalyst-style acceleration via proximal-point is elegant and simpler than Nesterov-style machinery in this setting.
- Assumptions: Assumption (A) requires M to be strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with an efficient proximal. Please clarify how often (A) holds in standard ML problems. For example, in SVMs with hinge loss, g is not strongly convex; do you require explicit smoothing or additional quadratic terms in the dual to ensure strong concavity? A short “when (A) holds” checklist would help practitioners.
- Constants and notation: In Theorem 2 the constant μ appears in the rate; please define μ precisely in the main text (is it the strong monotonicity parameter of A+B? How does it relate to λ and γ?). The transition from (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is currently confusing—state explicitly in the main text that (A)–(B) imply strong monotonicity of the composite operator (with constant μ), or provide the exact conditions under which this holds.
- Empirics: The experiments demonstrate benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, but they are limited (two datasets). The claims would be stronger with more datasets and comparisons, especially to accelerated primal–dual methods:
  - SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015) and related stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods for ERM.
  - Accelerated Chambolle–Pock–type methods (PDHG with acceleration) in batch and stochastic/coordinate variants.
  - Stochastic mirror-prox or accelerated mirror-prox baselines when applicable.
Include wall-clock comparisons, ablations on sampling (uniform vs non-uniform) and on split types (individual vs factored), and report proximal costs.

Clarity
- Organization: The monotone-operator view underpins the analysis; consider moving it earlier (or adding a brief overview in the introduction) so readers understand why cocoercivity is not required and how the rates are obtained.
- Notation: The letter L is used both as a Lipschitz constant for B (in Ω/Ω* norms) and informally as a “condition number.” Please avoid calling L a condition number; if you wish to discuss conditioning, define condition number explicitly in terms of L and μ. Also ensure consistent use of L vs L̄(π) and make the dependence on sampling explicit wherever rates/stepsizes appear.
- Forward–Backward references: While you cite Chen & Rockafellar (1997) and Chambolle–Pock, please add a standard forward–backward splitting reference (e.g., Combettes & Wajs, 2005; Bauschke & Combettes monograph) when introducing FB.
- Theorem 2: Clarify the role of resampling with non-uniform sampling; the text says “optional,” but the theorem requires resampling to maintain the bound. Please state this requirement explicitly and discuss practical deviations (as in your experiments).
- Assumption (C): It is a strength that the split need not correspond to gradients of scalar functions. Please clarify the precise requirements for the factored split and how this affects storage/update rules in SAGA.

Originality
- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems with linear convergence under strong monotonicity, without requiring cocoercivity of each component and allowing “derivative-only” splits. The Catalyst-style acceleration tailored to saddle-point proximal-point steps is also a nice contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first variance-reduced, linearly convergent, large-scale method applicable to non-separable saddle-point structures with proximable M.

Significance
- High potential impact. Many important ML formulations (AUC surrogates, overlapping group penalties, TV, robust optimization) fit the proposed framework when their prox is available. The non-uniform sampling insights and factored splits for bilinear K should be useful in practice. The extension to general monotone operators and variational inequalities broadens reach beyond ML.

Pros
- Sound, general analysis via monotone operators; linear rates for SVRG/SAGA.
- Handles non-separable losses/regularizers with proximal M; flexible splitting, including factored splits.
- Clear guidance and theory for non-uniform sampling; practical acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst.
- Extension to variational inequalities.

Cons
- Clarity issues around assumptions, strong monotonicity, and constants (μ, L, L̄).
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope; missing key baselines (SPDC, accelerated PDHG, mirror-prox variants).
- Some confusion about the necessity vs optionality of resampling in SAGA with non-uniform sampling.
- Notation occasionally calls L a “condition number,” which is misleading.

Questions and requests for clarification
1) Define μ in Theorem 2 in the main text and relate it explicitly to (λ,γ) and the monotonicity of A+B. Do (A)–(B) imply μ>0 automatically?
2) Under what common setups (e.g., SVM hinge/logistic, AUC surrogates, overlapping group lasso) does assumption (A) hold without additional smoothing? If smoothing is required, how does it affect rates?
3) For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, is resampling strictly required for correctness of the bound? If not, what weaker guarantees hold without it?
4) Provide guidance for choosing between individual vs factored splits in practice and discuss memory/runtime trade-offs for SAGA.

Minor comments
- Please unify the definitions of L and L̄(π) early and use one symbol consistently for the Lipschitz constant.
- Clearly state the per-iteration cost model, including Tprox(M), and report it in experiments.
- Add a reference to SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015) and discuss in related work; consider including it as a baseline where applicable.

Overall evaluation
A strong, technically solid contribution that advances stochastic variance reduction to important non-separable saddle-point settings with clear theoretical gains and practical design choices (splits, sampling, acceleration). With improved clarity and a more comprehensive empirical comparison (including accelerated primal–dual baselines), I would be in favor of acceptance.
