- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored splits.
- Novel analysis via monotone-operator framework (avoids co-coercivity), yielding linear rates and unifying variational inequalities.
- In-depth bilinear-case treatment with uniform vs. nonuniform sampling; complexity bounds mirror convex-minimization intuitions.
- Experiments on binary classification show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are necessary to outperform batch forward–backward (FB).
- Technical development is careful and largely sound; monotone-operator approach is appropriate and clarifying.
- Complexity bounds are thorough across split types and sampling schemes.
- Practical bottleneck: each incremental step requires an O(n + d) proximal operation, which can dominate runtime and blunt theoretical gains.
- Because of prox cost, accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling may not improve over accelerated batch FB in practice.
- Factored splits can perform worse than individual splits when n ≫ d (beyond storage concerns).
- Reviewer requested expanded discussion on: (i) whether factored splits are intrinsically worse than individual splits in theory, (ii) precise regimes where accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB given prox cost, and (iii) tightness of n, d dependence in the rates.
- Suggested mitigation strategies for prox cost: block/coordinate-friendly prox, inexact prox with controlled errors, or problem reformulations.
- Clarity is good overall; reviewer asks for an explicit guidance section on choosing individual vs. factored splits and setting nonuniform sampling.
- Originality: the Catalyst adaptation and linear-convergence extension to saddle points are novel and non-trivial relative to prior SAG/SVRG/SAGA and primal–dual work.
- Significance hinges on alleviating the proximal bottleneck; experiments are promising but limited—additional benchmarks would strengthen impact.
- Pros summarized: novel variance-reduced/accelerated methods for saddle points, valuable monotone-operator analysis, and sharp bilinear/sampling comparisons.
- Cons summarized: O(n + d) proximal bottleneck per iteration, potential underperformance of factored splits when n ≫ d, and limited empirical breadth.
- Post-rebuttal: authors satisfactorily addressed the three requested discussion points, but the reviewer’s overall assessment remains unchanged.
