Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M admits a proximal operator, and extends SVRG/SAGA with proximal steps to this setting. The core idea is to analyze these methods via monotone operator theory: cast the problem as finding a zero of A+B, where A is the (strongly) monotone subdifferential of M and B is a monotone, Lipschitz continuous forward operator derived from K. This allows linear convergence results without relying on co-coercivity, and the analysis extends beyond saddle-point problems to general variational inequalities. The paper also develops importance sampling, factored vs. elemental splits of B, and an acceleration scheme via a Catalyst-style proximal point regularization, yielding iteration complexity improvements over batch FB and accelerated FB. The work is related to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization (Le Roux et al. 2012; Johnson & Zhang 2013; Defazio et al. 2014), stochastic and primal–dual methods for separable saddle problems (e.g., Chambolle–Pock 2011; SPDC by Zhang & Xiao, 2015/2017), and generalized monotone operator splitting (Bauschke & Combettes; Raguet et al. 2013). The paper’s novelty is in handling non-separable K/M, allowing splits at the level of partial derivatives, and proving global linear rates in a general monotone inclusion setting.

Strengths
- Significant theoretical contribution: first linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems, with a clean monotone operator-based proof that also covers variational inequalities.
- Practical algorithmic insights: non-uniform sampling and factored splits; clear complexity expressions highlighting when variance reduction beats batch/accelerated FB.
- Acceleration via a proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper is simple and compelling for ill-conditioned problems; theory aligns with practice in the reported experiments.
- Broad applicability beyond standard separable ERM: supports non-separable losses and regularizers with efficient prox.

Weaknesses and questions
- Presentation: the monotone operator connection underpins all proofs but only appears “as an extension” in Section 6. This makes the analysis feel disconnected earlier on.
- Experimental scope is limited (two datasets), with sparse comparisons to state-of-the-art stochastic primal–dual methods, especially accelerated stochastic CP-type methods.
- Assumptions (A)–(C) need a clearer discussion of strength and practical applicability (notably when and how (A) holds).
- Some notational inconsistencies (use of L) and missing standard references (forward–backward splitting).

Detailed comments and requests for clarification
1) Relation to monotone operators. Please bring the monotone inclusion perspective to the main text earlier. Explicitly state that the analysis only needs: A maximal µ-strongly monotone A with resolvent (I+σA)−1; Bi Lipschitz; B=∑Bi monotone. Then saddle points are a corollary (Rockafellar, 1976). This would also clarify why co-coercivity is not assumed.

2) Assumptions (A)–(C).
- (A) Strong convex–concavity: In practice, when do we get γ-strong concavity in y? For SVM-type problems, the typical saddle formulation via Fenchel dual has g that is not strongly convex (box constraints), so strong concavity fails unless one adds a small quadratic (smoothing). Please discuss whether your rates hold with such additive smoothing (and the dependence on the added γ), or whether you can adopt restarting/local growth conditions.
- (B) L and interpretation: you define L via the Ω/Ω* operator Lipschitz constant yet sometimes refer to L as a “condition number” and use σ=1/L2. Please unify the role of L (Lipschitz constant of B in the Ω→Ω* norm), and distinguish it from condition numbers like L/µ.
- (C) Splits at operator level: Very nice generality. Please add guidance for practitioners: when do factored vs. elemental splits reduce memory/time? Any heuristics for p,q in factored splits beyond the given row/column norms?

3) Theorem 2 and the constant μ. μ is not defined in the theorem statement. Please confirm that μ is the strong monotonicity constant of A+B in the Ω-norm (likely μ=1 if you normalize by λ,γ, or μ=min{λ,γ} before normalization). Also explain why Theorem 1’s rate hides µ (because σ depends on L and the contraction is fixed per epoch), whereas Theorem 2’s per-iteration contraction depends explicitly on L/µ and L̄/(µ√m). A short paragraph comparing the dependencies in Thm 1 vs Thm 2 would help.

4) Do (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity? The appendix assumes strong monotonicity to invoke proximal point arguments. Please state explicitly in the main text that (A) implies A is strongly monotone (with parameter µ tied to λ,γ in the Ω-norm), and that B is monotone under (B), thus A+B is strongly monotone. If additional conditions are needed, spell them out.

5) Comparison to stochastic primal–dual and accelerated CP-type methods. Please position against and, if possible, compare empirically to:
- SPDC: Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 18(1):1–45, 2017 (earlier ICML 2015), a stochastic Chambolle–Pock-type method with acceleration and linear rates for separable ERM.
- Variants of accelerated stochastic primal–dual coordinate descent (e.g., Zhu & Storkey 2014; other CP accelerations).
Discuss similarities/differences: your methods handle non-separable K/M and only require the prox of M and forward evaluations of B; SPDC assumes separability and different sampling/duality gaps. Clarify when your bounds dominate theirs (e.g., non-separable settings where SPDC is inapplicable).

6) Experiments. Two datasets are insufficient to substantiate the breadth of claims. Please:
- Add more tasks highlighting non-separability and efficient prox (overlapping group sparsity, TV, submodular norms; structured AUC/AUPRC).
- Include comparisons to SPDC (and CP variants) on problems where they apply; include primal SAGA/SDCA baselines on separable problems.
- Report wall-clock time and PD-gap in addition to “passes”; ablate non-uniform vs uniform sampling, mini-batch size m, and factored vs elemental splits; include memory overheads for SAGA.

7) Differences vs standard primal–dual methods. Please clearly articulate how your variance-reduced FB differs from classical PD/CP: no need for separability; operator-level splits; linear convergence under strong monotonicity; non-uniform sampling and Catalyst acceleration. A short subsection contrasting update formulas and assumptions would improve clarity.

8) Minor issues.
- Notation: L is used both as a Lipschitz constant and informally as a condition number. Consider reserving κ=L/µ for condition number and keep L for Lipschitz.
- Forward–Backward reference: add a standard reference for FB splitting (e.g., Combettes & Wajs, 2005; Bauschke & Combettes, 2011; or Tseng, 2000).

Evaluation
- Quality: Strong theoretical results, sound and carefully argued. Some gaps in definitions (μ) and presentation need fixing. Experimental validation is too limited.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but the organization obscures the central monotone-operator idea; tighten notation and define constants precisely.
- Originality: High. Extends SVRG/SAGA in a genuinely new way to general saddle-point/monotone operator settings with linear rates and acceleration.
- Significance: High. Likely to influence both theory (monotone operator perspective) and practice for non-separable machine learning models.

Pros for acceptance
- First linear-rate variance-reduced methods for general saddle-point problems; clean analysis.
- Acceleration and non-uniform sampling are well-motivated and impactful.
- Broad applicability (non-separable losses/regularizers; variational inequalities).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experiments and missing key comparisons (SPDC/accelerated CP).
- Presentation/notation issues that may hinder accessibility.
- Assumption (A) practicality (e.g., SVM-like problems) not fully discussed.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. I lean accept contingent on clarifying μ and the monotone connection, improving related work (including SPDC), and expanding experiments. Confidence: medium.
