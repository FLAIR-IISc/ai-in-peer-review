- Proposes extending SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, yielding the first large-scale, linearly convergent incremental methods for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for a rigorous, general convergence analysis applicable beyond saddle-point problems (e.g., variational inequalities).  
- Analyzes two splitting strategies ("individual" and "factored") and shows the importance of non-uniform sampling.  
- Extends the "catalyst" acceleration framework to accelerate the proposed methods.  
- Novelty is high: fills a gap by bringing variance reduction to saddle-point optimization with linear convergence.  
- Technical contribution is strong and non-trivial; analysis is elegant and more general than co-coercivity-based approaches.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with a motivating introduction and understandable algorithm descriptions.  
- Experiments are limited to a single problem type but demonstrate benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.  
- Main practical weakness: per-iteration cost of computing prox_M is O(n + d), which each incremental iteration requires.  
- This O(n + d) proximal cost challenges the claim that the methods are "always superior" to accelerated batch algorithms.  
- Total complexity comparisons show an additive (n + d) term that can make gains modest unless problem is extremely ill-conditioned.  
- Acceptance arguments: strong novelty, rigorous monotone-operator-based analysis, and thorough study of splits/sampling and acceleration.  
- Rejection arguments: practical advantage is unclear due to proximal cost, and some claims of universal superiority appear overstated.  
- Requested clarifications: compare convergence rates for factored vs individual splits, especially when n >> d.  
- Requested clarifications: provide detailed complexity regimes (n, d, condition number L) where accelerated SVRG outperforms accelerated batch methods, explicitly including Tprox(M)=O(n+d).  
- Requested clarifications: clarify whether the O(n + d) cost for prox_M is fundamental for motivating non-separable examples or can be reduced in some cases.
