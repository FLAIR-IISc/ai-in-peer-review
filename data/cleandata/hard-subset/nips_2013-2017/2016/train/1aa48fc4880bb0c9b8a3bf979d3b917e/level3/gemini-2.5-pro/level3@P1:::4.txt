This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. This is the first work to provide linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for this class of problems, which are common in machine learning but often feature non-separable objectives that preclude the direct application of existing variance-reduced methods. The authors' contribution is not a mere algorithmic extension; they provide a novel convergence analysis rooted in the theory of monotone operators, which is more general and suitable for saddle-point problems than the standard analyses used for convex minimization. Key contributions include the proposal of two distinct splitting schemes for the gradient operator ("element-wise" and "factored"), a demonstration that non-uniform sampling is critical for achieving optimal theoretical rates and practical performance, and an extension of the "Catalyst" acceleration framework to further improve convergence. The work is positioned as a significant advancement over existing batch algorithms, which are slow for large datasets, and standard stochastic algorithms, which only achieve sub-linear convergence rates.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a crucial gap in the optimization literature. While variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA have had a major impact on large-scale convex minimization, their application to the broader class of saddle-point problems was an important open area. By providing the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this setting, the authors enable efficient training for a wide range of machine learning models with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., certain structured output problems, robust optimization), which were previously intractable for such fast methods.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength of this work. The authors correctly recognize that the convergence proofs from the convex minimization setting do not carry over. Their use of the monotone operator framework is sophisticated and appropriate, providing a sound basis for their convergence claims. This approach not only validates their algorithms for saddle-point problems but also demonstrates their applicability to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities, which is a valuable insight.

3.  **Comprehensive Treatment of the Problem:** The paper is thorough in its investigation. It analyzes both SVRG and SAGA variants, explores the practical implications of different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform), and considers different ways to decompose the problem structure (factored vs. individual splits). The inclusion of an accelerated variant using the Catalyst framework further strengthens the contribution, resulting in an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods in many cases.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The theoretical results are backed by solid experimental evidence. The experiments on the `sido` and `rcv1` datasets clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods. In particular, the results confirm that: (i) non-uniform sampling offers a significant performance boost over uniform sampling, (ii) the proposed SAGA and SVRG methods outperform existing batch and stochastic baselines, and (iii) the accelerated SVRG variant yields the best performance, especially in ill-conditioned settings. This validation provides confidence in the practical utility of the proposed algorithms.

### Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Adaptive Acceleration:** In Section 5, the paper states that the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in the Catalyst framework can be updated "more aggressively to speed-up the algorithm." This is an intriguing practical point, but the manuscript provides no further details on how such an adaptive update scheme would work. For reproducibility and practical implementation, it would be highly beneficial to elaborate on this, perhaps by providing a specific heuristic or a more formal adaptive rule.

2.  **Acceleration for SAGA:** The acceleration scheme is formally developed and proven for the SVRG-based algorithm. The authors claim that "the same scheme should work for SAGA," but a proof or a more detailed argument is not provided. While this is a plausible claim, the paper would be stronger if it either included a formal analysis for accelerating SAGA or provided a more detailed justification for why the SVRG analysis extends directly. As it stands, this part of the contribution feels slightly incomplete.

3.  **Minor Notational Ambiguity:** The manuscript uses the symbol `K` to represent both the smooth function `K(x, y)` in the saddle-point formulation and the design matrix in the examples of bilinear problems. While context usually makes the meaning clear, using distinct symbols (e.g., K for the function and a different letter like `A` or `X` for the data matrix) would improve clarity and remove any potential for confusion for the reader.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and timely contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. It successfully bridges the gap between variance-reduced stochastic methods and saddle-point optimization, providing a new set of powerful tools for the machine learning community. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the proposed algorithms. The identified weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to points of clarification or completeness that do not detract from the core strength and impact of the work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of high significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
