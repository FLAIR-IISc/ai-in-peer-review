This paper proposes the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for solving a broad class of convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors extend the popular variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to this setting. While the algorithmic extensions are straightforward, the main contribution lies in a novel convergence analysis. Unlike previous analyses for SVRG/SAGA which are specific to convex minimization, this paper provides a proof of linear convergence using the more general framework of monotone operators. This not only covers saddle-point problems but also generalizes to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper further shows that these algorithms can be accelerated using the Catalyst framework and demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is key to achieving state-of-the-art performance, outperforming existing batch and stochastic methods. The work addresses an important gap in large-scale optimization, enabling the efficient solution of problems with non-separable losses or regularizers, which are common in machine learning but cannot be tackled by standard variance-reduced methods.

**Quality:**
The paper is of high quality and appears technically sound. The central claim of achieving linear convergence for saddle-point problems with stochastic variance-reduced methods is a significant result, and it is well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The use of monotone operator theory is an elegant and powerful way to establish convergence in this more complex setting where standard potential function arguments for convex minimization do not apply. The work is a complete contribution, including theoretical analysis, algorithmic development, and empirical validation on relevant machine learning problems. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods (SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling and acceleration) over several strong baselines.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for tackling saddle-point problems is well-established with relevant examples from machine learning. The relationship to prior work is clearly articulated, highlighting the gap this paper fills. However, there are a few areas where clarity could be improved:

1.  The main novelty is the proof technique based on monotone operators. While the appendix likely contains the full details, including a high-level proof sketch in the main body of the paper would greatly benefit the reader and better showcase this core contribution.
2.  The discussion of non-uniform sampling is excellent for the bilinear case. However, for the general saddle-point problem, it is not clear how the sampling probabilities `π_i` should be chosen. Some guidance on this for non-bilinear problems would be valuable.
3.  In the experiments (Section 7), the method for selecting the step-size `σ` is not specified. Was it set according to the theoretical bounds, or was it tuned? This detail is important for reproducibility.

Minor typos:
- On line 78 (Section 2.1), "min x∈Rd max y∈Rn f(x)+y>Kx−g*(y)" appears to be a typo for the primal problem "min x∈Rd g*(Kx) + f(x)". In the bilinear formulation of Sec 2, the `y` term should be `y^T`.
- On line 121 (Section 2.2), in the description of the Arrow-Hurwicz method, the update for `y_t` seems to have an extra `y_{t-1}` term: `yt = proxσg ( yt−1 + γ−1σ∂yK(xt, yt−1))`. It should likely be `yt = prox_σg(yt-1 + σγ^-1 ∂yK(xt, yt-1))`.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While the algorithms are extensions of existing methods (SVRG and SAGA), the application and analysis in the context of saddle-point problems are novel and non-trivial. The key original contribution is the convergence proof via the monotone operator framework. This is a significant departure from existing analyses of variance-reduced methods and demonstrates a deeper connection between these algorithms and a more fundamental class of problems in optimization. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide linearly convergent *stochastic* algorithms for this general class of saddle-point problems. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from prior art in both stochastic optimization and saddle-point optimization.

**Significance:**
The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact on both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and optimization.
1.  **Practical Impact:** It provides efficient, scalable algorithms for a class of problems that are prevalent but have lacked specialized large-scale solvers. This includes important applications like AUC maximization, robust optimization, and models with structured/non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso).
2.  **Theoretical Impact:** The analytical framework based on monotone operators is a major contribution. It provides new insights and a powerful tool that could be used to analyze variance reduction methods for other problems beyond standard convex minimization.
3.  **Advancing the State of the Art:** The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art. The experiments show that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, significantly outperform existing batch, accelerated batch, and standard stochastic algorithms.

**Questions for the Authors:**
- Could you comment on the intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears to be even more critical for good performance in the saddle-point setting than in the standard convex minimization setting, as suggested by your experiments?
- As mentioned under "Clarity," could you provide more detail on the selection of step-sizes in your experiments and guidance on non-uniform sampling strategies beyond the bilinear case?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems.
2.  The novel convergence analysis using monotone operators is a significant theoretical contribution of independent interest.
3.  The work has high practical significance, enabling efficient optimization for numerous machine learning problems with non-separable objectives.
4.  The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed methods over strong baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The algorithms themselves are direct adaptations of SVRG/SAGA. However, this is a minor point, as the novelty and impact lie in the analysis and the new problem domain.
2.  Some details regarding experimental setup and the generalization of non-uniform sampling could be clearer. These are minor issues that can be easily addressed.

**Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to large-scale optimization. It addresses a long-standing problem with a novel and elegant analysis, backed by strong empirical results. The potential impact is high. I strongly recommend acceptance.
