This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common setting in machine learning. The authors successfully adapt these algorithms, providing the first linearly convergent methods for large-scale saddle-point problems, addressing a notable gap in the literature. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and thoroughly evaluated, making a strong contribution to the field.

The paper builds upon recent advances in stochastic optimization like SAG, SVRG, and SAGA [1, 2, 3], extending them beyond separable objectives to handle the non-separability inherent in many machine learning problems formulated as saddle-point problems. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods when applied to these scenarios and highlight the advantages of leveraging the saddle-point structure. The introduction of two gradient-operator splits (element-wise and factored) is insightful, and the theoretical analysis employing the monotone operator framework [8] is a key strength, enabling generalization beyond standard saddle-point problems to variational inequalities [5, 6].  The demonstration of the benefit of non-uniform sampling, both theoretically and empirically, is compelling.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with proven linear convergence is a significant advancement. The application to a broader class of problems via the monotone operator framework further enhances the work’s impact.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The convergence analysis is thorough and well-supported, using a sophisticated monotone operator framework.  The theoretical bounds are clearly presented and justified.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experimental results (in the main text and appendix) strongly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating practical effectiveness across different datasets and parameter settings. The comparison with existing methods, including batch algorithms and primal SAGA, is comprehensive.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the technical details are presented in a concise and accessible manner.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The identified applications in supervised learning with non-separable losses/regularizers, robust optimization, and convex relaxation of unsupervised learning highlight the practical relevance of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Acceleration Adaptivity:** The discussion regarding adaptive updating of the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in Section 5 is somewhat unclear. While the authors claim superior performance to accelerated batch algorithms, the practical details of how to adaptively update (x̄, ȳ) to achieve this speed-up are not fully explained.  Further clarification here would strengthen the paper.
* **SAGA Convergence Equivalence:** The question raised in the paper regarding whether the convergence proof for SVRG also holds for SAGA is valid. While the algorithms share similarities, a specific proof or justification for SAGA’s convergence based on the presented analysis would be beneficial.
* **Notational Ambiguity:** The use of ‘K’ for both the smooth function and the design matrix is confusing. Using distinct symbols (e.g., ‘K’ and ‘A’) would improve readability and avoid potential misinterpretations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning – solving large-scale saddle-point problems – with a novel and effective approach.
* The theoretical results are rigorously proven and well-supported by experimental evidence.
* The work extends the applicability of stochastic variance reduction methods to a broader class of problems.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides valuable insights for both researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity regarding adaptive acceleration could hinder practical implementation.
* The unresolved question about SAGA’s convergence, while acknowledged by the authors, is a minor concern.
* The notational ambiguity, though minor, could cause confusion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The strengths of the work far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The authors have successfully extended powerful stochastic variance reduction techniques to a challenging and practically important problem setting. I strongly recommend acceptance.
