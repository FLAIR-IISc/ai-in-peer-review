This paper presents a significant and timely extension of stochastic variance-reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors provide the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this problem class, which is highly relevant to machine learning applications involving duality, robust optimization, and structured prediction. The work builds directly upon foundational variance-reduction techniques presented at past NIPS conferences (e.g., Johnson & Zhang, 2013; Defazio et al., 2014) and successfully adapts the Catalyst acceleration framework (Lin et al., 2015) to further improve convergence. The theoretical analysis is grounded in the theory of monotone operators, providing a general and elegant proof framework that extends the applicability of the methods beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its originality and theoretical quality. The extension of variance reduction to the saddle-point setting is a natural but non-trivial step that fills an important gap in the optimization literature for machine learning. The convergence analysis is rigorous, and the successful application of an acceleration scheme that theoretically outperforms accelerated batch methods is a notable result. The inclusion of non-uniform sampling and the analysis of different data splitting strategies are also valuable practical and theoretical contributions. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the proposed algorithms and their analysis accessible.

The main weakness of the paper is its reliance on the assumption of strong convexity in the primal problem and strong concavity in the dual problem simultaneously. This joint assumption is quite restrictive and may not hold for many practical applications where, for instance, regularization imposes strong convexity on the primal variable but not on the dual. A more significant contribution would involve relaxing this requirement to need strong convexity in only one of the two variables. Furthermore, while the numerical results are promising, they are somewhat limited. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation on a wider variety of datasets and problem types would be beneficial to more robustly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical contributions to an important problem. Its significance is currently tempered by the restrictive strong convexity-concavity assumption. Addressing this limitation and expanding the experimental validation would substantially strengthen the work.
