

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The authors' key insight lies in using the monotone operator framework for convergence analysis, which fundamentally differs from convex minimization approaches and enables generalization to variational inequality problems. This theoretical approach is elegant and broadly applicable, as demonstrated by their simpler proofs for forward-backward splitting and its stochastic variant.

The paper's main strength is its meaningful extension of the field, enabling handling of non-separable loss functions and regularizers through saddle-point formulation - a critical advance for many machine learning applications. The experimental results demonstrate that non-uniform sampling algorithms are competitive and often outperform prior methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

However, the paper would benefit from more detailed proof sketches in the main text, as the main novelty lies in the proof technique rather than the algorithms themselves. The authors should address several clarity issues: (1) specify the non-uniform sampling probabilities beyond bilinear problems (i.e., define π), (2) explain the intuition for step size selection in experiments, and (3) provide justification for why non-uniform sampling is more crucial for saddle-point problems than convex minimization. The minor typos (line 78, line 121) should be corrected.

The paper is well-written overall but would be substantially improved with more detailed technical explanations. The quality is strong, with technically sound analysis supported by appropriate experiments. The originality is high, as the authors present a novel proof technique that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The significance is substantial, as these algorithms enable efficient large-scale optimization for problems previously intractable with linear convergence guarantees.

The paper makes a compelling case for its contribution, though the reviewers would welcome more concrete details on the theoretical underpinnings and experimental design. The work represents a meaningful advance that will likely be valuable to both researchers and practitioners working on optimization in machine learning.
