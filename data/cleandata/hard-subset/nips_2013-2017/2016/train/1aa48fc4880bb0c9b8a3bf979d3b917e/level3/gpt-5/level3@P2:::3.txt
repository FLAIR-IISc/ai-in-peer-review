Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is (proximal-friendly) strongly convex–concave but potentially non-smooth. The authors extend variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting, prove linear convergence, and provide an acceleration via a catalyst-style outer regularization. A key technical choice is to analyze these algorithms through monotone operator theory: the saddle-point problem corresponds to finding a zero of A+B, with A the subdifferential of M and B the smooth operator built from ∇K. Unlike convex minimization analyses relying on co-coercivity, the present analysis only needs monotonicity of A+B and Lipschitz continuity of the Bi’s, and thus applies beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.

Algorithmically, the paper considers two splitting schemes for B: (i) individual component splits (e.g., per entry of a bilinear K), and (ii) factored splits along rows/columns, which reduce storage for SAGA and enable non-uniform sampling. The authors derive iteration and time complexities in terms of L (global Lipschitz), L̄(π) (sampling-dependent smoothness), Tprox(M), and oracle costs T(B), T(Bi), and emphasize the role of non-uniform sampling. For bilinear problems they provide explicit bounds in terms of ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max, and show that accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling can dominate batch forward–backward (FB) and its accelerated variant.

Experiments on binary classification with a non-separable loss (AUC surrogate) and a non-separable proximal regularizer (OSCAR-like) show that: (i) non-uniform sampling matters; (ii) uniform SAGA can be no better than batch methods; and (iii) accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is best, especially under ill-conditioning. On separable problems, primal SAGA (not applicable in the non-separable setting) is faster, but primal accelerated batch methods are weaker.

Quality
– Theory: The convergence proofs via monotone operators are sound and technically non-trivial for saddle points, where co-coercivity is absent. The linear rates for SVRG/SAGA and their catalyst acceleration are clearly stated. The analysis of L̄(π) under several sampling schemes is helpful.
– Complexity trade-offs: The paper carefully separates oracle costs and proximal costs. A key practical caveat is that each incremental iteration incurs the O(n+d) cost of proxM; thus overall gains depend critically on whether Tprox(M) is cheap relative to T(Bi). This bottleneck is acknowledged (Sec. 2.4, Thm. 1 discussion) but could be discussed more prominently when comparing to accelerated batch FB.
– Experiments: They are informative but limited in scope and scale, with few datasets and no wall-clock comparisons across varying Tprox(M) regimes. The non-uniform SAGA implementation deviates from the theory (resampling step omitted); this should be clarified.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a clean monotone-operator presentation that unifies saddle points and VIs. The distinctions between individual and factored splits, and the role of non-uniform sampling, are clearly explained. Some suggestions:
– Bring the per-iteration proximal bottleneck to the foreground in the introduction and in the table summarizing complexities.
– Provide a concise table mapping regimes (n≪d, n≈d, n≫d; dense vs sparse K) to recommended split/sampling choices and the resulting L̄(π).
– Expand the accelerated scheme description in the main text (currently deferred to the appendix).

Originality
The extension of SVRG/SAGA and catalyst-style acceleration to general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems analyzed via monotone operators (without co-coercivity) is original and noteworthy. The identification and analysis of two split notions and non-uniform sampling in this context are also novel. The connection to variational inequalities broadens scope.

Significance
The work advances the algorithmic toolkit for non-separable saddle-point problems common in ML, with the first linearly convergent incremental methods in this class, and a practical acceleration mechanism. Its impact will be strongest in settings where proxM is inexpensive and the finite-sum structure is not available in the primal/dual. The proximal bottleneck tempers practical gains in some regimes, but the methods and analysis are likely to be used and built upon.

Strengths
– First linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for general convex–concave saddle points; elegant monotone-operator analysis that extends to VIs.
– Careful complexity analysis for different splits and non-uniform sampling; clear bilinear specialization.
– Catalyst acceleration adapted to saddle points with a simple proximal-point view; crisp condition for acceleration in terms of L̄, T(B), T(Bi).
– Solid experimental evidence that non-uniform sampling and acceleration matter.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
– Proximal bottleneck: Each incremental step pays Tprox(M)=O(n+d). Please more explicitly characterize regimes where the proposed methods beat accelerated batch FB in wall-clock time. For instance, provide a simple sufficient inequality comparing L̄√T(B)maxiT(Bi) (accelerated SVRG) against L·T(B)+Tprox(M) (accelerated FB), with practical proxies for these quantities.
– Factored vs individual splits: The theory hints that factored splits may have worse L̄(π) but lower storage; in practice, when n≫d, factored updates can be slower. Can you provide a theorem or proposition comparing the tightest known upper bounds on L̄(π) under both splits (non-uniform sampling), and conditions on K (spectral decay, leverage scores) under which one dominates?
– Tightness in n and d: Are the dependences via ‖K‖F and ‖K‖max tight? Can you show lower bounds or adversarial constructions indicating that the stated L̄(π) scalings are unimprovable up to constants for each split/sampling?
– SAGA with non-uniform sampling: Your theory requires resampling stored gradients; experiments do not always do this. Please comment on the empirical impact and whether a practical workaround exists that preserves guarantees.
– Empirics: Include wall-clock plots and experiments varying Tprox(M) (e.g., different group structures, TV vs OSCAR) and sparsity to illustrate when accelerated SVRG decisively outperforms accelerated batch FB. Report sensitivity to mini-batch size m and to mis-specification of λ,γ.

Pros for acceptance
– Novel, rigorous, and general analysis yielding linear convergence for saddle-point variance reduction.
– Practical guidance on sampling and splitting; catalyst acceleration that is simple to implement.
– Addresses important non-separable settings not covered by prior incremental methods.

Cons for acceptance
– Per-iteration proximal cost can negate variance-reduction gains; limited empirical exploration of this bottleneck.
– Comparisons to strong batch baselines could be clearer in terms of wall-clock and regime characterization.
– Some theoretical prescriptions (resampling in SAGA) are not followed in practice.

Post-rebuttal
The authors addressed the key concerns above to my satisfaction, clarifying when accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB and providing additional discussion on split choices and complexity. I keep my scores unchanged but view the paper favorably.
