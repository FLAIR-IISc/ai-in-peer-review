This paper extends the highly successful stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, from the standard convex minimization setting to the more general class of strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems. This is an important and timely contribution, building upon foundational work in variance reduction (e.g., SVRG, SAGA) and applying it to a problem structure that captures non-separable losses and regularizers, robust optimization, and other key machine learning tasks. The authors provide the first linear convergence guarantees for these algorithms in this setting. A key aspect of their analysis is the use of the monotone-operator framework, which is more general than standard convex analysis and highlights the applicability of these methods to an even wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities in game theory.

The paper has several significant strengths.
-   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is novel and impactful. It provides the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for a broad and practical class of non-separable saddle-point problems, demonstrably advancing the state of the art.
-   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and complete. The theoretical claims of linear convergence are rigorously proven, and these claims are well-supported by a thorough set-of experiments. The authors demonstrate the practical effectiveness of their proposed methods, including the theoretically-justified benefit of non-uniform sampling over uniform sampling.
-   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly outline the problem setting, the proposed algorithms, and their theoretical underpinnings. The inclusion of different gradient-operator splits (element-wise and factored) adds practical depth.

There are a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed to further improve the manuscript.
-   The acceleration technique presented in Section 5 is compelling. However, the paper is slightly vague on how to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in practice to achieve the best speed-up. A more explicit description or pseudo-code would enhance reproducibility.
-   The convergence and acceleration proofs are provided for SVRG. The authors suggest that the same scheme should work for SAGA, but it is not explicitly confirmed whether the analysis holds directly. A brief comment on this would be valuable.
-   A minor notational issue exists where the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K share the same symbol. Using distinct notation would prevent potential confusion for the reader.

Despite these minor points, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong theoretical and practical contribution to the field of large-scale optimization.
