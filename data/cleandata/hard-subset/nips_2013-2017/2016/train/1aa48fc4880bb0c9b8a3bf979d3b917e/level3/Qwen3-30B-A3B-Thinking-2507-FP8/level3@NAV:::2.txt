

This paper extends stochastic variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, a significant contribution to the optimization literature. The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, which is particularly valuable as it shows the methods extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The extension of the Catalyst framework for acceleration is elegant and demonstrates clear theoretical advantages over batch methods. The paper also makes a notable contribution by formalizing two different notions of splits (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) and demonstrating the importance of non-uniform sampling.

The technical quality is strong, with well-supported theoretical results that build upon recent advances in stochastic optimization. The clarity is generally good, with well-structured exposition of the algorithms and their analysis, though some of the monotone operator theory could be more accessible to machine learning readers. The originality is significant - while variance reduction has been extensively studied for convex minimization, this is the first work to extend these methods to saddle-point problems with linear convergence guarantees.

The significance is high, as saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction, and dual formulations of non-separable problems). The numerical results on real machine learning problems demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods, though the experimental section could be strengthened with more diverse problem instances and comparisons to state-of-the-art saddle-point solvers.

The main weakness is the assumption of strong convexity-concavity on both primal and dual variables, which is unnecessarily restrictive. The authors should relax this to require strong convexity on only one side (as suggested in the key points), which would significantly broaden the applicability of their methods. Additionally, while the experiments are relevant, they could be expanded to include more problem types and deeper analysis of the acceleration framework.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With minor revisions to address the strong convexity assumption and expand the experiments, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The technical soundness, theoretical novelty, and practical relevance make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
