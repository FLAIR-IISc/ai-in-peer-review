

# Review for "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. While the algorithmic extensions are straightforward, the paper makes significant contributions through a novel convergence analysis using monotone operators, which generalizes beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Previous work on saddle-point problems has primarily focused on batch methods like forward-backward (FB) algorithms or stochastic variants with sublinear convergence rates. The authors demonstrate that their methods achieve linear convergence for saddle-point problems, which is a major advance over existing approaches.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution: the use of monotone operator theory to prove convergence, which differs fundamentally from the convex minimization analysis. This approach not only provides a rigorous foundation for their algorithms but also shows they apply to a broader class of problems than saddle-point formulations alone. The authors carefully analyze different splits (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) and demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically. Their acceleration via the catalyst framework leads to algorithms that outperform accelerated batch methods, a significant practical contribution.

The paper also makes an important practical contribution by enabling the handling of non-separable loss functions and regularizers through saddle-point formulations. The authors provide compelling examples from machine learning (supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, unsupervised learning) where their approach is uniquely applicable when existing methods fail. The experimental results consistently show that their algorithms with non-uniform sampling outperform existing methods, with the accelerated variants providing particularly strong gains on ill-conditioned problems.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though it could benefit from more detailed proof sketches in the main text (the reviewer notes that the main novelty is the proof technique, not the algorithms themselves). There are minor clarity issues that need addressing, particularly regarding the non-uniform sampling probabilities π beyond bilinear problems and the step size selection σ in experiments. The authors should clarify why non-uniform sampling is more crucial for saddle-point problems than for convex minimization, and provide more intuition about the choice of sampling probabilities in practical scenarios.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous convergence proofs using monotone operator theory. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, though the paper could better address the question of how to choose sampling probabilities π for non-bilinear problems. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear and well-structured, but some technical details (particularly regarding sampling probabilities and step size selection) could be made more explicit. The minor typos noted (line 78, line 121) should be corrected. Including a proof sketch in the main text would improve accessibility.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution by providing the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in saddle-point problems. The monotone operator approach is novel and extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, which is a valuable theoretical contribution that distinguishes this work from previous approaches.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for machine learning, as they provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for a broad class of problems that arise in practice (non-separable losses, robust optimization, unsupervised learning). The practical advantages demonstrated in experiments make this a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems
- Novel monotone operator analysis that extends to variational inequalities
- Practical advantages demonstrated in experiments
- Addresses important machine learning problems with non-separable losses/regularizers
- Provides a simpler theoretical framework for saddle-point problems

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some technical details (sampling probabilities, step size selection) need clarification
- Could benefit from more detailed proof sketches in main text
- Minor clarity issues and typos need addressing

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The authors have addressed an important gap in the literature by providing the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems, with a theoretically grounded analysis that generalizes to a broader class of problems. The practical advantages demonstrated in experiments further strengthen the paper's contribution. With minor revisions to address the clarity issues noted, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
