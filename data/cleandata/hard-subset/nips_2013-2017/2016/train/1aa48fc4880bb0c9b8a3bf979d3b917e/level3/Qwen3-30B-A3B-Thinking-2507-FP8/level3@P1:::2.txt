

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as previous variance reduction techniques were designed for convex minimization, not saddle-point problems. The work builds on recent advances in variance reduction for convex optimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) but extends them to a more general framework, showing how the same algorithmic principles can be applied to saddle-point problems with appropriate modifications.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a novel convergence analysis for saddle-point problems using monotone operators, which differs significantly from the convex minimization setting. This analysis shows that the algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Second, the authors identify two distinct notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) and demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. Third, they successfully extend the Catalyst framework to accelerate their algorithms, showing that the accelerated version always outperforms batch methods. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with well-justified assumptions and detailed proofs in the appendix.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the technical contributions are well-explained, and the experimental section provides concrete evidence of the method's effectiveness. The authors do an excellent job of relating their work to previous NIPS papers (e.g., SAGA, SVRG) and clearly articulating how their work differs. The discussion of bilinear saddle-point problems and their application to supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers is particularly strong, showing the practical relevance of their theoretical contributions.

The main strengths of the paper are:
1. The novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, which is a significant theoretical contribution
2. The comprehensive convergence analysis using monotone operator theory
3. The demonstration of acceleration via the Catalyst framework
4. The clear connection to practical machine learning applications
5. The well-executed experiments showing clear improvements over existing methods

However, there are several important weaknesses that need to be addressed. The most significant is the assumption of strong convexity-concavity on both the primal and dual variables. As noted in the key points, this assumption is overly strong for many practical applications. In many saddle-point problems (such as those arising from duality in convex optimization), strong convexity is typically only required on one side (e.g., the primal problem). The authors should consider relaxing this assumption to only require strong convexity on one side, which would significantly broaden the applicability of their results. The current analysis would benefit from a discussion of how the algorithm would behave under weaker conditions.

Additionally, while the experiments are generally good, they are somewhat limited. The authors only present results on two datasets (sido and rcv1), and it would be valuable to see more extensive comparisons across different problem types, including non-bilinear saddle-point problems. The paper mentions that their methods apply to variational inequalities, but there are no experiments demonstrating this broader applicability. More detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods for specific applications would strengthen the empirical validation.

The significance of this work is substantial. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., in robust optimization, supervised learning with non-separable losses, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning). The proposed algorithms provide the first linearly convergent methods that can handle large-scale problems with separable structures in the saddle-point formulation. This has the potential to significantly impact the field, particularly for problems where the primal or dual formulations are non-separable.

The paper is technically sound, with careful theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The authors are appropriately honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the strong convexity-concavity assumption. The clarity of the writing is excellent, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a novel contribution by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic extensions are straightforward, the theoretical analysis using monotone operators is new and significant. The connection to variational inequalities and the extension to monotone operators further enhance the originality of the work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by addressing a problem class that has been largely neglected in the variance reduction literature.

For the recommendation, I would suggest accepting the paper with minor revisions. The core contribution is strong and significant, and the paper makes a clear step forward in the field. The main issue that needs addressing is the strong convexity-concavity assumption, which should be relaxed to only require strong convexity on one side. The authors should also include additional experiments to better demonstrate the broader applicability of their methods.

To summarize, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical results are sound, the applications are well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The main weakness is the overly strong assumption of strong convexity-concavity on both sides, which should be addressed in revision. With these revisions, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
