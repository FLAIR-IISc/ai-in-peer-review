

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" (NIPS 2016)

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications such as robust optimization, supervised learning with non-separable losses, and variational inequalities. The authors address a critical gap in the literature, as previous variance reduction methods were designed for convex minimization problems but not for saddle-point structures. Their work builds upon prior NIPS contributions by Le Roux et al. (2012), Johnson & Zhang (2013), and Defazio et al. (2014) for convex optimization, but extends them to a fundamentally different problem class requiring novel theoretical analysis.

The paper's key strengths are its rigorous theoretical framework and practical impact. The authors successfully prove linear convergence for both SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting using monotone operator theory—a sophisticated approach that differs substantially from standard convex analysis. This framework demonstrates that their algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities common in game theory. The paper's theoretical contributions are well-supported by empirical evidence across multiple datasets, with experiments showing clear advantages of their non-uniform sampling strategy over uniform sampling and batch methods.

The paper's technical novelty lies in several key innovations. First, it introduces two distinct gradient-operator splits (element-wise and factored) that are specifically tailored to saddle-point problems, which is a crucial insight for non-separable settings. Second, it provides the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems, with the monotone operator approach being particularly elegant and general. Third, the experimental results (both in main text and appendix) convincingly demonstrate the practical effectiveness of their methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper excels in addressing a meaningful gap in the literature. Many important machine learning problems (e.g., structured output prediction, non-separable regularizers) naturally lead to saddle-point formulations that cannot be efficiently solved by existing convex optimization methods. The authors clearly articulate this gap in their examples section and demonstrate how their methods provide a solution where prior approaches fail.

For weaknesses, the paper has a few minor concerns that should be addressed. First, the adaptive update of the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in the acceleration section (Section 5) is not clearly explained. The authors state that τ is defined for bilinear problems but do not provide a general adaptive scheme for choosing this parameter, which would be valuable for practical implementation. Second, it's unclear whether the convergence proof for SVRG (Theorem 1) also holds for SAGA (Theorem 2). The authors mention that the same scheme should work for SAGA but do not provide a proof or detailed argument. Third, there's a notational issue with using K for both the smooth function and the design matrix, which could cause confusion for readers.

Despite these minor concerns, the paper is of high quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are comprehensive, and the contribution is clearly significant for the machine learning community. The paper is well-written, with clear problem formulation, algorithm descriptions, and experimental setup. The connection to variational inequalities in game theory is particularly noteworthy and expands the potential impact of this work.

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for non-separable saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning applications. Their methods outperform existing batch and stochastic algorithms both theoretically and empirically, with the acceleration framework offering additional speedups. The paper's approach to non-uniform sampling is particularly insightful, as it demonstrates both theoretical advantages and practical performance gains.

In summary, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution that extends the applicability of variance reduction techniques to a broader class of problems. The work is original, technically sound, and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The minor concerns noted above are easily addressable in a revision, and they do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. This work represents a clear advancement in the field and should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
