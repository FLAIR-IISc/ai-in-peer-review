This paper presents extensions of Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning arising from duality. The authors claim to provide the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems, a significant advancement. The core contribution lies in adapting the convergence analysis, moving beyond the convex minimization framework to leverage the theory of monotone operators, thereby broadening the applicability of the algorithms to variational inequalities as well.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear need for scalable optimization methods for saddle-point problems, which frequently appear in contexts like regularized learning with non-separable losses or regularizers, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning tasks. The connection to monotone operator theory is insightful, providing a more general framework for analysis and extending the reach of the proposed algorithms. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly with non-uniform sampling, outperforming existing techniques in several scenarios.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, coupled with a novel convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory, is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, establishing linear convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithms. The generalization to monotone operators is particularly noteworthy.
* **Practical Relevance:** Addressing saddle-point problems is crucial in modern machine learning, and the proposed algorithms offer a scalable solution.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on diverse datasets support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical efficacy of the algorithms, especially those employing non-uniform sampling.
* **Clarity of Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem, its relevance, and the paper's contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity in Details:** While the overall ideas are well-presented, certain details lack sufficient clarity. Specifically, the choice of the probability vector π for non-uniform sampling beyond the bilinear case isn't explicitly detailed, leaving the reader to infer its application to more general settings. The step size selection (σ) in the experiments is also not elaborated upon, hindering reproducibility and understanding of its impact.
* **Proof Sketches:** Given that the primary novelty resides in the proof technique, including a more detailed proof sketch within the main paper, rather than relegating everything to the appendices, would substantially improve accessibility and demonstrate the key steps.
* **Limited Discussion of Non-Uniform Sampling Intuition:** The paper asserts the importance of non-uniform sampling but doesn’t deeply explore *why* it’s more crucial for saddle-point problems compared to standard convex minimization. Providing intuition behind this difference would strengthen the contribution.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** A couple of minor typos are present (line 78, k -> d, and line 121, extra y_{t-1} term), though these do not detract significantly from the overall quality.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The work appears complete, offering a substantial contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate carefulness in evaluating both the strengths and limitations of their approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written but suffers from clarity issues regarding specific implementation details (sampling, step size). Improved elaboration on these points would enhance the reader’s understanding.
* **Originality:** The work is highly original, presenting a novel combination of existing techniques and theoretical frameworks. The application of monotone operator theory to analyze SVR algorithms for saddle-point problems is a distinct contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are significant, providing a new set of tools for solving a class of problems prevalent in machine learning. The potential impact is substantial, as these algorithms could enable the training of more complex models with improved scalability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems.
* Introduces a valuable new analysis framework based on monotone operator theory.
* Presents strong experimental evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* Addresses a practically important problem with significant implications for machine learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Lack of clarity regarding key implementation details (sampling scheme, step size selection).
* Insufficient detail regarding the proof technique, potentially hindering reproducibility and deep understanding.
* Limited discussion on the reasons behind the increased importance of non-uniform sampling in the saddle-point setting.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While minor improvements to clarity and detail would be beneficial, the paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of optimization. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor of the analysis, and the compelling experimental results outweigh the minor weaknesses. Addressing the points raised regarding clarity in a revised version would further strengthen the paper’s impact. The combination of established algorithms (SVRG/SAGA) with a new analytical framework (monotone operators) creates a valuable and broadly applicable tool for machine learning researchers.
