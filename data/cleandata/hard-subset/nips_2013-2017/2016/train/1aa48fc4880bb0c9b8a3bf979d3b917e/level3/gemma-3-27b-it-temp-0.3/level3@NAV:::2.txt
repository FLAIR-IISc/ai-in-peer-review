## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper tackles the important problem of minimizing convex-concave saddle-point functions, a common occurrence in machine learning, particularly in problems involving duality and regularization. The authors extend existing stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods like SVRG and SAGA to this setting, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems. This is a valuable contribution, as standard SVR methods are not directly applicable. The work is well-motivated, clearly presenting the challenges and opportunities arising from extending these methods to the saddle-point formulation. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical rigor. The authors provide a convergence analysis, leveraging the concept of monotone operators to broaden the applicability of their methods beyond strict saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The extension of the Catalyst framework for acceleration is a significant addition, demonstrating potential performance gains over standard accelerated batch algorithms. The inclusion of numerical results, while limited, provides initial evidence supporting the theoretical findings. The discussion of the two notions of splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) is insightful and highlights a key difference from convex minimization.

However, the paper suffers from a significant limitation: the strong convexity assumption on *both* the primal and dual problems. This is overly restrictive and limits the practical applicability of the proposed methods. As rightly pointed out by the authors, requiring strong convexity on only the primal *or* the dual would significantly broaden the scope of the work. Relaxing this assumption should be a priority for future research. 

Furthermore, the numerical evaluation is somewhat lacking. While the experiments demonstrate the method’s performance on specific datasets, more comprehensive testing with varying problem sizes, different datasets, and comparisons to a wider range of baseline methods would strengthen the empirical validation. Specifically, a comparison to specialized saddle-point solvers would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems with linear convergence guarantees.
*   Theoretical soundness and rigorous analysis, including extension to monotone operators.
*   Practical acceleration via the Catalyst framework.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overly strong assumption of strong convexity on both primal and dual problems.
*   Limited numerical evaluation.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the paper addresses an important problem. However, the authors should address the strong convexity assumption and expand the numerical evaluation to increase the paper’s impact and practical relevance. Addressing these points would elevate this work from a solid theoretical contribution to a truly impactful advancement in the field.
