

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" (NIPS 2016)

This paper extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linear convergence guarantees for these methods in this context. The authors demonstrate that the same stochastic variance reduction techniques used for convex optimization can be extended to saddle-point problems by leveraging monotone operator theory. This is significant because saddle-point problems arise frequently in machine learning through duality (e.g., in supervised learning, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning), but existing methods were limited to separable problems or batch approaches.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) it provides the first convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory; (2) it identifies two distinct notions of splitting (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) that are crucial for non-separable problems; (3) it demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is theoretically and practically essential; and (4) it shows how to accelerate these algorithms via the "catalyst" framework.

The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights. By framing saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, the authors establish a broader theoretical foundation that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. This approach reveals that the convex minimization analysis does not apply directly, requiring a different theoretical framework. The paper's connection to monotone operators is a major strength, though the presentation of this connection is weak—appearing late as an extension despite underpinning the entire analysis.

The paper's significance is substantial. It provides the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for non-separable saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning but previously lacked efficient scalable solutions. The theoretical improvements are complemented by practical benefits demonstrated in experiments.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The presentation is poor: the connection to monotone operators is unclear and appears late, making it difficult for readers to understand the theoretical foundation. The paper should clarify whether assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity, as this is crucial for convergence guarantees. The constant μ in Theorem 2 is unclear—authors should confirm if it's the monotonicity constant from the appendix and explain its relationship to Theorem 1.

The experimental section is limited to only two datasets (sido and rcv1), and the comparison to accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock-type methods (Zhang & Xiao 2015, JMLR) is missing. The paper should compare to these methods, which are state-of-the-art for saddle-point problems. The authors should also clarify whether their assumptions (particularly A) hold for SVM-induced saddle-point problems, which are common in practice.

The paper contains minor issues: inconsistent use of L (sometimes denoting condition number, sometimes Lipschitz constant), and a missing reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm. The distinction between standard stochastic primal-dual methods and the authors' proposed method is not clearly articulated.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a novel contribution by applying stochastic variance reduction to saddle-point problems with a rigorous monotone operator framework. This is distinct from previous work on separable saddle-point problems (e.g., Zhu & Storkey 2014, Zhang & Xiao 2015) that either assumed separability or used different algorithmic approaches.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important gap in scalable optimization for machine learning. The ability to handle non-separable losses and regularizers is valuable for many real-world applications (e.g., structured output prediction, non-separable regularizers like grouped norms). The theoretical insights about the role of monotonicity and non-uniform sampling are particularly valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- First convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems
- Strong theoretical contribution using monotone operator theory
- Practical benefits demonstrated through experiments
- Provides a unified framework extending beyond saddle-point problems

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Weak presentation of the monotone operator connection
- Insufficient experimental validation (only two datasets)
- Missing comparison to relevant accelerated methods (Zhang & Xiao 2015)
- Unclear assumptions and theoretical transitions
- Minor technical inconsistencies

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant theoretical contribution, but the weak presentation and limited experiments prevent it from being fully compelling. With revisions to clarify the theoretical foundation, expand the experimental section, and add missing comparisons, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the paper's main contribution is significant but its presentation and validation are insufficient to fully demonstrate its impact.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. The theoretical insights are valuable, but the paper would benefit from a clearer exposition of the monotone operator framework and more comprehensive experimental validation.
