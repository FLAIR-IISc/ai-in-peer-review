- Extends SVRG/SAGA-style variance reduction to large-scale convex–concave saddle-point problems and provides linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity.  
- Introduces Catalyst-style acceleration for stochastic forward–backward variants.  
- Provides a unifying monotone-operator analysis that avoids co-coercivity and extends to general variational inequalities.  
- Studies two decomposition schemes (function vs. partial-derivative/factored), non-uniform sampling, and storage–time tradeoffs.  
- Theory is technically sound: clear linear rates and complexity bounds reflecting sampling distributions and Lipschitz constants.  
- Monotone-operator derivation is elegant; acceleration analysis is simple and convincing.  
- Empirical results show the methods outperform batch and unaccelerated baselines, but experiments are narrow in scope.  
- Paper is well written; distinctions between splits and role of L̄(π) are useful.  
- Request more practical guidance: choosing sampling probabilities, clarifying SAGA storage overheads under different splits, and summarizing step-size choices/sensitivity.  
- Originality: novel extension to nonseparable saddle problems and monotone operators without co-coercivity; Catalyst adaptation is impactful.  
- Significance: advances algorithms for large-scale min–max and variational inequality problems and is useful for practitioners.  
- Main weakness: requiring strong convexity on both primal and dual is overly restrictive; should address one-sided strong convexity (via proximal regularization, restarts, or error bounds) and associated rates.  
- Recommended empirical improvements: broader datasets, varying conditioning, ablations on sampling, comparisons to Mirror-Prox/extragradient/accelerated PDHG, and wall-clock timings for non-uniform sampling.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theory with linear rates, clean monotone-operator extension, practical acceleration, and insightful sampling analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive bilateral strong convexity assumption and limited experimental breadth/baselines.
