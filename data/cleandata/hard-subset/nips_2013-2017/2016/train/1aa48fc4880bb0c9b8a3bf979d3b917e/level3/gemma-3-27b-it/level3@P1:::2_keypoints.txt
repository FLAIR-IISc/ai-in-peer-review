- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale saddle-point settings.  
- Employs monotone-operator theory for convergence proofs, extending applicability to variational inequalities beyond saddle-points.  
- Highlights two distinct notions of "splits" (functions vs. partial derivatives) and argues that non-uniform sampling is important for efficiency.  
- Introduces a Catalyst-based acceleration technique that outperforms accelerated batch algorithms.  
- Significant theoretical contribution with rigorous convergence analysis and novel adaptation of variance-reduction methods to saddle-points.  
- Empirical results demonstrate practical improvements over batch methods and SAGA with uniform sampling; presentation and related-work coverage are clear.  
- Key weakness: requires strong convexity–concavity on both primal and dual sides, an often unrealistic and restrictive assumption.  
- Recommendation to relax the strong-convexity requirement (e.g., only primal or dual) to broaden applicability.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited; should include more datasets, scenarios relaxing strong convexity, and comparisons to additional state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Suggest further analysis of sensitivity to non-uniform sampling strategies and the impact/trade-offs of mini-batch size m on proximal cost vs. variance reduction.  
- Overall assessment: recommend acceptance — important and well-supported contribution, contingent on addressing the strong-convexity limitation and expanding experiments in future work.
