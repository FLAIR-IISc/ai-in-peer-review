This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVRG/SAGA) methods to convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The authors provide theoretical guarantees of linear convergence under suitable assumptions and propose acceleration techniques. While the technical contributions are strong, the presentation and experimental evaluation require improvement.

The core idea – adapting SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems – is novel and valuable. Existing methods often struggle with the non-separable nature of many machine learning objectives, and this work directly addresses that limitation. Relating the problem to monotone operator theory is a clever move, broadening the applicability of the proposed algorithms beyond traditional saddle-point scenarios, encompassing variational inequalities. The identification of non-uniform sampling as crucial for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically, is a key insight. Furthermore, the acceleration framework based on a catalyst approach demonstrates a clear advantage over standard batch methods. This work builds upon recent advancements in stochastic optimization ([1, 2, 3]) and provides a specific, powerful tool for a particularly relevant class of problems. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. The connection to monotone operators feels like an afterthought, introduced in Section 6 as an “extension” despite being fundamental to the analysis. This obscures the underlying mathematical framework and makes it difficult to grasp the broader implications of the work. The authors should integrate this concept earlier and more seamlessly into the introduction and core methodology. 

The assumptions (A)-(C) require more careful explanation. Specifically, the applicability of assumption (A), strong convexity-concavity, to SVM-induced saddle-point problems (mentioned as an example) is not immediately obvious and deserves discussion. The relationship between assumptions (A)-(C) and the stronger notion of strong monotonicity (mentioned in the appendix) is unclear. Do (A)-(C) *imply* strong monotonicity? This needs to be explicitly stated. The meaning of the constant μ in Theorem 2 is ambiguous – the authors should confirm if it’s the monotonicity constant from the appendix and clarify its relation to the constants in Theorem 1.

A major weakness is the limited experimental evaluation. Only two datasets are used, which is insufficient to establish the robustness and generalizability of the proposed methods. More datasets, covering a wider range of problem characteristics, are necessary. Crucially, the authors fail to compare against accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock-type methods [20, e.g., Zhang & Xiao 2015 JMLR]. This is a significant omission, as Chambolle-Pock is a well-established alternative for saddle-point problems.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear distinction between the authors’ proposed method and standard stochastic primal-dual methods. A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the unique contributions of this work. Minor issues include inconsistent use of ‘L’ (sometimes representing the condition number, other times a Lipschitz constant) and the lack of a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical contributions:** Linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point setting are significant.
*   **Novelty:** Extending these methods to a broader class of problems through the monotone operator framework is innovative.
*   **Practical relevance:** The work addresses a common and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   **Demonstrated improvement:** Experiments show gains over existing methods, even with limited evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity issues:** The presentation is convoluted and lacks a cohesive narrative.
*   **Limited experimental validation:** Insufficient datasets and missing comparisons to relevant baselines.
*   **Unclear assumptions:** The applicability and implications of key assumptions need further elaboration.
*   **Missing comparison:** Lack of comparison with Chambolle-Pock type methods.

**Overall:**

This paper presents valuable theoretical results and a novel approach to solving saddle-point problems. However, the lack of clarity in the presentation, the limited experimental evaluation, and the absence of key comparisons significantly detract from its overall impact. I recommend **conditional acceptance** pending major revisions that address these concerns. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Reorganize the paper to emphasize the monotone operator framework from the beginning; 2) Provide a more thorough discussion of the assumptions and their implications; 3) Expand the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and a comparison with Chambolle-Pock-type methods; 4) Clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual algorithms; and 5) Address the minor issues regarding notation and references.
