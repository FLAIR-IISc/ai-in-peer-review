### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends two prominent variance-reduction stochastic optimization methods, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems. This is a significant generalization from their original application to finite-sum convex minimization problems. The authors provide the first linear convergence guarantees for these methods in this more general setting. The key technical contribution lies in analyzing the algorithms through the lens of monotone operators, a framework that is more powerful than standard convex analysis and reveals the methods' applicability to an even wider class of problems, including variational inequalities.

The paper makes several practical contributions. It introduces two different ways to split the gradient operator—an element-wise split and a factored split—which are tailored to the structure of saddle-point problems. It also demonstrates, both theoretically and empirically, that non-uniform sampling schemes can significantly outperform standard uniform sampling. Finally, the authors show how these methods can be accelerated using a simple extension of the "Catalyst" framework, leading to algorithms that are provably superior to accelerated batch methods in certain regimes. The theoretical claims are well-supported by experiments on machine learning tasks involving non-separable losses and regularizers, where the proposed methods show substantial improvements over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** This work is highly original in its successful extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems. This addresses a critical gap, as many machine learning problems, especially those with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or regularizers (e.g., total variation), are naturally formulated as saddle-point problems but could not previously benefit from these state-of-the-art variance-reduction techniques. The provision of the first linear convergence proofs for these methods in this context is a major theoretical advance.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The choice to frame the convergence analysis within the monotone operator framework is sophisticated and powerful. It not only provides a path to proving convergence where standard convex analysis techniques fall short but also unifies the treatment of saddle-point problems with other important areas like variational inequalities, highlighting the broad impact of the proposed algorithms.

*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins by motivating the problem with clear machine learning examples, reviews existing methods, presents the proposed SVRG and SAGA extensions, and then discusses acceleration and the generalization to monotone operators. The connection between theory and practice is strong, with theoretical constants (like the Lipschitz constant under non-uniform sampling) directly informing the experimental setup and results.

*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are thorough and compelling. They clearly validate the theoretical findings, showing that the proposed variance-reduced methods (particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) significantly outperform both standard and accelerated batch forward-backward algorithms, as well as simple stochastic versions. The experiments on non-separable objectives concretely demonstrate the practical utility of the work.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is very strong overall, and the following points are minor suggestions for further clarification and improvement.

*   **Clarity on Adaptive Acceleration:** In Section 5, when discussing acceleration, the paper mentions that one can "adaptively update (x̄, ȳ) more aggressively to speed-up the algorithm." This is an interesting and practical suggestion, but no details are provided on how this adaptive update might be implemented. A brief explanation, a heuristic, or a reference to a relevant technique would make this part of the contribution more complete and actionable for practitioners.

*   **Extension of Acceleration to SAGA:** The acceleration scheme and its proof of convergence are presented for SVRG. The paper then states that "the same scheme should work for SAGA." While this seems plausible, the paper would be strengthened by a brief justification for this claim. For instance, the authors could comment on whether the proof carries over directly or if any specific properties of SAGA require a modified analysis.

*   **Minor Notational Ambiguity:** A small point on notation: the symbol `K` is used to represent both the smooth function `K(x, y)` and the design matrix `K` in the bilinear case. While context usually makes the meaning clear, using distinct symbols (e.g., a calligraphic font for the function) would eliminate any potential for confusion and improve the paper's readability.

### **Detailed Comments**

This paper represents a solid and important contribution to the optimization and machine learning community. By extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems, it provides powerful new tools for a wide range of applications that were previously intractable for such methods. The theoretical analysis is elegant and general, and the practical benefits are clearly demonstrated. The inclusion of non-uniform sampling is a key feature that provides a significant performance boost, and the analysis of different operator splits is insightful. The minor weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and could be easily addressed in a final version of the manuscript. The work is of high quality and is highly likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.
