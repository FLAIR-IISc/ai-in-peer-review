- Extends SVRG, SAGA and Catalyst-accelerated methods from strongly convex finite-sum minimization to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.
- Introduces two novel splitting schemes: individual splits and factored splits.
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis for both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies.
- Convergence analysis is rigorous and grounded in monotone operator theory.
- Theoretical comparisons for bilinear saddle points are insightful and align with convex minimization intuition.
- Experiments show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are essential for empirical gains over batch methods.
- Major practical concern: each incremental update requires O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator, creating a performance bottleneck.
- Due to the high per-iteration cost, total complexity may not improve over accelerated batch forward-backward (FB) algorithms.
- Factored splits appear potentially much worse than individual splits when n >> d, raising concerns about their utility.
- Requests for clarification: is factored-split sub-optimality fundamental or an artifact of the analysis?
- Requests for clarification: precisely characterize regimes (n, d, conditioning) where accelerated SVRG outperforms accelerated batch FB given O(n + d) per-iteration cost.
- Requests for clarification: how tight is the derived dependence on n and d in the complexity bounds?
- Overall assessment: technically strong and original, but practical impact is unclear until efficiency concerns are addressed.
