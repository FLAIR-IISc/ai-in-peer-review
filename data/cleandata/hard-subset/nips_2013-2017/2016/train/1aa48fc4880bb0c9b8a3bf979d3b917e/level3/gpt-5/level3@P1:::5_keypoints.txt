- Studies convex–concave saddle-point problems with smooth K and proximable, strongly convex–concave M; proposes SVRG/SAGA variants that exploit finite-sum structure and achieve linear convergence.
- Key novelty is a convergence analysis framing saddle-point optimization as finding a zero of a sum of monotone operators A + B, avoiding co-coercivity assumptions.
- Provides the first linearly convergent large-scale incremental methods applicable to general convex–concave saddle points and variational inequalities.
- Algorithms follow standard SVRG/SAGA templates but the monotone-operator analysis broadens their applicability.
- Compared to batch primal–dual methods (PDHG/Chambolle–Pock), achieves linear rates with lower per-iteration cost via mini-batching.
- Compared to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC), does not require separability and supports more general and factored splits.
- Introduces a factored split for bilinear K that reduces SAGA storage requirements.
- Technical contribution: linear convergence proof for forward–backward schemes with strongly monotone A and Lipschitz B=∑Bi without needing Bi co-coercivity.
- Formalizes two splitting notions (by functions or by partial derivatives) and emphasizes that splits must preserve convex–concave structure.
- Develops non-uniform sampling theory using an L̄(π) constant that controls rates and shows gains tied to ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and ‖K‖max in the bilinear case.
- Extends proximal-point/catalyst acceleration to saddle points, yielding improved iteration complexity with a simpler analysis than Nesterov-style acceleration.
- Theoretical results are carefully stated with explicit step sizes and complexities, aligning with batch and stochastic limits.
- Experiments demonstrate benefits on non-separable ML tasks and validate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.
- Reviewer requests a high-level proof sketch (e.g., Lyapunov function, contractive operator argument, role of σ and L̄) in the main text for accessibility.
- Clarity issue: limited guidance on choosing sampling distribution π beyond bilinear K; heuristics or adaptive schemes would help practitioners.
- Clarity issue: need details on whether σ and epoch lengths in experiments follow theory or were tuned, and sensitivity to these choices.
- Requests more intuition for why non-uniform sampling has larger impact here than in convex minimization, e.g., contrast L̄(π) gaps in bilinear case.
- Originality: monotone-operator analysis without co-coercivity and the combination of factored split, storage-saving SAGA, and catalyst-style acceleration are novel.
- Significance: fills a gap for linearly convergent incremental methods for non-separable saddle problems and may spur work on stochastic methods for variational inequalities and games.
- Strengths: first linear-convergence SVRG/SAGA results for general saddle points, clear complexity improvements, practical acceleration, and relevant experiments.
- Weaknesses/questions: need principled recipe for π selection, more experimental tuning details, ablations (mini-batch size, SAGA factored trade-offs), comparisons to stochastic PDHG/SPDC, and clarification of invalid splits.
- Minor comments/typos: correct line 78 (k -> d) and line 121 (extra y_{t-1}); define µ when switching to monotone-operator section; ensure λ, γ consistency; state SAGA factored storage and update costs.
- Reproducibility: algorithms are implementable from the paper, but reviewer asks for released code and full hyperparameters.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, broad applicability, clear empirical gains, and simple implementable algorithms with principled acceleration.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited guidance for non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear cases and experiments could be broader and more detailed on tuning.
- Overall recommendation: reviewer leans toward acceptance.
