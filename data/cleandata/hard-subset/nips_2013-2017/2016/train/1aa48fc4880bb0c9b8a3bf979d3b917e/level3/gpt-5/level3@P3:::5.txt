Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends SVRG and SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure, providing the first linearly convergent incremental methods for this class. The key technical contribution is a convergence analysis that does not rely on convex minimization tools (e.g., co-coercivity), but instead interprets saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator A + B and analyzes forward–backward (FB) splitting in that framework. This yields linear rates under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz continuity of the forward operator, and the analysis further extends to general monotone inclusions and variational inequalities. The work also emphasizes (i) two splitting notions (function vs. partial-derivative/factored), (ii) the necessity of convex–concave splits, and (iii) the critical role of non-uniform sampling. An acceleration via the Catalyst proximal-point scheme is proposed, yielding the best theoretical and empirical performance.

Compared to prior work: SAG/SVRG/SAGA give linear rates for strongly convex finite-sum minimization [1–3,24]. Primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock; Arrow–Hurwicz) and FB splitting provide batch schemes for saddle points [19,20], with stochastic FB giving sublinear rates [22]. Primal–dual coordinate methods with linear rates assume separability [11,12]. Operator-splitting works relying on co-coercivity (e.g., SMART [30]) do not cover general saddle points. This paper thus fills a clear gap: linearly convergent, variance-reduced incremental methods for non-separable saddle-point problems, with an analysis that also simplifies and unifies proofs for FB splitting and its stochastic variant.

Strengths
- Novel and impactful analysis: The monotone-operator view replaces cocoercivity assumptions with monotonicity, enabling linear convergence proofs for SVRG/SAGA in saddle-point settings and generalizing to variational inequalities.
- Breadth: The framework accommodates non-separable losses and regularizers via saddle-point formulations, an important practical regime not covered by prior linearly convergent incremental methods.
- Clear algorithmic design: Straightforward extensions of SVRG/SAGA with careful handling of factored vs. individual splits and storage trade-offs; practical non-uniform sampling schemes are derived (e.g., row/column sampling proportional to Gram diagonals).
- Acceleration: A clean Catalyst-based proximal-point outer loop yields improved complexity without requiring Nesterov-style machinery; the approach also improves batch FB, not only bilinear K.
- Empirics: Non-uniform sampling substantially improves convergence; accelerated SVRG is consistently competitive and often superior on both separable and non-separable benchmarks.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Main novelty is the proof technique; the paper would benefit from including a concise proof sketch in the main text (beyond appendices): (i) construction of the Lyapunov function, (ii) the key variance decomposition under non-uniform sampling, (iii) how monotonicity, not co-coercivity, yields the step-size and linear contraction.
- Parameter selection details are underspecified. The experiments do not clearly state how σ was chosen (theoretical vs. tuned), whether L and L̄(π) were estimated, and how λ, γ were scaled in practice.
- Non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear K: for general splits B = ∑i Bi, the recommended π is unclear. The natural heuristic is πi ∝ Li (block Lipschitz) or πi ∝ Ω∗-norms of Bi to minimize L̄(π), but concrete guidance and examples would aid practitioners.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step in the bound; the empirical choice to omit it (mixtures of uniform/non-uniform) deserves discussion of any observed bias/variance effects.
- Storage/compute trade-offs for factored vs. individual splits could be elaborated, especially when K is sparse or streamed.
- Minor clarity: The roles and selection of λ, γ (the metric Ω) matter numerically; a short guidance on scaling to balance primal/dual curvature would help.

Technical questions for authors
1) Non-uniform sampling in non-bilinear cases: What π do you use when Bi are not matrix rows/columns? Is πi ∝ Li (or πi ∝ Li T(Bi)) recommended? Any results comparing these choices?
2) Step size σ in experiments: Did you use σ = 1/(L2 + 3L̄2/m) (SVRG) and its SAGA counterpart, or tune σ? How were L and L̄(π) estimated? Please provide sensitivity plots or heuristics.
3) Why is non-uniform sampling more crucial here than in convex minimization? A useful intuition: in saddle-point settings without co-coercivity, the effective smoothness constant is governed by anisotropic primal–dual couplings; heavy rows/columns (or blocks with large Li) dominate L̄(π) unless sampled proportionally, so adaptive π dramatically reduces variance and condition numbers.

Quality
- The theory is sound and carefully positioned; the monotone-operator analysis is a genuine contribution, with linear rates for SVRG/SAGA and cleaner proofs of FB splitting. Complexity comparisons are thoughtful. Experiments support the claims, though more implementation details would strengthen reproducibility.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized. Including a proof sketch and more implementation details (π, σ, parameter estimation) in the main text would improve accessibility and reproducibility.

Originality
- High. First linear-rate incremental algorithms for general saddle-point problems via a broadly applicable operator-theoretic analysis; provides simpler proofs for FB and a stochastic FB variant.

Significance
- High. The methods and proof techniques should influence both theory (monotone inclusions, variational inequalities) and practice (non-separable ML models). The acceleration and non-uniform sampling insights are practically meaningful.

Pros for acceptance
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for saddle points
- Strong, generalizable monotone-operator analysis and simpler FB proofs
- Handles non-separable losses/regularizers; competitive and accelerated in practice
- Clear advancement over batch and separable-only methods

Cons for acceptance
- Limited main-text proof detail given the centrality of the analysis
- Incomplete guidance on non-uniform sampling and step-size selection in general settings
- Some reliance on knowledge of strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz constants

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a compact proof sketch highlighting the monotone-operator arguments and variance reduction under π.
- Provide practical recipes for π in non-bilinear problems (e.g., πi ∝ Li or data-driven estimates).
- Clarify how σ, L, L̄(π), λ, γ are selected/estimated in experiments; include sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss the resampling requirement for SAGA with non-uniform sampling and what you used in practice.

Minor issues
- Typos: line 78 “k” should be “d”; line 121 includes an extra y_{t−1} term in the second equality.
- Define L̄(π) and the factored split more prominently; ensure consistent notation for norms and probabilities.

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes a meaningful and technically original contribution, opens a path to broader applications (variational inequalities), and presents algorithms that are practically competitive. With additional proof sketching and parameter/sampling guidance, it will be an excellent NIPS contribution.
