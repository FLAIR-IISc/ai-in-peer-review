### Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

**Summary**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. The primary contribution is the development of the first stochastic algorithms for this problem class that achieve a linear convergence rate. The authors demonstrate that while the algorithmic extensions of SVRG and SAGA are relatively straightforward, their convergence analysis requires a significant departure from the standard techniques used for convex minimization. The core of the theoretical contribution is a novel convergence proof rooted in the theory of monotone operators, which interprets the saddle-point problem as finding the zero of a sum of operators. This framework is more general, allowing the analysis to cover a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The paper also explores the benefits of non-uniform sampling for saddle-point problems and shows how these new algorithms can be accelerated using the "catalyst" framework. Experimental results on machine learning tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and increasingly important class of problems in machine learning. Saddle-point formulations are natural for applications like robust optimization, AUC maximization, structured prediction, and models with non-separable regularizers. By proposing the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this general setting, this work represents a major advance and significantly pushes the state of the art in large-scale optimization.

2.  **Originality and Technical Depth:** The main novelty lies in the sophisticated and elegant proof technique. Instead of adapting proofs from the convex minimization setting (which, as the authors note, does not apply), the paper frames the problem in the more general context of finding zeros of monotone operators. This is a powerful and insightful approach that not only provides a sound basis for the convergence guarantees but also broadens the applicability of the algorithms and analysis to other areas like variational inequalities. This re-framing also leads to simpler proofs for existing algorithms like Forward-Backward splitting.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Performance:** The proposed algorithms are not just of theoretical interest. The saddle-point formulation directly enables the handling of non-separable loss functions and regularizers, which cannot be easily addressed by standard stochastic methods that rely on sum-decomposable objectives. The experiments convincingly show that the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants, especially when combined with non-uniform sampling and acceleration, outperform existing batch and stochastic gradient methods. The performance gains are particularly notable in ill-conditioned settings, highlighting the practical value of variance reduction.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is generally well-written and the contribution is strong. However, there are several points where clarity could be improved, which would strengthen the paper and make its contributions more accessible and reproducible.

1.  **Details on Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper emphasizes that non-uniform sampling is "key" to efficiency, and the theoretical analysis for the bilinear case is clear. However, it is not specified how the sampling distribution π should be constructed for more general saddle-point problems that are not bilinear. This is a crucial practical detail, as practitioners would need guidance on how to define these importance sampling weights to realize the claimed performance benefits.

2.  **Experimental Setup and Hyperparameters:** The experiments demonstrate strong results, but crucial details about hyperparameter selection are missing. Specifically, how was the step size σ chosen for the different algorithms in the experiments? The performance of these methods is often sensitive to the step size, and providing details on its selection (e.g., grid search, theoretical value, etc.) is essential for reproducibility and for understanding the practical robustness of the methods.

3.  **Intuition on Non-Uniform Sampling:** Could the authors provide more intuition as to why non-uniform sampling is potentially even *more* critical for saddle-point problems than for standard convex minimization? While it is generally understood to help by sampling more "important" components more often, an explanation specific to the dual-variable structure of saddle-point problems would be enlightening.

4.  **Presentation of the Main Contribution:** Given that the novel proof technique is the paper's core intellectual contribution, the manuscript would benefit from including a high-level proof sketch or key steps of the monotone operator analysis in the main body. Currently, the details are relegated to the appendix, which may cause readers to miss the most innovative aspect of the work.

**Minor Typos:**
*   Line 78: In the description of the "factored" split, the summation index for `B_x_k` appears to be `k`, but the text says `∑ k∈K`, which might be a typo for `d` if `x` is in `R^d`. Please clarify.
*   Line 121 (inferred): In the description of the Forward-Backward algorithm, the second equality in the serial update for `y_t` seems to contain an extra `y_{t-1}` term. Please double-check the expression.

**Overall Evaluation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It successfully bridges the gap between the powerful variance reduction techniques and the broad class of saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis via monotone operators is both novel and insightful, and the practical utility of the proposed algorithms is well-supported by strong experimental results. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and can be addressed in a revision. This work meaningfully extends the field and presents an analysis approach that others are likely to build upon. I strongly recommend acceptance.
