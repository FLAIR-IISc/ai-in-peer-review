- Extends SVRG and SAGA to achieve linear convergence for saddle-point problems, a significant theoretical advance.  
- Frames saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, enabling analysis beyond convex minimization to variational inequalities.  
- Well-motivated by practical ML applications (non-separable losses, robust optimization) and clearly stated problem setup.  
- Extends to the catalyst acceleration framework, improving practical utility of proposed algorithms.  
- Empirical results show the accelerated SVRG variant outperforms existing methods on tested datasets.  

- Novel convergence analysis is the primary novel theoretical contribution, filling a gap for saddle-point optimization.  
- Monotone operator framework broadens applicability to a wider class of problems beyond saddle points.  
- Practical relevance: handles non-separable loss functions and regularizers important in real-world ML.  
- Clear motivation and connection to applied ML scenarios.  

- Clarity could be improved, especially the transition from algorithmic extensions to the monotone operator framework.  
- Needs more examples distinguishing the two notions of splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) beyond the bilinear case.  
- Main proofs are insufficiently detailed in the paper; relying heavily on appendices limits accessibility.  
- Non-uniform sampling details are incomplete: the specific distribution π and rationale outside the bilinear case are not fully explained.  
- Experimental step-size selection (σ) is not described; lacks discussion of sensitivity and choice rationale.  

- Typos/corrections: Line 78 replace "k" with "d"; Line 121 remove extra "y_{t-1}" term in second equality.  

- Arguments for acceptance: major theoretical advance, broad applicability via monotone operator framework, strong empirical validation, and high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity issues, insufficient proof detail, and missing specifics on non-uniform sampling and step-size selection.  

- Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing clarity, providing more proof detail, and clarifying non-uniform sampling and step-size choices.
