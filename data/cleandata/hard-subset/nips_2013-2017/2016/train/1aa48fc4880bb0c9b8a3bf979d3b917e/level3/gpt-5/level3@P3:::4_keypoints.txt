- Main contribution: first variance-reduced SVRG/SAGA algorithms with provable linear convergence for strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems.
- Core idea: apply variance reduction to stochastic forward–backward splitting for the monotone operator of saddle-point optimality conditions.
- Monotone-operator analysis: departs from standard convex minimization and extends to general monotone inclusions and variational inequalities.
- Two operator decompositions: element-wise split and factored split, the latter reducing SAGA storage from O(nd) to O(n + d).
- Handles non-separable losses/regularizers, enabling applications like AUC surrogates, structured/overlapping-group regularizers, and TV.
- Connects to and generalizes batch primal–dual methods (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock) and finite-sum variance-reduced methods (SAG/SVRG/SAGA).
- Non-uniform sampling: theory and empirically shown to outperform uniform sampling via improved smoothness constants L̄(π).
- Catalyst-style acceleration: adapted to the setting and empirically effective on ill-conditioned problems.
- Acceleration proof: provided and solid for SVRG but not for SAGA (claimed to "should work" without proof).
- SAGA resampling issue: theory requires resampling after iterate updates with non-uniform sampling, but experiments use a mixed heuristic without clarification.
- Practical tuning gap: algorithms need strong convexity and smoothness constants (L, L̄(π)) for stepsizes/epochs; no practical estimation or adaptive heuristics provided.
- Experimental validation: supports theory—variance-reduced methods beat batch baselines, non-uniform sampling helps, and acceleration aids ill-conditioned cases.
- Empirical coverage lacking ablations: mini-batch size effects, wall-clock and memory comparisons (element-wise vs factored SAGA), sensitivity to mis-specified strong convexity, and a simple VI example.
- Notation confusion: reuse of symbol K for both smooth function and design matrix is misleading and should be fixed.
- Clarity/readability: paper is dense; exposition around operator splits, probability design, and roles of L vs L̄(π) should be improved; reiterate assumptions at algorithm statements.
- Pseudocode: useful but should explicitly highlight resampling option and its necessity for the theoretical guarantees.
- Technical quality: proofs are sound and carefully track Lipschitz and variance terms; acceleration analysis is rigorous for SVRG.
- Overall assessment: highly original and significant contribution to theory and practice; recommendation: Accept.
