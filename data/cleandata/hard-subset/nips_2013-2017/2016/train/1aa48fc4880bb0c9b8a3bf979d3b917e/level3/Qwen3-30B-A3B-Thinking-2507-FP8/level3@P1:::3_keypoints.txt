Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst acceleration to convex–concave saddle‑point problems.

Novel convergence analysis using monotone-operator theory rather than convex‑minimization techniques.

Analysis shows methods apply beyond saddle‑point problems to variational inequalities.

Identifies two split types (individual/function‑based vs. factored/partial‑derivative‑based) and requires convex‑concave splits.

Establishes non‑uniform sampling as critical for theoretical efficiency gains.

Shows Catalyst acceleration produces methods superior to accelerated batch forward‑backward on many problems.

First convergence analysis of variance‑reduction methods for saddle‑point problems, broadening applicability.

Thorough complexity analysis across split types and sampling strategies; non‑uniform sampling improves bounds.

Experiments (binary classification with non‑separable losses/regularizers) validate importance of non‑uniform sampling and acceleration; accelerated SVRG excels on ill‑conditioned problems.

Technical quality: rigorous proofs and careful treatment of extending variance reduction to saddle‑points.

Clarity: well organized and clearly explained theoretical and experimental contributions.

Originality: strong — significant departure from prior work focused on convex minimization.

Significance: high — addresses ubiquitous ML problems and provides first large‑scale linearly convergent algorithms for them.

Major weakness: proximal operator cost O(n + d) per iteration is a fundamental bottleneck that may negate theoretical advantages.

Insufficient discussion/analysis of regimes (n, d) where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch methods and lack of tight n,d dependence in bounds.

Unclear theoretical comparison between factored and individual splits; storage vs. complexity tradeoffs not fully resolved.

Requested revisions: provide detailed characterization of when methods beat batch methods and clarify whether factored splits are generally worse than individual splits.

Overall recommendation: technically strong and likely acceptable if proximal‑cost concerns are addressed.
