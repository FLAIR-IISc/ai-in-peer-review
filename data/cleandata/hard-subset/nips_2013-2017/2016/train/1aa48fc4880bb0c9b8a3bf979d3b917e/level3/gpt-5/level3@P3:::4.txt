Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, yielding the first large-scale algorithms with provable linear convergence in this setting. The core idea is to apply variance reduction to stochastic forward–backward splitting for the monotone operator corresponding to the saddle-point optimality conditions. Two complementary decompositions of the smooth gradient operator B are proposed: an element-wise split (e.g., per matrix entry in bilinear problems) and a factored split (rows/columns), which is particularly advantageous for SAGA due to dramatically reduced storage (O(n + d) vs. O(nd)). The analysis departs from standard convex minimization: it uses monotone operator theory rather than smooth strongly convex objectives, enabling extensions beyond saddle-point problems to general monotone inclusions and variational inequalities common in game theory.

The work relates to and generalizes batch primal–dual/forward–backward methods (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock), stochastic forward–backward schemes with O(1/t) rates, and finite-sum variance-reduced methods (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) for convex minimization. It also connects to Catalyst acceleration and shows how to accelerate the variance-reduced saddle-point methods. Non-uniform sampling is analyzed and shown to be superior to uniform sampling theoretically (via improved smoothness constants L̄(π)) and empirically.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: First linear convergence guarantees for SVRG/SAGA in strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, with a clean monotone-operator analysis that applies to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities.
- Handles non-separable losses/regularizers: Unlike most prior stochastic primal–dual and coordinate methods that rely on separability, the proposed algorithms target non-separable K and M with simple proximal maps, addressing important machine learning settings (AUC surrogates, structured losses, overlapping/group regularizers, TV).
- Operator splits: The element-wise and factored splits are well-motivated; the factored split substantially reduces SAGA’s memory footprint and aligns with sketching/subsampling strategies.
- Variance reduction for forward–backward: Conceptually clear extension; the theorems provide explicit linear rates in terms of condition numbers L and data-dependent L̄(π).
- Non-uniform sampling: Sound theory and careful choices of probabilities for bilinear problems; experiments validate the gains over uniform sampling.
- Acceleration: Catalyst-style regularization improves complexity and, in practice, yields the best method on ill-conditioned problems.
- Experiments: Results on non-separable and separable tasks support the theory, including comparisons against strong batch baselines and primal variants when applicable.

Weaknesses and questions
- Acceleration details (Section 5): While the Catalyst framework is adapted elegantly, it remains unclear how to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in practice to realize the predicted speedups. The paper mentions more aggressive updates but does not specify a principled schedule or stopping rule per proximal-point subproblem. Providing a concrete adaptive strategy and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the practical guidance.
- SAGA acceleration: The acceleration proof is provided only for SVRG, with a claim that it “should work” for SAGA. Either a proof sketch for SAGA or clearly stated conditions under which the same rate holds would improve completeness.
- Resampling in SAGA with non-uniform sampling: The linear-rate guarantee requires resampling updates after the iterate step. The experiments use a mixture strategy without resampling. Please clarify the gap between the theoretical requirement and the practical heuristic, and whether the observed behavior can be partially justified.
- Assumption knowledge and tuning: The methods require strong convex–concave parameters and smoothness constants (L, L̄(π)) to set stepsizes/epoch lengths. Discussion or heuristics for estimating these in practice (e.g., backtracking, adaptive stepsizes) would aid reproducibility and robustness.
- Notation: The smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K share the same symbol, which is confusing. Use distinct symbols (e.g., Φ(x, y) for the smooth part and A for the matrix) throughout.
- Empirical coverage: Additional ablations would be helpful: (i) effect of mini-batch size m, (ii) wall-clock comparisons including memory overhead for SAGA with element-wise vs. factored splits, (iii) sensitivity to mis-specified strong convexity constants, and (iv) a simple variational inequality example to illustrate the broader applicability.

Quality
The paper appears technically sound. Proofs leverage a nontrivial monotone-operator framework, carefully tracking Lipschitz constants and variance terms to obtain linear rates. The acceleration analysis is solid for SVRG. The empirical results (main text and appendix) are consistent with the theory: non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform, variance-reduced methods beat batch baselines, and acceleration helps in ill-conditioned regimes.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized but dense. Improving exposition around the operator splits, probability design, and the role of L vs. L̄(π) would help readers implement the algorithms. Fix the notation collision for K, and reiterate the assumptions (A–C) at algorithm statements. Pseudocode is useful; highlighting the resampling option and its necessity for theory in Algorithm 2 would avoid confusion.

Originality
High. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with linear convergence via monotone-operator analysis—and the explicit handling of non-separable structures and non-uniform sampling—is a significant conceptual and technical step beyond prior variance-reduction work and beyond classical stochastic primal–dual methods.

Significance
High. Many ML problems are naturally posed as saddle points beyond separable cases. The results advance the state of the art in both theory and practice, and the operator-theoretic view should inspire follow-up work (e.g., to weaker assumptions, adaptive variants, and broader VI applications).

Pros
- First linear convergence for variance-reduced methods in strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems.
- Monotone-operator framework broadens applicability (including VIs).
- Practical, storage-efficient factored split for SAGA.
- Strong theory and experiments; non-uniform sampling clearly beneficial.
- Catalyst-based acceleration is effective and simple to integrate.

Cons
- Acceleration section lacks a concrete adaptive scheme for updating (x̄, ȳ).
- No acceleration proof for SAGA; theoretical–practical gap for resampling in SAGA.
- Reliance on known condition numbers; limited guidance for tuning.
- Notational confusion between function K and matrix K.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a substantial, well-supported contribution with both theoretical depth and practical relevance. Addressing the noted clarity and acceleration-details issues would further strengthen the final version.
