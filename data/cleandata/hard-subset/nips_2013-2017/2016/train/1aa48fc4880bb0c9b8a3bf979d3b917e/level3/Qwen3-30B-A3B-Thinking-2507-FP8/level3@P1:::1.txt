

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The work builds on recent advances in stochastic optimization for finite sums (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) but addresses the key challenge of extending these methods to saddle-point problems where the convex minimization analysis does not apply. The authors use monotone operator theory to prove convergence, showing the approach extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Key contributions include: (1) establishing the need for convex-concave splits rather than convex minimization splits; (2) demonstrating non-uniform sampling is critical for efficiency; and (3) showing how to accelerate the algorithms using the catalyst framework.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical novelty. By framing saddle-point problems through monotone operators, the authors provide a unified convergence analysis that reveals the broader applicability of their approach. This is significant as previous work on saddle-point problems has primarily focused on batch methods or sublinear convergence rates. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates the work's theoretical breadth beyond machine learning applications. The paper also makes important distinctions between different types of problem splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) and provides clear theoretical bounds that explain why non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform sampling.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The presentation of the monotone operator connection is particularly problematic - it appears late in the paper as an extension despite being central to the analysis. The paper would benefit from reorganizing to foreground this theoretical foundation earlier. The experimental section is limited to two datasets (sido and rcv1), which is insufficient to demonstrate the algorithm's broad utility. More datasets, particularly those with non-separable losses and regularizers, would strengthen the empirical claims. The paper also fails to compare against accelerated stochastic primal-dual methods like Chambolle-Pock (Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015), which are directly relevant to the problem class.

The theoretical analysis has some confusing elements. The constant μ in Theorem 2 is not clearly defined - it's unclear whether it refers to the strong convexity-concavity constant from assumption (A) or a monotonicity constant. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in Appendix B) is also confusing - the paper should explicitly state whether these assumptions imply strong monotonicity. Additionally, the paper inconsistently uses L (sometimes as condition number, sometimes as Lipschitz constant), which could mislead readers.

The applicability of the assumptions to practical machine learning problems needs clarification. For instance, the authors question whether assumption (A) holds for SVM-induced saddle-point problems, but don't provide concrete examples or analysis. A brief discussion of how the assumptions apply to common machine learning problems would strengthen the paper's relevance.

The paper's significance is high as it addresses a fundamental gap in large-scale saddle-point optimization. The theoretical advances are sound and the proposed algorithms have clear practical advantages over existing methods, particularly for non-separable problems. The acceleration framework via the catalyst technique is particularly compelling and shows how to achieve superior performance to batch methods.

To improve the paper, the authors should: (1) reorganize to present the monotone operator foundation earlier; (2) expand experiments to include more datasets and comparisons against relevant stochastic primal-dual methods; (3) clarify all theoretical constants and assumptions; (4) provide concrete examples of how assumptions apply to practical machine learning problems; and (5) fix the inconsistent use of L.

Overall, this is a theoretically strong contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. While the current presentation has some weaknesses, the core ideas are novel and valuable. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical and experimental concerns noted above. For a NIPS paper, the significance is high, the quality is good with some theoretical gaps to be resolved, and the originality is strong in extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems with monotone operator analysis. The clarity needs improvement, but the theoretical contribution is substantial. The paper would benefit from more concrete examples of how the assumptions apply to machine learning problems and a more comprehensive experimental comparison.
