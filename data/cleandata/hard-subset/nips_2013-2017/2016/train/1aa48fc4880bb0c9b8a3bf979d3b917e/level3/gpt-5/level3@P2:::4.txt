Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to strongly convex–concave composite saddle-point problems. The authors give the first linear-convergence guarantees for large-scale, non-separable saddle-point objectives of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is simple via a proximal operator. The core technical contribution is to abandon convex-minimization tools (e.g., co-coercivity) and analyze convergence through the lens of monotone operators: the saddle-point optimality system is cast as finding a zero of A+B, where A is strongly monotone with an accessible resolvent (prox of M) and B is a sum of Lipschitz operators (gradients of K or partial components). This yields linear rates for SVRG and SAGA in this setting and, importantly, extends to broader classes such as variational inequalities.

Algorithmically, the paper:
- Introduces two natural decompositions for B: (i) element-wise splits and (ii) “factored” splits aligned with the saddle structure (separate x- and y-components), enabling different storage/complexity trade-offs in SAGA/SVRG.
- Designs non-uniform sampling schemes and associated smoothness constants L̄(π), showing theoretically and empirically that tailored sampling improves convergence over uniform.
- Adapts the catalyst/proximal-point acceleration to the saddle setting, obtaining an algorithm that (up to logarithmic factors) improves upon accelerated batch methods and is particularly advantageous when per-component access is cheap.

The work is well positioned relative to prior NIPS and related literature: it contrasts with SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization; batch primal–dual/forward–backward algorithms like Chambolle–Pock and accelerated variants; stochastic forward–backward with noise that only achieve O(1/t); methods for separable saddle problems (e.g., SPDCD) that do not cover non-separable cases; and recent variance reduction for monotone operators that rely on co-coercivity. The authors’ analysis covers non-co-coercive settings required by saddle-point and VI problems.

Strengths
- Clear and principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to a central class of ML problems where separability is absent, with the first linear-convergence guarantees in this regime.
- Monotone-operator analysis is technically sound and broadens applicability (variational inequalities, general monotone inclusions).
- Careful treatment of sampling: explicit L̄(π) constants, concrete non-uniform distributions for bilinear cases, and complexity comparisons that recover known advantages of variance reduction over batch methods.
- Practical design choices (factored vs element-wise splits, mini-batching, storage trade-offs) and discussion of per-iteration costs.
- Acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper with a simple analysis that avoids Nesterov machinery and dominates accelerated batch under natural regimes.
- Experiments on non-separable losses/regularizers and a separable case support the theoretical claims; non-uniform sampling matters in practice.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Acceleration section: while τ selection and complexity trade-offs are discussed, the practical policy to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) is under-specified in the main text. More guidance or ablations on update frequency and stopping within inner loops would help reproducibility.
- The acceleration proof is given for SVRG; it is stated that the same should work for SAGA, but a formal result is not provided. Clarifying what technical hurdles (if any) prevent a SAGA proof would strengthen the paper.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step in the analysis. The experiments follow a mixture scheme without resampling; a brief discussion of the gap between theory and practice would be useful.
- The methods assume knowledge of strong convexity–concavity parameters (λ, γ) and smoothness constants (L, L̄). Practical estimation strategies (and robustness to misspecification) are not discussed; this is important for usability.
- Notation: the same symbol K denotes both the smooth function and the design matrix in bilinear examples, which is potentially confusing. Distinguishing them would improve clarity.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The theoretical results (Theorems 1–3) are well stated under clear assumptions (A–C). The monotone-operator framework is appropriately used to avoid co-coercivity. The complexity discussions are careful and connect to concrete norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max). Minor concerns remain around acceleration details, SAGA acceleration proof, and practical parameter selection.
- Clarity: Overall well-organized with algorithms and assumptions explicit. Suggested improvements: separate notation for the matrix and function K; centralize definitions of Ω, L, and L̄(π); bring more of the acceleration details from the appendix into the main text.
- Originality: Strong. Moving variance reduction to non-separable saddle-point problems via monotone operators and introducing factored splits with non-uniform sampling is novel relative to prior NIPS work.
- Significance: High. Many ML problems (robust optimization, structured losses like AUC surrogates, complex regularizers) fit this framework. The practical impact is evidenced by experiments and by the improved theoretical bounds over accelerated batch methods.

Questions for the authors
- Beyond the bilinear case, how should practitioners choose π and approximate L̄(π) without expensive precomputation? Are there adaptive sampling strategies compatible with your analysis?
- Can you provide a formal acceleration result for SAGA, or explain the main obstacles?
- How sensitive are the methods to mis-specified λ, γ, L? Could backtracking or adaptive step-size schemes be incorporated while preserving linear convergence?
- Can your analysis accommodate inexact proximal computations for M (common for complex regularizers), and what error tolerance preserves linear rates?
- For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, can you comment on the theoretical impact of the practical “mixture sampling without resampling” heuristic?

Pros for acceptance
- First linear-convergent VR algorithms for non-separable saddle-point problems with solid theory.
- Broad applicability via monotone operator view; includes variational inequalities.
- Non-uniform sampling and factored splits are both theoretically motivated and empirically valuable.
- Acceleration that can dominate accelerated batch methods in relevant regimes.
- Clear empirical gains on realistic non-separable setups.

Cons against acceptance
- Acceleration details (policy for updating (x̄, ȳ)) and a formal SAGA acceleration proof are missing.
- Dependence on problem constants without guidance on estimation may hinder plug-and-play use.
- Some theoretical requirements (resampling in SAGA non-uniform) are relaxed in experiments without discussion.

Minor comments
- Use distinct symbols for the smooth function (e.g., Φ) and the data matrix (K) to avoid confusion.
- Collect the norms and constants in a self-contained notation block early in the paper.
- Briefly summarize appendix acceleration details in the main text.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a substantive, well-supported contribution by bringing variance reduction with linear rates to non-separable saddle-point problems, grounded in a clean monotone-operator analysis, and demonstrates meaningful practical benefits. Addressing the noted clarity and acceleration concerns would further strengthen the final version.
