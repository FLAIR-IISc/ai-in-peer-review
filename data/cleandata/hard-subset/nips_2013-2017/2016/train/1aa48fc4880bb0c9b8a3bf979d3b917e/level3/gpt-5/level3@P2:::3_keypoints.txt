- Paper studies strongly convex–concave minx maxy K+M with smooth K and proximal-friendly non-smooth M, extending SVRG/SAGA and catalyst acceleration to this setting.  
- Analysis uses monotone operator theory (A+B splitting) avoiding co-coercivity, thus applying to variational inequalities beyond saddle points.  
- Two splitting schemes for B are considered: individual component splits and factored row/column splits, enabling lower storage and non-uniform sampling.  
- Theoretical results: linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA and catalyst-accelerated variants, with iteration/time complexities expressed via L, L̄(π), Tprox(M), T(B), T(Bi).  
- Non-uniform sampling is emphasized and analyzed; bilinear problems receive explicit bounds in terms of ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max.  
- Accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling can outperform batch forward–back and its accelerated version under stated conditions.  
- Experiments (AUC surrogate + OSCAR-like regularizer) show non-uniform sampling matters and accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling performs best, especially under ill-conditioning.  
- On separable problems, primal SAGA is faster but not applicable to non-separable settings; primal accelerated batch methods are weaker.  
- Theory quality: monotone-operator convergence proofs are sound and technically non-trivial; linear rates and L̄(π) analysis are clearly stated.  
- Complexity trade-offs: authors separate oracle and proximal costs; key practical caveat is proxM cost per incremental iteration may negate gains—acknowledged but could be emphasized more.  
- Experiments: informative but limited in datasets/scale, lack wall-clock comparisons across varying Tprox(M), and SAGA non-uniform implementation deviates from theoretical resampling.  
- Clarity: paper is well written with a unified monotone-operator presentation and clear explanation of splits and non-uniform sampling.  
- Suggested clarity improvements: foreground proximal bottleneck in intro/table, add table mapping regimes (n vs d, sparsity) to split/sampling choices, expand main-text description of accelerated scheme.  
- Originality: novel extension of variance-reduced methods and catalyst acceleration to general strongly convex–concave problems via monotone operators; novel split notions and non-uniform sampling analysis.  
- Significance: advances methods for non-separable saddle points with first linear-convergence incremental methods in this class; impact strongest when proxM is inexpensive.  
- Strengths: first linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for general saddle points, careful complexity analysis, catalyst adaptation, and solid experimental evidence for key choices.  
- Weakness — proximal bottleneck: need explicit characterization (e.g., inequality) showing when methods beat accelerated batch FB in wall-clock time.  
- Weakness — factored vs individual splits: request for formal comparison/theorem on L̄(π) bounds and conditions (spectral decay, leverage scores) favoring one split.  
- Weakness — tightness: ask for lower bounds or adversarial constructions showing ‖K‖F and ‖K‖max dependences are unimprovable up to constants.  
- Weakness — SAGA resampling: experiments omit theoretical resampling; request comment on empirical impact and practical workarounds preserving guarantees.  
- Weakness — empirics: request wall-clock plots, experiments varying Tprox(M) and sparsity, sensitivity to mini-batch size and regularization mis-specification.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, rigorous analysis; practical sampling/splitting guidance; simple-to-implement catalyst acceleration; addresses important non-separable settings.  
- Cons for acceptance: per-iteration proximal cost may negate gains; limited wall-clock/regime empirical exploration; some theoretical prescriptions not followed in experiments.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors addressed key concerns on when accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB and on split choices; reviewer remains favorable and keeps scores unchanged.
