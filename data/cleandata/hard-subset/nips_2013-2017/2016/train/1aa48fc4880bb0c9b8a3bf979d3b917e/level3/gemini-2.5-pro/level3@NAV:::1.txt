This paper extends stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to solve large-scale convex-concave saddle-point problems. Building on the rich literature of variance reduction for convex minimization (e.g., SVRG, SAGA), the authors leverage a connection to monotone operators to establish a theoretical framework proving linear convergence. Key contributions include analyzing these methods in this more general setting, introducing acceleration and non-uniform sampling schemes, and demonstrating applicability to problems with non-separable functions, which are common in machine learning but often challenging for existing variance-reduced methods.

The paper's main strength is its significant theoretical contribution. It provides the first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for this important class of saddle-point problems, advancing the state-of-the-art beyond existing batch or standard stochastic methods. The analysis via monotone operators is a powerful insight, revealing that the proposed methods apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The proposed acceleration and non-uniform sampling techniques are also valuable practical and theoretical additions.

Despite its strong core, the paper suffers from major weaknesses in presentation and evaluation. The connection to monotone operators, which underpins the entire analysis, is presented late as an "extension" rather than being introduced as the central analytical tool, making the paper's logical flow difficult to follow. The experimental evaluation is limited, using only two datasets, which is insufficient to validate the claims broadly. Critically, the paper lacks a comparison to highly relevant work, such as accelerated stochastic Chambolle–Pock-type methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015). 

Several key technical points also require clarification. The practical applicability and strength of Assumptions (A)–(C) are not well-justified; for instance, do they hold for SVM-induced saddle-point problems? The relationship between the proposed method and standard stochastic primal-dual methods should be explicitly detailed. Furthermore, the constant μ in Theorem 2 is ambiguous, and the transition from assumptions (A)–(C) to the strong monotonicity property used in the appendix is confusing. Minor issues include inconsistent notation for L and a missing reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm. While the core ideas are original and significant, the paper requires a major revision to improve clarity, strengthen its experimental validation, and address these technical ambiguities.
