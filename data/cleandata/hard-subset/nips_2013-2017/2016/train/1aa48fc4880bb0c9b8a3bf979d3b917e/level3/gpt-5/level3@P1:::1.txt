This paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y) where M is proximable and strongly convex–concave and K is smooth. The main contribution is to extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting and prove linear convergence without requiring separability of the loss/regularizer in the primal or dual. The analysis proceeds via monotone operator theory by viewing saddle-point problems as zeros of A+B, with A a strongly monotone set-valued operator (prox-friendly) and B a smooth monotone operator split as a finite sum of Lipschitz components, allowing two natural notions of splitting: by functions or by partial derivatives (“factored” split). The paper further (i) emphasizes the importance of non-uniform sampling for tighter complexity bounds and better empirical performance, and (ii) adapts the catalyst framework to obtain an accelerated variant whose complexity dominates accelerated batch methods. The techniques also extend to variational inequalities and more general monotone inclusions.

Relation to prior work: Compared to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization, the paper broadens scope to saddle-point problems and monotone operators, where co-coercivity does not generally hold. It contrasts with batch primal–dual forward–backward and Chambolle–Pock-type methods, which achieve linear rates under strong convexity but do not exploit finite-sum structure. Prior stochastic forward–backward analyses yield O(1/t) rates; here, variance reduction yields linear rates. The paper also connects to separable-coordinate primal–dual methods such as SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015), highlighting that those require separability while the present approach covers non-separable losses/regularizers.

Strengths
- New analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems via monotone operators, with clear convergence guarantees and explicit complexity bounds.
- Careful treatment of splitting strategies (individual vs. factored) and of non-uniform sampling; the bounds match intuition from matrix sketching and lead to practical guidance.
- Acceleration via a simple proximal-point/catalyst wrapper is well motivated and yields improved complexity that dominates accelerated batch PD methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems.
- The framework naturally extends beyond saddle points to monotone inclusions and variational inequalities.
- Empirical evidence (two datasets) supports linear convergence, the benefits of non-uniform sampling, and the advantage of the accelerated variant under ill-conditioning.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
- Exposition: The monotone-operator perspective underpins both algorithms and proofs, yet it is introduced late as an “extension.” Bringing the monotone inclusion A+B viewpoint (and strong monotonicity) forward would clarify why the convex minimization analyses do not apply and how the present proof differs.
- Assumptions: Please clarify the strength and scope of (A)–(C).
  - (A) Strong (λ,γ)-convex–concavity of M: specify when common ML formulations satisfy this. For ERM with hinge/logistic losses and ℓ2-regularization, is strong concavity in y always ensured (i.e., is g strongly convex), or is smoothing/regularization required? Do standard SVM/AUC formulations fall into the required class without modification?
  - (B) L: the text sometimes calls L a “condition number”; it is the Lipschitz constant of B under Ω/Ω*. Please unify terminology and explicitly relate L, L̄(π) and the spectral/Frobenius norms in bilinear cases.
  - (C) Splits into convex–concave terms are needed here (unlike convex minimization). Make this necessity explicit and provide guidance on choosing the split in general non-bilinear settings.
- Theorem 2: The constant μ appears in the rate but is not defined in the main text. Confirm that μ is the strong monotonicity parameter (e.g., min{λ,γ} under Ω), and explain how the SAGA rate compares to Theorem 1 (SVRG) as a function of μ, L, and L̄(π), including when resampling is required for non-uniform sampling.
- Transition from (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity: Make explicit whether (A)–(C) imply the strong monotonicity of A (and monotonicity of B) used in the appendix, and state the exact constants in Ω and Ω* norms.
- Computing constants: Discuss practicality of estimating L and L̄(π) to set stepsizes; provide robust defaults or backtracking/line-search options if exact values are unavailable.
- Experimental evaluation: Only two datasets are used, with limited baselines. To substantiate the “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” claim, please add:
  - More datasets with varying n,d and conditioning, including dense/sparse and ill-conditioned cases.
  - Comparisons to accelerated/stochastic primal–dual methods beyond batch CP, e.g., SPDC (Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015) and accelerated Chambolle–Pock variants, clarifying the separability assumptions and whether your method applies when theirs does not.
  - Ablations on sampling (uniform vs. non-uniform), mini-batch size, factored vs. individual splits, and memory/computation trade-offs (esp. SAGA storage for individual splits).
- Minor presentation issues:
  - Inconsistent notation for L (Lipschitz constant vs. “condition number”); unify.
  - Provide a clear citation for forward–backward splitting (e.g., Bauschke & Combettes, 2011; Combettes & Pesquet survey) in addition to [19].
  - Define μ, τ, and all norms upon first use; ensure the prox parameters and scaling by (λ,γ) are consistent across sections.
  - The resampling detail for non-uniform SAGA (after the iterate update) is easy to miss; highlight it and discuss practical heuristics (as in your experiments).

Quality: The theoretical development appears sound and carefully connected to monotone operator theory. The linear rates and complexity bounds are credible and align with empirical trends. Some constants and assumptions need clearer definition, and broader experiments would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity: Generally well written, but the narrative would benefit from reorganizing around the monotone operator framework, unifying notation for L/μ/Ω, and clarifying the role of assumptions and constants.

Originality: High. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems via monotone operators (without co-coercivity) appears novel, as does the systematic treatment of non-uniform sampling and a simple catalyst acceleration in this setting.

Significance: High. Many ML formulations with non-separable losses/regularizers fit this framework. The methods are likely to influence practice and spur further work on variance reduction for monotone inclusions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (linear convergence) for an important problem class not covered by prior separable methods.
- Practical insights on sampling/splitting with supporting experiments.
- Acceleration that dominates accelerated batch PD methods under broad conditions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and missing comparisons to strong stochastic primal–dual baselines.
- Expositional issues around assumptions, constants, and the centrality of the monotone-operator view.

Suggested references to add/discuss
- Zhang, Y. and Xiao, L. (2015), Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method (SPDC), JMLR: accelerated stochastic Chambolle–Pock-type method under separability; contrast assumptions and rates with your approach.

Overall, this is a strong paper; with improved clarity and a more comprehensive experimental suite including additional baselines, it would make a solid contribution.
