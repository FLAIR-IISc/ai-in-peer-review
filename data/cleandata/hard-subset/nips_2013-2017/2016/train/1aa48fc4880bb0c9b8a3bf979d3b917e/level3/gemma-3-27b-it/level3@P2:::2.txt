This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning arising from duality. The authors extend algorithms like SVRG and SAGA to this setting, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating acceleration via a catalyst framework. A notable extension is showing the applicability of the analysis to a broader class of problems characterized by monotone operators. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem with a theoretically sound approach, and backed by experimental validation.

The core idea is to adapt stochastic variance reduction techniques, successful in convex minimization, to the saddle-point setting. This is not trivial, as the convex minimization analysis doesn't directly translate. The authors cleverly utilize the concept of monotone operators to provide a more general convergence proof, expanding the applicability of their algorithms. They also highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically. The extension to the catalyst framework provides a further acceleration, demonstrably outperforming accelerated batch algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution, providing tools for large-scale optimization in a frequently encountered problem setting.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis is rigorous, moving beyond the standard convex minimization framework and leveraging monotone operator theory for a broader application scope.
* **Practicality:** The extension to the catalyst framework demonstrates a clear path towards improved performance in practice.
* **Generality:**  The extension to monotone operators showcases the broader applicability of the proposed methods.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, though relatively limited in scope, provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims and demonstrate performance gains.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details, making it accessible to researchers familiar with optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The assumption of strong convexity *on both* the primal and dual problems (Assumption A) feels overly restrictive. Many practical applications only exhibit strong convexity on one side. Relaxing this assumption to require strong convexity on either the primal *or* the dual problem would significantly broaden the applicability of the results. This point is also raised in the authors’ self-criticism.
* **Limited Experiments:** While the provided experiments are promising, they are limited to two datasets and a specific problem setup. More comprehensive experiments, including comparisons with state-of-the-art solvers on a wider range of saddle-point problems, would strengthen the empirical validation. Specifically, the performance compared to specialized saddle-point solvers warrants further investigation.
* **Catalyst Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of the catalyst parameter (τ) is crucial for performance. The paper provides a guideline, but a more detailed discussion of its sensitivity and practical tuning strategies would be beneficial.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks details on implementation specifics (e.g., software libraries used, parameter settings). Providing this information would aid reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning optimization.
* The proposed methods are theoretically sound and offer a significant contribution to the field.
* The experimental results, while limited, are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the approach.
* The extension to monotone operators adds further value and generality.
* The clear and well-structured presentation makes the paper accessible and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong convexity assumption limits the applicability of the results.
* The limited experimental evaluation weakens the empirical validation.
* The lack of implementation details hinders reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The authors have successfully extended stochastic variance reduction methods to the challenging setting of saddle-point problems and demonstrated their effectiveness. While the strong convexity assumption and limited experimental evaluation are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work outweighs these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the implications of relaxing the strong convexity assumption to require it on only the primal *or* the dual problem.
2.  Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and problem setups, and compare the performance with state-of-the-art saddle-point solvers.
3. Provide a more detailed discussion of the catalyst parameter tuning and its sensitivity.
4. Briefly mention the implementation details, including software and key parameter choices.
