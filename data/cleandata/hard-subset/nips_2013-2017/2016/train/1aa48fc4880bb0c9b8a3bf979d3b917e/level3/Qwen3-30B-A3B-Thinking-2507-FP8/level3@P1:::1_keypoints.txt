- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory to prove convergence and extends applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Key contributions: need for convex-concave splits (not convex minimization splits), importance of non-uniform sampling, and acceleration via the catalyst framework.  
- Theoretical novelty: unified convergence analysis via monotone operators that broadens applicability beyond batch methods and sublinear rates.  
- Section 6 extension to monotone operators is particularly valuable for theoretical breadth.  
- Clearly distinguishes different problem splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) and provides bounds explaining superiority of non-uniform sampling.  
- Presentation flaw: monotone operator connection appears late and should be foregrounded earlier.  
- Experimental weakness: limited to two datasets (sido, rcv1), insufficient to demonstrate broad utility.  
- Missing empirical comparisons to accelerated stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., Chambolle-Pock / Zhang & Xiao 2015).  
- Theoretical clarity issues: the constant μ in Theorem 2 is not clearly defined (strong convexity-concavity vs. monotonicity).  
- Confusing transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity; paper should state if assumptions imply strong monotonicity.  
- Inconsistent notation: L is used ambiguously (condition number vs. Lipschitz constant), risking reader confusion.  
- Practical applicability unclear: lacks concrete examples or analysis showing assumptions hold for common ML problems (e.g., SVM-induced saddle points).  
- Significance is high: fills a fundamental gap in large-scale saddle-point optimization with sound theoretical advances and practical advantages for non-separable problems.  
- Recommendations: reorganize to present monotone operator foundation earlier; expand experiments and include relevant comparisons; clarify constants and assumptions; provide practical examples; fix inconsistent use of L.  
- Overall verdict: theoretically strong and original contribution with high significance, but needs improved clarity and broader empirical validation.
