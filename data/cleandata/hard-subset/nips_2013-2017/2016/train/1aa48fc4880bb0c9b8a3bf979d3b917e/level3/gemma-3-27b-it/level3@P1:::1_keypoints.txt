- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems and provides the first linear convergence guarantees.  
- Emphasizes non-uniform sampling and proposes a catalyst-based acceleration technique.  
- Frames the problem via monotone operator theory, extending applicability to variational inequalities beyond standard saddle-points.  
- Builds on SVRG/SAGA literature and correctly identifies limits of directly applying those methods to saddle-point formulations.  
- The monotone-operator connection is novel and potentially powerful for generalization.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and establishes linear convergence under stated assumptions.  
- Experimental results (limited) show effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, especially the accelerated variant, and validate non-uniform sampling.  
- Practical insight on two notions of splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) useful for implementation.  
- Presentation lacks clarity, with the monotone-operator connection introduced late instead of being integrated early.  
- Experiments are limited to two datasets and a single metric (distance to optimizer); broader evaluation needed.  
- Missing comparisons to relevant state-of-the-art methods (e.g., accelerated stochastic Chambolle–Pock).  
- Assumptions (A)-(C), particularly strong convex-concavity (A), need clarification and discussion of applicability (e.g., SVM-induced problems).  
- Paper does not clearly distinguish its approach from standard stochastic primal-dual methods; advantages need clearer articulation.  
- Technical issues: ambiguous meaning of μ in Theorem 2 and abrupt transition to strong monotonicity in the appendix.  
- Notation inconsistencies (e.g., ‘L’ used for both condition number and Lipschitz constant) cause confusion.  
- Missing a reference for the Forward–Backward algorithm.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-points, linear convergence, monotone-operator insights, potential impact, and supportive experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear presentation, insufficient experimental evaluation and comparisons, and ambiguous assumptions/technical details.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, expand experiments and comparisons, and clarify assumptions and technical points.
