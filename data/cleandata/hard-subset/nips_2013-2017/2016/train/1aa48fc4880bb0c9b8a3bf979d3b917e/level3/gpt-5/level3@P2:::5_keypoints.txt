- Studies min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with M proximal and K smooth; extends SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle points, giving first large-scale algorithms with linear convergence.  
- Uses a monotone-operator framework: cast problem as finding zero of A+B (A strongly monotone from M, B sum of Lipschitz forward operators), extending applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Algorithmic contributions: two natural splits for B (by functions or partial derivatives), non-uniform sampling analysis, and catalyst-style acceleration for SVRG with improved complexities.  
- Provides rates in terms of problem constants (L, L̄(π)), mini-batch size, and proximal costs; gives complexity bounds for bilinear K under different samplings.  
- Experiments (AUC surrogate, non-separable proximal regularizer, rcv1) show accelerated SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling outperform batch methods and uniform sampling, especially under ill-conditioning.  
- Theory: assumptions and linear convergence guarantees are clear; extension to general monotone inclusions is elegant and technically meaningful; catalyst adaptation is well justified.  
- Algorithms: nontrivial, careful extensions of variance reduction to forward–backward saddle-point setting, including storage/resampling and non-uniform sampling handling.  
- Empirics: supportive but on a limited dataset set; implementation choices are discussed but experiments could be broader.  
- Clarity issues: need a general recipe for non-uniform π beyond bilinear cases and guidance on estimating Li/L̄(π) in practice.  
- Clarity issues: lack of practical details on step sizes (σ), epoch lengths, mini-batch choices, and whether λ,γ,L,L̄ are known or estimated/adjusted online.  
- Clarity issues: request more intuition why non-uniform sampling helps (tie L̄(π) improvements to saddle-point geometry and factored splits).  
- Clarity suggestion: include a brief proof sketch in the main text for a core theorem to aid readers.  
- Originality: builds on SVRG/SAGA templates but offers original monotone-operator analysis, split/sampling identification, and catalyst-based acceleration for saddle points.  
- Significance: practically relevant for non-separable losses/regularizers; bringing linear rates and acceleration to broad saddle-point and VI formulations is impactful.  
- Questions for authors: recommend concrete π choices beyond bilinear, computable surrogates or online estimation for Li/L̄, and sensitivity to misspecified constants or adaptive step-size rules.  
- Questions: request comment on SAGA resampling requirement for non-uniform π and implications of mixing heuristics in experiments.  
- Questions: ask for comparisons to extragradient/Mirror-Prox and accelerated primal–dual algorithms (batch and stochastic).  
- Experimental suggestions: add ablations on uniform vs non-uniform π, mini-batch sizes, conditioning/strong-convexity sensitivity, and prox vs Bi evaluation cost; report wall-clock times and prox dominance.  
- Minor issues/typos: line 78 “k”→“d”; line 121 extra y_{t−1} term; clarify pseudocode (where (x̄,ȳ) enters accelerated SVRG and resampling step in SAGA).  
- Pros: first linear-convergent variance-reduced methods for general saddle points; clean monotone-operator analysis; careful sampling treatment; practical catalyst acceleration; solid theoretical bounds and consistent experiments.  
- Cons: incremental algorithmic novelty (main novelty in analysis), limited practical guidance on π/step sizes/constants, and modest experimental scope with missing baselines.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept.
