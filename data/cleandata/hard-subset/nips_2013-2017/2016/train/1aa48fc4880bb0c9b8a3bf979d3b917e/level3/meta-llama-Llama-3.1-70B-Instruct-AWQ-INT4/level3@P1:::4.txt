This paper proposes a novel extension of the stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates and running-time complexities of the proposed algorithms, demonstrating their effectiveness in handling non-separable saddle-point problems.

The paper's main contributions include the extension of SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, the provision of both theoretical and empirical evidence of the variance-reduction methods' effectiveness, and the applicability of the analysis to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

The authors propose two gradient-operator splits: element-wise split and factored split for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. They also consider non-uniform sampling, which is shown to outperform uniform sampling in theory and experiments.

The experimental results presented in the paper support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. However, there are some minor concerns, such as the need for clarification on how to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) to achieve speed-up in the acceleration section.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the convergence and acceleration proof given for SVRG also holds for SAGA. Additionally, there is a notational issue, as the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K should use distinct symbols to avoid confusion.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of optimization, offering a novel approach to tackle non-separable saddle-point problems. The authors' thorough analysis and experimental results make a strong case for the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
