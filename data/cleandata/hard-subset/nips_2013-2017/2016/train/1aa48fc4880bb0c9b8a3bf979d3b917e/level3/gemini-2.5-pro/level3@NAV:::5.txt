This paper proposes the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems by extending the well-known variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA. While these methods have been highly successful for convex minimization over finite sums, their analysis does not directly apply to the saddle-point setting. The paper's main contribution is a novel convergence proof that reframes the problem as finding the zero of a monotone operator. This powerful and generalizable proof technique not only establishes linear convergence for the proposed algorithms but also extends their applicability to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities.

The strengths of this work are significant. It addresses a crucial gap in large-scale optimization, providing a theoretical and practical path to efficiently solve problems with non-separable losses or regularizers, which are common in machine learning. The claim of achieving the first linear convergence rate for stochastic methods in this domain represents a major advance over previous batch or sub-linear stochastic methods. The originality lies in the sophisticated proof technique; interpreting saddle-point optimization through the lens of monotone operators is an elegant approach that could inspire further research and has the added benefit of providing simpler proofs for existing algorithms like forward-backward splitting. The experimental results validate the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods with non-uniform sampling are highly competitive and often outperform prior art.

Despite the paper’s high quality, there are several points that could be clarified to improve its impact. Given that the core novelty is the proof technique rather than the algorithms themselves, the authors should consider including a high-level proof sketch in the main paper to better highlight this contribution. Furthermore, some key details require elaboration: (1) The paper should specify the non-uniform sampling strategy (π) used for problems beyond the bilinear case. (2) The method for selecting the step size σ in the experiments needs to be described for reproducibility. (3) An intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears more critical for saddle-point problems than for convex minimization would be valuable. Finally, there are minor typos on line 78 (k should be d) and line 121 (an extra y_{t-1} term).

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that meaningfully advances the field of stochastic optimization. The new analysis is interesting and the results are important. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors address the points of clarity.
