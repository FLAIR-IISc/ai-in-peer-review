This paper presents an important and timely extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. The primary contribution is providing the first large-scale algorithms for this problem class that are proven to converge linearly. While the algorithms themselves are straightforward adaptations of their convex minimization counterparts, the main novelty and technical contribution lies in the convergence analysis. The authors correctly note that standard analyses for strongly-convex minimization do not apply here. Instead, they provide a new proof framework based on the theory of monotone operators, which is more general and powerful. This framework not only proves convergence for the proposed saddle-point algorithms but also applies to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper further explores practical aspects, including different ways to split the objective function (e.g., "factored" splits), the critical role of non-uniform sampling for performance, and an acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature. Saddle-point formulations are common in machine learning, arising from duality, robust optimization, and structured prediction problems (e.g., AUC maximization). They are particularly crucial for handling non-separable loss functions or regularizers where standard primal methods like SVRG/SAGA cannot be directly applied. By providing the first linearly convergent stochastic methods for this general class, this work represents a major advance for large-scale optimization in machine learning.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The core novelty lies in the convergence proof technique. Shifting the analysis from convex functions to the more general framework of monotone operators is a strong and elegant contribution. This approach is not a trivial extension of existing proofs and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying optimization theory. This perspective also allows the authors to unify saddle-point problems and variational inequalities under the same algorithmic umbrella and provides a clear path for analyzing such methods.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed methods are not just of theoretical interest. The authors demonstrate through experiments that SVRG and SAGA, particularly with non-uniform sampling and acceleration, significantly outperform existing batch and stochastic methods on problems with non-separable objectives. The ability to handle complex, non-separable regularizers (like clustering-inducing norms) and losses (like AUC surrogates) with fast, scalable algorithms is a valuable practical contribution.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and structured. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the context of prior art, and systematically presents the proposed algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and the accelerated variant). The summary table of complexities (Table 1) is very effective at comparing the different methods and highlighting the theoretical advantages of the proposed approaches.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on the Main Contribution:** While the algorithms are simple extensions, the analysis is the key contribution. However, the main paper provides very little detail or intuition about the monotone operator proof, deferring the entire analysis to the appendix. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a short proof sketch or a high-level overview of the key steps of the analysis in the main body (perhaps in Section 6). This would make the paper more self-contained and allow readers to better appreciate the technical novelty.

2.  **Details on Practical Implementation:** The paper could be improved by providing more detail on a few practical points:
    *   **Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper clearly explains how to define non-uniform sampling probabilities for bilinear problems (based on row/column norms of the matrix K). However, it is less clear how a practitioner would define these probabilities (πi) for a general smooth, convex-concave function K(x, y). Guidance on this would increase the practical applicability of the methods.
    *   **Step-Size Selection:** The theoretical step-size σ depends on constants like L and L̄. Were these theoretical values used directly in the experiments, or was some tuning required? Clarifying this is important for reproducibility.
    *   **Intuition on Non-Uniform Sampling:** The results show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for good performance, even more so than in some convex minimization settings. The paper would benefit from a discussion providing more intuition as to why this is the case. Is it because the coupling between the primal (x) and dual (y) variables makes certain components of the gradient operator disproportionately more important?

**Detailed Questions and Comments:**

*   Could the authors elaborate on the choice of non-uniform sampling for general problems beyond the bilinear case?
*   In Section 2.2, the description of the Forward-Backward algorithm appears to have a typo in the second equality. The expression `proxσM ( xt−1 − σλ−1∂xK(xt−1, yt−1) + σγ−1∂yK(xt−1, yt−1))` seems to be missing the `(x_{t-1}, y_{t-1})` terms to which the gradient updates are applied. It should likely be written as `proxσM ( (xt−1 − σλ−1∂xK(...)), (yt−1 + σγ−1∂yK(...)) )` to be unambiguous.
*   In Section 2.4, for the bilinear case with individual splits, the text states `Bjk(x, y) = Kjk(yj,−xk)`. This seems to imply `∂x K(x,y)` corresponds to `y` and `∂y K(x,y)` corresponds to `x`, but if `K(x,y) = y^T K x`, then `∂x K = K^T y` and `-∂y K = -Kx`. The component-wise gradients seem to be mismatched. For `K_{jk} x_k y_j`, the partial w.r.t `x_k` is `K_{jk} y_j` and the partial w.r.t `y_j` is `K_{jk} x_k`. The provided expression seems to have these reversed. Please clarify.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to both the theory and practice of large-scale optimization for machine learning. The extension of variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems is an important step forward, and the use of monotone operator theory for the analysis is novel and powerful. Addressing the minor clarity issues raised above would further improve an already excellent paper.
