This paper proposes the extension of stochastic variance reduction gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods, originally developed for convex minimization, to the setting of strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems. This represents, to my knowledge, the first set of large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this important class of problems, which arise in areas like robust optimization and supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis grounded in the theory of monotone operators, which is more general than the standard analysis for convex minimization. Key contributions include the analysis of two different ways to split the problem structure ("individual" and "factored" splits), a demonstration of the crucial role of non-uniform sampling, and an elegant extension of the "catalyst" framework to accelerate the proposed methods.

**Quality and Originality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a novel and significant contribution. Extending variance reduction techniques to the saddle-point setting is non-trivial, and the authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis. The use of monotone operator theory is a key strength, as it not only provides the necessary tools for the proof but also shows that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. This is a more general and powerful approach than relying on co-coercivity, which holds for convex minimization but not for saddle-point problems.

The analysis of different splitting strategies and sampling schemes is comprehensive. The complexity results for bilinear saddle-point problems, summarized in Table 1, provide clear theoretical comparisons that align well with the known benefits of variance reduction and non-uniform sampling in the convex minimization setting. The originality is high, as this work fills a clear gap in the literature by providing the first efficient, linearly convergent incremental methods for this problem class.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem formulation, assumptions, and algorithmic descriptions are easy to follow. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating the contributions within the context of prior work on both saddle-point optimization and variance reduction methods. The experiments, while limited to a single problem type, effectively demonstrate the key empirical findings, namely the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

**Significance and Weaknesses**

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper's primary weakness lies in its assessment of the practical significance and a potential bottleneck in the overall complexity. The main concern is the computational cost of the proximal operator, `Tprox(M)`. For the non-separable problems that motivate this work, computing `prox_M` incurs a cost of `O(n + d)`. Since every iteration of the proposed incremental methods (SVRG and SAGA) requires one such computation, the per-iteration cost is fundamentally tied to the problem dimensions `n` and `d`.

This high per-iteration cost substantially challenges the claim that the proposed methods are "always superior to accelerated batch algorithms." The total complexity of an accelerated batch method is roughly `O((T(B) + Tprox(M)) * L * log(1/eps))`, which translates to `O((nd + n + d) * L * log(1/eps))`. The complexity of the proposed accelerated SVRG method is roughly `O((T(B) + Tprox(M)) * L_bar * log(1/eps))`, which is `O((nd + n + d) * L_bar * log(1/eps))`. While `L_bar` can be much smaller than `L`, the large additive `(n+d)` term in the complexity per epoch/iteration remains. This suggests that the real-world performance gain over an accelerated batch method may be modest unless the problem is extremely ill-conditioned (i.e., `L` is very large). The claim of universal superiority seems too strong and is not fully supported by the complexity analysis when the proximal cost is considered.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** It is the first paper to successfully extend variance-reduced methods like SVRG/SAGA to provide linear convergence for finite-sum saddle-point problems.
2.  **Theoretical Depth:** The convergence analysis based on monotone operators is rigorous, elegant, and applicable to a wider class of problems beyond saddle-point optimization.
3.  **Thoroughness:** The paper carefully analyzes different splitting schemes, the important role of non-uniform sampling, and provides an effective acceleration scheme.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Questionable Practical Advantage:** The `O(n+d)` per-iteration cost of the proximal operator is a major bottleneck that is not fully addressed. This makes the practical advantage over well-tuned accelerated batch methods unclear in many regimes.
2.  **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims its methods are "always superior" to accelerated batch methods, which appears to be an overstatement given the complexity analysis.

**Detailed Questions for the Authors:**

The paper is a strong contribution, but its impact would be clearer with a more nuanced discussion of the complexity trade-offs. I would encourage the authors to address the following points:

1.  **Factored vs. Individual Splits:** The paper analyzes the storage cost for factored splits but does not compare the convergence rates in detail. In common machine learning settings where `n >> d`, could the factored split lead to a theoretically worse convergence rate than the individual split? A clarification on this would be valuable.

2.  **Comparison with Accelerated Batch Methods:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis comparing the total complexity of accelerated SVRG (with non-uniform sampling) against an accelerated batch forward-backward method? Specifically, by explicitly incorporating `Tprox(M) = O(n+d)` into the total complexity, could you characterize the regimes (in terms of `n`, `d`, and the condition number `L`) where your method offers a clear computational advantage?

3.  **Fundamental Cost of `prox_M`:** For the motivating non-separable examples, is the `O(n+d)` cost for the proximal operator fundamental, or are there cases where this can be substantially reduced? The significance of the proposed algorithmic improvement is heavily dependent on this cost.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and important theoretical contribution. My main reservation concerns the practical implications of the per-iteration complexity, which seems to temper the significance of the results. A more careful and transparent discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper significantly.
