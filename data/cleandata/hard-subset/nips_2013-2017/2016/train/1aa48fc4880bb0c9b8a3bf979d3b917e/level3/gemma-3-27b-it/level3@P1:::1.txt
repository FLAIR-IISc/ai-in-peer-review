## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper investigates stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning applications such as regularized learning with non-separable losses or robust optimization. The authors extend these algorithms to this setting, providing the first linear convergence guarantees. They highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling and present an acceleration technique based on a catalyst framework. The work’s central contribution lies in framing the saddle-point problem through the lens of monotone operators, extending the applicability of their algorithms beyond traditional saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

The paper builds upon recent advances in stochastic optimization, notably SVRG [Johnson & Zhang, 2013] and SAGA [Defazio et al., 2014], which accelerate stochastic gradient descent for strongly-convex finite-sum problems.  It correctly identifies the limitations of these methods when applied directly to saddle-point formulations, emphasizing the need for a tailored analysis. The connection to monotone operator theory [Rockafellar, 1997] is a novel and potentially powerful approach, allowing a broader interpretation and generalization of the proposed algorithms.  Prior work on saddle-point problems often relies on batch algorithms or specialized stochastic methods [Zhu & Xiao, 2015], but this paper aims to bridge the gap by adapting efficient SVR techniques. 

The strengths of this paper are its theoretical rigor and the potential for practical impact. The convergence analysis is a key contribution, establishing linear convergence under reasonable assumptions. The extension to monotone operators is significant, potentially unlocking applications in a wider range of problems. The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly the accelerated variant, and validate the importance of non-uniform sampling. The insights regarding the two notions of splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) are valuable for practical implementation and algorithm design. 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The presentation suffers from a lack of clarity, particularly in the initial sections. The connection to monotone operators feels tacked on, introduced late despite being fundamental to the analysis. A more integrated presentation would strengthen the paper.  The experiments are a significant limitation. Only two datasets are used, and the analysis focuses primarily on a single metric (distance to the optimizer). A more thorough evaluation, including comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods on a broader range of datasets, is crucial. Notably absent is a comparison to accelerated stochastic Chambolle–Pock-type methods [Zhang & Xiao, 2015], which are relevant to saddle-point problems. 

The assumptions (A)-(C) need further clarification.  Specifically, the strength and applicability of assumption (A), concerning strong convex-concavity, deserve careful consideration. It's unclear whether this assumption holds for standard support vector machine (SVM)-induced saddle-point problems.  A discussion of these limitations would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the paper doesn’t adequately differentiate its approach from standard stochastic primal-dual methods. A clearer articulation of the advantages offered by this specific formulation is needed. 

Technical details also require refinement. In Theorem 2, the meaning of the constant μ is ambiguous – confirmation that it corresponds to the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix is necessary. The transition from the assumptions (A)-(C) to the concept of strong monotonicity in the appendix is abrupt and requires a more explicit explanation; a statement clarifying whether (A)-(C) *imply* strong monotonicity is essential. Throughout the paper, the notation is inconsistent, particularly the use of ‘L’ to represent both a condition number and a Lipschitz constant, leading to potential confusion.  Finally, a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm itself is missing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems.
*   Linear convergence guarantees and insightful analysis leveraging monotone operator theory.
*   Potential for significant impact in machine learning applications with saddle-point formulations.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through experimental validation (although limited).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in presentation, particularly regarding the connection to monotone operators.
*   Insufficient experimental evaluation with limited datasets and metrics.
*   Missing comparisons to relevant state-of-the-art methods.
*   Ambiguous assumptions and technical details requiring clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The theoretical results are solid, and the extension to monotone operators is a significant advance. However, the weaknesses in presentation, experimental evaluation, and clarity detract from its overall impact. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the concerns regarding clarity, expand the experimental evaluation, and provide more detailed explanations of the assumptions and technical details. A stronger comparison to existing methods is also crucial. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the quality and impact of this work.
