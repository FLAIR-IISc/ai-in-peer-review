The paper "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems. The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and uniform/non-uniform sampling strategies, demonstrating the efficiency of their approach.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The authors carefully evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of their work, acknowledging the challenges and opportunities that come with extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems.

The main contribution of the paper is the extension of SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems, which is a novel and technically non-trivial achievement. The authors provide a clear and thorough analysis of the complexity of their algorithms, including the impact of different splits and sampling strategies.

The experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, showing that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beating batch algorithms. The results are impressive, especially for ill-conditioned problems, where the accelerated version of SVRG shows a significant gain.

However, the paper also highlights a major concern: the incremental iteration incurs an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator, creating a fundamental bottleneck. This limitation may impact the overall complexity of the algorithm, potentially preventing it from improving over accelerated batch FB, especially when n >> d.

The reviewer requests a deeper discussion addressing three key points: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (ii) when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) whether the n, d dependence is tight. The authors' response to these concerns is satisfactory, but the reviewer leaves the scores unchanged.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions, including a thorough discussion of related work.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results have the potential to impact the field of optimization, providing a new tool for solving saddle-point problems. The paper also provides a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach, which is likely to influence future research in the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. While there are some limitations and concerns, the authors' response is satisfactory, and the paper is likely to have a lasting impact on the field.
