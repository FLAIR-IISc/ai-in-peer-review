- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, filling a gap in the literature.  
- Provides the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for this class of saddle-point problems.  
- Main theoretical contribution: convergence analysis using monotone operator framework rather than convex-minimization techniques.  
- Introduces two split types (individual vs. factored) with distinct theoretical and computational trade-offs.  
- Shows how the Catalyst acceleration framework can be extended to accelerate these saddle-point methods.  
- Demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically.  
- Provides complexity bounds that improve on batch methods and characterizes dependence on n, d, and problem structure.  
- Strong technical quality: thorough theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments validating claims.  
- Experiments (binary classification with non-separable regularizers/losses) confirm non-uniform sampling and acceleration are necessary to beat batch algorithms.  
- Originality: meaningful extension of variance-reduction methods to saddle-point problems and connections to variational inequalities.  
- Section 6’s monotone operator exposition broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems and is well explained.  
- Major practical concern: proximal operator computation incurs an O(n + d) cost per incremental iteration, creating a bottleneck.  
- Authors’ rebuttal: factored splits have different trade-offs (not worse), and they provide clear conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB; n,d dependence is tight in bilinear case.  
- Overall: significant, well-written contribution meeting NIPS criteria, though practical applicability may be limited by proximal-cost bottleneck.
