- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, giving first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Establishes convergence using monotone operator theory instead of convex minimization analysis.  
- Presents two natural problem decompositions: function-based split and derivative-based split.  
- Proves non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency.  
- Shows acceleration via the Catalyst framework.  
- Builds on prior SVRG/SAGA work but addresses separability in the saddle-point formulation, a distinct problem class.  
- Originality/significance: novel extension addressing many ML problems (robust optimization, non-separable losses, AUC maximization) where prior variance-reduction methods fail.  
- Theoretical quality: technically sound convergence proofs (Theorems 1–3) and justified Catalyst acceleration.  
- Clarity: well-organized, clear definitions, accessible bilinear examples and complexity analysis (Table 1).  
- Major weakness: assumes strong convexity in both primal and dual (λ>0 and γ>0), an unnecessarily restrictive and practically limiting assumption.  
- Experimental weakness: limited empirical evaluation (two datasets), no comparisons to specialized saddle-point solvers (e.g., Chambolle–Pock), and inadequate validation of non-uniform sampling benefits.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept pending major revisions—relax or justify Assumption A, add comparisons to primal-dual methods on non-separable problems (e.g., AUC), and provide stronger empirical validation of non-uniform sampling.  
- Notable positive: extension to monotone operators (Section 6) broadens applicability beyond saddle-points and is particularly compelling.
