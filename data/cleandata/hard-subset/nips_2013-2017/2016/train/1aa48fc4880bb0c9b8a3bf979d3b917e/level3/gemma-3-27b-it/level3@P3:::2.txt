This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to the challenging setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems, common in machine learning. The authors convincingly demonstrate that these methods, with relatively straightforward modifications, can achieve linear convergence in this setting, a substantial improvement over existing batch algorithms. The extension to monotone operators further broadens the applicability of their work. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a problem of considerable importance. It builds on recent advances in SVR methods ([1, 2, 3]) and leverages the connection to monotone operator theory ([4, 5, 6]).

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous and well-supported. The use of monotone operator theory is a clever and appropriate tool to establish convergence in this more general setting. The derivation of convergence rates for both SVRG and SAGA is a valuable contribution.
* **Novelty:** Extending SVR methods to saddle-point problems, and subsequently to monotone operators, is a novel contribution. The insights regarding the two notions of splits and the importance of non-uniform sampling are insightful and contribute to a deeper understanding of these algorithms in this context. The acceleration using the Catalyst framework ([7]) is a welcome addition.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a critical problem in machine learning where saddle-point formulations are prevalent (e.g., supervised learning with non-separable regularizers, robust optimization – see examples in Section 2.1). The potential for faster and more scalable algorithms in these areas is significant. The results are likely to be of interest to a broad range of researchers and practitioners.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is generally clear, and the authors provide sufficient details for the reader to understand the key ideas and the technical arguments. The examples provided in Section 2.1 help to ground the theoretical results in practical applications.
* **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments, while relatively limited, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms and their improvements over baseline methods. The comparisons to batch and stochastic alternatives are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The assumption of strong convexity on *both* the primal and dual problems (Assumption A) is overly restrictive. Many real-world applications do not satisfy this condition. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it significantly impacts the generality of the results. Relaxing this assumption to require strong convexity on only the primal *or* only the dual problem would greatly enhance the paper’s applicability.
* **Limited Numerical Experiments:** The numerical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the results are encouraging, they focus on two datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of problems, including those with non-separable losses and regularizers, and comparing against more recent state-of-the-art solvers, would significantly strengthen the paper. It would also be helpful to investigate the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of sampling probabilities.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical analysis. A more in-depth discussion of practical considerations, such as parameter tuning, the computational cost of the proximal operator, and the challenges of implementing the algorithms in high-dimensional settings, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithms.
* The paper addresses a significant problem with potential for broad impact in machine learning.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The strong convexity assumption limits the generality of the results.
* The numerical evaluation is somewhat limited and could be strengthened with additional experiments.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems is a significant achievement, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. While the strong convexity assumption and limited numerical evaluation are weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the strong convexity assumption by discussing the implications of relaxing it and potentially providing theoretical results for the case where only one of the problems is strongly convex. Furthermore, expanding the numerical experiments would strengthen the paper and provide a more compelling demonstration of the algorithms’ effectiveness.
