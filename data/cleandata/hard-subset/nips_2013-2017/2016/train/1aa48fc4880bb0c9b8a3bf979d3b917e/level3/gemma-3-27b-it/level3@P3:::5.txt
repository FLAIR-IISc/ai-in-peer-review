This paper presents a significant contribution to the theory of stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems, a class of problems ubiquitous in machine learning. The authors extend SVRG and SAGA to achieve linear convergence for these problems, a result previously lacking. The key innovation lies in framing saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, enabling a convergence analysis that generalizes beyond traditional convex minimization setups, extending to variational inequalities. The work is well-motivated, clearly identifying the limitations of existing methods and highlighting the relevance of saddle-point formulations in diverse ML applications (supervised learning with non-separable terms, robust optimization, etc.). The extension to the catalyst framework further enhances the practical utility of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength is the novel convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA applied to saddle-point problems using the monotone operator framework. This is a substantial theoretical advance, establishing linear convergence where it was previously unknown.
* **Generality:**  The monotone operator framework expands the applicability of the results beyond saddle-point problems to a broader class of problems like variational inequalities.
* **Practical Relevance:** The ability to handle non-separable loss functions and regularizers is crucial for many real-world machine learning tasks. The acceleration via the catalyst framework adds to the practical appeal.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly the accelerated SVRG variant, outperforming existing methods on the tested datasets.
* **Clear Motivation & Problem Setting:** The paper effectively explains the motivation behind studying saddle-point problems and connects them to practical machine learning scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity Issues:** While generally well-written, there are points where clarity could be improved. The transition between the algorithmic extension and the more general monotone operator framework could be smoother. Specific examples illustrating the two notions of splits (functions vs. partial derivatives) beyond the bilinear case would be beneficial.
* **Limited Proof Details:** Given that the primary novelty lies in the proof technique, providing more details or a sketch of the proof within the main paper would significantly enhance understanding and impact. The current reliance on appendices feels somewhat limiting.
* **Non-Uniform Sampling Specifics:** The paper emphasizes the importance of non-uniform sampling but lacks sufficient detail regarding the specific probability distribution *π* used beyond the bilinear case.  The rationale for its effectiveness in the saddle-point setting, compared to convex minimization, is not fully articulated.
* **Step Size Selection:** The paper doesn’t detail how the step size σ was chosen in the experiments. A discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to step size and the rationale behind the selected values would be valuable.

**Specific Comments:**

* Line 78: Replace "k" with "d".
* Line 121: Correct the extra "y_{t-1}" term in the second equality.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant Theoretical Advance:**  The linear convergence results for saddle-point problems represent a major step forward in stochastic optimization.
* **Broad Applicability:** The monotone operator framework and the ability to handle non-separable problems make the work broadly applicable to various machine learning domains.
* **Positive Empirical Results:**  The experimental validation supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the algorithms.
* **Impactful Contribution:** The paper addresses a crucial limitation in existing optimization techniques and opens up new avenues for research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Clarity Concerns:** The limited proof details and lack of specificity regarding non-uniform sampling and step-size selection hinder full comprehension.
* **Potential for Further Elaboration:** Expanding on the details of the monotone operator framework and providing more illustrative examples would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor clarity issues and a desire for more detailed proofs, this paper represents a high-quality contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, the motivation is strong, and the experimental validation is encouraging. The work meaningfully extends the state-of-the-art in stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems. I recommend **acceptance** with the request that the authors address the points raised regarding clarity, proof details, and specifics of non-uniform sampling/step size selection in a revised version. Addressing these points would further enhance the impact and accessibility of this valuable research.
