

This paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated methods to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with two distinct split types (individual and factored). The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and uniform/non-uniform sampling strategies, demonstrating through experiments that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming batch algorithms. The extension is technically non-trivial, with clear theoretical connections to convex minimization intuition for bilinear saddle points, and the paper successfully extends the analysis to monotone operators, broadening applicability beyond saddle-point problems.

The paper excels in originality and significance. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing the first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for saddle-point problems in machine learning, particularly valuable for non-separable losses and regularizers. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

However, the paper has significant quality concerns regarding the computational bottleneck. Each incremental iteration incurs an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator, potentially making the overall complexity worse than accelerated batch forward-backward (FB) methods, especially when n >> d. The authors' claim that accelerated SVRG always outperforms batch FB appears questionable without addressing this bottleneck. The reviewer requested deeper discussion on three specific points: (i) theoretical comparison between factored and individual splits, (ii) precise conditions under which accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of the n,d dependence. While the authors' rebuttal was satisfactory, these concerns remain valid.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with excellent connections to previous work in convex optimization. The theoretical framework is sound, though the practical implications of the computational bottleneck require more careful treatment.

Pro: Novel extension to saddle-point problems, thorough complexity analysis, strong experimental validation for non-separable cases, broad applicability to monotone operators.
Con: Computational bottleneck not adequately addressed, theoretical advantage over batch methods questionable without resolving the proximal operator cost, insufficient discussion of split type comparisons.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but the unresolved computational bottleneck significantly impacts its practical significance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the computational concerns.
