This paper proposes stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems, extending previous work on SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors provide both theoretical and empirical evidence of the effectiveness of these methods, which handle non-separable saddle-point problems where the loss and regularizer can be non-separable.

The paper's contributions are significant, as it provides the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems, which are common in machine learning. The authors use a monotone-operator framework to prove convergence, which is more complex than standard convex analyses. The analysis indicates applicability to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

The paper also proposes two gradient-operator splits: element-wise split and factored split for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The authors apply variance reduction to stochastic forward-backward algorithms common in saddle-point problems and consider non-uniform sampling, which theory and experiments show outperforms uniform sampling.

The experimental results support the theoretical claims and show practical effectiveness. The paper's strengths include its novelty, technical soundness, and clarity. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithms and their convergence properties, making the paper a valuable contribution to the field.

However, there are some minor concerns. In the acceleration section, it is unclear how to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) to achieve speed-up. Additionally, it is unclear whether the convergence and acceleration proof given for SVRG also holds for SAGA. Furthermore, there is a notational issue with the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K, which should use distinct symbols to avoid confusion.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. The paper's quality, clarity, and originality make it a strong candidate for acceptance.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes novel stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems, extending previous work on SVRG and SAGA.
* The authors provide both theoretical and empirical evidence of the effectiveness of these methods.
* The paper's analysis indicates applicability to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.
* The experimental results support the theoretical claims and show practical effectiveness.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper's notation could be improved, particularly with regards to the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K.
* The acceleration section could be clarified, particularly with regards to adaptively updating the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ).
* It is unclear whether the convergence and acceleration proof given for SVRG also holds for SAGA.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
