This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods – SVRG and SAGA – to address convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these algorithms, demonstrating linear convergence under appropriate conditions, and explore the impact of different splitting strategies and sampling schemes. They also propose an acceleration technique based on the “catalyst” framework. The work is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, extending well-established optimization techniques to a broader class of problems.

The main contribution lies in providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems, moving beyond the standard convex minimization setup. This analysis leverages the concept of monotone operators, broadening the applicability of the algorithms to variational inequalities. The discussion of the two types of splits – in terms of functions or partial derivatives – and the emphasis on non-uniform sampling are insightful. The extension of the catalyst framework for acceleration is also a valuable addition. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a comprehensive review of related work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, alongside a rigorous convergence analysis, is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The use of monotone operator theory provides a solid foundation for the analysis, extending the applicability of the algorithms beyond saddle-point problems.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Saddle-point problems are prevalent in machine learning, making the developed techniques potentially valuable for various applications.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper thoroughly examines the impact of different splitting strategies and sampling methods.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proximal Operator Cost:** The O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator in each iteration represents a significant bottleneck. This potentially negates the advantages of incremental methods, particularly when compared to accelerated batch forward-backward (FB) algorithms. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully explore its implications.
*   **Split Comparison:** The theoretical comparison between “factored” and “individual” splits, especially regarding their respective complexities, remains somewhat unclear. The authors mention that factored splits can be worse when *n* >> *d*, but a deeper discussion of when and why this occurs would be beneficial.
*   **Acceleration Advantage:** The conditions under which accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB are not explicitly articulated. While the experiments suggest an advantage, a more precise characterization would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity Tightness:** The tightness of the derived complexity bounds (specifically the dependence on *n* and *d*) is not rigorously justified. Establishing this would further solidify the theoretical contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant extension of existing optimization techniques to a relevant class of problems.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The clarity of the writing and organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The proximal operator cost poses a significant practical limitation, potentially diminishing the benefits of the incremental approach.
*   The lack of a detailed comparison of splitting strategies and a precise characterization of the acceleration advantage weaken the paper.
*   The absence of a discussion regarding the tightness of the complexity bounds leaves some theoretical questions unanswered.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions. While the proximal operator cost and the limited discussion of split comparison and acceleration are concerns, they do not fundamentally detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in optimization for saddle-point problems and is worthy of acceptance.

*   Quality: 8/10
*   Clarity: 9/10
*   Originality: 8/10
*   Significance: 7/10

**Recommendation:** Accept.
