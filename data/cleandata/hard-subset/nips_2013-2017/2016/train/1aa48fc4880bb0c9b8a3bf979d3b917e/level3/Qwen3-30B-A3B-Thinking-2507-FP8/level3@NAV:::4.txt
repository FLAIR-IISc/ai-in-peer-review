

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with proven linear convergence. The authors address an important gap in the literature, as prior variance-reduction methods were designed for convex minimization but not for saddle-point problems. Their work builds upon recent advances in stochastic optimization while introducing novel theoretical and algorithmic insights.

The paper's main strengths include a thorough convergence analysis using the monotone-operator framework, which is more sophisticated than standard convex analyses but enables broader applicability to variational inequalities in game theory. The authors propose two novel gradient-operator splits (element-wise and factored) that handle non-separable saddle-point problems where loss and regularizer can be non-separable. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by comprehensive empirical evidence demonstrating that non-uniform sampling significantly outperforms uniform sampling both theoretically and in practice.

The paper excels in its technical quality, with careful analysis of the saddle-point structure and clear presentation of algorithms. The experimental results (both in main text and appendix) strongly support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical effectiveness across multiple machine learning applications, including non-separable regularizers and losses.

Minor concerns include: (1) the unclear adaptive update mechanism for the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in the acceleration section (Section 5), and (2) the question of whether the convergence and acceleration proofs for SVRG also hold for SAGA. Additionally, the notational confusion between the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K should be resolved.

The paper demonstrates high originality by extending variance-reduction techniques to saddle-point problems and showing their applicability beyond the standard convex minimization setting. This work is significant as it enables efficient large-scale solution of non-separable saddle-point problems that arise frequently in machine learning applications, including robust optimization, structured prediction, and matrix factorization.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution that advances the state of the art in stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems. The paper meets all quality standards with a clear theoretical foundation, compelling empirical results, and meaningful practical implications. The minor concerns are easily addressable and do not undermine the paper's substantial contributions.
