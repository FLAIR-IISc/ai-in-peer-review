- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems and provides the first large-scale algorithms with provable linear convergence.  
- Algorithms are straightforward adaptations; main novelty is the convergence analysis rather than algorithmic changes.  
- Convergence proof uses monotone operator theory, a more general framework that also covers variational inequalities.  
- Practical contributions include different objective splits (e.g., factored splits), emphasis on non-uniform sampling, and a catalyst-based acceleration scheme.  
- Significance: fills an important gap for ML problems with saddle-point structure, non-separable losses, and complex regularizers.  
- Originality/technical quality: monotone-operator-based analysis is nontrivial, elegant, and unifies several problem classes.  
- Practical relevance: experiments show SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling and acceleration outperform batch and stochastic baselines on non-separable problems.  
- Clarity/organization: paper is well-written, motivated, and includes an effective complexity comparison table.  
- Weakness: main analytical contribution is relegated to the appendix; the main text lacks a proof sketch or high-level intuition.  
- Weakness: insufficient guidance on defining non-uniform sampling probabilities πi for general smooth convex-concave K(x,y).  
- Weakness: unclear whether theoretical step-sizes (involving L and L̄) were used in experiments or required tuning; needs clarification for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: lacks intuition why non-uniform sampling is more critical in saddle-point settings—possible role of primal-dual coupling should be explained.  
- Question: possible typo in Forward-Backward algorithm presentation—prox expression should clearly indicate applied gradients to (x_{t-1}, y_{t-1}).  
- Question: apparent mismatch in component-wise gradient definitions for bilinear individual splits (B_jk expression) — need clarification/rectification.
