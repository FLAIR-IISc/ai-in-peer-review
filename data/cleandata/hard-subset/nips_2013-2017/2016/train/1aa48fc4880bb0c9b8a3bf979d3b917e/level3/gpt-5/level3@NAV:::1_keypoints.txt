- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point and general monotone operator problems, proving linear convergence with Catalyst-like acceleration and non-uniform sampling.  
- Operator-based analysis is novel and enables handling non-separable losses/regularizers and general variational inequalities beyond saddle points.  
- Strong technical contribution: linear rates for SVRG/SAGA in a strongly monotone operator setting, non-uniform sampling analysis, and an acceleration strategy with improved complexities.  
- Assumptions/notation unclear: (A)–(C) need explicit statement whether they imply monotonicity of B and strong monotonicity of A.  
- Theorem 2 ambiguity: μ is not clearly defined—confirm it is the strong-monotonicity parameter and express rates relative to L/μ for comparison to Theorem 1.  
- Clarify in the appendix how (A)–(C) transition to strong monotonicity and make that mapping explicit in the main text.  
- Presentation ordering issue: monotone-operator framing appears late—move it earlier and sharpen the mapping between saddle-point and operator assumptions.  
- Notation inconsistency: L is used both as a Lipschitz constant and a “condition number”; recommend using κ = L/μ (or appropriate normalization) and reserving L for Lipschitz.  
- Missing standard references: add Forward–Backward splitting reference (e.g., Bauschke & Combettes, 2011).  
- Experiments limited: only two datasets and no comparisons to accelerated stochastic primal–dual methods (e.g., SPDC, accelerated stochastic Chambolle–Pock/PDHG variants).  
- Empirical suggestion: add Zhang & Xiao (2015, JMLR) and benchmark against SPDC and accelerated stochastic PD methods.  
- Originality: high — first variance-reduction analysis for non-separable saddle points and monotone operators with linear rates.  
- Significance: potentially high impact for large-scale non-separable problems; acceleration and non-uniform sampling are practical contributions.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel linear-convergence analysis, applicability to non-separable functions/variational inequalities, and effective acceleration/non-uniform sampling.  
- Cons for acceptance: unclear presentation/notation and constants, limited experimental evaluation and missing baselines, and unclear applicability to common settings (e.g., SVM hinge loss).
