This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning by extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods to convex-concave saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The authors provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems and demonstrate their effectiveness through numerical experiments.

The paper starts by introducing the convex-concave saddle-point problem and its relevance to machine learning. The authors then present their main contribution, which is the extension of stochastic variance reduction methods (such as SVRG or SAGA) to this setting. They provide a convergence analysis for these algorithms and show that they can be accelerated via an extension of the Catalyst framework.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to tackle non-separable losses or regularizers, which is a common challenge in machine learning. The authors demonstrate that their methods can be applied to a wide range of problems, including supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers, robust optimization, and convex relaxation of unsupervised learning.

The paper also extends the approach to monotone operators, which is a broader class of problems that includes saddle-point problems as a special case. This extension is significant because it shows that the authors' methods can be applied to a wider range of problems beyond just saddle-point problems.

The numerical experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The authors show that their methods can improve significantly over existing batch or stochastic algorithms, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

However, one criticism of the paper is that it assumes strong convexity on both the primal and dual problems, which is an overly strong assumption. In practice, it is often the case that only one of the problems is strongly convex. Therefore, it would be desirable to relax this assumption and require strong convexity on only the primal or only the dual problem.

Another recommendation for the paper is to include more numerical experiments. While the authors provide some numerical results, it would be beneficial to include more experiments to demonstrate the robustness of their methods across different problem settings.

In terms of the quality of the paper, it is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and their approach, and the technical details are presented in a clear and easy-to-follow manner.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication. It presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods through numerical experiments.
