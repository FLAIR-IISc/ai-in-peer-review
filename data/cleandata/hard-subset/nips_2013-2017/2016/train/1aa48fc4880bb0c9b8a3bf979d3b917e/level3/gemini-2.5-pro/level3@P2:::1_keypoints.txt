- Proposes first large-scale, linearly convergent SVRG/SAGA-style stochastic methods for convex-concave saddle-point problems common in ML.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, generalizing results to variational inequalities.  
- Presents non-uniform sampling analysis showing practical and theoretical advantages over uniform sampling.  
- Introduces a simple "catalyst"-style acceleration that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Original and significant: fills a gap by extending variance reduction to saddle-point settings where primal/dual approaches fail.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: novel use of monotone operators rather than adapting convex minimization proofs.  
- Practical value: implementation-friendly acceleration and sampling insights with empirical gains.  
- Major clarity/organization issue: monotone operator framework is introduced too late (Section 6), making earlier sections and proofs hard to follow.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to only two datasets and needs broader, more diverse benchmarks.  
- Missing discussion/comparison with related stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., accelerated stochastic Chambolle–Pock variants).  
- Assumptions (A)-(C) lack detailed discussion and examples; unclear which ML problems satisfy them (e.g., SVMs).  
- Presentation lacks clarity on differences from standard stochastic primal-dual algorithms and on definition of constants (e.g., µ in Theorem 2).  
- Need explicit link showing (A)-(C) imply the strong monotonicity used in the appendix and clearer rate comparisons between Theorems 1 and 2.  
- Minor issues: reuse of symbol L for different meanings may confuse readers; cite specific linear convergence result for batch Forward–Backward.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel paper with excellent theory and useful practical contributions, but needs reorganization and expanded experiments; recommend acceptance with revisions.
