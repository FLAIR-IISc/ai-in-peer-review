## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper presents a significant contribution to the optimization of saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, by extending stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods like SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The authors demonstrate the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for these problems, addressing a key challenge in many modern machine learning applications where separability assumptions don’t hold. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates a strong understanding of both the theoretical foundations and practical implications of their approach.

The core idea is to adapt existing SVR techniques to the saddle-point formulation (min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y)). While the algorithmic extension is conceptually straightforward, the authors meticulously address the nuances that arise compared to standard convex minimization. They highlight that the convex minimization analysis doesn’t directly apply and instead leverage the framework of monotone operators to prove convergence, thereby extending the applicability of their methods to a broader class of problems like variational inequalities.  This broadened scope represents a valuable theoretical contribution.  The paper clearly articulates the subtleties of splitting the gradient operator (in terms of functions vs. partial derivatives) and emphasizes the necessity of convex-concave splits for effective performance.

A key strength of this work is the insightful analysis of sampling strategies. The authors demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficient performance. This practical guidance is particularly valuable to researchers implementing these algorithms.  Furthermore, the integration of the "catalyst" framework provides a notable acceleration, consistently outperforming accelerated batch algorithms. This acceleration technique, while briefly described, showcases the authors’ ability to build upon existing optimization techniques.

The paper’s connection to related work is comprehensive.  It correctly positions the work within the existing literature on stochastic gradient descent, SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated methods. It accurately points out the limitations of previous approaches when dealing with non-separable loss functions or regularizers commonly found in machine learning. The references to foundational work on monotone operator theory (Rockafellar, Zhu & Marcotte) are appropriate and demonstrate a solid grounding in the theoretical underpinnings of the approach. A minor notational issue exists with the use of ‘K’ for both a smooth function and a design matrix; differentiating these symbols would improve clarity.

The experimental validation is thorough, utilizing both synthetic and real-world datasets.  The results demonstrably support the theoretical claims, showcasing the effectiveness of their algorithms in practice. The comparison to existing batch and stochastic methods (including primal-dual approaches where applicable) provides a strong benchmark and clearly illustrates the advantages of the proposed techniques, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The inclusion of detailed experimental results in the appendix adds to the reproducibility and credibility of the findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of SVR techniques to saddle-point problems with provable linear convergence is a significant advancement.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The use of monotone operator theory provides a robust theoretical foundation and expands the applicability of the results.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The analysis of sampling strategies and the acceleration framework are directly applicable to real-world machine learning problems.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental results strongly support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical performance gains.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Adaptive Step-Sizing:** The discussion regarding adaptive updating of the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in the acceleration section (Section 5) is brief and could benefit from further elaboration. A clear strategy for adaptively adjusting this iterate is crucial for practical implementation.
*   **SAGA Acceleration Proof:** It remains unclear whether the acceleration proof presented for SVRG can be readily extended to SAGA. Clarification on this point would strengthen the overall argument.
*   **Notational Consistency:** The dual use of ‘K’ for a function and a matrix could lead to confusion.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   This paper addresses a challenging and important problem in optimization with significant implications for machine learning.
*   The technical contributions are substantial, including the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems.
*   The work is well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the contributions accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The minor weakness regarding adaptive step-sizing in the acceleration technique does slightly detract from the overall completeness of the work.
*   The question of whether the acceleration proof translates to SAGA needs clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.  The contributions are significant, the work is technically sound, and the presentation is clear. Addressing the minor concerns regarding adaptive step-sizing and the SAGA acceleration proof would further strengthen the paper, but they do not fundamentally undermine its value. This work represents a substantial advance in the field of optimization and will likely have a significant impact on the development of large-scale machine learning algorithms.
