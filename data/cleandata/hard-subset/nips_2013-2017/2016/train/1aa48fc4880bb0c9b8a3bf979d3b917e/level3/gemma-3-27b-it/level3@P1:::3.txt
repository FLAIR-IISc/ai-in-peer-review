This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods—specifically SVRG and SAGA—to address saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning arising from duality and various regularizations. The authors provide convergence analysis, highlighting the applicability to a broader class of problems like variational inequalities, and demonstrate the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration via a catalyst framework. The work is a natural and valuable extension of existing stochastic optimization techniques to a more general problem setting.

The paper begins with a clear motivation for studying saddle-point problems, providing concrete examples in supervised learning, robust optimization, and unsupervised learning. They correctly identify the limitations of existing stochastic methods tailored for separable objectives and propose adapting SVRG and SAGA.  The theoretical contributions are significant, extending convergence analysis beyond standard convex minimization by leveraging the notion of monotone operators. The discussion of the two different splitting notions (function-level and partial derivative-level) is insightful and correctly points out the necessity of convex-concave splits.  The analysis of non-uniform sampling, both theoretically and empirically, is crucial for understanding efficient implementation.  Furthermore, the extension of the catalyst framework for acceleration is a valuable addition.

A key strength of the paper lies in its thoroughness. The authors rigorously analyze the algorithms, providing detailed complexity bounds under various assumptions. The exploration of different sampling strategies and splitting techniques, coupled with their performance implications, demonstrates a deep understanding of the subject. The experimental validation on synthetic datasets further reinforces the theoretical findings, showcasing the effectiveness of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.  The connections made to monotone operator theory provide broader applicability and a more general theoretical foundation. The analysis of the cost of the proximal operator in terms of n and d is also a very important point.

However, a major weakness, and the primary bottleneck, is the O(n + d) cost per iteration for the proximal operator. While the authors acknowledge this, its implications for overall complexity aren’t fully discussed. The paper’s claim that accelerated SVRG *always* outperforms accelerated batch algorithms feels unsubstantiated given this cost. The analysis suggests that, for many practical scenarios (especially when *n* is much larger than *d*), the advantages of SVRG might be limited, or even outweighed by the overhead of the proximal operator calculations.  The potential drawbacks of factored splits, particularly their potentially higher complexity compared to individual splits when *n* is significantly larger than *d*, should be more thoroughly investigated. The paper doesn’t provide a clear delineation of when accelerated SVRG truly outperforms accelerated batch Forward-Backward (FB). It also doesn't fully discuss whether the *n* and *d* dependencies are tight—could further optimizations reduce these costs?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems.
*   Thorough theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Practical relevance demonstrated through experiments.
*   Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability.
*   Clear exposition and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The O(n + d) proximal operator cost is a significant bottleneck that may negate the benefits of stochastic variance reduction in many realistic scenarios.
*   Insufficient discussion of conditions under which accelerated SVRG demonstrably outperforms batch FB.
*   Lack of thorough investigation into the theoretical properties and practical implications of factored splits.
*   Limited discussion of the tightness of the complexity bounds related to *n* and *d*.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. While the proximal operator cost poses a practical challenge, the theoretical advancements and comprehensive analysis are commendable. The paper is well-written, and the experimental results are supportive of the claims. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors should address the concerns regarding the proximal operator cost and the comparison with batch algorithms in the camera-ready version. A clearer articulation of the circumstances under which the proposed methods provide a tangible advantage would greatly strengthen the paper. The analysis of factored versus individual splits deserves further attention.
