This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically extensions of SVRG and SAGA, for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are common in machine learning, arising from duality or in applications like robust optimization and structured prediction. The core contribution is the development of the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems, particularly when the objective function is a sum of many components. The authors leverage the framework of monotone operators for their convergence analysis, which allows the results to generalize beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The paper also explores important practical and theoretical aspects, including different ways to split the saddle-point operator, the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and an acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework that provably outperforms accelerated batch methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem. While variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA have revolutionized stochastic optimization for convex minimization, their application to saddle-point problems was not established. This work fills an important gap by providing the first provably linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for this setting. This is particularly valuable for modern machine learning problems involving non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., total variation, AUC maximization), where traditional primal or dual methods are not applicable.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. Instead of adapting proofs from the convex minimization setting, the authors use the more general and powerful theory of monotone operators. This not only provides a solid foundation for the convergence proofs but also demonstrates that the proposed algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. This is a novel and insightful connection that elevates the paper's contribution beyond a simple algorithmic extension.

3.  **Practical Contributions:** The paper introduces several key insights that are valuable for practitioners. The analysis of non-uniform sampling, showing its superiority over uniform sampling both in theory (Table 1) and practice (Figure 1), is a strong point. Furthermore, the proposed acceleration scheme is simple to implement and offers significant performance gains, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The fact that this "catalyst"-style acceleration leads to an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods is a compelling result.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's presentation could be substantially improved. The reliance on monotone operator theory is central to the analysis, yet this concept is only formally introduced in Section 6 as an "extension." This makes the preceding sections and the appendix proofs difficult to follow, as the reader is unaware of the underlying mathematical framework. Introducing the monotone operator perspective early on would provide crucial context and significantly improve the paper's readability and logical flow.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, the evaluation is based on only two datasets. To make a more compelling case for the practical utility of the algorithms, it would be beneficial to include experiments on a wider variety of datasets and problem settings, perhaps including more diverse examples of the non-separable regularizers and losses mentioned in Section 2.1.

3.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper should include a discussion and comparison with other related stochastic methods for saddle-point problems. For instance, accelerated stochastic versions of primal-dual methods like the Chambolle-Pock algorithm have been proposed (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, "Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization," JMLR 2017, which builds on their ICML 2014 work). A comparison, even if theoretical, would help situate the present work within the broader literature.

4.  **Clarity of Assumptions and Details:**
    *   The assumptions (A)-(C) should be discussed in more detail. Specifically for assumption (A), it would be helpful to clarify for which common machine learning problems it holds. For example, does the standard saddle-point formulation of a support vector machine satisfy this strong convexity-concavity assumption on M?
    *   The paper should more clearly delineate the differences between the proposed methods and standard stochastic primal-dual algorithms.
    *   In the statement of Theorem 2, the constant `µ` is not defined in the main text. I presume this refers to the strong monotonicity constant from the appendix, but this should be clarified. A more direct comparison of the convergence rates in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 would also be useful.
    *   The link between assumptions (A)-(C) and the strong monotonicity property used in the appendix should be made explicit. Do (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity of the overall operator?

### Minor Issues

*   The symbol `L` appears to be used for both the Lipschitz constant of the gradient operator and as a measure of the problem's condition number. While related, using distinct notation would prevent potential confusion.
*   A specific citation for the linear convergence of the batch Forward-Backward algorithm (mentioned in Section 2.2) would be appropriate.

### Summary of Arguments

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   First work to provide linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems.
*   Addresses an important problem class with many applications in machine learning.
*   Strong and general theoretical analysis based on monotone operators.
*   Includes significant practical contributions like non-uniform sampling and an effective acceleration scheme.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The presentation and organization are confusing, particularly the delayed introduction of the monotone operator framework.
*   Experimental validation is limited.
*   Lacks comparison to some relevant stochastic primal-dual methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant novel contributions. The theoretical analysis is excellent, and the proposed algorithms are of high practical relevance. The primary weaknesses lie in the presentation and the scope of the experimental section. I believe these are addressable in a revision. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to improve the clarity of the paper by restructuring the narrative around the monotone operator framework.
