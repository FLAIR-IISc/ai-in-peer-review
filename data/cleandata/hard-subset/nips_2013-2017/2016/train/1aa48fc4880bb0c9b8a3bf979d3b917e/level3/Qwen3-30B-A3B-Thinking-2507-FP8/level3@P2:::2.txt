

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG/SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class. The authors establish convergence via monotone operator theory (rather than convex minimization analysis), demonstrate two natural splits (function-based and derivative-based), prove non-uniform sampling is essential, and show acceleration via Catalyst framework. The work builds on SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014) but addresses a distinct problem class where separability arises in the saddle-point formulation—not in the primal/dual minimization.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality & Significance:** The extension to saddle-point problems is novel and addresses a critical gap. Many ML problems (robust optimization, non-separable losses, AUC maximization) naturally form saddle-points where prior variance-reduction methods fail. The monotone operator generalization (Section 6) significantly broadens applicability beyond saddle-points to variational inequalities.  
- **Quality:** Convergence proofs (Theorems 1–3) are technically sound, using monotone operator theory to avoid pitfalls of convex minimization analysis. The Catalyst acceleration extension is elegant and theoretically justified.  
- **Clarity:** Well-organized with clear definitions (e.g., (λ,γ)-convex-concavity). The bilinear examples and complexity analysis in Table 1 are particularly accessible.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Overly Strong Convexity Assumption:** The paper assumes strong convexity in *both* primal (λ > 0) *and* dual (γ > 0) variables (Assumption A). This is unnecessarily restrictive—standard saddle-point theory (e.g., Rockafellar, 1976) requires only one side to be strongly convex (e.g., λ > 0 suffices for linear convergence). This limitation weakens practical impact, as many problems (e.g., robust optimization) may lack dual strong concavity.  
- **Insufficient Experiments:** The numerical results (Section 7) are limited to two datasets and lack comparisons to specialized saddle-point solvers (e.g., Chambolle-Pock). The "non-uniform sampling" claim needs stronger empirical validation (e.g., comparing to uniform sampling on more diverse problems). The "accelerated SVRG" advantage in ill-conditioned problems (middle plot) is noted but not deeply analyzed.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**  
*Pro:* First linearly convergent variance-reduction method for saddle-points with rigorous convergence guarantees; extends to monotone operators; solves practical ML problems (non-separable losses, AUC maximization) where prior methods fail.  
*Con:* Strong convexity assumption on both sides is theoretically unjustified and practically limiting; experiments insufficient to fully validate claims (e.g., no comparison to state-of-the-art saddle-point solvers).  

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept** (with major revisions). The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution with clear significance for ML, but the strong convexity assumption must be relaxed or justified. The authors should: (1) revise Assumption A to allow γ = 0 (dual strong concavity only) and prove convergence under this weaker condition; (2) add experiments comparing to Chambolle-Pock and primal-dual methods on non-separable problems (e.g., AUC maximization); (3) clarify the practical necessity of non-uniform sampling via additional benchmarks.  

The paper aligns with NIPS scope (optimization for ML) and addresses a meaningful gap. While the current version has a critical theoretical limitation, the core idea is strong and the revisions are feasible. The authors’ extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly compelling and demonstrates broader relevance beyond saddle-points. With the suggested revisions, this would be a significant contribution to the literature.
