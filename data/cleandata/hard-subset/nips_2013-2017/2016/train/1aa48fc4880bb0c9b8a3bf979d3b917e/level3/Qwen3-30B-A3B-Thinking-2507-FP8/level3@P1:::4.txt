

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this class. The work addresses a critical gap: prior stochastic methods like SAGA [3] and SVRG [2] were limited to separable convex minimization, while saddle-point problems (e.g., robust optimization, non-separable regularizers) are ubiquitous in ML but lacked efficient scalable solvers. The authors generalize the convex minimization framework by using monotone operator theory (Section 6), which avoids relying on convexity properties and enables broader applicability to variational inequalities. This distinguishes their analysis from standard convex optimization approaches [8, 28] and establishes a unified theoretical foundation.

The paper’s strengths are substantial. **Originality** is clear: extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems with linear convergence is novel, as prior work [1, 2, 3] did not consider non-separable saddle-point structures. The authors rigorously prove convergence using monotone operators—a non-trivial extension beyond convex minimization—and demonstrate that the same algorithm applies to variational inequalities [5, 6]. **Significance** is high: the method solves practical ML challenges (e.g., non-separable losses like AUC surrogates [13, 14] and grouped regularizers [15]), where existing approaches fail. Experiments (Section 7) confirm that non-uniform sampling (e.g., for matrix K) outperforms uniform sampling and batch methods, with accelerated SVRG achieving superior runtime complexity (Table 1). **Quality** is strong: theorems (Theorems 1–3) are technically sound, with careful handling of two split types (element-wise vs. factored) and explicit complexity bounds. The empirical results align with theory, and the catalyst acceleration framework [7] is elegantly adapted to saddle-point problems.

Minor weaknesses exist but are addressable. The adaptive update for the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) in acceleration (Section 5) lacks detail—how to dynamically set τ without knowing L̄ or T(B) remains unclear. While the authors note this as future work, a brief discussion of adaptive strategies (e.g., line search) would strengthen the practicality. Additionally, the convergence proof for SAGA (Theorem 2) is not explicitly extended from SVRG, though the paper states it should generalize. This is a minor gap in the exposition but not a flaw in the core contribution. Notationally, using K for both the smooth function and design matrix (e.g., in Section 2.1) risks confusion; a distinct symbol (e.g., Φ) would improve clarity.

The paper excels in **clarity** overall, with well-structured sections and clear examples (Section 2.1). The connection to real ML problems—e.g., supervised learning with non-separable losses (sido, rcv1 datasets)—makes the significance tangible. The theoretical analysis (Section 6) is particularly valuable, showing the method’s applicability beyond saddle-point problems, which elevates its impact. The experiments are thorough, comparing non-uniform vs. uniform sampling, batch methods, and primal-dual approaches, with compelling results (Figures 1–2).

This work significantly advances the state of the art by enabling scalable optimization for non-separable saddle-point problems—a common yet previously underserved class in ML. It builds on NIPS trends [1, 2, 3] but extends them to a broader, practically critical setting. The theoretical and empirical evidence is robust, and the minor weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a meaningful gap in large-scale optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
