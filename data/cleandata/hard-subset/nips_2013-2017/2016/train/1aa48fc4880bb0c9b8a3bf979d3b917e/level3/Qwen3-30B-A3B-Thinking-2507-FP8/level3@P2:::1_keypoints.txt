- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for convergence analysis instead of convex minimization, offering a unifying theoretical framework.  
- Demonstrates importance of non-uniform sampling for algorithmic efficiency.  
- Extends the catalyst acceleration framework to the saddle-point setting.  
- Shows applicability to non-separable machine learning problems (non-separable losses and regularizers).  
- Theoretical contribution is substantial and addresses a clear gap, extending to variational inequalities.  
- Identifies a key distinction in splitting structure required for saddle-point vs. convex minimization problems.  
- Experiments show clear improvements, especially on ill-conditioned problems where accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms batch methods.  
- Presentation is weak: monotone operator connection appears late and should be introduced earlier to frame the paper.  
- Experimental validation is limited to two datasets; needs more datasets and broader comparisons (e.g., Zhang & Xiao 2015).  
- Assumptions A–C lack clear explanation and need explicit discussion of applicability to standard ML problems (e.g., SVM-induced saddle-points).  
- The constant μ in Theorem 2 is not well-defined and the move to strong monotonicity is confusing.  
- Inconsistent notation: L is used ambiguously (condition number vs. Lipschitz constant); missing reference for Forward-Backward algorithm.  
- Theoretical analysis appears sound, but presentation of assumptions and monotone-operator connection requires significant improvement.  
- Paper suffers from poor organization and unclear definitions of technical terms.  
- The SVRG/SAGA extension to separable saddle-point problems is novel and significant.  
- Work is significant for large-scale ML applications with non-separable components due to improved convergence rates.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first linear convergence guarantees, novel monotone-operator analysis, demonstrated theoretical and practical advantages, and ML applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor organization, limited experiments, missing relevant comparisons, and unclear technical definitions.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions — core idea is sound and important but needs substantial improvements in clarity and empirical validation.
