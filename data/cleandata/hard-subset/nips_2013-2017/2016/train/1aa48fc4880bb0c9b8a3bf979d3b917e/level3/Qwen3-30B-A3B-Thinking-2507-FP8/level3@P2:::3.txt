

The paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. Previous work on SVRG/SAGA (Le Roux et al., Johnson & Zhang, Defazio et al.) focused exclusively on convex minimization, while saddle-point problems require fundamentally different analysis due to the interplay between minimization and maximization. The authors cleverly use monotone operator theory to establish convergence, which is a key technical contribution that generalizes beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. This represents a significant theoretical advancement that properly extends the existing SVRG/SAGA framework to a new domain.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it identifies two natural split types for saddle-point problems (in terms of functions or partial derivatives), which differ from convex minimization. Second, it rigorously demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is critical for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically. Third, it extends the catalyst framework to accelerate these algorithms, showing that the resulting method is always superior to accelerated batch algorithms. The complexity analysis is thorough, with clear comparisons across different split types, sampling strategies, and problem structures.

The experimental results on binary classification with non-separable losses and regularizers support the theoretical claims, showing that non-uniform sampling and acceleration significantly outperform batch methods and uniform sampling variants. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the complexity dependencies on problem parameters, which is valuable for practitioners.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a good balance between technical detail and accessibility. The key ideas are well-motivated, and the connection to previous work is appropriately discussed.

However, the paper has a significant weakness that needs addressing: each incremental iteration incurs an O(n+d) cost to compute the proximal operator. This creates a fundamental bottleneck that may undermine the claimed advantages of the proposed methods. For example, when n >> d (which is common in machine learning), the overall complexity of the proposed methods may not improve over accelerated batch forward-backward algorithms, despite the theoretical claims. The paper doesn't sufficiently address when the proposed methods are actually better than batch methods, which is critical for assessing their practical utility.

Additionally, the analysis of factored splits (which the authors claim are more efficient) is incomplete. The paper suggests factored splits can be more efficient when n and d are large, but the theoretical analysis doesn't clearly show this advantage when n >> d. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between factored and individual splits, especially in terms of the n,d dependence.

The paper also doesn't adequately address whether the n,d dependence in the complexity bounds is tight. While the authors claim accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling provides significant improvements, the experimental results don't consistently show this advantage, especially when n is much larger than d.

Strengths: First extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with rigorous analysis; thorough complexity analysis covering different split types and sampling strategies; good experimental validation showing benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Weaknesses: Major bottleneck in proximal operator computation (O(n+d) per iteration) that may undermine practical advantages; insufficient discussion of when the proposed methods outperform batch algorithms; incomplete analysis of factored vs individual splits, especially for n >> d; lack of clear evidence that n,d dependence is tight.

Overall, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution to extending stochastic optimization methods to saddle-point problems. However, the practical significance is limited by the computational bottleneck and insufficient discussion of when the proposed methods are actually advantageous. With revisions addressing the concerns about the proximal operator bottleneck and clearer discussion of when the methods outperform batch algorithms, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (with major revisions)
