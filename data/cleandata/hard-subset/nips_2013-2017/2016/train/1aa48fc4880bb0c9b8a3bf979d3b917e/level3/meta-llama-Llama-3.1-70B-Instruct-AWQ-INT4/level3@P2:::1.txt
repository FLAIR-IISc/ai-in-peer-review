This paper proposes a novel extension of Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVRG) and Stochastic Average Gradient (SAGA) methods to convex-concave saddle-point problems, leveraging proximal operators to achieve linear convergence. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a monotone operator setting, and introduce acceleration techniques and non-uniform sampling strategies. The contributions of this work are significant, offering new insights into the applicability of SVRG and SAGA to non-separable functions.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, building upon the foundations of stochastic gradient descent and variance reduction techniques. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach in solving composite saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The connection to monotone operators, although introduced late in the paper, provides a crucial underpinning for the analysis.

The presentation of the paper could be improved. The authors may consider reorganizing the structure to make the connection to monotone operators more explicit from the outset. Additionally, the experimental section is limited, with only two datasets used for evaluation. Including more datasets and comparisons to other methods would strengthen the paper.

A comparison to accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock-type methods, such as those proposed by Zhang and Xiao (2015), is missing and would be a valuable addition. Furthermore, the authors should clarify the strength and applicability of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption A, and discuss whether these assumptions hold for SVM-induced saddle-point problems.

The differences between standard stochastic primal-dual methods and the authors' proposed method should be clarified. In Theorem 2, the constant μ is unclear, and the authors should confirm whether it is the monotonicity constant from the appendix and explain the comparison to Theorem 1. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is confusing, and the authors should state whether A-C imply monotonicity.

Minor issues include the inconsistent use of L (condition number vs. Lipschitz constant) and the missing reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful in evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but the presentation could be improved. The authors may consider reorganizing the structure to make the connection to monotone operators more explicit.

**Originality:** The paper proposes a novel extension of SVRG and SAGA methods to convex-concave saddle-point problems, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The results are important, as they provide a new approach to solving composite saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The paper has the potential to impact the field of machine learning and optimization.
