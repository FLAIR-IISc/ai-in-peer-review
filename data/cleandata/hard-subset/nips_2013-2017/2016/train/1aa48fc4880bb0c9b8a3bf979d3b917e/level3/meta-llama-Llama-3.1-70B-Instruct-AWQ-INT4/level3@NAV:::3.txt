The paper "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" presents a novel extension of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with two split types (individual and factored). The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and uniform/non-uniform sampling strategies. The experiments on a binary classification example show that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beat batch algorithms.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, related work, and their contributions. The technical content is sound, and the proofs are well-organized and easy to follow. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the results, including the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

The main strength of the paper is its novelty and technical non-triviality. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The authors also provide a clear comparison with bilinear saddle points, which aligns with convex minimization intuition.

However, there are some concerns regarding the computational complexity of the algorithm. Each incremental iteration incurs an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator, which creates a fundamental bottleneck. As a result, the overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch FB, and factored splits can be worse when n >> d.

To address these concerns, the authors are requested to provide a deeper discussion on the following points:

1. Whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits.
2. When accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB.
3. Whether the n, d dependence is tight.

In conclusion, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of optimization, and the authors have made a strong effort to provide a thorough analysis of their approach. However, addressing the concerns regarding computational complexity will strengthen the paper and provide a more complete understanding of the proposed method.
