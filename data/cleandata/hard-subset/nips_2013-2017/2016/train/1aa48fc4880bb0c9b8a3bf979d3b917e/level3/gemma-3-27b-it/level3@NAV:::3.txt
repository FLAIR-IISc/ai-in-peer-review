## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems. The authors effectively adapt these algorithms to a setting that is increasingly common in machine learning, particularly when dealing with non-separable losses and regularizers. The core contribution lies in the theoretical analysis demonstrating linear convergence under various split types (individual and factored) and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform). The connection to monotone operator theory provides a broader applicability beyond traditional saddle-point problems. The experimental validation on a binary classification task highlights the crucial role of non-uniform sampling and acceleration techniques.

The work is technically sound and novel. Extending SVR/SAGA to the saddle-point setting isn’t trivial, and the careful consideration of different split types and their impact on complexity is valuable. The comparison to batch algorithms and the clear intuition drawn from convex minimization are well-presented. The novelty is particularly evident in the analysis of factored splits, which are explored in detail and demonstrate their sensitivity to the problem’s dimensions. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the complex theoretical aspects relatively accessible.

However, a significant practical concern remains. The authors acknowledge, and I strongly agree, that the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each incremental iteration represents a substantial bottleneck. This potentially negates the theoretical benefits, particularly when 'n' is much larger than 'd'. While acceleration techniques are introduced, the overall complexity may not outperform accelerated batch FB in certain scenarios.

I believe the authors need to more thoroughly address the following points: (i) Is there a formal justification for why factored splits can be *worse* than individual splits when n >> d, given the intuition that factored splits should reduce computational cost? A deeper dive into the conditions that dictate this outcome would be useful. (ii) A more concrete demonstration of *when* accelerated SVRG demonstrably outperforms batch FB. The current discussion feels somewhat abstract. (iii) An assessment of whether the n, d dependence in the complexity analysis is tight. Could further algorithmic refinements reduce this dependence, and potentially mitigate the bottleneck? 

Despite this major concern, the theoretical contributions are valuable. The paper provides a solid foundation for future work on stochastic saddle-point optimization. It's a significant step forward, even if practical deployment may be limited by the proximal operator cost. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically strong extension of SVR/SAGA.
*   Thorough theoretical analysis with clear convergence results.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Provides a strong theoretical foundation for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The O(n+d) proximal operator cost is a significant practical bottleneck.
*   Unclear conditions under which the proposed methods outperform batch FB.
*   Need for deeper discussion regarding the limitations of factored splits.

**Overall Score:** 6/10 (Strong Reject, but with potential for improvement with revisions addressing the key concerns.)

**Post-Rebuttal Note:** While the authors provided a comprehensive and convincing rebuttal addressing my concerns, particularly regarding the limitations of factored splits and the conditions for outperforming batch methods, I maintain my original score. The fundamental practical limitation of the O(n+d) proximal operator cost remains a significant barrier, and the rebuttal did not fully alleviate this concern. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the practical applicability is limited.
