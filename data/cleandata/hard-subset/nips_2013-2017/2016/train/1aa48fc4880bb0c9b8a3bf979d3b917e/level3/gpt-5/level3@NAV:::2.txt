This paper studies large-scale convex–concave saddle-point problems decomposed into many components and extends modern variance-reduction techniques (SVRG/SAGA) to this setting. The authors provide linear convergence guarantees under strong convexity–concavity and show that their stochastic forward–backward variants can be accelerated via a Catalyst-style regularization scheme. A notable contribution is a unifying convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, which also extends the methods beyond saddle points to general variational inequalities. The paper discusses two decomposition schemes (function vs. partial-derivative/factored splits), non-uniform sampling, and storage–time tradeoffs, and includes numerical experiments comparing against batch and stochastic baselines. The work is well-situated relative to SVRG/SAGA (NIPS’13–’14), Catalyst (NIPS’15), and primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock; SPDC for separable problems), and argues novelty for nonseparable saddle problems and general monotone operators without co-coercivity.

Quality: The theory appears technically sound, with clear linear rates and complexity bounds that reflect sampling distributions and Lipschitz constants. The monotone-operator derivation is elegant and avoids co-coercivity. The acceleration analysis is simple and convincing. Empirically, the methods outperform batch and unaccelerated baselines on the provided tasks; however, the experimental section is relatively narrow in scope.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The distinctions between the two splitting strategies and the role of L̄(π) are useful. Suggestions: provide more concrete guidance for choosing sampling probabilities in practice, clarify storage overheads for SAGA under different splits, and summarize step-size choices and sensitivity.

Originality: Extending variance reduction to nonseparable saddle-point problems and to monotone operators (without co-coercivity) is novel and timely. The Catalyst adaptation in this setting is straightforward but impactful.

Significance: The results advance the algorithmic toolbox for large-scale min–max and variational inequality problems and should be useful for practitioners facing nonseparable losses/regularizers.

Main weakness: Requiring strong convexity on both primal and dual variables is overly restrictive. Many important applications are only strongly convex on one side (or after adding mild regularization). The authors should aim to relax to strong convexity on either the primal or the dual (potentially via proximal-point regularization, restarts, or error bounds), and discuss convergence and rates in that regime.

Further recommendations: broaden experiments (more datasets, varying conditioning, ablations on sampling, comparisons to Mirror-Prox/extragradient and accelerated PDHG), and report wall-clock with/without non-uniform sampling.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theory with linear rates; clean extension to monotone operators
- Practical acceleration via Catalyst; insightful sampling analysis
- Addresses an important gap beyond separable settings

Arguments against acceptance:
- Strong convexity on both sides limits applicability
- Experimental evaluation is limited in breadth and baselines
