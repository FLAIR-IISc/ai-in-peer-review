This paper extends finite-sum variance-reduction techniques (SVRG/SAGA) from strongly convex minimization to convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure and shows linear convergence. The authors (i) recast the analysis in the monotone operator framework rather than relying on co-coercivity, thereby covering saddle-point problems and, more generally, variational inequalities; (ii) highlight two natural “splits” of the smooth part K into components—function-based and derivative-based (“factored”)—and analyze how these choices affect storage and complexity; (iii) show that non-uniform sampling is important both theoretically (through tighter bounds in terms of L̄(π)) and empirically; and (iv) incorporate acceleration via a simple Catalyst-style regularization schedule, leading to improved iteration complexity and outperforming accelerated batch methods in their experiments.

Relative to prior work: For convex minimization, SAG/SVRG/SAGA [1–3,24] provide linear convergence under strong convexity. For saddle-point problems and variational inequalities, classical forward–backward/extragradient and primal–dual methods (e.g., Arrow–Hurwicz, Korpelevich/Nemirovski’s extragradient/Mirror-Prox, Chambolle–Pock [20]) yield O(1/t) without strong convexity and linear rates with strong convex–concave structure in the batch setting. Stochastic FB-type methods with noise [22] attain O(1/t) rates. Separable, coordinate-friendly primal–dual methods (SPDC; stochastic PD coordinate descent) [11,12] exploit separability. This paper’s contribution is to bring finite-sum variance reduction to general (non-separable) composite saddle-point problems and monotone inclusions, with new linear-rate analyses that do not assume co-coercivity of individual components and that leverage non-uniform sampling and Catalyst.

Quality
- Theory: The convergence analysis is careful and, to my reading, correct. The move to monotone operators is technically clean and reveals why co-coercivity-based step sizes are insufficient for saddle-point problems. The role of the weighted norm Ω and the Lipschitz constant L (and sampling-dependent L̄(π)) is clearly articulated. The acceleration analysis via a proximal-point (Catalyst) outer loop is simple and compelling in the saddle-point context.
- Algorithms: The SVRG and SAGA variants are natural and implementable; the factored split reduces storage for SAGA from O(nd) to O(n + d), which is important.
- Complexity: The bounds show the expected improvements over batch FB and over uniform sampling; the dependence on ‖K‖F versus ‖K‖op and ‖K‖max is informative. The acceleration yields the usual L̄√(T(B) max T(Bi)) trade-off.
- Experiments: The numerical results illustrate the main claims: non-uniform sampling matters; SVRG-accel and SAGA outperform batch and stochastic FB; acceleration helps more on ill-conditioned problems. However, the empirical section is limited in scope (two datasets, a few settings).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The presentation of the two splits (individual vs factored) and the practical storage/time implications is clear and useful. The use of Ω/Ω* and scaling by (λ, γ) is motivated well.
- Suggestions: add a concise figure/table early that contrasts the storage/compute profiles of SVRG vs SAGA under both splits, and a small “recipe” for choosing sampling probabilities in practice, including the cost of computing (KKT) diagonals for non-uniform sampling.

Originality
- Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with a monotone-operator analysis (without co-coercivity) and supporting non-uniform sampling is novel and important. The Catalyst-style acceleration for this setting (with a proximal-point interpretation) is also a noteworthy contribution. The extension to general monotone inclusions broadens impact beyond saddle points.

Significance
- Many modern ML problems are naturally formulated as (non-separable) saddle-point problems—robust optimization, structured losses (AUC), and non-separable regularizers with efficient prox. Linearly convergent, variance-reduced methods for these settings can change practice, particularly when batch primal–dual methods are too slow and separability-based methods are inapplicable.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption strength: Requiring strong convexity-concavity (on both x and y via M) is restrictive. In many applications only one side is strongly convex (e.g., regularization only on x) or neither is. The authors should (i) discuss one-sided strong convexity (e.g., strong convexity in x or y only) and whether linear convergence could still be obtained via a Mirror-Prox/extragradient inner step plus variance reduction; (ii) clarify whether their Catalyst/proximal-point outer loop can compensate for missing strong convexity on one side (by adding and removing regularization) without degrading complexity too much; and (iii) relate to known results for one-sided strong convex–concave problems.
- Comparisons: Include stronger baselines such as accelerated Chambolle–Pock with optimal step sizes/backtracking, Mirror-Prox (deterministic and stochastic variants), and SPDC where applicable, to better position the gains. Reporting wall-clock times and sensitivity to step size would be helpful.
- Experimental scope: Add more datasets (dense/sparse, varying n/d/conditioning), more non-separable regularizers (e.g., overlapping group lasso, TV), and ablations on mini-batch size m, resampling vs. reusing stored components in SAGA, and the cost/benefit of non-uniform sampling when computing probabilities is nontrivial.
- Practicalities: Non-uniform sampling requires access to row/column norms or leverage scores; discuss how to approximate these cheaply and the robustness of performance to approximate probabilities. For SAGA with the individual split, the O(nd) storage is prohibitive; emphasize when the factored split is applicable and how to proceed otherwise.
- Parameter selection: Provide guidance for choosing m and for setting the number of inner iterations per epoch in SVRG, including heuristics that avoid computing L or L̄ explicitly.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- First linear-rate variance-reduced methods for general composite saddle-point problems (non-separable), with clean monotone-operator analysis.
- Meaningful acceleration via an adapted Catalyst framework; theory matches practice.
- Careful treatment of sampling (non-uniform) and splitting strategies with concrete complexity improvements.
- Extension beyond saddle points to monotone inclusions increases relevance.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Strong convexity on both primal and dual sides limits applicability; no theoretical results for one-sided strong convexity or purely convex–concave cases beyond adding regularization.
- Experimental section is relatively narrow and lacks some strong modern baselines and broader tasks.
- Some practical costs (storage for SAGA, computing non-uniform probabilities) are underexplored.

Overall assessment: A strong, well-executed theoretical contribution with practical promise. With a broadened empirical evaluation and a more thorough discussion (or partial results) for one-sided strong convexity, this would be an easy accept. As is, I recommend acceptance, with the above revisions to strengthen the paper.
