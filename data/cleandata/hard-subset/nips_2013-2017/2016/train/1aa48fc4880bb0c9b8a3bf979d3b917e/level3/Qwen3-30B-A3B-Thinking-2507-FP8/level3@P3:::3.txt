

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" (NIPS 2016)

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst) to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors extend recent incremental gradient techniques from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The work builds on seminal papers by Johnson & Zhang (SVRG) and Defazio et al. (SAGA), while introducing novel theoretical insights for saddle-point problems.

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) providing the first convergence analysis for saddle-point problems using monotone operators (rather than convex minimization techniques), (2) introducing two distinct split types (individual and factored) with different theoretical and practical implications, and (3) demonstrating how the Catalyst framework can be extended to accelerate these methods. The authors show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically, and provide complexity bounds that improve upon batch methods.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with thorough theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies is particularly impressive, with the authors carefully characterizing the dependence on problem dimensions (n, d) and problem structure. The experiments on a binary classification problem with non-separable regularizers and losses confirm that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform batch algorithms, with accelerated SVRG showing significant gains for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper's originality is strong, as it addresses a non-trivial extension of existing convex optimization techniques to saddle-point problems. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior literature, particularly in Section 6 where they extend their analysis to monotone operators, demonstrating broader applicability beyond saddle-point problems. The connection to variational inequalities and the careful treatment of two split types (individual vs. factored) represent meaningful technical advances.

The significance of this work is high for machine learning practitioners dealing with problems that naturally formulate as saddle-point problems but do not admit separable structures in their primal or dual forms. The authors provide compelling evidence that their methods can handle non-separable losses and regularizers where existing approaches fail.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The exposition of the monotone operator framework in Section 6 is particularly elegant and accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization.

The major concern raised by the reviewer pertains to the computational cost of the proximal operator, which incurs an O(n+d) cost per incremental iteration. This creates a fundamental bottleneck that potentially negates the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods. The authors' response to this concern was satisfactory, clarifying that factored splits are not theoretically worse than individual splits but rather have different computational trade-offs. They also provided clear conditions under which accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB (when L̄√(max_i T(B_i)/T(B)) > 1), and confirmed that their n,d dependence is tight in the bilinear case.

The paper's main weakness is the practical computational bottleneck mentioned above, which requires careful consideration in real-world applications. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide clear theoretical conditions under which their methods are advantageous.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems with rigorous theoretical guarantees. The technical depth, careful analysis, and practical relevance make it a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. The authors' response to reviewer concerns was thorough and convincing.

Strengths: Novel extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems, thorough complexity analysis, clear experimental validation, elegant monotone operator framework.

Weaknesses: The O(n+d) cost per proximal operator computation creates a practical bottleneck that may limit applicability in some settings.

This paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is of high technical quality, clearly written, original in its approach to saddle-point problems, and significant for machine learning practitioners dealing with non-separable losses and regularizers. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the state of the art for stochastic optimization of saddle-point problems.
