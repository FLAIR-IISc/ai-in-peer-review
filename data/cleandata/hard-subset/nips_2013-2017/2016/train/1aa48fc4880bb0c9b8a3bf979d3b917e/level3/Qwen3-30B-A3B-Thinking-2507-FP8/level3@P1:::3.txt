

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) and the Catalyst acceleration framework to convex-concave saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. While the algorithmic extensions are straightforward, the authors provide a novel convergence analysis using monotone operators rather than convex minimization techniques, demonstrating that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems including variational inequalities. The work identifies two distinct split types for saddle-point problems (function-based vs. partial derivative-based), shows that splits must be convex-concave rather than convex, and establishes non-uniform sampling as critical for efficiency. The authors also show how to accelerate these incremental methods using Catalyst, leading to algorithms superior to accelerated batch methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for variance reduction methods in saddle-point problems, using monotone operator theory rather than the convex minimization framework. This analysis reveals that the algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, broadening their applicability. Second, the paper thoroughly analyzes the complexity under different split types (individual vs. factored) and sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform), showing that non-uniform sampling significantly improves theoretical bounds. Third, the experimental results on a binary classification problem with non-separable losses and regularizers demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming existing methods, with accelerated SVRG showing the strongest gains on ill-conditioned problems.

The paper excels in technical quality, providing rigorous proofs for all claims and carefully addressing the challenges of extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections that carefully explain the theoretical contributions and experimental setup. The originality is strong, as the extension to saddle-point problems represents a significant departure from existing work on variance reduction, which has focused almost exclusively on convex minimization. The significance is clear: saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., in robust optimization, convex relaxations of unsupervised learning, and structured prediction), and the proposed methods provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for these problems.

However, the paper has a critical weakness that needs addressing: the cost of computing the proximal operator, which is O(n + d) per iteration. This creates a fundamental bottleneck that may negate the theoretical advantages of the proposed methods. For instance, even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling may not improve over accelerated batch forward-backward methods when n >> d, and factored splits can be worse than individual splits in such regimes. The paper does not sufficiently address when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch methods, nor does it provide tight bounds on the n, d dependence. While the authors acknowledge the importance of the proximal operator cost, they do not adequately discuss its implications for practical scalability.

The paper also lacks a thorough comparison of factored vs. individual splits in terms of theoretical complexity. The authors note that factored splits require storing O(n + d) elements versus O(nd) for individual splits, but don't analyze whether factored splits lead to worse theoretical bounds in general. This is particularly important since the paper's key examples (like bilinear saddle-point problems) rely on factored splits.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the major concern about the proximal operator bottleneck needs to be addressed in the final version. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of when their methods are advantageous over batch methods, including a precise characterization of the problem sizes (n, d) where their approach wins. They should also clarify whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits in general, or only in specific regimes.

In terms of the four criteria: Quality is excellent with rigorous proofs and thorough analysis; Clarity is high with well-structured presentation; Originality is strong with a novel extension to saddle-point problems; Significance is high as the methods address a class of problems with broad applications in machine learning.

Pro: First linearly convergent incremental algorithms for saddle-point problems; Thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies; Clear theoretical connections to monotone operators; Strong experimental validation showing importance of non-uniform sampling.

Con: O(n + d) cost of proximal operator creates fundamental bottleneck; Insufficient discussion of when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch methods; Unclear theoretical comparison between factored and individual splits; n, d dependence not shown to be tight.

The paper represents a significant contribution to optimization theory with clear practical implications. While the proximal operator bottleneck is a serious concern, the authors' response to this issue (in the rebuttal) was satisfactory, and the theoretical framework is sound. The work is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
