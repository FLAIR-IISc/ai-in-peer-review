This paper proposes the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods (such as SVRG and SAGA). The authors provide both theoretical and empirical evidence of the effectiveness of these methods, handling non-separable saddle-point problems where the loss and regularizer can be non-separable.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow for experts in the field. The authors adequately inform the reader about the problems and approaches, and the related work is well-referenced. The paper is a complete piece of work, with a clear description of the algorithms, their convergence analysis, and experimental results.

The main contribution of the paper is the extension of SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing linear convergence guarantees. The authors also propose two gradient-operator splits: element-wise split and factored split for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The paper shows that non-uniform sampling is key to an efficient algorithm, both in theory and practice, and that these incremental algorithms can be easily accelerated using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework.

The convergence analysis uses a monotone-operator framework, which is more complex than standard convex analyses. However, the authors provide a clear explanation of the analysis and its implications. The paper also discusses the applicability of the methods to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

The experimental results support the theoretical claims and show the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods. The authors compare their methods with existing batch and stochastic algorithms, demonstrating the superiority of their approach.

One minor concern is the unclear adaptation of the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) to achieve speed-up in the acceleration section. Additionally, it is unclear whether the convergence and acceleration proof given for SVRG also holds for SAGA. Finally, there is a notational issue with the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K, which should use distinct symbols to avoid confusion.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the contributions are significant. The authors provide a clear and concise description of the algorithms, their convergence analysis, and experimental results. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning.
