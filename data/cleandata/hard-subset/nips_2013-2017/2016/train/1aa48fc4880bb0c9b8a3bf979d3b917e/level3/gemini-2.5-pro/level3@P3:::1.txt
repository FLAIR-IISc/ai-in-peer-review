This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y). Here, K is a smooth, decomposable function, and M is a "simple" (proximable) strongly convex-concave function. The paper proposes algorithmic adaptations of SVRG and SAGA for this setting and provides the first linear convergence guarantees for such methods on this class of problems. The analysis is grounded in the theory of monotone operators, revealing that the algorithms apply more broadly to problems like variational inequalities. The authors also introduce an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework and leverage non-uniform sampling to improve practical performance. This work extends the successful line of research on variance reduction for finite-sum convex minimization to the more general and challenging saddle-point setting, which arises frequently in machine learning, especially with non-separable objectives.

### Strengths

*   **Significant Contribution:** This work makes a significant contribution by being the first to develop stochastic variance-reduced algorithms with provable linear convergence for a general class of saddle-point problems. This represents a clear advance over existing batch methods, which are slow on large datasets, and standard stochastic methods, which typically achieve sub-linear rates for this problem class.
*   **Novel and General Theoretical Framework:** The use of monotone operator theory to prove convergence is a major strength. This approach is more general and insightful than a direct saddle-point analysis, as it identifies monotonicity as the core property enabling convergence. This framing elegantly shows that the proposed algorithms can solve a wider range of problems than just saddle-point optimization, including variational inequalities.
*   **Broad Applicability to Non-Separable Functions:** A key practical advantage of this framework is its ability to handle non-separable loss functions and regularizers, provided they admit an efficient proximal operator. This overcomes a major limitation of many fast stochastic methods that are restricted to separable objectives and opens the door to applying variance reduction techniques to problems like AUC maximization or structured-output prediction.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

Despite the strong contributions, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily related to clarity, experimental validation, and comparison with related work.

1.  **Presentation and Organization:** The paper's organization significantly obscures its main theoretical insight. The connection to monotone operators is the foundation of the analysis, yet it is presented in Section 6 as a late "extension." This makes the paper difficult to follow, as the proofs and motivations are disconnected from the initial problem statement. The paper would be much stronger and clearer if it were framed from the outset as finding the zero of a sum of monotone operators, with saddle-point problems introduced as a primary application. This reorganization would make the theoretical contributions more transparent and the overall narrative more coherent.

2.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is insufficient to fully substantiate the paper's claims. Using only two datasets is not enough to demonstrate the general effectiveness of the proposed methods. A more convincing experimental section would include:
    *   A wider variety of datasets with different characteristics (e.g., varying n, d, and sparsity).
    *   More extensive comparisons against relevant baselines.

3.  **Missing Comparison to Key Baselines:** The paper fails to compare against a crucial class of related methods: accelerated stochastic primal-dual algorithms. Methods like the one proposed by Zhang and Xiao are highly relevant competitors for this problem class and should be included as a baseline. The omission is significant, as it is unclear how the proposed methods perform relative to this state-of-the-art.
    *   *Reference:* Zhang, Y., & Xiao, L. (2017). Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization. *Journal of Machine Learning Research, 18*(1), 1-49. (An earlier version appeared in ICML 2015).

4.  **Clarity and Applicability of Assumptions:** The assumptions, particularly (A), need more discussion regarding their practical implications. Assumption (A) requires M(x, y) to be strongly convex-concave. This is a very strong condition. For example, in a standard regularized ERM problem like SVM, the objective is strongly convex in the primal variable `x` due to the regularizer, but the dual problem is often not strongly concave in `y`. The authors should explicitly discuss whether their framework applies to such fundamental ML problems and clarify the limitations imposed by this assumption.

5.  **Lack of Clarity on Technical Details:**
    *   **Comparison with Primal-Dual Methods:** The paper should more clearly articulate the differences between its approach and standard stochastic primal-dual methods. While the variance reduction aspect is novel, a more explicit comparison of the update rules and mechanics would help readers understand the precise contribution.
    *   **Unclear Constants in Theorems:** In Theorem 2, the constant `μ` appears in the convergence rate without being defined in the main text. I presume it is the strong monotonicity constant of the operator A from the appendix, but this must be stated explicitly. A direct comparison of the rates in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 is also needed to understand the trade-offs between SVRG and SAGA in this setting.
    *   **Logical Gap in Analysis:** The transition from Assumptions (A)-(C) to the strong monotonicity property used in the proofs is abrupt. The paper should explicitly state whether (A)-(C) imply the strong monotonicity of the corresponding operator and briefly explain why. This connection is critical for the validity of the analysis.

6.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   The notation `L` is used inconsistently to denote both a Lipschitz constant and a condition number. Please use distinct notation for clarity.
    *   A citation is missing for the Forward-Backward algorithm mentioned in Section 2.2. A standard reference, such as Bauschke & Combettes (2011), should be added.

In summary, this paper presents important and novel results. However, its current form suffers from significant clarity issues and an incomplete experimental evaluation. Addressing the points above, especially by restructuring the paper around the monotone operator framework and strengthening the experiments, would dramatically improve its quality and impact.
