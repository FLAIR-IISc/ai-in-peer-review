This paper proposes an extension of the Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVRG) and Stochastic Average Gradient (SAGA) methods to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors provide a linear convergence analysis for these algorithms, which is a significant contribution to the field of optimization.

The paper begins by introducing the concept of saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The authors then review existing algorithms for solving these problems, including batch and stochastic methods. They highlight the limitations of these methods, particularly in terms of their convergence rates and computational complexities.

The main contribution of the paper is the extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems. The authors propose two different splits for the gradient operator: an element-wise split and a factored split. They show that these splits can be used to derive variance-reduced algorithms for saddle-point problems, which have faster convergence rates than existing methods.

The authors provide a detailed convergence analysis for their algorithms, which is based on the monotone-operator framework. This framework is more complex than standard convex analysis, but it allows the authors to prove linear convergence for their algorithms.

The paper also presents experimental results that support the theoretical claims. The authors show that their algorithms outperform existing methods in terms of convergence rate and computational complexity. They also demonstrate the effectiveness of non-uniform sampling, which is a key component of their algorithms.

One minor concern is that the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how to adaptively update the reference iterate (x̄, ȳ) to achieve speed-up in the acceleration scheme. However, this is a relatively minor issue, and the paper overall makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization.

The notation used in the paper is generally clear, but there is one notational issue that could be improved. The smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K are denoted by the same symbol, which could lead to confusion. It would be better to use distinct symbols for these two objects.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a new and important result, and the convergence analysis is thorough and well-done.

The significance of the paper is also high. Saddle-point problems are common in machine learning, and the algorithms proposed in this paper have the potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of many machine learning applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The authors provide a thorough and well-done convergence analysis, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the notation is generally clear.
