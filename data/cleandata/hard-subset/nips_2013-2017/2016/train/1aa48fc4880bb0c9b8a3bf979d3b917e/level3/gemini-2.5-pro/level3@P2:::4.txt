This paper proposes the extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are prevalent in machine learning, often arising from duality or in applications like robust optimization and structured prediction. While SVRG and SAGA have proven highly effective for minimizing finite sums of convex functions [1, 2, 3], their application to the saddle-point structure is not straightforward. The authors provide the first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for this problem class. The core of their theoretical analysis departs from traditional convex minimization proofs, instead leveraging the more general framework of monotone operators. This not only establishes convergence for saddle-point problems but also shows the algorithms' applicability to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper also investigates practical and theoretical aspects unique to this setting, such as different ways to split the gradient operator ("factored" vs. "element-wise"), the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework [7].

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the optimization literature. Extending the highly successful variance-reduction paradigm to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution. This opens the door for efficiently solving a wide range of machine learning problems that feature non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization), which could not be tackled by previous fast incremental methods that rely on separability in the primal or dual formulations.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. The use of the monotone operator framework for the convergence proof is elegant and powerful. It is more general than a direct, and likely more complex, extension of the standard SVRG/SAGA analyses. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying mathematical structure and ensures the results are robust. The derivations for the convergence rates, including the analysis of non-uniform sampling and the accelerated variants, appear sound and complete.

3.  **Completeness and Practical Relevance:** The paper is a comprehensive piece of work. It not only presents the algorithms and their theoretical guarantees but also explores practical considerations that are critical for performance. The discussion on different splitting strategies and the empirical and theoretical justification for non-uniform sampling are valuable. Furthermore, the inclusion of an acceleration method, which provably improves upon accelerated batch methods in certain regimes, makes the proposed framework very compelling.

4.  **Clarity and Empirical Validation:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The problem setting, motivations, and contributions are clearly articulated. The experimental results convincingly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) outperform existing batch and stochastic baselines in practice on real-world datasets with non-separable objectives.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance)

The weaknesses of this paper are minor and mainly relate to small points of clarification that could be addressed in a final version. They do not undermine the core contributions.

1.  **Clarity on Adaptive Acceleration:** In Section 5, the paper mentions the possibility of adaptively updating the reference iterate `(x̄, ȳ)` more aggressively to speed up the algorithm. This is an interesting practical suggestion, but no further details are provided on how such an adaptive scheme would be implemented. A sentence or two on the intuition or a specific strategy would make this point more concrete.

2.  **Acceleration for SAGA:** The acceleration framework is formally developed and proven for SVRG. The authors state that "the same scheme should work for SAGA." While this is a reasonable claim, the paper would be strengthened by a brief justification for why this extension is expected to hold, or a mention of any potential challenges that might arise in the analysis for SAGA.

3.  **Minor Notational Ambiguity:** The symbol `K` is used to denote both the smooth component of the objective function, `K(x, y)`, and the design matrix in the machine learning examples. While the context generally makes the meaning clear, using a distinct symbol for the design matrix (e.g., `X` or `Φ`) would eliminate any potential for confusion.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is excellent and presents a valuable and timely contribution to the optimization and machine learning communities. The proposed algorithms are practical, well-motivated, and backed by strong theory. The connection to monotone operators is particularly insightful and broadens the impact of the work. The experimental section is solid and provides clear evidence of the methods' effectiveness. The minor weaknesses listed above are primarily suggestions for improving the clarity of an already well-written manuscript and do not detract from its overall high quality. This paper is a clear accept for NIPS.
