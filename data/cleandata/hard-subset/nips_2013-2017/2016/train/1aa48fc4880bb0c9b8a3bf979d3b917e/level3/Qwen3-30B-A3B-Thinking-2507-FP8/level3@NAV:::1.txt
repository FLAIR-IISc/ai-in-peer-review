

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG/SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this class of problems. The authors prove convergence using monotone operator theory, revealing that their approach applies beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Key contributions include handling non-separable functions (a significant advance over existing methods), proposing non-uniform sampling strategies, and demonstrating acceleration via the catalyst framework. The work connects to prior NIPS contributions on stochastic optimization (SAGA, SVRG) but extends them to the saddle-point setting where standard convex minimization analysis fails.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical novelty and significance. The analysis reveals important insights about the applicability to non-separable functions, which is crucial for many machine learning problems (e.g., structured prediction, robust optimization). The acceleration technique is well-motivated and shows theoretical advantages over batch methods.

However, several weaknesses significantly impact the paper's quality and clarity. The connection to monotone operators is buried in Section 6 despite being fundamental to the analysis, creating confusion about how assumptions (A)-(C) relate to strong monotonicity. The constant μ in Theorem 2 is undefined, and the transition from assumptions to strong monotonicity is unclear. The authors should clarify whether assumptions A-C imply monotonicity. The presentation of the Forward-Backward algorithm lacks a key reference (e.g., [20]), and the use of L for both condition number and Lipschitz constant is inconsistent.

Experiments are limited to two datasets (sido and rcv1), with insufficient comparison to relevant methods like accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithms (Zhang & Xiao 2015). The paper should address whether assumptions hold for common SVM-induced saddle-point problems and clarify differences between standard stochastic primal-dual methods and their proposed approach.

While the theoretical contributions are significant, the weak presentation, limited experiments, and theoretical ambiguities prevent this work from achieving the completeness expected for NIPS. The paper would be substantially stronger with clearer exposition of the monotone operator connection, additional experimental validation, and proper comparison to accelerated stochastic primal-dual methods.
