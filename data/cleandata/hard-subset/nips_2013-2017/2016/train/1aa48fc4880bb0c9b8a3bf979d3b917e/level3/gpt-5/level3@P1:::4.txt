This paper extends stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with provable linear convergence. The authors tackle the setting min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where M is proximable and strongly convex–concave, K is smooth convex–concave, and the smooth operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK) decomposes into many components. The key algorithmic idea is a stochastic forward–backward scheme with variance reduction, paired with non-uniform sampling and an optional “catalyst”-style proximal regularization to accelerate convergence. A central analytical contribution is to reframe convergence via monotone operators rather than standard convex minimization tools, which yields linear rates without requiring co-coercivity and extends beyond saddle points to variational inequalities. The paper explores two operator splits—element-wise and factored—that align with the saddle-point structure (e.g., bilinear K), analyzes non-uniform sampling policies, and provides storage/running-time trade-offs for SAGA vs. SVRG. Experiments on non-separable objectives and a separable benchmark demonstrate that non-uniform sampling matters, variance reduction substantially improves over batch and plain stochastic methods, and the catalyst extension further helps on ill-conditioned problems.

Relation to prior work: Prior finite-sum acceleration for strongly convex objectives (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) targets separable convex minimization. Primal–dual and coordinate methods for separable saddle-point problems (e.g., SPDC and related) do not address non-separable losses/regularizers. The forward–backward and Chambolle–Pock lines provide batch or noisy variants with sublinear rates. This work appears to be the first to deliver large-scale linearly convergent incremental methods for general convex–concave saddle-point problems, with a monotone-operator analysis that also covers variational inequalities. The acceleration via catalyst is adapted to the saddle-point setting and requires only proximal-point intuition (no Nesterov machinery), which is conceptually clean.

Strengths:
- Technical novelty: A careful monotone-operator analysis for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point regime, avoiding co-coercivity assumptions and proving linear convergence under strong convex–concavity.
- Scope: Applicability to non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient prox, and to broader monotone-inclusion problems (e.g., VIs).
- Algorithmic design: Two decomposition schemes (element-wise and factored) tailored to bilinear structure; explicit use of non-uniform sampling with tight constants L̄(π); practical memory/runtime trade-offs for SAGA vs. SVRG.
- Acceleration: A simple catalyst-style regularization delivering improved complexity bounds and empirically better performance on ill-conditioned problems.
- Empirical evidence: Non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform; variance reduction beats batch and noisy forward–backward; accelerated SVRG is best among tested methods.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Acceleration details: Section 5 leaves some ambiguity about adaptively updating the anchor (x̄, ȳ). The paper states that more aggressive updates can help; concrete guidance (criteria, schedules, or safeguards) would aid reproducibility and practical tuning.
- SAGA acceleration: The acceleration theory is presented for SVRG, with the claim that the same scheme “should work” for SAGA. A proof sketch or conditions under which it carries over would strengthen the contribution.
- Parameter knowledge: Step sizes depend on strong convex–concavity (λ, γ) and smoothness constants (L, L̄). Discuss robustness to misspecification and whether adaptive strategies (e.g., backtracking, online estimates) can be incorporated, akin to adaptive variants in convex minimization.
- Experimental scope: Results are convincing but limited in breadth. Including more datasets, wall-clock comparisons, sensitivity to sparsity/conditioning, and ablations on the factored vs. element-wise splits (including storage vs. speed trade-offs) would further validate claims.
- Resampling in SAGA: The bound for non-uniform sampling requires resampling operator values after the iterate update. Empirical practice uses a uniform/non-uniform mixture without resampling; a short discussion of the gap between theory and observed behavior would be helpful.
- Notation: The symbol K denotes both the smooth function and the design matrix in the bilinear case; using distinct symbols would improve clarity.

Quality: The analysis is technically sound and clearly identifies the departures from convex minimization (e.g., lack of co-coercivity, need for convex–concave splits). Theorems state linear convergence with explicit dependence on L and L̄(π), and complexities are broken down into oracle and prox costs. The monotone-operator framing is convincing and broadens the scope to VIs. Some parts (acceleration tuning, SAGA acceleration) would benefit from additional theoretical or empirical detail.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The roles of L vs. L̄(π) and of the two splits are explained with interpretable bounds in the bilinear case. Suggestions: separate symbols for function vs. matrix K; summarize practical recipes for choosing sampling distributions; and provide guidance for selecting mini-batch size m and handling expensive prox operators.

Originality: High. Extending variance-reduction with linear rates to non-separable saddle-point problems and analyzing it via monotone operators is novel and impactful relative to prior NIPS work on SVRG/SAGA and primal–dual methods.

Significance: High. Many modern ML problems require non-separable losses/regularizers and/or saddle-point formulations. The proposed methods offer substantial theoretical and practical gains, and the monotone-operator view opens avenues in VIs and game-theoretic applications.

Pros for acceptance:
- First linearly convergent incremental methods for general convex–concave saddle points, with solid analysis.
- Clean extension to monotone operators and practical acceleration via catalyst.
- Strong motivation for non-uniform sampling with clear theoretical and empirical benefits.
- Demonstrated practical improvements on realistic non-separable tasks.

Cons for acceptance:
- Acceleration details for anchoring and SAGA extension are not fully nailed down.
- Limited experimental breadth and missing wall-clock analyses.
- Reliance on known problem constants without adaptive schemes.

Suggestions to authors:
- Clarify/adaptively specify the update schedule for (x̄, ȳ) and provide a short ablation.
- Either prove or delimit conditions for the catalyst scheme to apply to SAGA.
- Discuss adaptive estimation of L, L̄, λ, γ or backtracking line-search variants.
- Expand experiments with wall-clock time, more datasets, and an ablation on splits and sampling distributions.
- Fix notation to avoid overloading K.
