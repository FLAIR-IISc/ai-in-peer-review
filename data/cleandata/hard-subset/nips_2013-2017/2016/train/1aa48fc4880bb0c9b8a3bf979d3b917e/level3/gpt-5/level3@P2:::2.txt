Summary and contributions
The paper addresses large-scale convex–concave saddle-point (SP) problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is strongly convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator. The authors extend variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting and prove linear convergence. The analysis departs from standard convex minimization by framing SP as a zero of a monotone operator and proving convergence using monotonicity rather than co-coercivity. The work further:
- Clarifies two natural ways to “split” the operator for variance reduction: by functions or by partial derivatives (factored splits), and emphasizes the necessity that splits respect the convex–concave structure.
- Shows that non-uniform sampling is essential to obtain the best theoretical and practical complexity.
- Provides an acceleration via a simple “catalyst”/proximal-point wrapper, yielding improved complexity and, in theory, methods that dominate accelerated batch algorithms.
- Extends the analysis to general monotone operator inclusions and variational inequalities.
- Presents experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers and a separable case, demonstrating the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Relation to prior work
This paper builds directly on SAG/SVRG/SAGA (NIPS’12–’14) by moving from smooth, strongly convex finite sums to convex–concave SP problems. It is complementary to primal–dual methods such as forward–backward and accelerated variants (e.g., Chambolle–Pock), and to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods tailored to separable SP problems (Zhu & Storkey; Zhang & Xiao). The monotone operator viewpoint connects to classical work by Rockafellar and modern operator splitting (Bauschke–Combettes). Compared to recent stochastic operator methods that rely on co-coercivity, this work allows general monotone B (not necessarily co-coercive), which is crucial for SP and variational inequalities.

Quality (soundness and evaluation)
- Theory: The algorithms are clear extensions of SVRG/SAGA with appropriate proximal steps. The monotone operator analysis is technically clean and appears sound; linear rates are obtained under strong convexity–concavity of M and Lipschitz continuity of B, with careful dependence on L and on a sampling-adapted L̄. The acceleration via a proximal-point “catalyst” is well motivated and yields improved iteration complexity; the derivations match known proximal-point linear rates and avoid delicate momentum machinery.
- Assumptions: Requiring M to be strongly convex in x and strongly concave in y is a strong assumption. Many applications satisfy only one-sided strong convexity (e.g., primal regularization only). The paper notes a proximal-point wrapper can create strong monotonicity; however, it would be impactful to relax to one-sided strong convexity (or to strong monotonicity of A alone) in the core algorithms/analysis.
- Practicality: The role of non-uniform sampling is well quantified; storage/computation trade-offs for factored vs. individual splits are discussed. The need to know or estimate L, L̄, and strong convexity parameters could be limiting; adaptive step-size strategies would improve usability.
- Experiments: The results are promising and consistent with theory, particularly showing the gains of non-uniform sampling and acceleration. However, the empirical section is limited in scope (two datasets, one non-separable loss/regularizer example, one separable baseline).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear problem setup, assumptions, and algorithm boxes. The discussion of splits and sampling is helpful. Some sections (e.g., the exact conditions for resampling in SAGA with non-uniform sampling) could benefit from clearer guidance for practitioners. A small pass to fix minor typos and improve notation consistency (e.g., consistent use of λ, γ, µ and norms) would help.

Originality
High. To my knowledge, these are the first linearly convergent, variance-reduced incremental methods for general convex–concave SP problems (beyond separable cases) with a convergence proof that does not rely on co-coercivity. The simple acceleration via a proximal-point catalyst in the SP setting is also novel and elegant. The extension to monotone operators broadens scope beyond SP.

Significance
High. Many ML problems with non-separable losses or regularizers are more naturally solved in SP form. Providing scalable, linearly convergent methods with principled non-uniform sampling and acceleration should influence both theory and practice. The monotone operator perspective also opens doors to applications in variational inequalities and game-theoretic models.

Strengths
- First linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for general convex–concave SP under standard smoothness/strong convexity–concavity.
- Clean monotone operator analysis; generalizes beyond SP.
- Practical, provably beneficial non-uniform sampling strategies; factored vs. individual splits are well motivated.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point catalyst.
- Solid complexity comparisons and supportive experiments.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Strong convexity–concavity on M is restrictive. Suggest developing theory for one-sided strong convexity (primal or dual) or for strongly monotone A with possibly non-strong B, and clarifying when the catalyst wrapper suffices to bridge the gap with mild overhead.
- Experimental scope is narrow. Recommend: more datasets; ablations on factored vs. individual splits; sensitivity to sampling schemes; effect of mini-batch size; robustness to mis-specified λ, γ, L; wall-clock comparisons including proximal operator costs; and at least one variational inequality or robust optimization example.
- Practical guidance on estimating L and L̄ and on constructing non-uniform probabilities at scale (and their maintenance cost) should be expanded.
- The claim “always superior to accelerated batch” should be qualified (constants, prox costs, and uniform sampling may negate the advantage empirically).
- For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, clarify the necessity and overhead of the post-update resampling step and report its empirical impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and important problem setting with clear theoretical advances.
- Strong, general analysis via monotone operators; acceleration is simple and broadly applicable.
- Practical insights (non-uniform sampling; factored splits) with demonstrable gains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Assumption of strong convexity–concavity on both sides limits applicability; no empirical study of one-sided cases.
- Experimental evaluation is relatively limited for a methods paper; broader, more diverse benchmarks would strengthen the case.
- Some practical aspects (parameter estimation, resampling overhead) are underexplored.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The methodological and theoretical contributions are substantial and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community. With expanded experiments and a discussion/partial relaxation of the strong convexity–concavity assumption (or clearer guidance via the catalyst wrapper), this could be a strong accept.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
