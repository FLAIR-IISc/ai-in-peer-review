**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a stochastic variance reduction method for solving saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The authors extend the SVRG and SAGA algorithms to handle strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with two types of splits: individual and factored. They provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and uniform/non-uniform sampling strategies. The experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch algorithms.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Extension**: The paper proposes a novel extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, which is technically non-trivial.
2. **Thorough Complexity Analysis**: The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies, which helps to understand the behavior of the algorithms.
3. **Clear Comparisons**: The paper provides clear comparisons for bilinear saddle points that align with convex minimization intuition.
4. **Experiments**: The experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational Cost**: Each incremental iteration incurs an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator, creating a fundamental bottleneck.
2. **Overall Complexity**: The overall complexity of the proposed methods may not improve over accelerated batch FB, and factored splits can be worse when n >> d.
3. **Lack of Discussion**: The paper lacks a deeper discussion on whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and whether the n, d dependence is tight.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, which is technically non-trivial. The authors also provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies, which is original and contributes to the understanding of the behavior of the algorithms.

**Significance**

The paper is significant because it proposes a new method for solving saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The method has the potential to improve the efficiency of solving these problems, especially in large-scale settings.

**Clarity**
