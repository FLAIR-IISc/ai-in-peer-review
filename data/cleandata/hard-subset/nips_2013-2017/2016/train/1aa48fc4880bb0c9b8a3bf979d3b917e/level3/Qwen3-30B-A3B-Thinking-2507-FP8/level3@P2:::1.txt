

# Review for "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The authors demonstrate that the standard SVRG/SAGA framework can be extended to saddle-point problems by using monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, rather than the convex minimization approach. This represents a significant theoretical contribution, as prior stochastic methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., stochastic forward-backward) typically only achieved sublinear convergence rates.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) providing the first convergence analysis for SVRG/SAGA on saddle-point problems using monotone operators, (2) showing the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, (3) extending the catalyst framework for acceleration, and (4) demonstrating applicability to non-separable machine learning problems (e.g., non-separable losses and regularizers in supervised learning).

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical contribution is substantial and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The monotone operator approach provides a unifying framework that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.
- The paper identifies an important distinction between saddle-point problems and convex minimization regarding the necessary structure for splitting (convex-concave terms vs. convex terms).
- The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms batch methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The presentation is weak: the connection to monotone operators, which underpins the entire analysis, appears late in the paper as an extension rather than being established early as the foundation. This makes the paper difficult to follow.
- Experiments are limited to only two datasets, which is insufficient to establish generalizability across different problem structures and scales. More datasets and comparisons to relevant methods (e.g., Zhang & Xiao 2015's accelerated stochastic Chambolle-Pock method) would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Assumptions A-C require clearer explanation, particularly regarding their applicability to standard machine learning problems. The authors should explicitly address whether these assumptions hold for SVM-induced saddle-point problems (a common application).
- The constant μ in Theorem 2 is not well-defined (it appears to be the monotonicity constant, but this isn't clearly stated), and the transition from assumptions A-C to strong monotonicity is confusing.
- The paper inconsistently uses L (sometimes as condition number, sometimes as Lipschitz constant), and misses a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and correct, though the presentation of assumptions and the connection to monotone operators needs significant improvement. The experimental results support the theoretical claims but are limited in scope.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from poor organization and explanation. The key insight about monotone operators should be introduced earlier to frame the entire analysis. Many technical terms (e.g., μ in Theorem 2) are not clearly defined.

**Originality:** The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with separable structure is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The application to non-separable problems in machine learning is particularly valuable.

**Significance:** The work is important for machine learning applications where saddle-point problems with large-scale separable structures arise, particularly in non-separable losses and regularizers. The improved convergence rates over existing methods have practical implications for large-scale optimization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First linear convergence guarantees for saddle-point problems with separable structure
- Novel use of monotone operator theory to unify analysis
- Clear demonstration of advantages in both theory and practice
- Applicability to important machine learning problems with non-separable components

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Poor organization and presentation of the key theoretical insight (monotone operators)
- Limited experimental validation with only two datasets
- Missing comparison to relevant accelerated stochastic primal-dual methods (Zhang & Xiao 2015)
- Unclear definitions of key technical terms (e.g., μ, inconsistent use of L)

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. With significant revisions to improve clarity, expand the experimental validation, and clarify key assumptions and technical details, this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The core idea is sound and significant, but the presentation needs substantial improvement to fully communicate its contributions. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
