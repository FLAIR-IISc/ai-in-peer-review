- Paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems, extending SVRG/SAGA and Catalyst-style acceleration using a forward–backward splitting viewpoint in a monotone operator framework.  
- Two split notions for the smooth part B = ∑i Bi are treated: “individual” (per-entry) and “factored” (row/column), with non-uniform sampling advocated as essential.  
- Complexity bounds give linear rates expressed via global Lipschitz L and sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π); Catalyst yields extra gains via a proximal-point approach without Nesterov extrapolation.  
- Experiments on non-separable supervised learning show accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms batch methods; uniform sampling can lag behind accelerated batch.  
- Prior variance-reduction work for minimization does not directly handle non-separable saddle structures; primal–dual splitting and coordinate methods provide baselines but have limitations (O(1/t) stochastic rates or require separability).  
- Main technical novelty: monotone operator analysis proving linear convergence for variance-reduced forward–backward under strong monotonicity + Lipschitzness, beyond co-coercive assumptions.  
- Clear complexity decomposition separating costs T(B), T(Bi), Tprox(M) and clarifying roles of L vs L̄(π); for bilinear K non-uniform sampling links L̄ to ‖K‖F.  
- Practical guidance discusses non-uniform sampling and split strategies, and storage/time trade-offs between SAGA vs SVRG and factored vs individual splits.  
- Simple Catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration is proposed with provable and practically meaningful improvements and simpler analysis than convex minimization counterparts.  
- Empirical evidence supports theoretical claims but is limited in scope (two datasets, few baselines).  
- Proximal bottleneck: incremental steps require proxM costing O(n + d), potentially negating per-iteration savings unless amortized via mini-batching; more quantitative regime analysis is needed.  
- Split trade-offs are not fully distilled into actionable guidance; factored splits have lower storage and better L̄ bounds in some regimes but can be penalized (e.g., rectangular n ≫ d) making them worse than individual-element SVRG with suitable m.  
- Missing crisp, easy-to-check conditions for when accelerated SVRG beats accelerated batch FB (practitioners would benefit from formulas in terms of nnz(K), ‖K‖F, λ, γ, n, d, Tprox).  
- Tightness concerns: dependencies like √max{n,d} in norm relations may be analysis artifacts; lower bounds or adversarial examples are not provided to justify tightness.  
- Practicalities not fully addressed: computing/estimating L and L̄(π), choosing π in non-bilinear cases, and adaptivity to unknown strong convexity parameters (λ, γ).  
- SAGA resampling practice and sensitivity (uniform/non-uniform mixtures) need empirical guidelines.  
- Prox cost mitigation strategies beyond larger m (proximal reuse, warm starts, inexact prox with controlled error) are suggested but not analyzed for preserved linear rates.  
- Analysis is careful and technically sound; assumptions and constants are explicitly stated.  
- Clarity is good overall, but the paper would benefit from a concise practitioner section about choosing split, sampling, and minibatch size m.  
- Originality is high: extends variance reduction into general saddle-point/monotone operator settings and supports non-function splits with a simple proximal acceleration.  
- Significance is meaningful: many ML problems are saddle-point and non-separable, so linearly convergent incremental methods here are valuable.  
- Requests for clarification include formal comparisons of factored vs individual splits, explicit conditions when accelerated SVRG wins for bilinear K, lower-bound evidence for tightness, adaptivity of step sizes/strong-convexity, SAGA resampling guidance, and prox-cost mitigation techniques.  
- Pros summarized: first variance-reduced linear methods for general strongly convex–concave saddle points, well-motivated non-uniform sampling and Catalyst acceleration, and clear complexity expressions.  
- Cons summarized: per-iteration prox cost can dominate, limited empirical validation and practitioner guidance, and possible looseness in dependence on problem constants.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — solid and novel theoretical contribution but needs clearer delineation of practical regimes and expanded experiments.  
- Post-rebuttal: Authors addressed main concerns (prox bottleneck regimes, split comparison, conditions for acceleration) satisfactorily; reviewer remains positive and keeps scores unchanged.
