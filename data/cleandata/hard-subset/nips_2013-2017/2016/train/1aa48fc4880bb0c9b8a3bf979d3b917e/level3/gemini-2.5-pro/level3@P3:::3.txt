### **Summary**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods SVRG and SAGA to the setting of strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems. This is a significant step, as many machine learning problems, particularly those with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization) or regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso), are naturally formulated as saddle point problems but could not previously benefit from these fast incremental methods. The authors propose variants for two types of finite-sum structures ("individual" and "factored" splits), provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their convergence rates, and explore the benefits of non-uniform sampling. The analysis is performed within the more general framework of monotone operators, highlighting its broader applicability. Furthermore, the paper shows how to accelerate the SVRG variant using the Catalyst framework, leading to a method that is argued to be superior to accelerated batch algorithms. Experimental results on a binary classification task with non-separable components corroborate the theoretical findings, demonstrating that acceleration and non-uniform sampling are key to achieving strong performance.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to successfully extend SVRG/SAGA to provide linearly convergent algorithms for finite-sum saddle point problems. This fills an important gap in the literature, connecting the powerful variance-reduction paradigm with a broad and practical class of optimization problems that are not directly amenable to standard stochastic methods on the primal or dual alone. The potential impact is high, as it enables efficient optimization for a new range of machine learning models.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very solid. The extension from the standard convex minimization setting is non-trivial, and the authors correctly identify that the analysis requires the more general machinery of monotone operators, as co-coercivity does not hold. The complexity analysis is comprehensive, carefully considering the impact of different problem structures (individual vs. factored splits) and sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform). The theoretical comparisons for the bilinear case are insightful and align well with the established intuition from convex optimization.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, and methodically builds up the algorithms and their analysis. The inclusion of Table 1 is particularly helpful for comparing the complexities of the various methods at a glance. The algorithmic descriptions are clear, and the connection to the Catalyst framework for acceleration is elegant.

### **Weaknesses**

The primary concern with this work stems from the per-iteration computational cost of the proposed methods, which appears to create a fundamental bottleneck that challenges the practical utility and theoretical superiority of the approach compared to accelerated batch methods.

1.  **High Per-Iteration Cost:** The core update step in all proposed algorithms involves the computation of a proximal operator `prox_M`, which, for the motivating non-separable examples, seems to have a complexity of `O(n + d)`. This cost is incurred at *every single incremental step*. In contrast, standard SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization typically has a per-iteration cost of `O(d)`. This `O(n + d)` cost is substantial, as it is on the same order as computing one row *and* one column of the full gradient in the bilinear case.

2.  **Questionable Overall Complexity Improvement:** This high per-iteration cost significantly impacts the overall runtime complexity. The authors claim in the abstract that the accelerated algorithm is "always superior to accelerated batch algorithms," but this seems unlikely when the per-iteration cost is factored in. For instance, the total complexity of Accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling on a bilinear problem appears to be roughly `O(nd + (n+d) * (||K||_F / sqrt(λγ)) * sqrt(max{n,d}))`. An accelerated batch forward-backward method would have a complexity of `O(nd * (||K||_op / sqrt(λγ)))`. Given that `||K||_F` can be up to `sqrt(min{n,d}) * ||K||_op`, it is not at all clear that the stochastic method offers an improvement, and it could be substantially worse, particularly when `n` and `d` are large and the problem is not extremely ill-conditioned.

### **Questions for the Authors**

The paper would be substantially strengthened if the authors could address the following points related to the per-iteration cost bottleneck:

1.  **Factored vs. Individual Splits:** The paper highlights the storage benefits of factored splits (`O(n+d)` vs. `O(nd)`). However, what is the trade-off in terms of overall computational complexity? Factored splits have cheaper gradient component computations (`T(B_i)`), but does this advantage matter when the `O(n+d)` proximal operator cost dominates each iteration? Could you provide a clear analysis of when factored splits are theoretically better or worse than individual splits in terms of total runtime, especially in regimes where `n >> d`?

2.  **Conditions for Outperforming Batch Methods:** Could you provide a more precise and rigorous characterization of the conditions (in terms of `n`, `d`, condition number `L`, and matrix norms) under which your proposed Accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is truly faster than an accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm? It appears the benefit may be limited to cases with extremely large condition numbers, where the reduction in iteration count is massive enough to overcome the high per-iteration cost.

3.  **Tightness of the `(n,d)` Dependence:** Is the `O(n+d)` cost for the proximal operator an inherent feature of this class of non-separable saddle-point problems, or is it specific to the chosen examples (AUC, total variation)? Could you comment on whether this dependence is tight and if there are other important applications where this cost could be substantially lower (e.g., `O(d)` or `O(log n)`), which would make the proposed methods unequivocally superior?

In conclusion, this is a very strong paper that introduces a novel and theoretically sound extension of variance-reduced methods to an important problem class. However, the claims of practical superiority are weakened by a potentially critical computational bottleneck. A deeper discussion of this issue and the conditions under which the proposed methods are genuinely advantageous would make this an outstanding contribution.
