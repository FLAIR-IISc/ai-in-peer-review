- Novel contribution: extends SVRG/SAGA and accelerated variants to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, filling a gap in literature.  
- Theoretical foundation: connects to monotone operator theory and provides rigorous convergence and complexity analyses.  
- Distinction of splits: clearly identifies and explains two split types (individual vs. factored) and their different implications.  
- Non-uniform sampling & acceleration: demonstrates importance of non-uniform sampling strategies and acceleration for performance.  
- Clarity and organization: well-written paper with clearly stated problem setting, assumptions, and comparisons.  
- Practical significance: addresses relevant ML problems (e.g., non-separable losses/regularizers) with supportive empirical validation.  
- Comparison to batch methods: provides clear benchmarks against batch forward-backward and accelerated variants.  
- Major computational bottleneck: incremental iterations incur O(n + d) cost due to proximal operator computation, limiting benefits of acceleration.  
- Complexity limitation: dependence on both n and d is problematic, especially when n >> d; factored splits can worsen performance in that regime.  
- Unresolved theoretical questions: needs clearer theoretical comparison between factored and individual splits and quantification of hidden constants.  
- Performance conditions unclear: more nuanced discussion required on when accelerated SVRG truly outperforms batch FB given proximal costs and condition number interactions.  
- Tightness of bounds uncertain: must address whether the O(n + d) dependence is tight or can be improved for specific problem structures.  
- Acceptance pros: novel, technically sound, comprehensive analysis, clear writing, and empirical support.  
- Acceptance cons: significant proximal-cost limitation, potential worse behavior of factored splits, and unanswered questions after rebuttal.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to (1) justify factored vs. individual splits theoretically, (2) detail when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (3) discuss/tighten the O(n + d) complexity dependence.
