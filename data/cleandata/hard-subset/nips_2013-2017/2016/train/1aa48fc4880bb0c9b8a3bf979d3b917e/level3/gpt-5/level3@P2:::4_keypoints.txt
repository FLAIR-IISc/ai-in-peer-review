- Core contribution: Extends SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex–concave composite saddle-point problems and gives first linear-convergence guarantees for non-separable objectives via monotone-operator analysis.
- Technical approach: Casts saddle-point optimality as finding a zero of A+B with A strongly monotone (prox accessible) and B a sum of Lipschitz operators, enabling variance-reduced methods and applications to VIs.
- Algorithms: Proposes element-wise and factored decompositions for B, non-uniform sampling with explicit L̄(π) constants, and a proximal-point/catalyst acceleration tailored to the saddle setting.
- Relation to prior work: Contrasts with variance reduction for convex minimization, batch primal–dual methods, stochastic forward–backward O(1/t) results, separable-only methods, and co-coercivity-based analyses; covers non-co-coercive settings.
- Strength: Provides a clear, principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle problems with first linear rates.
- Strength: Monotone-operator analysis is technically sound and broadens applicability to variational inequalities and monotone inclusions.
- Strength: Careful treatment of sampling with explicit constants and theoretical/empirical evidence that non-uniform sampling helps.
- Strength: Practical algorithm design choices (factored vs element-wise splits, mini-batching, storage vs computation trade-offs) and per-iteration cost discussion.
- Strength: Acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst improves over accelerated batch methods in relevant regimes and uses a simple analysis.
- Strength: Experiments on non-separable and separable cases support theoretical claims and show practical benefits of non-uniform sampling.
- Weakness: Acceleration section lacks practical guidance on adaptive update frequency for the reference iterate and inner-loop stopping rules.
- Weakness: Acceleration proof is shown for SVRG only; no formal SAGA acceleration result is provided.
- Weakness: SAGA non-uniform sampling analysis requires resampling, but experiments use a mixture scheme without discussing the theory-practice gap.
- Weakness: Methods assume known strong convexity–concavity and smoothness constants (λ, γ, L, L̄) with no practical estimation or robustness discussion.
- Weakness: Notation overload — using K for both the smooth function and the data/design matrix is confusing.
- Evaluation (Quality): Theorems are well stated with clear assumptions and careful complexity discussions; minor concerns remain about acceleration and parameter selection.
- Evaluation (Clarity): Paper is overall well-organized with explicit algorithms; suggested improvements include separating notation and centralizing constants, and moving some acceleration details to the main text.
- Evaluation (Originality): Novel extension of variance reduction to non-separable saddle problems via monotone operators and factored splits with non-uniform sampling.
- Evaluation (Significance): High — framework applies to many ML problems (robust optimization, AUC surrogates, complex regularizers) with empirical and theoretical advantages.
- Question: How should practitioners choose π and approximate L̄(π) beyond the bilinear case without expensive precomputation, and are adaptive sampling strategies compatible with the analysis?
- Question: Can the authors provide a formal acceleration result for SAGA or explain the obstacles to such a proof?
- Question: How sensitive are the methods to misspecified λ, γ, L, and can backtracking or adaptive step-size schemes preserve linear convergence?
- Question: Does the analysis tolerate inexact proximal computations for M, and what error tolerances preserve linear rates?
- Question: For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, what is the theoretical impact of the practical “mixture sampling without resampling” heuristic?
- Pros for acceptance: First linear-convergent VR algorithms for non-separable saddle problems, broad applicability via monotone operators, theoretically and empirically motivated non-uniform sampling and factored splits, effective acceleration in relevant regimes, and clear empirical gains.
- Cons against acceptance: Missing acceleration policy details and SAGA acceleration proof, reliance on problem constants without estimation guidance, and theoretical assumptions (resampling) relaxed in experiments without discussion.
- Minor: Use distinct symbols for the smooth function and data matrix to avoid confusion.
- Minor: Collect norms and constants in a self-contained notation block early in the paper.
- Minor: Summarize appendix acceleration details in the main text for clarity.
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
