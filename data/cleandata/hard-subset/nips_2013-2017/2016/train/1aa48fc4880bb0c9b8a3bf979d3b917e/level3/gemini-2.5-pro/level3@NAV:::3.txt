This paper extends the highly successful family of stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods, including SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants, from strongly convex finite-sum minimization to the more general class of strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems. The authors introduce two novel splitting schemes—individual and factored—and provide a comprehensive complexity analysis for both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. This work represents a significant and non-trivial step in bridging modern stochastic optimization techniques with the saddle-point formulations prevalent in machine learning.

The paper’s primary strengths are its originality and technical quality. The extension is novel, and the convergence analysis, grounded in monotone operator theory, is thorough and sound. The paper is clearly written, and the theoretical comparisons for bilinear saddle points are particularly insightful, aligning well with established intuitions from the convex minimization literature. The significance of the work is supported by experiments which effectively demonstrate that key components like non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for the proposed methods to achieve empirical gains over batch algorithms.

However, the paper's practical significance is shadowed by a major concern regarding its computational complexity. The analysis reveals that each incremental update incurs an O(n + d) cost for computing the proximal operator, creating a fundamental performance bottleneck. This high per-iteration cost calls into question the method's overall efficiency. Consequently, the total complexity of the proposed methods, even the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, may not offer a clear improvement over standard accelerated batch forward-backward (FB) algorithms. This issue seems particularly acute for the proposed "factored" splits, which the analysis suggests can be substantially worse than individual splits when n >> d.

To strengthen the paper and clarify its contributions, I would appreciate a deeper discussion from the authors addressing the following:
1.  Is the apparent sub-optimality of factored splits a fundamental property, or an artifact of the current analysis?
2.  Can you more precisely characterize the regimes (in terms of n, d, and problem conditioning) where your accelerated SVRG method is guaranteed to outperform accelerated batch FB, given the O(n+d) per-iteration cost?
3.  How tight is the derived dependence on n and d in the complexity bounds?

Overall, this is a technically strong paper on an important topic, but its practical impact is unclear without addressing these critical efficiency concerns.
