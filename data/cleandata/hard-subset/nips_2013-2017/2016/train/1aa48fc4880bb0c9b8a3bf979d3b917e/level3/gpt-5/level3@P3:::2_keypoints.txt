- Paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure and proposes SVRG- and SAGA-style variance-reduced methods.  
- Key assumptions: M is strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator, K is smooth convex–concave, and B decomposes into Lipschitz finite-sum components.  
- Algorithms: SVRG and SAGA variants with linear convergence rates and a Catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration.  
- Analysis viewpoint: Uses monotone operator splitting, extending applicability to variational inequalities and general monotone inclusions beyond saddle points.  
- Practical design: Discusses factored vs element-wise splits of B, non-uniform sampling, mini-batching, and storage/compute trade-offs.  
- Empirics: Experiments on classification with non-separable losses/regularizers show gains over batch FB and uniform sampling; accelerated SVRG performs well on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Main technical contribution: First comprehensive variance-reduction analysis for general convex–concave saddle-point problems with clear linear rates under strong convexity–concavity.  
- Generality: Methods extend to maximal monotone A and Lipschitz Bi with B monotone, without requiring co-coercivity of components.  
- Acceleration: Catalyst adaptation via proximal-point regularization yields improved complexity bounds analogous to accelerated minimization.  
- Strength: Clarifies decomposition granularities and motivates non-uniform sampling for theoretical and practical gains.  
- Weakness: Requires strong convexity in both primal and dual (M), which is restrictive for many problems that are one-sided strongly convex.  
- Recommendation: Authors should analyze one-sided strong convexity or show how to induce/shift strong convexity via tricks or proximal regularization.  
- Weakness: Step-size choices depend on L and L̄(π), which are hard to estimate; paper lacks practical tuning heuristics or online estimation strategies.  
- Weakness: Experimental scope is narrow (two datasets); needs larger-scale, higher-condition-number tests, ablations (factored vs element-wise, mini-batch), wall-clock measures, and comparisons to strong baselines.  
- Implementation detail: Non-uniform SAGA requires resampling stored gradients to retain bounds; this needs clearer discussion and cost analysis.  
- Quality: Theoretical results are sound and the monotone-operator viewpoint cleanly avoids co-coercivity assumptions.  
- Clarity: Paper is dense but organized; should add a compact figure for storage/compute trade-offs, guidelines for selecting π and estimating norms, and clarify SAGA resampling cost.  
- Originality: Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and monotone problems without co-coercivity, combined with Catalyst-style acceleration and practical decomposition strategies.  
- Significance: High — broadens variance-reduction applicability to robust optimization, structured prediction with non-separable penalties, and variational inequalities.  
- Pros summary: Solid theory, broad applicability, practical sampling/design choices, and empirical improvements over baselines.  
- Cons summary: Needs dual strong convexity, sensitivity to unknown constants, and limited experimental breadth.  
- Recommendations to authors: Relax dual-primal strong convexity assumption, expand experiments (including a VI example), provide practical recipes for L/L̄(π) and π, and detail SAGA memory/runtime trade-offs and resampling regimes.  
- Overall assessment: Important and well-executed contribution with promising theory and experiments; acceptance recommended pending relaxed strong-convexity results and expanded empirical validation.
