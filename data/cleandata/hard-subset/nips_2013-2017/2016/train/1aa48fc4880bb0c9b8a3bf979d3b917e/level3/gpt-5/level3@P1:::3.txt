This paper studies finite-sum convex–concave saddle-point problems and extends variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) and Catalyst-style acceleration to this setting. The main algorithmic idea mirrors forward–backward splitting for saddle problems, but the analysis departs from convex minimization and is developed in the monotone operator framework. The authors treat two split notions for the smooth part B = ∑i Bi: (i) “individual” (e.g., per entry for bilinear K), and (ii) “factored” (e.g., row/column splits for bilinear K), and advocate non-uniform sampling as essential. They give linear convergence rates with complexities expressed in terms of L (global Lipschitz constant) and L̄(π) (sampling-dependent smoothness), and show how Catalyst yields additional gains via a proximal-point viewpoint without Nesterov-style extrapolation. Experiments on non-separable supervised learning demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration significantly outperform batch methods; uniform sampling can lag behind accelerated batch.

Relation to prior work: Variance reduction for convex minimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) is well-established, but does not directly cover non-separable saddle-point structures. Primal–dual splitting (e.g., Chambolle–Pock; Arrow–Hurwicz) provides batch baselines and O(1/t) stochastic rates with noise. Coordinate primal–dual methods (e.g., SPDC, stochastic primal–dual coordinate descent) require separability. The paper’s use of monotone operator theory to prove linear rates for variance-reduced forward–backward in strongly monotone + Lipschitz settings goes beyond co-coercive requirements used in SMART and related works, and thus covers general saddle-point problems and certain variational inequalities.

Strengths:
- Technical novelty and breadth: A clean monotone operator analysis that yields linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA-type methods in strongly convex–concave saddle problems, including non-function splits (on partial derivatives) and variational inequalities.
- Clear complexity story: Rates and running-time bounds that separate T(B) (full operator), T(Bi) (component), Tprox(M), and reveal the roles of L vs L̄(π). For bilinear K, non-uniform sampling ties L̄ to ‖K‖F, matching convex-minimization intuitions.
- Practical guidance: Emphasis on non-uniform sampling and the two splitting strategies; storage/time trade-offs are discussed (SAGA vs SVRG; factored vs individual).
- Acceleration: A simple Catalyst adaptation using proximal point yields provable and practically meaningful gains; analysis is simpler than in convex minimization.
- Empirical evidence: On non-separable losses/regularizers, accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is consistently best; uniform sampling can underperform batch acceleration—consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Proximal bottleneck: Each incremental step invokes proxM, which costs O(n + d) in the reported setups. Even with variance reduction, this can negate per-iteration savings compared to accelerated batch FB, unless mini-batching is used to amortize the prox cost. The paper mentions using larger m but the practical regime where this flips the advantage could be elaborated quantitatively.
- Split trade-offs: The claimed advantages of factored splits (lower storage; better L̄ bound for bilinear with non-uniform sampling) vs individual splits (potentially better variance with larger mini-batches) are not fully distilled into actionable guidance. In rectangular regimes (e.g., n ≫ d), the iteration constants involving max{|J|,|K|} can penalize factored SAGA, and the overall complexity bound can be worse than individual-element SVRG with suitable m.
- When do we beat accelerated batch? The paper provides conditions via the Catalyst τ trade-off, but an explicit, easy-to-check statement for bilinear problems in terms of (nnz(K), ‖K‖F, λ, γ, n, d, Tprox) would help practitioners decide.
- Tightness: The dependence on n, d enters through norm relations ‖K‖op ≤ ‖K‖F ≤ √max{n,d}‖K‖max. It is unclear which factors are intrinsic vs artifacts of analysis; lower bounds or adversarial examples would strengthen the message.
- Practicalities: Computing/estimating L and L̄(π), selecting π in general non-bilinear cases, and adaptivity to unknown strong convexity (λ, γ) are not fully addressed (though the authors note adaptivity as future work). Experimental scope is limited (two datasets; few baselines).

Quality: The analysis is careful and technically sound, with appropriate use of monotone operator tools and clear linear-rate results under strong convexity–concavity. Assumptions and constants are stated explicitly.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, with concrete algorithm statements and complexity discussions. It would benefit from a concise practitioner section: how to choose split, sampling, mini-batch size m, and when to expect acceleration over batch.

Originality: High. Extends variance reduction beyond co-coercive settings to saddle-point/monotone operators, supports non-function splits, and provides a simple proximal-point acceleration scheme in this context.

Significance: Meaningful. Many ML problems are naturally saddle-point with non-separable structures; bringing linearly convergent incremental methods to this class is valuable, and the monotone-operator perspective may inspire further advances.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Factored vs individual splits: Please provide a theorem or proposition that compares the two in terms of rate and memory, isolating regimes in n, d, and norm structure (e.g., row/column leverage) where each is preferable. Are there cases where factored is provably worse despite its smaller storage?
- When accelerated SVRG wins: For bilinear K with Tprox(M) ≈ O(n + d), give explicit conditions on (‖K‖F, λ, γ, n, d, nnz(K)) under which accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling and an optimal m yields lower total time than accelerated batch FB. A rule-of-thumb would be useful.
- Tightness: Are the √max{n,d} gaps in L̄ bounds necessary? Any lower bounds or constructions indicating the analysis is tight up to constants?
- Adaptivity: Can step sizes be made adaptive to L and L̄(π) (e.g., backtracking) and to unknown strong convexity (λ, γ), as in SAGA variants?
- Resampling in SAGA: In practice you use a uniform/non-uniform mixture without resampling. Could you report sensitivity and provide a guideline for choosing the mixture?
- Prox cost mitigation: Beyond larger m, can proximal reuse, warm starts, or inexact prox (with controlled error) be incorporated while preserving linear rates?

Pros:
- First variance-reduced, linearly convergent methods for general strongly convex–concave saddle points; principled operator-theoretic analysis.
- Non-uniform sampling and Catalyst acceleration are well-motivated and effective.
- Clear complexity expressions linking norms, sampling, and algorithm choices.

Cons:
- Per-iteration prox cost can dominate; practical regimes where the methods decisively beat accelerated batch are not crisply delineated.
- Limited empirical validation and guidance for choosing split/sampling/minibatch.
- Dependence on problem constants and norm inequalities may be loose in worst case.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a solid and novel theoretical contribution with practical implications; a clearer account of regimes where the methods outperform batch, and an expanded empirical section, would further strengthen it.

Post-rebuttal: The authors addressed the main concerns (prox bottleneck regimes, split comparison, and conditions for acceleration) satisfactorily. I keep my overall scores unchanged but remain positive about the contribution.
