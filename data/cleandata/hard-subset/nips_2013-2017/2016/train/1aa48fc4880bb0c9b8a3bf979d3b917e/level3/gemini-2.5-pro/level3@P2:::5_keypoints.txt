- Proposes the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad class of convex-concave saddle-point problems.  
- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point settings; algorithmic changes are straightforward but important in practice.  
- Main novelty is a convergence proof based on monotone operator theory, generalizing beyond convex minimization to variational inequalities.  
- Shows acceleration via the Catalyst framework and demonstrates that non-uniform sampling yields state-of-the-art performance.  
- Enables efficient optimization for problems with non-separable losses or regularizers common in machine learning.  

- Paper is high quality, technically sound, and provides rigorous theoretical analysis supporting linear convergence.  
- Monotone operator-based analysis is elegant and appropriate where standard potential-function arguments fail.  
- Empirical validation is convincing, showing practical advantages over strong baselines.  

- Paper is well-written and organized with clear motivation and relation to prior work.  
- Recommend adding a high-level proof sketch in the main text to better showcase the monotone-operator contribution.  
- Unclear how to choose non-uniform sampling probabilities π_i for general (non-bilinear) saddle-point problems.  
- Experiments do not specify how step-size σ was chosen (theoretical vs tuned), which impacts reproducibility.  

- Minor typos: incorrect primal problem notation in Sec 2.1 (line 78) and a likely incorrect y-update expression in Sec 2.2 (line 121).  

- Highly original: extends variance-reduced methods to a new problem class via a novel analytical framework.  
- Significant practical impact for large-scale ML problems (AUC maximization, robust optimization, structured regularizers).  
- Significant theoretical impact: monotone-operator framework may inform analysis of other variance-reduction methods.  
- Advances state of the art: accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms batch and stochastic baselines.  

- Questions for authors: explain intuition why non-uniform sampling is more critical for saddle-point problems; clarify step-size selection and provide sampling guidance beyond the bilinear case.  

- Arguments for acceptance: first linear-convergence stochastic algorithms for broad saddle-point class; novel monotone-operator analysis; high practical significance; strong empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: algorithms are direct adaptations of SVRG/SAGA (minor); some experimental and sampling-details need clarification (minor).  

- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
