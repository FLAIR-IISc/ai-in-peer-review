- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory, yielding first large-scale incremental methods with linear convergence.
- Analysis generalizes beyond saddle points to variational inequalities and unifies non-separable settings via two natural splits of the smooth operator B.
- Highlights importance of respecting convex–concave structure when splitting B and of using non-uniform sampling; adapts Catalyst acceleration for tighter complexities.
- Relates work to prior variance-reduction and primal–dual methods, generalizing variance-reduced methods to non-separable, non-bilinear settings.
- Conceptual novelty: reframes variance reduction through monotone operator theory to obtain linear rates and broader applicability.
- Generality: supports non-separable losses/regularizers and both “function” and “factored” splits.
- Practical guidance: analyzes non-uniform sampling effects (L and L̄), minibatching, and SVRG vs. SAGA storage/compute trade-offs.
- Acceleration: Catalyst-style regularization gives better theoretical complexity and practical speedups, especially for ill-conditioned problems.
- Additional contributions: provides simpler convergence arguments for forward–backward and its stochastic variant.
- Empirical evidence: non-uniformly sampled SAGA/SVRG (notably accelerated SVRG) outperform batch and uniform variants on tested datasets.
- Weakness: unclear guidance for choosing non-uniform sampling π and estimating L̄(π) for general non-bilinear K; needs practical heuristics.
- Weakness: insufficient detail on step-size σ selection in experiments and lack of robust/default or adaptive strategies for practitioners.
- Weakness: limited intuition and theoretical connection explaining why non-uniform sampling is more critical for saddle-point problems.
- Weakness: reliance on known strong convexity/concavity and Lipschitz constants without discussion of adaptive estimation schemes.
- Weakness: SAGA’s memory cost can be large for individual splits; needs quantified implementation notes comparing factored vs. individual splits.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation scope is limited—more datasets, sampling ablations, and sensitivity to condition numbers are needed.
- Overall quality: technically sound with careful operator-theoretic analysis and clear convergence guarantees under standard assumptions.
- Clarity: generally well written; would benefit from a main-text proof sketch and more detail on sampling and step-size selection.
- Originality: high—novel application of monotone operator theory to obtain linear convergence for saddle-point variance-reduction.
- Significance: high—addresses many ML problems with non-separable structure and offers impactful, scalable methods.
- Pros for acceptance: first linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for general saddle points, clean analysis, practical sampling/acceleration guidance, supportive experiments.
- Cons for acceptance: limited guidance for non-uniform sampling and step sizes, dependence on problem constants without adaptivity, and narrower experimental breadth.
- Suggested improvements: add a proof sketch, provide practical recipes for π and σ for non-bilinear K, discuss adaptivity to unknown constants, and include ablations and memory/runtime trade-offs.
- Minor comments: typo on line 78 (“k”→“d”) and extra y_{t−1} term on line 121.
