This paper extends recent incremental algorithms—SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants—to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure, arising under two types of decompositions: individual and factored splits. The authors present a thorough complexity analysis across these splits and under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a particular binary classification task indicate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch methods.

Although extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, the results here are genuinely novel and the technical development is nontrivial. For the important bilinear saddle-point case, the paper provides clear comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward (FB) algorithms. These comparisons mirror known phenomena in convex minimization and are consistent with my understanding.

My main concern is computational. While formulating the problem as a saddle point resolves the non-separability of the loss, the proposed incremental methods appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as reported in Tables 1 and 2—is not necessarily better than that of the accelerated batch counterpart. In particular, when the number of data points n is much larger than the dimension d (a common scenario in machine learning), the complexity under factored splits (the setting used in the simulations) is actually worse than for batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization setting, I do not see a clear theoretical advantage for variance reduction here.

I suggest the authors provide a deeper discussion that clarifies:
- Whether factored splits are (theoretically) worse than individual splits;
- Under what regimes accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB;
- Whether the dependencies on n and d are tight.
