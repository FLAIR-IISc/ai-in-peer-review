

The authors leverage variance reduction techniques such as SVRG and SAGA to develop the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems. Since standard convex minimization analysis does not extend to saddle point problems, the paper's primary contribution lies in employing monotone operator theory to establish convergence. This approach yields broader applicability, encompassing variational inequality problems. The framework enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers by reformulating them as saddle point problems, addressing a key challenge in machine learning model design. The proposed algorithms achieve linear convergence rates for saddle point problems, representing a significant advancement. The proof technique—interpreting saddle point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators—is both novel and potentially widely applicable. Additionally, the paper provides simpler proofs for algorithms like the forward-backward splitting (including its stochastic variant). The proposed methods with non-uniform sampling demonstrate competitive performance, often outperforming prior alternatives. Overall, the paper makes an important theoretical contribution by extending the field through its analysis approach, though the core algorithms themselves are straightforward extensions of existing methods. Given that the main novelty resides in the proof technique, the authors should consider including a proof sketch in the main text (if space allows) to enhance accessibility. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, but requires clarification on two specific points:  
1. For non-bilinear saddle point problems, what is the exact form of the non-uniform sampling distribution $\pi$?  
2. How is the step size $\sigma$ determined in experiments? The authors should discuss why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle point problems but less critical for convex minimization.
