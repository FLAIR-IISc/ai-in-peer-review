## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - Please Insert]

This paper addresses the challenging problem of solving saddle-point problems possessing a convex-concave structure, a common occurrence in many machine learning applications. The authors demonstrate how existing stochastic variance reduction techniques – specifically SVRG and SAGA – can be effectively adapted for this setting by incorporating a proximal operator. Their analysis, framed within the theory of monotone operators, establishes linear convergence guarantees. Furthermore, they introduce an accelerated variant of their algorithm and investigate the performance of a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty and Originality:** The contributions of this work are significant. The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, providing valuable new insights into the convergence behavior of algorithms applied to saddle-point problems. The authors explore several extensions to their core method, with the applicability to non-separable functions being particularly noteworthy. This expands the scope of these techniques beyond traditionally separable settings.

**Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are compelling; however, the presentation hinders the paper’s accessibility. While the connection to monotone operator theory underpins the analysis, this connection could be made more explicit earlier in the paper. Currently, it appears as an extension in Section 6, when it fundamentally motivates the approach. A clearer upfront explanation would greatly benefit readers less familiar with this theoretical framework. 

On the experimental front, the evaluation is limited to two datasets. While demonstrating performance is a good start, a more comprehensive empirical analysis with a wider variety of problem instances is needed to bolster the claims and establish practical effectiveness. For the case of separable functions, a comparison to accelerated proximal gradient methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm [Zhang & Peyré, 2015], is crucial. This comparison would position the proposed method relative to existing state-of-the-art approaches.

I have several specific questions for the authors that would strengthen the paper:

1. **Assumption Strength:** How restrictive are assumptions (A)-(C), especially assumption (A)? It would be beneficial to discuss the conditions under which these assumptions hold for common saddle-point problems, such as those arising in Support Vector Machines (SVMs). Providing concrete examples would clarify the practical applicability of the theoretical results.

2. **Relationship to Primal-Dual Methods:** The distinction between existing stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach is not entirely clear. The authors should elaborate on the key differences in algorithmic structure and theoretical properties. A precise comparison would help highlight the advantages of their method.

3. **Theorem 2 and Constant μ:** In Theorem 2, the constant μ requires clarification. Is this the same monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? Further, how does the convergence rate presented in Theorem 2 compare to the rate achieved in Theorem 1? Providing a direct comparison would aid in understanding the benefits of the accelerated variant.

4. **Strong Monotonicity:** The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to the implication of strong monotonicity (detailed in the appendix) is confusing. The authors should explicitly state whether assumptions (A)-(C) *imply* strong monotonicity, and if so, under what conditions. A clearer explanation of this connection is necessary.

**Minor Issues:**

*   There is some ambiguity in the use of the constant *L*. It is used both to represent the condition number of the problem and as a Lipschitz constant. This should be clarified to avoid confusion.
*   The Forward-Backward algorithm is mentioned without citation. A reference to a standard source on this algorithm would be helpful (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, 2009).



**Recommendation:** 

Despite the presentation issues and the need for a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial. I believe that addressing the concerns raised above would significantly improve the clarity and impact of the work.  I recommend acceptance with major revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the points outlined in this review.

**References:**

*   Beck, A., & Teboulle, M. (2009). A fast iterative shrinkage-thresholding algorithm for linear models. *SIAM Journal on Imaging Sciences, 2*(1), 183–205.
*   Zhang, A., & Peyré, G. (2015). Chambolle-Pock Algorithm for First-Order Optimization. *Journal of Machine Learning Research, 16*, 3643-3666.
