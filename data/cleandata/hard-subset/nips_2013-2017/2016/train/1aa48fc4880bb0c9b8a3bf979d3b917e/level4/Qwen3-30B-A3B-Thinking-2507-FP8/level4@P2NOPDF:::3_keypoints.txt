- Paper extends SVRG/SAGA and accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, treating individual and factored data splits with rigorous complexity analysis and experiments.  
- Authors analyze different sampling strategies (uniform/non-uniform) and show empirically that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are essential to outperform batch methods.  
- The extension to saddle points is non-trivial and yields novel, technically significant results, especially for bilinear saddle problems where comparisons align with convex minimization intuition.  
- Major concern: proximal operator computation costs O(n + d) per iteration, creating a practical computational bottleneck.  
- As a result, accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling may not improve complexity over accelerated batch forward–back (FB), especially when n ≫ d.  
- In typical ML settings (d ≪ n), the factored split can yield O(n) per-iteration cost, matching or exceeding batch methods and undermining expected variance-reduction gains.  
- Reviewer asked the authors to clarify: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior, (ii) when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) whether n, d dependencies in bounds are tight.  
- Authors’ rebuttal satisfactorily addressed those points: factored splits are not universally worse, accelerated SVRG wins when d is small relative to n, and the n, d dependencies are tight.  
- Overall verdict: complexity analysis is thorough, technical contributions are solid and novel, experiments support practical viability after rebuttal — paper merits acceptance.
