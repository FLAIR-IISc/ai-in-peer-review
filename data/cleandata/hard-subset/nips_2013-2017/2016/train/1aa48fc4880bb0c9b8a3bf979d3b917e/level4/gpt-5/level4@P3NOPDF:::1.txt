Summary
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems through the lens of monotone operators and shows how to solve them using stochastic variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG, SAGA) combined with proximal operators. The authors prove linear convergence under appropriate conditions, present an accelerated variant, and analyze non-uniform sampling. An important contribution is that the approach extends beyond separable objectives.

Strengths
- Theoretical significance: The analysis is thorough and yields new insights for saddle-point problems via the monotone operator framework, including linear convergence guarantees and acceleration.
- Generality: The methods are applicable to non-separable objectives, which substantially broadens their scope beyond many existing primal–dual or coordinate-based methods.
- Extensions: The accelerated variant and non-uniform sampling analysis are well-motivated and potentially impactful for practice.

Weaknesses and presentation concerns
- Centrality of monotone operators: The paper positions the monotone operator connection as an extension late in the paper (Section 6), yet the main analysis fundamentally relies on this framework. This disconnect makes the exposition harder to follow. Bringing the monotone operator perspective forward (with a brief primer and clear mapping from the saddle-point problem to the corresponding operator) would improve accessibility.
- Experimental evaluation: The experiments are limited to two datasets. This makes it difficult to assess robustness, scalability, and the practical benefits of the proposed variants (acceleration, non-uniform sampling).
- Missing baselines: For separable problems, the paper should compare against accelerated primal–dual methods, in particular stochastic/accelerated variants of the Chambolle–Pock algorithm. Please include, at minimum, the following reference and an empirical comparison:
  - Zhang and Xiao (2015), Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized ERM, JMLR W&CP 37. http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf

Requests for clarification
1) Strength of assumptions (A–C): Please clarify the practical strength of these assumptions, especially (A). Do they hold for commonly used saddle-point formulations such as SVMs (e.g., hinge or squared-hinge loss with standard regularizers) in both separable and non-separable settings? A small table mapping standard models to the assumptions would be helpful.
2) Relation to stochastic primal–dual methods: Conceptually and algorithmically, how does your method differ from stochastic primal–dual or PDHG-type methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock and its stochastic variants)? Please delineate:
   - The update structure (operator splitting vs. variance-reduction on a finite-sum operator),
   - Where variance reduction enters relative to primal–dual gaps,
   - The assumptions required for linear convergence,
   - Complexity and oracle calls per iteration, and
   - Regimes (separable vs. non-separable) where one approach has clear advantages.
3) The constant μ in Theorem 2: Is μ the strong monotonicity (or cocoercivity-related) constant introduced in the appendix? If so, please state this explicitly in the theorem and align the notation. It would also help to provide a short discussion on how μ relates to the problem parameters (e.g., strong convexity/concavity or blockwise curvature) and to contrast the resulting rate with Theorem 1 under matched assumptions.
4) From (A)–(C) to monotonicity: The transition in the appendix is confusing. Do (A)–(C) imply monotonicity or strong monotonicity of the composite operator associated with the saddle-point problem? If monotonicity is assumed separately, please make this dependence explicit in the main text, and clarify which parts of the analysis need monotonicity vs. strong monotonicity or Lipschitz continuity.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Restructure the exposition: Move the monotone operator formulation to the main body early on. Provide a concise mapping from the saddle-point objective to the monotone inclusion, define the operator’s Lipschitz and monotonicity parameters, and then present SVRG/SAGA-style variance reduction in that language.
- Unify notation and constants: The symbol L appears to denote both a Lipschitz constant and a condition number. Please disambiguate (e.g., use L for Lipschitz continuity of the operator, µ for strong monotonicity, and κ = L/µ for the condition number) and maintain this consistently across theorems and proofs.
- Compare against stronger baselines: In addition to the suggested stochastic Chambolle–Pock variant, consider including PDHG with acceleration, SPDC-type methods, and other recent variance-reduced primal–dual algorithms where applicable. Highlight cases where your method’s support for non-separability offers a concrete advantage.
- Expand experiments: Evaluate on more datasets and tasks (e.g., multiclass classification, structured prediction, or non-separable regularizers) and report wall-clock time, iteration complexity, and sensitivity to sampling schemes. Provide ablations for acceleration and non-uniform sampling to quantify their impact.
- Practical guidance: Offer recommendations for step-size selection, epoch length (for SVRG-like variants), and sampling distributions, tying them to the theoretical constants when possible.
- Citations for Forward–Backward splitting: Please add standard references, e.g., Combettes and Wajs (2005), Signal Recovery by Proximal Forward–Backward Splitting; Beck and Teboulle (2009), A Fast Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm.

Minor issues
- Clarify the meaning of L where it is alternately called the “condition number” and the “Lipschitz constant.” Reserve “condition number” for ratios such as L/µ.
- Add a formal definition or citation for the Forward–Backward algorithm where first introduced.
- Typos/wording: “can not” → “cannot”; ensure consistent capitalization of algorithm names; consider tightening statements like “we need the Lipschitz constant L” to specify which operator or function it pertains to.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper in terms of theoretical contributions and scope, particularly its treatment of non-separable objectives within a monotone operator and variance-reduction framework. The main weaknesses are in presentation clarity and the limited experimental validation and baselines. With improved exposition around the operator-theoretic foundations, clearer notation, and more comprehensive experiments—including comparisons to accelerated stochastic primal–dual methods—the paper would make a compelling contribution.
