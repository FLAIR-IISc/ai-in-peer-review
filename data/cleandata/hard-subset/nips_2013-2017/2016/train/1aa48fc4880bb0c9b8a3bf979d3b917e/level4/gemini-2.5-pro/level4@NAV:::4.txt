This paper adapts the stochastic variance reduction methods SVRG and SAGA for strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing a proof of linear convergence for this setting. The efficacy of this variance-reduced approach is substantiated by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, accommodating scenarios where both the loss function (e.g., in structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., a fusion term) are non-separable. A significant contribution lies in the convergence analysis; the authors adeptly handle the increased complexity inherent in saddle-point problems by employing the framework of monotone operators to establish their theoretical guarantees. This analysis also demonstrates that the extended SVRG and SAGA methods can be applied to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities found in game theory.

For the stochastic implementation, the authors introduce two distinct strategies for splitting the gradient operator: an element-wise split and a factored split, designed for SAGA and SVRG, respectively. These variance reduction schemes are integrated into the stochastic forward-backward algorithm, a standard framework for solving saddle-point problems. The work also investigates non-uniform sampling, demonstrating through both theory and experiments that this strategy outperforms uniform sampling. The experiments, detailed in both the main paper and the appendix, offer comprehensive validation for the theoretical claims and effectively showcase the performance of the proposed methods on problems with non-separable loss and regularization.

Some minor comments:
*   Regarding the acceleration technique in Section 5, which follows [8] by introducing a regularization term tied to an iterate point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$, clarification is needed on the adaptive update rule for this point to realize the intended speed-up.
*   The paper provides a proof for the convergence and acceleration of the SVRG-based method. It would be beneficial to clarify whether equivalent theoretical guarantees also hold for the SAGA-based variant.
*   To improve clarity, it is recommended to use distinct notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K to prevent potential ambiguity.
