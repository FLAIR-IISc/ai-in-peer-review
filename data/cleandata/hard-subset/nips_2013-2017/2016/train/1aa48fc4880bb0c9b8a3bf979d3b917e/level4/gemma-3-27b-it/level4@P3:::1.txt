This paper addresses the challenging problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, using stochastic variance reduction techniques. The authors extend SVRG and SAGA to this setting, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale instances. A key contribution is framing the problem within the theory of monotone operators, broadening the applicability of their approach beyond traditional saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. They further enhance their algorithms with acceleration via a “catalyst” framework and demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling.

The work builds on recent advances in stochastic optimization, specifically SVRG [2] and SAGA [3], adapting these methods to a non-trivial extension of separable objective functions. This is a valuable contribution, as many machine learning problems naturally present this saddle-point structure. The connection to monotone operator theory is a significant theoretical insight, offering a more general framework for analysis and potentially wider applicability. The analysis of non-uniform sampling and its benefits aligns with recent trends in optimization, aiming for faster convergence through informed sampling strategies. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and its practical relevance to machine learning. The convergence results are well-established, and the proposed acceleration technique further improves performance. The experimental evaluation, while limited to two datasets, provides empirical support for the theoretical findings. 

However, the paper suffers from a presentation that, while technically sound, can be difficult to follow. The motivation for transitioning to the monotone operator framework (Section 6) feels somewhat abrupt; a more gradual introduction would improve clarity. While the authors state the extension applies to non-separable functions, a clearer explanation of *how* this happens and the implications for specific applications would be beneficial. The experimental section could be strengthened by including results on additional datasets and a comparison with related work like the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm [http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf], especially for separable cases.

Here’s a list of specific points for the authors to address:

1. **Assumptions A-C:** How restrictive are these assumptions, particularly assumption (A) regarding strong convex-concavity? Does it hold for standard SVM formulations? Providing a discussion of the practical limitations of these assumptions would be valuable.
2. **Primal-Dual Methods:** Clarify the relationship between their proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual approaches. What are the key distinctions and advantages of their formulation?
3. **Theorem 2 and μ:** Define the constant μ in Theorem 2 more precisely, connecting it to the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix. Provide a direct comparison of the convergence rate in Theorem 2 with that of Theorem 1, highlighting the trade-offs.
4. **Monotonicity:** The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is unclear. Do (A)-(C) *imply* strong monotonicity, or is an additional assumption required? This connection should be explicitly stated.
5. **Notation:** Ensure consistent use of the notation 'L'. Differentiate clearly between 'L' as the condition number and 'L' as the Lipschitz constant throughout the paper.

Minor issues include occasional awkward phrasing and a missing reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm (e.g., [20]).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis is thorough and provides valuable insights.
*   **Generality:** The connection to monotone operator theory broadens the applicability of the methods.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem setting is common in machine learning, and the proposed algorithms offer a potential speedup.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The presentation is sometimes dense and lacks sufficient motivation for key theoretical steps.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** A more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those designed for separable cases, is needed.



**Overall assessment:**

Despite the areas for improvement, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and potential practical impact. The limitations primarily relate to clarity of presentation and the scope of the experimental evaluation. I recommend **acceptance** with the request that the authors address the points raised above to improve the accessibility and completeness of the work.
