- Extends SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, motivated by non-separable ML losses/regularizers.  
- Develops algorithms for two structural decompositions ("individual" and "factored" splits) and analyzes convergence under uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Shows how to combine the proposed methods with the "catalyst" acceleration framework.  
- Provides rigorous, linearly-convergent analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory.  
- Thorough theoretical comparison of complexity bounds for batch, stochastic, and variance-reduced methods, with emphasis on bilinear saddle-point cases.  
- Well-written and logically organized presentation, with clear motivation and summary tables.  
- Experimental results indicate non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming accelerated batch algorithms.  
- Primary practical weakness: each iteration requires a proximal computation costing O(n + d), creating a computational bottleneck.  
- Consequently, overall complexity (even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) is not always better than accelerated batch forward-backward, especially when n >> d.  
- In the common high-dimensional regime and under the factored-splits scheme used in experiments, the proposed methods can be theoretically worse than the accelerated batch baseline.  
- Requests for clarification: (1) is there a theoretical winner between individual and factored splits or is it problem-dependent; (2) specify regimes in n, d, and condition number where accelerated SVRG is favorable to batch; (3) are the n and d dependencies in the bounds tight or improvable?
