This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linear convergence proofs for these methods in this setting. The theoretical and empirical results effectively demonstrate the advantages of applying variance reduction to this class of problems.

The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of machine learning problems involving non-separable saddle-point formulations, such as those with non-separable loss functions (e.g., in structured output prediction) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation or grouped norms). A significant contribution of this work is its novel convergence analysis. Unlike previous analyses for convex optimization, the saddle-point setting is considerably more complex. The authors successfully address this by leveraging the theory of monotone operators to prove convergence. This approach also reveals that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms are applicable to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.

From a practical standpoint, the authors propose two distinct splitting schemes for the gradient operator—an "element-wise" split and a "factored" split—and apply variance reduction to the standard forward-backward algorithm for saddle-point problems. The paper also highlights the importance of non-uniform sampling, showing both theoretically and empirically that it is superior to uniform sampling. The experimental results presented in the main paper and appendix provide compelling evidence for the theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems featuring both non-separable losses and regularizers.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** This paper fills an important gap by developing the first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for a general class of saddle-point problems. This significantly expands the applicability of variance reduction methods beyond the standard setting of separable convex minimization.

2.  **Rigorous and General Analysis:** The use of monotone operator theory for the convergence proof is a major strength. While more challenging than the analysis for the standard convex case, this framework yields more powerful and general results, extending the algorithms' applicability beyond saddle-point problems.

3.  **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper thoughtfully explores practical implementation choices, such as the different splitting schemes and the use of non-uniform sampling. The analysis and empirical validation of non-uniform sampling, in particular, provide valuable guidance for practitioners.

4.  **Acceleration Framework:** The authors successfully adapt the "Catalyst" framework to accelerate their SVRG variant, providing an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.

5.  **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed, validating the proposed methods on relevant machine learning tasks with non-separable components. The results clearly support the theoretical findings and show marked improvements over existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

### Questions and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  The acceleration scheme described in Section 5, based on the "Catalyst" framework, solves a sequence of regularized subproblems centered at an iterate $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. The paper notes that this iterate can be updated "more aggressively to speed-up the algorithm." Could the authors provide more intuition or specific details on how to implement such an adaptive update scheme?

2.  A convergence proof and acceleration analysis are provided for the SVRG variant. Can similar theoretical guarantees be established for an accelerated version of SAGA?

3.  For clarity, I would suggest using different symbols for the smooth function $K(\cdot, \cdot)$ and the design matrix $K$. This would help avoid potential confusion for the reader.
