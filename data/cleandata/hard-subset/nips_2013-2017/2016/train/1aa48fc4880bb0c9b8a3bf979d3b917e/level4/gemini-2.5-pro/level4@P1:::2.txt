### Summary

This paper extends the recent and highly successful stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to solve large-scale convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are prevalent in machine learning, especially for models with non-separable loss functions or regularizers, where traditional stochastic methods were not applicable. The authors provide the first linear convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. A key aspect of their theoretical contribution is the use of monotone operator theory, which not only enables the convergence proof but also shows that the proposed methods apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper also explores important practical considerations, including different strategies for splitting the objective function and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling in achieving efficiency. Finally, the authors demonstrate that these methods can be readily accelerated using a simple extension of the "Catalyst" framework, resulting in an algorithm that is shown, both in theory and practice, to outperform standard accelerated batch methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** This work fills a notable gap in the literature by being the first to successfully adapt modern variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point setting with proven linear convergence rates. This is a significant contribution, as it extends a class of powerful, large-scale optimization algorithms to a wider range of machine learning problems, including robust optimization, structured output prediction, and models with complex, non-separable regularizers.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness and Generality:** The convergence analysis is rigorous and elegantly leverages the general framework of monotone operators. This approach is a major strength, as it broadens the applicability of the proposed methods beyond saddle-point formulations to other important problems like variational inequalities.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The paper provides a thorough treatment that includes practical enhancements. The discussion on "factored" splits and the demonstrated benefits of non-uniform sampling is valuable for practitioners. Furthermore, the proposed acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework is straightforward to implement and is shown to be highly effective, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, outlining the associated challenges, and summarizing their contributions.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** A primary limitation of the proposed framework is the assumption that the function M is strongly convex-concave. This requires strong convexity with respect to the primal variable `x` *and* strong concavity with respect to the dual variable `y`. This assumption can be quite restrictive for many practical applications, where strong convexity may be present in only the primal problem (e.g., via L2 regularization) or the dual, but not simultaneously in both. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could discuss this limitation and comment on potential avenues for extending their analysis to settings where only one of the strong-monotonicity conditions holds.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the numerical experiments effectively demonstrate the promise of the proposed methods, their scope could be broadened. The results are based on two classification datasets with a specific type of non-separable regularizer and loss function. To better substantiate the broad applicability claimed in the introduction, it would be beneficial to include experiments on other types of saddle-point problems mentioned in the paper, such as those arising from robust optimization or convex relaxations for unsupervised learning. This would provide a more compelling case for the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms across different domains.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a novel and important contribution to large-scale optimization for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the proposed methods are practical and effective. The main weakness is the reliance on a strong convexity-concavity assumption, which may limit immediate applicability in some areas. Addressing this limitation and expanding the experimental evaluation would further increase the impact of this excellent work.
