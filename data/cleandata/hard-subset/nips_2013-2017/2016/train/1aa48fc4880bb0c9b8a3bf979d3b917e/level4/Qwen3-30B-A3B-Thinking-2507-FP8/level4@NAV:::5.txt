

The authors employ variance reduction techniques such as SVRG and SAGA to develop the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems. As convex minimization frameworks do not apply to saddle point problems, the core contribution lies in utilizing monotone operators to establish convergence. This approach extends the analysis to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The methodology enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers by framing them as saddle point problems. The paper presents the first algorithms achieving linear convergence for saddle point problems, addressing a significant challenge in machine learning where non-separable losses and regularizers commonly arise. The proof technique—interpreting saddle point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators—is novel and potentially broadly applicable. Additionally, the paper provides simplified proofs for algorithms like the forward-backward splitting (and its stochastic variant). The proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling demonstrate competitive performance, often outperforming prior alternatives. Overall, the work makes a meaningful advancement by extending the field through an insightful analytical approach. Given that the primary contribution is the proof technique (with algorithms being straightforward extensions of existing methods), the authors should consider including a proof sketch or enhanced technical details on this technique in the main text if space allows. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, though it lacks clarity on two key points: 1. The specific form of non-uniform sampling (i.e., the distribution π) for non-bilinear saddle point problems; 2. The methodology for setting the step size σ in experiments, and deeper insight into why non-uniform sampling is critical for saddle point problems but less so for convex minimization. A discussion addressing this would strengthen the paper. Minor comments: 1. Typo on line 78: "k" should be "d"; 2. Typo on line 121 in the second equality (extraneous "y_{t-1}" term).
