1. Extends incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst) to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems with individual and factored splits.  
2. Provides thorough complexity analysis across different splits and sampling strategies (uniform and nonuniform).  
3. Experiment on a binary classification example shows nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch methods.  
4. Despite seeming straightforward, the extensions are novel and technically non-trivial.  
5. Presents a clear comparison for the bilinear saddle point case among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, analogous to convex minimization insights.  
6. Major concern: an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator each iteration creates a fundamental bottleneck for the incremental methods.  
7. Consequently, overall complexity (including accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not be better than accelerated batch FB per Tables 1 and 2.  
8. When n >> d (common in ML), factored splits can be worse than batch FB, so theoretical benefits of variance reduction are unclear.  
9. Reviewer requests clarification on: (i) whether factored splitting is theoretically worse than individual splitting, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG beats batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.  
10. Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors' response but keeps the same scores.
