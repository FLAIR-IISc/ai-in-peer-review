- Extends SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with the first linear convergence analysis using monotone operator theory.  
- Introduces two decomposition strategies: "individual" splits and "factored" splits.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for achieving superior performance.  
- Applies the Catalyst framework to accelerate the methods, yielding provable improvements over batch methods under certain conditions.  
- Provides a comprehensive theoretical complexity study across splits and sampling strategies, plus experiments on binary classification validating acceleration and non-uniform sampling.  
- Strength: novel and significant extension of variance-reduced methods to non-separable saddle-point problems relevant to many ML tasks.  
- Strength: technically sound convergence analysis grounded in monotone operator theory with thorough theoretical comparisons.  
- Strength: paper is well-written and organized; complexity results (Table 1) are clear and helpful.  
- Main concern: theoretical and practical advantages over accelerated batch algorithms are not as clear as in standard convex minimization.  
- Bottleneck: each incremental update requires an O(n + d) proximal operator, potentially negating the benefit of cheap stochastic updates.  
- Complexity comparison worry: for bilinear/factored splits accelerated SVRG includes a ||K||_F^2/(λγ) term that can exceed the batch term n||K||_op/√(λγ), making incremental methods potentially slower when n ≫ d.  
- Request: clarify theoretical trade-offs between individual and factored splits and practical reasons for using factored splits in experiments (e.g., storage).  
- Request: delineate regimes (n, d, problem structure, K norms) where accelerated SVRG provably outperforms accelerated batch forward-backward.  
- Request: state whether the n and d dependencies in the complexity bounds are tight or if refined analysis could improve theoretical advantages.  
- Summary: technically impressive and valuable extension, but needs a more nuanced theoretical comparison to batch baselines to clarify practical utility.
