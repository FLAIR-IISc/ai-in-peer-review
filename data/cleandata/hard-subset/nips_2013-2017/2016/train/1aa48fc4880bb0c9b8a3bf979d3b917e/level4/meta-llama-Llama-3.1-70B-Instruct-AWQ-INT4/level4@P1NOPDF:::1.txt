This paper addresses saddle-point problems with a convex-concave property, demonstrating how existing stochastic variance reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, can be adapted to solve these problems when coupled with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms in the context of monotone operators, establishing linear convergence. Additionally, they propose an accelerated variant and examine a non-uniform sampling scheme. 

The contributions of this paper are substantial, with thorough theoretical analysis that offers new insights into saddle-point problems. One of the most significant extensions proposed by the authors is the applicability of their method to non-separable functions.

Although the theoretical contributions of this paper are commendable, the presentation falls short in terms of accessibility. To enhance clarity, the authors could have more explicitly connected their work to monotone operators, rather than relegating this connection to an extension in Section 6. Given that the analysis relies heavily on monotone operators, this omission detracts from the overall coherence of the paper.

From an experimental perspective, the authors' decision to present results on only two datasets is somewhat limiting. Furthermore, in the context of separable functions, a comparison with accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang et al., 2015), would have strengthened the paper. I recommend adding this reference to the submission.

There are several points that require clarification from the authors:

1. The strength of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A), warrants further discussion. For instance, it is unclear whether these assumptions hold when solving the saddle-point problem induced by SVM.

2. The distinction between stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed method is not entirely clear. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation to facilitate understanding.

3. In Theorem 2, the constant \(\mu\) is not clearly defined. Is this the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? A comparison between the results of Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 would also be beneficial.

4. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is somewhat confusing. Do assumptions (A)-(C) imply monotonicity?

Minor issues include:

- Inconsistent usage of the constant L, which is referred to as both the condition number and the Lipschitz constant. For example, "The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem" and "we need the Lipschitz constant L" should be reconciled.

- The Forward-Backward algorithm is mentioned without a corresponding reference. Adding this reference would enhance the paper's completeness.
