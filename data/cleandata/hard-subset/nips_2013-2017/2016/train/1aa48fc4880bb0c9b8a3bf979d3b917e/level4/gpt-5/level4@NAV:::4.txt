This paper generalizes the stochastic variance reduction methods SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems and establishes linear convergence. Both the theory and the experiments support the efficacy of variance reduction on saddle-point tasks. This work extends SVRG and SAGA to the strongly convex–concave setting. The proposed algorithms handle a variety of non-separable saddle-point problems, allowing both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion terms) to be non-separable. A key contribution is the more intricate convergence analysis relative to standard convex cases; the authors leverage monotone operator theory to prove convergence. The analysis further indicates that the extended SVRG and SAGA methods cover a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities arising in game theory. Within a stochastic framework, the authors introduce two distinct decompositions of the gradient operator—an element-wise split and a factored split—for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The variance-reduced schemes are integrated into stochastic forward–backward algorithms commonly used for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also explored, and both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence show that non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform sampling under variance reduction. Experiments in the main text and the appendix provide ample validation of the theory and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods for both non-separable losses and regularizers.

Some minor comments: For acceleration in Section 5, the authors follow [8] by adding an extra regularization term that nudges the update toward a reference iterate. It remains unclear how to adaptively choose and update this reference point, (x̄, ȳ), to realize acceleration. While convergence and acceleration guarantees are given for SVRG, do analogous results hold for SAGA? Finally, using the same symbol K for the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K is potentially confusing; distinct notations are recommended.
