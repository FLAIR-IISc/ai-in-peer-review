

The paper extends recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite sum structure, arising from individual or factored splits. The authors conduct a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform/nonuniform). Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are essential to outperform competitive batch algorithms. While the extension of variance reduction to saddle point problems may appear straightforward, the established results are notably novel and technically non-trivial. For bilinear saddle point problems, the authors provide a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, which parallels known results for convex minimization and is logically consistent. However, a major concern remains: although solving saddle point problems addresses the non-separability challenge of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms face a fundamental bottleneck—the O(n + d) computational cost per iteration for computing the proximal operator. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2), is not necessarily superior to accelerated batch methods. Specifically, when n (data size) is substantially larger than d (dimension), which is typical in machine learning, the complexity under factored splits (used in simulations) is actually worse than batch FB algorithms (Table 2). Unlike convex minimization, the theoretical advantage of variance reduction here is unclear. I recommend an in-depth discussion addressing: i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, ii) when accelerated SVRG is favorable over batch FB, iii) whether the n, d dependence is tight.
