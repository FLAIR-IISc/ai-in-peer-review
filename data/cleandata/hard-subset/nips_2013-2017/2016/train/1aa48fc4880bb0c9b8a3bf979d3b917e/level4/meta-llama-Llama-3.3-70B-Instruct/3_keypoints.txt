Paper extends incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA, Catalyst) to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored splits.  
Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across splits and sampling strategies (uniform and nonuniform).  
Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration can outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
Applying variance reduction to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, but the results are innovative and technically non-trivial.  
For the bilinear saddle-point case, the paper gives a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms, analogous to the convex case.  
Major concern: computing the proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, creating a fundamental bottleneck for incremental algorithms.  
Consequently, overall complexity (even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling) is not necessarily better than accelerated batch FB.  
In common regimes where n >> d, factored splits (used in simulations) can yield worse complexity than batch FB (Table 2).  
Unlike in convex minimization, the theoretical benefits of variance reduction for these saddle-point problems are not clearly substantial.  
Reviewer asks the authors to discuss: (i) is factored splitting theoretically worse than individual splitting, (ii) when is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB, and (iii) is the n and d dependence tight?  
After the rebuttal the reviewer remained satisfied and left their evaluation scores unchanged.
