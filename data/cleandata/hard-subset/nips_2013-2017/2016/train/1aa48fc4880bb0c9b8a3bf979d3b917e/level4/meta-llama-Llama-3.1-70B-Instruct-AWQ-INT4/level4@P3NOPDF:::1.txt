This paper addresses saddle-point problems with a convex-concave property, leveraging existing stochastic variance reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, coupled with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms in the monotone operator setting, demonstrating linear convergence. Furthermore, they propose an accelerated variant and examine a non-uniform sampling scheme.

The contributions of this paper are significant, with thorough theoretical analysis that provides new insights into saddle-point problems. The authors propose several extensions, most notably that their method applies to non-separable functions.

While the theoretical contributions are commendable, the presentation falls short in terms of accessibility. Clarifying the connection to monotone operators would greatly enhance the paper's readability. Although the analysis relies heavily on monotone operators, this connection appears only as an extension in Section 6.

On the experimental side, the authors should consider including results on more than two datasets. For separable functions, comparisons to accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhao et al., 2015), would be beneficial. The addition of this reference would strengthen the paper.

Several points require clarification:

1. The strength of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A), warrants further discussion. It would be helpful to know whether these assumptions hold for solving the saddle-point problem induced by SVM.
2. The differences between stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed method are not entirely clear. Elaboration on this point would be appreciated.
3. In Theorem 2, the constant μ is not well-defined. Is it the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? A comparison between this result and Theorem 1 would also be beneficial.
4. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is unclear. Do assumptions (A)-(C) imply monotonicity?

Minor issues include:

* Inconsistent usage of the constant L, which is referred to as both the condition number and the Lipschitz constant. A clear distinction between these two definitions is necessary.
* A reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm would be a welcome addition.

Overall, the paper has the potential to make significant contributions to the field, but addressing these issues will enhance its clarity and impact.

References:
Zhao, P., Zhang, T., & Yang, Q. (2015). Stochastic Optimization for Non-convex Inf-Projection Problems. Proceedings of the 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning, 37, 1159–1168.
