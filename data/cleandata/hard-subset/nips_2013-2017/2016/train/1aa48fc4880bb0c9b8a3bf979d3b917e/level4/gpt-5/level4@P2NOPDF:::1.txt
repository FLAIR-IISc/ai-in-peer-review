This paper studies convex-concave saddle-point problems and shows how to solve them using stochastic variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG, SAGA) combined with proximal operators. The authors cast the problem in a monotone operator framework and prove linear convergence. They further present an accelerated variant and analyze a non-uniform sampling strategy.

Novelty and originality:
- The contributions are substantial. The theoretical analysis is careful and yields new insights for saddle-point optimization.
- A key strength is the range of extensions, particularly the capability to handle non-separable functions.

Technical quality and presentation:
- While the theory is strong, the presentation could be improved. The central role of monotone operators is not made sufficiently clear in the main text: it appears as an extension in Section 6, yet the analysis throughout relies on this framework. Bringing this connection forward would make the paper more accessible.
- Experiments are limited to two datasets; broader empirical validation would strengthen the work.
- For separable problems, please compare against accelerated stochastic primal–dual baselines, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle–Pock algorithm (Zhang et al., JMLR W&CP 37, 2015): http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf Please add this reference and include a comparison.

Points requiring clarification:
1) Assumptions A–C: How strong are these assumptions, especially (A)? Do they hold for standard instances such as the SVM-induced saddle-point formulation (e.g., hinge loss with regularization)? Concrete examples would help.
2) Relation to stochastic primal–dual methods: The paper references primal–dual schemes, but the distinctions from your proposed methods are not fully clear. Please elaborate on algorithmic differences (updates, variance reduction mechanisms, use of proximal operators), assumptions required, and convergence rates.
3) Theorem 2 and the constant μ: Is μ the strong monotonicity constant introduced in the appendix? If so, please state this explicitly and clarify how Theorem 2’s rate compares to Theorem 1 under the same condition-number and sampling assumptions.
4) From Assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity: The transition in the appendix is confusing. Do (A)–(C) imply monotonicity or strong monotonicity of the associated operator? Please make the logical implications precise and indicate where additional assumptions are needed.

Minor issues:
- Notation for L: There is a mix-up between using L as a condition number and as a Lipschitz constant. For example, “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” vs. “we need the Lipschitz constant L.” Please disambiguate (e.g., use κ for condition number and L for Lipschitz).
- Forward–Backward algorithm: Please add a standard reference.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: rigorous theoretical development, linear convergence guarantees in a monotone operator setting, extensions including acceleration and non-uniform sampling, and applicability to non-separable problems.
- Weaknesses: exposition obscures the central monotone operator perspective; limited experimental breadth; missing comparisons to strong stochastic primal–dual baselines; several assumptions and constants are not clearly tied between main text and appendix.

With improved exposition clarifying the monotone operator framework, expanded experiments (including additional datasets and comparisons to stochastic Chambolle–Pock–type methods), and clearer statements connecting assumptions, constants, and theorems, this paper would be a strong contribution.
