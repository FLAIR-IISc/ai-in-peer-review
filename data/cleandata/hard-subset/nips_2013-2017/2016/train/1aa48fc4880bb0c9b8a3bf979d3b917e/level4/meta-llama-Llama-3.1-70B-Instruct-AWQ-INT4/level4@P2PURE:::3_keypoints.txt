- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems (individual and factored splits).  
- Provides thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform and nonuniform).  
- Experiments on binary classification show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are key to beating competitive batch algorithms.  
- Results are novel and technically non-trivial despite seeming like straightforward extensions.  
- Clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms for bilinear saddle point problems.  
- Major concern: each iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, creating a bottleneck that can negate complexity gains.  
- When n >> d, overall complexity of accelerated SVRG (even with nonuniform sampling) may not beat accelerated batch methods.  
- Under factored splits, theoretical complexity can be worse than batch FB (per Table 2), questioning practical benefit.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on: (i) whether factored splitting is theoretically worse than individual splitting, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors' responses but did not change scores.
