

The paper extends recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite sum structure, arising from either individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and (uniform/nonuniform) sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems may appear straightforward, the established results are notably novel and technically nontrivial. For the important bilinear saddle point problem, the authors present clear comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, which resemble standard results for convex minimization and are theoretically sound. However, a major concern remains: while solving the saddle point problem addresses the non-separability of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck—specifically, an O(n + d) cost per iteration for computing the proximal operator. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2), is not necessarily superior to the accelerated batch version. Particularly when n (number of data points) substantially exceeds d (dimension), which holds in most machine learning applications, the complexity under factored splits (used in simulations) is actually worse than batch FB algorithms, as demonstrated in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization case, I do not observe a clear theoretical advantage for variance reduction here.
