- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems under individual and factored splits.  
- Provides thorough complexity analysis for both splits and for uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies.  
- Experiments (binary classification) indicate non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to beat competitive batch methods.  
- Results are genuinely novel and technically nontrivial; clear comparisons for the bilinear saddle-point case among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB that mirror convex minimization phenomena.  
- Main concern: computational bottleneck—proximal computation costs O(n + d) per iteration, so overall complexity may not improve over accelerated batch methods.  
- In particular, when n >> d (common in ML) and under factored splits (used in experiments), complexity can be worse than batch FB, casting doubt on theoretical advantage of variance reduction here.  
- Reviewer asks for deeper discussion on: whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits; regimes where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB; and tightness of n and d dependencies.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with authors’ response but keeps same scores.
