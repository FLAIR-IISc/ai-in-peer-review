- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, differing from standard convex minimization approaches.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous proof of linear convergence based on monotone operator properties rather than convexity.  
- Introduces and characterizes two splitting schemes: function-based and partial-derivative-based.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is critical for algorithmic efficiency.  
- Provides an acceleration framework via the "catalyst" method that outperforms accelerated batch algorithms.  
- Builds on prior SVRG/SAGA work while addressing a broader problem class than typical convex minimization.  
- Monotone-operator framing extends applicability to variational inequalities beyond saddle-point problems.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and represents a significant advance in saddle-point optimization.  
- Practical insights (splitting schemes, non-uniform sampling) fill an important gap in literature.  
- Acceleration framework is well-motivated and offers clear theoretical advantages.  
- Paper includes compelling ML examples (non-separable losses, robust optimization, convex relaxations).  
- Weakness: monotone operator perspective is introduced late and should be integrated cohesively throughout the paper.  
- Weakness: experimental validation is limited to two datasets (sido, rcv1) and is insufficiently diverse.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to relevant stochastic primal-dual baselines, notably stochastic Chambolle-Pock [32].  
- Technical clarification needed: whether assumptions (A)-(C) hold for standard SVM saddle-point formulations.  
- Technical clarification needed: better explain relationship to existing stochastic primal-dual methods [11,12,32] and how variance reduction differs.  
- Theorem 2 references a constant μ that appears undefined and should be tied to monotonicity properties in the appendix.  
- Notation ambiguity: L is used inconsistently as both condition number and Lipschitz constant and should be clarified.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with practical implications; recommend integrating monotone-operator view, expanding experiments and baselines, and resolving technical ambiguities.
