The authors adapt variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to obtain the first algorithms with linear convergence for saddle-point problems. Because standard convex minimization analyses do not carry over to saddle-point settings, the main contribution is a convergence analysis based on monotone operators. As a result, the analysis applies to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. By casting problems as saddle-point formulations, the method can also handle non-separable losses and regularizers—an important capability for many machine learning models.

The proof technique—interpreting saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator—is interesting and potentially widely applicable. It also yields simpler proofs for methods such as forward–backward (FB) splitting and its stochastic counterpart. Empirically, the proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling are competitive and, in most cases, outperform previous alternatives. Overall, the paper advances the field in a meaningful direction, and the analysis approach is compelling.

Since the main novelty lies in the proof technique (the algorithms themselves are fairly direct extensions of existing variance-reduction methods), the authors might consider adding more details about the analysis—e.g., a proof sketch—in the main paper, if space permits. The presentation is mostly clear and well-written, though a few points could be clarified:

- What form of non-uniform sampling is used for problems beyond bilinear saddle-point instances; specifically, what is the distribution π?
- How is the step size σ selected in the experiments?
- Do the authors have intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization? A discussion of this would strengthen the paper.
