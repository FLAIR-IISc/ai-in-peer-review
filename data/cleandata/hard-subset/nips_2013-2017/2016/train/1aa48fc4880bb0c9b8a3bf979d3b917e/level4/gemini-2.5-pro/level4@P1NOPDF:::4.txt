This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, proving that the proposed algorithms achieve a linear convergence rate. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of applying variance reduction to this class of problems.

The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of challenging non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., in structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., a fusion term) may be non-separable. A key contribution of this work lies in its convergence analysis. In contrast to previous analyses for convex optimization, the saddle-point setting is considerably more complex. The authors successfully address this by leveraging the theory of monotone operators to establish convergence. This analytical framework is also more general, showing that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms can be applied to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities in game theory.

Algorithmically, the authors integrate variance reduction into the stochastic forward-backward algorithm, which is commonly used for saddle-point problems. They propose two distinct splitting schemes for the gradient operator—an element-wise split and a factored split—for use with SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The paper also explores the use of non-uniform sampling, providing both theoretical and empirical evidence that it outperforms standard uniform sampling.

The experimental results, presented in both the main paper and the appendix, are comprehensive. They provide strong support for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems involving non-separable loss and regularization terms.

**Minor Points and Questions:**

1.  Regarding the acceleration technique in Section 5, the authors follow [8] by incorporating an additional regularization term to bias the update towards a reference iterate, $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. It is not clear how this reference point should be updated adaptively to achieve the desired speed-up.
2.  A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. Do similar theoretical guarantees hold for SAGA?
3.  For improved clarity, the notation could be revised. Specifically, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should be represented by different symbols to avoid potential confusion.
