

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence. The authors demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that their variance reduction approach effectively addresses saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms apply to a broad class of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion term) may be non-separable. A key contribution lies in the convergence analysis, which employs monotone operators to handle the significantly more complex case of saddle-point problems—unlike prior work focused on convex problems. This analysis further establishes that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms encompass a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory. The authors propose two distinct gradient operator splits for their stochastic approach: an element-wise split for SVRG and a factored split for SAGA. Variance reduction is applied to stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems, with non-uniform sampling also considered. Both theoretical and empirical results confirm that non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform sampling. Experimental results in the main text and appendix provide sufficient evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrate the proposed methods' effectiveness on non-separable loss and regularizer settings. Minor comments: For the acceleration technique in Section 5, the authors follow [8] by incorporating an additional regularization term to align updates with an iterate point (\bar{x}, \bar{y}). It is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate point to achieve speed-up. While a convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, it remains uncertain whether identical theoretical results hold for SAGA. Additionally, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should use distinct symbols to avoid confusion.
