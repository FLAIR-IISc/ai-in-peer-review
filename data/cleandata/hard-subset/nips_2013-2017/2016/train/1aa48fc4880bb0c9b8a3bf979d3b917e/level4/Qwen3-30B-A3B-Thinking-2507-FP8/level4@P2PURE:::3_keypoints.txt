- Extends incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants) to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems with individual and factored splits.
- Provides thorough complexity analysis under different data splits and (uniform/nonuniform) sampling strategies.
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform competitive batch algorithms.
- Results are novel and technically nontrivial despite appearing like straightforward extensions.
- For bilinear saddle problems, presents clear, theoretically sound comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms.
- Major concern: incremental methods incur an O(n + d) cost per iteration to compute the proximal operator, creating a fundamental bottleneck.
- As a result, accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling may not improve overall complexity versus accelerated batch methods.
- In typical regimes with n >> d, factored splits (used in simulations) can yield worse complexity than batch FB algorithms (as shown in Table 2).
- Unlike convex minimization, the reviewer sees no clear theoretical advantage for variance reduction in this saddle-point setting.
- Requests detailed discussion on: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (ii) when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.
