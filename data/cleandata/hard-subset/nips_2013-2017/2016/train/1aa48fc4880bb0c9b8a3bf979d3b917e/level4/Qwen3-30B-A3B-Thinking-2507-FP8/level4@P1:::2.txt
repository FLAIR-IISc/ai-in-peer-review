

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as previous variance reduction techniques were limited to convex minimization problems. The work builds on the recent advances in stochastic optimization (SAG [1], SVRG [2], SAGA [3]) but extends them to the more general saddle-point setting. Notably, the authors demonstrate that the saddle-point formulation allows for two distinct notions of splitting (in terms of functions or partial derivatives), requires splits into convex-concave terms, and shows that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. The paper also extends these methods to monotone operators, demonstrating broader applicability beyond saddle-point problems, and provides a simple acceleration technique using the catalyst framework [7].

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, the theoretical contribution is significant: the authors provide the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods in the saddle-point setting, using monotone operator theory rather than the convex minimization framework. This approach reveals that the algorithms actually apply to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities. Second, the paper carefully distinguishes between different splitting strategies (function-based vs. derivative-based) and demonstrates why non-uniform sampling is theoretically and practically superior to uniform sampling. Third, the acceleration technique via the catalyst framework is elegant and effectively shows that the proposed methods outperform accelerated batch algorithms. Finally, the experimental section provides convincing evidence of the method's efficiency on both synthetic and real-world problems.

However, the paper contains one significant technical issue that needs clarification. The authors assume strong convexity-concavity in both the primal and dual variables (assumption (A)), but this is overly restrictive. In standard saddle-point problems, it is sufficient for either the primal or dual problem to be strongly convex (not both), and this is well-established in the literature on saddle-point methods [4, 8]. For example, in bilinear saddle-point problems min_x max_y y^T Kx, strong convexity in x or y is sufficient, but not necessarily in both. The authors should clarify whether their strong convexity-concavity assumption is necessary for their analysis, or whether it can be relaxed to the standard assumption of strong convexity in one variable. This is particularly important because the paper claims to provide the first linearly convergent methods for this class of problems, but the strong convexity assumption may be unnecessarily restrictive.

Additionally, while the experimental section is generally convincing, it would be strengthened by including more diverse examples where non-separable losses or regularizers are critical. The current experiments focus on a single non-separable regularizer (clustering-inducing term) and a single non-separable loss (ROC curve surrogate), but additional examples would better demonstrate the generality of their approach. The comparison with primal methods on the separable RCv1 dataset is useful, but more experiments on non-separable problems would better highlight the paper's contribution.

The paper's presentation is generally clear, though some technical details in the analysis could be made more accessible. The connection between the saddle-point formulation and monotone operators is well-motivated, but the transition to the general monotone operator setting in Section 6 could be smoother. The algorithms (SVRG and SAGA for saddle points) are described clearly with pseudocode, which is helpful for reproducibility.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is sound and demonstrates important extensions beyond previous work, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The main concern is the unnecessarily strong convexity assumption, which should be clarified or relaxed. With this clarification, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

Pro: 
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for saddle-point problems
- Novel convergence analysis using monotone operators
- Clear distinction between different splitting strategies
- Effective acceleration via catalyst framework
- Strong experimental validation

Con:
- Overly strong convexity assumption (requires both primal and dual to be strongly convex, when standard is one or the other)
- Limited number of non-separable examples in experiments
- Some technical transitions could be smoother

Overall, this paper addresses an important gap in the literature and makes a solid theoretical contribution. With the clarification of the convexity assumption, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, it is a good paper that should be accepted with minor revisions.
