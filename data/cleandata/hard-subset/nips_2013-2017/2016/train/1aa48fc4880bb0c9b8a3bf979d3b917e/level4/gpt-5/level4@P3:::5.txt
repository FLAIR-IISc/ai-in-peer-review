Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems in which the smooth part K can be decomposed into many components. It extends stochastic variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to saddle-point settings and provides the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for this class. The key technical novelty is to analyze convergence through the lens of monotone operators rather than convex minimization arguments, thereby covering a broader class including certain variational inequalities.

Algorithmically, the authors adapt forward–backward splitting (FB) to stochastic variance-reduced updates for saddle points, propose both SAGA- and SVRG-style estimators with non-uniform sampling, and show how to accelerate via a “catalyst” proximal-point scheme tailored to saddle points. They also highlight two ways to “split” the operator: (i) by functions, and (ii) by partial derivatives (a factored split, e.g., rows/columns for bilinear forms), with corresponding storage and sampling implications. For bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, they provide sampling schemes with bounds in terms of ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and ‖K‖max, and demonstrate that non-uniform sampling leads to strictly better convergence constants than uniform sampling or batch methods. Empirically, the methods compare favorably to batch FB and its accelerated variants, with accelerated SVRG + non-uniform sampling performing best, particularly on ill-conditioned problems.

This work connects to and extends several lines of literature: SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization (Le Roux et al.; Johnson & Zhang; Defazio et al.), primal–dual/FB methods for saddle problems (Chambolle–Pock; Rockafellar; Chen–Rockafellar), stochastic FB (Rosasco–Villa–Vũ), and Catalyst acceleration (Lin–Mairal–Harchaoui). The analysis goes beyond co-coercive settings common in convex minimization and does not require each component operator to be monotone with a known resolvent, distinguishing it from earlier monotone-root-finding work.

Strengths:
- Original analysis framework: The monotone-operator viewpoint yields the first linear convergence guarantees for variance-reduced incremental methods in saddle-point problems and extends to variational inequalities.
- Practical relevance: Addresses non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient proximals, a regime where standard finite-sum primal or dual methods may not apply.
- Clear algorithmic design: SVRG/SAGA extensions are natural, and the paper explains both “individual” and “factored” splits, their storage needs, and their impact on sampling and complexity.
- Non-uniform sampling: Provides principled schemes with sharper constants than uniform sampling; the empirical results corroborate theory.
- Acceleration: A simple Catalyst-style proximal-point wrapping improves rates without requiring the full Nesterov machinery; results indicate superiority to accelerated batch counterparts under typical conditions.
- Empirical validation: Experiments on relevant datasets show consistent speedups, especially in ill-conditioned regimes.

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
Quality
- The algorithms themselves are straightforward adaptations; the novelty lies mainly in analysis and sampling/acceleration. This is fine, but it puts extra weight on the clarity and completeness of the theoretical exposition.
- The step-size and sampling choices in experiments could be better documented. It would help reproducibility to specify how σ and probabilities π were set in each dataset/setting, including any tuning.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step for the stated guarantees; experiments avoid resampling via a mixture scheme. Please clarify the precise sampling used and whether theory can be extended to that practical variant.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but a brief proof sketch in the main text for one of the core results (e.g., Theorem 1) would significantly aid readers. In particular, outline how monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity are combined to bound the variance-reduced iterates without co-coercivity.
- The two splitting notions are important; a small figure or table summarizing storage, per-iteration cost, and preferred sampling for each split would improve readability.

Originality
- The shift from convex-minimization analysis to monotone-operator analysis in a variance-reduction context for saddle points is novel and impactful. The careful handling of non-uniform sampling and Catalyst-style acceleration in this setting is also original.

Significance
- The results are likely to be used by researchers working on large-scale saddle-point and variational inequality problems, and they advance the state of the art in both theory and practice. The ability to handle non-separable structures with linear convergence is especially significant.

Questions for the authors:
1) Non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear K: For general (non-bilinear) saddle-point problems, what concrete π choices do you recommend? Can you provide a computable surrogate for L̄(π) that leads to practical π without excessive preprocessing?
2) Step-size selection: How is σ set in the experiments? Is σ based on upper bounds (L, L̄(π)) or tuned? Any heuristics for estimating L̄(π) efficiently?
3) Why non-uniform sampling helps more here: Do you have intuition for why non-uniform sampling is “essential” in saddle-point settings (vs. sometimes optional in convex minimization)? Is it tied to the two-sided coupling (x,y) or to the lack of co-coercivity?
4) Catalyst parameters: Could you provide a short guideline for selecting τ in practice, especially when Tprox(M) is non-negligible?

Pros for acceptance:
- First linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for saddle points with solid theory.
- Monotone-operator analysis broadens scope (variational inequalities).
- Practical, implementable sampling/acceleration strategies with strong empirical gains.
- Relevant to many ML problems with non-separable structures.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental details (σ, π choices, resampling) need clearer documentation.
- Main text could include more intuition/proof sketches to guide readers.
- Dependence on known strong convexity–concavity parameters; adaptivity is left to future work.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a concise proof sketch for a main theorem and a brief discussion contrasting co-coercivity-based analyses with the current monotone-operator approach.
- Provide a practical recipe for π and σ selection, including estimates for L and L̄(π), and discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Clarify the resampling requirement for SAGA under non-uniform sampling and the exact experimental protocol.
- Include a small ablation showing uniform vs non-uniform sampling and factored vs individual splits in terms of constants and wall-clock time.

Minor comments:
- Typo line 78: “k” should be “d”.
- Typo line 121: extra y_{t−1} term in the second equality.
- Notation: define Ω and Ω* early and keep consistent scaling of λ, γ across sections.
- When possible, report wall-clock time in addition to passes for the empirical plots, given the varying per-iteration costs across methods.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong paper with clear theoretical and practical contributions, extending variance-reduction to an important class of problems with linear rates and principled acceleration. With minor clarifications and additional experimental details, it would be a valuable addition to NIPS. Recommended for acceptance.
