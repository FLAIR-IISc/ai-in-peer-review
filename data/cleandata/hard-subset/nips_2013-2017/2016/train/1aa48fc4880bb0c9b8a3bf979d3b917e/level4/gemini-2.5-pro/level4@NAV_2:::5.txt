This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. This is a valuable contribution as it provides the first large-scale algorithms with proven linear convergence rates for this problem class, which is common in machine learning applications involving non-separable losses or regularizers. The core technical novelty lies not in the algorithms themselves, which are direct analogues of their convex minimization counterparts, but in the convergence analysis. The authors adeptly employ the theory of monotone operators to establish convergence, a technique that is more general than standard convex analysis and allows the results to extend to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper also explores practical aspects like the importance of non-uniform sampling and demonstrates how to accelerate these methods using the "catalyst" framework.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The work addresses an important class of optimization problems that arise frequently in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, structured output prediction) but for which large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms were previously lacking. This paper fills a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The primary contribution is the novel convergence proof rooted in monotone operator theory. This is a departure from the analyses of SVRG/SAGA in the convex setting and provides a powerful analytical tool. The generality of this approach, which covers variational inequalities, further highlights its value.
*   **Performance:** The proposed algorithms, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, are shown empirically to be highly competitive and to significantly outperform existing batch and stochastic methods on relevant machine learning tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper also provides simplified convergence proofs for some existing batch methods, such as forward-backward splitting, which is a useful pedagogical contribution.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Given that the main technical innovation is the proof technique, the paper would be strengthened by including a proof sketch or providing more intuition about the monotone operator analysis in the main body of the text, space permitting. This would help readers better appreciate the core contribution.
*   The paper's clarity could be improved on a few points:
    1.  The strategy for non-uniform sampling is well-defined for the bilinear case, but it is unclear how the probabilities πi would be chosen for more general saddle-point problems. Providing some guidance on this would be beneficial.
    2.  The experimental section would be more reproducible if details on the selection of hyperparameters, such as the step-size σ, were included.
    3.  The experiments convincingly show that non-uniform sampling is critical for good performance. A discussion explaining why this appears to be more essential for saddle-point problems than for standard convex minimization would offer valuable insight to the reader.
