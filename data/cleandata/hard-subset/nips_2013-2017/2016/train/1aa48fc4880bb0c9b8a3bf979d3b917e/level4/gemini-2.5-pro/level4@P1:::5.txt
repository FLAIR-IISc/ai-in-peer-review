This paper extends stochastic variance reduction algorithms, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to solve large-scale convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors propose the first incremental algorithms for this problem class that are proven to converge linearly. A key contribution is the convergence analysis, which deviates from standard techniques used for convex minimization. Instead, the authors leverage the theory of monotone operators to establish their results, interpreting the saddle-point problem as one of finding the zeros of a monotone operator. A significant consequence of this approach is that the analysis and algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. This framework is particularly relevant for machine learning problems involving non-separable loss functions or regularizers, which can be naturally formulated as saddle-point problems but are challenging for existing primal or dual variance-reduced methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems. This is an important contribution, as it provides an effective way to handle non-separable losses and regularizers (e.g., for AUC maximization or structured-output prediction) that arise in many machine learning models. The work significantly extends the reach of variance-reduction techniques to a new and important class of problems.
*   **Novel Analysis:** The core theoretical contribution is the novel proof technique based on monotone operator theory. This approach is elegant and powerful, as it circumvents the limitations of standard convex analysis for this problem setting. Its generality also means the results apply beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, which may be of independent interest. The paper also provides simplified proofs for existing batch algorithms like forward-backward splitting.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results effectively demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, particularly when combined with non-uniform sampling and the "catalyst" acceleration scheme, are highly competitive and often outperform existing batch and stochastic alternatives.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly positions its contributions within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The paper is well-executed, but there are some points that could be clarified to further improve its impact.

1.  **Exposition of the Proof Technique:** While the algorithms themselves are straightforward extensions of SVRG/SAGA, the main novelty lies in the convergence analysis. The main text would benefit from a more detailed exposition of this proof technique. For instance, including a high-level proof sketch could provide readers with better intuition for why the analysis based on monotone operators succeeds where a standard convex analysis would fail.

2.  **Generality of Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper provides a detailed discussion of non-uniform sampling probabilities ($\pi_i$) for bilinear problems. However, it is less clear how these should be defined for more general saddle-point problems. Could the authors provide guidance on choosing an effective non-uniform sampling scheme in the general case?

3.  **Experimental Details:** Could the authors clarify how the step-size ($\sigma$) was selected in the experiments? Was it tuned, or was the theoretical value used directly?

4.  **Intuition on Non-Uniform Sampling:** The experiments highlight the critical importance of non-uniform sampling for achieving strong performance, noting that uniform sampling can be inferior even to accelerated batch methods. This seems to contrast with the standard convex minimization setting, where uniform sampling is often a strong baseline. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the intuition behind why non-uniform sampling is particularly crucial for the structure of saddle-point problems.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 78: In the description of the "factored" split for bilinear problems, "k-th column of K" suggests the sum should be over $k=1, ..., d$, not $k=1, ..., k$.
*   Line 121: The equation for the accelerated forward-backward update for $y_t$ seems to have an extra $y_{t-1}$ term inside the `prox` operator.
