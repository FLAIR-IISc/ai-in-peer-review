This paper addresses large-scale convex-concave saddle-point problems by adapting stochastic variance reduced gradient techniques (SVRG and SAGA) for use with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms within the monotone operator framework, proving linear convergence. The work is further extended to include an accelerated variant and an analysis of a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty and Originality**

The paper's contributions are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides valuable new insights into solving saddle-point problems using variance-reduced methods. A particularly strong contribution is the extension of the method and analysis to non-separable objective functions, which broadens the applicability of these techniques beyond what many existing methods can handle.

**Technical Quality and Clarity**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the presentation could be significantly improved. My main concern is with the paper's accessibility. The core analysis relies on the theory of monotone operators, yet this connection is not made clear until Section 6, where it is presented as an extension. This organizational choice makes the paper difficult to follow, as the main analytical tools are introduced too late. Introducing the monotone operator framework earlier and using it to motivate the algorithm and analysis would substantially improve the paper's clarity.

The experimental validation also feels preliminary and could be strengthened.
*   Results are presented on only two datasets, which is insufficient to make a compelling case for the method's practical advantages across a range of problems.
*   For the case of separable functions, a crucial comparison is missing. The authors should compare their approach against accelerated stochastic primal-dual methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm. This would provide a more complete picture of the proposed algorithm's performance relative to the state-of-the-art. I recommend comparing against:
    *   Zhang, Y., & Xiao, L. (2015). Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization. *Proceedings of the 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning (JMLR W&CP)*, 37, 353–361.

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:

1.  How restrictive are assumptions (A)-(C), particularly assumption (A)? For instance, does the saddle-point formulation of a standard Support Vector Machine (SVM) satisfy these assumptions? Providing such a canonical example would help readers gauge the applicability of the framework.

2.  Could you elaborate on the key differences between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual methods? A more detailed comparison would help to position the contribution more clearly within the existing literature.

3.  In Theorem 2, what does the constant `μ` represent? Is it the strong monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? If so, how does the convergence rate in Theorem 2 compare to the rate in Theorem 1? A direct comparison of the rates under their respective assumptions would be very helpful.

4.  The transition in the appendix from assumptions (A)-(C) to the property of strong monotonicity is confusing. Do assumptions (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity? Clarifying this logical link is essential for understanding the paper's theoretical foundation.

**Minor Issues**

*   There appears to be some notational confusion with the constant `L`, which is referred to as both the condition number ("The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem") and a Lipschitz constant ("we need the Lipschitz constant L"). Please use consistent notation.
*   Please add a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm when it is first mentioned.
