- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for convergence proofs, showing applicability beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.  
- Identifies two ways to split the gradient operator for saddle-points: element-wise splits and factored splits exploiting bilinear structure.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for theoretical and empirical efficiency gains.  
- Introduces a simple catalyst-style acceleration that yields algorithms outperforming accelerated batch methods.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear proofs of linear convergence rates.  
- Empirical validation is comprehensive across datasets and problem types, showing strong performance, especially on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Paper clarity and organization are excellent, with reproducible detail and well-chosen notation overall.  
- Originality is significant: novel extension of variance reduction methods to non-separable saddle-point objectives.  
- Significance is high due to applicability to important ML problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.  
- Minor weakness: adaptive update frequency of the center iterate (x̄,ȳ) in the acceleration section is not fully explained for practical use.  
- Minor weakness: the manuscript uses K for both a smooth function and a design matrix, causing notation confusion.  
- Minor weakness: convergence with acceleration is proved for SVRG, but the analogous theoretical statement for SAGA is not explicitly confirmed.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — substantial technical and practical contribution to optimization for machine learning.
