- Addresses minimizing convex-concave saddle point problems by extending variance reduction techniques and providing convergence analysis.
- Introduces Catalyst acceleration for the proposed methods and extends the approach to monotone operator settings, with supporting experiments.
- Core contribution: adaptation and analysis of variance reduction methods for difficult convex-concave saddle-point problems.
- Catalyst acceleration is a valuable addition that can yield practical speedups.
- Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability.
- Major limitation: requires strong convexity on both primal and dual, which is overly restrictive for many applications.
- Authors should relax the strong convexity requirement to only primal or dual, or justify why both are necessary and discuss implications when unmet.
- Convergence analysis appears sound but needs more detailed exposition of key steps and explicit dependence on problem parameters (condition number, Lipschitz constants).
- Numerical evaluation is limited and lacks comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art solvers (e.g., ADMM, FISTA variants, accelerated/extrapolated methods).
- Experiments should include larger-scale problems to demonstrate scalability.
- Require sensitivity analysis of method parameters and their effect on convergence.
- Need tests on a more diverse set of problems (varying ill-conditioning and sizes) to show robustness.
- Must quantify the empirical speedup from Catalyst acceleration rather than just claiming it works.
- Strengths: novel variance-reduction extension, effective Catalyst use, monotone-operator extension, and clear convergence results.
- Weaknesses: restrictive strong convexity assumptions, limited empirical evaluation, and insufficient detail on convergence-rate dependence.
- Overall: solid theoretical contribution but impact limited unless strong convexity concerns are addressed and numerical experiments are substantially expanded.
- Recommendation: currently a weak accept contingent on major revisions addressing strong convexity and expanding empirical comparisons.
