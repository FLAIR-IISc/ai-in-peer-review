

The paper extends recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite-sum structure, arising from two split types: individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform/non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming competitive batch methods. While extending variance reduction techniques to saddle point problems may seem straightforward, the resulting theoretical contributions are notably novel and technically non-trivial. For bilinear saddle point problems, the authors offer clear comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, drawing parallels consistent with convex minimization theory—these parallels are well-motivated and compelling.

My primary concern, however, lies in a fundamental limitation of the proposed incremental algorithms: the O(n + d) per-iteration cost for computing the proximal operator. This bottleneck undermines the theoretical advantage of variance reduction in high-dimensional settings. As shown in Tables 1 and 2, the overall complexity of even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling does not necessarily improve over accelerated batch FB methods. Crucially, when n (data size) is substantially larger than d (dimension)—the typical scenario in machine learning—the complexity under factored splits (used in experiments) is actually worse than batch FB algorithms, as explicitly demonstrated in Table 2. Unlike convex minimization, where variance reduction typically yields clear theoretical gains, the benefit here is not evident from a complexity perspective. 

I urge the authors to address three key theoretical questions:  
(i) Is the factored split theoretically inferior to the individual split?  
(ii) Under what conditions does accelerated SVRG outperform batch FB?  
(iii) Are the dependencies on n and d tight?  

The post-rebuttal response satisfactorily addresses these concerns, and I maintain my original assessment.
