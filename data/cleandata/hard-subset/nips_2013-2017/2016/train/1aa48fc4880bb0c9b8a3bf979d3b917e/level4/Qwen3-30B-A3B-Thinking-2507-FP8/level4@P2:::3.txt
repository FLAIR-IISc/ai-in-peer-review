

This paper extends recent stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their accelerated variants to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite sum structure, a class of problems that arise naturally in machine learning through duality. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis using monotone operators, which differs significantly from standard convex minimization analysis. They demonstrate that these incremental algorithms can be applied to a broader class of problems beyond saddle-point problems, including variational inequalities. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a convergence analysis for saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory, (2) a detailed exploration of two types of splits (function-based and derivative-based) with non-uniform sampling, and (3) an acceleration framework using the Catalyst approach that improves upon batch methods.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through its rigorous convergence proofs and thorough complexity analysis. The authors establish linear convergence rates for both SVRG and SAGA variants under different sampling schemes, with clear theoretical comparisons to batch methods. Their analysis extends beyond previous work by properly handling the saddle-point structure, which requires convex-concave splits rather than the convex splits used in standard optimization. The experimental section provides compelling evidence that non-uniform sampling and acceleration significantly improve performance, particularly for ill-conditioned problems, as shown through experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses and regularizers.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature by addressing a gap in the application of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic extension may seem straightforward, the theoretical challenges are non-trivial, as the convex minimization analysis does not apply. The authors demonstrate how to properly adapt these methods to the saddle-point structure using monotone operator theory, which represents an important advancement over previous work on separable saddle-point problems [11, 12]. The paper's theoretical contributions are particularly valuable as they generalize to variational inequalities, expanding the applicability of these methods beyond saddle-point problems.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections that clearly present the theoretical framework and experimental results. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, though some of the technical notation could be made more accessible. The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical implementation is well-explained, particularly in the discussion of factored versus individual splits.

The significance of the paper is evident in its practical applications to machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors demonstrate that their methods are the only ones applicable to large-scale linearly convergent optimization for these specific problem structures, which are common in real-world applications such as robust optimization and structured output prediction. The experimental results on binary classification with non-separable regularizers and loss functions provide strong empirical validation of their theoretical claims.

However, I have concerns about the theoretical advantage of the proposed methods in practical settings. While the paper shows that accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling theoretically improves upon batch methods, the computational complexity analysis reveals a potential bottleneck. For factored splits (which are used in the experiments), the complexity is O(n+d) per iteration for the proximal operator computation, which may not provide a clear advantage over batch methods when n >> d, as is common in machine learning applications. Specifically, the paper states that "accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is the most efficient method," but the complexity analysis shows that for factored splits, the complexity is proportional to [T(B) + L̄²(1 + τ)⁻¹ maxᵢ T(Bᵢ)] where T(B) is proportional to n+d. When n >> d, this becomes dominated by n, making it potentially less efficient than batch methods that may have lower constant factors. This contradicts the intuition from convex optimization where variance reduction typically provides significant speedups. The authors should clarify when the proposed methods are theoretically advantageous over batch methods and provide a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs.

I recommend the following clarifications:
1) The paper should provide a more detailed comparison of factored versus individual splits, particularly regarding their theoretical complexity advantages.
2) The authors should explain under what conditions (e.g., specific n/d ratios, problem structures) the proposed methods provide a clear theoretical advantage.
3) The paper should discuss whether the dependence on n and d in the complexity bounds is tight, or if there are ways to reduce this dependence.

In summary, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, with solid theoretical foundations and promising experimental results. The main concern is the theoretical advantage of the proposed methods in practical settings, particularly for large-scale machine learning problems where n >> d. If the authors can provide a more detailed explanation of when and why their methods are advantageous, this would significantly strengthen the paper.

Pros: Novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, rigorous theoretical analysis using monotone operators, clear experimental validation of key claims, important applications to non-separable machine learning problems.

Cons: Theoretical advantage over batch methods is not clearly established for many practical scenarios, complexity analysis does not fully demonstrate clear advantages for common machine learning applications where n >> d, potential bottleneck in proximal operator computation.
