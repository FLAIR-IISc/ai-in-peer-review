This paper presents the first linearly convergent algorithms for solving saddle point problems, leveraging variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA. A key contribution is the application of monotone operator theory to analyze these problems, a necessary departure from standard convex minimization techniques which do not directly apply to saddle points. This innovative approach not only establishes convergence for saddle point problems but also generalizes to variational inequality problems, broadening the scope of the analysis. The ability to recast non-separable loss functions and regularizers as saddle point problems is particularly valuable, as such structures appear frequently in modern machine learning models. 

The authors convincingly demonstrate the importance of this work by highlighting the limitations of existing methods for these problems and establishing a significant theoretical advance: algorithms with provable linear convergence rates. The paper’s theoretical contribution is substantial, as the reinterpretation of saddle point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators offers a potentially widely applicable and insightful perspective. Notably, the authors also provide simplified proofs for foundational algorithms like F-B splitting (and its stochastic variant), adding to the overall value of the work.  Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, employing non-uniform sampling strategies, achieve competitive or superior performance compared to existing alternatives. 

Overall, this paper represents a significant extension of the field, offering a novel and powerful analytical approach to a challenging problem. While the algorithms themselves build upon existing variance reduction methods, the core innovation lies in the convergence analysis facilitated by the monotone operator framework.  To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the authors consider including a proof sketch, if space allows, to provide readers with a more concrete understanding of the key theoretical arguments. This would enhance accessibility and potentially broaden the impact of their work.

However, some aspects of the paper could benefit from clarification. Specifically:

1. **Non-uniform Sampling for General Saddle Point Problems:** The paper details the non-uniform sampling distribution (\pi) for bilinear saddle-point problems.  However, it lacks specifics regarding how this sampling is adapted – or if a different strategy is used – for more general saddle point problems. Expanding on this point would significantly improve clarity.

2. **Step Size Selection:** The experimental section would be strengthened by providing details on how the step size (\sigma) was selected.  Justification for the chosen values and sensitivity analysis (if performed) would be valuable.

3. **Intuition for Non-Uniform Sampling:**  The authors observe that non-uniform sampling appears crucial for saddle point problems, but less so for convex minimization.  A discussion exploring the potential reasons for this difference would be highly insightful.  Is it related to the geometry of the saddle point landscape, or to the nature of the monotone operator? 

Finally, I note a few minor points:

1. There is a typo on line 78, where “k” should be “d”.
2. On line 121, the second equality contains an extra  "y_{t-1}" term and should be corrected.



Despite these minor issues, the paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The contribution is significant, and I believe this work will be highly influential in the area of saddle point optimization.
