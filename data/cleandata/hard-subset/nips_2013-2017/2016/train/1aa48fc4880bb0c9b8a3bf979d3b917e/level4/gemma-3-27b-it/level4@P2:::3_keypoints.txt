- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated methods to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored data splits.
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis for different split types and sampling strategies (uniform vs non-uniform).
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are important to beat competitive batch algorithms.
- Extending variance-reduction/incremental methods to saddle-points is conceptually straightforward but the results are novel and technically demanding.
- For bilinear saddle-point problems the paper gives a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms, consistent with convex minimization results.
- Major concern: proximal operator cost is O(n + d) per iteration, making overall complexity potentially worse than accelerated batch methods.
- When n >> d (common in ML) the complexity under factored splits (used in simulations) appears worse than batch FB, undermining practical advantage.
- Reviewer does not see a clear substantial theoretical benefit to variance reduction in this setting, unlike in convex minimization.
- Requests clarification: (i) is factored split inherently less efficient than individual split?
- Requests clarification: (ii) under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB?
- Requests clarification: (iii) are the stated dependencies on n and d sufficiently tight?
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the rebuttal but keeps the same scores.
