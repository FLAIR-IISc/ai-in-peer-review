

This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems with a separable structure, extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors demonstrate that these algorithms can be analyzed using monotone operator theory, which allows them to establish linear convergence guarantees and extend their applicability beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The work builds upon previous NIPS contributions in stochastic optimization (such as SAGA [3] and SVRG [2]) while addressing a significant gap in the literature for saddle-point problems. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of different split structures (function vs. partial derivative splits), the necessity of convex-concave splits, the importance of non-uniform sampling, and an acceleration framework using the "catalyst" approach.

Strengths: The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-motivated. The paper provides the first convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction methods applied to saddle-point problems, revealing important distinctions from convex minimization analysis. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly valuable, showing that the framework applies to a broader class of problems than just saddle-point problems. The accelerated variant using the catalyst framework is well-justified and provides a clear theoretical advantage over batch methods. The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly with non-uniform sampling, and provide a convincing comparison to existing approaches.

Weaknesses: The paper would benefit from a more coherent presentation of the monotone operator framework, which is currently treated as an afterthought in Section 6 despite being fundamental to the theoretical analysis. The experimental section is limited to two datasets, which is insufficient to establish the generality of the proposed approach. For the separable case, the comparison to accelerated methods like the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm [32] would strengthen the claims, as the current comparison to primal methods does not fully address the question of whether the proposed approach provides advantages over existing specialized methods for separable problems.

Specific points for clarification: 
1. Assumption (A) regarding strong convex-concavity requires further justification. The authors should explicitly verify whether their assumptions hold for common saddle-point problems such as those induced by SVMs, where the regularizer is typically separable.
2. The relationship between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual methods needs elaboration. The paper should clearly distinguish how this approach differs from and improves upon existing stochastic primal-dual frameworks.
3. The constant μ in Theorem 2 is not well-defined. It appears to be related to monotonicity but is not clearly connected to the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix. The authors should clarify its definition and relationship to the problem parameters.
4. The connection between assumptions (A)-(C) and the strong monotonicity required for the convergence analysis in the appendix needs to be made explicit. It is unclear whether assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity, which is a critical point for the analysis.

Minor issues: There is some confusion in the notation regarding the constant L, which is described as both the condition number of the problem and the Lipschitz constant. The Forward-Backward algorithm section should include a proper reference to the original work on this method.
