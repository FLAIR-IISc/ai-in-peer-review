Summary
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems and extends recent variance-reduction techniques to this setting. It provides convergence analyses for the proposed algorithms, shows how to accelerate them via an adaptation of the Catalyst framework, and sketches an extension to general monotone operators. Empirical results are included.

Strengths
- Timely topic: variance reduction for saddle-point and monotone operator problems is of high interest, with clear applications in machine learning and optimization.
- Theoretical contributions: the paper provides convergence guarantees and discusses how to incorporate Catalyst-style acceleration, which is nontrivial in primal–dual contexts.
- Generality: the claimed extension to monotone operators, if rigorous and practical, broadens the applicability beyond standard bilinear or structured saddle problems.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions: Requiring strong convexity on both the primal and the dual sides appears unnecessarily restrictive for many applications. In practice, one often has strong convexity on only one side (or neither). The results would be significantly more impactful if they covered (i) strong convexity on only the primal or only the dual variable, and (ii) merely convex–concave problems with appropriate rates (e.g., sublinear).
- Clarity of contributions relative to prior work: It is currently hard to pinpoint what is fundamentally new versus what is a synthesis of known variance-reduction schemes in the primal–dual setting. The relation to existing stochastic primal–dual and extragradient-type methods (and to SVRG/SAGA-style primal–dual variants) should be more explicitly discussed, including similarities, differences, and where the proposed analysis strictly improves known results.
- Catalyst acceleration details: While the paper states that acceleration can be “easily” achieved using Catalyst, the practical and theoretical details matter. The overhead of inner loops, parameter choices (regularization strength, number of inner iterations), and how inexactness affects the outer convergence rate should be clearly stated, with proofs or tight references. Without this, it is difficult to assess the real benefit.
- Extension to monotone operators: The monotone operator extension is an attractive direction but seems underdeveloped. It is unclear what oracle/model assumptions are required (e.g., finite-sum structure, Lipschitz continuity, cocoercivity), whether the proposed method handles composite structures, and how the convergence guarantees compare to operator-splitting baselines.
- Empirical evaluation: The experimental section appears limited. To convincingly support the claims, more numerical experiments are needed, including comparisons against strong baselines, sensitivity analyses, and ablation of the Catalyst acceleration.
- Presentation: Some statements are vague (e.g., “easily accelerated”) and key quantities (rates, constants, assumptions) are not always spelled out. This hinders readability and verification.

Requests for clarification and improvement
- Relaxed assumptions: Can the theory be extended to the case where only one side is strongly convex? If not, can you add a regularization/restart strategy with guarantees that recover near-linear convergence in that case, and quantify any bias introduced?
- Non-strongly-convex case: Please state explicit convergence rates and conditions for the general convex–concave setting (e.g., O(1/k) or O(1/k^2) with acceleration under additional smoothness).
- Catalyst specifics: Provide (i) the exact outer–inner iteration complexity with all constants, (ii) guidance or theory for choosing the regularization parameter and inner accuracy, and (iii) a discussion of when acceleration yields net wall-clock improvements.
- Prior work positioning: Add a dedicated related-work section comparing to stochastic primal–dual/extragradient methods and variance-reduced counterparts. A table or concise summary of assumptions and rates would help.
- Monotone operators: Clarify the precise class covered, the oracle assumptions (finite-sum vs general), and provide either full proofs or a clear reduction to known results. Include at least one nontrivial monotone operator task in the experiments.
- Experiments: 
  - Include more datasets and problem classes (e.g., bilinear saddle problems, regularized empirical risk minimization with linear coupling, and at least one non-bilinear monotone operator task).
  - Compare against strong baselines (e.g., stochastic extragradient, primal–dual hybrid gradient, variance-reduced primal–dual variants) with careful tuning.
  - Report iteration complexity, wall-clock time, and sensitivity to step sizes and Catalyst parameters.
  - Isolate the effect of variance reduction and of Catalyst in ablations.
- Technical details: Clearly state smoothness, Lipschitz, and bounded variance assumptions; specify whether the gradient/VI oracle is unbiased; and include pseudocode with all parameters.

Minor comments
- Tighten writing and remove vague phrases like “easily accelerated.” Replace with precise statements and references.
- Ensure notation is consistent across primal, dual, and operator-theoretic sections.
- Add proof sketches in the main text to guide readers, with full proofs in the appendix.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem and suggests a promising combination of variance reduction and Catalyst-style acceleration for saddle-point and monotone operator problems. However, the current version relies on strong assumptions (strong convexity on both sides), lacks a clear comparison to closely related methods, and offers limited empirical evidence. Strengthening the theoretical coverage to less restrictive settings, clarifying the novelty and acceleration details, and substantially expanding the experiments would significantly improve the paper.

Recommendation: Weak reject. I encourage the authors to address the above points; with broader assumptions and stronger empirical support, this work could be competitive.
