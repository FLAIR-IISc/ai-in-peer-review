### Summary

This paper proposes the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for saddle point problems by extending the variance reduction techniques of SVRG and SAGA. The core contribution lies in a novel proof technique that frames the saddle point problem as finding the zero of a monotone operator. This analysis circumvents the limitations of standard convex minimization analysis and is consequently applicable to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The authors demonstrate that this approach can effectively tackle machine learning models with non-separable loss functions and regularizers.

### Strengths and Significance

1.  **First Linear Convergence Rate:** A key strength of this work is establishing the first linear convergence rate for stochastic algorithms on this class of saddle point problems. This is a significant theoretical milestone for an important and widely applicable problem class.

2.  **Problem Relevance:** The ability to efficiently solve saddle point problems is crucial for many modern machine learning tasks that involve non-separable loss functions and regularizers (e.g., certain forms of structured prediction, robust optimization). This work provides a practical and provably fast method for these scenarios.

3.  **Novel and Generalizable Analysis:** The proof technique, which interprets the problem through the lens of monotone operator theory, is particularly innovative and elegant. This approach is not only powerful but also potentially widely applicable to other problems in optimization. As a testament to its utility, the authors also provide simplified proofs for existing algorithms like Forward-Backward splitting and its stochastic variants.

4.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The empirical results show that the proposed algorithms, especially when combined with non-uniform sampling, are highly competitive and often outperform previous state-of-the-art methods across the tested problems.

Overall, this paper advances the field in an important direction. The combination of a significant theoretical breakthrough with a novel and elegant analysis makes this a strong contribution.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. However, there are a few points that could be clarified to further improve its impact and reproducibility.

1.  **Emphasis on the Proof Technique:** Given that the primary contribution is the analysis technique (as the algorithms themselves are direct extensions of SVRG/SAGA), the paper would be significantly strengthened by including more details of this technique in the main body. A proof sketch, for instance, would provide readers with a clearer intuition behind the monotone operator framework, if space permits.

2.  **Clarity on Non-uniform Sampling:** The paper is unclear about the specifics of the non-uniform sampling scheme for general problems. For the bilinear saddle-point problem, the sampling distribution is clear, but how is the distribution `π` defined for problems beyond this specific case? Clarifying this is essential for the reproducibility and general applicability of the method.

3.  **Experimental Details:** The experimental setup lacks detail regarding the step size `σ`. How was this hyperparameter chosen or tuned in the experiments? Providing these details is important for a fair comparison.

4.  **Intuition on Non-uniform Sampling:** The experiments highlight the remarkable effectiveness of non-uniform sampling for saddle point problems, which appears more critical here than in standard convex minimization settings. The paper would benefit greatly from a discussion on the authors' intuition for why this is the case. Is there a structural property of saddle point problems that makes non-uniform sampling particularly advantageous?
