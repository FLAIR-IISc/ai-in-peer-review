### **Summary**

This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The primary contribution is the development of SVR-based algorithms that achieve a linear convergence rate for this problem class. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis and compelling empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed methods.

The framework is shown to be applicable to a broad range of problems, including important machine learning tasks with non-separable structures, such as structured output prediction with a non-separable loss or regularization (e.g., a fusion term). A key theoretical innovation is the convergence analysis, which diverges from traditional analyses for convex optimization. Recognizing the increased complexity of saddle-point problems, the authors skillfully employ the theory of monotone operators to establish their convergence guarantees. This analysis also reveals that the proposed algorithms are applicable to an even wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities in game theory.

From a methodological standpoint, the paper introduces two distinct strategies for splitting the gradient operator to enable variance reduction within a stochastic forward-backward algorithm: an "element-wise split" and a "factored split." Furthermore, the authors investigate the use of non-uniform sampling, showing both theoretically and empirically that it can lead to superior performance compared to standard uniform sampling.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Contribution:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a proven linear convergence rate is a novel and valuable contribution to the optimization literature.
2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The use of monotone operator theory to analyze convergence is sophisticated and well-suited to the complexities of the saddle-point setting. This represents a notable departure from and advance over existing analyses for purely convex problems.
3.  **Generality and Broad Applicability:** The proposed algorithms are applicable to a wide array of problems featuring non-separable loss and regularization functions, which are common in modern machine learning. The connection to variational inequalities further underscores the breadth of the framework.
4.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, presented in both the main paper and the appendix, are comprehensive. They sufficiently validate the theoretical findings and clearly demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods on challenging problems.
5.  **Practical Enhancements:** The inclusion and analysis of non-uniform sampling provide a practical mechanism for further improving algorithmic performance, which is well-supported by both theory and experiments.

### **Questions and Minor Comments**

The paper is well-written and the results are strong. The following are some minor points for clarification and improvement:

1.  **Clarity on Acceleration:** In Section 5, the authors describe an acceleration technique, following [8], that incorporates an additional regularization term to guide the update towards an iterate point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. However, the paper does not specify the mechanism for adaptively updating this point to achieve the desired speed-up. Could the authors provide more detail on this adaptive strategy?
2.  **Theoretical Guarantees for SAGA Acceleration:** The convergence and acceleration proof is provided for the SVRG-based variant. It is not immediately clear if the same theoretical results hold for the SAGA-based algorithm. Do similar guarantees exist for SAGA, and if so, does the proof follow a similar argument?
3.  **Notational Ambiguity:** The symbol 'K' is used to denote both the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix. To improve readability and avoid potential confusion, it would be beneficial to use distinct symbols for these two entities.
