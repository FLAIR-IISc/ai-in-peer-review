This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this class of problems, common in machine learning. The authors demonstrate linear convergence of these algorithms under a monotone operator framework and propose an accelerated variant utilizing a non-uniform sampling scheme. The core contribution lies in showing that this approach applies not only to saddle-point problems but also to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. 

The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides novel insights into saddle-point problem structure. Extending existing variance reduction techniques to this setting represents a significant step, and the demonstration of linear convergence is valuable. The exploration of non-uniform sampling and the catalyst acceleration framework further enhance the practical relevance of the work. However, the paper's presentation could be improved for accessibility. While the connection to monotone operators is powerful, it feels somewhat tangential in the initial presentation; the foundational role of monotone operator theory would benefit from clearer emphasis earlier in the paper. The experimental evaluation, while promising, is limited to two datasets. Including results on a wider range of problems would strengthen the empirical validation. For cases with separable functions, comparison with accelerated primal-dual methods like the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm [Zhang & Chambolle, 2015] would provide valuable context. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and establishing linear convergence is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis utilizing monotone operators provides a strong theoretical foundation and generalizes the results beyond saddle-point problems.
*   **Practical Improvements:**  The proposed acceleration technique and non-uniform sampling offer potential performance gains.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The method extends to variational inequalities, increasing its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation Clarity:** The connection to monotone operators could be more clearly established and motivated early in the paper.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** Results are presented for only two datasets.
*   **Missing Comparison:** A comparison with related accelerated primal-dual methods (e.g., stochastic Chambolle-Pock) is absent.
*   **Assumptions:** The strength of assumptions (A)-(C) warrants further discussion, particularly regarding their applicability to practical problems like SVM.

**Specific Questions/Requests for Clarification:**

1.  **Assumption Strength:** How restrictive are assumptions (A)-(C), particularly (A)? Does the framework apply directly to saddle-point problems arising from Support Vector Machines (SVMs)?
2.  **Primal-Dual Relation:** Could the authors elaborate on the relationship between their method and stochastic primal-dual methods? What are the key differences and advantages of their approach?
3.  **Theorem 2 - Parameter μ:** The meaning of the constant μ in Theorem 2 is unclear. Is it the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? How does this result compare to Theorem 1 in terms of practical performance and assumptions?
4.  **Monotonicity Derivation:** The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is unclear. Do assumptions (A)-(C) necessarily imply strong monotonicity?

**Minor Issues:**

*   There is some ambiguity in the use of the constant 'L', switching between its role as a condition number and a Lipschitz constant. Clarification would be helpful.
*   Please include a citation to [Zhang & Chambolle, 2015] – "A stochastic primal-dual method for convex problems with applications to imaging." *Journal of Mathematical Imaging and Vision*, 40(1):120–145.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of established optimization techniques to a challenging problem setting.
*   The linear convergence guarantees are a significant result.
*   The potential for practical improvements through acceleration and non-uniform sampling is promising.
*   The broader applicability to monotone operator problems enhances the overall impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility.
*   The limited experimental evaluation weakens the empirical validation.
*   The lack of comparison to relevant accelerated primal-dual methods leaves a gap in the evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. Despite some weaknesses in presentation and experimental validation, the theoretical results are strong, and the potential for practical impact is significant. I believe this paper is **acceptably strong** and recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding clarifying the assumptions, expanding the experimental results, and providing a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.



**Reference:**

Zhang, A., & Chambolle, A. (2015). A stochastic primal-dual method for convex problems with applications to imaging. *Journal of Mathematical Imaging and Vision*, *40*(1), 120–145.
