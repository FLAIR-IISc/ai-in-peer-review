

The paper extends recent incremental gradient algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite sum structure, which can arise from two distinct splitting schemes: individual or factored. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under different splitting approaches and sampling strategies (uniform or non-uniform). Experimental results on a binary classification problem demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are essential for outperforming competitive batch methods. While the extension of variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, the established results are notably novel and technically sophisticated.

For bilinear saddle-point problems, the authors present clear comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms, which align with known results in convex minimization. However, I have a significant concern regarding the computational complexity: the proposed methods require an O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2), may not necessarily be superior to accelerated batch algorithms. Specifically, when the number of data points n substantially exceeds the dimension d (which is typical in machine learning applications), the complexity under factored splits (used in the experiments) is actually worse than batch forward-backward algorithms, as demonstrated in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization case, I do not observe a clear theoretical advantage for using variance reduction methods in saddle-point problems. I recommend the authors provide a more thorough discussion addressing the following questions:
(i) Is factored splitting theoretically worse than individual splitting?
(ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch forward-backward algorithms?
(iii) Are the dependencies on n and d in the complexity bounds tight?
