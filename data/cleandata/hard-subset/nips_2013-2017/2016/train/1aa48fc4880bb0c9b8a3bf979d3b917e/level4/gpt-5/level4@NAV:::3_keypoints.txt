- Generalizes SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored splits.  
- Presents thorough complexity analysis for different splits and for uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Experiments on binary classification indicate non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch algorithms.  
- Contribution is genuinely novel and technically non-trivial despite apparent straightforwardness of extending variance reduction to saddle points.  
- For the bilinear saddle-point problem, provides a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms, consistent with convex minimization analogies.  
- Main concern: incremental algorithms incur an O(n + d) cost per proximal computation, creating a fundamental bottleneck.  
- Consequently, overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch variants, especially when n >> d; factored splits can be worse (per Table 2).  
- Reviewer requests clarification on: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits; (ii) when accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB; (iii) whether the n and d dependences are tight.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but leaves scores unchanged.
