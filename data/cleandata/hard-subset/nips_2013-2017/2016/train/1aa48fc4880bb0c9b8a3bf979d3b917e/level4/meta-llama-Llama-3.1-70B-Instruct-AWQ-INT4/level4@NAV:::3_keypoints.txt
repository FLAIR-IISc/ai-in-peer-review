- Paper extends incremental variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems (individual and factored splits).
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across splitting strategies and sampling schemes (uniform and nonuniform).
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are important to outperform competitive batch algorithms.
- Application of variance reduction to saddle points is intuitive but the results are novel and technically non-trivial.
- Offers a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward methods for the bilinear saddle-point case.
- Main concern: each incremental iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, which may fundamentally limit practical gains.
- Consequently, overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling) may not beat accelerated batch FB when n ≫ d; factored splits can be worse than batch FB (Table 2).
- Unlike convex minimization, there is no clear theoretical advantage of variance reduction for these saddle-point problems.
- Reviewer requests deeper discussion on: (i) whether factored splitting is theoretically inferior to individual splitting, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors’ responses but keeps original scores.
