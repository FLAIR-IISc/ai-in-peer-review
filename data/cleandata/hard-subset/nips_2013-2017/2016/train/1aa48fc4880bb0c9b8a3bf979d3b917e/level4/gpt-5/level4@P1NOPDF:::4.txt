This paper extends the stochastic variance-reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, and establishes linear convergence. Theoretical analysis and empirical results jointly demonstrate the effectiveness of variance reduction in this setting.

A key merit is the ability to handle a broad class of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion terms) can be non-separable. Unlike convergence analyses for purely convex problems, the saddle-point setting is substantially more intricate; the authors leverage monotone operator theory to prove convergence. The analysis further indicates that the proposed SVRG and SAGA extensions cover a wider class of problems, including certain variational inequalities arising in game theory.

From a stochastic approximation perspective, the paper proposes two distinct splits of the gradient operator: an element-wise split for SVRG and a factored split for SAGA. The variance-reduction techniques are integrated into stochastic forward–backward algorithms, which are standard for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also considered, and both the theory and experiments show that non-uniform sampling yields superior performance to uniform sampling.

The experimental results, in both the main paper and the appendix, provide solid evidence supporting the theory and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

Minor comments and questions:
- Acceleration (Section 5): Following [8], the authors add a regularization term that encourages the iterates to stay near a reference point, aiming to accelerate convergence. It remains unclear how to adaptively update this reference iterate, denoted (x̄, ȳ), to reliably achieve speed-ups in practice. Clarifying an adaptive strategy or providing guidance on tuning would be helpful.
- The paper provides convergence and acceleration guarantees for SVRG. Do the same theoretical results (including acceleration) extend to SAGA under comparable assumptions?
- Notation: The smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K should be denoted by different symbols to avoid confusion.
