- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA and accelerated (Catalyst) variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Thorough complexity analysis across two split types (individual vs factored) and sampling schemes (uniform vs nonuniform).  
- Experiments on a binary classification example show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform batch algorithms.  
- Results are novel and technically non-trivial despite seeming like a straightforward extension.  
- Major concern: each iteration requires O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator, creating a potential bottleneck.  
- Due to that bottleneck, accelerated incremental methods may not improve overall complexity vs accelerated batch FB, especially when n >> d.  
- Under factored splits (used in simulations), complexity can be worse than batch FB when n is much larger than d.  
- From a theoretical perspective, the reviewer did not see a clear substantial advantage of variance reduction for these saddle-point problems.  
- Requests clarification: (i) Is factored splitting theoretically worse than individual splitting?  
- Requests clarification: (ii) Under what regimes is accelerated SVRG favorable compared to batch FB?  
- Requests clarification: (iii) Is the dependence on n and d in the complexity bounds tight?  
- Strengths summarized: comprehensive complexity study, clear batch/stochastic/incremental comparisons for bilinear case, supportive experiments.  
- Weaknesses summarized: proximal O(n + d) bottleneck, accelerated SVRG not clearly superior to batch in many regimes, possible loose n,d dependence.  
- Suggested improvements: add in-depth discussion of limitations, contrast factored vs individual splits, analyze regimes favoring accelerated SVRG, and tighten n,d dependence if possible.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing the above concerns and providing more detailed discussion.
