This paper presents the first algorithms, based on variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA, that achieve a linear convergence rate for saddle point problems. The central contribution lies in the novel proof of convergence, which leverages the theory of monotone operators. This is a necessary departure from standard convex minimization analysis, which is not applicable in this setting. A significant consequence of this approach is its applicability to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.

The proposed method is valuable for tackling machine learning models with non-separable loss functions and regularizers, which can be formulated as saddle point problems. The technique of framing these problems as the search for zeros of a monotone operator is a powerful and interesting insight that could be broadly applicable. As a benefit, the paper also provides simpler proofs for existing methods like Forward-Backward splitting and its stochastic counterpart. The empirical results show that the proposed algorithms, particularly when equipped with non-uniform sampling, are competitive with and often outperform prior methods.

Overall, this paper represents a significant advancement in an important research area, distinguished by its novel analytical perspective. Given that the core innovation lies in the proof technique—as the algorithms themselves are relatively direct extensions of existing methods—the paper's impact could be enhanced by elaborating on this central contribution. The authors might consider including a proof sketch in the main paper, space permitting, to better convey the key ideas.

The manuscript is generally well-written and clearly presented. However, a few points could be clarified:

1.  For non-uniform sampling beyond the bilinear case, the definition of the distribution `π` is unclear.
2.  The methodology for setting the step size `σ` in the experiments is not specified.
3.  An intuitive discussion on why non-uniform sampling appears to be particularly crucial for saddle point problems, more so than for convex minimization, would be a valuable addition to the paper.
