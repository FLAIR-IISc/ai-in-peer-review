**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper presents an extension of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, showing linear convergence of these algorithms for saddle-point problems. They also propose an accelerated variant and analyze a non-uniform sampling scheme. The contributions of this paper are significant, as they provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

Strengths:

* The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, including linear convergence rates.
* The authors propose an accelerated variant of the algorithm, which improves the convergence rate.
* The paper presents a non-uniform sampling scheme, which can lead to improved performance in practice.
* The authors provide experimental results on two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

Weaknesses:

* The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in making the connection to monotone operators more clear.
* The experimental results are limited to two datasets, and it would be beneficial to see results on more datasets.
* The authors do not compare their approach to accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm.
* There are a few points that require clarification, such as the strength of the assumptions A-C and the differences between stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach.

**Originality and Significance**

The contributions of this paper are significant, as they provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems. The authors' approach applies to non-separable functions, which is a novel extension of existing stochastic variance reduced techniques. The theoretical analysis is thorough and reveals new insights for saddle-point problems.

**Quality of the Paper**

Although the theoretical contributions of this paper are strong, the presentation could be improved. The connection to monotone operators is not clearly explained, and the analysis provided by the authors is based on monotone operators. The experimental results are limited, and it would be beneficial to see results on more datasets.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a non-uniform sampling scheme, which can lead to improved performance in practice.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

**Minor Issues**

* There is a mix-up with the constant L used as the condition number and the Lipschitz constant.
* The Forward-Backward algorithm is not referenced.

**Recommendations**

* Clarify the strength of the assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A).
* Elaborate on the differences between stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach.
* Clarify the constant \mu in theorem 2 and its relation to the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix.
* Improve the presentation of the paper, particularly in making the connection to monotone operators more clear.
* Provide experimental results on more datasets.
* Compare the proposed approach to accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm.
