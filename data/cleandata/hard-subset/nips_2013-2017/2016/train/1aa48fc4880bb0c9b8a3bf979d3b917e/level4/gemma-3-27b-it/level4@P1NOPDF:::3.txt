The paper extends several recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst – to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite sum structure, arising from either individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under different split types and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems might appear straightforward, the results presented here are notably novel, and the technical details are certainly non-trivial. Specifically for the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, the authors offer a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms, mirroring known results for convex minimization and behaving as expected. 

However, I have a major concern. Although solving the saddle point problem addresses the challenge of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck: a computational cost of O(n+d) for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity – even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Tables 1 and 2 – is not necessarily better than that of the accelerated batch version. In particular, when the number of data points *n* is significantly larger than the dimension *d* (a common scenario in machine learning applications), the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulation) is actually *worse* than that of the batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the well-established benefits for convex minimization, I do not see a clear and substantial advantage to using variance reduction here, from a theoretical perspective.

I recommend the authors provide a more in-depth discussion of this matter, and clarify the following questions: i) Is a factored split (theoretically) less efficient than an individual split? ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to the batch FB algorithm? iii) Is the observed dependence on *n* and *d* tight?
