This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, where linear convergence is established. Both theoretical and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the variance reduction method on these problems.

The proposed algorithms can be applied to a variety of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. A significant contribution of this paper is that, unlike previous convergence analyses on convex problems, the analysis of saddle-point problems is much more complex, and the authors utilize monotone operators to prove convergence. This analysis also reveals that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms can be applied to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

Considering a stochastic approach, the authors propose two distinct splits of the gradient operator, namely the element-wise split and factored split, for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The variance reduction is applied to stochastic forward-backward algorithms, which are commonly used for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also examined, and both theoretical and empirical results show that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms that with uniform sampling.

Experimental results presented in both the main article and the appendix provide sufficient evidence to support the theoretical results and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both non-separable loss and regularizer.

Some minor comments: In Section 5, the authors follow [8] to incorporate an additional regularization term to push the update towards an iterate point. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate point, namely (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve acceleration. A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, but it is uncertain whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA. Furthermore, to avoid confusion, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should use distinct symbols.
