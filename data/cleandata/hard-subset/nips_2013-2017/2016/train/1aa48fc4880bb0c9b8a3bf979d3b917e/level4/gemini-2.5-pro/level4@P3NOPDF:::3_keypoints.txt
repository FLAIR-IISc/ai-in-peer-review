- Extends variance-reduced incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, accelerated Catalyst variants) to strongly convex–concave saddle point problems.  
- Analyzes finite-sum structure under two settings: "individual splits" and "factored splits."  
- Provides comprehensive convergence rate analysis for uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies.  
- Results are novel and technically non-trivial despite appearing like a straightforward extension.  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch algorithms.  
- Gives a clear complexity comparison for bilinear saddle point problems across batch, stochastic, and incremental methods.  
- Major concern: per-iteration proximal operator costs O(n + d), creating a significant computational bottleneck.  
- Because of that cost, accelerated incremental methods are not always theoretically better than accelerated batch Forward–Backward (FB), especially in the factored splits setting.  
- This disadvantage is pronounced when n >> d (common in ML), where Table 2 shows worse complexity for incremental methods than batch FB.  
- Requests clarification on: whether factored splits are inherently worse than individual splits, which regimes (n, d, condition numbers) favor accelerated SVRG over batch FB, and whether n/d dependencies in bounds are tight or improvable.
