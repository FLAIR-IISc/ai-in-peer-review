

The paper presents a significant contribution by extending variance reduction techniques (SVRG/SAGA) to saddle point problems, establishing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class. The core innovation lies in leveraging monotone operator theory to prove convergence—a critical departure from standard convex minimization analysis, which fails for saddle points. This approach not only resolves the convergence gap but also broadens applicability to variational inequalities, a substantially wider problem class. The framework enables addressing non-separable loss functions and regularizers, which are prevalent in modern machine learning (e.g., adversarial training, robust optimization), making the work both theoretically and practically impactful.

The authors’ proof technique—interpreting saddle points as finding zeros of monotone operators—is elegant and potentially influential beyond the immediate context. It also yields simpler analyses for existing methods like the forward-backward splitting (FB), including its stochastic variant, enhancing accessibility. Empirically, the proposed non-uniform sampling algorithms demonstrate strong performance, consistently outperforming prior alternatives across benchmarks. The theoretical and practical advances position this work as a meaningful extension of the field.

That said, the paper would benefit from greater clarity on two key technical points, which currently hinder reproducibility and deeper understanding:  
1. **Non-uniform sampling distribution (\(\pi\))**: The paper specifies non-uniform sampling for bilinear saddle points but does not define \(\pi\) for general problems. Clarifying how \(\pi\) is derived (e.g., from problem structure or Lipschitz constants) for non-bilinear cases is essential.  
2. **Step size \(\sigma\) selection**: The experimental setup omits details on how \(\sigma\) is set (e.g., fixed, adaptive, or based on tuning). An explanation of the rationale behind this choice, particularly why non-uniform sampling proves essential for saddle points (unlike convex minimization), would strengthen the contribution. The authors should address whether this necessity stems from the non-monotonicity of saddle point operators or other structural properties.

The writing is generally clear and well-structured, though minor issues persist:  
- Line 78: "k" should be "d" (typo in dimensionality).  
- Line 121: The second equality contains an extraneous \(y_{t-1}\) term.  

Given that the primary contribution centers on the proof technique (with algorithms being natural extensions of SVRG/SAGA), the authors should consider adding a concise proof sketch in the main text. This would help readers grasp the novelty of the monotone operator approach without overwhelming the paper, especially since the technique’s generality suggests broader applicability.

**Summary**: This is a strong, timely contribution that resolves a key gap in saddle point optimization. The monotone operator framework is a notable theoretical advance, and the empirical results validate its practical utility. Addressing the two technical clarifications above—particularly the definition of \(\pi\) and the role of non-uniform sampling—would substantially elevate the paper. The minor typos should be corrected. Overall, the work merits publication with these revisions.
