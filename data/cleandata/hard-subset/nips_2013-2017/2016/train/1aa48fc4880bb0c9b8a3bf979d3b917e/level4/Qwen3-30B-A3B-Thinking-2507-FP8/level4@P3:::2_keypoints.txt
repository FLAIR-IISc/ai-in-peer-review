- Introduces the first large-scale, linearly convergent stochastic variance-reduction algorithms (SVRG, SAGA) for convex–concave saddle-point problems.  
- Provides a rigorous convergence analysis using monotone operator theory that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.  
- Distinguishes and clarifies two split notions—function-based vs. derivative-based—and explains their practical implications.  
- Proves and empirically demonstrates the necessity and advantage of non-uniform sampling for efficiency and tighter complexity bounds.  
- Presents an effective acceleration technique via a simple extension of the catalyst framework, yielding methods superior to accelerated batch approaches.  
- Convergence proofs are careful and sound, correctly addressing differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization.  
- Complexity bounds are clear, account for sampling schemes, and highlight theoretical and practical gains from non-uniform sampling.  
- The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems, a significant technical contribution.  
- Paper clarity is excellent: problem setup, assumptions, consistent notation, and reproducible algorithmic details.  
- Experimental section is well-structured, showing accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperforms existing methods, especially on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Originality is substantial: fills a literature gap by adapting variance-reduction methods to saddle-point and monotone-operator settings.  
- Minor weakness: strong convexity assumption should clarify if only one side (primal or dual) suffices rather than both.  
- Minor weakness: experiments limited to sido and rcv1—additional datasets and problem structures would better demonstrate generality.
