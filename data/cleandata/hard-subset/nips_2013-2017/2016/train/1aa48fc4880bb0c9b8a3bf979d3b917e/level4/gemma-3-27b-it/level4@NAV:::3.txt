This paper presents an extension of recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants – to address strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite-sum structure, arising from either individual or factored data splits. The authors offer a comprehensive complexity analysis of these algorithms under varying split types and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification task demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. Extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems, while conceptually simple, yields demonstrably novel results requiring significant technical skill. For the crucial bilinear saddle point problem, the authors provide a lucid comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe algorithms, mirroring known behavior in convex minimization – a sensible and convincing connection. 

However, a key concern arises from the O(n+d) computational cost of the proximal operator at each iteration, a fundamental bottleneck when applying these incremental algorithms to saddle point problems. Consequently, even with accelerated SVRG and non-uniform sampling (as detailed in Tables 1 and 2), the overall complexity isn’t consistently superior to accelerated batch methods. Particularly when the number of data points, *n*, significantly exceeds the dimension, *d* – a common scenario in machine learning – the complexity under factored splits (utilized in the simulation) is, in fact, worse than that of batch Frank-Wolfe, as shown in Table 2.  In contrast to convex minimization, the theoretical benefits of variance reduction in this context are not immediately apparent. 

I recommend the authors provide a detailed discussion addressing these points, specifically clarifying: i) whether factored splits are inherently less efficient than individual splits; ii) the conditions under which accelerated SVRG offers an advantage over batch Frank-Wolfe; and iii) the tightness of the dependence on *n* and *d*.
