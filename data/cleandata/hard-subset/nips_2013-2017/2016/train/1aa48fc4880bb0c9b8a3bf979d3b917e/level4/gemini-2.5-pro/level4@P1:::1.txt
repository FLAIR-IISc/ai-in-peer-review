### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of solving large-scale, convex-concave saddle-point problems where the objective function can be decomposed into a sum of many components. The authors propose extending recent stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to this setting. The core contribution is the development and analysis of the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems, which is particularly relevant for machine learning tasks involving non-separable loss functions or regularizers. The theoretical analysis is grounded in the more general framework of monotone operators, which not only provides a basis for the convergence proofs but also shows the applicability of the methods to a broader range of problems, such as variational inequalities. The authors also investigate important practical and theoretical extensions, including an analysis of non-uniform sampling schemes and an acceleration technique based on the "Catalyst" framework.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first linearly convergent, incremental algorithms for saddle-point problems. This is particularly valuable for problems with non-separable structures (e.g., in the loss or regularizer), where applying variance-reduction techniques to the primal or dual formulations alone is not possible. The theoretical analysis is thorough and reveals new insights into solving these problems efficiently.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical foundation of the paper is strong. By framing the analysis within the theory of monotone operators, the authors provide a rigorous and generalizable convergence proof. This approach is more powerful than a direct analysis for convex-concave functions and extends the applicability of the proposed algorithms beyond the immediate scope of saddle-point problems.

*   **Completeness:** The paper is comprehensive in its treatment of the problem. It proposes and analyzes both SVRG and SAGA-type algorithms, explores the critical role of non-uniform sampling for improving performance, and presents an accelerated variant that is shown to be superior to accelerated batch methods.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** While the technical content is strong, the paper's presentation could be improved for accessibility. The analysis relies heavily on the monotone operator framework, yet this concept is formally introduced late in the paper (Section 6) as an "extension." Introducing this framework earlier and making the connection to the saddle-point problem more explicit from the beginning would significantly clarify the theoretical development and make the paper easier to follow.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation, while promising, is somewhat limited as it is based on only two datasets. To strengthen the claims about the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods, it would be beneficial to include experiments on a broader range of datasets and problem types.

*   **Comparison to Related Work:** For the case of separable functions where other methods are applicable, the experimental comparison could be more complete. The authors should consider comparing their methods against other relevant stochastic primal-dual algorithms, for instance, the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm presented by Zhang et al. (ICML 2015) [1].

    [1] Zhang, Y., Duchi, J., & Wainwright, M. (2015). A Stochastic Primal-Dual Method with Line-Search for General Convex Problems. *Proceedings of the 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML)*.

### Questions for Authors

To better understand the work, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  **Scope of Assumptions:** Could you comment on the strength of assumptions (A)-(C), particularly the strong convex-concave property in assumption (A)? How restrictive is this in practice? For example, does the standard saddle-point formulation of a Support Vector Machine (SVM) satisfy this assumption?

2.  **Relation to Primal-Dual Methods:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed methods and the broader class of stochastic primal-dual algorithms. Could you elaborate on the key differences and potential advantages of your approach?

3.  **Clarity of Theorem 2:** In Theorem 2, the constant µ is not defined in the main text. Assuming it refers to the monotonicity constant from the appendix, it is not immediately clear how the convergence rate of SAGA compares to that of SVRG in Theorem 1. Could you clarify this constant and provide a more direct comparison of the theoretical rates for the two algorithms?

4.  **Assumptions and Monotonicity:** The connection between the saddle-point assumptions (A)-(C) and the strong monotonicity property used in the appendix for the proofs is a bit abrupt. Could you explicitly state whether assumptions (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity and briefly outline this connection for the reader?

### Minor Issues

*   There appears to be a slight inconsistency in the use of the constant `L`. It is first introduced as "the condition number of the problem" and later referred to as "the Lipschitz-constant L". Please clarify the terminology and use it consistently.
*   In Section 2.2, providing a specific citation for the Forward-Backward (FB) algorithm would be helpful.
