- Paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems via monotone operators and applies SVRG/SAGA with proximal operators, proving linear convergence, an accelerated variant, and non-uniform sampling, extending beyond separable objectives.

- Theoretical significance: thorough analysis giving new monotone-operator insights, linear convergence guarantees, and acceleration.

- Generality: method applies to non-separable objectives, broadening scope beyond many primal–dual or coordinate methods.

- Extensions: proposed accelerated variant and non-uniform sampling analysis are well-motivated and practically impactful.

- Monotone-operator framing is introduced late despite being fundamental, making exposition harder to follow.

- Experiments are limited to two datasets, so robustness, scalability, and practical benefits of variants are unclear.

- Missing baselines: lacks comparisons to accelerated stochastic primal–dual methods (e.g., stochastic/accelerated Chambolle–Pock); specific reference (Zhang & Xiao 2015) should be included.

- Clarify strength of assumptions (A–C), especially (A), and map common models (e.g., SVM hinge/squared-hinge) to these assumptions.

- Explain conceptual and algorithmic differences from stochastic primal–dual/PDHG methods, covering update structure, where variance reduction enters, convergence assumptions, complexity/oracle calls, and regimes of advantage.

- Make constant μ in Theorem 2 explicit (strong monotonicity/cocoercivity), relate it to problem parameters, and compare rates with Theorem 1 under matched assumptions.

- Clarify whether (A)–(C) imply monotonicity/strong monotonicity of the composite operator and state any monotonicity assumptions explicitly, indicating which analysis parts need which properties.

- Restructure exposition: move monotone operator formulation early, provide mapping to monotone inclusion, and express variance-reduction in that language.

- Unify notation and constants: disambiguate L (use L for operator Lipschitz, μ for strong monotonicity, κ=L/μ for condition number) and maintain consistency across theorems/proofs.

- Compare against stronger baselines (stochastic Chambolle–Pock, accelerated PDHG, SPDC, recent variance-reduced primal–dual methods) and highlight non-separability advantages.

- Expand experiments to more datasets/tasks (multiclass, structured prediction, non-separable regularizers), report wall-clock time, iteration complexity, sampling sensitivity, and include ablations for acceleration and non-uniform sampling.

- Provide practical guidance on step-size selection, epoch length for SVRG, and sampling distributions tied to theoretical constants.

- Add citations for Forward–Backward splitting (e.g., Combettes & Wajs 2005; Beck & Teboulle 2009).

- Clarify L’s meaning where it’s alternately called “condition number” and “Lipschitz constant”; reserve “condition number” for ratios like L/μ.

- Add formal definition or citation for the Forward–Backward algorithm at first introduction.

- Fix minor typos/wording (e.g., “can not” → “cannot”) and ensure consistent capitalization of algorithm names.

- Overall: strong theoretical contribution and scope, especially for non-separable objectives, but needs clearer operator-theoretic exposition, consistent notation, and more comprehensive experiments/baselines to be fully compelling.
