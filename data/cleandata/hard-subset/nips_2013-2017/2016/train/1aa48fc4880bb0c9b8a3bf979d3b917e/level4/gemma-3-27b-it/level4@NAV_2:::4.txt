This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence guarantees. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the efficacy of this variance reduction approach for saddle-point problems. The work tackles a significant challenge: extending algorithms designed for separable objectives to scenarios involving non-separable losses and regularizers, common in machine learning applications like structured output prediction and problems with fusion penalties. A key contribution lies in adapting the convergence analysis from convex optimization; the authors leverage the theory of monotone operators to prove convergence, a more complex undertaking than previous analyses. This broader framework allows the algorithms to apply to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities relevant to game theory. The paper thoughtfully considers different splitting strategies for the gradient operator – element-wise and factored – within the SVRG and SAGA implementations. Employing a stochastic approach, the authors analyze the impact of non-uniform sampling, demonstrating through both theory and experiments that it outperforms uniform sampling. Comprehensive experimental results, presented in the main text and appendix, corroborate the theoretical findings and showcase the methods' effectiveness with non-separable losses and regularizers. 

Minor comments: The acceleration technique presented in Section 5 builds upon [7] by incorporating an additional regularization term, effectively introducing a momentum-like step. However, the paper does not detail an adaptive strategy for updating the momentum term (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), which could further enhance performance. While a convergence proof is provided for SVRG, it remains unclear whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA with this acceleration. To avoid potential confusion, the smooth function K(x, y) and the design matrix K could be represented with distinct notations.
