This paper extends modern incremental variance-reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure, allowing both “individual” and “factored” splits of the operator. The authors develop a convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory rather than standard convex minimization arguments. They derive linear rates, show how non-uniform sampling improves both bounds and practice, and discuss how to accelerate the incremental methods via a proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper. For bilinear saddle-point problems, the paper carefully compares batch forward–backward (FB), stochastic FB, and variance-reduced FB, drawing parallels to known results for convex minimization. Experiments on binary classification with non-separable loss/regularizer validate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch baselines.

Relation to prior work: The work connects to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization, primal–dual methods (Chambolle–Pock), and monotone operator splitting, but targets a setting where existing linear-convergence, large-scale incremental methods are not available. The use of monotone operator analysis sidesteps co-coercivity assumptions common in convex minimization and broadens applicability to variational inequalities.

Strengths
- First linearly convergent incremental/variance-reduced methods specialized to convex–concave saddle-point problems and, more generally, to sums of Lipschitz operators plus a strongly monotone operator.
- Clean and general analysis via monotone operators; avoids assumptions (co-coercivity) that fail for saddle-point problems.
- Careful treatment of two decomposition schemes (individual vs factored) and of non-uniform sampling; the bilinear case is deeply analyzed with clear complexity expressions.
- Acceleration via a proximal-point/Catalyst extension that is simpler than in the convex minimization setting and empirically effective.
- Experiments substantiate the theory: non-uniform sampling and acceleration matter; uniform SAGA can underperform batch methods, consistent with the bounds.

Main weakness and questions
My main concern is a potential bottleneck in per-iteration cost: the O(n + d) cost of the proximal operator in many relevant models. As a result, even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling may not dominate accelerated batch FB in theory (Tables 1–2), especially when n ≫ d. Under factored splits (used in experiments), the complexity can be worse than batch FB (Table 2). Unlike convex minimization, where variance reduction typically yields clear complexity gains, I do not see an unambiguous theoretical advantage here.

I recommend an explicit discussion addressing:
i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits? The current bounds suggest they can be, depending on norms and sampling; a precise comparison and guidance on when to prefer each split would be useful.
ii) Under which regimes does accelerated SVRG provably beat accelerated batch FB? Clear conditions in terms of ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max, λ, γ, and the cost of prox vs Bi evaluations would help practitioners.
iii) Are the dependences on n and d tight? For bilinear problems, can the gaps between ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max be exploited to refine the bounds, or are there lower bounds indicating tightness?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Practical impact hinges on the structure of M. When prox(M) is not O(n + d), the advantage of incremental methods may increase; conversely, when prox(M) is O(n + d), mini-batching helps amortize cost, but guidance on optimal batch sizes given prox cost would be helpful.
- SAGA vs SVRG: the storage vs epoch trade-off is well discussed; for factored splits, clarifying memory and update cost more explicitly (and when resampling is required for non-uniform sampling) would improve clarity.
- The acceleration section is a nice contribution. It would help to summarize, for bilinear problems, the threshold condition on τ (or on ‖K‖F/(√λγ)) at which acceleration becomes strictly better than the non-accelerated incremental method and than accelerated batch FB.
- Experiments: the chosen non-separable loss/regularizer are appropriate. It would be informative to include a case with cheaper prox (e.g., separable or block-separable) and a case with highly ill-conditioned K to illustrate the full range where variance reduction shines.
- Clarity: the notation L vs L̄(π) is clear but dense; a short table of constants and their interpretations (condition number vs sampling-dependent smoothness) would help readers.

Evaluation per criterion
- Quality: Technically sound, with careful proofs and correct connections to monotone operator theory. The per-iteration cost issue limits the apparent theoretical advantage in some regimes.
- Clarity: Generally well written and well organized, though some complexity expressions could be highlighted with more explicit regime guidance.
- Originality: High. Extends variance reduction to saddle-point/monotone operator settings beyond co-coercive operators and introduces a simple acceleration mechanism.
- Significance: Potentially high for large-scale saddle-point problems with suitable proximals and for variational inequalities; significance is more mixed when prox is O(n + d) and n ≫ d.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel extension with rigorous analysis and linear rates beyond convex minimization.
- Useful guidance on non-uniform sampling and decomposition strategies.
- Effective acceleration and solid empirical support.

Cons against acceptance
- In important regimes (n ≫ d, prox cost O(n + d)), the theoretical complexity can be no better—or worse—than accelerated batch FB.
- Limited guidance on when each split and method is preferable in practice.
