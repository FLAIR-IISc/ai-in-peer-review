- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Considers both individual and factored data splits and analyzes uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis for the proposed incremental algorithms.  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch baselines.  
- Theoretical contributions are novel and technically demanding despite conceptual straightforwardness of applying variance reduction to saddle points.  
- Establishes a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank–Wolfe-type algorithms for bilinear saddle points, mirroring convex minimization results.  
- Major concern: per-iteration proximal operator cost O(n + d) limits practical gains of incremental methods.  
- As a result, overall complexity (even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) is not always better than accelerated batch methods.  
- When n >> d, factored-split complexity can be worse than batch FB algorithms (as shown in Table 2).  
- Reviewer did not see a clear theoretical advantage to variance reduction here and requests further discussion on: (i) factored vs individual split, (ii) when accelerated SVRG beats batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors’ responses but keeps the same overall assessment.
