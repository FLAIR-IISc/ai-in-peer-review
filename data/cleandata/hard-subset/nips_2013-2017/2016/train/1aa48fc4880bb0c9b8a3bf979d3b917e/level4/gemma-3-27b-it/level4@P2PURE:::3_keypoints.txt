- Paper generalizes SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems for individual and factored data splits.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across split types and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform).  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are necessary to outperform strong batch baselines.  
- Extending variance reduction/incremental methods to saddle points is conceptually simple but yields novel results and substantial technical challenges.  
- For the bilinear saddle point, the paper gives a clear, convincing comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms analogous to convex minimization results.  
- Major concern: a fundamental computational bottleneck—the O(n + d) cost of computing the proximal operator per iteration—limits practical gains of incremental methods.  
- As a result, accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling does not consistently improve complexity over accelerated batch methods.  
- In the common regime n >> d, factored splits (used in simulations) can yield worse complexity than batch FB (per Table 2).  
- Reviewer does not observe a clear theoretical advantage of variance reduction for saddle points, unlike convex minimization.  
- Requests authors to clarify: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, (ii) conditions under which accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB, and (iii) whether the n and d dependence is tight.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors’ response and retains original scores.
