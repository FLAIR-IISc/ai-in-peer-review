- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems for both individual and factored data splits with complexity analysis under uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Experimental binary classification shows non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to beat competitive batch algorithms.  
- Application of variance reduction to saddle point problems is conceptually straightforward but the presented results are novel and technically challenging.  
- Provides a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank–Wolfe (FB) algorithms for bilinear saddle point problems, aligning with convex minimization insights.  
- Major concern: computing the proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, creating a fundamental bottleneck for the incremental algorithms.  
- Due to the proximal cost, accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling does not consistently improve overall complexity versus accelerated batch methods.  
- In regimes with n >> d (common in ML), the complexity under factored splits (used in simulations) can be worse than batch FB algorithms (per Table 2).  
- Unlike convex minimization, the theoretical advantages of variance reduction in this saddle point setting are not immediately apparent.  
- Reviewer requests discussion on: (i) whether factored splits are inherently less efficient than individual splits, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG clearly outperforms accelerated batch FB, and (iii) tightness or possible refinement of the n and d complexity bounds.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer found the rebuttal satisfactory but leaves assessment and scores unchanged.
