This paper addresses saddle-point problems possessing a convex-concave structure. The authors demonstrate how existing stochastic variance reduction techniques – specifically SVRG and SAGA – can be effectively applied to these problems through the incorporation of a proximal operator. Their analysis, conducted within the framework of monotone operators, establishes linear convergence for these algorithms. Furthermore, they introduce an accelerated variant and investigate a non-uniform sampling scheme. 

**Novelty/Originality:** The contributions of this work are substantial. The thorough theoretical analysis provides new insights regarding saddle-point problems. The authors explore several extensions, with the applicability of their method to non-separable functions being particularly noteworthy.

**Technical Quality:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper’s presentation could be improved. Greater clarity regarding the connection to monotone operators would enhance accessibility; this connection, foundational to the analysis, currently appears as an extension in Section 6. Regarding the experimental evaluation, results on more than two datasets would be beneficial. For cases involving separable functions, a comparison to accelerated methods like the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang & Peyré, 2015) is recommended – please include this reference: http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf. 

Several points require further clarification from the authors:

1.  What is the strength of assumptions A-C, especially assumption (A)? Specifically, do these assumptions hold when applied to saddle-point problems arising from Support Vector Machines?
2.  Could the authors elaborate on the distinctions between their proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual approaches?
3.  In Theorem 2, the constant μ is unclear. Is this the monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? How does this result compare to that of Theorem 1?
4.  The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is presently unclear. Do assumptions (A)-(C) necessitate monotonicity?

**Minor Issues:** There is some ambiguity in the usage of the constant *L*, which is used both as a condition number and as a Lipschitz constant (e.g., "The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem" and "we need the Lipschitz constant L"). A reference to the Forward-Backward algorithm would also be helpful.
