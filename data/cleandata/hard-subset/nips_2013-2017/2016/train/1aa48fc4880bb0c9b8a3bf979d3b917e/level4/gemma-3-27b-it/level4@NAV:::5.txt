The authors present the first linearly convergent algorithms designed for saddle point problems, leveraging variance reduction techniques such as SVRG and SAGA. Recognizing the inapplicability of standard convex minimization analysis to saddle point settings, a key contribution of this work is the innovative application of monotone operator theory to establish convergence. This approach not only validates the algorithms’ performance but also broadens the scope of the analysis to encompass related problems like variational inequalities. Furthermore, the framework facilitates the handling of non-separable loss functions and regularizers by reformulating them as saddle point problems. Consequently, the paper introduces algorithms that achieve a linear convergence rate for saddle point problems – a significant advancement given the prevalence of non-separable components in numerous machine learning models. The reinterpretation of saddle point problems as monotone operator zero-finding is a particularly insightful and potentially generalizable technique. Notably, the work also delivers simplified proofs for algorithms like FB splitting and its stochastic counterpart. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, employing non-uniform sampling, perform comparably to, and often surpass, existing methods. Overall, this paper represents a valuable extension to the field, offering a compelling analytical perspective. Given that the novelty lies primarily in the proof technique – while the algorithms are relatively straightforward extensions of existing ones – the authors could strengthen the paper by providing additional insight into the proof strategy itself, perhaps through the inclusion of a proof sketch, if space allows. The paper is generally well-written and clear; however, certain aspects could benefit from further elaboration. Specifically: 1. Clarification of the non-uniform sampling strategy (π) used for problems beyond bilinear saddle points would be helpful. 2. Details regarding the step size (σ) selection in the experiments are needed.  Finally, exploring the authors’ intuition regarding the apparent necessity of non-uniform sampling for saddle point problems – a phenomenon not consistently observed in convex minimization – and including a discussion of this observation would enhance the paper’s impact.
