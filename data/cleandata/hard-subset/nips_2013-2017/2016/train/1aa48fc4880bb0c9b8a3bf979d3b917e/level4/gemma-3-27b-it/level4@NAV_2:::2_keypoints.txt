- Addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems by extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) beyond separable objectives.  
- Provides convergence analysis for these extended algorithms, filling a gap in existing theory.  
- Identifies and discusses two notions of splitting, the necessity of convex-concave structure, and the benefit of non-uniform sampling.  
- Extends catalyst acceleration to the saddle-point setting, showing faster performance than accelerated batch methods.  
- Connects saddle-point algorithms to monotone operator theory, broadening theoretical applicability.  
- Empirical results on sido and rcv1 validate theoretical claims, highlighting gains from non-uniform sampling and acceleration.  
- Well-motivated and clearly explained relevance to ML tasks (non-separable losses/regularizers, robust optimization, convex relaxations).  
- Rigorous theoretical analysis and sound convergence proofs.  
- Clear articulation of differences between splitting strategies and thorough complexity comparisons to prior work.  
- Good literature review and proper positioning of contributions.  
- Main weakness: restrictive strong convexity assumption on both primal and dual problems (Assumption A).  
- Unclear how difficult it would be to relax adaptivity requirements or require strong convexity for only one side.  
- Experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more diverse/larger-scale datasets and sensitivity analyses to parameter settings.  
- Overall recommendation: strong technical contribution with practical validation; recommended for acceptance despite noted limitations.
