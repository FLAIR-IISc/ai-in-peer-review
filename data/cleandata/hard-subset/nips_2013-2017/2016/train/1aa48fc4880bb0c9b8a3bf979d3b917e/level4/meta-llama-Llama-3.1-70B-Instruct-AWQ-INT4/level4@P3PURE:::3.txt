The paper extends several recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite sum structure, which can arise from two types of splits: individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough study on the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and different (uniform/nonuniform) sampling strategies. Experiments on a special binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch algorithms. Although the idea of extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems may seem straightforward, the results established here are notably novel and technically non-trivial. For the important bilinear saddle point problem, the authors provide a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, which share some resemblance to what is usually known for the convex minimization case, and their findings are convincing.

However, I have a major concern. While solving the saddle point problem alleviates the difficulty of non-separability of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck, namely the O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Tables 1 and 2, is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version. Especially when the number of data points n is substantially larger than the dimension d, which is the case for most machine learning applications, the complexity under factored splits (later used in simulation) is actually worse than the batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike what is usually known for convex minimization, I do not see a clear substantial advantage of using variance reduction from a theoretical point of view. I would suggest that the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions: i) Is factored splitting (theoretically) worse than individual splitting? ii) When is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? iii) Is the dependence on n and d tight enough?
