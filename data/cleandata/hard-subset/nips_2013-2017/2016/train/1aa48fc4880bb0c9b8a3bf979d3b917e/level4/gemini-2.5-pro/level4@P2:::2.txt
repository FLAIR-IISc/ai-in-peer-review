This paper addresses the convex-concave saddle-point problem, particularly for objectives with a finite-sum structure. The authors extend recent and popular stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, such as SVRG and SAGA, to this setting. This is a notable contribution, as prior SVR methods were primarily designed for convex minimization problems and could not be directly applied to many machine learning tasks that are more naturally formulated as saddle-point problems, especially those involving non-separable losses or regularizers.

The core of the paper lies in providing the first linear convergence analysis for SVR-type algorithms in the saddle-point context. The analysis cleverly departs from standard convex minimization proofs and instead utilizes the more general framework of monotone operators. This approach not only establishes convergence for saddle-point problems but also shows that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The authors further enhance their methods by incorporating non-uniform sampling, which is shown to be crucial for efficiency, and by integrating an acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework. The experimental results on binary classification tasks with non-separable objectives demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms over existing batch and stochastic methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper fills an important gap by developing the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for finite-sum saddle-point problems. Extending variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA to this domain is a novel and significant step. This work makes a class of powerful optimization methods available for a wider range of machine learning problems, including structured output prediction, robust optimization, and certain convex relaxations in unsupervised learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. The use of monotone operator theory to prove convergence is elegant and provides a more fundamental understanding of why these algorithms work. This is a non-trivial extension of the existing analyses for SVRG/SAGA and demonstrates a deep command of the underlying mathematical principles. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical proofs.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and clearly articulating their contributions. The different types of splits (functional vs. factored) and the importance of non-uniform sampling are explained well.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed acceleration technique, adapted from the catalyst framework, provides a principled way to achieve faster convergence rates and results in an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods. The experiments, though limited to a specific problem type, effectively validate the theoretical claims and show a significant empirical performance gain for the proposed methods, especially the accelerated variant.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Strength of Assumptions:** The primary theoretical weakness is the reliance on the assumption that the function `M(x, y)` is strongly convex in `x` and strongly concave in `y`. While this is a standard assumption for proving linear convergence in this setting, many practical saddle-point formulations in machine learning only satisfy one of these conditions. For instance, a problem might involve a strongly convex regularizer on the primal variable `x` but lack a corresponding strongly concave term for the dual variable `y`. It would greatly enhance the paper's impact if the authors could discuss the possibility of relaxing this assumption, perhaps achieving sub-linear rates or linear rates under alternative conditions (e.g., strong convexity in only the primal or dual part).

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The paper mentions several compelling applications, including robust optimization and convex relaxations for unsupervised learning. However, the experiments are focused solely on a supervised classification task. While the chosen task effectively highlights the benefits of handling non-separable objectives, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on at least one other distinct problem class (e.g., a small-scale robust optimization problem) would provide stronger evidence for the broad applicability claimed in the introduction and make the empirical evaluation more comprehensive.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong and timely contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is novel, and the proposed algorithms are both practically relevant and empirically effective. The main limitation is the strong convexity-concavity assumption, which may not hold in all applications of interest. Nevertheless, the work is a significant advancement and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
