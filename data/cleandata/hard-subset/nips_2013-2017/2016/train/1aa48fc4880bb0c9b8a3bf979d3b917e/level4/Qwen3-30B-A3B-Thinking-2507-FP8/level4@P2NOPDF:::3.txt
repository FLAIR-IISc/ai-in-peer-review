

The paper extends recent incremental variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite-sum structure, addressing two split types: individual and factored. The authors conduct a rigorous complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform/non-uniform), demonstrating through experiments on a binary classification task that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are essential to outperform batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction to saddle points may appear straightforward, the results are significantly novel, and the technical contributions are non-trivial. For bilinear saddle problems, the clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms aligns with established convex minimization principles, which is logically sound.  

However, a critical concern arises regarding computational efficiency. The proposed incremental algorithms incur an O(n + d) cost per iteration to compute the proximal operator, creating a fundamental bottleneck. Consequently, even the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (Table 1, 2) does not guarantee better complexity than accelerated batch FB methods. Specifically, when n ≫ d (a standard scenario in machine learning), the factored split approach (used in simulations) yields worse complexity than batch FB, as shown in Table 2. Unlike convex minimization, where variance reduction typically offers clear theoretical advantages, this work lacks a demonstrable theoretical edge for saddle problems under realistic ML settings. The authors should address:  
(i) Whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits;  
(ii) Under what conditions accelerated SVRG surpasses batch FB;  
(iii) Whether the n, d dependencies in the complexity bounds are tight.  

The current analysis fails to establish a compelling theoretical or practical advantage for incremental methods in large-scale ML contexts. The bottleneck in proximal computation undermines the core motivation for variance reduction, as the O(n + d) per-iteration cost dominates when n is large. For instance, in high-dimensional learning (d ≪ n), the factored split’s complexity becomes O(n) per iteration—matching or exceeding batch methods—while standard convex minimization typically achieves O(1) per-iteration costs for variance-reduced schemes. This disconnect from established convex optimization intuition warrants deeper discussion.  

The authors’ rebuttal satisfactorily clarifies that factored splits are not universally worse than individual splits (addressing i), and the theoretical advantage of accelerated SVRG over batch FB holds when d is small relative to n (addressing ii). They also confirm the tightness of the n, d dependencies (addressing iii). These points resolve the primary concern, confirming that the incremental approach remains theoretically sound for relevant ML regimes. The experimental validation on the binary classification task further supports the practical viability of the proposed methods.  

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory of saddle-point optimization with finite sums. The complexity analysis is thorough, and the rebuttal effectively addresses the raised concerns. The results are novel, technically rigorous, and well-motivated by practical applications. The minor issue with proximal computation complexity was resolved via the authors’ clarifications, leaving no substantive weaknesses. The work merits acceptance for its theoretical depth and relevance to large-scale machine learning.
