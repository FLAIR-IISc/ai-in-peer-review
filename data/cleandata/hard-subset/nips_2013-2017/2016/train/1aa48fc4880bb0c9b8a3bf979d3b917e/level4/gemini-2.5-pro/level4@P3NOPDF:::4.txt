### Summary

This paper presents a significant extension of the stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving linear convergence for their proposed algorithms. A key strength of this work is its applicability to a broad class of problems involving non-separable components in both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., the fusion term). The theoretical and empirical results compellingly demonstrate the effectiveness of applying variance reduction to saddle-point optimization.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Contribution:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a timely and valuable contribution. It successfully bridges the gap between powerful variance reduction methods, which have been highly successful in convex optimization, and the important class of saddle-point problems that arise in machine learning, game theory, and other domains.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis is a core strength of the paper. Unlike the analysis for standard convex problems, the saddle-point setting introduces significant complexity. The authors adeptly employ the theory of monotone operators to establish their convergence proofs, a sophisticated approach that also shows the potential for these algorithms to be applied to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The proof of linear convergence for the strongly convex-concave case is a solid theoretical result.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Practicality:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed algorithms can handle challenging non-separable saddle-point problems. The authors thoughtfully propose two different splitting strategies for the gradient operator—the element-wise split and the factored split—which are adapted for SVRG and SAGA within the stochastic forward-backward framework.

4.  **Non-Uniform Sampling:** The inclusion and analysis of non-uniform sampling is a valuable addition. The paper provides both theoretical justification and empirical evidence that non-uniform sampling can lead to superior performance compared to standard uniform sampling, which is a useful practical insight.

5.  **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, detailed in both the main article and the appendix, are thorough and convincing. They provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims and clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods over existing baselines on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

### Minor Comments and Questions

The paper is very well-executed. My comments are minor and intended to help further clarify the work.

1.  **Acceleration Scheme:** In Section 5, the paper discusses an acceleration technique based on [8], which incorporates an additional regularization term to steer the update towards a reference point `(\bar{x}, \bar{y})`. It is not entirely clear how this reference point should be adaptively updated in practice to achieve the desired speed-up. Further clarification or a brief algorithmic description of this update strategy would be beneficial.

2.  **Acceleration for SAGA:** A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for the SVRG-based method. Do the same theoretical guarantees for acceleration hold for the SAGA-based variant? If so, it would be worth stating. If not, a brief discussion of the potential challenges in extending the proof to SAGA would be informative.

3.  **Notation:** There is a minor notational ambiguity where the symbol `K` is used to represent both the smooth function `K(·)` and the design matrix `K`. To avoid any potential confusion for the reader, it would be clearer to use distinct symbols for these two different mathematical objects.
