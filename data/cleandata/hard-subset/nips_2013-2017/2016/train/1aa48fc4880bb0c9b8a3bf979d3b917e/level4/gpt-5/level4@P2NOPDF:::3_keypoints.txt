- Extends SVRG, SAGA and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with two decomposition types (individual and factored splits).
- Provides a careful complexity analysis for both split types and for uniform vs. nonuniform sampling strategies.
- Experiments (specialized binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform strong batch baselines.
- Results are novel and technically nontrivial despite apparent straightforwardness of extending variance-reduction methods.
- For the bilinear saddle-point case, the paper gives a clear, sound comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms, mirroring known convex minimization patterns.
- Main concern: proximal computations appear to cost O(n + d) per iteration, creating a computational bottleneck that may eliminate benefits over batch methods when n >> d.
- Under factored splits (used in experiments) the resulting complexity can be worse than batch FB, so variance reduction lacks a clear theoretical advantage here.
- Asks authors to clarify: (i) is factored split theoretically worse than individual split; (ii) in which regimes is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB; (iii) are the n and d dependencies in the bounds tight?
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with the authors’ response but leaves scores unchanged.
