- Extends SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems using a monotone-operator forward–backward analysis, yielding linear convergence without separability.  
- Handles broader problem classes (e.g., variational inequalities) under the monotone operator framework.  
- Proposes two operator decompositions: element-wise splits and factored splits (e.g., rows/columns for bilinear K), enabling tailored importance sampling.  
- Develops a catalyst-style acceleration interpreted as a proximal-point on the center (x̄, ȳ), improving iteration complexity in analyzed regimes.  
- Connects and extends classical primal–dual and recent variance-reduction methods to the non-separable saddle-point setting.  
- Technical strength: clean monotone-operator proofs that avoid co-coercivity assumptions and establish linear rates for SVRG/SAGA.  
- Modeling breadth: supports non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient prox operators and offers storage/runtime trade-offs via split choices.  
- Importance sampling: provides problem-dependent non-uniform sampling bounds (L̄(π)) with clear complexity gains over uniform sampling.  
- Acceleration: integrates catalyst-style scheme with SVRG epochs for favorable complexities.  
- Empirical validation: experiments on two datasets show gains for variance-reduced and accelerated methods, especially in ill-conditioned cases.  
- Requires knowledge/estimates of strong convexity–concavity (λ, γ) and smoothness constants (L, L̄); practical guidance on estimation and robustness is lacking.  
- Empirical baselines limited; missing comparisons to standard primal–dual/extragradient methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock, Mirror-Prox).  
- Practicality of importance sampling unclear: cost of computing/maintaining row/column norms (especially for sparse/streaming data) is not discussed.  
- SAGA storage can be O(nd) for element-wise splits; more discussion on memory and preference for factored splits needed.  
- Acceleration implementation details under-specified: adaptive update strategies for (x̄, ȳ), stopping rules, and update frequency are not concrete.  
- Theory–practice gap: non-uniform SAGA requires resampling after updates per theory, but experiments omit this; effects are not quantified.  
- Paper is technically sound and careful; complexity summaries and sampling bounds are informative.  
- Clarity is generally good but notation (K vs matrix), grouped assumptions, and practical guidance (parameter estimation, default hyperparameters) should be improved.  
- Originality: novel extension of variance-reduction to non-separable saddle points via monotone operators and insightful importance sampling analysis.  
- Significance: high relevance to ML problems with non-separable structure; fills a gap by providing incremental, linearly convergent methods.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong theory, practical algorithms with importance sampling and acceleration, and supportive empirical evidence.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical baselines, under-specified practical aspects (parameter estimation, adaptivity, memory/runtime), and possibly overstated acceleration claims without wall-clock evidence.  
- Key author questions: how to estimate λ, γ, L, L̄; concrete adaptive policy for (x̄, ȳ) and τ; whether acceleration guarantees extend to SAGA; overhead of maintaining non-uniform sampling on large sparse data.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — substantial advance with convincing theory and promising experiments, but needs clearer practical guidance and broader empirical comparisons.
