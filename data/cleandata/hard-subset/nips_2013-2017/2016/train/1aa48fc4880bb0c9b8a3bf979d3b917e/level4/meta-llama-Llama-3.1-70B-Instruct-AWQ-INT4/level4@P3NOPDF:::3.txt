The paper extends several recent incremental algorithms, including SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite sum structure. These problems can arise from two types of splits: individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough study on the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies, including uniform and nonuniform sampling. Experimental results on a special binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch algorithms.

Although the idea of extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems may seem straightforward, the results established in this paper are notably novel, and the technicality involved is non-trivial. For the important bilinear saddle point problem, the authors present a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, which shares some resemblance to what is usually known for the convex minimization case. The findings are intuitive and well-supported.

However, I have a major concern regarding the proposed incremental algorithms. While solving the saddle point problem alleviates the difficulty of non-separable loss functions, these algorithms seem to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck: the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Table 1 and 2, is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version. This is particularly concerning when the number of data points n is substantially larger than its dimension d, which is often the case in most machine learning applications. Under factored splits, the complexity is actually worse than the batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2.

Unlike the convex minimization case, I did not observe a clear substantial advantage of using variance reduction from a theoretical perspective. To address this concern, I suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion on the following questions: (i) Is factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits? (ii) Under what conditions is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? (iii) Is the dependence on n and d tight enough?
