This paper proposes the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning. The authors extend recent stochastic variance reduction methods, such as SVRG and SAGA, to this class of problems. The algorithmic extension is straightforward, but it comes with challenges and opportunities. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these algorithms, which differs significantly from the associated convex minimization set-up. They also show that the saddle-point formulation allows for two different notions of splits, in terms of functions or in terms of partial derivatives, and that non-uniform sampling is key to an efficient algorithm.

The paper makes several contributions, including providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms for saddle-point problems, showing that the same algorithm applies to a larger class of problems, such as variational inequalities, and demonstrating that the incremental algorithms can be easily accelerated using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework.

The authors also provide numerical results, which show that the proposed methods improve significantly over existing batch or stochastic algorithms. The results demonstrate that uniform sampling for SAGA does not improve over batch methods, while SAGA and accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling improve significantly over existing methods.

However, the strong convex assumptions on both primal and dual problems are very strong. It would be more realistic to assume that only one of the problems is strongly convex. Additionally, more numerical examples should be added to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of machine learning by proposing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems. The convergence analysis and numerical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, and the extension to monotone operators provides a broader framework for solving a wider range of problems.
