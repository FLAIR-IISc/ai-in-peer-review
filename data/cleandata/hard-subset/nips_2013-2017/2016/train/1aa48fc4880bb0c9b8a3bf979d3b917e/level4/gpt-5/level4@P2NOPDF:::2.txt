This paper studies convex–concave saddle-point optimization and extends recent variance-reduced stochastic methods to this setting. The authors provide convergence analyses and demonstrate that their algorithms can be accelerated via an adaptation of the Catalyst framework. They further outline an extension to monotone operators and include numerical experiments.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important class of problems by bringing variance reduction techniques to convex–concave saddle-point formulations.
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees.
- Shows how to incorporate acceleration through a Catalyst-style scheme.
- Discusses a broader monotone operator setting and offers empirical validation.

Concerns and suggestions:
- The assumption of strong convexity on both the primal and dual sides appears unnecessarily stringent. In many saddle-point problems, it is typical (and sufficient) to assume strong convexity on only one side (either primal or dual). Please clarify whether both-sided strong convexity is truly required by your analysis, or relax this to one-sided strong convexity if possible. If both-sided strong convexity is retained, provide justification and discuss whether adding a small regularization to one side would suffice to obtain similar rates, along with the impact on constants and conditioning.
- The experimental section is limited. To substantiate the claims, please add more numerical examples across diverse datasets and problem types. Include comparisons with strong baselines (e.g., non–variance-reduced primal–dual methods, extragradient variants, and existing accelerated methods), report wall-clock time and iteration/gradient-oracle complexity, and provide ablations on the effect of Catalyst acceleration and key hyperparameters (step sizes, batch sizes, inner-loop tolerances). A sensitivity analysis and scalability study (problem size, condition number) would improve the empirical narrative.
- The presentation would benefit from greater clarity. Consider adding precise statements of assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity/monotonicity), self-contained algorithm pseudocode with all parameters, and a short proof sketch highlighting the key ideas behind the variance-reduction and acceleration analyses. Make explicit the complexity bounds (in terms of gradient/prox evaluations) and how they depend on condition numbers. For the monotone-operator extension, specify the operator properties required and whether convergence is for last iterates or ergodic averages.
- Please clarify the compatibility with proximal terms and inexact inner solves within the Catalyst framework, and how such inexactness affects the overall convergence rate.

Overall, the theory is promising and the acceleration connection is appealing, but the current assumptions seem overly strong and the experiments are not yet comprehensive. Relaxing the strong convexity requirements to one side (or clearly justifying the two-sided assumption) and expanding the empirical evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.
