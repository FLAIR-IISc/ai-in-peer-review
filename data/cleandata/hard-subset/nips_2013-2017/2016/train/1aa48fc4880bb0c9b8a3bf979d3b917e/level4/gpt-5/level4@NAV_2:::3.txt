The paper broadens recent incremental variance-reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their Catalyst-based acceleration to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure, accommodating two types of decompositions (individual vs. factored). The authors present a careful complexity analysis across these decompositions and under different sampling schemes (uniform and nonuniform). Experiments on a specific binary classification setup indicate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to surpass strong batch baselines. While the algorithmic extension may seem straightforward, the results are genuinely new and the analysis is technically nontrivial. For the important bilinear saddle-point case, the paper provides a transparent comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms, closely paralleling what is known for convex minimization; the narrative is consistent and convincing.

My principal concern is a potential bottleneck: evaluating the proximal operator appears to cost O(n + d) per iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling, as summarized in Tables 1 and 2—is not always superior to accelerated batch methods. In particular, when n ≫ d (a common regime in machine learning), the complexity under factored splits (the setting used in the experiments) can be worse than batch forward–backward, as indicated in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization setting, I do not yet see a clear theoretical regime where variance reduction offers a decisive advantage. I encourage the authors to address this point more thoroughly and to clarify: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, (ii) under what conditions accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch forward–backward, and (iii) whether the dependencies on n and d in the bounds are tight.
