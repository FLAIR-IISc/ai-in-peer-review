- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems for two split types (individual and factored).
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across split types and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform).
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch methods.
- Results are novel and technically challenging despite apparent straightforwardness of extension.
- For bilinear saddle problems, the paper gives a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, paralleling convex minimization theory.
- Major concern: each iteration requires prox_M costing O(n + d), which may negate complexity gains.
- When n >> d (common in ML), factored splits used in experiments can be worse than batch FB, per Table 2.
- Theoretical advantage of variance reduction is unclear in this setting—no substantial improvement over batch methods is evident.
- Reviewer requests clarification on whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits.
- Reviewer asks under what conditions accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB.
- Reviewer asks how tight the dependence on n and d is in the complexity bounds.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, rigorous monotone-operator-based analysis, thoroughness (theory + experiments), clarity, and potential significance.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear practical advantage due to proximal cost and heavy dependence on tuning regularization parameters (λ, γ).
