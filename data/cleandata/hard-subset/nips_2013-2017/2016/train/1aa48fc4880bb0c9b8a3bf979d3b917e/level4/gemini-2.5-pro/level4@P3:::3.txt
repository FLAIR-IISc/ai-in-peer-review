### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends recent variance-reduced stochastic gradient methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are common in machine learning, particularly for models with non-separable losses or regularizers. The authors make several key contributions:
1.  They provide the first linear convergence analysis for these incremental algorithms in the saddle-point setting. The analysis is notably based on the theory of monotone operators, making it more general and applicable to problems like variational inequalities.
2.  They introduce and analyze two different ways to decompose the problem structure: "individual" and "factored" splits.
3.  They demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that non-uniform sampling is crucial for achieving superior performance.
4.  They show how to accelerate these new methods using the Catalyst framework, leading to an algorithm that is provably better than accelerated batch methods under certain conditions.

The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical study of the complexity of these algorithms under various splits and sampling strategies, culminating in experiments on a binary classification task that validate the benefits of acceleration and non-uniform sampling.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The extension of variance-reduced methods to saddle-point problems is a novel and important contribution. It opens the door for applying these powerful techniques to a broader class of machine learning problems (e.g., AUC maximization, robust optimization, structured prediction) where the primal or dual formulations are not easily separable. The technical analysis is non-trivial and represents a significant step forward from the standard convex minimization setting.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, grounded in the theory of monotone operators, is elegant and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The theoretical comparisons between batch, stochastic, and the new incremental methods are thorough and clearly presented.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, outlining their contributions, and comparing their results to existing work. The complexity results summarized in Table 1 are particularly helpful for understanding the trade-offs between different approaches.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

My main concern relates to the theoretical complexity and the practical advantage of the proposed methods compared to existing batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction to this new domain is an excellent idea, the theoretical gains are not as clear-cut as in the standard convex minimization case.

1.  **Proximal Operator Bottleneck:** The proposed incremental algorithms appear to suffer from a potential bottleneck: the `O(n+d)` cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Since this full-dimensional operation is required in every single update, it may diminish the benefits of using a cheap stochastic gradient estimate.

2.  **Comparison with Accelerated Batch Methods:** Consequently, the overall complexity of the proposed methods is not necessarily superior to the accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm. Specifically, looking at the complexity results in Table 1 for the bilinear case with factored splits (which were used in the experiments), the dependency on `n` and `d` for accelerated SVRG is `(nd + ||K||_F^2 / (λγ)) log(1/ε)`. The accelerated batch method has a complexity of `(nd + n||K||_op / sqrt(λγ)) log(1/ε)`. In many machine learning scenarios where the number of samples `n` is substantially larger than the dimension `d`, the term `||K||_F^2` can be much larger than `n||K||_op`, potentially making the incremental method theoretically slower than its batch counterpart. This contrasts sharply with the convex minimization case, where variance-reduced methods often show a clear theoretical advantage.

This leads to the following questions, which I would appreciate the authors addressing:

*   Could you provide more insight into the theoretical trade-offs between **individual splits** and **factored splits**? The complexity for individual splits appears potentially better in some regimes, yet factored splits were used in the experiments. Is there a practical reason for this choice (e.g., storage)?
*   Could you more clearly delineate the regimes (in terms of `n`, `d`, problem structure, and the relative norms of `K`) where the proposed accelerated SVRG method offers a clear **theoretical advantage** over the accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm? The experiments show a practical advantage, but the theoretical justification seems less universal than for standard convex problems.
*   Are the derived dependencies on `n` and `d` in the complexity bounds **tight**? It would be valuable to understand if a more refined analysis could potentially mitigate the concerns raised above and demonstrate a broader theoretical advantage for the variance-reduction approach.

In summary, while the paper presents a valuable and technically impressive extension of SVRG/SAGA, the discussion of its practical utility would be strengthened by a more nuanced comparison of the theoretical complexity against strong batch baselines, clarifying when and why these new methods are expected to be superior.
