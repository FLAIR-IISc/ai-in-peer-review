The authors propose the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems by leveraging variance reduction algorithms such as SVRG and SAGA. Notably, the convex minimization analysis does not directly apply to saddle point problems, making the primary contribution of this paper the utilization of monotone operators to establish convergence. As a result, the analysis can be extended to a broader class of problems, including variational inequality problems. This approach enables the tackling of non-separable loss functions and regularizers by formulating the problem as a saddle point problem. The paper presents the first algorithms with a linear convergence rate for saddle point problems, which is a significant achievement, as it facilitates the handling of non-separable loss functions and regularizers commonly encountered in various machine learning models.

The proof technique, which involves interpreting saddle point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator, is intriguing and potentially widely applicable. Additionally, the authors provide simpler proofs for algorithms such as the FB splitting (as well as its stochastic version). The proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling demonstrate competitiveness and, in most cases, outperform previous alternatives.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by extending the analysis approach in an important direction. However, since the primary contribution lies in the proof technique (with the algorithms being relatively straightforward extensions of existing ones), the authors may consider including more details about the proof technique in the main paper, such as a proof sketch, if space permits.

The paper is generally well-presented and clearly written, but some aspects could be clarified. Specifically, the following points require further explanation: 

1. The type of non-uniform sampling used for problems other than bilinear saddle-point problems is unclear. What is the specific form of π in these cases?
2. The method for setting the step size σ in the experiments is not explicitly stated. Furthermore, the authors may provide insight into why non-uniform sampling appears to be essential for saddle point problems, but not necessarily for convex minimization problems. A discussion on this topic could enhance the paper.
