

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The work builds upon previous variance reduction techniques for convex optimization (such as SVRG [2] and SAGA [3]) but addresses the unique challenges of saddle-point problems through a novel theoretical framework using monotone operators. Unlike convex minimization where the analysis relies on convexity properties, the authors demonstrate that the saddle-point problem can be interpreted as finding zeros of monotone operators, allowing them to prove linear convergence for their extended algorithms. This theoretical contribution is significant as it extends beyond saddle-point problems to cover variational inequalities, a broader class of problems relevant to game theory [5, 6].

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) it establishes the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in saddle-point settings, (2) it introduces two distinct gradient operator splits (element-wise for SVRG and factored for SAGA) that leverage the saddle-point structure, (3) it demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is theoretically and empirically superior to uniform sampling for these problems, and (4) it shows how to accelerate these algorithms using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework [7], resulting in methods that consistently outperform accelerated batch algorithms.

The paper excels in quality through its rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical validation. The convergence proofs are technically sound, and the experimental results on both non-separable loss and regularizer problems (including structured output prediction and fusion terms) provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-organized sections that make the complex material accessible to readers familiar with optimization techniques. The originality is substantial, as the authors successfully adapt variance reduction methods to a new problem class where they previously did not apply, while also demonstrating broader applicability to variational inequalities. The significance of this work is high, as saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., in robust optimization, structured prediction, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning), and the proposed methods offer substantial practical improvements over existing approaches.

Strengths:
- First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems using variance reduction
- Novel monotone operator-based convergence analysis that extends to variational inequalities
- Comprehensive theoretical comparison showing non-uniform sampling superiority
- Strong empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world problems
- Practical acceleration framework that consistently outperforms batch methods

Weaknesses:
- The adaptive update of the iterate point (\bar{x}, \bar{y}) in the acceleration framework (Section 5) is not clearly explained, making it unclear how to optimally update this point for maximum speed-up
- While the paper provides convergence and acceleration proofs for SVRG, it does not explicitly address whether identical theoretical results hold for SAGA, which would strengthen the theoretical foundation
- The notation is confusing as the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K are represented using the same symbol, potentially causing reader confusion

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the optimization literature with clear theoretical and practical implications for machine learning applications. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational concerns and clarify the adaptive update procedure for the acceleration framework.
