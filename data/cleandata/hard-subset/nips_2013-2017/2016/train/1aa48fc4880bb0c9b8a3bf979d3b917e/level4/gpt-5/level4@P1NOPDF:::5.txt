This paper uses variance-reduction methods such as SVRG and SAGA to develop the first algorithms with linear convergence for saddle-point problems. Because standard convex minimization analyses do not carry over to saddle points, the key contribution is a convergence proof based on monotone operator theory. Interpreting the saddle-point problem as finding a zero of a monotone operator not only delivers the desired convergence guarantees but also extends the analysis to a broader class, including variational inequalities. Framing non-separable losses and regularizers as a saddle-point problem is particularly useful, as such structures arise in many machine learning models.

The proof technique is interesting and likely broadly applicable. In particular, the paper provides simpler proofs for forward–backward (FB) splitting and its stochastic counterpart. Empirically, the proposed non-uniform sampling variants are competitive and, in most cases, outperform previous alternatives. Overall, the paper advances the field in an important direction with a clean and general analysis.

Since the primary novelty lies in the analysis rather than in algorithmic design (the algorithms themselves are relatively straightforward extensions of existing methods), it would strengthen the paper to include more details of the proof technique in the main text, space permitting—for example, a concise proof sketch that highlights the key monotone-operator arguments and where they diverge from standard convex minimization proofs.

Clarity questions and suggestions:
- What specific non-uniform sampling distributions (π) are used beyond the bilinear saddle-point setting? A clear prescription or guideline for general problems would be helpful.
- How is the step size σ chosen in the experiments? Please specify whether it is theoretically motivated, tuned on a validation set, or selected via heuristic rules, and report the sensitivity if possible.
- Do the authors have intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization? A discussion contrasting the variance structure, conditioning, or coupling between primal and dual variables in the saddle-point setting would be valuable.
