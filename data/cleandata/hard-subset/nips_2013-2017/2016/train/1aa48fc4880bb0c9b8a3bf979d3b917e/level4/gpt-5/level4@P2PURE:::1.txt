This paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems and shows how to tackle them using existing stochastic variance-reduction methods (e.g., SVRG and SAGA) combined with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms within a monotone-operator framework and establish linear convergence. They also propose an accelerated variant and examine a non-uniform sampling scheme.

Novelty/originality: The contributions are substantial. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides new insights into saddle-point problems. The paper also presents several extensions, the most notable being that the method applies to non-separable functions.

Technical quality and presentation: While the theoretical contributions are strong, the presentation could be improved. In particular, the connection to monotone operators should be made clearer. Although this appears as an extension in Section 6, the core analysis relies on monotone operators. On the experimental side, results on more than two datasets would strengthen the empirical support. For separable functions, the paper should also compare against accelerated methods such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle–Pock algorithm: http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf Please add this reference to the submission.

Points for clarification:
1. How strong are Assumptions (A)–(C), especially (A)? For instance, do these assumptions hold for the saddle-point formulation of SVMs?
2. I do not fully understand the differences between standard stochastic primal–dual methods and the proposed approach. Could the authors elaborate on this comparison?
3. In Theorem 2, I am unsure about the constant μ. Is it the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? How should this result be compared to Theorem 1?
4. The transition from Assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is confusing. Do Assumptions (A)–(C) imply monotonicity?

Minor issues:
- There seems to be a mix-up regarding the constant L, used both for the condition number and for a Lipschitz constant: “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” versus “we need the Lipschitz constant L.”
- Please add a reference for the Forward–Backward algorithm.
