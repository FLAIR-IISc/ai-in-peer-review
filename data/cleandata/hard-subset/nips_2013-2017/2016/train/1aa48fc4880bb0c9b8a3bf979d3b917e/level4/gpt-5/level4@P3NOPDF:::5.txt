Summary:
The paper adapts variance-reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to saddle-point problems and establishes the first linear convergence rates for this setting under a monotone operator framework. By interpreting saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, the analysis extends beyond convex minimization to broader classes such as variational inequalities. The approach also yields simpler proofs for Forward–Backward splitting (and its stochastic variant). Empirically, the proposed non-uniform sampling schemes are competitive and often outperform prior alternatives. The work addresses an important gap, particularly for handling non-separable losses and regularizers common in modern machine learning.

Strengths:
- Conceptual contribution: The monotone operator perspective is elegant and broadly applicable, enabling unified analyses and cleaner proofs. This could influence future work on stochastic methods for structured non-smooth problems.
- Novelty and significance: Establishing linear convergence for finite-sum saddle-point problems is an impactful advance, especially given the limitations of standard convex minimization analyses in this setting.
- Practical relevance: The ability to handle non-separable structures expands applicability to realistic models.
- Empirical performance: Non-uniform sampling variants perform strongly and appear to be robust across tasks.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Scope of the “first” claim: Please clearly delimit the setting in which your methods are the first to achieve linear convergence (e.g., finite-sum, strongly monotone–Lipschitz operators, stochastic variance-reduction). It would help to contrast with classical methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock, Mirror-Prox, extragradient) and specify conditions where those do or do not achieve linear rates.
- Assumptions and constants: The theory appears to rely on monotonicity/strong monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity. Please state these assumptions explicitly upfront, and summarize the resulting convergence rates with their dependence on problem condition numbers and n. An at-a-glance theorem statement or table (even in the appendix) comparing rates and oracle complexities would aid readers.
- Non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear cases: For general (non-bilinear) saddle-point problems, what exact sampling distribution π do you use? Is π proportional to coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants or some block-wise smoothness/strong monotonicity measure? How is π computed or estimated in practice, and what is the overhead? If adaptive, how often is it updated?
- Stepsize selection: How is the stepsize σ chosen in experiments? Is it derived from theory (e.g., via Lipschitz constants) or tuned on a validation set? Please include the range/grid used, any warm-starting or scaling rules, and sensitivity analyses. Reporting performance under uniform vs non-uniform sampling across a range of σ would be informative.
- Why non-uniform sampling helps: The empirical results suggest non-uniform sampling is more critical for saddle-point problems than for standard convex minimization. Please provide intuition and, if possible, a brief theoretical discussion. For example, do imbalances in operator Lipschitz constants across blocks create a bottleneck that non-uniform sampling mitigates? Are there counterexamples where uniform sampling performs comparably?
- Practical guidance: Given the simplified proofs via the operator view, it would be helpful to include a brief proof sketch in the main text summarizing the key inequalities and how variance reduction is controlled for monotone operators. Also, offer guidance on when to prefer SVRG vs SAGA variants and how to set epoch lengths and batch sizes in practice.
- Experimental details: Please expand the experimental section with:
  - Datasets, models, and exact formulations of the saddle-point problems tackled (including non-separable terms).
  - Baselines and their tuning protocols to ensure fair comparison.
  - Runtime vs iteration plots and per-iteration cost comparisons, since variance-reduced methods may incur higher overhead.
  - An ablation on sampling distributions (uniform, Lipschitz-based, other heuristics) and their computational costs.

Presentation:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The monotone operator framing is a strong unifying theme. However, given that the main novelty lies in the analysis, the paper would benefit from:
- A concise proof sketch in the main text highlighting the core ideas and where the saddle-point setting diverges from convex minimization analyses.
- A brief discussion connecting your results to classical operator-splitting methods (FB, stochastic FB, extragradient), clarifying similarities and differences.

Overall assessment:
This work makes a solid theoretical and practical contribution by extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems with linear convergence and by using a clean monotone operator framework that appears broadly useful. With additional clarity on assumptions, sampling strategies, stepsize choices, and experimental protocols, the paper would be strong. I lean positive on acceptance.
