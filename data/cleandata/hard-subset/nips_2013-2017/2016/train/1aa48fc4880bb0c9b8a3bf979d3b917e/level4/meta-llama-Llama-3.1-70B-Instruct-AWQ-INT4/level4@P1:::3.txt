The paper extends several recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite sum structure, which could result from two types of splits, individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough study on the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and different (uniform/nonuniform) sampling strategies. Experiments on a special binary classification example show that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beating competitive batch algorithms. While the idea of extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle-point problems might sound straightforward, the results established here are notably novel, and the technicality is surely non-trivial.

For the important bilinear saddle-point problem, the authors show a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, which share some resemblance to what's usually known for the convex minimization case, and it all makes sense. However, here is my major concern. While solving the saddle-point problem cures the difficulty of non-separability of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms seemingly suffer from a fundamental bottleneck, i.e., O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Table 1 and 2, is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version. Especially, when the number of data n is substantially larger than its dimension d, which is the case for most machine learning applications, the complexity under factored splits (later used in simulation) is actually worse than the batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike what's usually known for convex minimization, I did not see a clear substantial advantage of using variance reduction here, from a theoretical point of view.

I would suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions: i) Is factored splits (theoretically) worse than individual splits? ii) When is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? iii) Is the dependence on n, d, tight enough?

Strengths: 
- The paper provides a thorough study on the complexity of incremental algorithms for solving strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite sum structure.
- The authors show a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms for the important bilinear saddle-point problem.
- The experiments demonstrate the importance of nonuniform sampling and acceleration in beating competitive batch algorithms.

Weaknesses:
- The proposed incremental algorithms suffer from a fundamental bottleneck, i.e., O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration.
- The overall complexity of the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version, especially when n is substantially larger than d.
- The dependence on n, d might not be tight enough.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide an in-depth discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms, including the bottleneck of computing the proximal operator.
- Clarify the advantages and disadvantages of factored splits compared to individual splits.
- Investigate the conditions under which the accelerated SVRG is favorable against the batch FB.
- Tighten the dependence on n, d to provide a more accurate complexity analysis.

Acceptance: 
Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would suggest accepting the paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns raised above and provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and advantages of their proposed algorithms.
