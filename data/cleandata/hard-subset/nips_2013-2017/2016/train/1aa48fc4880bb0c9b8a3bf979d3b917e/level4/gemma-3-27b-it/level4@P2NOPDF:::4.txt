This paper presents extensions of the stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods SVRG and SAGA to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence guarantees. The authors demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that these variance reduction techniques are effective in this challenging setting. A key contribution lies in extending SVR methods to non-separable saddle-point problems, applicable to scenarios with non-separable loss functions (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., fusion terms).

Unlike previous convergence analyses for convex optimization, analyzing saddle-point problems necessitates a more sophisticated approach. The authors successfully leverage the theory of monotone operators to prove convergence, showcasing a significant analytical advance. This analysis broadens the applicability of the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms to a wider range of problems, including variational inequalities prevalent in game theory.

To tackle the stochastic nature of saddle-point problems, the paper explores two gradient operator splitting strategies – element-wise and factored splits – tailored for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. These variance reduction techniques are applied within the context of stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly employed for solving saddle-point problems. Furthermore, the authors investigate the impact of non-uniform sampling, demonstrating through both theoretical analysis and experimental results that it outperforms uniform sampling in their proposed methods. 

The experimental evaluations, presented in the main text and supplementary material, provide strong support for the theoretical findings and illustrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms on problems featuring both non-separable losses and regularizers.

However, a few minor points warrant further clarification. In Section 5, the authors employ a regularization term, inspired by [8], to accelerate convergence by guiding the update towards a previous iterate point (\bar{x}, \bar{y}). The mechanism for *adaptively* updating this iterate point to maximize speed-up remains unclear. While a convergence and acceleration proof is provided specifically for SVRG, it is not evident whether analogous theoretical results would hold for SAGA. Finally, to improve clarity, the authors should consider using distinct notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K, as their current identical symbol usage introduces potential confusion.
