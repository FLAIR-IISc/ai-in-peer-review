- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated (Catalyst) variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems with individual and factored splits.  
- Provides a thorough complexity analysis under different splits and uniform/nonuniform sampling strategies.  
- Experiments (binary classification) indicate nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch methods.  
- The extension of variance reduction/incremental algorithms to saddle points is novel and technically non-trivial.  
- For the bilinear saddle point case, the paper gives a clear, convincing comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms.  
- Major concern: incremental algorithms require an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator per iteration, creating a fundamental bottleneck.  
- Consequently, overall complexity (even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch FB; factored splits can be worse than batch when n >> d.  
- Reviewer requests an in-depth discussion addressing: (i) Is factored splitting theoretically worse than individual splitting? (ii) When is accelerated SVRG favorable versus batch FB? (iii) Are the n and d dependence bounds tight?  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors’ response but keeps the original scores.
