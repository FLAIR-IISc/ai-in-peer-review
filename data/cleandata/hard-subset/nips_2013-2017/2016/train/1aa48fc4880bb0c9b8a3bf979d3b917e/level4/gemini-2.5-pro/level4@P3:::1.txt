This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems, particularly those where the objective function can be decomposed into a sum of many components. The authors propose extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to this class of problems. The core of their analysis is based on the theory of monotone operators, which not only provides a framework for proving linear convergence but also shows that the algorithms apply to a broader set of problems, such as variational inequalities. The paper explores different ways to split the objective (functional vs. partial derivative splits), highlights the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and introduces an accelerated variant using the "Catalyst" framework. This work builds upon the successful line of research in variance reduction for convex minimization (e.g., SVRG, SAGA) and extends it to the more general saddle-point setting, which has been previously tackled primarily by batch or standard stochastic algorithms.

**Strengths (Originality and Significance)**

The primary contribution of this paper is significant and timely. It provides the first large-scale, linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems. The key strengths are:

1.  **Practical Impact:** The ability to handle non-separable loss functions and regularizers is a major advantage. Many existing fast stochastic methods require separability in either the primal or dual formulation, which is not always present in modern machine learning problems (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization). This work unlocks the potential for variance-reduced methods in these important application areas.

2.  **Thorough Theoretical Analysis:** The analysis is comprehensive and rigorous. By grounding the convergence proofs in the theory of monotone operators, the authors provide a powerful and generalizable framework. This approach is more fundamental than a direct extension of the convex minimization analysis and correctly identifies that properties like co-coercivity are not required.

3.  **Novel Insights:** The paper offers valuable new insights specific to the saddle-point setting. The discussion on different splitting strategies (factored vs. individual) and the clear theoretical and empirical benefits of non-uniform sampling are important practical contributions.

4.  **Acceleration:** The integration with the "Catalyst" framework to provide an accelerated algorithm is a strong addition, demonstrating a principled way to achieve even faster convergence rates, which is confirmed by the experiments on ill-conditioned problems.

**Weaknesses (Clarity and Quality)**

While the technical contributions are excellent, the paper's presentation and experimental evaluation could be improved.

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper's structure is somewhat confusing. The analysis is fundamentally based on monotone operators, yet this is presented as a late "extension" in Section 6. This framing makes the main sections harder to appreciate and obscures the foundation of the theoretical results, which are detailed in the appendix from this perspective. The paper would be much stronger and more accessible if the monotone operator framework were introduced earlier as the core analytical tool for the saddle-point problem, rather than as an afterthought.

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The empirical results are promising but based on only two datasets. To make a more compelling case for the practical superiority of the proposed methods, an evaluation on a wider variety of datasets and problem types would be beneficial.

3.  **Missing Experimental Comparisons:** For the case of separable functions, the comparison is incomplete. The authors should compare their methods against other relevant stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems. A particularly relevant baseline would be a stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm, such as the one proposed by Zhang and Xiao (2015). This reference should also be included in the related work section.
    *   *Zhang, Y., & Xiao, L. (2015). Stochastic primal-dual coordinate method for regularized empirical risk minimization. In Proceedings of the 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML).*

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  **Assumptions:** How restrictive are Assumptions (A)-(C) in practice, particularly the strong convex-concave property in Assumption (A)? For example, does the standard saddle-point formulation of a linear SVM satisfy this assumption?

2.  **Relation to Stochastic Primal-Dual Methods:** Could you further elaborate on the key differences between the proposed methods and the broader class of stochastic primal-dual algorithms? While the paper compares against batch and standard SGD-style methods, a clearer positioning with respect to other advanced stochastic primal-dual techniques would be helpful.

3.  **Clarity of Theorem 2:** In the statement of Theorem 2, the constant `μ` is not defined in the main body of the paper. Assuming it is the strong monotonicity constant from the appendix, could you provide a more direct comparison of the convergence rates between Theorem 1 (SVRG) and Theorem 2 (SAGA)? This would make it easier to understand the theoretical trade-offs between the two algorithms.

4.  **Theoretical Framework Transition:** The connection between Assumptions (A)-(C) and the strong monotonicity framework used in the appendix proofs is not explicitly drawn. Could you clarify how Assumptions (A)-(C) for the saddle-point problem imply the strong monotonicity of the operator `A+B`? Making this link explicit would significantly improve the paper's clarity.

**Minor Issues**

*   The constant `L` appears to be used interchangeably as a condition number ("The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem") and a Lipschitz constant ("we need the Lipschitz-constant L"). Please clarify the definition and usage.
*   In Section 2.2, please add a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions that advance the state-of-the-art in optimization for machine learning. The weaknesses are primarily related to presentation and the scope of the experimental section, which can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
