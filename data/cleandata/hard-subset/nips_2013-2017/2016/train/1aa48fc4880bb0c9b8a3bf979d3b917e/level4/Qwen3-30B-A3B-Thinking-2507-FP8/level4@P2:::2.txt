

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this important class of problems in machine learning. The authors demonstrate how to adapt existing techniques from convex minimization to saddle-point settings, addressing key differences in the convergence analysis through the lens of monotone operators. This work builds upon seminal NIPS papers on variance reduction (e.g., Johnson & Zhang 2013, Defazio et al. 2014) while addressing a distinct problem class that has received less attention in the literature.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a convergence analysis using monotone operators that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities; (2) a careful examination of two different notions of splits (in terms of functions versus partial derivatives) and the necessity of convex-concave splits; (3) a thorough analysis of non-uniform sampling strategies that yield theoretical and practical improvements; and (4) a simple extension of the catalyst framework for acceleration, resulting in algorithms that consistently outperform accelerated batch methods.

Quality: The paper provides technically sound theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees. The authors carefully address the differences between convex minimization and saddle-point problems, using monotone operators to establish linear convergence without relying on convex minimization techniques. The complexity analysis is comprehensive, with careful attention to the interplay between problem structure and algorithmic performance. The experiments are well-designed to validate the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The paper acknowledges the strong convexity-concavity assumptions as a limitation and discusses potential extensions for future work.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient background to make the technical content accessible to readers familiar with optimization. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, though the extension to monotone operators might require some familiarity with operator theory. The authors provide concrete examples of machine learning applications where their methods are particularly relevant, including non-separable losses in supervised learning and robust optimization. The experimental section clearly demonstrates the advantages of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Originality: This work makes a significant contribution by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, a class of problems that has been largely overlooked in the stochastic optimization literature despite their prevalence in machine learning applications. While prior work (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) focused on convex minimization, this paper demonstrates how to adapt these techniques to saddle-point problems with a fundamentally different convergence analysis. The extension to monotone operators further broadens the applicability of the results, making this work substantially different from previous NIPS papers on variance reduction.

Significance: The proposed algorithms address an important gap in the literature, providing the first linearly convergent methods for large-scale saddle-point problems. This has clear practical implications for machine learning applications where saddle-point formulations are natural (e.g., robust optimization, supervised learning with non-separable losses, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning). The experimental results confirm the theoretical advantages, showing consistent improvements over existing batch and stochastic methods. The acceleration via the catalyst framework is particularly valuable for practitioners seeking efficient implementations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First to provide linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms for saddle-point problems
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with careful attention to differences from convex minimization
- Strong experimental validation demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods
- Practical contributions including non-uniform sampling strategies and acceleration framework
- Well-motivated examples of real-world machine learning applications

Arguments against acceptance:
- The strong convexity-concavity assumptions, while well-justified, may limit applicability to some problems
- The paper could include more examples of non-separable losses and regularizers in practice
- The connection to recent NIPS work on saddle-point problems (e.g., Zhu & Storkey 2014, Zhang & Xiao 2014) could be more thoroughly discussed

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with clear practical significance. The authors have carefully considered the differences between convex minimization and saddle-point problems, and their analysis provides valuable theoretical insights. The experimental results robustly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating practical advantages over existing methods.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS and would be of interest to the broader machine learning community. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for machine learning, and their work has the potential to impact a wide range of applications. While the strong convexity assumptions are a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and suggest potential extensions for future work.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is original, significant, and technically sound, with clear contributions to both theory and practice. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would likely stimulate further research in this important area.
