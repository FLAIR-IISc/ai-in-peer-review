The authors leverage variance reduction algorithms such as SVRG and SAGA to present the first algorithms with a linear convergence rate for saddle point problems. Because standard convex minimization analysis does not directly apply to saddle point problems, a key contribution of this work is the use of monotone operators to establish convergence. A significant consequence of this approach is that the analysis generalizes to a broader class of problems, including variational inequality problems. This methodology offers a pathway to address non-separable loss functions and regularizers by reformulating them as saddle point problems. The paper delivers the first algorithms achieving linear convergence for saddle point problems – an important advancement given the prevalence of non-separable loss functions and regularizers in numerous machine learning models. The proof technique, which interprets saddle point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator, is insightful and potentially widely applicable. Furthermore, the paper provides simpler proofs for algorithms like the forward-backward (FB) splitting method and its stochastic variant. The proposed algorithms, employing non-uniform sampling, demonstrate competitive performance and generally outperform existing alternatives. Overall, this work represents a substantial extension to the field, and the analytical approach is particularly compelling. Given that the primary contribution lies in the proof technique—while the algorithms themselves are relatively straightforward extensions of existing methods—the authors might consider including additional details regarding the proof, such as a proof sketch, within the main paper if space allows. The paper is generally clearly presented and well-written. However, I found certain aspects somewhat unclear. Specifically: 1. For problems beyond bilinear saddle-point problems, what specific non-uniform sampling strategy is employed, and what is the form of \pi? 2. How is the step size \sigma determined in the experimental setup? Do the authors have any intuition as to why non-uniform sampling appears so critical for saddle point problems, but not necessarily for convex minimization problems?
