This paper proposes an extension of the stochastic variance reduction techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, achieving linear convergence. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their variance reduction method through both theoretical and empirical results. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a wide range of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable.

A significant contribution of this paper is its convergence analysis on saddle-point problems, which is more complex compared to previous analyses on convex problems. The authors employ monotone operators to prove convergence, showcasing the applicability of the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

The authors propose two distinct splits of the gradient operator, namely element-wise and factored splits, for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. These splits are used in conjunction with stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly employed for saddle-point problems. The paper also explores non-uniform sampling, demonstrating through theoretical and empirical results that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms its uniform counterpart.

Experimental results presented in the main article and the appendix provide substantial evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both non-separable loss and regularizer.

Some minor comments and suggestions for improvement are as follows:

* In Section 5, the authors incorporate an additional regularization term to accelerate the update, following the approach in [8]. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update the iterate point, namely (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve acceleration. A clear explanation or strategy for updating this point would be beneficial.
* A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. It would be valuable to confirm whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA, ensuring consistency between the two algorithms.
* To avoid confusion, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should be denoted using distinct symbols.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of stochastic variance reduction techniques, providing a comprehensive analysis and demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods on saddle-point problems.
