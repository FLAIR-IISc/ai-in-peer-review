Summary
This paper extends variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure. The authors analyze two ways to exploit decomposability: (i) “individual” splits (over component functions/elements) and (ii) “factored” splits (separate sampling of rows/columns or partial derivatives). They provide linear convergence guarantees via a monotone-operator viewpoint, which also broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems to certain variational inequalities. They further study non-uniform sampling, derive complexity bounds under multiple sampling/splitting schemes, and show how to accelerate the incremental methods with a proximal-point/Catalyst-style outer loop. Experiments on a binary classification setup with non-separable losses and regularizers suggest that non-uniform sampling and acceleration can markedly improve over batch methods.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization, primal–dual and accelerated methods such as Chambolle–Pock and Nesterov acceleration, and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods. The technical contribution is to adapt variance reduction to saddle points through monotone operators, including complexity comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward schemes, with regimes reminiscent of convex minimization but with important differences due to proximal costs and splitting.

Strengths
- Provides, to my knowledge, the first linearly convergent variance-reduced incremental methods for general convex–concave saddle-point problems, with a clear monotone-operator analysis.
- Treats both individual and factored splits and develops non-uniform sampling strategies with improved constants; the bounds and their dependence on operator norms are carefully derived.
- The acceleration via a proximal-point/Catalyst framework is neat and simpler than in convex minimization due to the proximal-point interpretation, yielding favorable rates in certain regimes.
- Experiments demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are important in practice, especially on ill-conditioned problems.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Per-iteration cost is dominated by computing proxM, which the paper models as O(n + d). This creates a potential bottleneck: even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is not uniformly better than accelerated batch methods because the proximal cost is paid every iteration in the incremental schemes.
- In regimes common in ML (n ≫ d), and under factored splits (also used in the experiments), the total complexity in Tables 1–2 can be worse than accelerated batch forward–backward. This contrasts with convex minimization, where variance reduction typically brings a clear theoretical advantage.
- The paper would benefit from sharper guidance on when the accelerated incremental methods should be preferred to accelerated batch methods, beyond asymptotic constants (e.g., regimes in terms of ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and prox cost).
- Experimental evaluation is limited to a specific setting; additional tasks/datasets would strengthen claims about practical regimes where the proposed methods shine.

Quality
- The analysis appears sound and technically non-trivial. The monotone-operator framework is appropriate, and the results cleanly recover known convex-minimization behavior where applicable.
- The main technical caveat is the proximal cost assumption. Since proxM is required at every iteration, the overall end-to-end complexity may not reflect typical variance-reduction gains unless proxM is cheap or can be amortized (e.g., through mini-batching, inexact prox, or partial separability).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The presentation of splits, norm constants, and sampling strategies is careful.
- Suggestions: more prominently summarize which split is preferable under which structural/complexity assumptions; clearly state the assumptions on proxM and discuss possible inexact/approximate proximal steps; unify and highlight the regimes underlying Tables 1–2.

Originality
- Extends variance reduction to saddle points and, more broadly, to certain monotone-inclusion problems without relying on co-coercivity. The two notions of splitting and the non-uniform sampling analysis are valuable.

Significance
- The contribution is potentially impactful for large-scale saddle-point and variational inequality problems. Its practical significance depends on the structure of M and the cost of proxM; when prox is cheap or partially separable, the methods are likely to be attractive. The acceleration scheme is also of independent interest.

Questions for the authors
i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits in general, or only under certain norm regimes and cost models? Can you provide guidance on when to use each?
ii) Under what precise conditions (e.g., relationships among ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, n, d, prox cost) is accelerated SVRG provably favorable to accelerated batch FB?
iii) Are the dependences on n and d in Tables 1–2 tight? Can any be improved with alternative sampling/sketching or mini-batching strategies?

Pros
- First linear-convergent variance-reduced methods for general saddle points; rigorous and broad analysis.
- Clear role of non-uniform sampling; practical acceleration via Catalyst-style proximal-point scheme.

Cons
- Per-iteration proximal cost can negate variance-reduction gains; factored splits may be worse when n ≫ d.
- Limited experimental breadth; dependence on known strong convexity–concavity parameters.
