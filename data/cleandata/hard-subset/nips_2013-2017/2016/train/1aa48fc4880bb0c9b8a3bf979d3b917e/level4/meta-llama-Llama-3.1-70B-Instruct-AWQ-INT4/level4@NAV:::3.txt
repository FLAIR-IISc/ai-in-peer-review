This paper presents an extension of recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite sum structure, which can arise from individual or factored splits. The authors conduct a comprehensive analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under various splits and sampling strategies (uniform and nonuniform). Experimental results on a binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial in outperforming competitive batch algorithms. Although the concept of applying variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems may seem intuitive, the findings presented here are remarkably novel and technically non-trivial. The authors provide a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms for the important bilinear saddle point problem, which bears some resemblance to the convex minimization case and appears logical. However, my primary concern lies in the fact that the proposed incremental algorithms seem to be hindered by a fundamental limitation, namely the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Tables 1 and 2, may not necessarily be better than the accelerated batch version. Specifically, when the number of data points (n) significantly exceeds the dimension (d), which is common in most machine learning applications, the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulation) is actually worse than the batch FB algorithms, as indicated in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization case, I did not observe a clear substantial advantage of using variance reduction from a theoretical perspective. I recommend that the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and address the following questions: i) Is factored splitting (theoretically) inferior to individual splitting? ii) Under what conditions is the accelerated SVRG favorable over the batch FB? iii) Is the dependence on n and d tight enough?
