Summary:
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure and extends recent stochastic variance-reduction methods to this setting. The authors propose incremental algorithms that adapt variance-reduction ideas (e.g., in the spirit of SVRG/SAGA) to saddle-point formulations and provide convergence analyses. The analysis leverages monotone operator theory to establish linear convergence and further shows that the same framework applies beyond saddle points to more general monotone operator problems. The paper also demonstrates that the proposed methods can be accelerated using an extension of the catalyst framework, and it includes numerical results to illustrate empirical performance.

Strengths:
- The paper tackles an important class of problems common in machine learning: composite convex–concave saddle-point formulations where separability can be exploited algorithmically.
- It extends stochastic variance-reduction techniques to this setting and provides convergence analyses, which is valuable for understanding the behavior of such methods beyond standard convex minimization.
- The use of monotone operator theory is a solid analytical choice and broadens the applicability of the approach (e.g., to variational inequalities).
- The acceleration via a catalyst-style scheme is appealing and shows that the incremental methods can be made faster with a relatively simple modification.
- The paper presents numerical results that support the proposed approach.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The assumption of strong convexity–concavity on both sides (i.e., strong convexity in the primal variable and strong concavity in the dual variable) is very strong. It would be significantly more compelling if the analysis or algorithms could handle the case where only one side is strongly convex/concave (e.g., strong convexity in the primal or strong concavity in the dual), which is more common in practice.
- The experimental section would benefit from more numerical examples to better assess robustness across datasets, problem sizes, and conditioning, and to support the empirical claims more thoroughly.

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a meaningful extension of variance-reduction techniques to saddle-point problems, provides convergence guarantees through a monotone operator perspective, and proposes a catalyst-style acceleration. The contributions are technically sound and relevant. However, the requirement of strong convexity–concavity on both sides limits the scope, and adding more extensive empirical evaluations would strengthen the submission.
