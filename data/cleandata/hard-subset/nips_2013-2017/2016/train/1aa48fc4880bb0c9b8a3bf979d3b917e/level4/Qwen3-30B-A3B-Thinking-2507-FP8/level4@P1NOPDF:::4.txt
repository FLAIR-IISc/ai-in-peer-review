

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the efficacy of these methods for saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms handle a broad class of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion terms) may be non-separable. A key contribution lies in the convergence analysis, which differs significantly from prior work on convex problems. The authors employ monotone operator theory to establish convergence—a notably more complex framework than standard convex analysis—and show that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms apply to wider problem classes, including variational inequalities in game theory.  

The paper introduces two distinct gradient operator splits tailored to each method: an element-wise split for SVRG and a factored split for SAGA. Variance reduction is integrated with stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also considered, with both theoretical and empirical results confirming its superiority over uniform sampling. The experimental results in the main text and appendix provide convincing evidence supporting the theoretical claims, demonstrating the proposed methods' effectiveness on problems with non-separable loss and regularizer functions.  

Minor concerns:  
1. Section 5 discusses acceleration by incorporating an additional regularization term to align updates with an iterate point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. It remains unclear how to adaptively update $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ to achieve acceleration.  
2. A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. It is not evident whether identical theoretical guarantees extend to SAGA.  
3. The smooth function $K(\cdot)$ and design matrix $K$ should use distinct symbols to avoid confusion.
