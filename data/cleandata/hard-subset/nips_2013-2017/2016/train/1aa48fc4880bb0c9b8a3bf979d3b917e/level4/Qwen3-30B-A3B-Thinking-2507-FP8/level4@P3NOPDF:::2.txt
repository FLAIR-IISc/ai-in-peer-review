

This paper addresses convex-concave saddle point problems by extending recent variance reduction techniques. The authors provide a convergence analysis and demonstrate how their methods can be accelerated via the Catalyst framework, offering a notable contribution to the literature. The extension to monotone operator inclusions is also well-motivated and technically sound. However, the work contains one significant technical flaw and a minor gap that require attention.

The primary concern lies in the strong convexity assumption. The paper states that "both the primal and dual problems are strongly convex," which is unnecessarily restrictive. In standard saddle point theory (e.g., as in the seminal work of Nemirovski or Chambolle-Pock), only *one* side—typically the primal or the dual—needs to be strongly convex for efficient convergence. Requiring strong convexity on *both* sides significantly limits applicability, as many important problems (e.g., certain minimax formulations in robust optimization or generative adversarial networks) satisfy strong convexity on only one variable. The authors should explicitly justify why both sides must be strongly convex or revise the assumption to align with established practice. Without this correction, the theoretical guarantees lose generality and practical relevance. For instance, the convergence rates derived under this assumption may not hold for common cases where only one side is strongly convex, undermining the paper's contribution.

The paper’s numerical experiments are insufficient to validate the claimed advantages. While the authors include one or two test cases, additional experiments on diverse problem instances—particularly those where only primal or dual strong convexity holds—would strengthen the empirical support. For example, including comparisons on logistic regression with adversarial noise (where the dual may be strongly convex but the primal is not) or on non-strongly convex minimax problems (e.g., matrix games) would better illustrate the method’s flexibility and superiority over existing approaches. The current results do not convincingly demonstrate the necessity of the proposed extension or its practical benefits over simpler baselines.

The Catalyst acceleration extension is well-executed and correctly applied, though the paper could more clearly articulate *why* Catalyst is suitable here (e.g., by noting the equivalence to minimizing a composite objective with a smooth term). The monotone operator extension is a valuable addition but lacks a concise explanation of how it generalizes beyond saddle point problems (e.g., to broader variational inequalities). A brief discussion of potential applications in distributed optimization or online learning would enhance the contribution.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound extension of variance reduction to saddle points with a plausible Catalyst acceleration path. However, the overly strong strong convexity assumption represents a critical flaw that must be addressed. The authors should either: (1) revise the assumption to require strong convexity on only one side (with a clear proof adaptation), or (2) provide a compelling justification for the two-sided requirement. Additionally, supplementary numerical results are needed to substantiate the method’s effectiveness in realistic scenarios.

The writing is generally clear but contains minor grammatical issues (e.g., "the convergence analysis... are provided" → "is provided"; "authors shows" → "authors show"). These should be corrected in the final version. With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the strong convexity oversight risks diminishing the perceived novelty and impact of the work. I recommend major revision to address these points before acceptance.
