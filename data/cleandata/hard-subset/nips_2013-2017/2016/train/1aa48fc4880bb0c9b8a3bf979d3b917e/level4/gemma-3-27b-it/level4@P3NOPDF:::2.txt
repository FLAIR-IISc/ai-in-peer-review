## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please Replace*]

This work addresses the challenging problem of minimizing convex-concave saddle point problems, extending recent variance reduction techniques to this setting. The authors present novel methods and provide a convergence analysis demonstrating their efficacy. Importantly, they show how these methods can be further accelerated using an extension of the Catalyst framework, and also extend their approach to monotone operator settings, supporting the theoretical results with numerical experiments.

The core contribution of this paper lies in adapting and analyzing variance reduction methods for a class of problems notoriously difficult to optimize: convex-concave saddle points. The Catalyst acceleration is a valuable addition, demonstrating potential for significant speedups in practice. The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed framework. 

However, the paper’s strong convexity assumptions are a significant limitation. The requirement of strong convexity for *both* the primal and dual problems feels unnecessarily restrictive. Many practical applications of saddle point optimization, particularly in areas like game theory and distributed optimization, do not satisfy this condition. Relaxing this assumption to require strong convexity on only the primal *or* the dual problem would substantially broaden the scope and relevance of the work. Justification for the necessity of strong convexity on both sides should be provided, or a discussion of the limitations when this condition isn’t met.

The convergence analysis appears sound, though a more detailed explanation of the key steps and the dependence of the convergence rate on problem parameters (e.g., condition number, Lipschitz constants) would be beneficial. While the theoretical results are encouraging, the numerical evaluation feels somewhat limited. The presented results, while demonstrating the method’s functionality, do not provide a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art solvers specifically designed for convex-concave saddle point problems, such as those based on ADMM or accelerated gradient descent with extrapolation techniques. 

Specifically, it would be valuable to see:

* **Comparison to baselines:**  A comparison to established methods like ADMM, FISTA variants adapted for saddle points, or other relevant solvers. 
* **Scalability:** Results on larger-scale problems to demonstrate the practical scalability of the proposed approach. 
* **Sensitivity Analysis:**  An investigation of the performance sensitivity to the parameters of the method, and how these parameters influence the convergence rate.
* **Problem Diversity:**  Tests on a more diverse set of problems exhibiting different characteristics (e.g., different degrees of ill-conditioning, different problem sizes) to demonstrate robustness.
* **Catalyst performance:** A clear demonstration of the speedup achieved by employing the Catalyst acceleration in the numerical experiments.  Simply stating it works isn't sufficient; quantifying the improvement is crucial.

**Strengths:**

* Novel extension of variance reduction methods to convex-concave saddle points.
* Effective use of the Catalyst framework for acceleration.
* Extension to monotone operator settings.
* Clear convergence analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* Overly restrictive strong convexity assumptions.
* Limited numerical evaluation and lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* Insufficient detail in explaining the convergence rate dependence on problem parameters.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the area of convex-concave saddle point optimization. However, the strong convexity assumption and the limited empirical evaluation detract from its overall impact.  If the authors can address the strong convexity concern – either by relaxing the assumption or providing a strong justification – and significantly expand the numerical experiments to include comparisons to relevant baselines and a more thorough analysis of the method's performance, this work would be substantially strengthened. 

**Recommendation:**

I currently lean towards **weak accept**, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the strong convexity assumption and significantly expanding the numerical results to provide a more compelling demonstration of the method’s practical performance.  A major revision addressing these points would be necessary for a stronger recommendation.
