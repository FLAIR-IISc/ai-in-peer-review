- Introduces the first linearly convergent stochastic variance-reduced algorithms (SVRG/SAGA) for convex–concave saddle-point problems.  
- Proves convergence using monotone operator theory, enabling extension beyond saddle-points to variational inequalities.  
- Distinguishes function-based vs derivative-based splitting and shows saddle-points require convex–concave term splitting.  
- Establishes that non-uniform sampling is theoretically and empirically essential for efficient performance.  
- Demonstrates that the "catalyst" acceleration improves these methods beyond accelerated batch approaches.  
- Shows practical relevance for ML problems with non-separable losses and regularizers (e.g., AUC, grouped norms, total variation).  
- Strong empirical results: accelerated and non-uniform-sampling variants outperform batch and stochastic baselines, especially in ill-conditioned settings.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides thorough theoretical and implementation detail overall.  
- Major concern: insufficient description of how to compute non-uniform sampling probabilities π for general (non-bilinear) saddle-point problems.  
- Major concern: lack of practical details on step-size (σ) selection in experiments, hindering reproducibility.  
- Major concern: limited theoretical explanation for why non-uniform sampling is critical for saddle-point problems but not for convex minimization.  
- Major concern: extension to variational inequalities is claimed but lacks concrete examples or discussion of practical implications.  
- Minor issues: two typos noted (line 78: "k" → "d"; line 121: extra "y_{t-1}" term).  
- Overall recommendation: important theoretical contribution but currently incomplete on practical details; recommend rejection with option to revise addressing sampling, step-size, and extension discussions.
