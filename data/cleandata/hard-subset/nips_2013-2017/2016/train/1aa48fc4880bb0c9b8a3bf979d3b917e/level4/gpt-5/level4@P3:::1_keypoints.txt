- Paper studies min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with smooth K and proximable, strongly convex–concave M, extending SVRG/SAGA to this saddle-point setting.  
- Key analytic tool: reinterpret optimality as a monotone inclusion and analyze forward–backward splitting with variance-reduced forward steps.  
- Distinguishes two granularities of stochastic splits: by functions vs by partial derivatives/blocks (factored vs individual splits).  
- Emphasizes non-uniform sampling as crucial for improved theoretical complexity and empirical performance.  
- Provides acceleration via a proximal-point (“catalyst”) outer loop tailored to this setting.  
- Framework extends beyond saddle points to general monotone variational inequalities.  
- Advances prior work by delivering the first linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems with a single proximable M.  
- Analysis departs from standard convex minimization by relying on monotonicity rather than co-coercivity.  
- Acceleration is simpler than Nesterov-style schemes by leveraging the proximal-point viewpoint.  
- Strength: high originality—novel SVRG/SAGA extensions and catalyst acceleration for monotone/saddle problems.  
- Strength: strong technical quality—careful proofs, unified treatment, and quantified role of non-uniform sampling.  
- Strength: significant practical relevance for large-scale, non-separable problems with meaningful complexity improvements.  
- Empirical evidence shows clear gains of (accelerated) variance-reduced methods and supports choices like non-uniform sampling and split types.  
- Clarity concern: monotone-operator perspective appears late; introducing it earlier would clarify assumptions, step-sizes, and constants.  
- Assumptions concern: central strong convexity–concavity of M—need discussion when this holds (e.g., hinge loss/SVMs) and how to handle non-strong cases (e.g., smoothing or outer proximal regularization).  
- Comparisons concern: experiments limited to two datasets and missing stronger baselines for separable cases (stochastic/accelerated Chambolle–Pock, SPDC, Zhang & Xiao 2015).  
- Notation concern: L used ambiguously as Lipschitz constant and condition number; recommend κ-type notation (κ = L/μ).  
- Quality evaluation: technically sound with clear linear rates; clarify some details (e.g., exact meaning of μ in Theorem 2).  
- Clarity evaluation: overall good but improve flow by foregrounding monotone operators and unifying notation (L, μ, κ, L̄(π)).  
- Originality evaluation: high—first variance-reduced linear-rate methods for general saddle/monotone problems; nontrivial catalyst adaptation.  
- Significance evaluation: high—likely influential for theory and practice in large-scale saddle-point and VI optimization.  
- Request 1: Clarify when Assumption (A) (strong (λ,γ)-convex–concave and proximable M) holds in standard models and how to handle non-strong cases (e.g., smoothing or outer catalyst).  
- Request 2: Elaborate differences vs stochastic primal–dual methods (SPDC/PDHG), including per-iteration cost, memory, and complexity comparisons.  
- Request 3: Define μ precisely (strong monotonicity parameter) and restate theorems in terms of μ and κ to ease comparison between SVRG and SAGA rates.  
- Request 4: Explicitly show how (A)–(C) imply A is maximally/strongly monotone and B is monotone and L-Lipschitz, so A+B is strongly monotone in the Ω-metric.  
- Experimental suggestions: add stochastic/accelerated Chambolle–Pock and SPDC (include Zhang & Xiao 2015), more datasets, ablations (sampling, splits, memory/runtime, sensitivity to λ,γ, step-sizes).  
- Minor comments: avoid calling L the condition number (use κ), cite standard FB/PDHG references (Rockafellar, Chen & Rockafellar, Chambolle–Pock), define norms/constants early, and fix typographical/notation inconsistencies.  
- Pros for acceptance: first linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for non-separable saddle problems, solid monotone-operator analysis, clean acceleration, and practical sampling/split guidance.  
- Cons for acceptance: organization/clarity around monotone viewpoint and constants, limited empirical baselines/datasets, and reliance on strong convexity–concavity restricting direct applicability.
