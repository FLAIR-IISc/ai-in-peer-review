This paper extends several recent variance-reduced incremental algorithms, including SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated Catalyst variants, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems. The problem structure is a finite sum, which the authors analyze under two distinct settings arising from "individual splits" and "factored splits." The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the convergence rates for these algorithms, considering both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies.

The paper makes several notable contributions. While the extension of variance reduction to saddle point problems might appear straightforward at first glance, the results presented are novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial. The experiments on a binary classification task effectively demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming competitive batch algorithms in practice. For the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, the authors present a clear comparison of complexity between batch, stochastic, and the proposed incremental methods. This comparison draws a useful and intuitive parallel to the well-understood trade-offs in the convex minimization setting.

My primary concern, however, relates to the practical and theoretical advantage of the proposed incremental methods over their batch counterparts. While formulating the problem as a saddle point problem elegantly addresses the challenge of non-separable loss functions, the proposed algorithms appear to be constrained by a significant computational bottleneck: the proximal operator at each iteration incurs a cost of O(n+d).

As a result, the overall complexity of the incremental methods, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, is not always superior to the accelerated batch Forward-Backward (FB) algorithm, as detailed in Tables 1 and 2. This issue is particularly pronounced in the "factored splits" setting (which is used for the experiments) when the number of samples `n` is substantially larger than the dimension `d`—a common scenario in machine learning. In this regime, the theoretical complexity shown in Table 2 is actually worse than that of the batch FB algorithm. Consequently, and in contrast to the clear benefits observed in standard convex minimization, the theoretical advantage of applying variance reduction techniques in this saddle point context is not immediately apparent from the provided analysis.

I would appreciate it if the authors could provide a more in-depth discussion on this matter in their rebuttal and address the following specific questions:

1.  Is the "factored splits" setting theoretically inferior to the "individual splits" setting in terms of overall complexity? The tables suggest this might be the case, and a clarification would be helpful.

2.  Under what specific conditions or problem regimes (e.g., relationships between `n`, `d`, and condition numbers) is the proposed accelerated SVRG method theoretically favorable against the accelerated batch FB algorithm?

3.  Are the derived dependencies on `n` and `d` in the complexity bounds believed to be tight? Could there be room for improvement in the analysis that might reveal a clearer advantage for the incremental approach?
