This paper presents extensions of the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, achieving linear convergence. Both theoretical and empirical results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of these variance reduction methods in the context of saddle-point optimization. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a broad range of non-separable saddle-point problems, including scenarios with non-separable losses (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., fusion terms). 

A key contribution of this work lies in the challenging analysis required for saddle-point problems, which is significantly more complex than previous convergence analyses focused on purely convex optimization. The authors skillfully employ the theory of monotone operators to establish convergence guarantees. Notably, this analysis extends the applicability of the modified SVRG and SAGA algorithms to a wider class of problems, encompassing variational inequalities frequently encountered in game theory.

To address the specific structure of saddle-point problems, the authors propose two distinct gradient operator splitting strategies – element-wise and factored splits – tailored for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. This variance reduction is applied within the framework of stochastic forward-backward algorithms, a common approach for solving saddle-point problems. Furthermore, the paper investigates the impact of non-uniform sampling, demonstrating through both theoretical analysis and empirical results that non-uniform sampling consistently outperforms uniform sampling. 

The experimental evaluation, presented in the main text and extended in the appendix, provides compelling evidence supporting the theoretical findings and showcases the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems featuring both non-separable losses and regularizers. The results are well-presented and thoroughly validate the theoretical claims.

However, several minor points merit further clarification. In Section 5, the authors introduce an acceleration technique, inspired by [8], which involves adding a regularization term to guide the update toward a reference point. The mechanism for *adaptively* updating this reference point, denoted as (x̄, ȳ), to achieve optimal speed-up remains unclear. While a convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, it would be beneficial to know whether similar theoretical results can be established for SAGA. Finally, to avoid potential confusion, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should be represented with distinct symbols. 

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic saddle-point optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithms are well-motivated, and the empirical validation is comprehensive. Addressing the minor comments outlined above would further strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance.
