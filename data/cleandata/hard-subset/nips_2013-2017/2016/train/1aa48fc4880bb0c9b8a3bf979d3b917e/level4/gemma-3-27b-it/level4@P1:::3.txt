The paper extends recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants using the Catalyst framework) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite sum structure, which arise when objective functions are not directly separable. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under different splitting strategies (individual or factored) and sampling approaches (uniform vs. non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification task demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems might seem straightforward, the results presented here are notably novel and technically non-trivial. For the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, the authors provide a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms, with results that align with established understanding in convex minimization. 

However, a significant concern remains. While solving the saddle point problem addresses the challenge of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Table 1 and 2), is not necessarily better than accelerated batch methods. Particularly, when the number of data points *n* is substantially larger than the dimension *d*—a common scenario in machine learning—the complexity with factored splits (used in the simulations) can be worse than that of batch forward-backward algorithms, as illustrated in Table 2.  Unlike the situation in convex minimization, I do not observe a clear, substantial advantage to using variance reduction from a theoretical perspective. 

I suggest the authors provide a more in-depth discussion addressing the following questions: i) Is a factored split (theoretically) worse than an individual split? ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch forward-backward? iii) Is the dependence on *n* and *d* tight enough in the complexity bounds?
