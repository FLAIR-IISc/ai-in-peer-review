This paper explores saddle-point problems with a convex-concave property and demonstrates how existing stochastic variance reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, can be combined with a proximal operator to address these problems. The authors examine these algorithms in the context of monotone operators, establishing linear convergence. They also propose an accelerated variant and analyze a non-uniform sampling scheme.

The contributions of this paper are significant, and the theoretical analysis is thorough, providing new insights into saddle-point problems. A notable extension is the authors' method, which applies to non-separable functions.

Although I appreciate the theoretical contributions of this paper, I was disappointed by its presentation. The authors could have made the paper more accessible by clarifying the connection to monotone operators. While this connection appears as an extension in Section 6, the authors' analysis relies heavily on monotone operators.

On the experimental side, I would have liked to see results on more than two datasets. In the case of separable functions, the authors should have compared their method to accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhao et al., 2015). I suggest adding this reference to the submission.

There are several points that I would like the authors to clarify:

1. How strong are assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A)? For instance, do these assumptions hold when solving the saddle-point problem induced by SVM?

2. I am unclear about the differences between stochastic primal-dual methods and the authors' suggested method. Could the authors elaborate on this point?

3. In Theorem 2, I am unsure about the constant μ. Is it the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? How can this result be compared to the result of Theorem 1?

4. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is confusing. Do assumptions (A)-(C) imply monotonicity?

Minor issues:

- There is some confusion in the use of the constant L, which is referred to as both the condition number and the Lipschitz constant. For example, "The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem" and "we need the Lipschitz constant L."

- The authors should add a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
