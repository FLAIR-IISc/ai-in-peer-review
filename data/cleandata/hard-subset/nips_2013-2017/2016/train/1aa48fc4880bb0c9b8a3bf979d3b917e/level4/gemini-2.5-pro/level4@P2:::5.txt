**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors propose the first incremental algorithms for this problem class that achieve a linear convergence rate. A key contribution of this work lies in the convergence analysis. Since standard analysis for convex minimization does not apply, the authors leverage the theory of monotone operators to establish their convergence results. This proof technique is more general, and as a consequence, the analysis also applies to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The practical motivation for this work is to efficiently solve machine learning problems that involve non-separable loss functions or regularizers, which can be naturally formulated as saddle-point problems. The paper also explores the benefits of non-uniform sampling and demonstrates how to accelerate the proposed methods using the "catalyst" framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for composite decomposable saddle-point problems. This is an important and timely contribution, extending a highly successful line of research on variance reduction to a new, practical problem setting.
2.  **Practical Relevance:** The ability to handle non-separable losses and regularizers is a significant advantage. Many important machine learning models, including those in structured output prediction (e.g., AUC maximization) and robust optimization, fall into this category. The proposed methods provide a new, efficient tool for these problems.
3.  **Novel Proof Technique:** The core technical contribution is the use of monotone operator theory for the convergence analysis. This is an elegant and powerful approach that sidesteps the difficulties of extending proofs from the convex minimization setting. Its generality means the results are potentially applicable to an even wider range of problems beyond saddle-point optimization.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants over existing batch and stochastic methods. In particular, the results highlight the significant performance gains achieved through non-uniform sampling and acceleration, confirming the theoretical findings.
5.  **Clarity of Contributions:** The paper clearly delineates its contributions, which include not only the new algorithms and their analysis but also simplified proofs for existing methods like forward-backward splitting, viewed through the lens of their framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Intuition in the Main Paper:** While the algorithmic extensions of SVRG/SAGA are straightforward, the main novelty lies in the proof technique. The paper would be strengthened if it included a high-level proof sketch or more intuition in the main text about why the monotone operator framework is necessary and how it works. This would make the core technical contribution more accessible to a broader audience.
2.  **Ambiguity in Practical Implementation:** The paper is unclear on some key details required for implementation and reproducibility.
    *   For problems other than the bilinear case, it is not specified how the non-uniform sampling distribution π should be chosen.
    *   The method used to set the step-size σ in the experiments is not described.
3.  **Need for Deeper Discussion:** The experiments show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for achieving good performance, seemingly more so than in standard convex minimization. The paper would benefit from a discussion providing intuition as to why this is the case for saddle-point problems. This would add significant insight beyond the empirical observation.
