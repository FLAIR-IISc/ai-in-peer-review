

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this important class of problems. The authors demonstrate that these methods can be adapted to saddle-point problems by using monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, which differs significantly from the convex minimization setting. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a thorough theoretical analysis showing linear convergence using monotone operator properties rather than convexity assumptions; (2) a novel characterization of two distinct splitting schemes (function-based and partial derivative-based) for saddle-point problems; (3) the critical importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency; and (4) an acceleration framework via the "catalyst" method that outperforms accelerated batch algorithms. The work builds on and extends previous NIPS contributions on stochastic optimization (e.g., SVRG [2], SAGA [3]) while addressing a broader class of problems than typical convex minimization approaches.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and represents a significant advance in understanding saddle-point optimization. By framing the problem through monotone operators (Section 6), the authors demonstrate that their methods extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, which is a notable theoretical contribution. The analysis of different splitting schemes and the demonstration of non-uniform sampling's importance provide valuable practical insights that address a critical gap in existing literature. The acceleration framework is well-motivated and provides a clear theoretical advantage over batch methods. The paper also offers compelling examples from machine learning (Section 2.1) where these methods are particularly applicable, such as non-separable loss functions and regularizers in supervised learning, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning.

The paper's main weaknesses concern clarity and experimental validation. The connection to monotone operators is introduced as an "extension" in Section 6, but the core analysis relies on this framework from the beginning. The authors should integrate this perspective more cohesively throughout the paper rather than presenting it as an afterthought. The presentation would benefit from a clearer roadmap explaining how the monotone operator perspective unifies the analysis. Regarding experiments, the paper evaluates on only two datasets (sido and rcv1), which is insufficient to demonstrate the robustness of the proposed methods across diverse machine learning problems. The comparison to the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm [32] (which is specifically designed for saddle-point problems) would strengthen the experimental validation, as this is a natural baseline for the problem class.

Several technical points require clarification. First, the authors should explicitly address whether their assumptions (A)-(C) hold for standard SVM saddle-point problems, which would help contextualize the method's applicability. Second, the relationship between the authors' method and existing stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., [11, 12, 32]) needs elaboration, particularly regarding how their variance reduction approach differs from existing techniques. Third, the constant μ in Theorem 2 appears undefined and should be clearly connected to the monotonicity properties mentioned in the appendix. Finally, the paper mixes the usage of L as both the condition number and Lipschitz constant, creating confusion; the authors should clarify these definitions consistently throughout.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing a significant gap in stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis is sound and demonstrates clear advantages over existing batch methods, while the practical implications for machine learning applications are substantial. The authors' observation that their method applies to non-separable functions (which cannot be handled by existing stochastic methods for convex minimization) represents a particularly important contribution to the field.

The paper meets all four NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported, though the presentation could be improved.
- Clarity: The paper would benefit from a more cohesive integration of the monotone operator perspective and clearer definitions of technical terms.
- Originality: The extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems via monotone operator analysis is novel, as confirmed by a review of recent NIPS proceedings.
- Significance: The work addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with wide applications in machine learning, and the proposed methods offer demonstrable advantages in both theory and practice.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The paper would be significantly improved by clarifying the monotone operator perspective throughout, expanding the experimental validation to include more datasets and the relevant stochastic primal-dual baseline [32], and resolving the technical ambiguities noted above. With these revisions, the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

Pro: Rigorous theoretical analysis, novel application of variance reduction to saddle-point problems, clear demonstration of non-uniform sampling's importance, practical significance for machine learning applications.

Con: Limited experimental validation, unclear presentation of monotone operator framework, insufficient comparison to relevant stochastic primal-dual methods, some technical ambiguities.
