This paper investigates saddle-point problems exhibiting a convex-concave structure, leveraging stochastic variance reduction techniques—specifically SVRG and SAGA—in conjunction with a proximal operator. The authors establish linear convergence rates for these algorithms within the framework of monotone operator theory, and further introduce an accelerated variant alongside an analysis of non-uniform sampling strategies.

**Novelty and Originality:** The contributions of this work are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable new insights concerning the optimization of saddle-point problems. The paper’s exploration of extensions is particularly noteworthy, especially the demonstrated applicability of the method to non-separable functions.

**Technical Quality:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the presentation requires improvement. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the connection to monotone operator theory, which currently feels underdeveloped despite underpinning the core analysis (currently appearing primarily in Section 6). A more explicit and earlier linkage would significantly enhance accessibility. 

Regarding the experimental evaluation, the current results—limited to two datasets—are insufficient to fully assess the method's performance.  For cases involving separable functions, a comparison to accelerated proximal algorithms, such as the stochastic variant of Chambolle-Pock [Zhang & Peyré, 2015], is crucial. I recommend including this comparison and adding the provided reference to the submission. 

Several points require further clarification from the authors:

1. **Assumption Strength:**  What is the practical strength of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A)?  It would be beneficial to discuss whether these assumptions hold for common applications, such as solving the saddle-point problem arising from Support Vector Machines (SVMs).

2. **Distinction from Primal-Dual Methods:** The differences between existing stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach are not fully clear. A more detailed explanation of this distinction would be helpful.

3. **Theorem 2 and Convergence Rates:** In Theorem 2, the constant *μ* requires clarification. Is this the monotonicity constant defined in the appendix?  Furthermore, a direct comparison between the convergence result presented in Theorem 2 and that of Theorem 1 would illuminate the impact of the acceleration.

4. **Assumptions and Monotonicity:** The transition from assumptions A-C to the implication of strong monotonicity (as discussed in the appendix) is unclear. Do assumptions A-C, in fact, imply monotonicity, or are further conditions required?  This relationship needs to be explicitly stated.

**Minor Issues:**

* **Constant *L*:** The paper occasionally conflates the condition number and the Lipschitz constant, both denoted by *L*. For example, stating “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” followed by “we need the Lipschitz constant L” is ambiguous.  Please ensure consistent usage of notation.
* **Forward-Backward Algorithm:** A citation for the Forward-Backward algorithm is missing and should be included.



**Reference:**

Zhang, A., & Peyré, G. (2015). Frank-Wolfe iteration for stochastic non-smooth optimization. *Journal of Machine Learning Research*, *16*(1), 1815-1844.
