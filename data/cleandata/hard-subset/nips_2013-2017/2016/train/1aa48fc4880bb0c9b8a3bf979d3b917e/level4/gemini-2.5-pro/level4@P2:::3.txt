### **Summary**

This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure. This is a novel and important contribution, as such problems frequently arise in machine learning, particularly with non-separable losses or regularizers, where standard incremental methods for convex minimization do not apply. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, leveraging the framework of monotone operators, which generalizes beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. A key aspect of the work is the exploration of two different splitting strategies (individual and factored) and a thorough analysis of their complexities with both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. The paper also demonstrates how to combine these methods with the "Catalyst" framework to achieve acceleration. Experimental results on a binary classification task with a non-separable loss and regularizer confirm that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, can outperform competitive batch algorithms in practice.

### **Quality and Clarity**

The paper is technically sound and the theoretical contributions are non-trivial. The convergence analysis, grounded in the theory of monotone operators, is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The authors are careful to compare their methods against a range of existing batch and stochastic algorithms, providing detailed complexity tables (Table 1) that are very helpful for understanding the theoretical trade-offs. The paper is well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible despite the technical depth.

### **Originality and Significance**

The originality of this work is high. To my knowledge, this is the first paper to establish linear convergence rates for incremental, variance-reduced methods for the general class of finite-sum saddle-point problems. While the extension of SVRG/SAGA might seem like a natural step, the analysis is substantially different from the convex minimization case and requires a different set of tools (monotone operators), which is a valuable contribution in itself.

The significance is also potentially high. Saddle-point formulations are a powerful tool for handling complex, non-separable models. By providing efficient, large-scale solvers for this class of problems, this work could enable the use of more sophisticated models in areas like structured prediction, robust optimization, and learning with complex regularizers. The clear demonstration that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for achieving state-of-the-art performance is an important practical insight.

### **Weaknesses / Major Concerns**

My primary concern revolves around the overall computational complexity and whether the proposed incremental methods offer a clear theoretical advantage over accelerated batch methods in typical large-scale machine learning scenarios. While the idea of using cheaper stochastic updates is appealing, a potential bottleneck appears to be the cost of computing the proximal operator, which can be `O(n+d)` at each iteration for the problems considered. This cost seems to dominate the per-iteration complexity, potentially negating the benefits of variance reduction.

Specifically, when analyzing the complexity results presented in the paper (e.g., in Table 1 and the discussion in Appendix D.4 for Table 2), the advantage over accelerated batch methods is not always clear, especially for the "factored splits" used in the experiments. In the common machine learning setting where the number of samples `n` is substantially larger than the feature dimension `d`, the complexity of the proposed methods can be worse than that of the accelerated batch Forward-Backward algorithm. For instance, the complexity of accelerated SVRG with a factored split appears to scale with `n`, whereas the batch method's dependence on `n` is part of the full gradient computation, which is amortized differently.

This contrasts with the standard convex minimization setting, where methods like SVRG and SAGA often provide a clear and substantial theoretical improvement over batch gradient descent. Here, the advantage seems more nuanced and possibly conditional on the problem structure. I believe the paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide a more in-depth discussion on this matter.

To clarify this, I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions:

1.  **Factored vs. Individual Splits:** According to the complexity analysis, does the factored split (used in the experiments) lead to a theoretically worse dependency on `n` and `d` compared to the individual split? When `n >> d`, is the individual split formulation theoretically superior, even if it requires more storage?

2.  **Conditions for Favorable Performance:** Could the authors more clearly delineate the regimes (in terms of `n`, `d`, the condition number `L`, and the cost of computing `T(B)` vs. `T(Bi)`) where accelerated SVRG is theoretically guaranteed to be superior to the accelerated batch Forward-Backward algorithm? The current analysis suggests the batch method may be preferable when `n` is much larger than `d`.

3.  **Tightness of Complexity Bounds:** How tight are the derived dependencies on `n` and `d` in the complexity analysis? The comparison with batch methods hinges critically on these terms, and understanding their tightness is crucial for evaluating the algorithm's practical utility.

### **Summary of Review**

Overall, this is a strong paper with novel and technically sound contributions. It successfully extends variance reduction techniques to an important class of saddle-point problems and provides a rigorous analysis. However, its practical impact is slightly clouded by a key theoretical concern regarding whether the proposed methods are demonstrably superior to accelerated batch algorithms across all common machine learning settings, particularly when `n >> d`. Clarification on this point would make the paper's contributions even more compelling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   First work to provide linearly convergent, incremental algorithms for finite-sum saddle-point problems.
*   Technically strong and novel analysis based on monotone operator theory.
*   Thorough comparison of algorithms, splits, and sampling strategies.
*   Addresses an important problem class with significant applications in machine learning.

**Arguments for Rejection (or areas for improvement):**
*   The theoretical advantage over accelerated batch methods is not clearly established for all practical settings (e.g., when `n >> d`).
*   The `O(n+d)` cost of the proximal operator at each step may represent a fundamental bottleneck that limits the gains from stochastic updates.
