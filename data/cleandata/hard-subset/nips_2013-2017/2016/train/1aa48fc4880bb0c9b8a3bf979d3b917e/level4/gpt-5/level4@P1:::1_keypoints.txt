- Paper studies min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) and extends SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems by variance-reducing the forward operator B=(∂_x K, −∂_y K) with proximal steps for M.  
- Analysis uses monotone-operator theory (rather than convex minimization) to prove linear convergence and extends to variational inequalities.  
- Introduces two splitting notions (by functions or partial derivatives, including a factored split), emphasizes non-uniform sampling, and provides data-dependent complexity bounds.  
- Proposes a catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration compatible with epoch-based SVRG, improving complexity in ill-conditioned regimes.  
- Technical strength: careful convergence analysis using monotone operator tools to obtain linear rates without co-coercivity.  
- Generality strength: handles non-separable losses/regularizers provided M is proximable, relevant for many ML problems.  
- Practical guidance: analyzes multiple splitting schemes and non-uniform sampling, with operator/Frobenius/max-norm dependent bounds.  
- Claim: first linearly convergent incremental methods for large-scale saddle-point problems with this level of generality.  
- Presentation weakness: monotone-operator perspective appears late; should be introduced earlier and integrated into the main analysis.  
- Experimental weakness: only two datasets used; needs more datasets and ablations (uniform vs non-uniform sampling, factored vs individual splits, varying mini-batch m).  
- Missing comparisons: in separable cases, compare against accelerated primal–dual baselines and stochastic Chambolle–Pock (add citation Zhang & Xiao 2015).  
- Positioning weakness: unclear distinction vs stochastic primal–dual methods—clarify algorithmic (control variates vs unbiased gradients; resolvent of M vs separate proxes) and theoretical regimes (separability, smoothness, strong convexity–concavity).  
- Question: Clarify strength/practicality of assumptions (A)–(C), especially (A); give examples (e.g., SVM hinge/squared-hinge) and when prox_M is efficient.  
- Question: Elaborate when stochastic primal–dual methods are preferable vs when this variance-reduced approach gives provable or practical advantages.  
- Question: Define μ in Theorem 2 inline (is it the strong-monotonicity parameter?) and compare Theorem 2’s rate to Theorem 1 in terms of L, L̄, |I|, m.  
- Question: Provide a concise lemma showing how (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity/Lipschitz properties of A+B under the Ω-norm to clarify the appendix transition.  
- Minor issue: inconsistent notation for L (Lipschitz constant vs condition number); clarify and define condition numbers explicitly.  
- Minor issue: add/clarify references for Forward–Backward step convergence in saddle-point settings (e.g., Chen–Rockafellar 1997, Chambolle–Pock 2011) and ensure claimed rates match citations.  
- Minor issue / experiments: in separable experiments, also report results against accelerated batch primal–dual baselines and stochastic Chambolle–Pock variant.  
- Overall: strong, original contribution with solid theory and practical potential, contingent on improved exposition and broader empirical comparisons.
