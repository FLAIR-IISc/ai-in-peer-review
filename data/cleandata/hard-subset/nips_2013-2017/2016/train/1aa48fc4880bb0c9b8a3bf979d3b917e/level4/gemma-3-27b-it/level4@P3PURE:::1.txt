This paper considers saddle-point problems exhibiting a convex-concave structure. The authors demonstrate how existing stochastic variance reduction techniques – specifically SVRG and SAGA – can be effectively applied to this problem class through the incorporation of a proximal operator. Their analysis, conducted within the framework of monotone operators, establishes linear convergence guarantees. Furthermore, they propose an accelerated variant and investigate a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty/originality:** The contributions of this paper are significant, offering thorough theoretical analysis and revealing novel insights for saddle-point problems. The authors explore numerous extensions, with the applicability of their method to non-separable functions being particularly noteworthy.

**Technical quality:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the presentation detracts from the paper’s overall impact. The connection to the underlying theory of monotone operators could be made more explicit; currently, this appears as an extension in Section 6, yet the core analysis relies heavily on monotone operator properties. Regarding the experimental evaluation, the results are presented for only two datasets. When applied to separable functions, a comparison to accelerated methods such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang & Peyré, 2015) would be valuable. I recommend adding this reference to the submission: http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf. 

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  How restrictive are assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A)? Specifically, do these assumptions hold when solving the saddle-point problem induced by Support Vector Machines (SVM)?
2.  Could the authors elaborate on the distinctions between their proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual methods? A clearer articulation of these differences would be beneficial.
3.  In Theorem 2, the constant μ is not fully defined. Is this the monotonicity constant referenced in the appendix? How does this result compare to the convergence rate presented in Theorem 1?
4.  The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is unclear. Do assumptions (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity?

**Minor issues:** There is some inconsistency in the use of the constant *L*, which is used both as the condition number of the problem and as a Lipschitz constant. For example, the text states both "The quantity *L* represents the condition number of the problem" and "we need the Lipschitz constant *L*." Additionally, a reference to the Forward-Backward algorithm would be helpful.



Zhang, S., & Peyré, G. (2015). Frank-Wolfe algorithm with stochastic variance reduction. *Journal of Machine Learning Research*, *16*(1), 1937–1975.
