The paper extends several recent incremental algorithms, including SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite sum structure. These problems can arise from two types of splits: individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough study of the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies, including uniform and nonuniform sampling. An experiment on a special binary classification example demonstrates that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial in outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While the idea of extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems may seem straightforward, the results established here are notably novel, and the technicality is undoubtedly non-trivial.

For the important bilinear saddle point problem, the authors present a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, which bears some resemblance to what is typically known for the convex minimization case. The results are logical and well-presented. However, I have a major concern. Solving the saddle point problem alleviates the difficulty of non-separable loss functions, but the proposed incremental algorithms appear to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck: the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Tables 1 and 2, is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version.

Especially when the number of data points n is substantially larger than the dimension d, which is often the case in most machine learning applications, the complexity under factored splits (later used in simulation) is actually worse than the batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the typical advantages seen in convex minimization, I do not see a clear substantial benefit of using variance reduction from a theoretical perspective. I would suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions: i) Is factored splitting theoretically worse than individual splitting? ii) Under what conditions is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? iii) Is the dependence on n and d tight enough?
