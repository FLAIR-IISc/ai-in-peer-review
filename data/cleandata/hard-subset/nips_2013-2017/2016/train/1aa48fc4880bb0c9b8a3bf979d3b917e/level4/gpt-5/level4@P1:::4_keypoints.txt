- Extends SVRG and SAGA to general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems and proves linear convergence.  
- Uses a monotone operator viewpoint to analyze convergence, allowing application beyond saddle-points to monotone inclusions/variational inequalities.  
- Clarifies two decomposition strategies: element-wise (per-coordinate) and factored (row/column) splits of the gradient operator.  
- Incorporates non-uniform sampling; theory and experiments show it is crucial for efficiency.  
- Proposes an acceleration scheme via additional regularization around a reference point, improving complexity and outperforming accelerated batch methods in analysis.  
- Empirical results on non-separable losses and regularizers support theoretical claims and show significant gains from variance reduction and non-uniform sampling.  
- Builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA but fills a gap by providing the first linearly convergent incremental methods for general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems.  
- Adapts classical resolvent/forward–backward ideas to variance-reduced stochastic settings and non-co-coercive operators—a nontrivial contribution.  
- Theoretical analysis is careful and technically sound, with clear complexity bounds and treatment of non-uniform sampling and decompositions.  
- Algorithms are natural and clearly presented, with discussion of implementation and storage trade-offs for the two splitting strategies.  
- Experiments convincingly show variance reduction and non-uniform sampling outperform uniform/batch methods, with acceleration helping in ill-conditioned settings.  
- Notational ambiguity: reuse of K for both smooth function K(·,·) and design matrix K could confuse readers.  
- Originality: novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, monotone-operator analysis beyond co-coercivity, and careful decomposability treatment.  
- Significance: important for many ML problems with non-separable losses/regularizers; methods are likely useful in large-scale applications.  
- Pros summary: first linear incremental methods for general saddle-points, monotone operator framework, clear decomposition treatment, non-uniform sampling benefits, and effective acceleration.  
- Cons/questions: unclear how to adaptively update the acceleration reference (x̄, ȳ) in practice—guidance or heuristics needed.  
- Cons/questions: acceleration proof provided for SVRG only—unclear if analogous guarantees hold for SAGA; discussion or pointers requested.  
- Recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor clarifications on adaptive (x̄, ȳ), SAGA acceleration theory, and notation.
