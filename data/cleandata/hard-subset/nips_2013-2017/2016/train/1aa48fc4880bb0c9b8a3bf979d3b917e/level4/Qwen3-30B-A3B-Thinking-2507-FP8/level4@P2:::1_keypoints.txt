- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory (not convex minimization) to adapt variance reduction to saddle-point problems and variational inequalities.  
- Identifies two splitting strategies (by functions vs. partial derivatives) and shows non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency.  
- Shows acceleration via the catalyst framework, yielding algorithms that outperform accelerated batch methods.  
- Theoretical analysis is strong: detailed convergence proofs and linear rates for SVRG and SAGA variants.  
- Extension to monotone operators (Section 6) broadens applicability beyond separable problems and structured regularizers.  
- Presentation issues: monotone operator framework is introduced late, causing conceptual confusion.  
- Assumptions A–C need clearer connection to monotonicity and concrete examples (e.g., SVMs) to verify applicability.  
- Experimental validation is limited (only two datasets) and misses comparison to stochastic Chambolle-Pock [Zhao et al., 2015].  
- Some technical details require clarification (e.g., role of μ in Theorem 2; ambiguity of L as condition number vs. Lipschitz constant).  
- Significant practical impact for problems like robust optimization, grouped norms, and structured output prediction.  
- Strengths: novel convergence analysis for saddle-point variance reduction, practical linear-convergence algorithms, and clear benefits of non-uniform sampling.  
- Weaknesses: exposition/organization, limited experiments, missing method comparisons, and unclear links between assumptions and monotonicity.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested revisions—reorganize around monotone operators, add experiments and Chambolle-Pock comparison, clarify assumptions and technical quantities, and contrast with stochastic primal-dual methods.
