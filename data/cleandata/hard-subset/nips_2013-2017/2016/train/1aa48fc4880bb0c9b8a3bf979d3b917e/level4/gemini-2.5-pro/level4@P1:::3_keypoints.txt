- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Considers two problem decompositions: "individual" and "factored" splits.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis under uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to beat batch methods.  
- Novel and technically non-trivial extension of variance-reduction techniques to saddle-point settings.  
- Presents a clear, insightful comparison (especially for bilinear problems) between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward methods.  
- Major concern: O(n + d) proximal cost per iteration creates a bottleneck, limiting practical/theoretical advantage of incremental methods.  
- For n >> d, factored splits can be theoretically worse than accelerated batch forward–backward (per Table 2).  
- Requests clarification/discussion on: (1) whether factored split is theoretically worse than individual split; (2) when accelerated SVRG outperforms accelerated batch forward–backward; (3) tightness of n and d dependencies in the bounds.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors' response; scores remain unchanged.
