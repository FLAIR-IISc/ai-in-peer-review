Summary and contributions:
This paper extends the variance-reduced stochastic methods SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, and proves linear convergence. The authors use the monotone operator viewpoint to analyze convergence, which is essential because standard analyses for convex minimization do not carry over. This perspective also shows the methods apply beyond saddle-point problems to a broader class of monotone inclusions, such as variational inequalities. The paper further clarifies two natural ways to exploit decomposability: element-wise (per-coordinate/entry) and factored (row/column) splits of the gradient operator. The methods incorporate non-uniform sampling, which the theory and experiments both show to be crucial for efficiency. An acceleration scheme—adding an extra regularization centered at a reference point—yields improved complexity and, according to the authors’ analysis, strictly dominates accelerated batch methods. Empirical results on problems with non-separable losses and non-separable regularizers support the theoretical claims.

Relation to prior work:
The work builds on SAG, SVRG, and SAGA, which provide linear rates for strongly convex finite-sum minimization but do not directly address saddle-point structure. Existing primal–dual or forward–backward schemes for saddle-point problems either require separability or achieve only sublinear rates in the stochastic setting. This paper appears to provide the first linearly convergent incremental methods for general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, bridging a gap between convex minimization and saddle-point optimization. The monotone operator analysis relates to classical results on resolvents and forward–backward splitting, but adapted here to the variance-reduced stochastic setting and to non-co-coercive operators, which is nontrivial.

Quality:
- Theory: The convergence analysis is careful and technically sound. Using monotone operators (rather than co-coercivity) is appropriate for saddle-point problems and supports the linear-rate claims for both SVRG- and SAGA-style updates. The treatment of non-uniform sampling and the discussion of different decompositions (element-wise vs. factored) are well-motivated, with clear complexity bounds. The acceleration section follows a proximal point/catalyst-style regularization and provides a convergence guarantee for SVRG.
- Algorithms: The stochastic forward–backward framework with variance reduction is natural and clearly presented. The two splitting strategies are well aligned with common structures (e.g., bilinear forms K(x, y) = y^T K x), and the implementation/storage trade-offs are discussed.
- Experiments: The experiments, including non-separable losses (e.g., AUC surrogates) and non-separable regularizers (e.g., fusion-style penalties), convincingly demonstrate that variance reduction and non-uniform sampling significantly outperform uniform sampling and batch methods, with further gains from acceleration in ill-conditioned settings.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. One notational issue is potentially confusing: the smooth function K(·, ·) and the design matrix K use the same symbol. Please use distinct symbols to avoid ambiguity.

Originality:
The extension of SVRG/SAGA to general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with linear convergence, the monotone operator analysis beyond co-coercive settings, and the careful treatment of decomposable structures with non-uniform sampling all appear novel. The acceleration scheme adapted to this setting is also a meaningful contribution.

Significance:
Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous (e.g., via Lagrangian or Fenchel duality), and many modern ML problems have non-separable losses or regularizers for which existing incremental methods cannot be applied. Providing linearly convergent stochastic methods for this class is important, and the practical improvements reported here suggest the methods will be useful in large-scale applications.

Pros:
- First linearly convergent incremental methods for general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems.
- Monotone operator framework that extends applicability to variational inequalities.
- Clear treatment of two decomposition schemes and the benefits of non-uniform sampling.
- Acceleration that improves complexity over accelerated batch baselines.
- Empirical validation on relevant non-separable tasks.

Cons / questions:
- Acceleration (Section 5): The scheme follows prior work by incorporating an additional regularization term that pulls the updates toward a reference iterate (x̄, ȳ). It is not clear how to adaptively update (x̄, ȳ) in practice to maximize speed-up. More guidance or heuristics would improve reproducibility.
- Theory for SAGA acceleration: A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. Will analogous guarantees hold for SAGA? A brief discussion or pointer would strengthen the contribution.
- Notation: As noted above, please use different symbols for the smooth function K(·, ·) and the design matrix K to avoid confusion.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong paper that addresses an important gap with solid theory and convincing experiments. The variance-reduced stochastic forward–backward approach, the monotone operator analysis, and the attention to practical sampling/decomposition all contribute to a compelling and impactful work. I recommend acceptance, with minor clarifications on the adaptive choice of (x̄, ȳ), the SAGA acceleration theory, and notation.
