Summary:
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum or decomposable structure and extends variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting. While the algorithmic modification is simple, the analysis requires moving beyond convex minimization: the authors use a monotone operator viewpoint to establish linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity. They discuss two types of decompositions (by functions and by partial derivatives), emphasize the role of non-uniform sampling in rates and practice, and show that a simple catalyst-style regularization yields an accelerated method that is provably better than accelerated batch algorithms. The framework also extends to finding zeros of strongly monotone operators, covering variational inequalities. Experiments on supervised learning tasks with non-separable losses/regularizers and a separable case indicate gains over batch methods and over uniform-sampling variants.

Relation to prior work:
- Builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for strongly convex finite-sum minimization and adapts them to saddle-point problems.
- Complements primal–dual and forward–backward methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock/PDHG) and stochastic FB schemes, which typically provide O(1/t) rates without variance reduction.
- Related to SPDC and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods that assume separability; the present work targets non-separable losses/regularizers and more general monotone operator inclusions.
- The analysis departs from co-coercivity-based proofs common in minimization and uses only monotonicity, thereby broadening applicability (e.g., to variational inequalities).

Strengths:
- Conceptual contribution: clean extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point and monotone operator settings with linear convergence guarantees.
- Technical novelty: convergence proofs rely on monotone operator theory rather than minimization-specific properties.
- Practical insights: clear treatment of two splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling; mini-batch options; explicit complexity bounds tied to operator norms.
- Acceleration: catalyst-style scheme tailored to the saddle-point setting, with a simple analysis and improved complexity.
- Empirical evidence: consistent improvements over batch baselines; demonstrates importance of non-uniform sampling.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Strong convexity–concavity: Requiring strong convexity in x and strong concavity in y (and knowledge of the constants λ, γ) is restrictive. Many saddle-point problems are strongly convex only on one side. Can the theory be relaxed to single-sided strong convexity (as in SPDC) or to error-bound/PL-type conditions? Even partial results or discussion would strengthen the paper.
- Parameter knowledge: The algorithms rely on L and L̄. Guidance on estimating these constants and robustness to misspecification would be valuable.
- Empirical scope: The experiments cover two datasets. Additional datasets and settings (including variational inequalities) would better support general claims. Also, wall-clock comparisons, ablations on mini-batch size, and sensitivity to sampling distributions would help.
- Baselines: Comparisons to SPDC, stochastic/variance-reduced PDHG variants, and accelerated PDHG would contextualize the gains more fully.
- Practical costs: SAGA requires storing operator values; for the individual split this can be large (O(nd)). Discussion of memory–time trade-offs, and the cost of computing non-uniform sampling probabilities, would aid practitioners.
- Resampling detail: For non-uniform SAGA, the need to resample after updates can increase overhead. Please quantify this in experiments or discuss practical heuristics.

Quality:
- The analysis appears careful and technically sound. The use of monotone operator tools to prove linear convergence (without co-coercivity) is compelling.
- Complexity expressions are informative and connect to matrix/operator norms under various sampling strategies. Proof sketches match the algorithmic structure.
- Some clarity could be improved: (i) give concrete recipes for choosing the split and sampling probabilities in common cases; (ii) summarize how to estimate L, L̄ in practice; (iii) provide more guidance on step-size selection when constants are unknown.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized, with assumptions and examples clearly stated. The distinction between individual and factored splits is helpful.
- The presentation of the accelerated scheme is concise but could benefit from a short intuitive explanation of the τ trade-off and an explicit pseudo-code summary in the main text (not only in the appendix).

Originality:
- Novel extension of variance reduction to general convex–concave saddle-point problems with linear rates and a monotone operator analysis. The combination of non-uniform sampling, two splitting strategies, and a simple catalyst-style acceleration in this generality appears original.

Significance:
- Addresses an important gap: scalable, linearly convergent methods for non-separable saddle-point problems common in machine learning.
- The monotone operator perspective broadens applicability beyond saddle points to certain variational inequalities, potentially impacting areas like robust optimization and games.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Theoretically relax assumptions to single-sided strong convexity, or at least discuss feasibility and limitations.
- Expand empirical evaluation: more datasets; wall-clock time; comparisons to SPDC and accelerated PDHG; a variational inequality example; ablations on mini-batch size and sampling; memory footprint reporting for SAGA vs SVRG.
- Provide practical guidance on estimating λ, γ, L, and L̄, and on adaptive step-sizes when constants are unknown.
- Clarify the computational cost of constructing and maintaining non-uniform sampling distributions.
- Consider releasing code to facilitate reproduction.

Pros:
- Solid, general analysis via monotone operators.
- First-linearity claims for large-scale saddle-point methods with variance reduction.
- Practical acceleration and non-uniform sampling guidance.
- Empirical gains over strong batch baselines.

Cons:
- Strong convexity–concavity assumption on both sides and known constants.
- Limited experimental breadth and missing comparisons to some relevant stochastic primal–dual baselines.
- Memory overhead for SAGA in some splits.

Recommendation:
Promising and timely contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical section and clarifying assumptions and practical parameter choices.
