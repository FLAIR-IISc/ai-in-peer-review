

This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG/SAGA) combined with proximal operators. The authors establish linear convergence in the monotone operator framework and propose an accelerated variant alongside a non-uniform sampling scheme. The theoretical contributions are significant, offering new insights into saddle-point problems, particularly through the novel application to non-separable functions—a key advancement over prior work.  

**Novelty and Technical Quality**  
The paper's core novelty lies in extending stochastic variance reduction methods to non-separable saddle-point problems, a substantial departure from existing separable-function frameworks. The monotone operator analysis is thorough and well-motivated, though the presentation could better integrate this foundation. The connection to monotone operators is currently treated as an afterthought in Section 6, despite being central to the convergence proofs. The authors should explicitly frame the problem within monotone operator theory early on to improve accessibility and coherence.  

On experiments, the current two-dataset evaluation is insufficient. The authors should include results on at least one additional benchmark to strengthen empirical validation. For separable cases, a comparison to accelerated methods like the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang et al., 2015) is essential. The authors must cite this work (JMLR 37, 2015) to contextualize their method against state-of-the-art.  

**Key Clarifications Needed**  
1. **Assumption Strength**: The validity of assumptions (A)–(C), especially (A), requires justification. Specifically, do these hold for the saddle-point problem arising in SVMs? The authors should provide concrete examples or counterexamples.  
2. **Method Distinction**: The relationship between the proposed method and stochastic primal-dual approaches needs elaboration. How does it differ in structure, assumptions, or convergence guarantees?  
3. **Theorem 2 Constant μ**: Clarify whether μ in Theorem 2 denotes the strong monotonicity constant (referenced in the appendix). Explicitly state its relation to Theorem 1’s convergence rate to enable direct comparison.  
4. **Assumption-to-Monotonicity Link**: The transition from assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is unclear. Do (A)–(C) imply monotonicity, or is additional structure required? A concise proof sketch would resolve this.  

**Minor Issues**  
- **Notation Confusion**: The paper inconsistently uses *L* for both the condition number ("The quantity L represents the condition number") and Lipschitz constant ("we need the Lipschitz constant L"). These must be distinguished (e.g., use *κ* for condition number, *L* for Lipschitz constant).  
- **Forward-Backward Reference**: The description of the Forward-Backward algorithm lacks a citation; include a standard reference (e.g., Combettes & Wajs, 2005).  

**Overall Assessment**  
The theoretical framework is robust and advances the understanding of saddle-point optimization, particularly for non-separable settings. However, the presentation obscures the monotone operator foundation, and the experimental section lacks breadth and comparative depth. Addressing the above points—especially clarifying the assumptions, strengthening the experiments with additional datasets and relevant baselines, and resolving the notation—would significantly elevate the paper. The technical quality is high, but the current form risks limiting its impact due to accessibility issues.  

**Recommendation**  
**Accept with Minor Revisions** (with the above clarifications addressed). The contributions are meaningful, but the revisions are necessary to ensure the work is clearly communicated and rigorously contextualized. The authors should prioritize integrating the monotone operator perspective into the main narrative, expanding experimental validation, and resolving the technical ambiguities identified.
