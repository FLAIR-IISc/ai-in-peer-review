This paper extends recent stochastic variance reduction algorithms, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure. This work is motivated by machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers, which can be naturally formulated as saddle-point problems. The authors develop the algorithms for this new setting, considering two types of structural decomposition ('individual' and 'factored' splits) and analyze their convergence under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. The work also demonstrates how to combine these methods with the "catalyst" framework for acceleration. The experimental results on a binary classification task highlight that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for the proposed methods to outperform competitive accelerated batch algorithms.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** While extending variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems might seem like a natural next step from the convex minimization literature, this paper is the first to provide a rigorous, linearly-convergent analysis for this class of algorithms. The technical contribution is non-trivial, as the analysis cannot be directly ported from the convex case and instead relies on the theory of monotone operators. This broadens the applicability of these efficient methods to a new and important class of problems.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is thorough. The paper provides a clear and comprehensive comparison of complexity bounds for batch, stochastic, and the new variance-reduced methods, particularly for the important case of bilinear saddle-point problems. This comparison helps build intuition by drawing parallels to the well-established hierarchy of algorithms in convex optimization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the algorithmic extensions are presented straightforwardly, and the theoretical results are summarized effectively in tables.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness concerns the practical implications of the derived complexity bounds, which may limit the significance of the contribution. While the saddle-point formulation elegantly handles non-separable objectives, the proposed incremental methods appear to face a fundamental computational bottleneck. Specifically, each iteration requires computing a proximal operator at a cost of `O(n+d)`. This per-iteration cost can be substantial.

As a result, the overall complexity of the proposed methods, including the top-performing accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, is not always theoretically superior to the accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm. As shown in Table 1, in the common high-dimensional setting where the number of samples `n` is significantly larger than the dimension `d`, the complexity under the "factored splits" scheme (used in the experiments) is actually worse than the accelerated batch baseline. This contrasts sharply with the standard convex minimization setting, where variance-reduced methods typically offer a clear and substantial theoretical advantage over batch methods.

**Questions for the Authors:**
The potential lack of a clear theoretical advantage over batch methods is a critical point. I would encourage the authors to provide a more in-depth discussion on this matter in the paper. It would be helpful to clarify the following:
1.  Is there a clear theoretical winner between the "individual" and "factored" splits, or does the choice depend on the problem structure?
2.  Could you more precisely characterize the regimes (in terms of problem parameters like `n`, `d`, and the condition number) where the proposed accelerated SVRG is theoretically favorable compared to the accelerated batch forward-backward method?
3.  Are the dependencies on `n` and `d` in the derived complexity bounds believed to be tight, or could there be room for improvement in the analysis that might reveal a clearer advantage for the variance-reduced approach?
