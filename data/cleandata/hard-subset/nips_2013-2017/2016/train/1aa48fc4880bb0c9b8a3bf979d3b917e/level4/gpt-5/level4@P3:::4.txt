Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is proximable and strongly convex–concave. The key insight is to analyze forward–backward-type updates in the monotone operator framework, rather than relying on convex minimization tools. This yields linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA variants under strong convex–concavity, without requiring separability of either the loss or the regularizer. The analysis further shows the algorithms solve a broader class of problems (e.g., variational inequalities), provided the monotone operator setting holds.

Algorithmically, the paper considers two decompositions of the smooth operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK): (i) element-wise splits and (ii) factored splits (e.g., by rows/columns for bilinear K), enabling importance sampling schemes tailored to saddle-point structure. The authors also develop an accelerated variant via the catalyst idea, interpreted here as a proximal-point regularization on (x̄, ȳ), giving improved iteration complexity over accelerated batch methods in the regimes they analyze.

The work connects to and extends classical primal–dual and forward–backward methods (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock) and to recent variance-reduction methods for convex minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), while addressing the non-separable saddle-point setting where prior incremental linear-convergence guarantees were lacking. It also relates to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC), but those require separability; the present contribution covers non-separable losses/regularizers and a more general operator setting.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clean monotone-operator analysis establishing linear convergence of SVRG and SAGA for strongly convex–concave saddle-point and certain variational inequality problems. The analysis avoids co-coercivity assumptions that are standard for convex minimization.
- Modeling breadth: Supports non-separable losses and regularizers with efficient proximal operators, and covers bilinear and more general smooth K. The factored vs element-wise splits broaden applicability and storage/runtime trade-offs.
- Importance sampling: Well-motivated non-uniform sampling strategies with problem-dependent L̄(π) bounds (e.g., using row/column norms for bilinear K), and clear complexity improvements over uniform sampling.
- Acceleration: A catalyst-style scheme specialized to saddle points with a proximal-point interpretation, leading to favorable complexities and simple integration with SVRG epochs.
- Empirical validation: Experiments on two datasets with non-separable components demonstrate gains for variance-reduced and accelerated variants compared to batch and uniform-sampling baselines, especially in ill-conditioned regimes.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption clarity and parameter selection: The methods require knowledge (or estimates) of strong convexity–concavity parameters (λ, γ) and smoothness constants (L, L̄). More guidance on practical estimation, sensitivity, and robustness would strengthen the experimental section.
- Baselines and scope: While batch forward–backward and its acceleration are compared, additional comparisons to standard primal–dual/extragradient baselines (e.g., Chambolle–Pock/PDHG, Mirror-Prox) would better position the contribution empirically.
- Importance sampling practicality: Non-uniform sampling for bilinear problems relies on row/column norm information. Please discuss cost of computing/maintaining these statistics (especially for sparse/streaming data) and whether dynamic reweighting helps in practice.
- SAGA storage: For element-wise splits, SAGA’s storage can be O(nd). More discussion of memory footprints and the practical preference for factored splits would be useful.
- Acceleration details: The paper notes adaptive updates of (x̄, ȳ); please specify concrete strategies, stopping rules per subproblem, and how often the center is updated in practice. The claim “always superior to accelerated batch algorithms” seems strong; clarifying the regimes and assumptions under which the inequality in complexities translates to wall-clock improvements would help.
- Theory–practice gap: For non-uniform SAGA, the theory requires resampling after the iterate update; the experiments omit this for efficiency. Please comment on observed behavior vs theory and potential remedies (e.g., mixtures) with quantified impact.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and careful. The monotone-operator approach is well motivated, and the convergence arguments appear solid given the assumptions. The complexity summaries and sampling bounds are informative. Empirical results are consistent with the theory, though additional baselines and implementation details (step-size tuning, parameter estimation, memory use) would make the case even stronger.

Clarity
Overall clear and well structured, with helpful examples and algorithm boxes. A few suggestions:
- Notation: Using K to denote both the function and the design matrix is confusing. Consider distinct symbols (e.g., script K for the function, A for the matrix).
- Assumptions: Group (A)–(C) and emphasize the need for a proximable M and strong convex–concavity; summarize where these arise in practice.
- Practical guidance: Provide a short subsection on parameter estimation (λ, γ, L, L̄) and default choices for mini-batch sizes and sampling distributions.
- Resampling requirement: Explicitly state when it is needed for SAGA with non-uniform sampling and what happens otherwise.

Originality
The extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle-point problems with linear rates under strong convex–concavity, via monotone operators, is novel and valuable. The importance sampling analysis for factored vs element-wise splits is also insightful. The catalyst-style acceleration adapted to saddle points is natural yet nontrivial and practically useful.

Significance
High. Many machine learning problems are best cast as saddle points with non-separable structure (e.g., AUC surrogates, structured losses, total variation, group norms). Providing incremental, linearly convergent methods for this class fills a notable gap. The monotone-operator perspective may also catalyze extensions to broader VI settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general theoretical contribution with clear relevance to ML optimization.
- Practical algorithms with importance sampling and acceleration.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory on relevant non-separable problems.
- Clear relation to and extension of influential prior work (SAG/SVRG/SAGA, primal–dual methods).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical baselines; missing comparisons to PDHG/Mirror-Prox.
- Some practical aspects under-specified (parameter estimation, adaptivity in acceleration, memory/runtime trade-offs).
- Strong claims about acceleration could be better qualified and supported by wall-clock studies.

Questions for the authors
- How do you estimate λ, γ, L, and L̄ in practice? Do adaptive schemes (e.g., backtracking, line-search-like heuristics) help?
- Can you provide a concrete adaptive policy for updating (x̄, ȳ) in the accelerated variant, and guidance on choosing τ?
- Do the same acceleration guarantees extend to SAGA, and under what conditions?
- What is the empirical overhead of computing and maintaining non-uniform sampling probabilities on large sparse datasets?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a substantial and timely advance with a convincing theory and promising empirical results. Addressing the clarity and empirical comparison points above would further strengthen the submission.
