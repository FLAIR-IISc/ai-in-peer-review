

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this important class of problems. The authors demonstrate how these techniques can be adapted to saddle-point problems by leveraging monotone operator theory rather than the convex minimization framework used in prior work. This builds upon the NIPS 2013-2016 literature on variance reduction methods (SAG [1], SVRG [2], SAGA [3]) while addressing a critical gap: the lack of efficient large-scale algorithms for saddle-point problems with separable structures.

Originality: The paper makes several substantial contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems, which differs fundamentally from convex minimization. The authors use monotone operators to establish linear convergence, showing their approach extends to variational inequalities. Second, they identify two distinct ways to split the saddle-point problem (by functions or partial derivatives) and demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency. Third, they show how to accelerate their methods using the catalyst framework, resulting in algorithms that outperform accelerated batch methods. This work significantly extends the applicability of variance reduction techniques to non-separable problems, such as those with structured regularizers or non-separable losses.

Quality: The theoretical analysis is strong and rigorous. The authors provide detailed convergence proofs for both SVRG and SAGA variants, establishing linear convergence rates under appropriate assumptions. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is particularly valuable, demonstrating broader applicability. However, the connection to monotone operators, which forms the foundation of their analysis, is not clearly articulated until Section 6, creating conceptual confusion. The analysis of Assumptions A-C could be strengthened with concrete examples, particularly regarding whether these assumptions hold for standard problems like SVMs.

Clarity: While the technical content is sound, the presentation would benefit from reorganization. The monotone operator framework should be presented as the central theoretical foundation from the beginning rather than as an afterthought. The distinction between the two split types (by functions vs. partial derivatives) could be clarified with more intuitive examples. The experimental section is limited to two datasets, and the paper misses an opportunity to compare with the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm [Zhao et al., 2015], which would strengthen the empirical validation.

Significance: The paper addresses a practically important gap. Many machine learning problems—such as robust optimization, non-separable regularizers (e.g., grouped norms), and structured output prediction—lead to saddle-point problems that cannot be efficiently solved with existing methods. The authors' approach provides the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for these problems, with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over batch methods. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in convergence rates, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

Strengths:
- First convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems
- Clear demonstration of non-uniform sampling's importance
- Practical algorithms with linear convergence rates
- Extension to monotone operators, broadening applicability
- Empirical validation showing clear advantages

Weaknesses:
- Monotone operator framework not established as the central theme
- Limited experimental validation (two datasets only)
- Missing comparison to relevant accelerated methods
- Some technical details require clarification (e.g., role of μ in Theorem 2)
- Relationship between Assumptions A-C and monotonicity needs better explanation

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that extends variance reduction methods to a broader class of problems with clear practical implications. The authors have demonstrated both theoretical and empirical advantages over existing methods. To strengthen the paper, I suggest:
1. Reorganizing the presentation to emphasize the monotone operator framework from the beginning
2. Adding experiments on additional datasets and comparisons to the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm
3. Clarifying the relationship between Assumptions A-C and monotonicity conditions
4. Providing a more detailed explanation of the difference between their approach and stochastic primal-dual methods
5. Resolving the confusion between L as condition number vs. Lipschitz constant

The paper represents an important advance in optimization for machine learning, with clear theoretical and practical significance. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
