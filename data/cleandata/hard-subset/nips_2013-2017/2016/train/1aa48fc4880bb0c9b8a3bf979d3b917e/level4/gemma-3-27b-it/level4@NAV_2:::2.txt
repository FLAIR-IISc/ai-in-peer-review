This paper addresses the challenging problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending stochastic variance reduction methods like SVRG and SAGA to this setting. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these extended algorithms, a significant contribution as existing analyses typically apply only to separable objective functions. They highlight several key aspects of this saddle-point formulation, including two notions of splitting, the necessity of convex-concave terms, and the importance of non-uniform sampling. Furthermore, they demonstrate acceleration via a catalyst framework extension, achieving performance superior to accelerated batch algorithms. Finally, they broaden the applicability of their results by connecting saddle-point problems to the more general framework of monotone operators and provide experimental validation.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the relevance of saddle-point problems to machine learning applications like supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning. The connection to monotone operators is a notable theoretical contribution, expanding the scope of the proposed algorithms beyond saddle-point problems. The authors thoroughly compare their approach to existing batch and stochastic algorithms, presenting a detailed analysis of convergence rates and computational complexities, culminating in a clear understanding of the advantages of their method. The experimental results on the sido and rcv1 datasets support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach, especially with non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

A strength of this work is the rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proofs appear sound, and the connection to monotone operator theory is insightful. The paper also clearly articulates the distinctions between different splitting strategies and the benefits of non-uniform sampling. The inclusion of a detailed complexity analysis and comparison to other algorithms is valuable. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature, referencing relevant work and positioning their contribution appropriately. 

However, the strong convexity assumption on *both* the primal and dual problems (Assumption A) feels restrictive. Relaxing this assumption, potentially requiring only strong convexity for one of the problems, would broaden the applicability of the method. While the authors acknowledge the need for strong convexity-concavity constants and mention future work on adaptivity, it is unclear how challenging this adaptation would be. Furthermore, while the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, adding more diverse datasets or considering different problem settings (e.g., larger-scale problems, different types of non-separability) would further strengthen the empirical validation. While the choice of datasets and evaluation metrics are justified, exploring the sensitivity of performance to different parameter settings would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving a challenging and relevant optimization problem.
*   The convergence analysis is rigorous and extends existing results to a broader class of problems (monotone operators).
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The strong convexity assumption on both primal and dual problems limits the applicability of the method.
*   While the experimental evaluation is solid, expanding the dataset variety and exploring parameter sensitivity would further strengthen the results.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The technical soundness, theoretical depth, and practical validation make it a worthy addition to NIPS. While the strong convexity assumption and potential for expanded empirical evaluation are minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
