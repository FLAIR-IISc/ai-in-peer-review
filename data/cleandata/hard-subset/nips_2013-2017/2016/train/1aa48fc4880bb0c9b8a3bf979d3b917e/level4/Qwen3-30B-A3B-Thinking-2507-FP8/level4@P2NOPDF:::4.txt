

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence guarantees. The authors demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that their approach effectively addresses these problems, offering significant advantages over non-variance-reduced alternatives. A key contribution is the extension of these techniques to non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion terms) may lack separability. This broadens applicability beyond previous methods restricted to separable settings.

The theoretical analysis represents a notable advancement. Unlike prior work on convex optimization, the saddle-point setting demands a more intricate convergence proof, which the authors achieve using monotone operator theory—a sophisticated framework well-suited for variational inequalities. This analysis further establishes that the extended algorithms apply to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities arising in game theory. The authors propose two distinct gradient operator splits tailored to each method: an element-wise split for SAGA and a factored split for SVRG. These splits enable variance reduction within standard forward-backward schemes commonly used for saddle-point problems. Additionally, the work incorporates non-uniform sampling, with both theory and experiments confirming its superiority over uniform sampling in reducing variance and accelerating convergence.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, providing robust empirical support for the theoretical claims. Results in both the main paper and appendix consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on non-separable loss and regularizer configurations, aligning well with the convergence guarantees. The empirical superiority of non-uniform sampling is particularly well-documented.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  **Acceleration Mechanism (Section 5):** The acceleration technique incorporates an additional regularization term to guide updates toward an iterate point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$, following [8]. However, the adaptive update mechanism for $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ itself is not clearly explained. How is this point dynamically adjusted to achieve the claimed speed-up? Clarification is needed on the adaptive strategy for this key component.

2.  **SAGA Acceleration:** The convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. It is unclear whether the same theoretical guarantees (linear convergence with acceleration) hold for the SAGA variant. The authors should explicitly address whether the SAGA acceleration analysis follows similarly or requires additional justification.

3.  **Symbol Consistency:** The smooth function $K(\cdot)$ and the design matrix $K$ use the same symbol, creating potential confusion. These should be denoted with distinct symbols (e.g., $K(\cdot)$ vs. $\mathbf{K}$ or $K_{\text{mat}}$) to avoid ambiguity in the presentation and analysis.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that successfully adapts powerful variance reduction techniques to a broader class of saddle-point problems with rigorous analysis and compelling empirical validation. The use of monotone operators for the convergence proof is a significant technical strength. Addressing the minor points above would further enhance the clarity and completeness of the work. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and makes a valuable advance for the optimization community.
