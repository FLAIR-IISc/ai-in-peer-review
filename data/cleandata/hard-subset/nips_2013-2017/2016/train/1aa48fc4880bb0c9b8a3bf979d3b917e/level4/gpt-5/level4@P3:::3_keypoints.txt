- Extends SVRG/SAGA and Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems via a monotone-operator framework.
- Leverages finite-sum structure with two splitting strategies: individual (function-based) and factored (variable-tailored) splits with matched non-uniform sampling.
- Provides first linear convergence guarantees for variance-reduced methods on this class and extends to some variational inequalities.
- Situates work relative to SAG/SVRG/SAGA and primal–dual (forward–backward) algorithms, addressing non-separable losses/regularizers.
- Derives SVRG/SAGA variants for saddle-points with clear algorithmic design and distinctions between split types.
- Introduces a simple Catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration with transparent complexity trade-offs.
- Theory gives careful complexity comparisons and explicit dependence on operator norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) and sampling distributions.
- Experiments show non-uniform sampling and acceleration significantly improve over batch baselines; uniform sampling can be inferior.
- Major practical concern: per-iteration proximal computations over x and y (Tprox(M)) can be a bottleneck (often O(n+d)), possibly negating VR gains for large n.
- Mini-batching is suggested to amortize Tprox(M), but more detailed wall-clock comparisons are needed to show when VR methods win.
- Theoretical guidance on choosing individual vs factored splits is scattered; a consolidated comparison (assumptions, norms, sampling, storage) is requested.
- Factored splits reduce storage for SAGA but can be worse than batch FB in some n,d regimes, especially when n≫d.
- Practicalities of non-uniform sampling (preprocessing, updating, sampling costs) need discussion regarding runtime overhead.
- Methods assume knowledge of problem constants (λ, γ, L or L̄(π)); guidance on estimating or making methods adaptive is missing.
- Empirical evaluation is limited (two datasets, one non-separable example); broader tasks would strengthen generality claims.
- Technical development and proofs are careful, exploiting monotonicity rather than co-coercivity, with coherent complexity bounds.
- Clarity is good overall, but suggested additions: a summary table of split-dependent complexities, explicit per-iteration costs including Tprox(M), and succinct monotone-operator assumptions with an extra example.
- Novelty: variance-reduced saddle-point extensions and monotone-operator-based linear convergence analysis are original and non-trivial.
- Significance: fills an important gap by enabling linear convergence for non-separable saddle-point problems relevant for large-scale optimization.
- Specific requests: unified theorem comparing factored vs individual splits (including storage/sampling) under n≫d and d≫n regimes.
- Specific requests: a rule-of-thumb when accelerated SVRG outperforms accelerated batch FB in terms of L̄, T(B), max_i T(Bi), and Tprox(M), plus synthetic illustrations.
- Specific requests: comment on tightness of n,d dependence for bilinear K and necessity of non-uniform sampling when ‖K‖op vs ‖K‖F gap is large.
- Specific requests: discuss ways to reduce Tprox(M) (block/coordinate prox, lazy updates, approximate prox with error control).
- Specific requests: quantify empirical impact of skipping resampling in non-uniform SAGA and whether mixed sampling preserves gains.
- Pros: first linear VR guarantees for general saddle-points, principled non-uniform sampling, simple acceleration, and solid bilinear comparisons with supportive experiments.
- Cons: proximal bottleneck risks negating benefits in common regimes, limited empirical diversity, and reliance on known constants and implementation details left to reader.
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with authors’ clarifications on regimes where accelerated SVRG dominates and role of non-uniform sampling/mini-batching; scores unchanged.
