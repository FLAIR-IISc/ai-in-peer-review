- Paper extends SVRG/SAGA and Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Analyzes two decompositions: individual splits over components and factored splits (separate sampling of rows/columns/partials).  
- Uses a monotone-operator viewpoint to obtain linear convergence and broaden applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Develops non-uniform sampling strategies and derives complexity bounds for multiple sampling/splitting schemes.  
- Proposes proximal-point/Catalyst-style outer-loop acceleration for the incremental methods.  
- Experiments (binary classification, non-separable losses/regularizers) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration can outperform batch methods on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA, primal–dual and accelerated methods, and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods; adapts variance reduction to saddle points without co-coercivity.  
- Main technical contribution: monotone-operator adaptation of variance reduction and complexity comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward schemes.  
- Strength: first linearly convergent variance-reduced incremental methods for general convex–concave saddle points with a clear monotone-operator analysis.  
- Strength: careful treatment of individual vs factored splits and precise dependence of bounds on operator norms.  
- Strength: neat and simpler acceleration via proximal-point/Catalyst interpretation yielding favorable rates in some regimes.  
- Strength: empirical evidence that non-uniform sampling and acceleration matter in practice.  
- Major concern: per-iteration cost dominated by computing proxM (modeled as O(n + d)), which can negate incremental-method advantages.  
- Major concern: in common ML regimes (n ≫ d) and under factored splits, total complexity can be worse than accelerated batch forward–backward.  
- Major concern: paper lacks sharp guidance on when accelerated incremental methods should be preferred to accelerated batch methods (e.g., concrete regimes involving ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, prox cost).  
- Major concern: limited experimental evaluation; broader tasks/datasets needed to validate practical regimes.  
- Quality: analysis appears sound and technically non-trivial; recovers known convex-minimization behavior where applicable.  
- Technical caveat: proximal cost assumption (proxM required every iteration) may prevent typical variance-reduction gains unless proxM is cheap or can be amortized/inexact.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written; splits, norm constants, and sampling strategies are carefully presented.  
- Clarity suggestions: more prominent summary of recommended splits under specific structural/complexity assumptions and explicit assumptions/discussion on inexact/approximate proxM.  
- Originality: extends variance reduction to saddle points and monotone-inclusion problems, with valuable splitting and non-uniform sampling analyses.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for large-scale saddle-point/variational inequality problems when prox is cheap or partially separable; acceleration scheme is independently interesting.  
- Key questions for authors: (i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits generally or only under certain norms/costs? (ii) Precisely when (relations among ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, n, d, prox cost) is accelerated SVRG better than accelerated batch FB? (iii) Are n and d dependences in Tables 1–2 tight or improvable with alternative sampling/sketching/mini-batching?  
- Pros summary: first linear-convergent variance-reduced methods for general saddle points; rigorous analysis and practical non-uniform sampling plus Catalyst-style acceleration.  
- Cons summary: per-iteration proximal cost can negate gains; factored splits may be worse for n ≫ d; experiments limited and rely on known strong convexity–concavity.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer read the rebuttal, is satisfied with responses, and kept scores unchanged.
