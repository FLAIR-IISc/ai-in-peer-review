

The paper presents a significant extension of incremental variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite-sum structure. The authors systematically analyze these algorithms under two distinct problem splits (individual and factored) and various sampling strategies (uniform/non-uniform), providing a comprehensive complexity comparison. Their experimental evaluation on a binary classification task demonstrates that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming batch methods, which aligns with their theoretical claims. The theoretical contribution is notably novel: for bilinear saddle point problems, they establish clear performance comparisons between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, drawing meaningful parallels to convex minimization while addressing the unique challenges of non-separability in saddle point problems.

The core strength of the work lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors successfully adapt variance reduction principles to the saddle point setting—a non-trivial extension given the interplay between primal and dual variables—and establish tight complexity bounds under different splits and sampling schemes. Their results for the bilinear case are particularly compelling, as they clarify the theoretical landscape in a manner that resonates with established convex optimization intuition.

However, a fundamental concern arises regarding the computational complexity of the proposed incremental methods. Specifically, the per-iteration cost for computing the proximal operator under the factored split is O(n + d), where n is the number of samples and d is the dimension. This bottleneck critically impacts the overall complexity, as highlighted in Tables 1 and 2. For the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, the total complexity is O((n + d) * log(1/ε)), which does not necessarily improve upon the accelerated batch FB method (O(n d log(1/ε)) for batch). Crucially, when n ≫ d—typical in modern machine learning applications—the factored split complexity *exceeds* that of batch methods. For instance, Table 2 shows that for n = 10^4, d = 100, the factored split complexity is higher than batch. This undermines a primary motivation for incremental methods (reducing per-iteration cost), and the theoretical advantage of variance reduction over batch methods is not evident in this regime.

This leads to three critical questions requiring deeper discussion:  
(i) Is the factored split *theoretically* disadvantaged compared to individual splitting, particularly in the n ≫ d regime?  
(ii) Under what conditions (e.g., specific n/d ratios or problem structures) does accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling become preferable to batch FB?  
(iii) Are the stated dependencies on n and d in the complexity bounds tight, or could they be improved to better reflect practical scenarios?  

While the authors’ rebuttal addresses these points satisfactorily, the initial draft does not sufficiently clarify the trade-offs. The paper’s claim that "variance reduction provides substantial theoretical advantages" requires stronger justification, especially since the standard n vs. d scaling in ML applications appears to favor batch methods. The authors should explicitly contrast the *practical* complexity (e.g., operations per iteration) with the *theoretical* complexity (e.g., iterations to ε-accuracy), as the O(n + d) per-iteration cost is a practical barrier absent in convex minimization. For example, in the factored split, each proximal update requires O(n) operations for the dual variable, which dominates the O(d) cost for the primal variable when n ≫ d.

The experimental section partially mitigates this concern by showing empirical gains on binary classification (where n and d are comparable), but it does not address the theoretical gap for large n/d. A more thorough discussion of the n ≫ d regime—perhaps via synthetic experiments or asymptotic analysis—would strengthen the contribution. The paper’s current presentation risks misrepresenting the practical value of incremental methods in high-dimensional settings, where batch methods often dominate due to their lower per-iteration cost.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to saddle point optimization. The extension of variance reduction techniques is non-trivial and well-motivated, and the complexity analysis is thorough. However, the critical bottleneck in computational cost for the factored split must be addressed to fully justify the proposed methods. The authors’ rebuttal clarifies that the factored split is not universally inferior (e.g., it may offer advantages in specific structured problems), but the draft should explicitly acknowledge the n ≫ d limitation and its implications.
