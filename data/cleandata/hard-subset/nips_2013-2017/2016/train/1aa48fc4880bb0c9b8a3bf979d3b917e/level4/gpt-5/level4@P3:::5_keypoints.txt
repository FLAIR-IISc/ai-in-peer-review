- Extends SVRG/SAGA to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems with decomposable smooth part; first incremental methods with linear convergence for this class.
- Key technical novelty: convergence analysis via monotone-operator theory, covering variational inequalities beyond convex-minimization arguments.
- Algorithmic contributions: adapt forward–backward splitting to variance-reduced updates and propose SAGA- and SVRG-style estimators with non-uniform sampling.
- Introduces a Catalyst-style proximal-point acceleration tailored to saddle points to improve rates without full Nesterov machinery.
- Describes two operator-splitting strategies—by functions (individual) and by partial derivatives (factored)—with distinct storage and sampling trade-offs.
- For bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, provides sampling schemes and bounds in terms of ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, and ‖K‖max, showing non-uniform sampling yields strictly better constants.
- Empirical results: methods outperform batch FB and accelerated variants; accelerated SVRG + non-uniform sampling is best, especially for ill-conditioned problems.
- Strength: monotone-operator viewpoint gives first linear-convergence guarantees for variance-reduced saddle-point methods and extends to variational inequalities.
- Strength: addresses practical regime of non-separable losses/regularizers with efficient proximals where standard finite-sum methods may fail.
- Strength: clear algorithm design, explaining individual vs factored splits, storage needs, and sampling/complexity implications.
- Strength: principled non-uniform sampling with sharper theoretical constants and empirical corroboration.
- Strength: Catalyst-style acceleration is simple and empirically effective.
- Weakness: algorithmic ideas are straightforward adaptations; main novelty is analysis and sampling/acceleration, so theory/exposition must be very clear.
- Weakness: experiments lack full documentation of step-size σ and sampling probabilities π, hurting reproducibility.
- Weakness: SAGA with non-uniform sampling theoretically requires resampling, but experiments used a mixture—need clarification and possible theory extension.
- Clarity request: include a brief proof sketch (e.g., for Theorem 1) showing how monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity control variance-reduced iterates without co-coercivity.
- Clarity request: add a figure/table summarizing storage, per-iteration cost, and preferred sampling for individual vs factored splits.
- Originality: shifting variance-reduction analysis from co-coercive convex minimization to monotone-operator saddle-point setting is novel and impactful.
- Significance: advances theory and practice for large-scale saddle-point and variational-inequality problems, notably for non-separable structures with linear rates.
- Question 1: For general (non-bilinear) problems, what concrete π choices do you recommend and is there a computable surrogate for L̄(π)?
- Question 2: How was σ chosen in experiments—from upper bounds or tuned—and are there heuristics to estimate L̄(π) efficiently?
- Question 3: Why is non-uniform sampling more essential in saddle-point settings—due to two-sided coupling or lack of co-coercivity?
- Question 4: Practical guideline for selecting Catalyst parameter τ when Tprox(M) is non-negligible?
- Pros for acceptance: first linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for saddle points, broader monotone-operator analysis, practical sampling/acceleration with empirical gains.
- Cons for acceptance: need clearer experimental details, more intuition/proof sketches in main text, and current reliance on known strong convexity–concavity (no adaptivity).
- Suggestions: add concise proof sketch, provide practical π/σ recipes and robustness discussion, clarify SAGA resampling and experimental protocol, and include ablation studies (sampling and split variants).
- Minor comments: typos (line 78 "k"→"d"; line 121 extra y_{t−1}), define Ω and Ω* early, keep λ/γ scaling consistent, and report wall-clock time as well as passes.
