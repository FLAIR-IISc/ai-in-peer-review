- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with two splitting schemes (individual and factored).
- Provides comprehensive theoretical complexity analysis under different splitting approaches and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform).
- Experimental binary classification results show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch methods.
- Although conceptually straightforward, the extension is novel and technically sophisticated.
- For bilinear saddle-point problems, clear comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms align with known convex minimization results.
- Major concern: each iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, which can negate theoretical gains.
- When n ≫ d (common in ML), factored splitting (used in experiments) can be worse than batch forward–backward, per Table 2.
- Reviewer finds no clear theoretical advantage of variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems as presented.
- Requests more discussion addressing: (i) is factored splitting theoretically worse than individual splitting, (ii) when is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch forward–backward, and (iii) are the n and d dependencies in bounds tight.
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with authors’ responses, but scores remain unchanged.
