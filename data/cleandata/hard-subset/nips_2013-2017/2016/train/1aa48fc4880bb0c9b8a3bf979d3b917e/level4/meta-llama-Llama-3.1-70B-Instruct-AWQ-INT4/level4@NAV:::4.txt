This paper presents an extension of the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, achieving linear convergence. The proposed approach has been validated through both theoretical analysis and empirical results, showcasing the efficacy of variance reduction in addressing saddle-point problems.

The authors have successfully expanded the applicability of SVRG and SAGA to various non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. Notably, the convergence analysis for saddle-point problems is significantly more complex than that for convex problems, and the authors have employed monotone operators to establish convergence.

The analysis also highlights the broader applicability of the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms to problems such as variational inequalities in game theory. By adopting a stochastic approach, the authors have proposed two distinct splits of the gradient operator – element-wise split and factored split – for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The variance reduction technique is applied to stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems. Furthermore, the authors have explored non-uniform sampling, demonstrating through theoretical and empirical results that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms that with uniform sampling.

The experimental results presented in the main article and the appendix provide substantial evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on non-separable loss and regularizer.

Some minor suggestions for improvement:

* In Section 5, the authors follow [8] in incorporating an additional regularization term to push the update similar to an iterate point. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate point, (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve acceleration.
* A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, but it is uncertain whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA.
* To avoid confusion, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should be denoted using distinct symbols.
