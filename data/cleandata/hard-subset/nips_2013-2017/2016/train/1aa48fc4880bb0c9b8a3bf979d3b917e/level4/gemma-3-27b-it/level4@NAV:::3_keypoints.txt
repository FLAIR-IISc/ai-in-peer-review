- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems for individual and factored data splits.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis under different split types and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform).  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are important to outperform batch algorithms.  
- Extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle points is conceptually simple but yields novel, technically skilled results.  
- For the bilinear saddle-point case, offers a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank–Wolfe, mirroring known convex minimization behavior.  
- Major concern: proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, a fundamental bottleneck for these incremental methods on saddle problems.  
- As a result, even with accelerated SVRG and non-uniform sampling, overall complexity is not consistently better than accelerated batch methods.  
- When n >> d (common in ML), complexity under factored splits (used in simulations) can be worse than batch Frank–Wolfe (Table 2).  
- Unlike convex minimization, the theoretical benefits of variance reduction for these saddle problems are not immediately evident.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on: (i) whether factored splits are inherently less efficient than individual splits; (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch Frank–Wolfe; (iii) tightness of dependence on n and d.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer found the authors' response satisfactory but left scores unchanged.
