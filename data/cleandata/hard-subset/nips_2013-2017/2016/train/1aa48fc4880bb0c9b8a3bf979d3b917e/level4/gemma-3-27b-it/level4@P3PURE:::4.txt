This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, proving linear convergence. Both theoretical and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of these variance reduction methods in the context of saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. A significant contribution is the analysis of saddle-point problems, which is considerably more complex than previous convergence analyses for convex problems; the authors leverage monotone operators to establish convergence. This analysis also reveals that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities arising in game theory. To handle the stochastic nature of the problem, the authors propose two different gradient operator splits – an element-wise split and a factored split – for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. Variance reduction is applied within a stochastic forward-backward algorithm, commonly used for solving saddle-point problems. The authors also consider non-uniform sampling, and both theoretical and empirical results indicate that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms that with uniform sampling. Experimental results, presented in the main article and the appendix, provide strong support for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems with non-separable loss and regularizer terms. 

Several minor comments remain. Regarding the acceleration technique described in Section 5, which follows [8] by incorporating an additional regularization term to encourage updates towards a specific iterate point, it is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate point, denoted as (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve speed-up. A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG; it would be beneficial to know if similar theoretical results hold for SAGA. Finally, to avoid potential confusion, distinct symbols should be used for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K.
