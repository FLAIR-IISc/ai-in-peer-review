- Extends incremental variance-reduced algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, accelerated via Catalyst) to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems with individual and factored splits.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis under different splits and uniform/nonuniform sampling strategies.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
- Technical contributions are non-trivial and results are innovative despite the concept’s apparent straightforwardness.  
- For bilinear saddle points, gives clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward (FB) algorithms that aligns with known convex minimization results.  
- Major concern: each iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, which can negate incremental algorithm benefits when n >> d.  
- Under factored splits (used in experiments), complexity can be worse than batch FB, so variance reduction shows no clear theoretical advantage here.  
- Requests deeper discussion and answers to: (i) Is the factored split theoretically worse than the individual split? (ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB? (iii) Is the dependence on n and d tight?  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but leaves scores unchanged.
