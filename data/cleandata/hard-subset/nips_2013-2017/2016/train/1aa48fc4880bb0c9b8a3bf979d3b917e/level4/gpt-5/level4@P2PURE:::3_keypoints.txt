Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems for both individual and factored splits.  
Provides thorough complexity analysis under both splits and under uniform and nonuniform sampling strategies.  
Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to surpass competitive batch algorithms.  
Results are novel and technically nontrivial; clear comparison for bilinear saddle-point problem aligns with known convex minimization conclusions.  
Main concern: each iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, creating a bottleneck that can negate incremental methods’ advantages.  
For factored splits with n >> d, reported complexities (Table 2) can be worse than accelerated batch FB, questioning practical benefit of variance reduction.  
Reviewer requests clarification on: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependencies.  
Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with authors’ response but keeps the same scores.
