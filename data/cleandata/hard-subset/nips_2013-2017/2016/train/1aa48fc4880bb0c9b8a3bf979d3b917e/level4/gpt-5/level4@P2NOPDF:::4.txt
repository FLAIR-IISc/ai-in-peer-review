This paper extends stochastic variance-reduction methods—SVRG and SAGA—to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems and proves linear convergence. The algorithms handle a variety of non-separable saddle-point formulations in which both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion penalties) are non-separable.

A key contribution is the convergence analysis, which goes beyond standard convex optimization techniques. The authors employ monotone operator theory to analyze stochastic forward–backward splitting with variance reduction. The analysis also suggests that the proposed SVRG and SAGA extensions apply to a broader class of problems, such as certain variational inequalities in game theory.

Algorithmically, the paper introduces two stochastic gradient decompositions tailored to variance reduction: an element-wise split and a factored split, used in the SVRG and SAGA variants, respectively. These designs integrate variance reduction into commonly used stochastic forward–backward schemes for saddle-point problems. The authors also consider non-uniform sampling; both the theoretical results and empirical evidence indicate that well-chosen non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform sampling.

Empirically, the experiments in the main paper and the appendix substantiate the theoretical claims and demonstrate strong performance on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

Minor comments and questions:
- Acceleration (Section 5): Following [8], the method introduces an additional regularization term to pull the update toward a reference iterate. It is unclear how to adaptively update this reference point, (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to consistently achieve speed-ups in practice. Providing a principled update rule or practical guidance would improve clarity and reproducibility.
- Theory coverage: Convergence and acceleration guarantees are given for the SVRG variant. Do analogous results hold for SAGA under the same assumptions? If not, please clarify the challenges or provide partial guarantees.
- Notation: Using K(·) for a smooth function and K for a design matrix is confusing. Distinct symbols should be used to avoid ambiguity.

Overall, the paper makes a solid and technically nontrivial contribution by extending variance-reduced stochastic methods to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, providing linear rates under appropriate conditions, and supporting the theory with convincing experiments.
