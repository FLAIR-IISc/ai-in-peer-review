This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems by applying stochastic variance-reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, in conjunction with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms within the monotone operator framework, demonstrating linear convergence. The paper further introduces an accelerated variant and analyzes a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty and Significance**

The paper's contributions are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides new insights into solving saddle-point problems. A key contribution is the proposal of numerous extensions, most notably the applicability of the proposed method to non-separable functions.

**Technical Quality and Clarity**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper's presentation could be significantly improved for better accessibility. The connection to monotone operators, which is fundamental to the entire analysis, is not made clear until it is introduced as an extension in Section 6. This structural choice makes the core theoretical development difficult to follow. Clarifying this connection earlier in the paper would greatly benefit the reader.

The experimental evaluation could also be strengthened. The results are currently limited to only two datasets. Furthermore, for the case of separable functions, the authors should include a comparison against relevant accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm presented in [1].

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:

1.  Could the authors comment on the strength of Assumptions (A)-(C), particularly Assumption (A)? For instance, do these assumptions hold for the saddle-point formulation of a standard Support Vector Machine (SVM)?
2.  The distinction between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual methods is not entirely clear. Could the authors elaborate on the key differences?
3.  In Theorem 2, the definition of the constant \(\mu\) is unclear. Is this the same monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? Additionally, how does the convergence result in Theorem 2 compare to that of Theorem 1?
4.  The relationship between Assumptions (A)-(C) in the main text and the strong monotonicity assumption in the appendix is confusing. Do Assumptions (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity? A clearer explanation of this transition is needed.

**Minor Issues**

*   There appears to be inconsistent notation for the constant L, which is variously referred to as both the condition number ("The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem") and a Lipschitz constant ("we need the Lipschitz constant L"). Please clarify and use consistent terminology.
*   Please add a citation for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
