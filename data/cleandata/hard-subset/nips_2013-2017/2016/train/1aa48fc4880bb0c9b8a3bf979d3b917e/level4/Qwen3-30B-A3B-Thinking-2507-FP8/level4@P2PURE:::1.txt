

This paper addresses saddle-point problems with convex-concave structure by integrating existing stochastic variance-reduced techniques (SVRG, SAGA) with proximal operators. The authors analyze these algorithms within the monotone operator framework, establishing linear convergence. They further propose an accelerated variant and investigate non-uniform sampling.  

Novelty/Originality: The contributions are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides new insights for saddle-point problems. The most notable extension is the applicability to non-separable objective functions.  

Technical Quality: While the theoretical contributions are strong, the presentation could be improved. The connection to monotone operators should be clarified earlier, as the analysis fundamentally relies on this framework despite being introduced only in Section 6. Experimentally, results on more than two datasets would strengthen the evaluation. For separable cases, comparison with accelerated methods like the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang et al., 2015, http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf) is essential; this reference should be added.  

Key Clarifications Needed:  
1. How strong are assumptions A–C, particularly assumption (A)? Do they hold for SVM-induced saddle-point problems?  
2. Please clarify the distinction between the stochastic primal-dual method and the proposed approach.  
3. In Theorem 2, what is the constant $\mu$? Is it the monotonicity constant from the appendix? How does this result compare to Theorem 1?  
4. The transition from assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is unclear. Do (A)–(C) imply monotonicity?  

Minor Issues:  
- Confusion between the constant $L$ (used as condition number) and Lipschitz constant: "The quantity $L$ represents the condition number of the problem" versus "we need the Lipschitz constant $L$."  
- The Forward-Backward algorithm requires a proper reference.
