This paper extends several recent incremental algorithms, namely SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite-sum structure. The authors consider problem formulations arising from two types of splits: "individual" and "factored." The paper provides a thorough theoretical study of the complexity of these algorithms under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification example show that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming competitive batch algorithms.

While the idea of extending variance-reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems might appear straightforward, the results established here are notably novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial. For the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, the authors present a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms. This comparison shares a resemblance to what is known for the convex minimization case and provides an intuitive and coherent picture.

However, I have one major concern. While the saddle point formulation addresses the challenge of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms seem to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck: an O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity—even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling as shown in Tables 1 and 2—is not necessarily better than that of the accelerated batch version. This issue is particularly evident for factored splits (which are used in the simulations) in the common machine learning setting where the number of data points, n, is substantially larger than the dimension, d. In this regime, the theoretical complexity shown in Table 2 is actually worse than that of batch forward-backward algorithms. Unlike in the convex minimization setting, a clear and substantial theoretical advantage for using variance reduction is not demonstrated here.

I would suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions:
1.  Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits?
2.  Under what conditions is the accelerated SVRG algorithm favorable compared to the batch forward-backward algorithm?
3.  Is the theoretical dependence on n and d tight?
