This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques, SVRG and SAGA, to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, achieving linear convergence. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. A significant contribution of this work is the convergence analysis on saddle-point problems, which is more complex than that of convex problems. The authors employ monotone operators to prove convergence, demonstrating the applicability of the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

The authors propose two different splits of the gradient operator, namely the element-wise split and the factored split, for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. These variance reduction techniques are applied to stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems. The paper also explores non-uniform sampling, and both theoretical and empirical results show that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms that with uniform sampling. Experimental results in the main article and the appendix provide sufficient evidence to support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both non-separable loss and regularizer.

The authors' extension of SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems is a valuable contribution to the field. The use of monotone operators to prove convergence is a notable aspect of this work, as it allows for the analysis of more complex problems. The proposed algorithms' ability to handle non-separable loss and regularizer functions makes them applicable to a wide range of problems. The experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

Some minor comments and suggestions for improvement are as follows:

* In Section 5, the authors incorporate an additional regularization term to accelerate the update, following [8]. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update the iterate point, namely (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve speed-up. Providing more details on this aspect would strengthen the acceleration technique.
* A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, but it is unclear whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA. Clarifying this point would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed algorithms.
* To avoid confusion, it would be beneficial to use different symbols for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of stochastic variance reduction techniques, providing a valuable extension to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors' use of monotone operators and the proposal of two different splits of the gradient operator are notable aspects of this work. With some minor revisions to address the mentioned comments, this paper has the potential to make a substantial impact in the field.
