This paper extends stochastic variance-reduced methods, namely SVRG and SAGA, to address convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors ground their convergence analysis in the theory of monotone operators, establishing linear convergence rates for the proposed algorithms. The work is extended by incorporating an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework and by analyzing the benefits of a non-uniform sampling strategy. A key aspect of this work is its applicability to problems where the objective function is not separable, a common scenario in machine learning that is not handled by many existing variance-reduced methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents the first linearly convergent, incremental algorithms for this general class of saddle-point problems. This is a significant contribution, as such problems appear frequently in machine learning (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction, AUC maximization) but have lacked dedicated large-scale solvers with variance reduction.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-developed. By leveraging the framework of monotone operators, the proofs are general and provide new insights that go beyond the typical analysis for convex minimization. The application to non-separable functions is a particularly strong and novel aspect.
*   **Completeness:** The paper is comprehensive, addressing not only the direct extension of SVRG/SAGA but also practical and theoretical improvements through non-uniform sampling and acceleration. This provides a complete and powerful toolkit for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's organization could be improved. The analysis is fundamentally based on the monotone operator framework, but this is only formally presented as an "extension" in Section 6. Introducing this concept earlier would better motivate the theoretical analysis provided in the appendix and make the paper's core contributions more accessible.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evidence, while positive, is based on only two datasets. The paper's claims would be more convincingly supported by experiments on a broader range of datasets and problem types to demonstrate the general applicability and robustness of the proposed methods.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** For the special case of separable functions, the paper should compare against other relevant stochastic primal-dual algorithms. Specifically, the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm should be discussed and ideally included as a baseline in the experiments. Please add the following reference: Zhang, Y., & Xiao, L. (2015). Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization. *ICML*.

**Questions for Clarification:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the scope and limitations of assumptions (A)-(C)? In particular, how restrictive is assumption (A) regarding the strong convex-concave property of M? It would be helpful to discuss whether a canonical machine learning problem, such as the saddle-point formulation for SVMs, satisfies these assumptions.
2.  The relationship between the proposed methods and the existing literature on stochastic primal-dual methods could be clarified further. A brief discussion on the key conceptual or algorithmic differences would help position the contribution more precisely.
3.  In Theorem 2, the constant `µ` is not defined in the main text. Please state its definition explicitly (it appears to be the monotonicity constant from the appendix). Additionally, a more direct comparison of the theoretical rates in Theorem 1 (SVRG) and Theorem 2 (SAGA) would be valuable for understanding their relative trade-offs.
4.  The connection between assumptions (A)-(C) for saddle-point problems and the strong monotonicity property used in the proofs is left to the appendix. To improve the paper's readability, it would be beneficial to include a short explanation in the main text of how assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The constant `L` is referred to as both "the condition number of the problem" and "the Lipschitz-constant". For clarity, please ensure the definition is precise and consistent throughout the paper.
*   In Section 2.2, please provide a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
