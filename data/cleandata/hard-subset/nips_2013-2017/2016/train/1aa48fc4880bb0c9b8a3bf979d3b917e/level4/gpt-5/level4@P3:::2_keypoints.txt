- Studies min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with smooth K and efficiently-proximal, strongly convex–concave M.  
- Extends variance-reduced finite-sum methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this saddle-point/monotone-operator setting.  
- Shows linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz-gradient assumptions.  
- Uses monotone-operator formulation to obtain guarantees that cover broader problems (e.g., variational inequalities).  
- Introduces two decomposition notions (individual functions vs. factored/partial-derivative splits).  
- Emphasizes importance of non-uniform sampling and provides tailored sampling bounds.  
- Proposes a “catalyst”-style proximal-point acceleration that improves dependence on problem constants.  
- Empirical results on binary classification with non-separable loss/regularizer show gains over batch and stochastic baselines.  
- Builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA literature but departs by handling non-separable convex–concave problems.  
- Connects to primal–dual splitting and monotone-operator literature, distinguishing from methods requiring separability or co-coercivity.  
- Theoretical analyses appear sound and technically nontrivial, properly tracking λ, γ, L, and L̄(π) scalings.  
- Acceleration proof is provided for SVRG but not for SAGA; extension to SAGA is discussed only briefly.  
- Strong convexity–concavity assumption is restrictive; need clarity on one-sided strong curvature scenarios.  
- Request for practical regularization/restart strategies to induce required strong properties without harming models.  
- Guidance on tuning when constants are unknown (adaptivity/step-size selection) is lacking.  
- Non-uniform sampling is practical (e.g., row/column norms) but preprocessing costs and dynamic updates should be discussed.  
- SAGA’s memory–time trade-offs are noted (O(n+d) vs O(nd)), but could be articulated more explicitly.  
- Resampling required for non-uniform SAGA is mentioned; experiments use a heuristic mixture that should be quantified.  
- Experiments support claims, especially for acceleration and non-uniform sampling, but scope is limited.  
- Recommend additional comparisons to strong non-separable baselines (e.g., accelerated primal–dual methods) and ablations on sampling/mini-batch sizes.  
- Presentation is well organized; notation-heavy sections would benefit from summaries of key constants and a practical step-size guide.  
- The extension to non-separable saddle points, factored vs. individual split treatment, and tailored non-uniform sampling are novel contributions.  
- Catalyst-based acceleration in this setting is a meaningful, original addition.  
- The work is significant: first incremental methods with linear rates for general convex–concave saddle-point problems and broader monotone-operator impact.  
- Pros: first linear-rate variance-reduced incremental methods for general saddle-points; clear decomposition taxonomy; principled non-uniform sampling; simple proximal acceleration; comprehensive theory and useful practical guidance.  
- Cons: requires strong convexity–concavity; SAGA acceleration unproven; limited experimental breadth; insufficient discussion of sampling-preprocessing and SAGA memory costs.  
- Key author requests: handle one-sided strong curvature or propose practical schemes; include step-size procedure when constants unknown; quantify preprocessing cost and wall-clock impact; provide accelerated SAGA results or clarify proof obstacles; expand experiments with more datasets and strong baselines.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing assumption, tuning, and experimental breadth concerns.
