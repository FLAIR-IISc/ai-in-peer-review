This paper presents an extension of the stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. This work builds upon recent advances in accelerating stochastic gradient methods for finite sums in convex minimization [1, 2, 3], and provides the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for structured saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning applications involving non-separable losses or regularizers. The core of the theoretical contribution is a novel convergence analysis based on the theory of monotone operators, which departs from standard analyses for convex optimization and demonstrates the applicability of the methods to a broader problem class, including variational inequalities. The authors also investigate practical aspects such as different operator splitting strategies (factored vs. element-wise), the importance of non-uniform sampling, and an acceleration scheme based on the catalyst framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. While variance reduction techniques are well-established for convex minimization, their extension to saddle-point formulations is novel and significant. It enables the application of fast, linearly convergent methods to problems with non-separable structures (e.g., structured output losses, total variation regularization) where previous variance-reduced methods could not be applied directly to the primal or dual formulations.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. Shifting the proof framework from convex analysis to the more general language of monotone operators is insightful and necessary for this problem class. This provides a rigorous foundation for the linear convergence claims and correctly identifies that properties like co-coercivity, used in some related analyses, do not hold here. The derived complexity bounds clearly show the theoretical advantages of variance reduction and non-uniform sampling over batch and standard stochastic approaches.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem with relevant machine learning examples, reviews existing batch and stochastic algorithms, and presents the new SVRG and SAGA variants systematically. The investigation into different sampling schemes and splitting strategies adds practical depth. The inclusion of an acceleration framework further strengthens the contribution, leading to an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated full-batch methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide strong evidence for the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods. The results on datasets with non-separable objectives confirm the theoretical findings, showing that SVRG and SAGA with non-uniform sampling significantly outperform existing methods. The comparison also rightly shows that for ill-conditioned problems, the proposed accelerated SVRG variant offers the most substantial gains.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity on Adaptive Acceleration:** In Section 5, the acceleration scheme involves solving a sequence of regularized subproblems centered around an iterate `(\bar{x}, \bar{y})`. The paper suggests that this iterate can be updated "more aggressively to speed-up the algorithm." This is an interesting practical point, but the paper lacks specifics on how such an adaptive update would be implemented. A brief discussion of a concrete heuristic or strategy would be valuable for practitioners.
*   **Acceleration for SAGA:** The convergence proof for the catalyst acceleration is provided for SVRG. The authors state that the same scheme "should work for SAGA," but this claim is not formally substantiated. While a full proof may not be necessary, a more detailed argument or reference justifying this extension would make the claim more convincing and the work more complete.
*   **Minor Notational Ambiguity:** The symbol `K` is used to denote both the smooth function `K(x, y)` and the design matrix in the examples (e.g., `K \in R^{n \times d}`). While clear from context, using distinct notation (e.g., `\mathcal{K}` for the function and `K` for the matrix) would prevent any potential confusion for the reader and improve the overall clarity of the presentation.
