This paper presents an extension of the stochastic variance reduction methods SVRG and SAGA to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, with a demonstrated linear convergence rate. Both theoretical analysis and empirical validation confirm the efficacy of the proposed variance reduction approach for these saddle-point problems. The developed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, accommodating non-separable loss functions (such as those found in structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., fusion terms). A key contribution lies in the complex convergence analysis required for saddle-point problems, where the authors leverage monotone operators to establish convergence – a significant departure from prior analyses focused on convex problems. This analysis also reveals the applicability of the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms to broader problem classes, including variational inequalities in game theory. To facilitate a stochastic approach, the authors introduce two distinct gradient operator splitting strategies – element-wise and factored splits – tailored for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. Variance reduction is applied within the framework of stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly employed for saddle-point problems. The investigation also incorporates non-uniform sampling, and both theoretical and empirical findings indicate superior performance compared to uniform sampling. Comprehensive experimental results, presented in the main text and the appendix, strongly support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods with both non-separable losses and regularizers. 

Minor points for consideration: The acceleration technique described in Section 5, following [8], introduces an additional regularization term to guide the update towards a previous iterate. The adaptive updating strategy for this iterate, denoted as (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve speed-up remains unclear. While a convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, it is not immediately apparent whether equivalent theoretical results would hold for SAGA. Finally, to avoid potential confusion, distinct notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K would be beneficial.
