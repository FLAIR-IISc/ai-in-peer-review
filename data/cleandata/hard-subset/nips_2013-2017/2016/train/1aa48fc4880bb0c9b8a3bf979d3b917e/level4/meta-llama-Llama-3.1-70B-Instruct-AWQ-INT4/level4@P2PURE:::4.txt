This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, where linear convergence is established. Theoretical and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the variance reduction method in addressing saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms can be applied to a variety of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. A significant contribution of this work is the convergence analysis on saddle-point problems, which is more complex compared to convex problems. The authors employ monotone operators to prove convergence, and the analysis reveals that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms can be applied to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory. 

Considering a stochastic approach, the authors propose two distinct splits of the gradient operator: element-wise split for SVRG and factored split for SAGA. The variance reduction technique operates on stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also explored, and both theoretical and empirical results indicate that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms that with uniform sampling. Experimental results presented in the main article and the appendix provide substantial evidence for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both non-separable loss and regularizer.

Some minor comments are in order. In Section 5, the authors follow [8] and incorporate an additional regularization term to push the update towards an iterate point. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate point, namely (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve acceleration. A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, but it is unclear whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA. Furthermore, to avoid confusion, the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should be denoted using different symbols.
