This paper extends several recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants) to solve strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure arising from two types of decompositions: individual splits and factored splits. The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis of these methods under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform and nonuniform). An experiment on a specific binary classification task shows that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform strong batch baselines. Although extending variance-reduced incremental methods to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, the results here are genuinely novel and the technical development is nontrivial.

For the important bilinear saddle-point setting, the paper presents a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms. The observed relationships mirror what is typically known in convex minimization, and the conclusions are coherent.

My major concern is the following. While formulating and solving the saddle-point problem addresses the nonseparability of the loss, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: an O(n + d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling, as reported in Tables 1 and 2—is not necessarily better than that of accelerated batch methods. This becomes especially problematic when n is substantially larger than d, which is common in machine learning applications. In that regime, the complexity under factored splits (also used in the simulations) is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization setting, I do not see a clear, substantial theoretical advantage for variance reduction here.

I suggest the authors provide a more in-depth discussion and clarify:
i) Is the factored split (theoretically) worse than the individual split?
ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG favorable compared to batch FB?
iii) Are the dependences on n and d tight?
