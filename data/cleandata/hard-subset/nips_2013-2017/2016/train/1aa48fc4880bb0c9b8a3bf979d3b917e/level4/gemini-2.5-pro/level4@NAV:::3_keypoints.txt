- Generalizes SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored splits.  
- Presents comprehensive complexity analysis for both split types and for uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are necessary to beat batch algorithms.  
- Adaptation of variance-reduction/incremental methods is intuitive but the theoretical results and technical derivations are novel and non-trivial.  
- For bilinear saddle-point problems, provides clear, sound comparisons across batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, aligning with known convex minimization trade-offs.  
- Major reservation: inherent computational bottleneck—proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration—limits the incremental methods' practical advantage.  
- Consequently, accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling is not consistently superior to accelerated batch methods, particularly when n >> d.  
- Factored splits (used in simulations) can yield worse complexity than batch FB, so a clear theoretical benefit of variance reduction is not evident.  
- Requests for clarification: (i) are factored splits theoretically inferior to individual splits? (ii) under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB? (iii) are the n and d complexity dependencies tight?  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with authors' response, but keeps the same scores.
