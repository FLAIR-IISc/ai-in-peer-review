The paper generalizes several recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants) to tackle strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure, arising from two types of decompositions: individual or factored splits. The authors present a thorough complexity analysis of these methods under different splits and under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a specific binary classification example indicate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, the results here are genuinely novel and the technical development is clearly non-trivial. For the important bilinear saddle-point problem, the paper offers a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, resembling what is typically known in convex minimization, and this is consistent with my understanding.

However, my main concern is the following. Although solving the saddle-point formulation mitigates the non-separability of the loss, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck—namely, an O(n + d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as reported in Tables 1 and 2), is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch variant. In particular, when the number of samples n is much larger than the dimension d, which is common in many machine learning applications, the complexity under factored splits (used later in the simulations) is actually worse than for batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization setting, I do not see a clear, substantial theoretical advantage of variance reduction here. I suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion and clarify: (i) whether factored splits are (theoretically) worse than individual splits; (ii) when accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB; and (iii) whether the dependences on n and d are tight.
