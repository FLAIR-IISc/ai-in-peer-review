

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence. Theoretical and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The work significantly broadens the applicability of variance reduction to non-separable saddle-point problems, where both loss functions (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., fusion terms) may be non-separable. A key contribution lies in the convergence analysis, which leverages monotone operators to address the greater complexity of saddle-point problems compared to prior convex analyses. The analysis further demonstrates that the extended algorithms apply to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory. For the stochastic approach, the authors propose distinct gradient operator splits: an element-wise split for SVRG and a factored split for SAGA. Variance reduction operates within stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems, and non-uniform sampling is employed. Both theoretical and empirical results confirm that non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform sampling. Experimental results in the main text and appendix comprehensively support the theoretical claims and validate the methods' effectiveness on non-separable loss and regularizer settings.
