This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction gradient methods SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the broader class of convex-concave saddle-point problems. This formulation is particularly relevant for many machine learning tasks that involve non-separable loss functions or regularizers. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical and practical treatment. Key contributions include: (1) proposing SVRG and SAGA-style algorithms for saddle-point problems, (2) providing the first linear convergence analysis for such methods, which is notably based on the theory of monotone operators rather than potential function arguments used in convex minimization, thus generalizing the results to problems like variational inequalities, (3) analyzing different decomposition strategies and highlighting the practical and theoretical importance of non-uniform sampling, and (4) demonstrating that these new algorithms can be readily accelerated using a catalyst-like framework.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the claims of linear convergence are well-supported by the proofs in the appendix. The use of monotone operator theory is a sophisticated and appropriate choice for this problem class, as it naturally handles the saddle-point structure where standard descent-based analyses fail. The work is presented as a complete contribution, covering the algorithm design, theoretical guarantees, acceleration, and empirical validation. The authors are careful to state their assumptions clearly (e.g., strong convexity-concavity).

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and a concise summary of contributions. The problem formulation, assumptions, and connections to existing batch and stochastic methods are laid out logically, making the paper easy to follow for a reader familiar with the area. The algorithms are presented clearly. The discussion on different sampling schemes and their impact on complexity bounds is particularly insightful.

**Originality:** The contributions are highly original. While the algorithms are natural extensions of SVRG and SAGA, this work appears to be the first to successfully analyze their linear convergence for the general composite saddle-point setting. Moving beyond the standard analysis for convex minimization to a more general framework of monotone operators is a significant and novel theoretical contribution. The paper clearly delineates how its approach differs from and extends previous work on variance reduction and saddle-point optimization.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a substantial impact. Many important problems in machine learning, such as training models with structured non-separable penalties (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso) or certain formulations of robust optimization and structured prediction, fit naturally into the saddle-point framework. This work provides the first efficient, large-scale, and provably fast algorithms for this setting, making previously challenging optimization problems more tractable. Researchers and practitioners are likely to use these ideas and build upon them.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**
*   **Novelty and Impact:** This is a pioneering work that provides the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems, filling a notable gap in the optimization literature for machine learning.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The convergence analysis based on monotone operators is elegant, powerful, and more general than the analyses for the original SVRG/SAGA methods. This robust theoretical framework is a major strength.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper goes beyond pure theory by analyzing crucial practical aspects like non-uniform sampling and providing a simple yet effective acceleration technique. The experiments confirm that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG, significantly outperform existing batch and stochastic algorithms.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for improvement):**
*   **Strong Convexity-Concavity Assumption:** A primary limitation is the reliance on the strong convexity-concavity of the function M. In many machine learning applications (e.g., arising from Fenchel duality), the problem is often only strongly convex in the primal variable and merely concave in the dual. While the authors mention relaxing this assumption as future work, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this limitation and its implications. A discussion on whether the analysis could be extended to derive sub-linear rates for the non-strongly concave case would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on the chosen tasks. However, Section 2.1 motivates the work with a diverse set of applications, including robust optimization and unsupervised learning. Broadening the experimental validation to include at least one other distinct problem class would provide stronger evidence for the general applicability of the proposed framework.
