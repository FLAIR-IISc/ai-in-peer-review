The paper extends recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst – to address strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite-sum structure, arising from either individual or factored splits. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under various splits and sampling strategies (uniform/nonuniform). Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems might seem conceptually straightforward, the results presented here are notably novel and technically challenging. Specifically for the important bilinear saddle point problem, the authors offer a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms, exhibiting parallels with known results for convex minimization – a connection that I find logically sound.

However, a major concern remains. Although solving the saddle point problem addresses the difficulty of loss function non-separability, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: an O(n+d) computational cost for evaluating the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as detailed in Tables 1 and 2), is not necessarily superior to that of accelerated batch methods. In particular, when the number of data points, *n*, is significantly larger than the dimension, *d* – a common scenario in machine learning – the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulation) is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the well-established advantages in convex minimization, I do not observe a clear, substantial benefit from utilizing variance reduction here, from a theoretical perspective. 

I recommend the authors provide a more in-depth discussion of this matter, clarifying the following points: i) Is a factored split theoretically inferior to an individual split? ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG favorable compared to batch FB? iii) Is the observed dependence on *n* and *d* tight?
