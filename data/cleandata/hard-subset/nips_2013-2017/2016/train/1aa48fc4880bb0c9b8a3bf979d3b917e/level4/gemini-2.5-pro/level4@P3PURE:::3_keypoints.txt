- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems with “individual” and “factored” splits.  
- Provides a thorough theoretical complexity analysis under both uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
- The extension of variance-reduction/incremental methods to saddle points is novel and technically non-trivial.  
- For bilinear saddle points, presents a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward methods, analogous to convex minimization intuition.  
- Major concern: incremental methods require an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator per iteration, creating a fundamental bottleneck.  
- Consequently, overall complexity (including accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch methods; for factored splits with n >> d, theoretical complexity in Table 2 is worse than batch forward-backward.  
- Therefore, unlike in convex minimization, no clear substantial theoretical advantage for variance reduction is demonstrated here.  
- Reviewer requests clarification/discussion on: (1) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (2) when accelerated SVRG is favorable vs batch forward-backward, and (3) tightness of n and d dependence.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but keeps the same scores.
