- Proposes first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for saddle-point problems, extending SVRG and SAGA.  
- Provides convergence analysis specific to saddle-point problems, distinct from convex minimization theory.  
- Studies algorithmic complexity under individual vs. factored splits and uniform vs. nonuniform sampling strategies.  
- Shows nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform competitive batch algorithms in experiments.  
- Demonstrates easy acceleration via a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework.  
- Gives a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms for bilinear saddle-point problems.  
- Experimental validation on a real-world binary classification example supports theoretical claims.  
- Paper is technically sound with well-supported claims and careful evaluation.  
- Writing is clear, well-organized, and reproducible for expert readers.  
- Presents a novel and significant extension of variance-reduction methods to saddle-point settings.  
- Results are likely to be useful to practitioners and stimulate further research.  
- Major concern: per-iteration proximal operator cost of O(n + d) may negate incremental methods' complexity gains.  
- Reviewer requests deeper discussion on whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits.  
- Reviewer asks when accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB and whether the dependence on n and d is tight.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the stated concerns and provide further clarification.
