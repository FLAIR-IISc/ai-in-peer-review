This work addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems by adapting existing stochastic variance-reduced techniques, namely SVRG and SAGA, for use with a proximal operator. The authors conduct their analysis within the monotone operator setting, demonstrating linear convergence for the proposed algorithms. The paper further extends this contribution by presenting an accelerated variant and analyzing a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty/Originality:**
The contributions of this manuscript are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and offers new insights into solving saddle-point problems. Of the several extensions proposed, the method's applicability to non-separable functions is the most significant and impactful.

**Technical Quality:**
While the theoretical contributions are commendable, the presentation of the material could be significantly improved for accessibility. The connection to monotone operators, which underpins the core analysis, is introduced as an extension in Section 6. The paper's clarity would benefit greatly from making this connection more explicit from the outset.

On the experimental side, the evaluation would be more convincing if it were conducted on more than two datasets. For the case of separable functions, the authors should also provide a comparison to relevant accelerated methods, such as the stochastic Chambolle-Pock variant from Zhang et al., 2015 (http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf). This reference should be incorporated into the paper.

There are several points that require clarification:
1.  Could the authors elaborate on the strength of assumptions (A)-(C), with a particular focus on assumption (A)? For example, it is unclear whether these assumptions hold for the saddle-point formulation of an SVM.
2.  The differences between the proposed method and the stochastic primal-dual method are not immediately apparent. Further discussion on this point is needed.
3.  Regarding Theorem 2, the constant `μ` is not clearly defined. Is this the monotonicity constant from the appendix? A direct comparison between the results of Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 would also be helpful.
4.  The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to the strong monotonicity property in the appendix is unclear. Do assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity? This connection should be explained more clearly.

**Minor Issues:**
*   There is inconsistent usage of the constant `L`, which is defined as both the condition number ("The quantity L represents the condition number...") and a Lipschitz constant ("...we need the Lipschitz constant L").
*   Please add a citation for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
