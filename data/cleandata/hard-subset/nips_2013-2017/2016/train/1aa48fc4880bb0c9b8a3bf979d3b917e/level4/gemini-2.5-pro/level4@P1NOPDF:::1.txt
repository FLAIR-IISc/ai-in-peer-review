This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems by adapting existing stochastic variance-reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, for use with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms within the monotone operator framework, proving linear convergence. They also propose an accelerated variant and analyze a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty and Originality**

The contributions of this paper are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides new insights into solving saddle-point problems. Among the paper's several extensions, the most significant is the application of the proposed method to non-separable functions, which broadens its applicability.

**Technical Quality**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper's presentation could be significantly improved. The manuscript would be more accessible if the connection to monotone operators were made explicit from the beginning. This connection is central to the analysis, yet it is presented as an extension in Section 6, which may confuse the reader.

The experimental evaluation could also be strengthened. The results are based on only two datasets, which limits the empirical validation of the proposed methods. For the case of separable functions, the authors should include a comparison with accelerated methods like the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm presented in:

Zhang, Y. and Lin, Y. (2015). Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization. *Proceedings of the 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning*, JMLR W&CP 37.

Please add this reference and the corresponding comparison to your submission.

**Questions for the Authors**

I have a few points that I would like the authors to clarify:

1.  How restrictive are assumptions (A)-(C), particularly assumption (A)? For instance, do these assumptions hold for the saddle-point formulation of a Support Vector Machine (SVM)?

2.  The distinction between the proposed method and other stochastic primal-dual methods is not entirely clear. Could the authors elaborate on the key differences?

3.  In Theorem 2, the definition of the constant μ is ambiguous. Is this the strong monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? A clarification is also needed on how this result can be compared with the convergence rate in Theorem 1.

4.  The relationship between assumptions (A)-(C) in the main text and the strong monotonicity assumption in the appendix is confusing. Do assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity?

**Minor Issues**

*   There appears to be some notational confusion regarding the constant L, which is defined as both the condition number ("The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem") and a Lipschitz constant ("we need the Lipschitz constant L"). Please use distinct notation for these two concepts.
*   Please add a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
