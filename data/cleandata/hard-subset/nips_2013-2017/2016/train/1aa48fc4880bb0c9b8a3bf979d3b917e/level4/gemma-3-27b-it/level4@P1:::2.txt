This paper addresses the challenging problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, by extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods like SVRG and SAGA. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these adapted algorithms, demonstrating linear convergence – a significant contribution. They highlight several key insights: the applicability of their approach beyond standard saddle-point problems to monotone operators and variational inequalities, the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and the potential for acceleration using a catalyst framework. The paper also includes a discussion of different “splits” within the saddle-point formulation and presents experimental results validating their approach.

The work builds upon the successes of SVRG [1] and SAGA [2] in convex minimization by adapting these algorithms to the saddle-point setting. This extension is not trivial, as the standard convex minimization analysis does not directly apply. The authors skillfully leverage the concept of monotone operators to prove convergence, broadening the scope of their results to include variational inequalities [5, 6]. The exploration of different splitting strategies – in terms of functions versus partial derivatives – is insightful and provides a deeper understanding of the problem structure. The emphasis on non-uniform sampling, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, is a valuable contribution. Furthermore, the successful integration of the catalyst framework [7] demonstrates a practical method for further performance improvement.

A key strength of the paper is its thorough theoretical analysis, particularly the convergence analysis based on monotone operators. This allows for a more general framework and expands the applicability of the proposed algorithms. The authors also clearly articulate the differences between their approach and existing methods, and they provide a comprehensive comparison to related work. The experimental section, while focused on specific examples, provides evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms and confirms the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

However, the strong convexity assumptions (A) on both the primal and dual problems appear restrictive. Investigating the convergence properties under weaker assumptions (e.g., strong convexity on only one of the problems) would broaden the applicability of the work. While the experiments demonstrate good performance on the chosen datasets, including a wider range of problems and comparing against more state-of-the-art methods would further strengthen the evaluation.  The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges associated with implementing the non-uniform sampling schemes, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The dependence on parameters like λ and γ, and potential strategies for their tuning, could be elaborated on.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is a novel and significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis based on monotone operators is rigorous and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is important in machine learning, and the proposed methods have the potential to improve the efficiency of training algorithms.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Generality:** Extension to monotone operators expands the applicability of the work beyond saddle-point problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The requirement of strong convexity on both the primal and dual problems is a limitation.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:**  Expanding the range of experimental datasets and comparisons would strengthen the evaluation.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed methods are novel and practical, and the experimental results are encouraging. Addressing the limitations regarding the strong convexity assumption and expanding the experimental evaluation would further enhance the paper. I recommend acceptance.
