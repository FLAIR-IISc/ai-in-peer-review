- Extends SVRG and SAGA to separable convex-concave saddle-point problems, yielding the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Analyzes algorithms via monotone operator theory, enabling linear convergence guarantees and extension to variational inequalities.  
- Builds on prior stochastic variance-reduction work (SAGA, SVRG) and fills a gap for saddle-point problems.  
- Provides detailed analysis of split structures (function vs. partial-derivative splits), necessity of convex-concave splits, and benefits of non-uniform sampling.  
- Introduces an accelerated variant using the catalyst framework with theoretical improvement over batch methods.  
- Theoretical contributions are substantial and well-motivated, offering novel convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction on saddle points.  
- Extension to monotone operators (Section 6) meaningfully broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems.  
- Accelerated catalyst variant is well-justified and shows clear theoretical advantages.  
- Experiments demonstrate practical effectiveness, particularly highlighting the value of non-uniform sampling.  
- Monotone operator framework is presented as an afterthought and needs a more coherent, integrated treatment.  
- Experimental validation is limited to two datasets, which is insufficient to demonstrate generality.  
- Lacks comparison to specialized accelerated methods for separable problems (e.g., stochastic Chambolle-Pock), weakening empirical claims.  
- Assumption (A) on strong convex-concavity requires further justification and verification on common problems (e.g., SVMs with separable regularizers).  
- The relationship to existing stochastic primal-dual methods is insufficiently elaborated; distinctions and improvements should be clarified.  
- The constant μ in Theorem 2 is ill-defined and should be explicitly connected to the monotonicity constant in the appendix.  
- It is unclear whether assumptions (A)-(C) imply the strong monotonicity used in the appendix; this implication must be made explicit.  
- Notational confusion: L is inconsistently described as both condition number and Lipschitz constant.  
- Forward-Backward algorithm section should cite the original reference for the method.
