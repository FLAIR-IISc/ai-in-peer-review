This paper addresses saddle-point problems with a convex-concave property, demonstrating how existing stochastic variance reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, can be applied in conjunction with a proximal operator to solve these problems. The authors examine these algorithms within the monotone operator setting, establishing linear convergence, and propose an accelerated variant as well as analyze a non-uniform sampling scheme. 

The paper's contributions are significant, offering thorough theoretical analysis that provides novel insights into saddle-point problems. Notably, the authors' method can be applied to non-separable functions, which is a substantial extension.

However, despite the theoretical quality of the paper, its presentation falls short. To improve accessibility, the authors should clarify the connection to monotone operators more explicitly, as this concept appears to be a foundation of their analysis, yet is only explicitly mentioned as an extension in Section 6.

The experimental results are also limited, as they only cover two datasets. For the case of separable functions, the authors should compare their method to accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang et al., 2015). The authors should include this reference in their submission.

There are several points that require clarification from the authors: 

1. The strength of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A), should be discussed. For example, do these assumptions hold when solving the saddle-point problem induced by SVM?

2. The authors should elaborate on the differences between the stochastic primal-dual method and their proposed method, as these are not entirely clear.

3. In Theorem 2, the constant \mu is not well-defined. Is this the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? How does this result compare to the one in Theorem 1?

4. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is confusing. Do assumptions (A)-(C) imply monotonicity?

Minor issues include the inconsistent use of the constant L, which is referred to as both the condition number and the Lipschitz constant. Additionally, a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm should be included.
