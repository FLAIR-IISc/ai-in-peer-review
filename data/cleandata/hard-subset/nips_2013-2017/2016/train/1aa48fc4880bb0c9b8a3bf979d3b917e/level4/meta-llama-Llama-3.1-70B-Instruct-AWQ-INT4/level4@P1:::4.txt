This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to deal with strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, where linear convergence is proved. Both theoretical and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the variance reduction method on the saddle-point problems.

The paper provides a thorough extension of the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms can be applied to a number of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. A significant contribution of the paper is the convergence analysis on saddle-point problems, which is more complex than the previous convergence analysis on convex problems. The authors adopt monotone operators to prove the convergence, which is a novel approach. The analysis also shows that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms apply to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequality in game theory.

The paper considers a stochastic approach and proposes two different splits of the gradient operator, i.e., the element-wise split and factored split, for the SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The variance reduction works on the stochastic forward-backward algorithms, which are commonly used for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also considered, and both theoretical and empirical results show that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling is superior to that with uniform sampling.

Experimental results in both the main article and the appendix provide sufficient evidence of the theoretical results and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both non-separable loss and regularizer.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithms and their convergence analysis. The writing is clear, and the paper is easy to follow. The authors also provide a thorough review of the related work and clearly explain the contributions of the paper.

Some minor comments:

* In Section 5, the authors follow [8] to incorporate an additional regularization term to push the update similar to an iterate point. However, it is not clear how to adaptively update this iterate point, namely (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve speed-up.
* A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. It would be interesting to know if the same theoretical results exist for SAGA.
* The smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K should use different symbols to avoid confusion.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The proposed algorithms and their convergence analysis are novel and well-explained. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, and the paper is well-organized and easy to follow.
