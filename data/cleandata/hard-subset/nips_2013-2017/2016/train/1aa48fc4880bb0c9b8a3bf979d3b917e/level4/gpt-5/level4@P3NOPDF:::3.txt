This paper extends incremental variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants) to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure, considering both individual and factored splits. The authors conduct a careful complexity analysis under uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification task show that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are important to outperform competitive batch algorithms.

Strengths
- Novelty and technical depth: While extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems may appear straightforward, the results here are nontrivial and technically solid. The analysis is comprehensive and clearly distinguishes between individual and factored splits and between sampling strategies.
- Clarity of comparisons: For the important bilinear case, the paper presents clear complexity comparisons among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward (FB) methods. These results are consistent with known phenomena in convex minimization, lending credibility and intuition.
- Practical takeaways: The experimental observation that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are key to competitiveness is useful and well aligned with the theory.

Main concern
A central bottleneck appears to be the per-iteration cost of computing the proximal operator, which scales as O(n + d). This cost undermines the potential advantages of incrementalization: according to Tables 1 and 2, even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling does not always improve over the accelerated batch FB method. The issue is particularly acute when n ≫ d—a common regime in machine learning—where, under factored splits (the setting emphasized in the experiments), the complexity can be worse than that of batch FB (Table 2). Unlike the convex minimization setting, the theoretical benefits of variance reduction in this saddle-point framework are therefore not yet compelling.

Requests for clarification
- Are factored splits inherently worse than individual splits, from a theoretical standpoint? It would help to state conditions under which factored splits are provably suboptimal, or to identify problem classes where they are advantageous.
- Under what regimes is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB? Please characterize this in terms of n, d, condition numbers, smoothness/strong convexity–concavity constants, and any structural properties that reduce proximal costs.
- Are the dependencies on n and d tight? If the current upper bounds are loose, can you provide lower bounds or examples that suggest room for improvement? Conversely, if the bounds are tight, calling this out explicitly would clarify the fundamental limits.
- Proximal cost: Is the O(n + d) cost unavoidable under your assumptions? Are there problem structures (e.g., separable regularizers, low-rank factorizations, or maintainable sufficient statistics) that allow cheaper updates, or can the prox be approximated while retaining convergence guarantees?

Comments on experiments
- The binary classification example is helpful, but broader empirical validation would strengthen the case—especially in regimes with varying n/d ratios and for problems where the proximal operator admits faster implementations. Reporting wall-clock comparisons that isolate the prox computation cost would also clarify the practical impact of the O(n + d) bottleneck.
- Since non-uniform sampling appears crucial empirically, it would be useful to discuss its overhead, how sensitive performance is to sampling distribution misspecification, and whether adaptive schemes help.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances our understanding of incremental variance-reduced methods for saddle-point problems. The theoretical development is careful and the comparisons are clear. However, the practical and theoretical impact is tempered by the per-iteration proximal cost, which can negate the benefits of incrementalization in common regimes. A more explicit discussion of when variance reduction truly wins, the relative merits of individual versus factored splits, and the tightness of n and d dependencies would significantly strengthen the paper.
