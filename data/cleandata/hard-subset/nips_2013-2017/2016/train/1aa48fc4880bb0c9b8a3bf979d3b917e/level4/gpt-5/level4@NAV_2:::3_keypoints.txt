- Extends incremental variance-reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) and Catalyst acceleration to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with both individual and factored decompositions.  
- Provides careful complexity analysis for both decompositions and for uniform and nonuniform sampling schemes.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform strong batch baselines.  
- Although the algorithmic extension may appear straightforward, the results are new and the analysis is technically nontrivial.  
- For the bilinear saddle-point case, offers a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward methods, paralleling known convex minimization results; the narrative is consistent.  
- Main concern: evaluating the proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, which can be a bottleneck and negate complexity gains over accelerated batch methods.  
- In the common regime n ≫ d, complexity under factored splits (used in experiments) can be worse than batch forward–backward (as shown in Table 2).  
- Requests clarification on (i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG beats batch forward–backward, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependencies in the bounds.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but keeps original scores.
