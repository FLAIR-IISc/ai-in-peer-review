- Contribution: Extends SVRG/SAGA and Catalyst acceleration to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored splits, using monotone operator theory and proving linear rates.  
- Methods/analysis: Uses monotone operator framework (avoids co-coercivity), derives linear convergence, non-uniform sampling improvements, and proximal-point/Catalyst acceleration.  
- Experiments: Binary classification tests show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial; uniform SAGA can underperform batch baselines.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to SAG/SVRG/SAGA, primal–dual methods, and operator splitting, filling a gap where incremental linear-convergence methods were missing for saddle problems.  
- Strength — novelty: First linearly convergent incremental/variance-reduced methods tailored to convex–concave saddle points and sums of Lipschitz operators plus a strongly monotone operator.  
- Strength — analysis: Clean, general monotone-operator analysis that avoids co-coercivity assumptions and broadens applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Strength — decompositions & sampling: Careful treatment of individual vs factored splits and non-uniform sampling, with deep bilinear-case analysis and clear complexity expressions.  
- Strength — acceleration: Proximal-point/Catalyst acceleration is simpler than in minimization and empirically effective.  
- Strength — empirical support: Experiments substantiate theory: non-uniform sampling and acceleration materially improve performance.  
- Main concern — per-iteration cost: The O(n + d) cost of computing prox(M) in many models can negate theoretical advantages of incremental methods, especially when n ≫ d.  
- Main concern — factored splits: Factored splits (used in experiments) can lead to worse complexity than batch FB; needs explicit guidance when to prefer factored vs individual splits.  
- Main questions requested: (i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits and when? (ii) Under what regimes (in terms of ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max, λ, γ, prox vs Bi cost) does accelerated SVRG provably beat accelerated batch FB? (iii) Are n and d dependencies tight, or can gaps between norms be exploited or lower-bounded?  
- Additional comment — prox structure: Practical impact depends on M’s structure; when prox(M) is cheap, incremental methods shine; when prox is O(n + d), minibatching may be needed and guidance on optimal batch sizes is requested.  
- Additional comment — SAGA vs SVRG: Storage vs epoch trade-offs are discussed, but factored-split memory/update costs and resampling needs for non-uniform sampling should be clarified.  
- Additional comment — acceleration threshold: For bilinear problems, provide the threshold on τ (or ‖K‖F/√(λγ)) where acceleration outperforms non-accelerated incremental methods and accelerated batch FB.  
- Additional experiments suggested: Include cases with cheap separable/block-separable prox and highly ill-conditioned K to illustrate regimes where variance reduction helps most.  
- Clarity suggestion: Add a short table of constants (e.g., L vs L̄(π)) and their interpretations to aid readers.  
- Evaluation — Quality: Technically sound with careful proofs; per-iteration cost issue limits theoretical advantage in some regimes.  
- Evaluation — Clarity: Generally well written and organized; complexity expressions could use clearer regime guidance.  
- Evaluation — Originality: High — extends variance reduction to non-co-coercive monotone operator settings and adds a simple acceleration mechanism.  
- Evaluation — Significance: Potentially high for problems with suitable proximals, but mixed when prox is costly and n ≫ d.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel, rigorous extension with linear rates, useful sampling/decomposition guidance, effective acceleration, and solid experiments.  
- Cons against acceptance: In practical regimes (n ≫ d, costly prox), theoretical complexity can be no better or worse than accelerated batch FB; guidance on method choice is limited.  
- Post-rebuttal: Reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ rebuttal but keeps the same scores.
