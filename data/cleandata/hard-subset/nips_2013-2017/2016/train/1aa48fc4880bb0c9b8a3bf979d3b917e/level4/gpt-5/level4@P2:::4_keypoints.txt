- Extends SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems via monotone-operator forward–backward splitting, yielding first large-scale incremental algorithms with linear convergence for non-separable saddle objectives.  
- Analysis relies on monotonicity (not co-coercivity), broadening applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Studies two stochastic decompositions of B: element-wise splits and factored splits (e.g., row/column for bilinear forms).  
- Develops non-uniform sampling schemes based on component Lipschitz constants and proves they strictly improve theoretical rates over uniform sampling.  
- Introduces a proximal-point/catalyst acceleration wrapper that simplifies acceleration analysis in the saddle-point setting and further improves complexity.  
- Experiments on non-separable losses and regularizers show variance-reduced methods (with non-uniform sampling and acceleration) outperform batch primal–dual and uniform-sampling baselines.  
- Theoretical results: linear convergence proofs for SVRG/SAGA under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz gradients, with step sizes depending on L, component Lipschitzes, and strong convexity parameters.  
- Acceleration analysis is provided for SVRG; SAGA acceleration conditions are discussed but not proved.  
- Algorithms: stochastic forward–backward updates and variance-reduction estimators are natural extensions to the saddle setting; non-uniform SAGA requires resampling.  
- Practicality: complexity bounds include proximal and per-component oracle costs; factored splits greatly reduce SAGA storage in bilinear problems.  
- Notational issues: reuse of K for both the smooth function and design matrix causes confusion.  
- Practical guidance is scattered; a consolidated “practical recipe” for parameters (mini-batch size, probabilities, τ, epoch lengths) is needed.  
- The acceleration anchor (x̄, ȳ) role and update are only briefly discussed and should be explained in the main text.  
- Original contributions include first linear-rate incremental methods for non-separable saddle points, monotone-operator analysis, factored decompositions, tailored non-uniform sampling, and proximal-point acceleration for saddle points.  
- Significance: fills an important gap by providing scalable, linearly convergent algorithms for structured losses/regularizers and variational inequalities, with likely theoretical and practical impact.  
- Pros: rigorous linear rates, broad monotone-operator perspective, two complementary splitting schemes, motivated non-uniform sampling, simple effective acceleration, and solid experiments.  
- Cons: acceleration proof limited to SVRG; practical computation/estimation of L̄ and sampling probabilities not fully specified; anchor update and adaptive strategies under-described.  
- Additional cons: SAGA with individual splits can require large storage; empirical evaluation is limited in dataset breadth and baseline comparisons; notational overload persists.  
- Key suggestions: provide practical recipes for probability and L̄ estimation, detail adaptive anchor-update rules and robustness to τ, clarify if SAGA acceleration can be proved, address sensitivity to unknown strong convexity–concavity constants, and discuss memory/runtime trade-offs and comparisons to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution that advances variance reduction for saddle-point problems and merits acceptance after minor clarifications on acceleration, parameter selection, and notation.
