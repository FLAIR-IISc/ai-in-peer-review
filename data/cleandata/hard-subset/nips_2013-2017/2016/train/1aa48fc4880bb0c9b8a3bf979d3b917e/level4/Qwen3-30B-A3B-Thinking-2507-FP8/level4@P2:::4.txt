

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale problems with non-separable loss functions and regularizers. The authors demonstrate that these algorithms can be applied to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities, by leveraging monotone operator theory rather than standard convex optimization analysis. This represents a significant theoretical contribution, as the convergence analysis for saddle-point problems differs fundamentally from convex minimization due to the need to handle both minimization and maximization simultaneously.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it establishes a novel convergence analysis for saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory, showing that the same algorithms apply to variational inequalities beyond traditional saddle-point problems. Second, it introduces two distinct gradient operator splits (element-wise and factored) that are particularly suited to saddle-point structures, demonstrating that non-uniform sampling significantly improves performance both theoretically and empirically. Third, it shows how the catalyst framework can be adapted to accelerate these algorithms, achieving better complexity bounds than existing batch methods.

The paper excels in quality, providing rigorous theoretical analysis with well-structured proofs in the appendix. The authors carefully address the challenges of extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems, particularly the need for two different types of splits (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) and the importance of non-uniform sampling. The theoretical analysis is sound and properly contextualized within existing work on monotone operators and variational inequalities.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and accessible to researchers in optimization and machine learning. The introduction clearly establishes the problem's significance, and the technical sections flow logically from problem formulation to algorithms to theoretical guarantees. The authors effectively use examples to illustrate their approach, particularly in Section 2.1 where they demonstrate how their methods apply to non-separable losses in structured output prediction and robust optimization.

The originality is substantial, as the paper addresses a gap in the literature where previous work on stochastic variance reduction focused exclusively on convex minimization problems. By extending these methods to saddle-point problems, the authors open up new possibilities for large-scale optimization in machine learning, particularly for problems where the loss function or regularizer is non-separable. The paper also makes a clear connection to related work at NIPS (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 11, 12]) while demonstrating how their approach differs from and improves upon these prior methods.

The significance of this work is evident in both its theoretical contributions and practical applications. The paper demonstrates that the proposed algorithms outperform existing methods on real-world machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers. The experimental results (Section 7) provide strong empirical validation, showing that non-uniform sampling and acceleration lead to significant improvements over both batch methods and uniform sampling variants. The authors also demonstrate the applicability of their methods to important machine learning problems like structured output prediction and robust optimization.

The paper is well-suited to NIPS as it makes a clear scientific contribution to optimization methods with broad applicability in machine learning. The authors have carefully considered the connections to previous NIPS work, particularly in the references to SVRG, SAGA, and related approaches.

The main weaknesses are minor. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how to adaptively update the iterate point (\bar{x}, \bar{y}) in the acceleration framework (Section 5), which is currently left as a future work direction. While the authors prove convergence for SVRG, they do not explicitly address whether the same theoretical guarantees hold for SAGA (though this is likely true based on their analysis of the two algorithms). Additionally, the paper uses inconsistent notation for the smooth function K and the design matrix K, which could confuse readers.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature with clear implications for machine learning applications. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper addresses an important gap in the literature. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions.

Strengths:
- First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems with non-separable losses/regularizers
- Novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory
- Demonstrated applicability to variational inequalities and game theory
- Clear theoretical and empirical evidence of superiority over existing methods
- Well-structured paper with effective examples

Weaknesses:
- Inconsistent notation for K (smooth function vs. design matrix)
- Limited discussion of adaptive updates for (\bar{x}, \bar{y})
- No explicit proof that SAGA shares the same convergence guarantees as SVRG

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to optimization methods with clear implications for machine learning, addressing an important gap in the literature with both theoretical and empirical rigor.
