The authors propose the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems by leveraging variance reduction algorithms such as SVRG and SAGA. A key contribution of the paper lies in its use of monotone operators to establish convergence, as traditional convex minimization analysis does not apply to saddle point problems. This approach enables the analysis to be extended to a broader class of problems, including variational inequality problems. The method can be employed to tackle non-separable loss functions and regularizers by formulating the problem as a saddle point problem. Notably, the paper presents the first algorithms to achieve a linear convergence rate for saddle point problems, which is a significant advancement in addressing non-separable loss functions and regularizers commonly encountered in various machine learning models.

The proof technique, which involves interpreting saddle point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator, is intriguing and potentially widely applicable. Additionally, the authors provide simpler proofs for algorithms such as FB splitting and its stochastic version. The proposed algorithms incorporating non-uniform sampling are competitive and, in most cases, outperform previous alternatives.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by extending the analysis approach in an important direction. However, since the primary contribution lies in the proof technique (while the algorithms themselves are relatively simple extensions of existing algorithms), the authors may consider providing more details about the proof technique, such as including a proof sketch, in the main paper if space permits.

The paper is generally well-presented and clearly written. Nevertheless, there are certain aspects that require clarification. Specifically:

1. The authors should specify the type of non-uniform sampling used for problems other than bilinear saddle-point problems, i.e., what is \pi?
2. The authors should explain how the step size \sigma is set in the experiments and provide intuition on why non-uniform sampling appears to be crucial for saddle point problems, but not necessarily for convex minimization problems. A discussion on this issue would be beneficial.

Minor comments:

1. There is a typo on line 78: k -> d.
2. There is a typo on line 121 in the second equality (extra y_{t-1} term).

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field, and with some minor revisions to address the aforementioned points, it has the potential to make a substantial impact.
