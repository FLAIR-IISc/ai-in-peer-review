- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Leverages monotone operator theory to prove convergence, broadening applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Addresses non-separable objective functions common in modern machine learning.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficient performance, both theoretically and empirically.  
- Demonstrates acceleration via a catalyst framework, outperforming accelerated batch methods.  
- Provides simplified proofs for foundational algorithms like forward-backward splitting.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and offers valuable insights into algorithm behavior.  
- Experimental results validate effectiveness, especially when using non-uniform sampling.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, making core ideas accessible.  
- Weakness: proof technique details and connection between monotone operator analysis and convergence need clearer exposition.  
- Weakness: the form and determination of non-uniform sampling probabilities (π) beyond bilinear cases is not fully explained.  
- Weakness: step-size (σ) selection in experiments is not clearly articulated.  
- Weakness: lacking deeper intuition on why non-uniform sampling is more critical for saddle-point problems than for convex minimization.  
- For acceptance: tackles a significant optimization challenge with novel theory, practical algorithms, and supportive experiments.  
- Against acceptance: missing details on proofs and sampling strategy may hinder reproducibility and leave key questions unanswered.  
- Minor comments: Line 78 likely should read “d” instead of “k”; Line 121 contains an extra “y_{t-1}” term.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with significant novelty and practical relevance; recommend acceptance pending clarifications.
