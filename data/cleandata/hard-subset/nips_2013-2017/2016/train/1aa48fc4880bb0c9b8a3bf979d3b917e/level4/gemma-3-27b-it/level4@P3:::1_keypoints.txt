- Addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction (SVRG/SAGA), providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale instances.  
- Frames the problem in monotone operator theory, extending applicability to variational inequalities and beyond traditional saddle-point cases.  
- Introduces acceleration via a “catalyst” framework and highlights benefits of non-uniform sampling.  
- Builds on SVRG/SAGA to handle a non-trivial extension of separable objective functions relevant to many ML problems.  
- Offers rigorous theoretical analysis with well-established convergence results.  
- Empirical evaluation (two datasets) supports theory but is limited in scope.  
- Presentation is dense and sometimes hard to follow; motivation for the monotone operator transition is abrupt.  
- Need clearer explanation of how the extension applies to non-separable functions and its practical implications.  
- Experimental section should include more datasets and comparisons to related methods (e.g., stochastic Chambolle–Pock).  
- Point 1: Clarify restrictiveness of Assumptions A–C, especially strong convex-concavity (A), and applicability to standard SVMs.  
- Point 2: Clarify relationship to existing stochastic primal–dual approaches and state key distinctions/advantages.  
- Point 3: Precisely define μ in Theorem 2, link it to the monotonicity constant, and compare convergence rates of Theorems 1 and 2.  
- Point 4: Explicitly state whether (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity or if an additional assumption is required.  
- Point 5: Ensure consistent notation for 'L' and clearly distinguish condition number vs. Lipschitz constant.  
- Minor issues: occasional awkward phrasing and a missing reference for the Forward–Backward algorithm (e.g., [20]).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty (extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle points), theoretical rigor, generality via monotone operator connection, and practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity issues, limited experimental validation, and insufficient comparisons to related work.  
- Overall: Strong paper with significant theoretical contributions; recommend acceptance provided authors address clarity, exposition, and experimental/comparison concerns.
