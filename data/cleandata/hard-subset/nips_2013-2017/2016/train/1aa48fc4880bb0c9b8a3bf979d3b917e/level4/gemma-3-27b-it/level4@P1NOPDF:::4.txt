This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, proving linear convergence. Both theoretical and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of these variance reduction methods when applied to saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a variety of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., in structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., a fusion term) can be non-separable. A key contribution is that, unlike previous convergence analyses focused on convex problems, analyzing saddle-point problems is significantly more complex. The authors cleverly employ monotone operator theory to establish convergence. The analysis also reveals that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities arising in game theory.

To handle the stochastic nature of the problem, the authors propose two different gradient operator splits – an element-wise split and a factored split – for SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The variance reduction techniques are applied to stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for solving saddle-point problems. The authors also consider non-uniform sampling and show, both theoretically and empirically, that variance reduction performs better with non-uniform sampling than with uniform sampling.

The experimental results, presented in the main article and the appendix, provide strong support for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems with both non-separable losses and regularizers. 

I have a few minor comments: Regarding the acceleration technique described in Section 5, the authors follow [8] in incorporating an additional regularization term to guide the update towards a previous iterate. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate, denoted as $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$, to achieve the observed speed-up. A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. Will the same theoretical results hold for SAGA? Finally, to avoid potential confusion, the smooth function $K(\cdot)$ and the design matrix $K$ should be represented using different symbols.
