This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to address saddle-point problems, yielding the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. These problems are prevalent in machine learning, particularly when dealing with non-separable objective functions. The core contribution lies in leveraging the theory of monotone operators to establish convergence, a technique that broadens the applicability of the algorithms beyond traditional saddle-point settings to encompass variational inequalities. The authors demonstrate that careful consideration of sampling strategies – specifically, non-uniform sampling – is crucial for efficient performance, both theoretically and empirically. Furthermore, they showcase how to accelerate these incremental algorithms using a catalyst framework, achieving superior results compared to accelerated batch methods.

The work addresses a significant challenge in optimization, extending successful techniques from convex minimization to the more complex realm of saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms offer a valuable tool for tackling non-separable losses and regularizers frequently encountered in modern machine learning applications. The theoretical analysis, framing saddle-point problems through the lens of monotone operators, is insightful and potentially offers a broadly applicable approach. The inclusion of simplified proofs for foundational algorithms like the forward-backward splitting method adds to the paper's utility. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly with non-uniform sampling, positioning them favorably against existing alternatives. Overall, this paper represents a strong contribution to the field, offering both theoretical advancements and practical algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems, combined with the monotone operator analysis, is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The focus on non-separable problems addresses a common challenge in machine learning.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the efficacy of the algorithms, particularly the importance of non-uniform sampling.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proof Details:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper might benefit from a more detailed sketch of the proof technique, especially the connection between the monotone operator analysis and the algorithm's convergence, potentially within the main body or appendix.
*   **Sampling Strategy Clarification:** The specific form of the non-uniform sampling probability distribution (π) for problems *beyond* bilinear saddle-point problems is not fully explained. Providing more detail on how π is determined in these cases would enhance clarity.
*   **Step Size Selection:** The method for setting the step size (σ) in the experiments is not clearly articulated. Further explanation would be helpful.
*   **Intuition for Non-Uniform Sampling:** A deeper discussion regarding the reasons why non-uniform sampling appears so critical for saddle-point problems, but less so for convex minimization, would strengthen the analysis. Why does the benefit of non-uniform sampling appear to be more pronounced in the saddle-point setting?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in optimization with direct relevance to machine learning.
*   The theoretical contributions are substantial and offer a novel perspective on solving saddle-point problems.
*   The experimental results validate the proposed algorithms and demonstrate their practical benefits.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detail regarding the proof technique and sampling strategy could hinder reproducibility and understanding.
*   The absence of a deeper discussion on the role of non-uniform sampling leaves some questions unanswered.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 78: “k” should likely be “d”.
*   Line 121: The second equality appears to have an extra “y_{t-1}” term.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of optimization. While some clarifications would enhance its impact, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and practical relevance of the work clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
