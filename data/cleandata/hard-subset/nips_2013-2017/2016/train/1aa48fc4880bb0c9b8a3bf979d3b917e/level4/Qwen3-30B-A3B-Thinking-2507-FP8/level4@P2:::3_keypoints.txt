- Extends SVRG, SAGA and accelerated variants to convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, differing from standard convex minimization arguments.  
- Shows algorithms generalize beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.  
- Main contributions: convergence analysis via monotone operators, two split strategies (function-based and derivative-based) with non-uniform sampling, and Catalyst-style acceleration.  
- Provides rigorous convergence proofs and detailed complexity analysis with linear rates for SVRG and SAGA variants.  
- Properly handles saddle-point structure requiring convex-concave splits rather than convex splits used in minimization.  
- Demonstrates experimentally that non-uniform sampling and acceleration improve performance, especially for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Experiments focus on binary classification with non-separable losses and regularizers, validating theoretical claims.  
- Paper fills a gap by adapting variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, addressing non-trivial theoretical challenges.  
- Theoretical results are valuable and extendable to variational inequalities, broadening applicability.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce results.  
- Some technical notation could be made more accessible; factored vs individual splits are well-explained.  
- Practical significance claimed for non-separable ML problems, arguing these methods are often the only large-scale linearly convergent option.  
- Concern: theoretical advantage in practice is unclear because factored-split proximal cost is O(n + d) per iteration, which can be dominated by n when n >> d.  
- Table 2 indicates accelerated SVRG with factored splits can be worse than accelerated batch methods in many realistic n >> d scenarios.  
- Requests clarification: deeper comparison of factored vs individual splits, conditions (e.g., n/d ratios) where methods are advantageous, and whether n and d dependence in bounds is tight or reducible.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and novel, but needs clearer explanation of practical regimes where it outperforms batch methods.  
- Pros: novel extension to saddle-points, rigorous monotone-operator analysis, strong experimental validation, relevant applications.  
- Cons: unclear practical advantage over batch methods for common ML settings, complexity may not favor proposed methods, potential proximal computation bottleneck.
