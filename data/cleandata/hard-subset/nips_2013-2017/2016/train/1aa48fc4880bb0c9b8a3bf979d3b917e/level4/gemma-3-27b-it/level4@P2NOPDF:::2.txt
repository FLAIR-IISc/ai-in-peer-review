This paper addresses the problem of minimizing convex-concave saddle point problems and extends recent variance reduction techniques to this setting. The authors provide a convergence analysis for the proposed methods and demonstrate their compatibility with, and acceleration via, the catalyst framework. They also present extensions to the case of monotone operators, supported by numerical results.

My primary concern lies with the strength of the convexity assumptions. The requirement of *strong* convexity for *both* the primal and dual problems appears overly restrictive. Typically, strong convexity is only needed for either the primal *or* the dual problem to guarantee convergence and desirable properties. Relaxing this assumption would significantly broaden the applicability of the proposed methods. I recommend the authors carefully reconsider this condition and justify its necessity, or explore a version of the algorithm under weaker assumptions.

Beyond the convexity assumption, the numerical evaluation, while present, feels somewhat limited. While the results demonstrate the method's functionality, additional experiments comparing the performance against relevant baselines (e.g., existing saddle-point solvers, other variance reduction methods tailored for this problem) would substantially strengthen the paper.  Specifically, I’d like to see comparisons on a wider range of problem sizes and structures – perhaps including problems arising in specific applications like distributed optimization or game theory where saddle-point problems are common. Providing a more comprehensive empirical analysis would convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

The paper is clearly written and the technical contributions – the extension of variance reduction to the convex-concave setting, the convergence analysis, and the catalyst acceleration – are valuable. However, addressing the concerns regarding the strong convexity assumptions and expanding the numerical results are crucial for increasing the impact and overall strength of this submission.
