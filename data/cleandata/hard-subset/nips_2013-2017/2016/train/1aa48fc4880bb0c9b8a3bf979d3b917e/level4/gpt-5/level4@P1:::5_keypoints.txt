- Extends SVRG/SAGA to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale algorithms with linear convergence for this class.
- Key technical innovation: analyze variance-reduced methods via monotone operators instead of convex minimization, enabling broader convergence guarantees (including variational inequalities).
- Introduces two notions of splitting the smooth component K (by functions or by partial derivatives/factored splits) and emphasizes splitting into convex–concave terms.
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial in both theory and practice, and provides concrete sampling schemes and complexity bounds for bilinear problems.
- Provides a catalyst-style proximal regularization acceleration that improves over accelerated batch methods in theory and experiments.
- Gives simplified analyses of forward–backward (FB) and its accelerated/stochastic variants under the monotone operator view.
- Strength: first linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for non-separable losses/regularizers in saddle-point settings.
- Strength: monotone-operator analysis is conceptually clean, broadly applicable, and yields simpler proofs.
- Strength: careful treatment of different splits and scaling parameters (λ, γ).
- Strength: experiments on non-separable tasks show clear gains for (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling; comparisons to primal methods are informative.
- Weakness: algorithms are straightforward extensions; main novelty lies in the analysis—authors should include a proof sketch for accessibility.
- Weakness: unclear how to choose non-uniform sampling π, and how to estimate L̄(π) and Li for general (non-bilinear) K in practice.
- Weakness: experimental details missing—how stepsize σ, and parameters λ, γ were chosen or tuned.
- Weakness: intuition for why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle points (vs convex minimization) is lacking.
- Weakness: theoretical SAGA requires resampling for non-uniform sampling, but experiments use a practical variant without resampling—gap between theory and practice needs discussion.
- Weakness: storage vs compute trade-offs (e.g., O(nd) memory for individual splits) are not discussed.
- Weakness: methods rely on known strong convexity–concavity; more discussion on robustness to misspecification or adaptive schemes is needed.
- Quality: technically solid analyses, complexity bounds, and acceleration scheme; experiments support main claims.
- Clarity: generally well written; would benefit from a concise proof sketch, clearer sampling recipe for non-bilinear cases, and explicit stepsize choices in experiments.
- Originality: high on the analysis side—novel application of monotone operators to get linear rates for saddle points.
- Significance: significant for ML problems with non-separable losses/regularizers; monotone-operator perspective likely influential.
- Key questions for authors: how to choose/estimate π, L̄(π), Li for general K; how σ was set/tuned; intuition for non-uniform sampling’s importance; robustness to mis-specified λ/γ and on-the-fly estimation; empirical effect of skipping SAGA resampling.
- Pros for acceptance: first linear-rate variance-reduced methods for broad saddle-point class, clean analysis, practical sampling guidance, strong empirical results, effective acceleration.
- Cons for acceptance: novelty mainly analytical, missing practical guidance (sampling/stepsize), theory–practice gaps (resampling), dependence on known strong convexity–concavity.
- Minor comments: typos noted at Line 78 (“k” → “d”) and Line 121 (extra y_{t−1} term).
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
