Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
Introduces a novel theoretical framework using monotone operators to interpret saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators.  
Shows the analysis and results extend beyond saddle points to variational inequalities, broadening applicability.  
Establishes the first convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in saddle-point settings.  
Introduces two gradient operator splits: element-wise for SVRG and factored for SAGA, leveraging saddle-point structure.  
Demonstrates non-uniform sampling is theoretically and empirically superior to uniform sampling for these problems.  
Accelerates algorithms via a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework, yielding methods that consistently beat accelerated batch algorithms.  
Provides rigorous convergence proofs and comprehensive empirical validation on synthetic and real-world problems (including structured output and fusion terms).  
Presentation is clear and well organized, making complex material accessible to readers familiar with optimization.  
Originality is substantial; adapts variance reduction methods to a new problem class with practical impact for ML.  
Unclear description of the adaptive update of the iterate point (x̄, ȳ) in the acceleration framework—needs clarification for optimal updates.  
Does not explicitly show whether identical acceleration/convergence results hold for SAGA, weakening the theoretical completeness.  
Notation is confusing: the smooth function K(·) and the design matrix K use the same symbol, risking reader confusion.  
Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address notation and clarify the adaptive update procedure.
