The paper "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" presents a significant extension of recent stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under different splits (individual or factored) and sampling strategies (uniform or non-uniform). The experimental results on a binary classification example demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration in achieving better performance than competitive batch algorithms.

The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem, the proposed algorithms, and their analysis. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of the proposed algorithms with existing batch and stochastic algorithms, highlighting their advantages and limitations. The extension of the algorithms to monotone operators is also a valuable contribution, as it broadens the applicability of the methods.

However, I have some concerns regarding the overall complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly in the context of machine learning applications where the number of data points (n) is often substantially larger than the dimension (d). As shown in Table 1 and 2, the complexity of the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling under factored splits is actually worse than the batch FB algorithms. This raises questions about the theoretical advantages of using variance reduction in this context.

To strengthen the paper, I would suggest that the authors provide an in-depth discussion on the following points:

1. Is factored splits (theoretically) worse than individual splits? Under what conditions would one be preferred over the other?
2. When is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? Are there specific scenarios or problem settings where the proposed algorithms offer a clear advantage?
3. Is the dependence on n, d, tight enough? Are there opportunities to improve the complexity bounds or to develop more efficient algorithms that can better exploit the structure of the problem?

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning, and with some additional discussion and analysis, it has the potential to become an even more impactful work.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel extension of SVRG and SAGA to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the complexity of the proposed algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies.
* The experimental results demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration in achieving better performance.

Weaknesses:

* The overall complexity of the proposed algorithms may not be better than the accelerated batch version, particularly in the context of machine learning applications where n >> d.
* The dependence on n, d, may not be tight enough, and there may be opportunities to improve the complexity bounds.

Acceptance: Yes, but with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
