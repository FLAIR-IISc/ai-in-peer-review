This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems by adapting stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) and SAGA methods. The proposed algorithms incorporate a proximal operator and are analyzed within the framework of monotone operators, for which the authors establish linear convergence rates. The paper further extends this core contribution by introducing an accelerated variant and analyzing a non-uniform sampling strategy.

**Novelty and Significance**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its significant theoretical contributions. The analysis is thorough, providing novel insights into solving saddle-point problems using variance-reduced stochastic methods. A particularly notable contribution is the extension of this framework to handle non-separable objective functions, which broadens the applicability of the proposed methods. The development of an accelerated variant and the analysis of non-uniform sampling are also valuable additions.

**Technical Quality and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the theoretical results are strong, the paper's presentation and experimental evaluation could be significantly improved.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's accessibility is hindered by its structure. The core analysis relies heavily on the monotone operator framework, yet this connection is only formally introduced as an extension in Section 6. Introducing this concept earlier and using it to frame the main algorithm and its analysis would make the paper's structure more logical and easier for readers to follow.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is currently limited. To demonstrate the practical effectiveness and robustness of the proposed methods, experiments on a wider variety of datasets are needed. Furthermore, the empirical comparison is incomplete. For the case of separable functions, the authors should include a comparison against other relevant accelerated methods, such as the stochastic primal-dual algorithm proposed in the following paper, which is a strong baseline for this problem class:

    *   Zhang, Y. and Xiao, L. (2015). Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization. In *Proceedings of the 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML-15)*, pages 353–361.

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their revision:

1.  Could the authors comment on the strength of Assumptions (A)-(C)? In particular, how restrictive is Assumption (A)? It would be helpful to discuss whether these assumptions hold for common machine learning problems, such as the saddle-point formulation of a Support Vector Machine (SVM).

2.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the differences between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual methods. What are the key theoretical or practical advantages of this approach?

3.  In Theorem 2, the definition of the constant `μ` is unclear. Is this the strong monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? Additionally, please clarify how the convergence rate in Theorem 2 compares to the rate established in Theorem 1.

4.  The relationship between Assumptions (A)-(C) in the main text and the strong monotonicity property used in the appendix is not clearly established. Do Assumptions (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity? A more explicit explanation of this connection would be valuable.

**Minor Issues**

*   There appears to be an inconsistent use of the constant `L`, which is referred to as both the condition number and a Lipschitz constant. Please clarify the notation for consistency.
*   The Forward-Backward algorithm should be accompanied by a proper citation.
