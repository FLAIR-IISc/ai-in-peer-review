- Paper extends incremental variance-reduced algorithms (SVRG, SAGA) and Catalyst acceleration to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems.  
- Considers two data splits: individual and factored, and analyzes algorithmic complexity under both.  
- Studies both uniform and nonuniform sampling strategies and their impact on complexity.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch algorithms.  
- Despite seeming straightforward, the theoretical results are novel and technically non-trivial.  
- Provides a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms for bilinear saddle point problems.  
- Major concern: each iteration requires an O(n + d) proximal computation, creating a fundamental bottleneck.  
- Because of that cost, accelerated incremental methods may not improve over accelerated batch FB, especially when n >> d.  
- Under factored splits (used in simulations) complexity can be worse than batch FB, questioning practical benefit of variance reduction.  
- Requests clarification on: (i) whether factored splitting is theoretically worse than individual splitting, (ii) when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) whether the n and d dependence is tight.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors' response but kept scores unchanged.
