- Extends SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, yielding the first large-scale algorithms with linear convergence.  
- Applies variance reduction to a forward–backward splitting algorithm tailored for saddle-point optimization.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for convergence analysis, enabling proofs beyond standard convex-minimization techniques.  
- Shows applicability to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.  
- Introduces two gradient-operator splits ("factored" and "element-wise") and emphasizes non-uniform sampling for efficiency gains.  
- Proposes an acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework to improve practical performance.  
- Originality/significance: fills a gap by extending variance-reduction methods to non-separable saddle-point problems relevant to ML (e.g., structured outputs, complex regularizers).  
- Technical quality: rigorous, elegant analysis using monotone operators that correctly handles non-co-coercive operators in saddle-point settings.  
- Comprehensive treatment: detailed complexity analysis and comparison showing theoretical advantages over batch and standard stochastic methods.  
- Empirical validation: experiments demonstrate substantial performance gains, especially with acceleration and non-uniform sampling, and clarify trade-offs with primal methods.  
- Weakness — adaptive acceleration: paper lacks specifics on how to implement more aggressive updates of the reference point for acceleration.  
- Weakness — accelerated SAGA: claims accelerated SAGA should work but omits a formal convergence proof or detailed justification.  
- Weakness — notation: symbol K used for both a function and a data matrix, risking reader confusion.
