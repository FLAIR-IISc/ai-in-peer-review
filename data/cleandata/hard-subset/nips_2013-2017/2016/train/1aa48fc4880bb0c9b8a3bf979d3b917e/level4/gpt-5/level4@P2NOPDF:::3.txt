The paper extends recent incremental variance-reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure, arising from two types of decompositions: individual and factored splits. The authors provide a careful complexity analysis across these splits and under different sampling strategies (uniform vs. nonuniform). Experiments on a specialized binary classification task indicate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform strong batch baselines.

Although extending variance-reduced incremental methods to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, the results here are genuinely novel and technically nontrivial. For the important bilinear saddle-point case, the paper presents a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward (FB) algorithms, echoing known patterns from convex minimization; the conclusions appear sound.

My primary concern is a potential computational bottleneck: the proposed incremental methods seem to require O(n + d) work to compute the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling, as summarized in Tables 1 and 2—is not necessarily better than that of accelerated batch methods. This issue is particularly acute when the number of samples n greatly exceeds the dimension d, which is common in machine learning. Under factored splits (also used in the experiments), the resulting complexity can be worse than that of batch FB algorithms (Table 2). Unlike the convex minimization setting, I do not see a clear theoretical advantage for variance reduction in this context.

I encourage the authors to discuss this issue in depth and to clarify:
i) Is the factored split theoretically worse than the individual split?
ii) Under what regimes is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB?
iii) Are the dependencies on n and d in the bounds tight?
