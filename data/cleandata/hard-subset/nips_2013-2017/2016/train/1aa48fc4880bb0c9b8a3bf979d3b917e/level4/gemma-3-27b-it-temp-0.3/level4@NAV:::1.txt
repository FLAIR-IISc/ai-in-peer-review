This paper investigates saddle-point problems exhibiting a convex-concave structure, and demonstrates how existing stochastic variance reduction algorithms (SVRG, SAGA) can be effectively applied in conjunction with a proximal operator to solve them. The authors provide a convergence analysis within the framework of monotone operators, establishing linear convergence rates. Furthermore, they introduce an accelerated algorithm and analyze a non-uniform sampling strategy. 

**Novelty/Originality:** The work presents substantial contributions, with a rigorous theoretical analysis yielding new insights into saddle-point problem solutions. The authors explore several extensions, notably extending the method’s applicability to non-separable functions.

**Technical Quality:** While the theoretical results are strong, the presentation could be improved for clarity. Specifically, a more explicit connection to the underlying monotone operator framework would be beneficial, as the analysis heavily relies on this foundation despite its later introduction in Section 6.  The experimental evaluation would be strengthened by including results on a broader range of datasets. For separable functions, a comparison to accelerated primal-dual methods, such as the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm [Zhang & Peyré, 2015], would be valuable.  (http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf)

I request the authors address the following points:

1.  Please discuss the strength of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A). It would be helpful to understand if these assumptions hold for common applications like Support Vector Machines.
2.  Could the authors clarify the distinctions between their proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual approaches?
3.  In Theorem 2, the constant μ requires further explanation. Is this the monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? How does this result compare to the convergence rate presented in Theorem 1?
4.  The transition from assumptions A-C to strong monotonicity in the appendix is unclear. Do assumptions A-C guarantee monotonicity?

**Minor Issues:**

*   There is some ambiguity in the use of the constant *L*, which is sometimes used to denote the condition number and other times the Lipschitz constant.
*   Please include a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
