- Extends SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems using monotone operator framework — novel and important.  
- Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis and shows linear convergence for incremental variance-reduced methods on saddle-point/variational inequality problems.  
- Analyzes two splitting strategies (individual and factored) and both uniform and non-uniform sampling complexities.  
- Integrates Catalyst acceleration and demonstrates accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling performs well in experiments on non-separable loss/regularizer classification.  
- Technically sound and rigorous convergence proofs; paper is well-written and organized with helpful complexity tables.  
- Original: first work to establish linear convergence for incremental variance-reduction on general finite-sum saddle-point problems, using different analysis tools than convex minimization.  
- Potentially high significance: enables scalable solvers for non-separable models used in structured prediction, robust optimization, and complex regularizers.  
- Practical insight: non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to achieve state-of-the-art performance.  
- Major concern: overall computational advantage over accelerated batch methods is unclear due to potentially costly proximal computations (O(n + d) per iteration) that may dominate gains.  
- Factored split (used in experiments) may introduce worse dependence on n and d, making methods potentially inferior when n >> d.  
- Question 1: Need clarification whether factored vs individual splits lead to different theoretical n and d dependencies and if individual split is superior when n >> d despite higher memory.  
- Question 2: Request explicit delineation of regimes (n, d, condition number L, cost of T(B) vs T(Bi)) where accelerated SVRG outperforms accelerated batch Forward–Backward.  
- Question 3: Ask how tight the derived complexity bounds on n and d are, since comparisons with batch methods hinge critically on these terms.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and technically rigorous work, but practical impact is clouded by theoretical concerns for common ML settings (especially n >> d).  
- Acceptance arguments: first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for finite-sum saddle-point problems; strong monotone-operator analysis; thorough comparisons; addresses important problem class.  
- Rejection/improvement arguments: theoretical advantage over accelerated batch not convincingly established for typical settings; O(n+d) proximal cost may fundamentally limit stochastic gains.
