

The authors extend stochastic variance reduction methods such as SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems that are common in machine learning. While the algorithmic extension is straightforward, the primary contribution lies in using monotone operators to establish convergence, as standard convex minimization analysis does not apply to saddle-point problems. This approach demonstrates that the algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to a broader class, including variational inequalities. The method effectively addresses non-separable loss functions and regularizers by formulating them as saddle-point problems, which is significant as these occur in numerous machine learning models. The proof technique of interpreting saddle-point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators is novel and potentially widely applicable, offering a more general theoretical framework. The paper also provides simplified proofs for algorithms like the forward-backward splitting (and its stochastic variant), which is valuable for the community. The proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling demonstrate competitive performance, consistently outperforming existing alternatives in most cases. Overall, this work represents an important advancement in the field by extending variance reduction methods to a new problem class with a theoretically sound and practically effective approach.

While the main contribution is in the theoretical framework rather than the algorithm design itself, the authors could consider including a more detailed proof sketch in the main paper to better convey the intricacies of their monotone operator approach, should space permit. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, though some aspects require clarification: 
1. For non-bilinear saddle-point problems, it's unclear how the sampling probabilities π are determined beyond the specific bilinear case discussed.
2. The step size σ used in experiments is not explicitly specified, and the authors might provide more intuition on why non-uniform sampling proves essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization settings.
