This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to tackle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, achieving linear convergence. The theoretical and empirical results presented demonstrate the effectiveness of the variance reduction method in addressing these problems.

The authors extend the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to handle strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms can be applied to a variety of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion term) can be non-separable. A significant contribution of this work is the complexity of the convergence analysis for saddle-point problems, which differs from previous analyses on convex problems. The authors utilize monotone operators to prove convergence, and their analysis shows that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms can be applied to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory.

Considering a stochastic approach, the authors propose two distinct splits of the gradient operator: the element-wise split for SVRG and the factored split for SAGA. Variance reduction is applied to stochastic forward-backward algorithms, commonly used for saddle-point problems. Non-uniform sampling is also explored, and both theoretical and empirical results demonstrate that variance reduction with non-uniform sampling outperforms that with uniform sampling.

The experimental results presented in the main article and the appendix provide sufficient evidence for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both non-separable loss and regularizer. However, a few minor comments are worth noting. In Section 5, the authors follow [8] and incorporate an additional regularization term to push the update towards an iterate point. However, it is unclear how to adaptively update this iterate point, namely (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), to achieve acceleration. A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, but it is uncertain whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA. Furthermore, to avoid confusion, it would be beneficial to use different symbols for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of stochastic variance reduction techniques, providing a comprehensive analysis and experimental evaluation of the proposed methods.
