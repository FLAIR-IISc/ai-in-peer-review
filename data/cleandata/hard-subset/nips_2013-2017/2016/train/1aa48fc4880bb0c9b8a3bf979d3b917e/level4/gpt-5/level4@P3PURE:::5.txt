This paper proposes the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems by adapting variance-reduction methods such as SVRG and SAGA. Because standard convex minimization analyses do not apply to saddle-point settings, the main technical contribution is a convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory. As a consequence, the results extend to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The approach also provides a way to handle non-separable loss functions and regularizers by formulating them as saddle-point problems.

Strengths
- Novelty and significance: The paper presents the first algorithms with linear convergence rates for saddle-point problems, which is important for addressing non-separable losses and regularizers common in many machine learning models.
- Analysis technique: Interpreting saddle-point problems as root-finding for monotone operators is interesting and appears widely applicable. The paper also gives simpler proofs for algorithms such as forward-backward splitting and its stochastic counterpart.
- Empirical performance: The proposed methods with non-uniform sampling are competitive and, in most cases, outperform prior alternatives.
- Scope: The analysis extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally clearly written and well-presented.
- Since the core contribution lies in the proof technique (the algorithms themselves are relatively straightforward extensions of existing variance-reduction methods), it would strengthen the paper to include more details of the analysis in the main text—e.g., a concise proof sketch—if space permits.

Questions and points needing clarification
- Non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear cases: For problems other than bilinear saddle-point formulations, what specific non-uniform sampling strategy is used? In particular, please define the sampling distribution π in those settings.
- Step-size selection: How is the step size σ chosen in the experiments?
- Role of non-uniform sampling: Do the authors have intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears crucial for saddle-point problems but is not always necessary in convex minimization? A brief discussion of this phenomenon would be valuable.
