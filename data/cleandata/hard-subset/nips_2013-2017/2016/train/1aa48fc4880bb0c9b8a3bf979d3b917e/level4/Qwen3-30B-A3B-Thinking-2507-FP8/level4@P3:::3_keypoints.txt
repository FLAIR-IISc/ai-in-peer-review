- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems, delivering first linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for a novel convergence analysis rather than standard convexity arguments.  
- Identifies and analyzes two distinct split types (function-based vs derivative-based) specific to saddle-point problems.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is critical for theoretical efficiency and empirical performance.  
- Provides a simple Catalyst-style acceleration extension that outperforms batch methods in experiments.  
- Technical quality is strong, with rigorous convergence proofs addressing split types, sampling, and saddle-point duality.  
- Experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses validate theoretical claims, especially for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Clarity and presentation are good, with helpful Table 1 and practical applications discussed (supervised non-separable losses, robust optimization, convex relaxations).  
- Originality is substantial: saddle-point setting requires fundamentally different analysis and the split distinction is novel.  
- Significance is high: many ML problems map to saddle-point formulations where proposed methods can yield substantial speedups.  
- Concern: theoretical complexity claims need refinement—Table 1 suggests factored splits give O(n + d) per iteration which can be worse than batch when n ≫ d.  
- Requests for authors to clarify whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits.  
- Requests clarification on precisely when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch forward-backward (FB) algorithms.  
- Requests evidence or argument whether the provided complexity bounds are tight.  
- Pros: novel extension, rigorous analysis, convincing experiments, and important ML applications.  
- Cons: theoretical complexity analysis and discussion of factored splits need more precision; stronger justification needed for typical n ≫ d ML settings.  
- Overall: strong paper deserving acceptance after minor revisions to clarify theoretical complexity claims.
