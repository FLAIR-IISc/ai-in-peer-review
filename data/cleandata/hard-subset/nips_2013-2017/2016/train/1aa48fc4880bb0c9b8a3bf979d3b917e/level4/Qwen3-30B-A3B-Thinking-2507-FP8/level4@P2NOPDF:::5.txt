

This paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems using variance reduction techniques (SVRG/SAGA), overcoming the limitations of convex minimization analysis. The core innovation lies in employing monotone operator theory to prove convergence, which extends applicability to broader classes like variational inequalities. This approach enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers—common in machine learning models—without requiring separability, a notable advancement.  

The authors’ proof technique, interpreting saddle points as finding zeros of monotone operators, is elegant and potentially widely applicable beyond the specific algorithms. It also yields simpler proofs for existing methods like the forward-backward splitting (and its stochastic variant), adding theoretical value. The proposed algorithms, particularly with non-uniform sampling, demonstrate strong empirical performance, outperforming prior alternatives in most experiments. The work effectively extends the field by bridging variance reduction methods with saddle-point optimization, addressing a critical gap in the literature.  

While the algorithms themselves are straightforward extensions of SVRG/SAGA, the paper’s main contribution is the proof framework. To maximize impact, the authors should elaborate on this technique in the main text (e.g., including a proof sketch), especially given its novelty. The current presentation of the proof is somewhat dense, and more detail would help readers grasp the key insights without needing to consult supplementary material.  

The paper is generally well-written and clear, but two critical points require clarification:  
1. **Non-uniform sampling (π)**: The paper uses non-uniform sampling for bilinear saddle-point problems, but the definition of the sampling distribution π for *general* saddle-point problems remains ambiguous. The authors should explicitly specify how π is derived or computed for non-bilinear cases.  
2. **Step size σ**: In experiments, the step size σ is used without explanation of its selection. The authors should clarify whether σ is tuned per problem, set via theoretical bounds, or determined heuristically, and provide intuition for its role.  

Additionally, the paper lacks discussion on a key observation: why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization. This is a pivotal insight—the authors should address whether the structure of monotone operators (vs. strongly convex functions) necessitates non-uniform sampling, and how this differs from standard convex settings.  

Minor issues:  
- Line 78: "k" should be "d" (typo).  
- Line 121: Second equality contains an extra term "y_{t-1}" (typo).  

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear theoretical and practical implications. The monotone operator approach is a valuable tool for saddle-point optimization, and the empirical results validate its effectiveness. With minor clarifications on sampling and step-size selection, and enhanced emphasis on the proof technique, the paper would be even more impactful. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and represents a meaningful step forward for optimization in machine learning.
