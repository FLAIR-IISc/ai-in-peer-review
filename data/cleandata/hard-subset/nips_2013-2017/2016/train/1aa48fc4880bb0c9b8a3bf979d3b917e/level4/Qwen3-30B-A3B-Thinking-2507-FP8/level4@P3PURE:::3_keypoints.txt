- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems for individual and factored splits.  
- Provides thorough complexity analysis across splits and uniform/non-uniform sampling strategies.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beat competitive batch methods.  
- Although conceptually straightforward, the technical development and established results are novel and non-trivial.  
- For the bilinear saddle point case, presents a clear, well-motivated comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms paralleling convex minimization results.  
- Major concern: each iteration requires computing a proximal operator at O(n + d) cost, creating a fundamental bottleneck.  
- As a result, overall complexity (even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch FB, especially when n >> d.  
- In simulations using factored splits, complexity can be worse than batch FB (Table 2), so variance-reduction benefits are not evident here.  
- Requests the authors to discuss: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits; (ii) conditions under which accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB; (iii) whether the n and d dependence is tight.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but keeps scores unchanged.
