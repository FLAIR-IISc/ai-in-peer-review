- Main contribution: First large-scale, linearly convergent SVRG/SAGA-style algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending variance reduction to non-separable objectives.  
- Novelty: Extends variance-reduced stochastic methods beyond separable minimization to important ML settings (structured outputs, robust optimization).  
- Methods: Adapts SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, analyzes non-uniform sampling, and introduces a Catalyst-based accelerated variant.  
- Theoretical foundation: Convergence analysis grounded in monotone operator theory, establishing linear rates and applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Technical quality: Rigorous, comprehensive proofs for linear convergence of SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated variants.  
- Clarity of contributions: Paper clearly states its main advances—first linear convergence for these algorithms on saddle-points, splitting/sampling analysis, and acceleration.  
- Organization issue: Monotone operator framework is presented late (Section 6) despite being fundamental to proofs, which hinders readability.  
- Experimental limitation: Empirical validation is limited to two datasets and should cover more diverse problems that highlight non-separable advantages.  
- Related-work gap: Needs stronger comparison to stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., stochastic Chambolle-Pock variants) and specific recent works like Zhang & Xiao (2015).  
- Question on assumptions: Request clarification on practicality and restrictiveness of assumptions (A)-(C), especially strong convex-concavity (e.g., SVM applicability).  
- Distinction from other methods: Ask for clearer explanation of differences/advantages compared to stochastic primal-dual frameworks.  
- Theorem 2 clarity: `µ` (strong monotonicity) is undefined in main text; authors should compare Theorem 2’s rate with Theorem 1 more directly.  
- Link assumptions to monotonicity: Request an explicit explanation of how (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity used in proofs.  
- Minor issues: Clarify notation for `L` (condition number vs. Lipschitz constant) and add a citation for the Forward-Backward algorithm in Section 2.2.
