This paper extends recent incremental algorithms—namely SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated "Catalyst" variants—to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure. The authors consider two types of splits for the problem structure: "individual" and "factored." They provide a comprehensive complexity analysis for these algorithms under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification task demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for the proposed methods to outperform competitive batch algorithms.

While the extension of variance-reduction techniques to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward at first, the paper's results are novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial. For the important case of bilinear saddle-point problems, the authors present a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms. This comparison draws an insightful parallel to the well-understood case of convex minimization.

My major concern, however, relates to the overall complexity and practical advantage of the proposed methods. While formulating the problem as a saddle-point problem effectively addresses the challenge of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as shown in Tables 1 and 2—is not always better than that of the accelerated batch method. In particular, when the number of samples *n* is substantially larger than the feature dimension *d* (a common scenario in machine learning), the complexity under factored splits (which are used in the experiments) is theoretically worse than that of batch forward-backward algorithms, according to Table 2. Unlike in standard convex minimization, the theoretical advantage of using variance reduction is not immediately apparent in this setting.

I would suggest that the authors provide a more in-depth discussion on this trade-off and clarify the following points:
1. Is the factored split formulation theoretically worse than the individual split formulation?
2. Under what specific conditions is the accelerated SVRG method preferable to the accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm?
3. Are the derived dependencies on *n* and *d* in the complexity bounds tight?
