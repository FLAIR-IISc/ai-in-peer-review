Summary
The paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems by casting them as monotone operator root-finding with a forward–backward splitting: A encodes the proximal part M, and B aggregates Lipschitz components of the smooth part K. This yields the first large-scale incremental algorithms with linear convergence for non-separable saddle-point objectives common in machine learning. The analysis—based solely on monotonicity, not co-coercivity—covers a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.

Two stochastic decompositions of B are studied: (i) element-wise splits and (ii) factored splits aligned with the saddle structure (e.g., row/column splits for bilinear forms). The paper develops non-uniform sampling schemes tailored to the component Lipschitz constants and shows they strictly improve theoretical rates over uniform sampling. An acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper further improves complexity; in the saddle-point setting the proximal-point viewpoint simplifies the acceleration analysis relative to standard Nesterov arguments.

Experiments on supervised learning with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC surrogates) and non-separable regularizers (e.g., fused penalties) show that variance-reduced methods—especially with non-uniform sampling and acceleration—outperform batch primal–dual algorithms and uniform-sampling baselines. The framework is positioned relative to prior finite-sum methods for convex minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA), primal–dual splitting (Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock), stochastic forward–backward with O(1/t) rates, and “catalyst”-type acceleration.

Quality
- Theory: The paper proves linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA under strong convexity–concavity of M and Lipschitz gradients of K, with step sizes depending on L, component Lipschitz constants, and strong convexity parameters. The monotone-operator analysis is careful and addresses key differences from convex minimization. The acceleration analysis is provided for SVRG; conditions for SAGA are discussed but not proved.
- Algorithms: The stochastic forward–backward update is standard; the variance reduction estimators are natural extensions to the saddle setting. The need for resampling under non-uniform SAGA is noted.
- Practicality: Complexity bounds account for proximal cost and oracle cost per component. Factored splits significantly reduce storage for SAGA in bilinear problems.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, but some choices impede readability:
- The symbol K is used both for the smooth function and the design matrix; using distinct symbols would avoid confusion.
- Guidance on practical parameter choices (mini-batch size, probabilities, τ, epoch lengths) is scattered; a consolidated “practical recipe” would help.
- The role and update of the acceleration anchor (x̄, ȳ) could be explained more explicitly in the main text (only briefly discussed and deferred to the appendix).

Originality
- First linear-rate incremental methods for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems.
- Monotone-operator analysis avoiding co-coercivity extends applicability beyond convex minimization to variational inequalities.
- Introduction of factored decompositions and tailored non-uniform sampling in the saddle setting.
- Acceleration via a proximal-point view specialized to saddle points.

Significance
The methods address an important gap: scalable, linearly convergent algorithms for non-separable saddle-point formulations that arise with structured losses and complex regularizers, robust optimization, and convex relaxations. The techniques and analyses should be useful to both practitioners and theorists and likely to influence subsequent work on monotone operators and variational inequalities.

Pros
- Clear conceptual extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points with rigorous linear rates.
- General monotone-operator perspective broadens scope (e.g., variational inequalities).
- Two complementary splitting schemes (element-wise and factored) with memory/runtime trade-offs.
- Non-uniform sampling is well motivated and shown to improve rates and practice.
- Acceleration via a simple proximal-point wrapper with tangible empirical gains.
- Solid experiments demonstrating improvements on non-separable losses/regularizers.

Cons
- Acceleration proof is only for SVRG; the status for SAGA remains open.
- Practical computation/estimation of L̄ and sampling probabilities is not fully specified.
- The catalyst anchor update (x̄, ȳ) is described only briefly; adaptive strategies need clearer guidance.
- SAGA with individual splits may require large storage; limits and mitigations could be discussed further.
- Empirical evaluation spans few datasets; broader comparisons (including separable cases and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods) would strengthen claims.
- Notational overload (K for both function and matrix).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a practical recipe for choosing probabilities and estimating L̄ online (e.g., from row/column norms or mini-batch curvature estimates) without extra full passes.
- Detail an adaptive rule for updating (x̄, ȳ) in the accelerated scheme (triggering, frequency, and safeguards), and discuss robustness to mis-specified τ.
- Clarify whether the acceleration analysis can be extended to SAGA and, if not, what technical obstacle prevents it.
- Discuss sensitivity to unknown or mis-specified strong convexity–concavity constants and whether backtracking or adaptive step sizes could be incorporated.
- Elaborate on memory/runtime trade-offs for SAGA under the two splits and possible compression/sketching to reduce storage.
- Compare against stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods for separable cases where they apply.
- Use distinct notation for the smooth function and the data matrix.

Overall assessment
A strong contribution that advances stochastic variance reduction to an important and previously underserved class of problems. With minor clarifications (especially around acceleration, parameter selection, and notation), the paper merits acceptance.
