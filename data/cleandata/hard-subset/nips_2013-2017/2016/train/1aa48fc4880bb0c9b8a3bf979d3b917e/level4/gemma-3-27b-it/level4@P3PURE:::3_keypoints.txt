- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle point problems for individual and factored splits.
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across split types and uniform/nonuniform sampling strategies.
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch algorithms.
- Results are novel and technically challenging despite seeming conceptually straightforward to extend variance reduction to saddle points.
- Clear, logical comparison for the bilinear saddle point case between batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, paralleling convex minimization results.
- Major concern: per-iteration proximal operator cost is O(n + d), creating a bottleneck that can negate incremental method benefits.
- Consequently, overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling) may not beat accelerated batch methods, especially when n >> d.
- Under factored splits (used in simulations), complexity can be worse than batch FB, questioning theoretical advantage of variance reduction here.
- Reviewer requests deeper discussion on: (i) theoretical inferiority of factored vs individual splits, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with authors’ response; scores remain unchanged.
