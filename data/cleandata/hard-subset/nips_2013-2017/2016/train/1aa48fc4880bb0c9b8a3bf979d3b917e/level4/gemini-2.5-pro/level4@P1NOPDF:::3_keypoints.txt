- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems.  
- Handles two data-split models (individual and factored) and analyzes both uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Provides a thorough complexity analysis for the proposed algorithms under both splits and sampling schemes.  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform batch algorithms.  
- The extension is novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial.  
- Delivers a clear comparison for bilinear saddle-point problems between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward methods, drawing useful parallels to convex minimization.  
- Major practical concern: computing the proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, creating a fundamental bottleneck.  
- Because of the proximal cost, overall complexity (including accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch methods.  
- In the common regime n >> d, factored splits (used in experiments) can be worse than batch FB, so variance reduction lacks a clear theoretical advantage.  
- Requests clarification on: (1) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, (2) specific conditions (relations among n, d, condition numbers) where accelerated SVRG is favorable, and (3) tightness of the analysis regarding dependence on n and d.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with the authors' response but retains the same scores.
