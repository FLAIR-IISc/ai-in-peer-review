This paper addresses the challenging problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, by extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods like SVRG and SAGA. The authors provide a convergence analysis for these adapted algorithms and demonstrate that they can be further accelerated using a “catalyst” framework extension. They also showcase the broader applicability of their approach by connecting it to monotone operator theory and provide experimental validation.

The core contribution lies in adapting existing variance reduction techniques, originally designed for convex minimization, to the saddle-point setting. This is non-trivial, as the analysis differs significantly from the convex case, requiring the use of monotone operator theory. The authors highlight several key insights, including the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency and the existence of two distinct notions of splitting the problem. The extension to monotone operators broadens the potential impact of this work beyond traditional saddle-point problems, encompassing variational inequalities.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous convergence analysis, establishing linear convergence for the proposed algorithms. The connection to monotone operator theory is a valuable theoretical contribution.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to machine learning, and the proposed algorithms offer a potential speedup over existing methods, particularly for large-scale problems.
*   **Novelty:** Extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is a novel contribution, and the insights regarding splitting and sampling are valuable.
*   **Catalyst Acceleration:** The application of the catalyst framework provides a further performance boost, demonstrating a thoughtful approach to optimization.
*   **Experiments:** The experimental section provides empirical evidence supporting the theoretical findings, showcasing performance gains over baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The assumption of strong convexity for *both* the primal and dual problems feels restrictive. It would be beneficial to explore the conditions under which the algorithm converges with weaker assumptions (e.g., strong convexity on only one side).
*   **Limited Experimentation:** While the experiments are encouraging, the scope is somewhat limited. Additional examples with different datasets and problem settings would strengthen the evaluation. Specifically, demonstrating performance on a wider range of non-separable losses and regularizers (mentioned in the introduction) would be valuable.
*   **Clarity in Notation:** While generally clear, some of the notation (particularly concerning the different splitting options) could be made more accessible. A clearer visual representation of the different splitting approaches might be helpful.
*   **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** A brief discussion of practical considerations, such as parameter tuning and the computational cost of the proximal operator, would enhance the paper's usefulness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The results demonstrate significant potential for improving the efficiency of solving saddle-point problems in machine learning.
*   The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the work.
*   The experimental validation supports the theoretical claims.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong convexity assumption limits the generality of the results.
*   The limited scope of the experimental evaluation leaves some questions unanswered.
*   Minor clarity issues in notation and presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical results are solid, the proposed algorithms are novel and potentially impactful, and the experimental validation is encouraging. While the strong convexity assumption and limited experimental scope are weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The authors should consider addressing the reviewer's concerns regarding the strong convexity assumption and the scope of the experiments. Improving the clarity of notation and adding a discussion of practical considerations would also be beneficial. These revisions would further strengthen an already excellent paper.
