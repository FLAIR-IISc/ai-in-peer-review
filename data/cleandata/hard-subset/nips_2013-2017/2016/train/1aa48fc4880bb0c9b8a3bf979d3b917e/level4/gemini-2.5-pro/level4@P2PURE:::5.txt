This paper presents the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems by extending variance reduction methods like SVRG and SAGA. The central contribution is a novel proof technique based on monotone operator theory. This approach is necessary as standard convex minimization analysis does not apply to this problem class, and it has the added benefit of being generalizable to other problems, such as variational inequalities. The work is well-motivated, as saddle point formulations are a powerful tool for handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers common in machine learning.

The analysis technique of recasting the problem as finding the zeros of a monotone operator is elegant and potentially widely applicable. As a further contribution, the authors leverage this framework to provide simpler proofs for existing algorithms like Forward-Backward splitting and its stochastic variants. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, particularly when combined with non-uniform sampling, are competitive with and often outperform existing methods.

Overall, this paper provides a valuable extension to the literature in an important area, distinguished by its novel analytical approach. Given that the primary novelty lies in the proof technique rather than the algorithms themselves (which are straightforward extensions), the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed exposition of the analysis in the main body. If space permits, including a proof sketch would be particularly helpful for the reader.

The paper is generally well-written, but some aspects could be clarified:
1.  For problems beyond the bilinear case, the non-uniform sampling distribution, $\pi$, is not specified.
2.  The method for setting the step size, $\sigma$, in the experiments is not described.
3.  The empirical results highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the authors' intuition on why this technique appears to be significantly more crucial for saddle point problems than for standard convex minimization.
