This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing a linear convergence rate. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of applying variance reduction to this class of problems.

The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, where both the loss function (e.g., in structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., a fusion term) can be non-separable. A key contribution lies in the convergence analysis. Unlike previous analyses for convex problems, the saddle-point setting is significantly more complex, and the authors adeptly employ the theory of monotone operators to prove convergence. This analysis also reveals that the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms are applicable to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities in game theory.

From an algorithmic perspective, the authors integrate variance reduction into the stochastic forward-backward framework, which is commonly used for saddle-point optimization. They propose two distinct splits of the gradient operator—the element-wise split and the factored split—for SAGA and SVRG, respectively. The paper also incorporates non-uniform sampling, showing both theoretically and empirically that it is superior to uniform sampling. The experimental results, presented in both the main paper and the appendix, provide sufficient evidence to support the theoretical findings and confirm the effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems with non-separable loss and regularization.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Section 5 on acceleration, the authors follow [8] by incorporating an additional regularization term to guide the update towards an iterate point `(\bar{x}, \bar{y})`. It is not clear how this point should be adaptively updated to achieve the desired speed-up.
*   A convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG. Do the same theoretical results hold for the SAGA variant?
*   To avoid confusion, the smooth function `K(.)` and the design matrix `K` should be denoted by different symbols.
