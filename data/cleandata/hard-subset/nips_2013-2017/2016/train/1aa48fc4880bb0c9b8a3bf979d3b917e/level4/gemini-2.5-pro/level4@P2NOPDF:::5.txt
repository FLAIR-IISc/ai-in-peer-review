This paper proposes the first linearly convergent algorithms for finite-sum saddle point problems by adapting variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA. The central contribution is a novel proof technique based on the theory of monotone operators, which is necessary because standard analysis for convex minimization does not directly apply to the saddle point setting. This analytical framework is a significant strength, as it extends the applicability of these methods to a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities.

The problem of efficiently solving saddle point problems is of considerable importance in machine learning, particularly for models involving non-separable loss functions and regularizers. By achieving a linear convergence rate, this work represents a major theoretical advance in this area.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces the first algorithms with a provably linear convergence rate for this class of saddle point problems. This is a significant and impactful contribution to the optimization literature.

2.  **Elegant Proof Technique:** The key innovation is the interpretation of the saddle point problem as finding the zero of a monotone operator. This allows the authors to prove convergence in a setting where traditional convex analysis falls short. This approach is powerful, insightful, and likely to be influential in future work. As a beneficial side effect, the framework also provides simpler proofs for existing algorithms like Forward-Backward splitting and its stochastic variants.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed algorithms, particularly the variants using non-uniform sampling, demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods across several experiments.

4.  **Broad Applicability:** The method addresses an important practical challenge by providing an efficient way to handle models with non-separable objectives, which are common in machine learning.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is generally well-written and the contributions are clearly articulated. My main suggestions are focused on enhancing clarity and providing deeper intuition.

1.  **Emphasis on the Analytical Framework:** The proposed algorithms are themselves relatively straightforward extensions of SVRG and SAGA. The primary novelty and main contribution of the paper therefore lie in the sophisticated proof technique. Given this, the paper could be strengthened by allocating more space in the main body to the proof's intuition. If space permits, a high-level proof sketch would be very valuable for helping the reader grasp the core mechanics of the monotone operator analysis.

2.  **Clarity on Implementation Details:** I found the paper to be slightly unclear on a few points, which could hinder reproducibility and a full understanding of the method's practical application:
    *   **Non-Uniform Sampling:** The paper convincingly shows the benefits of non-uniform sampling. However, it is not clear how the sampling distribution π is defined for problems beyond the bilinear saddle-point case. Please clarify this for the general setting.
    *   **Step Size Selection:** How was the step size σ set in the experiments? Providing these details is crucial for assessing the algorithm's practical usability and sensitivity to this hyperparameter.

3.  **Deeper Intuition on Non-Uniform Sampling:** The experiments strongly suggest that non-uniform sampling is essential for achieving good performance on saddle point problems, seemingly more so than for standard convex minimization. The paper would benefit greatly from a discussion on this phenomenon. Is there a theoretical intuition for why non-uniform sampling is particularly well-suited to the geometry of saddle point problems? Adding such a discussion would provide valuable insight beyond the empirical results.
