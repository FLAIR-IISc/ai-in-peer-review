This paper leverages variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to obtain the first algorithms with linear convergence for saddle-point problems. Because standard convex minimization analyses do not directly apply, the core contribution is a convergence proof based on monotone operator theory. This perspective broadens the scope of the results to cover variational inequalities and enables handling non-separable losses and regularizers via a saddle-point formulation. The interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator is elegant and likely to be broadly useful. The paper also provides simpler proofs for forward–backward splitting and its stochastic variant. Empirically, the proposed non-uniform sampling schemes are competitive and often superior to existing alternatives. Overall, the work advances the field in a meaningful direction with a compelling and generalizable analysis.

Given that the primary novelty lies in the analysis rather than the algorithmic templates (which are relatively straightforward extensions of SVRG/SAGA), I recommend including more details of the proof technique in the main text—ideally a concise proof sketch—if space permits.

Clarity questions and requests:
- For non-bilinear saddle-point problems, what specific non-uniform sampling distribution pi is used? How is pi constructed in practice?
- How is the stepsize sigma selected in the experiments (e.g., theory-driven, tuned on a validation set, or set by heuristic rules)?
- Do the authors have intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears crucial for saddle-point problems but not always for convex minimization? A short discussion would be valuable.
