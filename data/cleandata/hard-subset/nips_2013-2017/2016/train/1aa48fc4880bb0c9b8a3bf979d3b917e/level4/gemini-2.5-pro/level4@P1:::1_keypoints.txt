- Paper proposes the first large-scale, linearly convergent SVRG/SAGA-style algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems decomposable into many components.  
- Analysis is framed within monotone operator theory, extending applicability to variational inequalities and non-separable losses/regularizers.  
- Includes non-uniform sampling schemes and a Catalyst-based acceleration technique with theoretical guarantees.

- Originality/significance: first linearly convergent incremental methods for non-separable saddle-point problems, addressing a gap where primal or dual variance-reduction alone is insufficient.  
- Technical quality: rigorous convergence analysis using a monotone-operator framework that generalizes beyond convex-concave functions.  
- Completeness: presents both SVRG and SAGA variants, studies sampling strategies, and offers an accelerated variant outperforming accelerated batch methods theoretically.

- Clarity/presentation: heavy reliance on monotone-operator theory introduced late; framework should be presented earlier to improve accessibility.  
- Experimental evaluation: empirical results limited to two datasets, weakening practical validation across problem types.  
- Comparison to related work: lacks fuller experimental comparison to other stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., stochastic Chambolle-Pock by Zhang et al. ICML 2015).

- Q1: Clarify how restrictive assumptions (A)-(C) are in practice, especially the strong convex-concave requirement and whether common formulations (e.g., SVM saddle-point) satisfy it.  
- Q2: Elaborate on relationships and key differences between the proposed methods and broader stochastic primal-dual algorithms, highlighting advantages.  
- Q3: Define the constant µ in Theorem 2 and provide a direct comparison of SAGA vs SVRG convergence rates.  
- Q4: Explicitly state whether assumptions (A)-(C) imply strong monotonicity and briefly outline that implication.

- Minor: inconsistent use of L (condition number vs Lipschitz constant) — clarify and standardize terminology.  
- Minor: add a specific citation for the Forward-Backward (FB) algorithm in Section 2.2.
