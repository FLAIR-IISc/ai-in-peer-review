This paper proposes the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems by adapting variance reduction techniques such as SVRG and SAGA. The key contribution lies in the novel proof technique, which frames the problem as finding the zero of a monotone operator. This approach circumvents the limitations of standard convex minimization analysis and is general enough to apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The proposed methods are particularly valuable for machine learning models involving non-separable loss functions and regularizers.

The paper makes a significant contribution by extending variance reduction methods to a new and important class of problems. The analysis is interesting and the approach appears powerful. The main strengths are:

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses the important problem of efficiently solving saddle-point problems, which arise in many machine learning contexts with non-separable objectives. It proposes the first algorithms for this problem class with a guaranteed linear convergence rate.
*   **Powerful Proof Technique:** The interpretation of saddle-point problems through the lens of monotone operator theory is a key technical insight. This framework is not only elegant but also powerful, providing simpler proofs for existing algorithms like Forward-Backward splitting and offering a potentially widely applicable analysis tool.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, particularly when combined with non-uniform sampling, are competitive and often outperform existing methods.

While the paper is generally well-written and clear, there are a few areas that could be improved:

Given that the primary contribution is the novel proof technique—the algorithms themselves being direct extensions of SVRG/SAGA—the paper would be strengthened by providing more insight into the analysis in the main text. If space permits, including a high-level proof sketch would be highly valuable for the reader.

I also have a few questions for the authors regarding clarity and experimental details:
1.  **Non-uniform Sampling:** The paper explains the non-uniform sampling distribution $\pi$ for bilinear problems. Could the authors clarify what distribution is used for the more general saddle-point problems discussed?
2.  **Experimental Details:** How was the step-size $\sigma$ selected in the experiments?
3.  **Intuition on Sampling:** The results suggest that non-uniform sampling is particularly effective for saddle-point problems, perhaps more so than for standard convex minimization. Do the authors have any intuition as to why this might be the case? A brief discussion on this point would be a welcome addition.
