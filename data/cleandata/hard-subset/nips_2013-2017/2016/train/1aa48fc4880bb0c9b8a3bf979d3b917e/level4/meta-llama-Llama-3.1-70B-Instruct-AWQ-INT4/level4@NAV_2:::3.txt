This paper extends several recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite sum structure. The authors provide a thorough study on the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and different (uniform/nonuniform) sampling strategies. The experiments on a special binary classification example show that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beat the competitive batch algorithms. 

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors have made a clear effort to provide a comprehensive review of the related work and to explain the contributions of the paper. The presentation of the algorithms and their analysis is also clear and easy to follow. 

The main contribution of the paper is the extension of the incremental algorithms to saddle-point problems, which is a non-trivial task. The authors have successfully shown that these algorithms can be applied to a wide range of problems, including bilinear saddle-point problems, and have provided a thorough analysis of their complexity. 

However, I do have some concerns regarding the practicality of the proposed algorithms. The computational complexity of the algorithms, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version, especially when the number of data n is substantially larger than its dimension d. This is because the proximal operator at each iteration has a cost of O(n+d), which can be a significant bottleneck. 

To address this concern, I would suggest that the authors provide an in-depth discussion on the practicality of the proposed algorithms and clarify the following questions: (i) Is factored splits (theoretically) worse than individual splits? (ii) When is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? (iii) Is the dependence on n, d tight enough?

In terms of the quality of the paper, I would say that it is good, but not excellent. The paper is well-written, but the presentation of the algorithms and their analysis could be improved. The authors could provide more intuition and explanations for the key steps in the analysis. 

In terms of the impact of the paper, I would say that it is significant. The paper extends the incremental algorithms to a wide range of problems, including saddle-point problems, and provides a thorough analysis of their complexity. This could have a significant impact on the field of machine learning and optimization.

Strengths:

* The paper extends the incremental algorithms to saddle-point problems, which is a non-trivial task.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the complexity of the algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized.

Weaknesses:

* The computational complexity of the algorithms is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version.
* The proximal operator at each iteration has a cost of O(n+d), which can be a significant bottleneck.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
