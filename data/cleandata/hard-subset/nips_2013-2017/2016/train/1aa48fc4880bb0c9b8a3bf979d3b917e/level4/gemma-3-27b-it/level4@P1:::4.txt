This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, proving linear convergence. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of these variance reduction methods in the saddle-point setting. The work tackles a significant problem: extending accelerated methods to non-separable optimization problems common in machine learning, where both loss functions (e.g., in structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., those employing fusion penalties) may lack separability. A key contribution lies in the more complex convergence analysis required for saddle-point problems compared to standard convex optimization. The authors cleverly leverage the framework of monotone operators to prove convergence, and importantly, demonstrate that the resulting algorithms generalize beyond saddle-point problems to the broader class of variational inequalities, with applications in game theory. The paper also carefully considers the nuances of splitting the gradient operator—element-wise versus a factored approach—within the SVRG and SAGA algorithms. By building upon stochastic forward-backward methods commonly used for saddle-point problems, and incorporating non-uniform sampling, the authors achieve superior performance, confirmed through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. The experiments, presented in the main text and the appendix, provide convincing evidence supporting the theoretical findings and showcase the effectiveness of the proposed methods with both non-separable losses and regularizers.

Some minor points could be further clarified: While the acceleration technique in Section 5, inspired by [7], introduces an additional regularization term to guide the update, the method for *adaptively* updating the parameters (x̄, ȳ) to maximize speed-up remains unclear.  It would be beneficial to elaborate on this aspect.  Additionally, while a convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, it is not explicitly stated whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA; addressing this would strengthen the completeness of the analysis. Finally, to avoid potential confusion, using distinct notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K would be preferable.
