The paper generalizes recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants – to address strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite-sum structure, arising from either individual or factored data splits. The authors present a comprehensive complexity analysis of these algorithms under various splits and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification problem demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are essential for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. Extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems, while conceptually simple, yields notably novel results with significant technical challenges. For the critical bilinear saddle point problem, the authors offer a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms, mirroring established results for convex minimization – a correspondence I find convincing. However, a key concern remains. While saddle point methods resolve the non-separability of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear constrained by a fundamental computational bottleneck: the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as detailed in Tables 1 and 2, does not consistently offer improved complexity compared to accelerated batch methods. Specifically, when the number of data points *n* significantly exceeds the dimension *d* – common in machine learning – the complexity with factored splits (used in the simulation) is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the situation in convex minimization, I do not observe a clear, substantial theoretical advantage to using variance reduction techniques in this context.
