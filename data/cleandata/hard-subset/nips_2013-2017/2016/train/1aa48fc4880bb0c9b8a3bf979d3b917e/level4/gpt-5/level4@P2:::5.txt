Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) from finite-sum convex minimization to convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x, y) + M(x, y), where K is smooth and M has a simple proximal operator. The key technical contribution is a convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory rather than convex minimization arguments. This yields the first large-scale incremental methods with linear convergence for general strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems, and the analysis carries over to broader settings such as variational inequalities.

Beyond the algorithmic extensions, the paper: (i) formalizes two natural “splits” of the smooth operator B = (∂xK, −∂yK), either by functions or by partial derivatives, (ii) highlights that splits must respect convex–concave structure, (iii) shows that non-uniform sampling is crucial, and (iv) adapts the Catalyst framework to further accelerate the variance-reduced methods, with complexity bounds that dominate accelerated batch methods in theory. The work relates to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for minimization, to primal–dual/forward–backward methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock) for saddle points, and to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods for separable problems; here, the variance-reduced methods are generalized to non-separable settings via a unified monotone operator analysis.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: reframes variance reduction for saddle-point problems through monotone operator theory, enabling linear rates and extending beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.
- Generality: supports non-separable losses/regularizers via proximal structure; accommodates both “function” and “factored” splits.
- Practical guidance: clear treatment of non-uniform sampling and its effect on constants (L and L̄) and complexity; analysis covers minibatching and storage trade-offs (SVRG vs. SAGA).
- Acceleration: Catalyst-style regularization yields tighter complexities and practical speedups, especially in ill-conditioned regimes.
- Additional contributions: provides simpler convergence arguments for forward–backward (FB) and its stochastic variant.
- Empirical evidence: on representative datasets, non-uniformly sampled SAGA/SVRG (especially accelerated SVRG) outperform batch baselines and uniform-sampling variants, consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear K: The paper is very clear for bilinear problems (rows/columns vs. entries) but is less explicit for general K. Please specify recommended π (and how to estimate L̄(π)) for common non-bilinear cases, and discuss practical heuristics when exact Lipschitz constants per component are unavailable.
- Step size selection: How is the step size σ set in the experiments (e.g., exact L and L̄, backtracking, or tuning)? Some guidance on robust defaults or adaptive strategies would improve reproducibility.
- Why non-uniform sampling matters more here: The experiments suggest non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems. Can the authors provide intuition (e.g., coupling of x/y blocks, asymmetry in operator Lipschitz constants, or the role of L̄ vs. L) and connect it to the theory?
- Assumptions and constants: The methods rely on known strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz constants. A brief discussion of adaptive schemes or practical estimation (beyond what is cited for minimization) would be valuable.
- Storage and implementation: For SAGA with individual splits, memory can be large; factored splits are cheaper. A short implementation note quantifying memory and update costs for typical settings would help practitioners.
- Empirical breadth: Results are promising but limited in scope. Additional datasets, ablations on sampling distributions, and sensitivity to condition numbers would strengthen the empirical case.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound, with clear convergence guarantees under standard strong convexity–concavity and smoothness assumptions. The operator-theoretic analysis is careful and extends prior variance-reduction work in a principled way.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Theoretical results are clearly stated. The paper would benefit from a proof sketch in the main text and more detail on non-uniform sampling and step-size selection.
- Originality: High. The use of monotone operator theory to obtain linearly convergent incremental methods for general saddle-point problems is novel, and the extension to variational inequalities is noteworthy.
- Significance: High. Many machine learning problems involve non-separable losses/regularizers or naturally arise as saddle points; providing linearly convergent, large-scale methods with practical sampling/acceleration strategies is impactful.

Pros and cons for acceptance:
Pros:
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for general convex–concave saddle-point problems.
- Clean, broadly applicable analysis via monotone operators; extensions to variational inequalities.
- Strong practical contributions: non-uniform sampling, minibatching, Catalyst acceleration, and complexity guidance.
- Empirical results align with theory and show clear improvements.

Cons:
- Limited guidance for non-uniform sampling and step-size selection beyond bilinear cases.
- Dependence on problem constants; lack of adaptivity discussion in the saddle-point setting.
- Experimental scope could be broader, with more ablations and comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include a proof sketch highlighting the key operator-theoretic ideas and how they differ from minimization analyses.
- Provide practical recipes for π and σ when K is not bilinear, including heuristics and diagnostics.
- Discuss adaptivity to unknown strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz constants.
- Add ablations on sampling strategies and sensitivity to condition number; report memory/runtime trade-offs for factored vs. individual splits.
