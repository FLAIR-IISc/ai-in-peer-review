Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly convex–concave. The authors extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting, proving linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity. A key analytical device is to reinterpret the saddle-point optimality conditions as a monotone inclusion and analyze forward–backward splitting with variance-reduced forward steps. The work further:
- distinguishes two granularities of “splits” for stochastic updates (by functions vs by partial derivatives/blocks),
- emphasizes non-uniform sampling as crucial in both theory and practice,
- provides an acceleration via a proximal-point (“catalyst”) outer loop,
- shows that the same framework applies beyond saddle points to monotone variational inequalities.

Relative to prior work, this advances beyond batch primal–dual methods (e.g., Arrow–Hurwicz, Chambolle–Pock and its accelerations) and stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC), by providing the first linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms for general (not necessarily separable) saddle-point problems with a single proximable M. The analysis differs from standard convex minimization (where co-coercivity is available); here, monotonicity is used instead. The acceleration is simpler than Nesterov-type schemes because it leverages the proximal-point viewpoint. The paper also clarifies how non-uniform sampling yields better complexity bounds, consistent with recent results in finite-sum optimization.

Strengths
- Originality: Extends SVRG/SAGA to general saddle-point and monotone operator settings with linear rates; the acceleration via catalyst in this context is novel and practically relevant.
- Technical quality: Proofs are careful, avoid co-coercivity, and unify several cases (saddle points, variational inequalities). The role of non-uniform sampling is well quantified.
- Significance: Addresses large-scale, non-separable problems that arise in practice, where existing separable primal–dual methods are not applicable. The complexity improvements are meaningful.
- Empirical evidence: Experiments demonstrate clear gains of (accelerated) variance-reduced methods over batch and uniformly sampled baselines; the factored vs individual splits and non-uniform sampling are motivated by the theory.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity: The monotone-operator lens underlies the analysis but appears late as an “extension.” Introducing it earlier would make assumptions, step-size choices, and constants more transparent.
- Assumptions: Strong convexity–concavity of M is central; the paper could better discuss when it holds in common models (e.g., SVMs with hinge loss) and how to handle non-strong cases.
- Comparisons: Experimental comparisons are limited (two datasets). For separable cases, stronger baselines such as stochastic/accelerated Chambolle–Pock and SPDC should be included. Please add Zhang and Xiao (ICML 2015) and compare empirically where applicable.
- Notation: L is used both as a Lipschitz constant and (informally) as a “condition number;” κ-type notation for condition numbers would avoid confusion.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with clear linear convergence guarantees and complexity bounds. Acceleration analysis is elegant. Some details (e.g., exact meaning of μ in Theorem 2) should be clarified.
- Clarity: Good overall, but the logical flow would improve by foregrounding monotone operators earlier, unifying notation (L, μ, κ), and tightening the presentation of sampling constants L̄(π).
- Originality: High; variance-reduced methods for general saddle-point/monotone problems with linear rates were not available, and the catalyst adaptation is nontrivial.
- Significance: High; likely to influence both theory and practice for large-scale saddle-point optimization and variational inequalities.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
1) Strength of assumptions A–C: Assumption (A) requires M to be strongly (λ,γ)-convex–concave and proximable. Do standard models satisfy this?
- For L2-regularized logistic regression, the primal–dual pair typically leads to strong convexity in x via the L2 penalty; is g (hence −g in the saddle form) strongly convex to ensure strong concavity in y?
- For SVMs with hinge loss, g is not strongly convex; thus (A) fails unless one smooths the hinge or adds a small quadratic to g. Please discuss how your theory handles or can be adapted to such non-strongly convex–concave cases (e.g., via proximal-point regularization/catalyst at the outer loop).

2) Relation to stochastic primal–dual methods (e.g., SPDC, PDHG variants): Please elaborate on key differences:
- Structure: Your methods require a single proximal for M and smoothness of K; no separability of M is needed. In contrast, SPDC/coordinate PDHG often rely on separable proximals and coordinate-wise updates.
- Rates and oracle costs: You achieve linear convergence via variance reduction on general splits (including factored ones) and leverage non-uniform sampling. A succinct comparison of per-iteration cost, memory, and complexity would help.

3) The constant μ in Theorem 2: Please define μ precisely in the main text. Is μ the strong monotonicity parameter of A+B in the Ω-metric (i.e., a function of λ and γ)? Provide a direct mapping to Assumption (A) and restate Theorems 1–2 using μ (and κ=L/μ or L̄/μ) so readers can compare the SVRG (per epoch) and SAGA (per iteration) rates.

4) From (A)–(C) to monotonicity: Clarify explicitly how (A)–(B) imply that A is maximally monotone and strongly monotone (via λ,γ), B is monotone and L-Lipschitz (in the Ω/Ω* norms), and hence A+B is strongly monotone. This will make the transition to the monotone inclusion framework seamless.

Experimental suggestions
- Add comparisons against stochastic/accelerated Chambolle–Pock (primal–dual hybrid gradient) and SPDC on problems where their assumptions hold; please include Zhang & Xiao (ICML 2015) as requested.
- Expand to more datasets and provide ablations: uniform vs non-uniform sampling, factored vs individual splits, memory/runtime trade-offs, and sensitivity to λ, γ, and step sizes.
- For separable instances, include strong primal baselines (e.g., accelerated variance-reduced methods) and clarify when your saddle-point approach is preferable.

Minor comments
- Notation: Avoid calling L the “condition number.” Consider κ = L/μ (or L̄/μ) to refer to conditioning.
- Forward–Backward references: Please cite standard references for FB/PDHG and convergence rates (e.g., Rockafellar 1976; Chen & Rockafellar 1997; Chambolle–Pock 2011).
- Define all norms and constants (Ω, Ω*, L, L̄(π), μ) once near the start; ensure consistent usage thereafter.
- Typographical: Ensure consistent capitalization/hyphenation (e.g., “bi-linear” vs “bilinear”), and fix minor grammar issues in Section 2.4 and captions.

Pros for acceptance
- First linear-convergence variance-reduced methods for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems.
- Solid, general analysis via monotone operators; clean acceleration result.
- Practical guidance on non-uniform sampling and splits; promising empirical results.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity/organization issues around the monotone-operator viewpoint and constants.
- Limited empirical comparisons and datasets; missing baselines (stochastic CP/SPDC).
- Strong convexity–concavity assumption limits immediate applicability to some canonical models unless smoothed/regularized.

Overall: Strong paper with substantial theoretical and practical contributions. With improved clarity, expanded comparisons, and a brief discussion of non-strongly convex–concave cases, it would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
