- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with both individual and factored splits.  
- Provides careful, comprehensive complexity analysis under uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies.  
- Distinguishes clearly between individual vs. factored splits and between sampling strategies in the theoretical treatment.  
- Presents clear complexity comparisons for the bilinear case among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB methods, aligning with known convex minimization phenomena.  
- Empirical finding: non-uniform sampling and acceleration are important to outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
- Main concern: per-iteration proximal cost scaling as O(n + d) can negate incrementalization benefits, especially when n ≫ d and under factored splits.  
- As a result, accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling does not always improve over accelerated batch FB in key regimes (Tables 1–2).  
- Request: clarify whether factored splits are inherently worse than individual splits and identify conditions or problem classes where each is preferable.  
- Request: characterize regimes (in n, d, condition numbers, smoothness/strong-convexity constants, and structural properties) where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB.  
- Request: state whether n and d dependencies in bounds are tight, provide lower bounds or examples if loose, or explicitly call out tightness if proven.  
- Request: clarify whether O(n + d) proximal cost is unavoidable and whether structures (separable regularizers, low-rank, maintainable statistics) or prox approximations can reduce cost while retaining guarantees.  
- Experiments comment: broader empirical validation across varying n/d ratios and problems with faster prox implementations would strengthen the paper.  
- Experiments comment: report wall-clock times isolating prox cost and discuss non-uniform sampling overhead, sensitivity to misspecification, and potential adaptive schemes.  
- Overall: strong, timely, and technically careful contribution, but practical/theoretical impact is limited by proximal-cost bottleneck; clearer statements on when variance reduction wins and split-wise merits would improve the paper.
