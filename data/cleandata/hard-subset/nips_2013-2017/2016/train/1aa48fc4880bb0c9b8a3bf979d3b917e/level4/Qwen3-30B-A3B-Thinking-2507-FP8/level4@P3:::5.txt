

This paper addresses a significant gap in optimization for machine learning by extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning but have not previously been addressed with linearly convergent incremental algorithms. The authors demonstrate that while the algorithmic extensions themselves are straightforward, the convergence analysis requires fundamentally different techniques from convex minimization, as the standard convex minimization analysis does not apply. Instead, they leverage monotone operator theory to prove linear convergence, which also reveals that their algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction methods in saddle-point problems, showing that monotone operator theory provides a natural framework for this analysis. Second, it establishes that saddle-point problems admit two distinct notions of function splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives), which is a critical difference from convex minimization. Third, it demonstrates that non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency in saddle-point problems, both theoretically and empirically. Finally, it shows how these algorithms can be accelerated using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework, resulting in algorithms that consistently outperform accelerated batch methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many machine learning problems, such as robust optimization, structured prediction, and non-separable regularizers (e.g., grouped norms, total variation), naturally lead to saddle-point formulations where existing optimization techniques fail due to non-separability. The authors provide a comprehensive set of examples from machine learning (Section 2.1) demonstrating where their approach is uniquely applicable, particularly for problems where the loss or regularizer is non-separable but has an efficient proximal operator. This addresses a real gap in the literature, as previous approaches either required separability (for which standard stochastic methods apply) or were limited to batch methods with sublinear convergence rates.

The paper is technically sound (Quality), with a careful analysis of the convergence properties and thorough experimental validation. The theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence on benchmark datasets (Section 7), where the authors demonstrate that their non-uniform sampling variants significantly outperform both uniform sampling approaches and batch methods. The clarity of the paper is generally high (Clarity), with well-organized sections that carefully explain the problem formulation, algorithmic extensions, and theoretical guarantees. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce their results, though the paper would benefit from a brief proof sketch in the main text to help readers grasp the key insights of the monotone operator approach.

The originality (Originality) is strong, as the paper identifies a significant gap in the literature and provides a novel solution using monotone operator theory. The authors thoroughly compare their work to previous methods (Section 2.2-2.3), demonstrating how their approach differs from existing batch and stochastic algorithms. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) further demonstrates the broader applicability of their technique beyond saddle-point problems.

However, the paper has several minor weaknesses that would benefit from clarification. First, while the paper discusses non-uniform sampling for bilinear saddle-point problems, it does not sufficiently explain how to determine the sampling probabilities π for non-bilinear problems (e.g., for general convex-concave K(x,y)). The authors should provide more guidance on how to select π in practical settings beyond the bilinear case. Second, the paper does not explain how the step size σ is set in the experiments (beyond noting that it's chosen based on theoretical bounds), which would help practitioners implement the method. Third, the paper could provide more insight into why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems but less so for convex minimization problems. This distinction is theoretically interesting and would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The paper contains two minor typographical errors (line 78: "k" should be "d"; line 121: extra "y_{t-1}" in the second equality) that should be corrected.

In summary, this paper makes an important contribution to optimization for machine learning by providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems. The theoretical insights using monotone operator theory are novel and broadly applicable, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and technically sound, with the main weakness being insufficient guidance on implementing the method for general problems. The authors should address the questions about sampling probabilities, step size selection, and the theoretical distinction between saddle-point and convex minimization problems in the final version.

The paper's contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. It advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning by solving a problem that has been previously overlooked, and the proposed techniques have clear potential for application in a wide range of machine learning problems with non-separable loss functions or regularizers.
