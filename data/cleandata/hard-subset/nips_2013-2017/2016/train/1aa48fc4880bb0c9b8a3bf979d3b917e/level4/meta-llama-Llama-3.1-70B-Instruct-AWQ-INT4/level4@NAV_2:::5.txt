This paper proposes the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems by extending stochastic variance reduction methods, such as SVRG and SAGA. The main contribution lies in using monotone operators to prove convergence, which differs significantly from the convex minimization analysis. This approach enables the application of the analysis to a broader class of problems, including variational inequality problems.

The paper tackles non-separable loss functions and regularizers by considering the saddle-point problem, making it an important contribution to the field. The authors provide simpler proofs for algorithms like the FB splitting and its stochastic version. The proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling are competitive and often outperform previous alternatives.

The paper is well-written, but some issues require clarification. For instance, the type of non-uniform sampling used for problems other than bilinear saddle-point problems is unclear, specifically the definition of π. Additionally, the method for setting the step size σ in the experiments is not explicitly stated. The authors could provide intuition on why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization problems.

Minor corrections include a typo on line 78 (k -> d) and an extra y_{t-1} term in the second equality on line 121.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to tackling saddle-point problems, the extension of the analysis to a broader class of problems, and the provision of simpler proofs for existing algorithms. The weaknesses are the lack of clarity on certain issues, such as non-uniform sampling and step size selection, and the absence of intuition on the importance of non-uniform sampling in saddle-point problems.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems. With some revisions to address the mentioned issues, the paper has the potential to be even more impactful.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems, making a significant contribution to the field.
* The approach of using monotone operators to prove convergence is novel and has the potential to be widely applicable.
* The paper provides simpler proofs for existing algorithms and extends the analysis to a broader class of problems.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks clarity on certain issues, such as non-uniform sampling and step size selection.
* The absence of intuition on the importance of non-uniform sampling in saddle-point problems may make the paper less accessible to some readers.
* Minor corrections are needed to address typos and errors in the paper.
