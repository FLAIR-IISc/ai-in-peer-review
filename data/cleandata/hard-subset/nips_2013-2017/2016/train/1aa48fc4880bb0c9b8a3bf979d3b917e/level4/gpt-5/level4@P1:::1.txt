Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form minx maxy K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is proximable and strongly convex–concave. The key idea is to extend stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting by operating on the forward operator B(x,y)=(∂xK,−∂yK) while handling M via proximal steps. The authors analyze these methods through the lens of monotone operators rather than convex minimization, proving linear convergence. They further discuss two notions of splitting (by functions or by partial derivatives, including a “factored” split), show the importance of non-uniform sampling, and propose an acceleration via a catalyst-style proximal-point regularization. The analysis also covers broader problems such as variational inequalities.

Relative to prior work, this extends finite-sum variance reduction methods (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) beyond standard convex minimization to general saddle points with non-separable terms, and goes beyond separable primal–dual methods such as Chambolle–Pock and its stochastic/coordinate variants that rely on separability or different splitting assumptions. The paper claims the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems with the stated generality.

Strengths:
- Technical quality: The convergence analysis is careful and uses monotone operator theory in a nontrivial way to establish linear rates without relying on co-coercivity. The results extend beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.
- Generality: The framework handles non-separable losses or regularizers (as long as M is proximable), which are common and important in machine learning.
- Practical guidance: The treatment of multiple splitting schemes (including factored splits) and the role of non-uniform sampling is useful, with complexity bounds that reflect data-dependent norms (operator vs. Frobenius vs. max).
- Acceleration: The catalyst-style acceleration is simple to implement in the epoch-based SVRG structure and yields improved complexity, particularly in ill-conditioned regimes.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Presentation and clarity: The monotone operator perspective appears as an “extension” in Section 6, yet it underpins the main convergence proofs. Bringing this perspective earlier and threading it through the analysis would improve accessibility. A high-level roadmap that contrasts the convex minimization vs. saddle-point vs. monotone operator settings would help.
- Experimental evaluation: Only two datasets are considered. More datasets and ablations (e.g., uniform vs. non-uniform sampling; factored vs. individual splits; varying mini-batch size m) would strengthen the empirical case. In the separable case, please compare against accelerated primal–dual baselines, including the stochastic Chambolle–Pock variant:
  http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf
  Please also add this citation to the related work.
- Positioning vs. stochastic primal–dual methods: The paper should more clearly articulate how the proposed methods differ from stochastic primal–dual algorithms (e.g., Chambolle–Pock and stochastic/coordinate extensions). In particular, clarify the algorithmic distinctions (variance-reduced control variates vs. unbiased stochastic gradients; resolvent of M vs. separate proxes) and the theoretical regimes (assumptions on separability, smoothness, and strong convexity–concavity).

Questions and points to clarify:
1) Assumptions (A)–(C): How strong are these in practice, especially (A)? Do they hold for standard SVM formulations (e.g., hinge or squared hinge loss with regularization) in their saddle-point form? It would help to detail common cases where the required proximal operator for M is efficient.
2) Relation to stochastic primal–dual methods: Please elaborate on the differences in both algorithm design and guarantees. Under what conditions would a stochastic primal–dual method be preferable, and when does your approach provide provable or practical advantages?
3) Theorem 2: Please define the constant μ inline. Is this the strong monotonicity parameter from the appendix? How should readers compare the rate in Theorem 2 to the epoch-wise rate in Theorem 1 (e.g., in terms of L, L̄, |I|, and m)?
4) From assumptions (A)–(C) to monotonicity: The transition to the monotone operator framework in the appendix is hard to follow. Do (A)–(C) imply that A+B is strongly monotone in the (x,y) variables under the Ω-norm? A concise lemma summarizing the mapping from (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity and Lipschitz constants would clarify the connection.

Minor issues:
- Notation for L: There is some confusion between “L as condition number” and “L as a Lipschitz constant.” Please use consistent terminology and clarify whether “condition number” refers to a ratio involving L and the strong convexity–concavity parameter(s).
- Forward–Backward algorithm: Please add or clarify references for the FB step and its convergence in the saddle-point setting (e.g., Chen–Rockafellar (1997) and Chambolle–Pock (2011), which you already cite) and ensure the stated rates match the cited results.
- Experimental comparisons: In the separable setting, also report results against accelerated batch primal–dual baselines and the stochastic Chambolle–Pock variant noted above.

Pros:
- First linear-convergence variance-reduced methods tailored to general saddle-point/monotone operator settings.
- Clear guidance on non-uniform sampling and splitting strategies with improved complexity bounds.
- Catalyst-style acceleration with practical implementation.

Cons:
- Presentation could better foreground the monotone operator viewpoint.
- Limited experiments and missing comparisons to strong stochastic primal–dual baselines.
- Some notation and assumption transitions are confusing.

Overall, this is a strong and original contribution with solid theory and promising practical implications, provided the clarity and empirical comparisons are improved as suggested.
