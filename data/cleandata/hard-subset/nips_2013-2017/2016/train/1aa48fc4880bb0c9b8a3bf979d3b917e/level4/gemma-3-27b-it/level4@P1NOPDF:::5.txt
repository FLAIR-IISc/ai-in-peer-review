The authors leverage variance reduction techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, to introduce the first algorithms with linear convergence guarantees for saddle point problems. Because standard convex minimization analysis does not directly apply to saddle point problems, a key contribution of this work is framing the analysis in terms of monotone operators to establish convergence. A notable consequence is that this approach extends to a broader class of problems, including variational inequality problems. This methodology provides a pathway to address non-separable loss functions and regularizers by reformulating them as saddle point problems. The paper presents the first algorithms demonstrating a linear convergence rate for these saddle point problems—an important advancement given their prevalence in machine learning models featuring non-separable terms. The proof technique, which interprets saddle point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator, is insightful and potentially broadly applicable. Moreover, the paper offers simpler proofs for algorithms like the Forward-Backward (FB) splitting method, and its stochastic counterpart. The proposed algorithms, employing non-uniform sampling, achieve competitive performance and generally outperform existing alternatives. Overall, this work represents a significant extension to the field, and the analytical approach is particularly compelling. 

Given that the primary contribution lies in the proof technique—the algorithms themselves being relatively straightforward extensions of existing methods—the authors might consider including more details regarding the proof, perhaps a sketch, within the main paper if space allows. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, though I found certain aspects somewhat unclear. Specifically:

1. For problems beyond bilinear saddle-point problems, what form does the non-uniform sampling take, i.e., what is the distribution \pi?
2. How was the step size \sigma determined in the experiments? Furthermore, do the authors have any intuition regarding why non-uniform sampling appears so crucial for saddle point problems, but less so for convex minimization problems? A discussion addressing this point would strengthen the paper.
