This paper extends several recent incremental algorithms—SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants—to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure, arising from two types of splits: individual and factored. The authors present a thorough complexity analysis for these methods under both splits and under uniform and nonuniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a specific binary classification example show that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to surpass competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward, the results here are genuinely novel and technically nontrivial. For the important bilinear saddle-point problem, the paper provides a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms; the conclusions resemble what is known in convex minimization, and they are consistent with my understanding.

My main concern is the following. Although solving the saddle-point formulation addresses the nonseparability of the loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: an O(n + d) cost to compute the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling, as reported in Tables 1 and 2—is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch counterpart. In particular, when the number of data points n is much larger than the dimension d (as in many machine learning applications), the complexity under factored splits (the setting later used in the simulations) is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization setting, I do not see a clear, substantial theoretical advantage of variance reduction here. I encourage the authors to discuss this issue in depth and clarify:
i) Whether factored splits are (theoretically) worse than individual splits;
ii) Under what conditions accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB;
iii) Whether the dependencies on n and d are tight.
