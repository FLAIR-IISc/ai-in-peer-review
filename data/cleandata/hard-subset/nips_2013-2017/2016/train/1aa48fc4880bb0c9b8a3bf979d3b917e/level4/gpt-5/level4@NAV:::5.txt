The authors build on variance-reduction methods such as SVRG and SAGA to present what appear to be the first algorithms with linear convergence for saddle-point problems. Since standard convex minimization analyses do not directly apply in this setting, the key contribution is a convergence analysis via monotone operators. Consequently, the results extend to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The approach also enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers by formulating them as saddle-point problems. To my knowledge, these are the first saddle-point methods with provably linear rates. This is an important advance because it facilitates dealing with non-separable losses and regularizers that arise in many machine learning models. The proof strategy—casting saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator—is compelling and likely broadly applicable. The paper also provides simpler proofs for methods like FB splitting and its stochastic counterpart. Empirically, the proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling are competitive and, in most cases, outperform existing alternatives. Overall, the paper advances the field in a significant direction, and the analytical framework is attractive. Given that the primary novelty lies in the analysis (the algorithms themselves are relatively straightforward adaptations), the authors might consider adding more details about the proof approach—e.g., a proof sketch—in the main text if space permits. The paper is generally clear and well written, but a few points remain unclear. For example:
1. For problems beyond bilinear saddle-point cases, what form of non-uniform sampling is used—i.e., how is \pi defined?
2. How is the step size \sigma chosen in the experiments? Do the authors have intuition for why non-uniform sampling seems crucial for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization? A brief discussion would strengthen the paper.
