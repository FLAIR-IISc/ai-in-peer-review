This paper extends several recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a finite-sum structure. This structure can arise from two types of data splits: individual and factored. The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis for these algorithms under both individual and factored splits, as well as for uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification task demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming competitive batch algorithms.

While the extension of variance-reduced incremental algorithms to saddle-point problems may seem straightforward at a high level, the results established here are novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial. For the important case of bilinear saddle-point problems, the authors provide a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms. This comparison draws insightful parallels to what is known for the convex minimization case and is well-justified.

However, I have a major concern regarding the practical implications of the proposed methods. While the saddle-point formulation addresses the non-separability of the loss function, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to face a fundamental bottleneck: the `O(n+d)` cost of computing the proximal operator in each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity—even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2)—is not necessarily an improvement over the accelerated batch method.

In particular, for the common machine learning setting where the number of samples `n` is much larger than the feature dimension `d`, the complexity under factored splits (which are used in the experiments) is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as indicated in Table 2. This contrasts with the standard convex minimization setting, where variance reduction typically provides a clear and substantial theoretical advantage. From a theoretical standpoint, such an advantage is not apparent here for the saddle-point problem.

I would encourage the authors to provide a more in-depth discussion on this issue and to clarify the following points:

1.  Are factored splits theoretically inferior to individual splits in terms of complexity?
2.  Under what specific conditions (e.g., relating `n`, `d`, and condition numbers) does the accelerated SVRG method become favorable compared to the batch FB algorithm?
3.  How tight is the analysis regarding the dependence on `n` and `d`?
