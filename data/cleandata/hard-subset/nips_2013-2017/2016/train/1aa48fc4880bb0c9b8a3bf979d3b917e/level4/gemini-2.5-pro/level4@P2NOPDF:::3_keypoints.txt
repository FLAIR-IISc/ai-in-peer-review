- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems (individual and factored splits).  
- Provides thorough theoretical complexity analysis for both uniform and non-uniform sampling.  
- Theoretical results are novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial.  
- Presents a clear, intuitive comparison (especially for bilinear problems) among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward methods.  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beat competitive batch algorithms.  
- Major concern: proximal operator per iteration appears to cost O(n + d), creating a potential computational bottleneck.  
- That per-iteration cost can negate overall gains: accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling may not improve over accelerated batch methods (Tables 1–2).  
- Problem is pronounced for the factored-split formulation used in experiments: when n >> d, proposed methods are theoretically worse than accelerated batch forward-backward.  
- This outcome contradicts the usual expectation that variance-reduction helps in the n >> d regime, so a clear theoretical advantage of the incremental approach is not evident.  
- Requested clarification 1: Is factored split formulation theoretically inferior to individual splits, and what practical trade-offs justify its use?  
- Requested clarification 2: Precisely which regimes (relations among n, d, condition numbers) give accelerated SVRG a theoretical advantage over accelerated batch forward-backward?  
- Requested clarification 3: Are the n and d dependencies in the complexity bounds tight, or could refined analysis reveal clearer advantages for incremental methods?
