The paper "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems" proposes the first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms for saddle-point problems, extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to provide efficient solutions for this class of problems common in machine learning. The authors provide a thorough study on the complexity of these algorithms under different splits (individual or factored splits) and different sampling strategies (uniform/nonuniform). Experimentation on a special binary classification example demonstrates that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform competitive batch algorithms.

The paper's main contributions include providing the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, which differs significantly from the associated convex minimization setup. The authors also show that the saddle-point formulation allows two different notions of splits and that nonuniform sampling is key to an efficient algorithm. Additionally, they demonstrate that these incremental algorithms can be easily accelerated using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework.

The paper's strengths lie in its thorough analysis of the algorithms' complexity and its experimental validation on a real-world example. The authors also provide a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms for the important bilinear saddle-point problem. However, the reviewer raises concerns regarding the fundamental bottleneck of O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration, which may limit the overall complexity advantage of the proposed incremental algorithms.

The reviewer suggests that the authors provide an in-depth discussion on the following questions: (i) Is factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits? (ii) When is the accelerated SVRG favorable against the batch FB? (iii) Is the dependence on n, d tight enough? Overall, the paper presents a notable contribution to the field, but the reviewer's concerns highlight the need for further clarification and discussion.
