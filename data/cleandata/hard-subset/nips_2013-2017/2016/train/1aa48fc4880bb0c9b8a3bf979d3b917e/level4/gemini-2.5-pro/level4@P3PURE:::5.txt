This paper proposes the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems by extending variance reduction methods like SVRG and SAGA. The central contribution is a novel proof technique based on the theory of monotone operators, which is necessary because standard convex minimization analysis does not directly apply. This analytical framework is a significant result, as it also encompasses a broader class of problems, such as variational inequalities. The proposed methods are particularly valuable for tackling machine learning models with non-separable loss functions and regularizers, which can be framed as saddle point problems.

The paper makes several strong contributions:
*   **Significance:** It addresses the important problem of efficiently solving saddle point problems, introducing the first algorithms with a linear convergence rate. This is highly relevant for many machine learning applications involving non-separable objectives.
*   **Novel Analysis:** The proof technique, which interprets saddle point problems as finding the zeros of a monotone operator, is elegant and potentially widely applicable. As a testament to its power, this framework also provides simpler proofs for existing algorithms like Forward-Backward splitting and its stochastic variants.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The proposed algorithms, especially when combined with non-uniform sampling, are shown to be competitive and often superior to existing alternatives in the experiments.

Overall, the paper extends the field in an important direction, and the analytical approach is compelling. While the algorithms themselves are straightforward extensions of their original counterparts, the novelty and strength of the paper lie in its theoretical analysis. Given this, the authors might consider including a proof sketch in the main paper, if space permits, to better highlight this core contribution.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. However, a few points could be clarified:
1.  For problems beyond the bilinear saddle-point case, the paper is unclear about the specific non-uniform sampling distribution ($\pi$) that was used.
2.  The method for setting the step size ($\sigma$) in the experiments is not specified.
3.  The experiments suggest that non-uniform sampling is essential for good performance on saddle point problems, which is not always the case for convex minimization. The paper would benefit from a discussion on the authors' intuition for why this might be the case.
