The authors propose extending stochastic variance reduction methods, such as SVRG and SAGA, to achieve the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. A key contribution is recognizing that the standard convex minimization analysis does not directly apply to saddle-point problems and instead leveraging the theory of monotone operators to establish convergence. This broader perspective also allows the algorithms to extend to variational inequality problems. The work addresses a significant challenge – efficiently solving problems with non-separable loss functions and regularizers – and provides a valuable theoretical and algorithmic contribution. The interpretation of saddle-point problems as finding zeros of a monotone operator is a particularly interesting and potentially widely applicable technique, offering a novel perspective on convergence analysis. The paper also provides simpler proofs for established algorithms like the forward-backward splitting method and its stochastic variants. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, especially with non-uniform sampling, perform competitively and often outperform existing alternatives. Overall, this paper represents a strong extension to the field, with a compelling analytical approach.

While the algorithmic extensions are relatively straightforward, the theoretical depth and applicability of the monotone operator framework are considerable. To further enhance the paper’s impact, the authors could consider including a proof sketch—even a high-level one—within the main text, if space allows. This would make the core technical contribution more accessible. 

I do have a few questions regarding the presentation. First, for problems beyond bilinear saddle-point problems, what specific non-uniform sampling scheme (i.e., what is π?) is employed? Second, how is the step size σ determined in the experiments? Finally, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the intuition behind why non-uniform sampling appears so crucial for saddle-point problems, whereas its impact isn't always as significant in convex minimization. Exploring this difference could provide valuable insights.
