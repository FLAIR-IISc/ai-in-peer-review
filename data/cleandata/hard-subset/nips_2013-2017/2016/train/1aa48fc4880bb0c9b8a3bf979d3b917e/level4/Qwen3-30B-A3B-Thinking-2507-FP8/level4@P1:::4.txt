

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale instances of this important class of problems. The authors demonstrate that these methods, which have been highly successful for convex minimization, can be effectively adapted to saddle-point problems through careful theoretical analysis and algorithmic design.

The main contribution lies in developing a convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, which differs significantly from standard convex minimization analysis. This approach not only establishes linear convergence for saddle-point problems but also reveals that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The paper carefully addresses the challenges that arise when moving from convex minimization to saddle-point problems, including the need for convex-concave splits (as opposed to purely convex splits), the importance of non-uniform sampling, and the development of two distinct gradient operator splits (element-wise and factored) that exploit the structure of saddle-point problems.

The technical contribution is strong and well-supported. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis that carefully addresses the nuances of saddle-point problems, demonstrating how the same algorithms can be applied to both bilinear saddle-point problems (e.g., in supervised learning with non-separable losses) and more general convex-concave problems. The paper's experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of non-uniform sampling over uniform sampling and show that the proposed methods significantly outperform existing batch and stochastic approaches, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature: while variance reduction methods have been extensively studied for convex minimization, their extension to saddle-point problems has been largely unexplored. The authors successfully bridge this gap and provide both theoretical justification and empirical validation for their approach. The applications to machine learning problems with non-separable losses (e.g., structured output prediction) and non-separable regularizers (e.g., grouped norms, total variation) are particularly compelling, as these are common in practical settings where traditional methods fail.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear presentation of the problem, proposed algorithms, and theoretical results. The experimental section provides sufficient evidence to support the theoretical claims, with both synthetic and real-world examples covering a range of problem types and conditions. The authors effectively demonstrate that their algorithms outperform existing methods across multiple benchmarks, including both separable and non-separable problems.

A few minor points for improvement:
1. The paper uses "K" for both the design matrix and the smooth function K(x,y), which can be confusing. It would be clearer to use different symbols for these two distinct concepts.
2. While the paper provides a convergence proof for SVRG with acceleration, it would be valuable to explicitly state whether the same acceleration technique applies to SAGA or whether additional considerations are needed for the SAGA case.
3. The adaptive update strategy for the auxiliary iterate (x̄, ȳ) in the acceleration framework could be clarified. The paper mentions that (x̄, ȳ) is updated regularly, but the precise mechanism for adaptive update is not detailed.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical insights are sound, the algorithms are well-motivated and carefully analyzed, and the experimental results confirm the theoretical advantages. The paper's focus on saddle-point problems, which are ubiquitous in machine learning through duality, makes it highly relevant to the NIPS community. The authors have addressed a meaningful gap in the literature and provided both the theoretical foundation and practical algorithms needed to solve these problems efficiently at scale.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in optimization with direct applications to machine learning. It advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning with both theoretical and practical contributions.

Strengths: Novel extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, rigorous monotone operator-based convergence analysis, clear demonstration of applicability to a wide range of machine learning problems, strong experimental validation.

Weaknesses: Minor notational confusion between K (design matrix) and K(x,y) (smooth function), lack of explicit discussion about acceleration for SAGA, and some details about adaptive update of (x̄, ȳ) could be clarified.

I recommend acceptance.
