### Summary

This paper extends the highly successful stochastic variance reduction methods, SAGA and SVRG, from the domain of finite-sum convex minimization to the more general class of strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. While such methods have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance for standard empirical risk minimization, their application and analysis for saddle-point formulations, which are common for problems with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., structured output prediction, robust optimization), had not been established.

The authors make several key contributions. First, they provide the first linear convergence guarantees for SVRG and SAGA in this setting. A significant aspect of this contribution is the proof technique itself; departing from the analysis typical for convex minimization, the authors leverage the framework of monotone operators. This approach is more general and reveals that the algorithms are applicable to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. Second, the paper investigates practical and theoretical aspects of the extension, such as different ways to split the gradient operator ("factored" vs. "element-wise") and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal performance. Finally, the authors show that these methods can be systematically accelerated using a "catalyst"-like framework, leading to algorithms with theoretical complexities superior to their batch counterparts. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on problems with non-separable objectives, demonstrating clear performance gains over existing batch and stochastic methods.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper fills a significant gap in the literature by being the first to establish linearly convergent variance-reduced stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems. This opens the door for efficient optimization of a wide range of machine learning models that are naturally posed as saddle-point problems but were not amenable to previous fast incremental methods. The work demonstrably advances the state of the art in large-scale optimization.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The convergence analysis based on monotone operators is a major strength. It is more general than a direct extension of the existing SVRG/SAGA proofs and provides deeper insight into why these algorithms work. This theoretical foundation strengthens the paper and broadens its applicability beyond the specific focus on saddle-point problems.

3.  **Thorough and Comprehensive Treatment:** The authors provide a detailed analysis, considering multiple facets of the problem. They explore different operator splitting strategies, rigorously analyze the benefits of non-uniform sampling (showing it is provably superior), and develop a simple yet effective acceleration scheme. This level of detail is commendable.

4.  **Clear Practical Implications and Strong Empirical Support:** The proposed methods address a real need for problems involving non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization surrogates) or regularizers (e.g., total variation or overlapping group norms). The experiments, both in the main text and appendix, are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) over existing batch, accelerated batch, and standard stochastic algorithms.

### Weaknesses

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for improvement rather than significant flaws.

1.  **Clarity on Adaptive Acceleration:** In Section 5, the acceleration is achieved by adding a proximal term centered at an iterate `(\bar{x}, \bar{y})`. The paper mentions that one can "adaptively update `(\bar{x}, \bar{y})` more aggressively to speed-up the algorithm." However, the details of this adaptive strategy are not provided. A brief explanation of how this might be done would improve the practical guidance for implementing the accelerated version.

2.  **Acceleration Analysis for SAGA:** The theoretical analysis and proof for the acceleration scheme are provided for SVRG. The paper states that "the same scheme should work for SAGA," which is a reasonable claim. However, for completeness, it would be beneficial to include a more formal statement or a brief argument explaining why the analysis readily extends to SAGA.

3.  **Minor Notational Ambiguity:** The symbol `K` is used to denote both the smooth function `K(x,y)` in the saddle-point formulation and the design matrix `K` in the bilinear examples. While context makes the meaning clear, using a different symbol (e.g., `\mathcal{K}(x,y)`) for the function would slightly improve readability and prevent any potential confusion.

### Detailed Comments on Review Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs, and the experimental methodology is sound. The work is complete and presents a self-contained, significant contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the algorithms are presented cleanly, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The connection to the broader class of monotone operators is explained well. The minor suggestions above could further enhance clarity.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. It is the first to provide a rigorous analysis of variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems with linear convergence rates. The analytical approach using monotone operators is also novel in this context.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. They provide practitioners and researchers with a new class of powerful, state-of-the-art algorithms for a broad set of important optimization problems in machine learning that were previously intractable for fast stochastic methods. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon in future research.
