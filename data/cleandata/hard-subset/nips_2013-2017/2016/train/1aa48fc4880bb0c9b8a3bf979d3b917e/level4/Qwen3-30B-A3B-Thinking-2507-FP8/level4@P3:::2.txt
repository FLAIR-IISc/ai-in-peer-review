

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors make several key contributions: (1) a convergence analysis using monotone operators that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, (2) clarification of two distinct notions of splits (function-based vs. derivative-based), (3) demonstration of the necessity of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, and (4) an acceleration technique using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound and carefully developed, with appropriate use of monotone operator theory to address the challenges of saddle-point problems (where standard convex minimization techniques do not apply). The convergence proofs are rigorous, and the authors correctly identify the differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization. The paper also provides clear complexity bounds that properly account for different sampling schemes, with the important insight that non-uniform sampling leads to superior theoretical guarantees and practical performance. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly significant, as it demonstrates the broader applicability of the approach beyond saddle-point problems.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors clearly define the problem setting, make precise assumptions, and systematically present the algorithmic extensions. The distinction between function-based and derivative-based splits is particularly well-explained, with concrete examples (such as the bilinear saddle-point problem) that help readers understand the practical implications. The notation is consistent throughout, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The experimental section (Section 7) is well-structured and provides meaningful comparisons with existing methods.

The originality of this work is substantial. While stochastic variance reduction methods have been extensively studied for convex minimization, their application to saddle-point problems represents a significant gap in the literature. The paper effectively demonstrates that the saddle-point structure requires different considerations than convex minimization (particularly regarding the split structure and sampling strategies), and the authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting. The extension to monotone operators further broadens the scope of the work, showing it applies to a wider class of problems than just saddle-point problems. The paper properly contextualizes these contributions against previous work, including a careful comparison with existing batch and stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems.

The significance of this work is high. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., in supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning), yet there has been a lack of scalable, linearly convergent algorithms for these problems. The proposed methods fill this important gap, providing both theoretical guarantees and practical improvements over existing approaches. The acceleration technique via the catalyst framework is particularly valuable, as it leads to algorithms that are always superior to accelerated batch methods. The experimental results confirm the theoretical advantages, with accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling showing significant improvements over existing methods, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper has two minor weaknesses that could be addressed. First, the strong convexity assumption is stated as requiring both the primal and dual problems to be strongly convex. However, for saddle-point problems, it is typically sufficient for only one of the two (primal or dual) to be strongly convex. This clarification would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Second, while the experiments on the sido and rcv1 datasets are informative, additional numerical examples with different problem structures (e.g., involving different types of non-separable regularizers or loss functions) would further demonstrate the generality of the approach.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The technical quality is excellent, the writing is clear, the novelty is substantial, and the significance is high. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

Pro: 
- First linearly convergent stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems
- Rigorous convergence analysis using monotone operators
- Clear distinction between function-based and derivative-based splits
- Practical demonstration of non-uniform sampling benefits
- Effective acceleration via catalyst framework

Con:
- Strong convexity assumption should clarify whether both primal and dual need strong convexity
- Additional numerical examples would further strengthen empirical claims

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical applicability of optimization methods for machine learning.
