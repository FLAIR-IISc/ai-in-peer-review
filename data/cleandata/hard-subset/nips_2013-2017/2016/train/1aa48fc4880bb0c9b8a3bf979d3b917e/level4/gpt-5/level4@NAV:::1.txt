This paper investigates convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors demonstrate how to handle these problems by leveraging existing stochastic variance-reduction methods (e.g., SVRG and SAGA) combined with a proximal operator. They analyze these algorithms within the monotone operator framework and establish linear convergence. They also introduce an accelerated version and examine a non-uniform sampling scheme.

Novelty/originality: The contributions are substantial. The theoretical analysis is careful and yields new insights for saddle-point problems. The paper presents several extensions, the most important of which is that the method applies to non-separable functions.

Technical quality: While I appreciate the theoretical advances, I was disappointed with aspects of the presentation. The link to monotone operators should be made more explicit. Although framed as an extension in Section 6, the analysis itself is built on monotone operators. On the empirical side, results on more than two datasets would strengthen the paper. For the separable case, the authors should also compare against accelerated methods such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle–Pock algorithm: http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf Please include this reference.

Points for clarification:
1. How strong are assumptions (A)–(C), especially (A)? For instance, do these assumptions hold for the saddle-point formulation of SVM?
2. I am not fully clear on the differences between the stochastic primal–dual method and the proposed approach. Could the authors elaborate?
3. In Theorem 2, I am unsure about the constant μ. Is it the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix? How should this result be compared with Theorem 1?
4. The transition from assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is unclear. Do (A)–(C) imply monotonicity?

Minor issues:
- There appears to be a confusion between using L as the condition number and as the Lipschitz constant: “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” and “we need the Lipschitz constant L.”
- Forward–Backward algorithm: please add a citation.
