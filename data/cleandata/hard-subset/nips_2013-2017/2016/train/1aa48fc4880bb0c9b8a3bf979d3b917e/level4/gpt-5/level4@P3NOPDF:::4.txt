Summary
The paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG and SAGA) to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems and establishes linear convergence. The framework covers a broad class of non-separable problems, including structured prediction losses and non-separable regularizers (e.g., fusion). The analysis leverages monotone operator theory to handle the saddle-point setting and indicates applicability beyond optimization to certain variational inequalities. The authors also study two stochastic gradient-splitting strategies (element-wise and factored) within stochastic forward–backward algorithms, and analyze non-uniform sampling. Experiments in the paper and appendix support the theory and show clear improvements over uniform sampling and non-variance-reduced baselines.

Strengths
- Clear and significant extension of SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with linear convergence guarantees.
- Use of monotone operator theory is technically sound and well-motivated for saddle-point analysis; it clarifies why standard convex minimization proofs do not directly transfer.
- Ability to handle non-separable losses and regularizers is practically important and broadens applicability.
- Consideration of both element-wise and factored operator splits provides useful algorithmic design choices.
- Non-uniform sampling is treated both theoretically and empirically, with consistent improvements demonstrated.
- Empirical results are thorough and align well with the theoretical predictions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Acceleration: Section 5 adapts an additional regularization term (following [8]) to “push” iterates towards a reference point (x̄, ȳ). It remains unclear how to choose and update this reference adaptively in practice. Please provide an explicit policy (e.g., scheduled refresh, adaptive criterion) and sensitivity analysis. Is there guidance for tuning the additional regularization strength versus the base step sizes?
- Scope of theory for SAGA: Convergence and acceleration proofs are presented for SVRG. Do analogous linear-convergence and acceleration guarantees hold for SAGA under the same assumptions? If not, what obstacles prevent a parallel analysis, and what partial results can be stated?
- Split strategies: The paper introduces element-wise and factored splits, seemingly pairing one with SVRG and the other with SAGA. Are these choices interchangeable? Under what conditions (e.g., structure of the loss/regularizer, data sparsity, operator Lipschitz constants) is one split preferable? Some empirical ablations comparing splits across both algorithms would be informative.
- Assumptions and generality: The results hinge on strong convexity–concavity. Can the approach or analysis be extended to merely convex–concave settings (e.g., sublinear rates with variance reduction)? A short discussion of limitations and potential extensions would strengthen the paper.
- Practical guidance on non-uniform sampling: How are sampling probabilities selected in practice (e.g., proportional to Lipschitz constants or empirical variance estimates)? What is the cost of estimating these quantities, and how sensitive are the gains to misspecification?
- Proximal operators for non-separable regularizers: The methods rely on forward–backward splitting. Please clarify the required proximal or resolvent computations for the non-separable regularizers considered (e.g., fusion). Are these proximals efficient and scalable, and are any approximations used in experiments?
- Complexity and memory: Variance-reduced methods often trade computation for reduced variance. What are the per-iteration costs and memory overheads for the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants in the saddle-point setting, especially with non-separable terms? A concise complexity table and wall-clock comparisons would be helpful.

Minor comments and clarity
- Notation: The paper uses K both as a smooth function K(·) and a design matrix K. Please disambiguate with distinct symbols.
- Acceleration details: Clearly state how (x̄, ȳ) are initialized, updated, and how often they are refreshed, including any restart or averaging schemes.
- Variational inequalities: Since the analysis extends to variational inequalities in game theory, a brief example or discussion of how to instantiate the method in that context would make the contribution more concrete.
- Reporting: Where possible, include wall-clock time, variance across runs, and sensitivity to step-size and sampling strategies to complement iteration-based plots.
- Writing: A quick pass to remove minor repetitions (e.g., repeated problem statement in the introduction) would improve readability.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances variance reduction for saddle-point problems with solid theory and convincing experiments. The monotone operator perspective is elegant and likely to influence future work. Addressing the practical aspects of acceleration (adaptive anchor updates), clarifying the scope of SAGA theory, and refining notation would further strengthen the paper. Subject to these clarifications, I view the work as a valuable addition to the literature on stochastic saddle-point optimization.
