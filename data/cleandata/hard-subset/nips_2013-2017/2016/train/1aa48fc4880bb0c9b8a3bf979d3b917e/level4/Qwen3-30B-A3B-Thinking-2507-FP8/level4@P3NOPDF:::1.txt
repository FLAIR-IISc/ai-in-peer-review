

This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems using stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG/SAGA) combined with proximal operators, establishing linear convergence in the monotone operator framework. The authors further propose an accelerated variant and analyze non-uniform sampling, with the most significant contribution being applicability to *non-separable* objective functions—a notable advancement over prior work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and reveals novel insights into saddle-point optimization, making the core contributions substantive and well-motivated.

The paper’s primary weakness lies in its presentation. The foundational reliance on monotone operator theory is not adequately introduced, creating a disconnect between the technical core (Section 2–4) and the later "extension" (Section 6). The analysis assumes monotonicity from the outset, yet the connection to monotone operators is only briefly acknowledged in Section 6. To improve accessibility, the authors should explicitly frame the problem within monotone operator theory early on (e.g., in Section 2), clarifying how the saddle-point formulation maps to this framework. This would strengthen the theoretical narrative and align the presentation with the analysis.

On experiments, the evaluation is limited to two datasets, which is insufficient to demonstrate robustness. The authors should include results on at least one additional benchmark (e.g., a larger-scale or more diverse dataset) to support their claims. Additionally, for separable objectives, the comparison to accelerated methods is incomplete. The authors must include a comparison with the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang et al., JMLR 2015), which is a standard benchmark for such problems. This reference should be added to the related work.

Several technical points require clarification:  
1. **Assumptions A–C**: The strength of these assumptions, particularly (A), needs justification. The authors should verify whether they hold for the SVM saddle-point problem (a standard test case), as this would validate the practical relevance of their framework.  
2. **Stochastic primal-dual vs. proposed method**: The relationship between the authors’ approach and existing stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., Chen et al., ICML 2015) is unclear. A concise comparison (e.g., in Section 3) highlighting differences in convergence guarantees, assumptions, and applicability to non-separable functions would be valuable.  
3. **Constant μ in Theorem 2**: The constant μ is referenced as the "monotonicity constant" in Appendix B, but its role relative to Theorem 1’s rate (which depends on strong convexity) is ambiguous. The authors should explicitly define μ and clarify how Theorem 2’s rate compares to Theorem 1’s, especially regarding the interplay between strong monotonicity and problem structure.  
4. **Assumptions A–C → Strong monotonicity**: The transition from Assumptions A–C to strong monotonicity in Appendix B is confusing. Do these assumptions *imply* strong monotonicity, or is this an additional requirement? A brief lemma or statement clarifying this logical dependency is needed.

Minor issues include:  
- **Confusion over constant L**: The text states "L represents the condition number" (Section 2) but later refers to "the Lipschitz constant L" (e.g., Section 3). These are distinct quantities; the authors must consistently use "κ" for condition number and "L" for Lipschitz constant.  
- **Forward-Backward algorithm**: A reference (e.g., Combettes & Pesquet, 2011) should be added when introducing the Forward-Backward method in Section 4.  

Overall, the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical potential. The core innovation—handling non-separable functions via monotone operator theory—is compelling and well-justified. However, the current presentation obscures this contribution by delaying the monotone operator context until Section 6, and the experimental validation is underdeveloped. Addressing the above points would significantly elevate the paper’s impact and accessibility.  

**Recommendation**: **Major Revision**. The theoretical results are sound and novel, but the presentation and experimental scope require substantial improvement to meet NIPS standards. The authors should: (1) restructure the presentation to integrate monotone operator theory early; (2) expand experiments to ≥3 datasets and include comparisons to Zhang et al. (2015); (3) clarify all technical points (assumptions, μ, L) as outlined; and (4) fix minor notational inconsistencies. With these revisions, this paper has strong potential for publication.
