The paper extends several recent incremental algorithms, including SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite sum structure. These problems can arise from two types of splits: individual or factored splits. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies, including uniform and nonuniform sampling. Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial in outperforming competitive batch algorithms. Although the idea of extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems may seem straightforward, the results presented here are notably novel and technically non-trivial. The authors also provide a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms for the important bilinear saddle point problem, which shares some resemblance to the convex minimization case.

However, my major concern is that while solving the saddle point problem alleviates the difficulty of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear to suffer from a fundamental bottleneck: the O(n+d) cost of computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2), is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version. This is particularly concerning when the number of data points n is substantially larger than the dimension d, which is often the case in machine learning applications. Under factored splits (later used in simulations), the complexity is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2. Unlike the convex minimization case, I did not see a clear substantial advantage of using variance reduction from a theoretical perspective. I suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions: i) Is factored splitting (theoretically) worse than individual splitting? ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG favorable against batch FB? iii) Is the dependence on n and d tight enough?
