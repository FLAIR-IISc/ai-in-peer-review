- Main contribution: Proposes the first large-scale, linearly convergent stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG/SAGA) for convex-concave saddle-point problems and related variational inequalities.

- Core analysis: Frames convergence proofs using monotone operator theory, yielding linear rates and broader applicability beyond convex minimization.

- Practical impact: Enables variance-reduced methods for non-separable losses/regularizers (e.g., AUC, total variation) where prior fast stochastic methods fail.

- Theoretical rigor: Comprehensive, rigorous analysis showing co-coercivity is not required and providing a generalizable framework.

- Novel insights: Examines functional vs. partial-derivative splitting and demonstrates the practical/theoretical benefits of non-uniform sampling.

- Acceleration: Integrates the Catalyst framework to obtain an accelerated variant with improved performance on ill-conditioned problems.

- Presentation weakness: Monotone operator framework is relegated to a late section/appendix, obscuring the paper’s foundational analysis and confusing the structure.

- Experimental weakness: Empirical evaluation is limited to only two datasets, reducing evidence of broad practical superiority.

- Missing baselines: Lacks comparisons to relevant stochastic saddle-point methods (e.g., stochastic Chambolle–Pock variants such as Zhang & Xiao 2015).

- Question on assumptions: Requests clarification on how restrictive Assumptions (A)–(C) are in practice, especially the strong convex-concave requirement.

- Question on positioning: Asks for clearer differentiation from other stochastic primal–dual algorithms beyond batch/SGD-style methods.

- Question on Theorem 2: Requests definition of μ in the main text and a direct comparison of convergence rates between Theorem 1 (SVRG) and Theorem 2 (SAGA).

- Question on theory link: Wants an explicit derivation showing how Assumptions (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity of operator A+B used in proofs.

- Minor issue — notation: The constant L is used ambiguously as both a condition number and a Lipschitz constant; clarify definition/usage.

- Minor issue — reference: Add a citation for the Forward–Backward algorithm in Section 2.2.

- Overall judgment: Strong theoretical contribution with presentation and experimental gaps; recommended for acceptance.
