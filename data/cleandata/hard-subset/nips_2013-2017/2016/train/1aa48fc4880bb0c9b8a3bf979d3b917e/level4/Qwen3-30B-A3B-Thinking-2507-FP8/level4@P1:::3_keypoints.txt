- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated methods to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with two split types (function-based and partial-derivative-based).  
- Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis across splits and sampling strategies, showing non-uniform sampling is crucial for guarantees and performance.  
- Uses monotone operator theory to establish linear convergence rates, extending the framework beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.  
- Demonstrates acceleration via Catalyst and variance reduction consistently outperform accelerated batch methods in experiments.  
- Delivers the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced algorithms on saddle-point problems without relying on convex minimization techniques.  
- Carefully distinguishes split types and shows saddle-point problems require convex-concave term splits, unlike convex minimization.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is essential both theoretically and practically.  
- Technical quality is high: rigorous convergence proofs, thorough complexity analysis across splits, sampling, and acceleration.  
- Experiments (binary classification with non-separable loss/regularizer) empirically confirm benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, especially for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some technical details could be made more accessible.  
- Originality is strong due to novel use of monotone operators and conceptual advances in split distinctions.  
- Significance is substantial: first linearly convergent algorithms for non-separable large-scale saddle-point problems with practical implications.  
- Main theoretical concern: complexity for factored splits may be worse when n >> d; accelerated SVRG under factored splits has O(n + d) complexity that may not beat batch methods.  
- Requests for clarification: (i) are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits when n >> d, (ii) when does accelerated SVRG provably outperform batch methods, and (iii) are the n and d complexity bounds tight or improvable?  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address the complexity/clarification concerns.
