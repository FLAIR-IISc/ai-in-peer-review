This work generalizes a suite of recent incremental algorithms—namely SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst—to the setting of strongly convex-concave saddle point problems that exhibit a finite-sum structure. This structure can be derived from two types of formulations: individual splits or factored splits. The authors present a comprehensive complexity analysis of these methods under the different splits and for both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. The experimental results, based on a specific binary classification task, highlight that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are essential for outperforming competing batch algorithms.

While the core idea of adapting variance reduction and incremental methods for saddle point problems may appear intuitive, the established results are notably novel and the technical derivations are far from trivial. For the important subclass of bilinear saddle point problems, the paper offers a clear comparison across batch, stochastic, and incremental Fenchel-Brouwer (FB) algorithms, which aligns well with the known trade-offs in the convex minimization case and appears entirely sound.

However, I have one major reservation. Although solving the saddle point formulation addresses the issue of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms seem to face an inherent computational bottleneck: the `O(n+d)` cost required to compute the proximal operator at every iteration. As a result, the overall complexity—even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling presented in Tables 1 and 2—is not consistently superior to its accelerated batch counterpart. This is especially pronounced when the number of data points `n` is substantially greater than the feature dimension `d`, which is typical for many machine learning problems. In this regime, the complexity under factored splits (as used in the simulations) is demonstrably worse than that of the batch FB algorithms, according to Table 2. Unlike in standard convex minimization, a clear and substantial theoretical advantage for using variance reduction is not apparent here.

I suggest that the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions:
i) Are factored splits theoretically inferior to individual splits?
ii) Under what conditions is the accelerated SVRG algorithm more favorable than the batch FB method?
iii) Are the complexity dependencies on `n` and `d` considered to be tight?
