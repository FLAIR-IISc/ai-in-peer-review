Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where M is proximable and strongly convex–concave and K is smooth. It extends stochastic variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) from convex minimization to this saddle-point setting by casting the problem as finding a zero of a strongly monotone operator A + B, and applying forward–backward splitting with variance-reduced estimates of B. The analysis yields linear convergence under standard smoothness and strong convex–concavity assumptions. The paper further (i) distinguishes two natural decompositions (by functions vs. by partial derivatives), (ii) shows that non-uniform sampling is crucial for tight bounds and good practice, and (iii) provides an acceleration via a catalyst-like proximal-point scheme. The framework also extends beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.

Relative to prior work, the paper builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization and on primal–dual methods such as Chambolle–Pock and its stochastic/coordinate variants (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015; Zhu & Storkey, ECML 2015). Unlike stochastic primal–dual methods that often rely on separability of the loss/regularizer or coordinate-friendly proximal operators, the proposed algorithms target non-separable K and M while retaining linear convergence and variance reduction.

Strengths:
- Originality: Provides, to my knowledge, the first linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms tailored to general convex–concave saddle-point problems with non-separable structure, via a monotone operator view. The extension to variational inequalities broadens applicability.
- Technical quality: The convergence proofs are careful and leverage strong monotonicity rather than co-coercivity, clarifying why the analysis departs from convex minimization. The sampling analysis (uniform vs. non-uniform, factored vs. element-wise splits) is insightful and yields practically relevant guidance.
- Significance: Addresses important large-scale problems beyond separable settings (e.g., non-separable regularizers/losses). The acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst scheme is simple and effective.
- Practical aspects: Clear discussion of storage/time trade-offs (SVRG vs. SAGA; factored vs. individual splits) and the benefits of non-uniform sampling.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Clarity and organization: The monotone-operator framework underpins the analysis but is presented as a later “extension.” Bringing that perspective earlier and unifying the narrative around A+B would improve readability. Some notation is overloaded (L as a Lipschitz constant vs. “condition number”); please standardize.
- Assumptions: The strength and scope of assumptions (A)–(C) should be discussed more concretely, with canonical examples and counterexamples. In particular, clarify when common models (e.g., SVMs, logistic regression with bias terms, group norms, TV) satisfy strong convex–concavity and proximability in your formulation.
- Baselines: Experiments are limited to two datasets and omit comparisons to strong stochastic primal–dual baselines. Please compare to accelerated/practical variants of Chambolle–Pock and related methods, e.g., Zhang & Xiao (JMLR 2015, SPDC; link provided), and Zhu & Storkey (ECML 2015), especially in separable cases.
- Experimental breadth: More datasets and ablations (effect of non-uniform vs. uniform sampling, factored vs. element-wise splits, mini-batch size, and the impact of acceleration across condition numbers) would strengthen the empirical case.
- Practical guidance: Elaborate on how to choose λ and γ (scaling), estimate L and L̄(π), and construct non-uniform sampling efficiently in practice (precomputation costs, updates).

Specific questions for the authors:
1) Assumptions (A)–(C): How strong are they in practice? For instance, for SVMs with ℓ2-regularization and hinge loss (or smoothed hinge), do M and K satisfy strong convex–concavity with an efficiently computable prox? If hinge loss is handled via the conjugate, what γ results, and is strong concavity guaranteed?
2) Relationship to stochastic primal–dual methods: Please clarify conceptual and practical differences from SPDC and stochastic/coordinate variants of Chambolle–Pock (e.g., requirements on separability, proximal oracles, variance reduction vs. coordinate sampling, and convergence rates/complexities).
3) Theorem 2: Define μ explicitly in the main text. Is μ the strong monotonicity parameter of A+B (e.g., min{λ,γ} under your norm)? How should a reader compare Theorem 2’s rate and constants with Theorem 1 (SVRG)—in which regimes does each dominate?
4) Monotone-operator transition: Make explicit how (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity of A and B under Ω and Ω*. A concise lemma connecting the saddle-point assumptions to the operator properties would remove ambiguity.
5) Resampling in SAGA: Your analysis for non-uniform sampling requires resampling after the update. How sensitive is performance to omitting this step in practice? Any guidance on mixing uniform and non-uniform sampling?
6) Storage and splits: For SAGA, could you quantify memory and update-time differences between factored and element-wise splits on large sparse K, and discuss when each is preferable?

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general extension of variance-reduced methods to saddle points with linear convergence and clear theoretical insights.
- Practical acceleration via a simple catalyst-style scheme.
- Useful guidance on non-uniform sampling and problem decompositions.
- Potentially broad impact (variational inequalities, robust optimization, structured prediction).

Cons for acceptance:
- Presentation could be clearer, especially the central role of monotone operators and consistent notation.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comparisons to strong stochastic primal–dual baselines.
- Assumptions and constants (μ, L vs. L̄) need clearer definitions and practical estimation advice.

Minor issues:
- Notation: Avoid overloading L; if you use L as a Lipschitz constant, do not call it a “condition number.” If you wish to discuss a condition number, define it separately (e.g., L/μ).
- Forward–Backward references: Please cite Rockafellar (1976) on the proximal point algorithm and standard FB/PDHG references (e.g., Chambolle–Pock, 2011; Chen & Rockafellar, 1997).
- Add the requested SPDC reference: Zhang & Xiao, JMLR 2015 (http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf).
- Define all constants (μ, L̄(π), Li) in the main text, and ensure consistency between main text and appendix.
- Clarify initialization costs and benefits (e.g., (x0,y0)=(0,0) for bilinear cases) and how to set mini-batch sizes.

Overall evaluation:
A strong and timely theoretical contribution with promising practical implications. With improved exposition and stronger empirical comparisons to stochastic primal–dual methods, this paper would be a compelling addition to NIPS.
