This paper presents the first linearly convergent algorithms for solving saddle point problems, leveraging variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA. A key contribution lies in adapting the analysis of these algorithms to the non-convex landscape of saddle point problems. Traditional convex minimization analysis is not directly applicable; the authors circumvent this by framing saddle point problems as equivalent to finding zeros of monotone operators, a powerful and potentially broadly applicable technique. This framing also extends the applicability of their analysis to variational inequality problems.

The significance of this work stems from the prevalence of saddle point problems in modern machine learning, particularly when dealing with non-separable loss functions and regularizers. Existing methods often struggle with slow convergence in these settings. By achieving linear convergence, this paper offers a substantial improvement and provides a practical pathway to address these challenges. Furthermore, the authors provide simplified proofs for foundational algorithms like Fenchel-splitting (FB) and its stochastic variant, enhancing our understanding of these techniques.

Empirically, the proposed algorithms, utilizing non-uniform sampling strategies, demonstrate competitive performance – and frequently outperform – existing approaches. Overall, this work represents a valuable extension to the field, presenting both a novel analytical framework and practical algorithmic advancements.

The strength of this paper resides primarily in its theoretical contribution – the insightful reformulation of saddle point problems through the lens of monotone operators. While the algorithms themselves build upon established variance reduction methods, this novel analytical perspective is what truly distinguishes the work. To further enhance the paper’s impact, the authors could consider incorporating a proof sketch, perhaps in the appendix, to illustrate the core ideas behind this key technique. This would aid readers in grasping the subtleties of the analysis.

I do have a few points that would benefit from clarification. First, for problems beyond bilinear saddle points, the specific form of the non-uniform sampling distribution, denoted as π, could be explicitly described. Second, the manuscript lacks detail regarding the selection of the step size σ in the experimental setup. Finally, the authors might benefit from discussing *why* non-uniform sampling appears so crucial for achieving accelerated convergence in saddle point problems, whereas it’s less critical for standard convex minimization. Providing intuition on this distinction would strengthen the paper’s insights. 

Minor issues include: a typo on line 78 where ‘k’ should be ‘d,’ and an extraneous  ‘y_{t-1}’ term in the second equality on line 121.
