- Studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with M proximable and strongly convex–concave and K smooth, extending SVRG/SAGA via monotone-operator forward–backward splitting with variance reduction.  
- Provides linear convergence analysis under standard smoothness and strong convex–concavity, and extends framework to variational inequalities.  
- Distinguishes two decompositions (by function vs. by partial derivatives) and shows non-uniform sampling is important for tight bounds and practice.  
- Proposes a simple catalyst-like proximal-point acceleration for the saddle-point methods.  
- Novelty: first linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms for general non-separable convex–concave saddle-point problems via a monotone-operator perspective.  
- Technical quality: careful convergence proofs using strong monotonicity rather than co-coercivity, with insightful sampling analysis (uniform vs non-uniform, factored vs element-wise).  
- Significance: addresses large-scale problems beyond separable settings (non-separable regularizers/losses) with practical acceleration.  
- Practical aspects: clear discussion of storage/time trade-offs (SVRG vs SAGA; factored vs individual splits) and benefits of non-uniform sampling.  
- Clarity/organization issue: monotone-operator framework is presented late; recommend bringing A+B perspective earlier and standardizing overloaded notation (e.g., L).  
- Assumptions (A)–(C) need clearer scope with canonical examples/counterexamples and concrete discussion of when common models satisfy them.  
- Baselines: experiments too limited—need comparisons to strong stochastic primal–dual baselines (e.g., SPDC, stochastic/coordinate Chambolle–Pock variants).  
- Experimental breadth: add more datasets and ablations (uniform vs non-uniform sampling, factored vs element-wise splits, mini-batch size, acceleration effects across condition numbers).  
- Practical guidance lacking: give advice on choosing λ and γ, estimating L and L̄(π), and efficiently constructing non-uniform sampling (costs and updates).  
- Specific question 1: Clarify strength of (A)–(C) in practice—do SVMs with ℓ2-regularization and hinge/smoothed hinge loss satisfy strong convex–concavity and have efficiently computable prox?  
- Specific question 2: Clarify conceptual/practical differences from SPDC and stochastic/coordinate Chambolle–Pock (separability requirements, proximal oracles, variance reduction vs coordinate sampling, rates).  
- Specific question 3: Define μ in main text (is it strong monotonicity of A+B, e.g., min{λ,γ} under your norm?) and compare Theorem 2’s rate/constants with Theorem 1 to state when each dominates.  
- Specific question 4: Provide a concise lemma showing how (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity of A and B under Ω and Ω* to remove ambiguity.  
- Specific question 5: For SAGA non-uniform sampling, how sensitive is performance to omitting resampling after updates and any guidance on mixing uniform/non-uniform sampling?  
- Specific question 6: Quantify memory and update-time differences between factored and element-wise splits for SAGA on large sparse K and when each split is preferable.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel generalization of variance reduction to saddle points with linear rates, simple effective catalyst acceleration, useful non-uniform sampling guidance, and broad potential impact.  
- Cons for acceptance: presentation needs clarity around monotone operators and notation, experimental evaluation is limited and misses strong baselines, and assumptions/constants need clearer definitions and estimation advice.  
- Minor issue: avoid overloading L; if L is Lipschitz constant, define condition number separately (e.g., L/μ).  
- Minor issue: add forward–backward/proximal point references (Rockafellar 1976; Chambolle–Pock 2011; Chen & Rockafellar 1997) and include Zhang & Xiao (JMLR 2015) SPDC reference.  
- Minor issue: define all constants (μ, L̄(π), Li) in main text and ensure consistency with appendix; clarify initialization costs/benefits and mini-batch sizing.  
- Overall evaluation: strong and timely theoretical contribution with promising practical implications; requires improved exposition and stronger empirical comparisons to be a compelling NeurIPS paper.
