This paper extends recent stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants using the Catalyst framework) to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a finite sum structure, arising from either individual or factored splits. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the algorithms' complexity under various split types and sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification task demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While the extension of variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle-point problems may appear straightforward, the results presented are genuinely novel and technically demanding. For bilinear saddle-point problems, the authors provide a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, exhibiting parallels to known results in convex minimization, and the analysis seems sound.

However, a central concern is the potential computational bottleneck of the proximal operator, requiring O(n+d) operations per iteration. Consequently, even the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, as indicated in Tables 1 and 2, doesn't always demonstrate a clear advantage over accelerated batch methods. Specifically, when the number of data points (n) significantly exceeds the dimensionality (d)—a common scenario in machine learning—the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulations) can be *worse* than that of batch FB algorithms, as Table 2 suggests. Unlike the well-established benefits in convex minimization, the theoretical advantages of variance reduction in this saddle-point setting are less apparent.

I recommend the authors provide a more detailed discussion addressing the following points: 

i) Is a factored split (theoretically) less efficient than an individual split?
ii) Under what conditions is the accelerated SVRG truly favorable compared to the batch FB algorithm?
iii) How tight are the complexity bounds presented? Are there scenarios where the constants hidden within the Big-O notation might significantly impact performance?

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extends powerful variance reduction techniques to a challenging problem setting (saddle-point problems).
*   **Thoroughness:** Provides a detailed complexity analysis considering different split types and sampling strategies.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis, utilizing monotone operator theory, is well-motivated and appears sound.  The extension beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities is a valuable contribution.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Demonstrates the practical benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the technical details accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proximal Operator Bottleneck:** The O(n+d) cost of the proximal operator potentially limits the advantages of the proposed algorithms, especially when n >> d.
*   **Limited Theoretical Advantage:** The theoretical gains over batch methods are not consistently clear, particularly concerning the dependency on n and d.
*   **Constant Factors:** The impact of hidden constant factors within the complexity bounds isn't fully discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning (saddle-point optimization).
*   It offers a novel and theoretically well-supported extension of existing algorithms.
*   The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The potential bottleneck of the proximal operator and the lack of consistently clear theoretical advantages raise concerns about the practical impact of the proposed algorithms in many real-world scenarios.
*   The dependency on problem parameters (n, d) warrants further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field. However, the limitations related to the proximal operator cost and the unclear theoretical advantage over batch methods prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.  A more in-depth discussion of these limitations and potential mitigations would significantly strengthen the paper. I currently score it as a **weak accept**. The authors have provided a substantial amount of work, and the results are interesting, but I’m not convinced the practical benefits will be substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS.
