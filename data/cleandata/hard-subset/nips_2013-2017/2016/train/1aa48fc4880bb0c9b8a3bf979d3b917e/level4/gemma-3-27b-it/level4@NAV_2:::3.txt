The paper extends recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via the Catalyst framework – to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite sum structure, arising from two types of splits: individual or factored. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the complexity of these algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform/non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental algorithms to saddle point problems may appear straightforward, the results presented are demonstrably novel and technically challenging. For the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, the authors provide a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, mirroring established results for convex minimization.

However, a major concern arises regarding the computational cost of the proximal operator. Each iteration requires computing prox<sub>M</sub>, which has a cost of O(n+d). Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Table 1), may not be superior to the accelerated batch version. Specifically, when the number of data points *n* is significantly larger than the dimension *d* – a common scenario in machine learning – the complexity using factored splits (employed in the simulation) can be worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as indicated in Table 2.  Unlike typical findings for convex minimization, I do not observe a clear, substantial theoretical advantage to using variance reduction in this setting. 

I suggest the authors address this further with an in-depth discussion clarifying: i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, ii) under what conditions accelerated SVRG is preferable to batch FB, and iii) how tight the current dependence on *n* and *d* is in the complexity bounds.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The work extends important algorithmic techniques to a relevant problem setting (saddle-point problems), filling a gap in the literature.
*   **Technical soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, utilizing monotone operator theory for a more general framework.
*   **Completeness:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis alongside experimental validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithms and analysis.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to impact areas where saddle-point formulations are prevalent in machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical advantage unclear:** The theoretical complexity analysis does not definitively demonstrate a clear advantage over existing batch methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The cost of the proximal operator appears to be a bottleneck.
*   **Dependence on parameters:** The performance relies heavily on correctly setting the regularization parameters (λ, γ), which may require careful tuning.
