

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this important class of problems common in machine learning. The authors build upon recent advances in stochastic optimization (SAG [1], SVRG [2], SAGA [3]) that have been successfully applied to convex minimization problems, but extend them to saddle-point formulations where the objective function may not be separable in the standard convex minimization sense. This extension is non-trivial because the standard convex minimization convergence analysis does not apply, and the authors appropriately adopt monotone operator theory to prove convergence, demonstrating that their algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction methods on saddle-point problems, using monotone operators to establish linear convergence. This analysis reveals that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems than just saddle-point problems. Second, the authors identify two distinct ways to split the gradient operator for saddle-point problems: element-wise splits and factored splits (which exploit the bilinear structure of many machine learning problems). Third, they demonstrate that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically. Finally, they show how to accelerate their methods using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework, resulting in algorithms that outperform accelerated batch methods.

The paper's quality is strong: the theoretical analysis is technically sound and carefully developed, with clear proofs of convergence rates. The experimental section provides comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem types, demonstrating that the proposed methods significantly outperform existing approaches, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The clarity of the paper is excellent; the organization is logical, the notation is well-chosen (though the authors should clarify the distinction between the smooth function K and the design matrix K), and the manuscript provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The originality is significant: extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems represents a novel contribution to the field, with the monotone operator approach offering a more general framework than previous work. The significance is high, as the paper addresses important machine learning problems where standard separable optimization techniques fail, such as structured output prediction with non-separable losses and non-separable regularizers like fusion terms or total variation.

Several strengths merit emphasis. The paper successfully demonstrates that saddle-point problems can be efficiently solved using variance reduction techniques, which was previously an open question. The analysis showing that the algorithms apply to variational inequalities (beyond standard saddle-point problems) significantly broadens the scope of the contribution. The empirical results are convincing and well-presented, with clear evidence that non-uniform sampling provides substantial performance improvements over uniform sampling. The acceleration framework is elegantly simple and provides a clear theoretical advantage over batch methods.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed. The adaptive update of the iterate point (\bar{x}, \bar{y}) in the acceleration section (Section 5) is not fully explained; the authors should clarify how to choose the optimal update frequency for practical implementation. Additionally, while the paper proves convergence for SVRG with acceleration, it does not explicitly state whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA (though it notes this is possible). The authors also use K for both the smooth function and the design matrix, which creates confusion; they should adopt different symbols for these distinct concepts.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is both technically sophisticated and practically relevant, addressing a gap in the literature that has important implications for many machine learning applications. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical validation is comprehensive, and the writing is clear and well-organized.

Pro: Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems; rigorous analysis using monotone operators; significant empirical validation across diverse problems; addresses important machine learning challenges with non-separable losses/regularizers.

Con: Minor clarification needed on adaptive update of (\bar{x}, \bar{y}); symbol confusion between smooth function K and design matrix K; theoretical extension to SAGA not explicitly confirmed.

This paper is highly relevant to NIPS, advancing the state of the art in optimization for machine learning with a contribution that is both theoretically sound and practically valuable. The results have the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications where saddle-point formulations are used, particularly for problems with non-separable components. Given the strength of the technical contribution, clarity of presentation, and significance of the results, I strongly recommend acceptance.
