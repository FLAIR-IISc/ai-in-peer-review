

This paper addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems by extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for such problems. The authors establish a novel theoretical framework using monotone operators to prove linear convergence, demonstrating that their approach applies to a broader class of problems including variational inequalities. They introduce two key insights: the necessity of splitting into convex-concave terms rather than convex terms alone, and the critical importance of non-uniform sampling for both theoretical and practical efficiency. The paper further proposes an accelerated variant using a modified catalyst framework, which achieves superior performance over batch methods.

Novelty/Originality: The paper makes significant contributions by providing the first linearly convergent stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems. The theoretical analysis reveals important insights specific to this class of problems, particularly regarding the role of monotone operators. The extension to non-separable functions—where existing methods fail—is especially noteworthy, as the authors demonstrate that their approach is applicable to problems like non-separable losses in supervised learning and robust optimization, which are common in machine learning.

Technical Quality: The theoretical contributions are strong and well-motivated. The convergence analysis is thorough, and the authors effectively demonstrate how their framework extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. However, the presentation would benefit from a clearer connection to monotone operators earlier in the paper, rather than presenting it as a later extension. The experimental section is limited to two datasets (sido and rcv1), and would be strengthened by including additional benchmarks. For separable problems, the authors should compare against accelerated methods like the stochastic Chambolle-Pock algorithm (Zhang & Xiao, ICML 2015), which is a relevant baseline for such problems.

The authors should clarify the following points:
1. How strong are assumptions (A)-(C), particularly assumption (A)? Do these assumptions hold for the saddle-point problem induced by SVMs?
2. The distinction between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual approaches requires clarification. How does the proposed method improve upon or differ from these existing approaches?
3. In Theorem 2, it is unclear whether the constant μ refers to the monotonicity constant mentioned in the appendix. The authors should clarify this relationship and explain how the convergence guarantees in Theorem 2 compare to those in Theorem 1.
4. The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is unclear. It should be explicitly stated whether assumptions (A)-(C) imply monotonicity.

Minor Issues: The paper uses the constant L ambiguously, stating at one point that "L represents the condition number of the problem" while later referring to "the Lipschitz constant L." These should be clearly distinguished. The Forward-Backward algorithm description should include a proper reference to its original formulation.

Strengths: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for applying stochastic variance reduction to saddle-point problems. The analysis of non-uniform sampling and its theoretical benefits is particularly valuable. The extension to monotone operators demonstrates the broader applicability of the framework. The experimental results confirm the theoretical advantages, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

Weaknesses: The presentation could be improved by better integrating the monotone operator perspective throughout the paper rather than relegating it to Section 6. The experimental evaluation is limited to two datasets, and the comparison to relevant accelerated methods for separable problems is incomplete. The assumptions require more detailed discussion regarding their applicability to standard machine learning problems like SVMs.

Overall, this paper makes an important contribution to the literature on optimization for machine learning. The theoretical framework is sound and the results are compelling. With improved presentation and expanded experimental evaluation, this work would be even stronger. The core contributions are significant and the method offers clear advantages over existing approaches for non-separable saddle-point problems.
