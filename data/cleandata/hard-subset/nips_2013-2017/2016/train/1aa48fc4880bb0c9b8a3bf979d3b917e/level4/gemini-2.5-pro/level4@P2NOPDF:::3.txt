This paper extends several recent incremental algorithms, including SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems. The analysis considers problems with a finite-sum structure arising from two distinct problem formulations, termed "individual splits" and "factored splits." The authors provide a thorough theoretical study of the complexity of these algorithms under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification task demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for these methods to outperform competitive batch algorithms.

The core idea of extending variance-reduction techniques to saddle point problems is a natural direction. However, the theoretical results established in this paper are novel, and the technical analysis is non-trivial. For the important special case of the bilinear saddle point problem, the authors present a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms. This comparison shares a compelling resemblance to the established hierarchy in the convex minimization setting and is both coherent and intuitive.

Despite these strengths, my major concern relates to a potential bottleneck in the proposed incremental algorithms. Specifically, the proximal operator at each iteration appears to incur a cost of O(n+d). This per-iteration cost significantly impacts the overall complexity. As a result, even for the most advanced variant—accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling—the total complexity is not necessarily superior to the accelerated batch version, as detailed in Tables 1 and 2.

This issue is particularly pronounced for the "factored splits" formulation, which is used in the simulations. In the common machine learning regime where the number of data points `n` is substantially larger than the feature dimension `d`, the complexity of the proposed methods is theoretically worse than that of accelerated batch forward-backward algorithms (Table 2). This finding is surprising and seems to contradict the primary motivation for using variance-reduction techniques, which typically offer significant advantages in the `n >> d` setting for convex minimization. From a theoretical standpoint, a clear and substantial advantage of the proposed incremental approach over batch methods is not evident from the current analysis.

To strengthen the paper, I would suggest the authors provide an in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following points:

1.  **Factored vs. Individual Splits:** Is the factored split formulation theoretically inferior to the individual split formulation in terms of complexity? If so, what are the practical trade-offs that motivate its use?

2.  **Favorable Regimes:** Under what specific conditions or problem structures (e.g., relationships between `n`, `d`, and condition numbers) does the accelerated SVRG method hold a theoretical advantage over the accelerated batch forward-backward algorithm?

3.  **Tightness of Bounds:** Are the derived dependencies on `n` and `d` in the complexity bounds believed to be tight? Could a more refined analysis potentially reveal a clearer advantage for the incremental methods?
