This paper investigates saddle-point problems possessing a convex-concave structure, and demonstrates how existing stochastic variance reduction methods – specifically SVRG and SAGA – can be effectively applied through the incorporation of a proximal operator. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of these algorithms within the framework of monotone operators, establishing linear convergence guarantees. Furthermore, they introduce an accelerated variant and explore the impact of a non-uniform sampling strategy. 

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper presents a substantial contribution with significant theoretical depth, offering new insights into the analysis of saddle-point problems. The breadth of extensions proposed is noteworthy, particularly the extension to non-separable functions. 

**Technical Quality:** While the theoretical results are strong, the presentation currently lacks clarity. Strengthening the connection to the underlying monotone operator framework – currently appearing primarily in Section 6 – would improve accessibility, as the core analysis fundamentally relies on this concept. The experimental evaluation would benefit from inclusion of results on a wider range of datasets, beyond the current two. For cases involving separable functions, a comparison to accelerated proximal algorithms, such as the stochastic variant of Chambolle-Pock [Zhang & Peyré, 2015], would be valuable. (http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf)

Several points require clarification:

1.  Could the authors discuss the strength of assumptions A-C, specifically the implications of assumption (A)? For instance, do these assumptions hold when applied to saddle-point problems arising from Support Vector Machines?
2.  A more detailed explanation of the differences between the proposed method and existing stochastic primal-dual approaches would be helpful.
3.  Regarding Theorem 2, the meaning of the constant μ requires further elaboration. Specifically, is it related to the monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? How does the convergence rate presented in this theorem compare to that of Theorem 1?
4.  The transition from assumptions A-C to strong monotonicity (as presented in the appendix) is unclear. Do assumptions A-C necessarily imply strong monotonicity?

**Minor Issues:**

*   There appears to be some inconsistency in the use of the constant *L*, which is sometimes used to denote the condition number and other times the Lipschitz constant (e.g., "The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem" vs. "we need the Lipschitz constant L").
*   Please include a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.
