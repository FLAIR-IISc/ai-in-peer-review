This paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems and shows how to solve them using existing stochastic variance-reduction techniques (e.g., SVRG and SAGA) combined with a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms in the monotone operator framework and establish linear convergence. They also propose an accelerated variant and analyze a non-uniform sampling scheme.

Novelty and originality: The contributions are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and yields new insights for saddle-point problems. The paper provides several extensions; the most notable is that the method applies to non-separable functions.

Technical quality and presentation: While I appreciate the theoretical contributions, I found the presentation disappointing. The connection to monotone operators is not made sufficiently clear. Although this appears as an “extension” in Section 6, the analysis throughout is grounded in monotone operator theory. Bringing this connection forward and explaining it earlier would make the paper much more accessible.

Experiments: The empirical evaluation is limited to two datasets; I would like to see results on a broader set. For separable functions, the paper should also compare against accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle–Pock algorithm:
http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf
Please add this reference and include a comparison.

Points requiring clarification:
1) Strength of assumptions A–C, especially (A). How strong are these assumptions in practice? For instance, do they hold for the standard saddle-point formulation of SVMs?
2) Relation to stochastic primal–dual methods. I cannot clearly discern the differences between your approach and stochastic primal–dual methods. Please elaborate on the similarities and differences (both algorithmic and theoretical), and clarify when one should expect advantages over primal–dual schemes.
3) Theorem 2 and the constant μ. In Theorem 2, what exactly is μ? Is it the strong monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? How should we compare the guarantees in Theorem 2 with those in Theorem 1 under this definition?
4) From assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity. The transition in the appendix is confusing. Do assumptions (A)–(C) imply (strong) monotonicity of the underlying operator? If so, please state this explicitly and provide a clear derivation; if not, clarify what additional conditions are required.

Minor issues:
- Notation for L: There is a mix-up between using L as a condition number and as a Lipschitz constant (“The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” vs. “we need the Lipschitz constant L”). Please disambiguate, ideally by using distinct symbols.
- Forward–Backward algorithm: Please add an appropriate reference.
- Organization: Since the analysis relies on monotone operator theory, consider moving the relevant definitions and assumptions from the appendix or Section 6 to earlier sections to guide the reader.

Overall assessment: This is a strong theoretical paper with meaningful contributions, including linear convergence results in the monotone operator setting, an accelerated variant, and a non-uniform sampling analysis. However, the presentation should clarify the role of monotone operators and the precise assumptions, and the experimental section needs to be expanded and include comparisons to strong baselines such as stochastic Chambolle–Pock. With these improvements, the paper would be significantly stronger.
