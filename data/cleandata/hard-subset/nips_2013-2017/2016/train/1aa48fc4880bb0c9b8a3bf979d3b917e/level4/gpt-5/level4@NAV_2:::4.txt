This paper adapts the stochastic variance-reduction techniques SVRG and SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems and establishes linear convergence. Theoretical guarantees together with empirical evidence demonstrate the effectiveness of variance reduction in this setting. The work develops SVRG and SAGA for strongly convex–concave saddle points and shows that the methods are applicable to a range of non-separable formulations in which both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and the regularizer (e.g., fusion penalties) are non-separable. A key contribution is that, unlike prior analyses for convex minimization, the convergence proof for saddle-point problems leverages the framework of monotone operators; this also implies that the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants extend to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities arising in game theory. In the stochastic setting, the paper introduces two decompositions of the gradient operator—a component-wise split and a factored split—used with SVRG and SAGA, respectively. The variance-reduced estimators are embedded within stochastic forward–backward iterations commonly employed for saddle-point problems. The paper further studies non-uniform sampling and shows, in both analysis and experiments, that non-uniform sampling outperforms uniform sampling. Experimental results in the main text and appendix support the theory and illustrate strong performance on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers.

Some minor comments:
- In the acceleration of Section 5, the method follows [8] by adding a regularization term that nudges the update toward an iterate-like reference point. It is unclear how to adaptively update the reference (x̄, ȳ) in practice to realize the speed-up; more guidance would be helpful.
- Convergence and acceleration guarantees are provided for SVRG. Do analogous results hold for SAGA?
- The symbol K is used both for the smooth function K(·) and for the design matrix K; using distinct notation would avoid confusion.
