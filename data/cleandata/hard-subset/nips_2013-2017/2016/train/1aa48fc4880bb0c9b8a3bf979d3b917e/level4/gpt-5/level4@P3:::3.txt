Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA) and their Catalyst-style acceleration to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M admits an efficient proximal operator. The key idea is to leverage finite-sum structure in two ways: (i) function-based (individual) splits and (ii) “factored” splits tailored to the saddle-point variables, with non-uniform sampling schemes matched to each split. The analysis departs from the convex minimization setting by recasting the problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator; this yields the first linear convergence guarantees for variance-reduced methods in this class and extends to certain variational inequalities.

The work situates itself with respect to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization and primal–dual (forward–backward) algorithms such as Chambolle–Pock for saddle-point problems. Unlike stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods that rely on separability, the proposed methods apply to non-separable losses/regularizers, a common obstacle in practice. The paper also develops an acceleration scheme via a proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper, deriving improved complexity bounds, especially for bilinear K(x,y)=y^T Kx with non-uniform sampling.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: Provides the first linear convergence theory for variance-reduced methods in general convex–concave saddle-point problems using a monotone-operator framework, with extensions to variational inequalities.
- Algorithmic design: Clear derivation of SVRG/SAGA variants for saddle-point problems, including factored vs individual splits and the role of non-uniform sampling.
- Acceleration: A simple and elegant Catalyst-style scheme tied to proximal-point iterations, with transparent complexity trade-offs.
- Theory: Careful complexity comparisons across batch, stochastic, and variance-reduced methods; explicit dependence on operator norms (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max) and sampling distributions.
- Practice: Experiments highlight that non-uniform sampling and acceleration substantially improve over batch baselines and that uniform sampling can be inferior.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Proximal cost bottleneck: Each iteration requires computing prox_M over both x and y; for many instances this appears to be O(n+d). As a result, even accelerated SVRG with optimal non-uniform sampling may not strictly dominate accelerated batch FB in regimes with large n (e.g., factored splits and n≫d). The paper briefly mentions using larger mini-batches to amortize Tprox(M), but a more detailed discussion of when the variance-reduced methods win in wall-clock time would be valuable.
- Split choice and theory: While the paper analyzes both individual and factored splits, the theoretical guidance on when each split is preferable is scattered. In bilinear problems, the factored split reduces storage for SAGA but can be worse than batch FB in certain n,d regimes. A consolidated comparison (assumptions, norms, sampling, storage) would help practitioners.
- Practicalities of non-uniform sampling: Efficiently computing, updating, and sampling from the proposed distributions (e.g., proportional to row/column norms or entry magnitudes) should be discussed in terms of preprocessing cost and dynamic updates during training.
- Parameter knowledge: The methods and proofs assume knowledge of strong convexity/concavity (λ,γ) and L or L̄(π). Guidance on estimating these in practice or making the methods adaptive would improve usability.
- Empirical breadth: Experiments are informative but limited in scope (two datasets; one notable non-separable example). Additional tasks (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction with different proximable non-separable terms) would strengthen claims of generality.

Quality
The technical development is careful, with proofs (per appendices) that appropriately exploit monotonicity rather than co-coercivity. The complexity bounds are coherent across methods and align with known convex minimization analogs. The proximal bottleneck and the need to resample under non-uniform SAGA are acknowledged, though more guidance on mitigating these issues would be useful.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. A few suggestions:
- Gather the split-dependent complexities, storage, and sampling choices into a single summary table with clear regimes (n≷d).
- State explicitly the per-iteration costs, including Tprox(M), and how mini-batches trade off oracle calls vs proximal computations.
- In the monotone operator extension, restate assumptions succinctly (A maximal strong monotone A; B=∑Bi Lipschitz and monotone) and give a compact example beyond bilinear saddle points.

Originality
The variance-reduced saddle-point extensions and the monotone-operator-based linear convergence analysis appear novel and non-trivial for NIPS 2016, distinguishing the work from primal-only variance reduction and separable primal–dual coordinate schemes.

Significance
Enabling linearly convergent variance reduction for non-separable saddle-point problems fills an important gap. The results should be of interest to researchers working on large-scale optimization, variational inequalities, and structured ML models.

Specific questions and requests
1) Factored vs individual splits: Provide a unified theorem or corollary that directly compares their complexities (including storage and sampling overhead) under typical regimes (n≫d, d≫n), and clarify whether the factored split is theoretically worse than individual splits in the dominant n≫d case.
2) When is accelerated SVRG favorable over accelerated batch FB? State a simple rule-of-thumb in terms of L̄, T(B), max_i T(Bi), and Tprox(M), and illustrate numerically on synthetic bilinear instances with controlled n,d and condition numbers.
3) Tightness: Comment on the tightness of the n,d dependence in the bounds for bilinear K; are there instances where the gap between ‖K‖op and ‖K‖F makes the non-uniform sampling provably necessary?
4) Proximal cost: Discuss possibilities to reduce Tprox(M), e.g., block/coordinate prox when M decomposes partially, “lazy” updates for certain proximals, or approximate prox (with error control) within the variance-reduced framework.
5) Non-uniform SAGA: Since resampling is needed for the stated bound, quantify the empirical impact of skipping resampling and whether a mixed uniform/non-uniform scheme preserves most of the gains.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- First linear convergence guarantees for variance-reduced methods in general saddle-point settings via a clean monotone-operator analysis.
- Practical and theoretically principled non-uniform sampling for two natural splits.
- Simple and effective acceleration via a proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper with clear complexity gains.
- Solid comparisons and insights for bilinear problems; experiments align with theory.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Potential proximal bottleneck can negate variance-reduction gains in common n≫d regimes, especially under factored splits.
- Limited empirical diversity; more evidence on broader classes and larger scales would strengthen the case.
- Reliance on known problem constants and some implementation details (non-uniform sampling, resampling) left to the reader.
