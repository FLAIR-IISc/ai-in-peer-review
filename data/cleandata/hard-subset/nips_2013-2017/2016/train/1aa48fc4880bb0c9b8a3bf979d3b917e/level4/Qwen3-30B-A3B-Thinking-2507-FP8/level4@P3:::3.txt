

The paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for this important class of problems in machine learning. Building on NIPS contributions such as SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014), and the Catalyst framework (Lin et al., 2015), the authors make a significant contribution by adapting these techniques to saddle-point problems, which require fundamentally different analysis than convex minimization.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory rather than convexity arguments; (2) a clear distinction between two types of splits (function-based and derivative-based) that are relevant for saddle-point problems but not for convex minimization; (3) a demonstration that non-uniform sampling is critical for both theoretical efficiency and practical performance; and (4) a simple extension of the Catalyst framework for acceleration that leads to algorithms superior to batch methods.

The technical quality is strong. The authors provide rigorous convergence proofs that carefully address the challenges of extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems. The analysis properly accounts for different splits, sampling strategies, and the dual nature of the saddle-point problem. The experimental results on a binary classification problem with non-separable losses and regularizers confirm the theoretical claims, showing that non-uniform sampling and acceleration provide substantial improvements over batch and stochastic methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

The clarity is generally good, with well-structured presentation and helpful comparisons in Table 1. The paper effectively connects the theoretical developments to practical machine learning applications in Section 2.1, which include supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning.

The originality is substantial. While the algorithmic extensions might appear straightforward, the authors demonstrate that the saddle-point setting requires fundamentally different analysis. The distinction between function-based and derivative-based splits is novel and important for understanding the applicability of these methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous NIPS work on convex minimization and from recent work on separable saddle-point problems.

The significance is compelling. Many important machine learning problems naturally lead to saddle-point problems that cannot be efficiently solved by existing convex optimization methods. The authors demonstrate that their approach can provide substantial speedups over batch algorithms, particularly when the problem is ill-conditioned, which has clear practical implications.

However, I have concerns about the theoretical complexity analysis. The paper claims that accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling provides the best theoretical complexity, but the analysis in Table 1 suggests that for factored splits (used in the experiments), the complexity is O(n + d) per iteration, which may be worse than batch methods when n ≫ d (typical in machine learning applications). The authors need to clarify when their methods provide a clear theoretical advantage over batch algorithms.

Specifically, I would like the authors to address:
1. Whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits (or vice versa)
2. Precisely when accelerated SVRG is favorable compared to batch FB algorithms
3. Whether their complexity bounds are tight

The paper makes a valuable contribution to optimization for machine learning, but these theoretical questions need to be resolved to fully establish the advantages of their approach. The authors have provided a strong foundation, but the theoretical claims require more precise qualification.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems
- Rigorous theoretical analysis using monotone operators
- Clear demonstration of advantages through experiments
- Important practical applications in machine learning

Cons for acceptance:
- Theoretical complexity analysis needs refinement to clarify when the proposed methods provide a clear advantage
- The discussion of complexity for factored splits needs to be more precise
- The connection to typical machine learning applications (where n ≫ d) needs stronger justification

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important contribution to the field. With minor revisions to clarify the theoretical complexity claims, it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
