- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems for both individual and factored data splits.  
- Thorough complexity analysis provided under different split types and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform).  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
- Results are notably novel and technically non-trivial despite seeming like straightforward extensions of variance-reduction methods.  
- For bilinear saddle point problems the paper gives a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms that mirrors known convex minimization results.  
- Major concern: each iteration requires computing a proximal operator at cost O(n + d), creating a fundamental computational bottleneck.  
- As a result, overall complexity—even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling—may not beat the accelerated batch method, especially when n >> d.  
- Under factored splits (used in simulations), complexity can be worse than batch FB (see Table 2), casting doubt on theoretical advantages of variance reduction here.  
- Reviewer asks for deeper discussion addressing: (i) is a factored split theoretically less efficient than an individual split? (ii) when is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB? (iii) is the n and d dependence tight?  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but leaves scores unchanged.
