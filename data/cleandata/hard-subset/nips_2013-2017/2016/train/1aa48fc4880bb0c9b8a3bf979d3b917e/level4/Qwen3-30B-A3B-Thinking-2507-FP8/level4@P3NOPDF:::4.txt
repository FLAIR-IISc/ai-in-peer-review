

This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG and SAGA) to strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence under appropriate conditions. The authors address a critical gap by demonstrating that these methods, originally designed for convex optimization, can be effectively adapted to saddle-point settings—specifically for problems where the objective function is strongly convex in one variable and strongly concave in the other. The core contribution lies in proving convergence using monotone operator theory, which is notably more complex than standard convex analysis and provides a robust theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. This approach also broadens the applicability to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities in game theory, which are not covered by prior work.

A key strength is the paper’s focus on non-separable problems, where both the loss (e.g., structured output prediction) and regularizer (e.g., fusion terms) may lack separability. The authors introduce two distinct gradient operator splits tailored to SVRG and SAGA: an element-wise split for SVRG and a factored split for SAGA. These splits enable variance reduction within stochastic forward-backward algorithms, a standard framework for saddle-point problems. The analysis further incorporates non-uniform sampling strategies, rigorously demonstrating their superiority over uniform sampling both theoretically and empirically. The experimental results—complemented by extensive appendix material—consistently validate the theoretical claims, showing clear performance gains across diverse non-separable loss and regularizer scenarios. This empirical rigor significantly strengthens the paper’s contribution.

The paper’s technical depth is commendable, particularly the use of monotone operators to handle the saddle-point structure. This avoids the pitfalls of ad hoc extensions seen in related work and provides a unified lens for convergence analysis. The treatment of non-uniform sampling is also noteworthy, as it aligns with recent trends in stochastic optimization and delivers tangible practical benefits. The authors effectively contextualize their work within existing literature, highlighting how their framework extends beyond separable settings—a limitation of prior variance-reduction approaches for saddle-point problems.

Minor concerns require attention to ensure clarity and completeness:
1. **Acceleration mechanism**: In Section 5, the authors incorporate an additional regularization term to accelerate convergence, following [8]. However, the adaptive update strategy for the auxiliary iterate point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ is not specified. It remains unclear how this point is dynamically adjusted to achieve speedup, potentially limiting the practical implementation. A brief discussion or pseudocode would clarify this step.
2. **SAGA acceleration**: The convergence proof for accelerated SVRG is provided, but the analogous theoretical guarantees for accelerated SAGA are missing. Given SAGA’s distinct update rule, the authors should explicitly address whether the same acceleration framework applies or requires modification.
3. **Notational consistency**: The smooth function $K(\cdot)$ and the design matrix $K$ use identical symbols, which risks confusion. These should be denoted with distinct notations (e.g., $K(\cdot)$ vs. $\mathbf{K}$) to align with standard mathematical conventions.

Overall, this work makes a compelling contribution to stochastic optimization for saddle-point problems. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable, strongly convex-concave settings is both timely and well-executed, with rigorous theory and strong empirical support. The use of monotone operators elevates the analysis beyond prior efforts, and the non-uniform sampling results offer practical value. The minor issues noted above are easily resolvable and do not undermine the paper’s core strengths. The writing is generally clear, though minor grammatical refinements (e.g., "the analysis on saddle-point problem" → "the analysis for saddle-point problems") would enhance readability. 

The paper is well-suited for publication at NeurIPS. It advances the field by providing a theoretically sound, broadly applicable framework for a challenging class of problems. The experimental validation is thorough, and the technical innovations are clearly articulated. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this work will serve as a valuable reference for researchers working at the intersection of optimization and machine learning.
