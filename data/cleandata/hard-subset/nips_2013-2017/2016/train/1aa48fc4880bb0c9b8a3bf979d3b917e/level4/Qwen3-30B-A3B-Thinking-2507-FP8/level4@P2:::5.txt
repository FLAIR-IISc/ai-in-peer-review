

This paper introduces the first linearly convergent stochastic variance reduction algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending SVRG and SAGA to this domain. Unlike convex minimization where standard variance reduction techniques apply, saddle-point problems require fundamentally different analysis due to the absence of convexity in both variables. The authors' key contribution lies in proving convergence using monotone operator theory, which not only establishes linear convergence for saddle-point problems but also demonstrates that the same framework applies to variational inequalities, thereby significantly broadening the scope of these algorithms.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems, using the monotone operator interpretation rather than the convex minimization approach. Second, it carefully distinguishes between two different notions of splitting (function-based versus derivative-based) and shows that saddle-point problems require splitting into convex-concave terms, unlike convex minimization. Third, it establishes that non-uniform sampling is essential for efficient performance, both theoretically and empirically. Fourth, it demonstrates how the "catalyst" framework can accelerate these methods, leading to algorithms superior to accelerated batch approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many machine learning problems, including non-separable loss functions (e.g., AUC maximization) and non-separable regularizers (e.g., grouped norms, total variation), naturally formulate as saddle-point problems where existing fast algorithms cannot be applied. The authors provide concrete examples where their methods outperform previous approaches, particularly in ill-conditioned settings where non-uniform sampling proves critical.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough treatment of the theoretical foundations and practical implementation details. The experimental section convincingly demonstrates the superiority of non-uniform sampling and the accelerated variants over both batch and stochastic alternatives. The authors also provide a valuable extension to monotone operators, showing how their framework generalizes beyond saddle-point problems.

However, I have several concerns about clarity and completeness that prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. First, the paper does not sufficiently explain how to implement non-uniform sampling for general saddle-point problems beyond the bilinear case. The authors mention "non-uniform schemes" but fail to specify how to compute the sampling probabilities π for problems where the matrix structure doesn't apply. This is a significant gap since the performance hinges on this choice.

Second, the paper does not explain how the step size σ is selected in practice, particularly in the experiments. While the theoretical analysis provides bounds, the experimental section lacks details on the step size selection procedure, making it difficult to reproduce the results. The authors could provide more insight into why non-uniform sampling is so crucial for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization, which would deepen the theoretical understanding.

Third, while the paper mentions that the monotone operator approach enables extensions to variational inequalities, it does not provide specific examples or discuss the practical implications of this broader applicability. A brief discussion of how this extension could benefit other problem domains would strengthen the significance argument.

Minor issues include two typos noted in the draft (line 78: "k" should be "d"; line 121: an extra "y_{t-1}" term), which should be corrected.

Strengths:
- First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems with variance reduction
- Novel convergence proof using monotone operator theory that extends to variational inequalities
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis of different splitting schemes and sampling strategies
- Strong empirical results demonstrating superiority over existing methods
- Clear connection to important machine learning applications with non-separable losses and regularizers

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient explanation of non-uniform sampling for non-bilinear problems
- Lack of details on practical implementation of step size selection
- Limited discussion of why non-uniform sampling is critical for saddle-point problems but not convex minimization
- Incomplete discussion of the broader implications of the monotone operator extension

Overall, this is an important theoretical contribution that addresses a significant gap in the optimization literature for machine learning. The paper is well-written and makes a compelling case for the utility of these algorithms. However, the lack of clarity regarding non-uniform sampling implementation and step size selection represents a substantial barrier to reproducibility and practical adoption. With the suggested clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the theoretical novelty and significance are strong, but the practical implementation details are insufficiently explained. I recommend rejection with the option to revise, as the authors could address these concerns in a revision that would significantly strengthen the paper.
