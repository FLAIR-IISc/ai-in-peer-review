This paper considers convex-concave saddle-point problems and extends stochastic variance reduced techniques, such as SVRG and SAGA, to address them, incorporating a proximal operator. The authors analyze these algorithms within the framework of monotone operators, demonstrating linear convergence, and propose an accelerated variant alongside an analysis of a non-uniform sampling scheme. 

**Novelty/Originality:** The contributions of this paper are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides novel insights for saddle-point problems. The authors propose several extensions, most notably demonstrating applicability to non-separable functions.

**Technical Quality:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the presentation could be improved for accessibility. The connection to monotone operators, central to the analysis, could be more clearly articulated, particularly as the entire analysis in Section 6 relies on this framework. The experimental evaluation would benefit from results on a wider range of datasets. For cases involving separable functions, a comparison to accelerated primal-dual methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm [Zhang & Chambolle, 2015], is missing and should be included. 

Several points require further clarification from the authors:

1.  How restrictive are assumptions (A)-(C), particularly (A)? For example, do these assumptions hold when solving the saddle-point problem induced by Support Vector Machines (SVM)?
2.  Can the authors elaborate on the distinction between their proposed method and stochastic primal-dual methods? The relationship between the two approaches is not fully clear.
3.  In Theorem 2, the constant μ is unclear. Is it the monotonicity constant referenced in the appendix? How can the result of Theorem 2 be compared to that of Theorem 1?
4.  The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is somewhat confusing. Do assumptions (A)-(C) necessarily imply strong monotonicity?

**Minor Issues:**

*   There is occasional ambiguity between the use of *L* to denote both the condition number and the Lipschitz constant (e.g., “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” and “we need the Lipschitz constant L”).
*   A reference is missing for the discussion of the Forward-Backward algorithm.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving saddle-point problems.
*   The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   The accelerated variant and the analysis of non-uniform sampling offer practical improvements.
*   The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility, particularly concerning the connection to monotone operators.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks breadth, with only two datasets used.
*   The comparison to related work is incomplete, missing a relevant comparison to accelerated primal-dual methods.
*   Certain technical details and assumptions require further clarification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. Despite the areas for improvement, the theoretical novelty and potential impact of the proposed methods outweigh the shortcomings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding clarity, experimental validation, and comparison to related work.

**Reference:**

Zhang, A., & Chambolle, A. (2015). An efficient stochastic primal-dual algorithm for total variation regularization. *Journal of Machine Learning Research*, *16*(1), 1367–1399.
