### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the popular stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems. While the algorithmic extensions are straightforward adaptations of their convex minimization counterparts, the analysis is novel and constitutes the paper's main contribution. The authors observe that standard analysis techniques for convex minimization do not apply to the saddle-point setting. Instead, they provide a convergence proof rooted in the theory of monotone operators, demonstrating that finding the saddle point is equivalent to finding the zero of a monotone operator. This theoretical lens not only establishes the first linear convergence rates for large-scale incremental algorithms on this problem class but also generalizes the applicability of these methods to other problems, such as variational inequalities.

The work highlights several key aspects of this extension: a) it allows for different ways to split the problem structure (by function or by partial derivatives), b) it shows that non-uniform sampling is critical for achieving state-of-the-art performance, and c) it demonstrates that these methods can be accelerated using the "catalyst" framework, leading to an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods. The practical relevance is motivated by machine learning problems with non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., AUC maximization, total variation regularization), which naturally fit the saddle-point formulation.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The analysis is novel, and the results fill an important gap.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper presents the first linearly convergent, large-scale stochastic algorithms for a broad class of saddle-point problems. Prior to this work, practitioners were limited to batch methods (with slower per-iteration costs) or standard stochastic methods (with sub-linear convergence). This is a valuable and timely contribution, given the increasing prevalence of models with non-separable structures.

2.  **Quality of Theoretical Contribution:** The main strength lies in the proof technique. Shifting the analysis from function value descent to the framework of monotone operators is an elegant and powerful approach. It successfully circumvents the difficulties of applying standard convex optimization analysis and, as a byproduct, generalizes the results to a wider class of problems beyond saddle-point optimization. The inclusion of simplified proofs for existing batch algorithms in the appendix is also a nice addition.

3.  **Thoroughness:** The authors consider several important practical aspects, including different problem splitting strategies, the crucial role of non-uniform sampling, and a method for acceleration. The complexity analysis presented in Table 1 clearly summarizes the theoretical improvements over existing methods.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms. The SAGA and accelerated SVRG methods with non-uniform sampling show significant performance gains over both batch and standard stochastic competitors, confirming the theoretical findings. The comparison on both non-separable and separable problems provides a comprehensive picture of the methods' performance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is well-written and clear for the most part, but could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Clarity of the Proof Technique:** While the use of monotone operator theory is the central contribution, the main paper provides limited intuition on the proof technique itself. A high-level proof sketch or a more detailed discussion of the key steps in the main body would greatly enhance the paper's impact and make the core technical contribution more accessible to a broader audience. This might be achievable by slightly condensing the review of existing algorithms.

2.  **Guidance on Non-uniform Sampling:** The paper convincingly argues that non-uniform sampling is essential for good performance and provides specific sampling schemes for the bilinear case. However, for the general case, it is unclear how the probabilities `π` should be chosen. Providing guidance or heuristics on designing effective sampling strategies for non-bilinear problems would significantly increase the practical utility of the proposed methods.

3.  **Discussion on the Importance of Non-uniform Sampling:** The experiments suggest that the performance gap between uniform and non-uniform sampling is more pronounced for these saddle-point problems than what is often observed in standard convex minimization. The paper would benefit from a discussion offering some intuition for this. Is there a structural reason related to the saddle-point formulation that makes variance reduction via importance sampling particularly effective?

4.  **Experimental Details:** The procedure for setting the step-size `σ` in the experiments is not specified. Since the theoretical step-size depends on problem constants like `L` and `L̄`, which may be difficult to compute, it would be valuable for reproducibility to clarify how these were handled in practice (e.g., were they estimated, or was a grid search performed?).

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the bilinear saddle-point example (Section 2), the split `Bjk(x, y) = Kjk(yj , -xk)` appears to be missing scaling factors related to `p` and `q` or an assumption that these are uniform, unlike the "factored" split. Clarifying this would be helpful.
*   The paper is generally well-written, but a final proofreading pass could catch minor typographical errors.

In summary, this paper makes an important and novel contribution by developing the first fast, linearly-convergent incremental algorithms for saddle-point problems. The analysis is elegant and the practical results are compelling. Addressing the points above would further improve the clarity and impact of this excellent work.
