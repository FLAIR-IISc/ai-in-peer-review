This paper proposes the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems, extending recent stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA). The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithms in the monotone operator setting, showing linear convergence, and also propose an accelerated variant and a non-uniform sampling scheme. The contributions of this paper are significant, particularly in addressing non-separable functions.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction to the problem and the proposed approach. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the algorithms, including the extension to monotone operators, and demonstrate their effectiveness through experiments on two datasets. The writing is clear, and the technical content is well-explained.

The quality of the paper is high, with a thorough analysis of the algorithms and their convergence properties. The authors provide a detailed comparison of their approach with existing methods, including batch and stochastic algorithms. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly in addressing non-separable functions.

The paper's significance lies in its ability to address a wide range of problems, including those with non-separable losses or regularizers. The proposed algorithms are shown to be effective in these settings, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their convergence properties. The paper's impact is likely to be significant, particularly in the machine learning community, where saddle-point problems are common.

In terms of novelty and originality, the paper makes significant contributions to the field of optimization and machine learning. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is novel, and the analysis of the algorithms in the monotone operator setting is thorough and insightful. The proposed accelerated variant and non-uniform sampling scheme are also novel and effective.

The paper's clarity and presentation are good, with clear explanations of the technical content. However, there are a few points that could be clarified, such as the strength of the assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A), and the differences between stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach. Additionally, the transition from assumption (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix could be clarified.

Minor issues include some mix-up with the constant L used as the condition number and the Lipschitz constant. Additionally, a reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm would be helpful.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the technical content is well-explained. The contributions are significant, and the paper's impact is likely to be substantial.
