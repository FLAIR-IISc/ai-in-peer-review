Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure, yielding the first large-scale algorithms with linear convergence for this class. The key technical innovation is to analyze these methods through the lens of monotone operators rather than convex minimization, which enables convergence guarantees for a broader class, including variational inequalities. The authors discuss two notions of “splits” for the smooth component K: by functions or by partial derivatives (factored splits), emphasize the necessity of splitting into convex–concave terms, and show that non-uniform sampling is crucial in both theory and practice. They also provide an acceleration via a catalyst-like proximal regularization scheme, and give simplified analyses of forward–backward (FB) and its accelerated/stochastic variants.

This work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization (NIPS), primal–dual and FB methods (Rockafellar; Chambolle–Pock; Nesterov), stochastic FB with O(1/t) rates, and recent non-uniform sampling strategies. Unlike co-coercivity-based analyses (which do not cover saddle points), the monotone operator approach yields linear rates under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz continuity without requiring each component to be monotone or to have a resolvent.

Strengths:
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced methods for composite convex–concave saddle-point problems, addressing important machine learning settings with non-separable losses/regularizers.
- Analysis via monotone operators is conceptually clean, broadly applicable (e.g., variational inequalities), and yields simpler proofs for FB and its variants.
- Careful treatment of different splits (including factored splits) and of scaling parameters (λ, γ).
- Non-uniform sampling is theoretically justified and empirically important; the paper provides concrete sampling schemes and complexity bounds for bilinear problems.
- Acceleration through a catalyst-style proximal regularization improves over accelerated batch methods in theory and experiments.
- Experiments on non-separable tasks show clear gains for (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling; the comparison to primal methods on separable cases is informative.

Weaknesses / points needing clarification:
- The algorithms themselves are straightforward extensions; the main novelty is in the analysis. A proof sketch in the main text (if space permits) would help readers follow the key ideas.
- Non-uniform sampling beyond bilinear cases: the paper does not clearly specify how to choose π for general K and how to estimate L̄(π) and Li in practice.
- Experimental details: how is the stepsize σ chosen/tuned in SVRG/SAGA and in stochastic FB? Are λ and γ assumed known, or estimated?
- Intuition for why non-uniform sampling is essential here (and less so in some convex minimization settings) would be valuable, especially connecting to operator smoothness and the factored vs individual splits.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step for the theory; the experiments use a practical variant without resampling. Some discussion of the gap between theory and practice would help.
- Storage vs compute trade-offs (especially for individual splits needing O(nd) memory) deserve a brief discussion.
- The methods depend on strong convexity–concavity parameters. While the authors note plans for adaptivity, a short remark on how mis-specification affects performance would be useful.

Quality:
Technically solid. The convergence analyses, complexity bounds, and acceleration scheme are carefully argued, and the monotone operator framework is appropriate for saddle points and beyond. The experimental evaluation supports the claims, including the role of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. Adding a concise proof sketch of the main convergence argument, a clearer recipe for non-uniform sampling in non-bilinear cases, and explicit stepsize choices in experiments would improve readability.

Originality:
High on the analysis side: extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with linear rates via monotone operator arguments appears novel. The algorithmic extensions are natural but non-trivial to analyze.

Significance:
Significant. Many modern ML problems involve non-separable losses/regularizers; having scalable, linearly convergent methods for saddle-point formulations is impactful. The monotone operator perspective may influence future work on variational inequalities and related problems.

Questions for the authors:
1) For general (non-bilinear) K, how should practitioners choose π (and possibly a factored split) and estimate L̄(π) and Li? Any heuristic or adaptive scheme?
2) How is σ set in the experiments for SVRG/SAGA and (stochastic) FB? Is there tuning or a theory-based fixed rule?
3) Can you provide intuition for why non-uniform sampling is critical for saddle points (versus convex minimization), perhaps tied to block Lipschitz constants and the asymmetry between x and y updates?
4) How robust is performance to misspecified λ and γ, and can they be estimated on the fly?
5) For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, what is the empirical effect of skipping the resampling step recommended by theory?

Pros for acceptance:
- First linear-rate variance-reduced methods for a broad class of saddle-point problems.
- Clean, general analysis via monotone operators; simpler proofs for FB.
- Practical guidance on non-uniform sampling; strong empirical results.
- Acceleration that outperforms accelerated batch methods on ill-conditioned instances.

Cons for acceptance:
- Core algorithms are straightforward extensions; novelty primarily in analysis.
- Some missing practical guidance (sampling/stepsize selection) and gaps between theory and practice (resampling in SAGA).
- Dependence on known strong convexity–concavity parameters.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper advances the state of the art on saddle-point optimization with both theoretical and practical contributions, and the monotone operator analysis is likely to have lasting impact.
