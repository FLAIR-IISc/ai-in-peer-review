This work addresses the problem of minimizing convex-concave saddle point problems and extends recent variance reduction methods to this setting. The authors provide a convergence analysis of their proposed methods and demonstrate that they can be readily accelerated using an extension of the Catalyst framework. Furthermore, they extend the methods to handle monotone operators and present supporting numerical results. 

However, the strong convexity assumption imposed on both the primal and dual problems feels overly restrictive. It would be more typical and potentially broader in applicability to assume strong convexity for only the primal *or* the dual problem. This limitation should be discussed more thoroughly. Additionally, the numerical evaluation could be strengthened by including more examples, particularly on problems where the proposed method demonstrably outperforms existing approaches.

Here's a slightly more detailed breakdown of my assessment:

The paper's contribution lies in adapting variance reduction techniques – a popular approach for optimization – to the challenging setting of convex-concave saddle point problems. These problems arise in many machine learning applications, such as min-max optimization for generative adversarial networks (GANs). The proposed methods are theoretically sound, with a convergence analysis that appears to be well-executed. The incorporation of the Catalyst framework for acceleration is a clever and practical extension.

The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the method beyond strictly convex-concave cases, which is a positive aspect. The numerical experiments provide a baseline validation of the approach, but they lack the comparative power needed to convincingly demonstrate its advantages. Currently, it is difficult to ascertain how the proposed methods perform relative to state-of-the-art solvers for similar problems. Including comparisons against well-established algorithms (e.g., those based on proximal gradient or accelerated gradient descent) on a wider range of problem instances would significantly strengthen the paper.

My main concern, as mentioned previously, is the requirement of strong convexity for both the primal and dual problems. This assumption is quite stringent and limits the practical applicability of the method. In many real-world scenarios, only one of the problems exhibits strong convexity, or neither does. Relaxing this assumption, even partially, would be a significant improvement. The authors should either justify this strong assumption or explore the possibility of extending their analysis to weaker conditions. A discussion of the implications of relaxing this assumption, even if it leads to slower convergence or requires different assumptions, would be valuable.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution. However, the overly strong convexity assumption and the limited numerical evaluation prevent it from being truly compelling. Addressing these points would significantly increase the paper’s impact and usefulness to the broader machine learning community.
