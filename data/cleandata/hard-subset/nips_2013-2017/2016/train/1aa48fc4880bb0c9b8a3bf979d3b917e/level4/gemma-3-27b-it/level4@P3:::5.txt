This paper presents a significant contribution to the optimization of saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning arising from duality and non-separable formulations. The authors extend stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to achieve the first linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems at large scale. A key insight is the application of monotone operator theory, allowing convergence analysis in a setting where standard convex minimization techniques fail and extending the results beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. This work addresses a critical need for efficient algorithms handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers, which are increasingly prevalent in modern machine learning applications.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical rigor, practical relevance, and clear presentation. The use of monotone operator theory is a novel and powerful technique, broadening the applicability of the proposed algorithms. The convergence analysis is thorough and provides valuable insights into the behavior of these methods. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms, particularly with non-uniform sampling, and establish their competitiveness against existing approaches. The connection to the “catalyst” framework further enhances the efficiency and practicality of the proposed methods. The provided proofs for simplified versions of existing algorithms like the forward-backward splitting are also welcome additions.

However, the paper could benefit from increased clarity in certain areas. Specifically, the choice of the probability distribution π for non-bilinear saddle-point problems remains somewhat opaque. Providing more detail on how π is determined and its influence on performance would strengthen the paper. Additionally, a more in-depth discussion of the step-size σ used in the experiments would be helpful. The observation that non-uniform sampling is crucial for saddle-point problems, yet not always essential for convex minimization, is intriguing, and elaborating on the underlying reasons for this difference would provide valuable insights. Including a proof sketch, if space permits, would enhance the accessibility of the core theoretical contribution.
