

This paper extends recent stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum structure, which can arise from two distinct types of splits: function-based and partial derivative-based. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of these algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies, demonstrating that non-uniform sampling is crucial for both theoretical guarantees and practical performance. The paper establishes convergence rates for these algorithms using monotone operator theory rather than convex minimization techniques, showing that the framework extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Experimental results on a binary classification problem with non-separable loss and regularizer confirm that non-uniform sampling and acceleration significantly outperform competitive batch methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides the first convergence analysis for variance-reduced algorithms on saddle-point problems, using monotone operators to establish linear convergence without relying on convex minimization techniques. Second, it carefully distinguishes between two types of problem splits (function-based and partial derivative-based) and demonstrates that saddle-point problems require splits into convex-concave terms, unlike convex minimization. Third, it shows that non-uniform sampling is essential for theoretical and practical efficiency, and that acceleration via Catalyst leads to algorithms that consistently outperform accelerated batch methods. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, with clear comparisons to existing work in convex optimization.

The paper excels in technical quality. The convergence proofs are rigorous and build upon the authors' novel use of monotone operator theory. The complexity analysis is comprehensive, considering different splits, sampling strategies, and acceleration schemes. The experimental section provides compelling evidence that non-uniform sampling and acceleration substantially improve performance, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the relationship between saddle-point problems and convex minimization, showing how their framework can be applied to problems where standard convex optimization techniques fail.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-organized and generally well-written, though some technical details could be presented more accessibly. The theoretical contributions are clearly articulated, and the experimental results are presented with appropriate metrics. The paper effectively demonstrates how the proposed algorithms differ from and improve upon existing batch and stochastic methods.

The originality is strong. While extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems might seem straightforward, the authors' theoretical analysis using monotone operators is novel, and the distinctions they make between different split types represent a significant conceptual advance. The paper's relationship to previous NIPS work is well-established, particularly in Section 6 where the authors discuss how their approach extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

The significance of this work is substantial. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., in robust optimization, supervised learning with non-separable losses, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning), but existing large-scale optimization methods are limited to separable cases. This paper provides the first linearly convergent algorithms for these non-separable saddle-point problems, with both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The results have clear implications for practitioners dealing with non-separable loss functions and regularizers.

My main concern relates to the theoretical complexity analysis. While the paper demonstrates that non-uniform sampling and acceleration provide substantial practical benefits, the theoretical complexity analysis (Tables 1 and 2) reveals a potential limitation: for high-dimensional problems where the number of samples n substantially exceeds the dimension d (a common scenario in machine learning), the factored split approach—used in the experiments—actually has a higher complexity than batch methods. Specifically, the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling under factored splits has complexity O(n + d) per iteration, which is not necessarily better than accelerated batch methods when n >> d. This contrasts with the standard convex minimization case, where variance reduction typically offers a clear theoretical advantage.

I would appreciate a more in-depth discussion of this matter, particularly addressing: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits for high-dimensional problems; (ii) under what conditions accelerated SVRG provides a clear theoretical advantage over batch methods; and (iii) whether the complexity bounds involving n and d are tight or could be improved. The paper's experimental results suggest practical advantages, but the theoretical analysis should clarify when these advantages are guaranteed.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature for saddle-point problems. The theoretical framework is novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems with non-separable structure. While the complexity analysis for high-dimensional problems warrants further clarification, the paper's contributions are significant and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the complexity concerns.
