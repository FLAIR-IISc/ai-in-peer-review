- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated methods to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems.
- Analyzes two formulations ("individual" and "factored" splits) with thorough complexity analysis under uniform and non-uniform sampling.
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to beat competitive batch algorithms.
- Theoretical results are novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial, with a clear comparison for bilinear saddle point problems.
- Findings align with established convex minimization literature, reinforcing the validity of the analysis.
- Main concern: a computational bottleneck—proximal operator cost of O(n + d) per iteration—which can negate theoretical gains over accelerated batch methods.
- In typical ML regimes (n >> d), factored splits (used in experiments) may be theoretically worse than batch forward–backward methods (Table 2), unlike standard convex minimization where variance reduction gives clear gains.
- Requests for clarification: (i) Are factored splits worse than individual splits and when? (ii) Which regimes make accelerated SVRG preferable to batch forward–backward? (iii) How tight are the n and d dependencies in the complexity bounds?
