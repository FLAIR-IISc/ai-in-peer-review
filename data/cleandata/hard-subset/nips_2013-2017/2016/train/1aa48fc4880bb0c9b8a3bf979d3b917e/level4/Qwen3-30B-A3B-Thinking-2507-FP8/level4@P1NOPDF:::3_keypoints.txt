- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems for individual and factored splits.  
- Provides thorough complexity analysis under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform).  
- Experiments (binary classification) show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical to outperform competitive batch methods.  
- Theoretical contributions are novel and technically non-trivial despite apparent straightforwardness of extension.  
- For bilinear saddle problems, gives clear comparisons between batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward (FB) methods, with compelling parallels to convex minimization theory.  
- Major concern: per-iteration proximal operator cost is O(n + d), creating a bottleneck in high-dimensional settings.  
- Because of this cost, overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch FB.  
- In typical regimes with n >> d, the factored split (used in experiments) can be worse than batch FB, as shown in Table 2.  
- Unlike convex minimization, variance reduction does not yield evident theoretical gains in complexity for these saddle problems.  
- Reviewer requests addressing three theoretical questions: (i) is the factored split inferior to the individual split? (ii) when does accelerated SVRG outperform batch FB? (iii) are the n and d dependencies tight?  
- Post-rebuttal responses satisfactorily addressed these concerns, but the reviewer maintains their original assessment.
