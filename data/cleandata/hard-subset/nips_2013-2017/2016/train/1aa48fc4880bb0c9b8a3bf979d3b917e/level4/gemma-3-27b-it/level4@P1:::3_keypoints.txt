- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle-point problems where objectives are not directly separable.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across splitting strategies (individual vs. factored) and sampling schemes (uniform vs. non-uniform).  
- Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
- Results are novel and technically non-trivial despite appearing as a straightforward extension of variance-reduction methods.  
- For bilinear saddle-point problems, authors give a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward–backward algorithms that matches known convex minimization intuition.  
- Major concern: computing the proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, creating a bottleneck that can negate incremental method benefits.  
- Consequently, overall complexity (even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch methods, especially when n >> d.  
- With factored splits (used in experiments), complexity can be worse than batch forward–back in common ML regimes (illustrated in Table 2).  
- Theoretical advantage of variance reduction for saddle-point problems is unclear from the provided bounds.  
- Reviewer requests deeper discussion addressing: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch forward–back, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence in the complexity bounds.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response but keeps original scores.
