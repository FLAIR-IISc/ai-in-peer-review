This paper considers the problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems and extends recent variance reduction methods, such as SVRG and SAGA, to this setting. The authors provide a convergence analysis for the proposed algorithms and demonstrate that their methods can be accelerated by adapting the Catalyst framework. The work also includes a generalization to the broader class of monotone operator problems and provides initial numerical results to support the theory.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a timely and important problem. The application of variance reduction techniques to the saddle-point setting is a valuable contribution, and the theoretical analysis appears to be sound. The connection to the Catalyst framework for acceleration is also a nice addition that broadens the practical utility of the proposed methods.

However, there are two primary areas where the paper could be significantly improved:

1.  **Strength of Assumptions:** A major concern is the theoretical assumption that the objective function is strongly-convex in the primal variable and strongly-concave in the dual variable. This is a very restrictive condition that limits the applicability of the analysis. Many important machine learning problems that fit the saddle-point formulation, such as regularized empirical risk minimization, are typically only strongly-convex in the primal variable but merely concave (not strongly-concave) in the dual. It would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution if the analysis could be relaxed to the more standard setting where strong convexity is required in only one of the two variables (either primal or dual).

2.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical evaluation is currently limited. While the provided results serve as a proof of concept, adding more comprehensive experiments would greatly enhance the paper's impact. It would be beneficial to compare the proposed methods against a wider range of state-of-the-art algorithms for saddle-point optimization. Furthermore, demonstrating the practical performance on real-world machine learning tasks, such as regularized AUC maximization, adversarial training, or robust optimization, would make the contribution more compelling and clearly illustrate the advantages of the proposed variance reduction and acceleration schemes.

In summary, the paper presents interesting ideas and a solid theoretical foundation. However, its overall impact is constrained by the strong theoretical assumptions and the limited scope of the numerical experiments. Addressing these points would make the paper a much stronger submission.
