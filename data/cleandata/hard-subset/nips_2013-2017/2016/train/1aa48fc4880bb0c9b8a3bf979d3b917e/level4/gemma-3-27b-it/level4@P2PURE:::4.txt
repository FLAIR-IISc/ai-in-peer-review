This paper extends stochastic variance reduction techniques – specifically SVRG and SAGA – to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, proving linear convergence. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of this variance reduction approach for these saddle-point problems. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, accommodating non-separable losses (e.g., in structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., fusion terms). A key contribution is the complex convergence analysis required for saddle-point problems, which the authors address by leveraging monotone operator theory – a significant departure from previous analyses focused on convex problems. This analysis also reveals the applicability of the extended SVRG and SAGA algorithms to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities arising in game theory. To handle the stochastic nature of the problem, the authors propose two gradient operator splitting strategies – element-wise and factored splits – tailored for SVRG and SAGA, respectively, within a stochastic forward-backward framework commonly used for saddle-point problems. Furthermore, they explore non-uniform sampling, and both theoretical and empirical results indicate its superiority over uniform sampling in reducing variance. Comprehensive experimental results, presented in the main text and appendix, provide strong support for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the methods’ effectiveness with both non-separable losses and regularizers. 

Minor comments: The acceleration technique described in Section 5, which follows [8] by adding a regularization term to encourage updates towards a specific iterate point, lacks clarity regarding the adaptive update of this iterate point, (\bar{x}, \bar{y}). While a convergence and acceleration proof is provided for SVRG, it is unclear whether the same theoretical results hold for SAGA. Finally, to avoid potential confusion, different notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K is recommended.
