This paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems and shows how to solve them by combining existing stochastic variance-reduction techniques (e.g., SVRG and SAGA) with proximal operators. The analysis is carried out in the monotone-operator framework and establishes linear convergence. The authors also present an accelerated variant and analyze a non-uniform sampling scheme.

Novelty and originality: The contributions are significant. The theoretical analysis is thorough and offers new insights for saddle-point problems, notably extending variance-reduced methods to non-separable objectives. Among the extensions, the applicability to non-separable functions is particularly important.

Technical quality and presentation: While I appreciate the theoretical contributions, the presentation could be clearer. In particular, the connection to monotone operators should be made explicit earlier and more prominently. Currently it appears as an “extension” in Section 6, yet the core analysis is formulated in terms of monotone operators; surfacing this framework in the main exposition would greatly improve accessibility.

Experimental evaluation: The empirical section is limited to two datasets; broader evidence would strengthen the paper. For the separable case, a comparison against accelerated primal–dual methods is missing. In particular, please compare to the stochastic variant of the Chambolle–Pock algorithm (Zhang and Xiao, 2015): http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf and include this reference.

Points to clarify:
1) Assumptions (A)–(C): How strong are these assumptions, especially (A)? Do they hold for the standard SVM saddle-point formulation?
2) Methodological distinction: What are the precise differences between your method and stochastic primal–dual methods? A conceptual and algorithmic comparison (update rules, oracle access, variance reduction mechanism, and convergence guarantees) would be helpful.
3) Theorem 2: What exactly is the constant μ? Is it the strong monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? Please make this explicit and explain how Theorem 2’s rate compares directly to Theorem 1 under matched assumptions and parameter regimes.
4) Monotonicity assumptions: The transition from (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity in the appendix is confusing. Do (A)–(C) imply (strong) monotonicity of the relevant operator, or are additional conditions required? Please state the logical dependencies clearly in the main text.

Minor issues:
- Notation for L: There is a mix-up between using L as a condition number and as a Lipschitz constant. The paper states both “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” and “we need the Lipschitz constant L.” Please disambiguate and use distinct symbols (e.g., L for Lipschitz, κ for condition number).
- Forward–Backward algorithm: Please add an appropriate reference.
