

The authors present a significant contribution by adapting variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA) to derive the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems. The primary technical novelty lies in their use of monotone operator theory to establish convergence, which is essential since standard convex minimization analysis does not apply. This approach not only resolves the convergence guarantee but also broadens applicability to variational inequality problems. The framework enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers—common in machine learning models—by reformulating them as saddle point problems. The paper successfully establishes the first linear convergence rates for such problems, addressing a key challenge in the field. The proof technique, interpreting saddle point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, is both novel and potentially widely applicable. Additionally, the authors provide simplified proofs for algorithms like the forward-backward splitting (and its stochastic variant), enhancing accessibility. The proposed algorithms with non-uniform sampling demonstrate strong empirical performance, often outperforming prior alternatives. Overall, the work makes an important theoretical advancement, with a compelling analysis approach.  

However, the paper’s focus on the proof technique as the core contribution (rather than the algorithms themselves, which are straightforward extensions of existing methods) warrants greater emphasis. The authors should consider including a concise proof sketch in the main text if space permits, to aid readers in grasping the technical innovation. While the paper is generally well-written, several aspects require clarification:  
1. For non-bilinear saddle-point problems, the paper specifies non-uniform sampling but does not define the sampling distribution \(\pi\). Clarification on how \(\pi\) is constructed for general cases is needed.  
2. The step size \(\sigma\) used in experiments is not justified. The authors should explain how \(\sigma\) is selected and provide intuition for why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle point problems but less critical for convex minimization. A discussion of this distinction would strengthen the paper.
