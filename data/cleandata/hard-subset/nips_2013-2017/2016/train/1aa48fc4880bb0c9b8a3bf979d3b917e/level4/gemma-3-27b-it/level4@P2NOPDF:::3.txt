This paper extends recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants – to address strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite-sum structure. The authors consider both individual and factored data splits, and provide a comprehensive complexity analysis for these algorithms under different sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform). Experimental results on a binary classification task demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for achieving performance superior to competitive batch algorithms. 

While applying variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems appears conceptually straightforward, the theoretical results presented here are genuinely novel and technically demanding. The authors establish a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms for the important case of bilinear saddle point problems. This comparison mirrors known results for convex minimization and feels intuitively sound.

However, a significant concern remains. Although solving the saddle point problem mitigates the non-separability of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear limited by the O(n+d) computational cost of the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, the overall complexity – even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2) – is not always advantageous compared to accelerated batch methods.  Specifically, when the number of data points (n) significantly exceeds the dimensionality (d) – a common scenario in machine learning – the complexity under factored splits (employed in the simulations) is actually worse than that of batch FB algorithms, as highlighted in Table 2.  

In contrast to the benefits typically observed in convex minimization, I did not observe a clear, substantial theoretical advantage to using variance reduction in this setting. I recommend the authors provide a more detailed discussion of this point, addressing the following questions: i) Is a factored split theoretically inferior to an individual split? ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB? iii) Is the derived dependence on *n* and *d* tight?
