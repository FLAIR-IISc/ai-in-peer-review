

The paper extends recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their accelerated variants via Catalyst) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with finite sum structure, which arises from two types of splits: individual or factored. The authors provide a thorough complexity analysis of these algorithms under different splits and sampling strategies (uniform/non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that nonuniform sampling and acceleration are critical for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While the extension of variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems may appear straightforward, the established results are notably novel, and the technical development is non-trivial. For the important bilinear saddle point problem, the authors present a clear comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental Forward-Backward (FB) algorithms, which parallels standard results for convex minimization and is well-motivated.  

However, a major concern arises: while solving the saddle point problem addresses the non-separability of loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms suffer from a fundamental bottleneck—computing the proximal operator at each iteration incurs an $O(n + d)$ cost. Consequently, the overall complexity, even for accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling (as shown in Tables 1 and 2), is not necessarily superior to accelerated batch versions. Specifically, when $n \gg d$ (typical in machine learning applications), the complexity under factored splits (used in simulations) is worse than batch FB algorithms, as demonstrated in Table 2. Unlike convex minimization, where variance reduction typically yields clear theoretical advantages, this benefit is not evident here. I urge the authors to provide an in-depth discussion addressing:  
i) Whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits,  
ii) Under what conditions accelerated SVRG is favorable against batch FB,  
iii) Whether the dependence on $n$ and $d$ is tight.
