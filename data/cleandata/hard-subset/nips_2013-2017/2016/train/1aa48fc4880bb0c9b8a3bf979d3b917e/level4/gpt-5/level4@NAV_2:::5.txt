This paper adapts variance-reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex–concave saddle-point problems with decomposable structure and establishes linear convergence. Because standard convex minimization analyses do not directly transfer, the core contribution is a convergence proof based on monotone operators. This viewpoint broadens applicability beyond saddle points to variational inequalities. The approach provides a way to handle non-separable losses and regularizers through a saddle-point formulation. The authors also give simpler arguments for forward–backward (FB) splitting (and its stochastic variant). Empirically, variants with non-uniform sampling are competitive and often outperform prior methods.

This is an important and timely contribution: it provides the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems relevant to learning with non-separable components. The proof strategy—casting saddle-point optimization as finding zeros of a monotone operator—is elegant and potentially broadly useful. While the algorithmic modifications are relatively straightforward extensions of existing SVRG/SAGA ideas, the analysis is the main novelty. If space allows, it would be helpful to include additional details or a concise proof sketch in the main text to aid readers in following the key steps. The paper is generally clear and well-written, and the experimental results support the claims that non-uniform sampling leads to strong practical performance.

A few points would benefit from clarification:
1. For problems beyond bilinear saddle points, what specific non-uniform sampling distribution π do the authors recommend or analyze?
2. How is the step size σ selected in the experiments?
Relatedly, do the authors have intuition for why non-uniform sampling appears especially important in the saddle-point setting, whereas it is not always essential in convex minimization? A short discussion could strengthen the narrative.
