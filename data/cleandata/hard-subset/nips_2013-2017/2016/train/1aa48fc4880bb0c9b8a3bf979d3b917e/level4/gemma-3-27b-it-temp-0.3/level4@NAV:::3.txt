This paper investigates the application of incremental algorithms – specifically SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated versions – to strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite-sum structure, arising from both individual and factored data splits. The authors present a comprehensive complexity analysis of these algorithms under various splitting strategies and sampling schemes (uniform and non-uniform). Empirical results on a binary classification task demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration for achieving performance competitive with batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction techniques to saddle point problems appears conceptually simple, the results presented are genuinely novel and technically demanding. The paper offers a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms for the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, mirroring established results for convex minimization and aligning with my understanding of the field. 

However, a significant concern remains. Although solving the saddle point problem addresses the challenge of non-separable loss functions, the proposed incremental algorithms appear limited by the O(n+d) computational cost of the proximal operator at each iteration. Consequently, even with accelerated SVRG and non-uniform sampling (as detailed in Tables 1 and 2), the overall complexity isn’t consistently superior to accelerated batch methods.  Notably, when the number of data points *n* significantly exceeds the dimension *d* – a common scenario in machine learning – the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulation) is actually higher than that of batch FB algorithms, as shown in Table 2.  In contrast to convex minimization, I do not observe a clear, substantial theoretical advantage to employing variance reduction in this context. 

I recommend the authors provide a more detailed discussion of these points, specifically addressing: i) whether factored splits are inherently less efficient than individual splits, ii) under what conditions accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and iii) the tightness of the dependence on *n* and *d*.
