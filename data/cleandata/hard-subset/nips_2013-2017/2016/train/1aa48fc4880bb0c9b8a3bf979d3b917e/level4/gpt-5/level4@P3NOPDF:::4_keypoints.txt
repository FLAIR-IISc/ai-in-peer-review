- Extends SVRG/SAGA to strongly convex–concave saddle-point problems with established linear convergence.
- Framework covers non-separable problems (structured prediction losses, non-separable regularizers like fusion).
- Analysis uses monotone operator theory, explaining why standard convex-minimization proofs don't transfer.
- Shows applicability beyond optimization to certain variational inequalities.
- Studies two stochastic operator-splitting strategies (element-wise and factored) and analyzes non-uniform sampling.
- Empirical results support theory and show improvements over uniform sampling and non-variance-reduced baselines.
- Strength: clear and significant theoretical extension with linear-convergence guarantees.
- Strength: monotone operator approach is technically sound and well-motivated for saddle points.
- Strength: ability to handle non-separable losses/regularizers broadens practical applicability.
- Strength: comparison of element-wise vs. factored splits offers useful algorithmic choices.
- Strength: non-uniform sampling treated theoretically and empirically with consistent gains.
- Weakness: Acceleration scheme (additional regularization toward an anchor) lacks practical policy for choosing/updating the reference and tuning sensitivity.
- Weakness: SAGA’s theoretical scope is unclear—linear-convergence and acceleration proofs are given for SVRG but not shown for SAGA.
- Weakness: Relationship/ interchangeability of element-wise vs. factored splits across SVRG and SAGA is unclear; needs empirical ablations and guidance by problem structure.
- Weakness: Results rely on strong convexity–concavity—extension to convex–concave (sublinear rates) is not discussed.
- Weakness: Practical guidance on selecting non-uniform sampling probabilities and sensitivity to misspecification is missing.
- Weakness: Proximal/resolvent computations for non-separable regularizers (e.g., fusion) need clarification on efficiency and any approximations used.
- Weakness: Per-iteration cost and memory overhead for SVRG/SAGA saddle-point variants, especially with non-separable terms, are not clearly quantified; wall-clock comparisons and a complexity table requested.
- Minor: Disambiguate notation (K used for both function and matrix).
- Minor: Specify how the anchor (x̄, ȳ) is initialized, updated, and refreshed (restarts/averaging).
- Minor: Give a concrete variational-inequality/game-theory example to illustrate applicability.
- Minor: Report wall-clock times, variance across runs, and sensitivity to step-size/sampling to complement iteration plots.
- Minor: Clean repetitive statements in the introduction for readability.
- Overall: Strong, timely contribution with solid theory and experiments; monotone operator perspective is elegant, but practical aspects (anchor updates, SAGA scope, notation) should be clarified.
