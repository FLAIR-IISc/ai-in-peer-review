This paper addresses saddle-point problems exhibiting a convex-concave structure. The authors demonstrate how existing stochastic variance reduction techniques – specifically SVRG and SAGA – can be effectively applied to these problems through the incorporation of a proximal operator. Their analysis, framed within the context of monotone operators, establishes linear convergence guarantees. Furthermore, they propose an accelerated variant and investigate a non-uniform sampling scheme.

**Novelty/Originality:** The contributions of this work are substantial. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides novel insights into the behavior of algorithms for saddle-point problems. The authors explore numerous extensions, with the applicability of their method to non-separable functions being particularly noteworthy.

**Technical Quality:** While I found the theoretical contributions of this paper to be strong, the presentation could be improved for clarity. I believe a more explicit connection to the underlying theory of monotone operators would significantly enhance accessibility. Currently, this connection appears as an extension in Section 6, yet the core analysis relies heavily on monotone operator properties. Regarding the experimental evaluation, I would have appreciated results on a wider range of datasets – beyond the current two. For cases involving separable functions, a comparison to accelerated methods, such as the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm [zhanga15], would be beneficial. Please include this reference in your submission. 

I have a few specific points that require clarification from the authors:

1.  Could the authors elaborate on the strength of assumptions A-C, particularly assumption (A)? I am curious whether these assumptions hold when applied to saddle-point problems arising from, for example, Support Vector Machines (SVMs).
2.  I am not fully clear on the distinctions between existing stochastic primal-dual methods and the proposed approach. A more detailed explanation of these differences would be helpful.
3.  In Theorem 2, the constant μ is used. Is this the same monotonicity constant defined in the appendix? Additionally, how does the convergence rate presented in Theorem 2 compare to that of Theorem 1?
4.  The transition from assumptions (A)-(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is somewhat unclear. Do assumptions (A)-(C) directly imply strong monotonicity?

**Minor Issues:**

*   There is some inconsistency in the use of the constant *L*, which is sometimes referred to as the condition number and at other times as a Lipschitz constant: “The quantity *L* represents the condition number of the problem” and “we need the Lipschitz constant *L*.”
*   Regarding the Forward-Backward algorithm, please include a relevant reference.
