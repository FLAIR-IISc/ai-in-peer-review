This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors establish linear convergence guarantees for these extended algorithms, a significant contribution given the challenges posed by saddle-point formulations compared to standard convex optimization. The work highlights the applicability of these techniques to a range of machine learning problems featuring non-separable losses and regularizers, exemplified by structured output prediction and certain regularization techniques. A key theoretical advance is framing the analysis using monotone operators, broadening the scope of the proposed algorithms beyond traditional saddle-point problems to include variational inequalities. The paper also thoughtfully examines different gradient operator splitting strategies and demonstrates the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency, both theoretically and empirically.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon recent advances in stochastic optimization for convex problems (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) and extending them to the more complex setting of saddle-point problems. The connection to monotone operator theory [4, 5] is insightful, providing a more general framework for analysis and demonstrating the wider applicability of the proposed algorithms. The discussion of the two gradient splitting options—element-wise and factored—and their impact on storage and computational complexity is valuable. The experimental validation, with results presented in the main text and extended in the appendix, supports the theoretical findings and showcases the practical effectiveness of the methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first linear convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems, a notable advancement.
*   **Generality:** The use of monotone operator theory expands the applicability of the algorithms beyond saddle-point problems to include variational inequalities.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a common challenge in machine learning – dealing with non-separable objectives – and offers effective solutions.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The investigation of different gradient splitting strategies and the importance of non-uniform sampling demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results confirm the theoretical findings and showcase the performance benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Adaptive Step Size/Momentum:** The discussion of acceleration (Section 5) mentions the need for an additional regularization term and the selection of  (\bar{x}, \bar{y}), but lacks detail on *how* this parameter should be adaptively updated for optimal performance. While not a fatal flaw, elaborating on this practical aspect would enhance the paper’s usability.
*   **SAGA Acceleration:**  The paper states the same acceleration results should hold for SAGA, but does not explicitly provide the corresponding theoretical analysis. Adding this would strengthen the completeness of the work.
*   **Notation:** The use of the same symbol 'K' for both a general function and the design matrix is potentially confusing. While not critically problematic, distinct notation would improve clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization, extending established methods to a challenging and practically relevant problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by experimental results.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   The findings have the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of specific guidance on adaptive parameter tuning for the acceleration technique and the missing SAGA acceleration proof are minor weaknesses, but could be addressed with further refinement. However, these do not fundamentally detract from the paper's overall contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, the analysis is thorough, and the experimental validation is convincing. While there are minor areas for improvement, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance.
