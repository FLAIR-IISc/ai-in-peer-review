This paper presents a significant contribution by extending the stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to solve strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems. The authors propose the first large-scale algorithms for this problem class that achieve a linear convergence rate. The core idea is to apply the variance reduction principle to a forward-backward splitting algorithm tailored for saddle-point optimization. The paper's analysis departs from the standard proofs for convex minimization, instead leveraging the more general framework of monotone operators to establish convergence. This theoretical approach not only provides a sound basis for the saddle-point case but also demonstrates that the proposed algorithms are applicable to a broader set of problems, including variational inequalities. The authors also explore practical and theoretical enhancements, such as two different ways to split the gradient operator ("factored" and "element-wise"), the critical role of non-uniform sampling for improving efficiency, and an acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** This work addresses a notable gap in the literature. While variance reduction techniques are well-established for convex minimization, their extension to the saddle-point setting with a rigorous linear convergence proof is novel and highly significant. The proposed methods are directly applicable to a range of important machine learning problems involving non-separable components, such as structured output prediction with AUC-based losses or models with complex regularizers like total variation, where existing primal or dual variance-reduced methods cannot be applied.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength of the paper. The choice to use monotone operator theory for the convergence proof is elegant and powerful. Unlike in standard convex minimization, the operators in saddle-point problems are not necessarily co-coercive, making traditional analyses inapplicable. The authors' approach is more general and correctly handles the problem structure, demonstrating a deep understanding of the underlying theory. This rigorous foundation increases confidence in the algorithm's correctness and stability.

3.  **Comprehensive Treatment:** The paper provides a thorough investigation of the proposed methods. It carefully analyzes the computational complexity under different settings, clearly showing the theoretical advantages of variance reduction and non-uniform sampling over batch and standard stochastic algorithms (as summarized in Table 1). The inclusion of an acceleration scheme further strengthens the practical utility of the work, resulting in an algorithm that is theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods in many regimes.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing and effectively support the paper's theoretical claims. On tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers, the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants, especially when combined with acceleration and non-uniform sampling, are shown to substantially outperform existing methods. The comparison with primal methods on a separable problem further clarifies the trade-offs and highlights the unique value of the proposed saddle-point framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity on Adaptive Acceleration:** In Section 5, the authors mention that the reference point `(\bar{x}, \bar{y})` in the accelerated algorithm can be updated "more aggressively to speed-up the algorithm." This point is left underdeveloped. For better clarity and reproducibility, it would be beneficial to provide more specific details or intuition on how this adaptive update could be implemented in practice.

2.  **Incomplete Analysis for Accelerated SAGA:** The paper provides a convergence proof for the accelerated SVRG algorithm. However, it only states that "the same scheme should work for SAGA" without providing a formal proof or justification. While this claim is plausible, including a theoretical analysis for accelerated SAGA would make the paper's contributions more complete and self-contained.

3.  **Minor Notational Ambiguity:** The symbol `K` is used to denote both the generic smooth convex-concave function `K(x,y)` and the data design matrix in the examples. While context usually makes the meaning clear, using distinct symbols (e.g., `\mathcal{K}` for the function and `K` for the matrix) would eliminate any potential for confusion and improve the paper's overall readability.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful extension of variance reduction methods. The strengths, particularly the originality of the contribution and the rigorousness of the analysis, far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarity and completeness and could be addressed in a future version. The paper makes a solid contribution to the optimization and machine learning communities.
