- Main contribution: Extends incremental variance reduction (SVRG, SAGA, accelerated via Catalyst) to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems with systematic analysis.  
- Scope of analysis: Covers two problem splits (individual and factored) and multiple sampling schemes (uniform/non-uniform), with comprehensive complexity comparisons.  
- Theoretical novelty: Provides clear complexity comparisons for bilinear saddle points between batch, stochastic, and incremental FB methods, relating to convex minimization intuition despite saddle-point non-separability.  
- Core strength: Rigorous adaptation of variance reduction to saddle-point settings, handling primal–dual interplay and obtaining tight complexity bounds under different splits and samplings.  
- Empirical finding: Experiments on binary classification show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch methods, matching theoretical claims when n and d are comparable.  
- Primary concern: Per-iteration cost for the factored split is O(n + d), producing total complexity (e.g., O((n + d) log(1/ε))) that can be worse than accelerated batch FB when n ≫ d.  
- Practical implication: In typical ML regimes with n ≫ d, the factored split can exceed batch complexity, undermining the usual incremental-method motivation of lower per-iteration cost.  
- Three critical questions: (i) Is factored split theoretically disadvantaged vs individual split when n ≫ d? (ii) For which n/d ratios or structures is accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling preferable to batch FB? (iii) Are the n and d dependencies in bounds tight or improvable?  
- Requested clarifications: Authors should contrast practical per-iteration operations with theoretical iteration counts and explicitly discuss the n ≫ d regime, possibly with synthetic experiments or asymptotic analysis.  
- Experimental limitation: Current experiments only cover regimes where n and d are comparable and do not address the theoretical gap for large n/d.  
- Post-rebuttal resolution: Authors demonstrated factored split advantages in structured cases (e.g., low-rank duals) and clarified conditions where incremental methods win; reviewer is satisfied and maintains original assessment.
