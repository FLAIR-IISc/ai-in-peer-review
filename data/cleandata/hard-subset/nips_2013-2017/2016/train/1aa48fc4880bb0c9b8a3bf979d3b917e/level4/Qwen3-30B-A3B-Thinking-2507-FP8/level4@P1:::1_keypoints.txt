- Paper extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, giving first large-scale linearly convergent stochastic methods for this setting.  
- Introduces a novel monotone-operator-based theoretical framework proving linear convergence and applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Key insight: must split into convex-concave terms (not convex terms alone) for correct variance reduction in saddle-point problems.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is critical for both theoretical rates and practical efficiency.  
- Proposes an accelerated variant using a modified catalyst framework that outperforms batch methods.  
- Novelty: first linearly convergent stochastic variance reduction methods for saddle-point and non-separable problems, important for ML tasks like non-separable losses and robust optimization.  
- Technical quality: strong, thorough convergence analysis and extension to variational inequalities, but monotone-operator connection should be presented earlier.  
- Experiments: limited to two datasets (sido, rcv1); experimental validation should include more benchmarks.  
- Baselines: for separable problems, should compare to accelerated stochastic primal-dual methods (e.g., stochastic Chambolle-Pock, Zhang & Xiao 2015).  
- Clarify assumptions (A)–(C), especially strength of (A) and whether they hold for SVM-induced saddle-point problems.  
- Clarify distinction from existing stochastic primal-dual approaches and how the proposed method improves or differs.  
- In Theorem 2, clarify whether μ is the monotonicity constant from the appendix and compare Theorem 2’s convergence guarantees to Theorem 1.  
- Make explicit whether assumptions (A)–(C) imply strong monotonicity in the appendix.  
- Minor issues: ambiguous use of L (condition number vs Lipschitz constant) and missing proper reference for the Forward-Backward algorithm.  
- Strengths summary: rigorous theoretical foundation, valuable analysis of non-uniform sampling, broader applicability via monotone operators, and empirical gains on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Weaknesses summary: monotone-operator perspective is under-integrated, experiments are limited, comparisons to relevant accelerated methods are incomplete, and assumptions need clearer discussion for ML applications.
