- Extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems.  
- Considers two problem splits (individual and factored) and analyzes complexity under uniform and nonuniform sampling.  
- Experiments (binary classification example) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are crucial to outperform batch algorithms.  
- Results are novel and technically non-trivial; provides a clear, intuitive comparison for the bilinear saddle point case.  
- Major concern: per-iteration proximal computation costs O(n + d), creating a bottleneck that can negate incremental algorithm advantages.  
- When n >> d, overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling) may not improve over accelerated batch FB.  
- Under factored splits the complexity can be worse than batch forward–backward (Table 2).  
- Reviewer did not find a clear theoretical advantage of variance reduction in this setting compared to convex minimization.  
- Requests further discussion on: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically worse than individual splits, (ii) conditions where accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of n and d dependence.
