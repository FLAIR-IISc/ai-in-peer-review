This paper extends recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants – to address strongly convex-concave saddle point problems with a finite-sum structure. The authors consider both individual and factored data splits and provide a comprehensive complexity analysis under various sampling strategies (uniform and non-uniform). Experimental results on a binary classification problem demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are crucial for achieving performance superior to competitive batch algorithms. While the application of variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle point problems appears conceptually straightforward, the results presented are demonstrably novel and technically challenging.

The paper offers a clear comparison between batch, stochastic, and incremental Frank-Wolfe (FB) algorithms specifically for the important case of bilinear saddle point problems. This comparison reveals parallels with established understanding in the convex minimization setting, and the findings are logically consistent.

However, a significant concern arises regarding the computational cost of the proximal operator. Solving saddle point problems addresses the challenges posed by non-separable loss functions, but the proposed incremental algorithms appear to encounter a fundamental bottleneck: an O(n+d) cost for computing the proximal operator at each iteration, where *n* is the number of data points and *d* is the dimension of the problem. Consequently, even with accelerated SVRG and non-uniform sampling (as summarized in Tables 1 and 2), the overall complexity isn't consistently better than that of accelerated batch methods. 

Specifically, when *n* is substantially larger than *d* – a common scenario in many machine learning applications – the complexity under factored splits (the configuration used in the simulation) is, in fact, *worse* than that of batch FB algorithms, according to Table 2.  Unlike the well-established benefits for convex minimization, the theoretical advantages of variance reduction in this saddle point setting are not immediately apparent. 

I recommend the authors provide a more in-depth discussion of these points, specifically addressing the following questions:

i) Is a factored split inherently less efficient than an individual split from a theoretical standpoint?
ii) Under what conditions does accelerated SVRG offer a clear advantage over accelerated batch FB algorithms?
iii) Are the derived complexity bounds on *n* and *d* tight, or is there potential for further refinement?
