- Extends SVRG/SAGA and accelerated (Catalyst) variants to strongly convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with individual and factored splits.  
- Provides comprehensive complexity analysis across split types and sampling strategies (uniform vs non-uniform).  
- Experiments show non-uniform sampling and acceleration are important to outperform competitive batch algorithms.  
- Novel and technically demanding extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems; analysis appears rigorous and uses monotone operator theory.  
- Includes clear comparison for bilinear saddle-point problems between batch, stochastic, and incremental FB algorithms, paralleling convex minimization results.  
- Central concern: proximal operator cost is O(n + d) per iteration, creating a potential computational bottleneck.  
- When n >> d (common in ML), factored splits used in simulations can yield worse complexity than batch FB (Tables 1–2), reducing practical advantages.  
- Theoretical advantages of variance reduction in this saddle-point setting are not consistently evident compared to batch methods.  
- Key open questions for authors: (i) Is a factored split theoretically less efficient than an individual split? (ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG truly favorable over batch FB? (iii) How tight are the complexity bounds and could hidden constants materially affect performance?  
- Weakness: limited discussion of the impact of constant factors hidden in Big-O bounds.  
- Strengths: novelty, thoroughness, theoretical rigor, extension to variational inequalities, solid experimental validation, and clear presentation.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important problem, offers novel theory and experiments, and is well-written.  
- Rejection concerns: proximal cost and unclear theoretical/practical advantages across common n, d regimes.  
- Overall assessment: solid contribution but limitations (proximal bottleneck, parameter dependencies) lead to a “weak accept” recommendation.
