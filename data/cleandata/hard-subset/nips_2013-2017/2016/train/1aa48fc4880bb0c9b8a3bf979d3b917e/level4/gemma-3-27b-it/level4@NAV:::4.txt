This paper presents an extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, accompanied by a proof of linear convergence. The effectiveness of this variance reduction approach for saddle-point problems is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The proposed algorithms are applicable to a range of non-separable saddle-point problems, accommodating non-separable loss functions (such as those arising in structured output prediction) and regularizers (e.g., fusion terms). A key contribution lies in the complex convergence analysis required for saddle-point problems, where the authors leverage monotone operator theory – a significant departure from prior work on convex problems. This analysis further reveals applicability to broader problem classes, including variational inequalities relevant to game theory. The authors explore two gradient operator splitting strategies – element-wise and factored – within the stochastic forward-backward framework commonly used for saddle-point problems. Importantly, they demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that variance reduction performs better with non-uniform sampling compared to uniform sampling. The experimental results, detailed in the main text and the appendix, robustly corroborate the theoretical findings and showcase the efficacy of the proposed methods with both non-separable losses and regularizers. 

Minor points for consideration: The acceleration technique in Section 5, employing an additional regularization term inspired by [8], lacks clarity regarding the adaptive updating of the reference point (\bar{x}, \bar{y}). While a convergence and acceleration proof is presented for SVRG, it remains open whether similar theoretical guarantees hold for SAGA. Finally, to avoid potential confusion, differentiating the notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K would be beneficial.
