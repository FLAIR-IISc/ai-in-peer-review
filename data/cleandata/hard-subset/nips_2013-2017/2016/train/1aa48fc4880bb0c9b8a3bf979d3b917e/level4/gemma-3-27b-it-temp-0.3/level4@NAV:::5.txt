This paper presents the first linearly convergent algorithms for solving saddle point problems, leveraging variance reduction techniques such as SVRG and SAGA. A key contribution lies in the authors’ innovative approach to analyzing these non-convex problems; circumventing the limitations of traditional convex minimization analysis, they demonstrate convergence through the lens of monotone operators. This framework not only establishes convergence for saddle point problems but also extends to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The proposed methodology offers a pathway to address challenges posed by non-separable loss functions and regularizers, prevalent in many machine learning applications. 

The work’s significance stems from achieving a linear convergence rate – a crucial advancement for tackling these complex problems. The authors’ reinterpretation of saddle point problems as monotone operator zero-finding problems is a particularly insightful and potentially impactful technique. Furthermore, the paper provides simplified proofs for established algorithms like FB splitting and its stochastic counterpart. Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, employing non-uniform sampling, achieve competitive performance, often surpassing existing methods. Overall, this work represents a valuable extension to the field, introducing a novel and promising analytical approach.

Given the strength of the theoretical contribution – specifically the proof technique – the authors might consider expanding on its details within the main paper, perhaps through the inclusion of a proof sketch, if space allows. While the paper is generally well-written and clear, some aspects could benefit from further clarification. Specifically: 1) The definition of the non-uniform sampling distribution, \pi, should be explicitly stated for problem classes beyond bilinear saddle-point problems. 2) The step size selection, \sigma, used in the experiments warrants further explanation. Finally, a discussion exploring the authors’ intuition regarding the necessity of non-uniform sampling for saddle point problems – a feature less critical in convex minimization – would be a valuable addition.
