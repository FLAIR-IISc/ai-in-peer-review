This paper considers the problem of solving large-scale convex-concave saddle point problems. The primary contribution is the extension of recent variance reduction techniques, such as SVRG, to this setting. The authors propose a new algorithm and provide a detailed convergence analysis. Additionally, the paper shows that the proposed method can be further improved by applying an acceleration scheme based on the Catalyst framework. The work also includes an extension of the method to the more general class of monotone operator problems and provides initial numerical results.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The extension of variance reduction methods to the saddle point setting is a timely and important research direction. Given the success of these methods in standard convex minimization, exploring their applicability to min-max problems is a logical and valuable contribution to the optimization community.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis for the proposed algorithm. The theoretical results are clearly presented and appear to be sound.

3.  **Generality:** The inclusion of an accelerated variant using a Catalyst-like framework demonstrates a path to achieving optimal complexity rates. The discussion on extending the approach to monotone operators also broadens the applicability and theoretical scope of the work.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Restrictive Theoretical Assumptions:** The main limitation of this work is the assumption that the objective function is strongly convex in the primal variable and strongly concave in the dual variable. This "joint strong convexity" assumption is very restrictive and excludes many important applications of saddle point problems (e.g., constrained optimization where only the primal objective is strongly convex, or regularized problems like SVMs). The standard and more practical assumption in the literature is strong convexity in *either* the primal *or* the dual variable, but not necessarily both. The authors should discuss the practical implications of this assumption and, if possible, relax their analysis to align with the more common and general setting. Without this, the applicability of the proposed method is limited to a narrow class of problems.

2.  **Insufficient Numerical Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough to fully validate the practical performance of the proposed methods. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding more numerical examples that:
    *   Compare the proposed algorithm against a wider range of state-of-the-art baselines for saddle point optimization.
    *   Demonstrate performance on diverse datasets and problem types, including those that arise in machine learning applications.
    *   Include an ablation study to clearly illustrate the benefits of the variance reduction and acceleration components separately.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a promising approach for solving saddle point problems by leveraging variance reduction techniques. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the core idea is valuable. However, the work in its current form is constrained by a very strong theoretical assumption of joint strong convexity/concavity and a lack of thorough empirical evidence. Addressing these two points, particularly by relaxing the theoretical assumptions and expanding the experimental section, would significantly enhance the impact and contribution of the paper.
