This paper extends recent incremental algorithms (SVRG, SAGA, and their Catalyst-accelerated variants) to solve strongly convex-concave saddle point problems that have a finite-sum structure. The authors analyze two types of formulations, termed "individual" and "factored" splits, and provide a thorough complexity analysis of these algorithms under both uniform and non-uniform sampling strategies. Experiments on a binary classification example demonstrate that non-uniform sampling and acceleration are critical for the proposed methods to outperform competitive batch algorithms.

Although extending variance-reduction techniques to saddle point problems might seem straightforward, the theoretical results established here are novel and the technical analysis is non-trivial. For the important case of bilinear saddle point problems, the authors present a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms. The findings are consistent with established knowledge from the convex minimization literature, which reinforces the validity of the analysis.

However, my primary concern is a potential computational bottleneck in the proposed incremental algorithms. While the saddle point formulation effectively addresses the non-separability of certain loss functions, the proposed methods appear to incur a cost of O(n+d) for computing the proximal operator at each iteration. As a result, the overall complexity, even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, is not necessarily better than the accelerated batch version, as shown in Tables 1 and 2. Specifically, in the common machine learning setting where the number of samples (n) is substantially larger than the dimension (d), the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulations) is theoretically worse than that of batch forward-backward algorithms (Table 2). This contrasts with the standard convex minimization setting, where variance-reduced methods typically offer a clear and substantial theoretical advantage over batch methods. From a theoretical perspective, this advantage is not evident here.

I would suggest the authors provide a more in-depth discussion on this matter and clarify the following questions:
i) Are factored splits theoretically worse than individual splits, and under what conditions?
ii) In what specific regimes is the accelerated SVRG variant favorable compared to the batch forward-backward algorithm?
iii) How tight is the theoretical dependence on n and d in the complexity bounds?
