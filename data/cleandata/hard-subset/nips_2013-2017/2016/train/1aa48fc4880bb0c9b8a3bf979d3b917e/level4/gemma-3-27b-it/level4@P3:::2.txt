This paper addresses the challenging problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, using stochastic variance reduction methods like SVRG and SAGA. The authors extend these methods to the saddle-point setting and provide a convergence analysis, demonstrating linear convergence under suitable assumptions. A key contribution is the application of a “catalyst” framework for acceleration, resulting in an algorithm demonstrably superior to accelerated batch methods. Furthermore, the work extends the analysis beyond saddle-point problems to the more general class of monotone operators, and supports the theoretical findings with experimental results.

The paper builds upon prior work in stochastic optimization, particularly SVRG [Johnson & Zhang, 2013] and SAGA [Defazio, Bach & Lacoste-Julien, 2014], which have proven effective for convex minimization. However, adapting these methods to saddle-point problems is not straightforward, as the standard convex minimization analysis does not apply. The authors correctly identify this challenge and address it by leveraging the theory of monotone operators [Rockafellar, 1997]. This broader perspective allows for a more general convergence analysis. Related work on accelerated methods for saddle-point problems [Lin, Mairal & Harchaoui, 2015] and robust optimization [Ben-Tal, Ghaoui & Nemirovski, 2009] are appropriately referenced. The connection to variational inequalities [Harker & Pang, 1994] is also a valuable extension.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** The adaptation of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant contribution, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory is sound and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. The extension to monotone operators further broadens the applicability of the work.
*   **Acceleration Framework:** The application of the “catalyst” framework is a clever and effective way to accelerate the algorithms, demonstrating a performance improvement over standard batch methods.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a practically important problem in machine learning with applications to supervised learning, robust optimization and unsupervised learning.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental section demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms on relevant datasets and provides insightful comparisons with existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The strong convexity assumption on both the primal and dual problems is quite restrictive. Exploring weaker assumptions, or analyzing the algorithm's behavior under less restrictive conditions, would enhance the paper’s generality. As the authors state, it could be a situation where only the primal or dual problem is strongly convex.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the paper presents a thorough theoretical analysis, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical considerations, such as parameter tuning and the computational cost of different components of the algorithm.
*   **Notation:** The notation could be improved for clarity, particularly with respect to the different splitting notions (functions vs. partial derivatives).
*   **Catalyst Parameter Choice:** The choice of the acceleration parameter τ could be further justified and investigated.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to solving a challenging problem in optimization.
*   The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms in practice.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong convexity assumption may limit the applicability of the algorithms in certain scenarios.
*   The discussion of practical considerations is somewhat limited.
*   The notation could be more consistent and easier to follow.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The extension of stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems is a valuable result, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by experimental evidence. While the strong convexity assumption is a limitation, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above. Specifically, the authors should consider discussing the implications of relaxing the strong convexity assumption and providing more practical guidance on parameter tuning.
