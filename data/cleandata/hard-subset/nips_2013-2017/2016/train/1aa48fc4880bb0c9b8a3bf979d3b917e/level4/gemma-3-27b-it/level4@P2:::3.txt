This paper extends recent incremental algorithms – SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated variants using the Catalyst framework – to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems with a finite sum structure, arising from either individual or factored splits. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of these algorithms’ complexity under different split types and sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform). Experiments on a binary classification problem demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration for outperforming competitive batch algorithms. While extending variance reduction and incremental methods to saddle-point problems appears conceptually straightforward, the results presented are novel and technically demanding. For bilinear saddle-point problems, the authors present a clear comparison of batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward (FB) algorithms, mirroring known results for convex minimization and demonstrating consistency.

However, a significant concern arises from the computational cost of the proximal operator (O(n+d) per iteration). Consequently, the overall complexity – even for the accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling – doesn’t necessarily outperform accelerated batch methods, as shown in Table 1. Specifically, when the number of data points (n) substantially exceeds the dimensionality (d), which is common in machine learning, the complexity under factored splits (used in the simulations) appears worse than that of batch FB algorithms (Table 2).  Unlike typical convex minimization scenarios, I don’t observe a clear, substantial theoretical advantage to using variance reduction in this setting. 

I recommend the authors address this matter in more detail and clarify the following: (i) Is a factored split inherently less efficient than an individual split? (ii) Under what conditions is accelerated SVRG preferable to batch FB? (iii) Are the stated dependencies on *n* and *d* sufficiently tight?
