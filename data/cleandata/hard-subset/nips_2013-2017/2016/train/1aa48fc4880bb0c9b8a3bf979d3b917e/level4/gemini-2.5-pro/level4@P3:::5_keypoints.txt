- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems with novel analysis using monotone-operator theory, yielding first linear convergence rates for large-scale incremental methods.  
- Shows saddle-point finding is equivalent to finding the zero of a monotone operator, enabling generalized results (e.g., variational inequalities).  
- Allows multiple problem-splitting strategies (by function or partial derivatives) and emphasizes critical role of non-uniform sampling.  
- Demonstrates acceleration via the catalyst framework, producing provably superior algorithms to accelerated batch methods.  
- Motivates practical relevance for ML tasks with non-separable losses/regularizers (AUC maximization, total variation).  
- Significant and original contribution: first fast, linearly convergent incremental algorithms for a broad class of saddle-point problems.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: elegant shift from function-value descent to monotone-operator analysis, with simplified batch-proofs in appendix.  
- Thorough treatment: discusses splitting strategies, non-uniform sampling, acceleration, and summarizes complexity gains in Table 1.  
- Solid experimental validation: SAGA and accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling outperform batch and standard stochastic baselines on separable and non-separable problems.  
- Needs clearer high-level intuition or proof sketch of the monotone-operator technique in the main paper to improve accessibility.  
- Lacks practical guidance or heuristics for choosing non-uniform sampling probabilities π in the general (non-bilinear) case.  
- Should discuss why non-uniform sampling yields a larger performance gap in saddle-point problems compared to standard convex minimization (structural intuition).  
- Missing experimental details: procedure for setting step-size σ (estimation vs. grid search) should be clarified for reproducibility.  
- Minor: clarify scaling factors in the bilinear split example and perform final proofreading to fix typos.
