This paper presents a significant extension of stochastic variance reduction methods, such as SVRG and SAGA, to the domain of convex-concave saddle-point problems. While previous variance-reduced stochastic methods have demonstrated great success in accelerating convex minimization for finite sums, their application has been largely restricted to problems with separable objective functions. The key contribution of this work is to provide the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems, which often arise from machine learning tasks involving non-separable losses or regularizers (e.g., structured output prediction, robust optimization).

The authors adapt SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point setting and provide a thorough convergence analysis rooted in the theory of monotone operators. This not only establishes linear convergence but also shows that the algorithms apply to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. The paper further enhances these methods by analyzing the benefits of non-uniform sampling and by introducing an accelerated variant based on the "catalyst" framework, which is shown to be theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature. Extending variance-reduced methods to handle non-separable saddle-point problems is a novel and important contribution. This makes fast, incremental optimization accessible to a wider range of machine learning models that were previously reliant on slower batch or standard stochastic algorithms. The analysis of different splitting schemes (functional vs. partial derivative) and the emphasis on non-uniform sampling provide valuable new insights.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and comprehensive. Grounding the analysis in the framework of monotone operators is a powerful approach that generalizes the results beyond the specific saddle-point setting. The proofs for linear convergence of the SVRG and SAGA variants, as well as the accelerated version, appear to be sound.
*   **Clarity of Contributions:** The paper clearly articulates its main contributions: the first linearly convergent analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems, the exploration of different splitting and sampling schemes, and an effective acceleration technique.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity and Organization:** While the technical content is strong, the paper's presentation could be improved. The core analysis relies on the theory of monotone operators, yet this connection is relegated to Section 6 and presented as an "extension." This framing is confusing, as the proofs provided in the appendix are built on this very foundation. The paper would be more accessible and logical if the monotone operator framework were introduced earlier as the basis for the analysis, rather than as an afterthought. This would help the reader better understand the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed algorithms from the outset.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while demonstrating the promise of the proposed methods, are limited to two datasets. To make a more compelling case for the practical utility of the algorithms, it would be beneficial to include results on a more diverse set of problems and datasets, particularly those that highlight the unique advantages of handling non-separable structures.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to related stochastic primal-dual algorithms. For the separable case evaluated on the `rcv1` dataset, a comparison against recent stochastic variants of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm would be highly relevant. For instance, the method proposed in:
    *   Zhang, Y., & Xiao, L. (2015). Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Method for Regularized Empirical Risk Minimization. *ICML*.
    This comparison would help situate the proposed methods within the broader landscape of saddle-point optimization. Please consider adding and discussing this reference.

**Questions for the Authors:**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:

1.  **Scope of Assumptions:** Could you comment on the restrictiveness of assumptions (A)-(C)? Specifically, how practical is assumption (A) regarding the strong convex-concave property of the function M? For example, would these assumptions hold for the standard saddle-point formulation of a Support Vector Machine (SVM)?
2.  **Relation to Stochastic Primal-Dual Methods:** Could you elaborate further on the key distinctions between the proposed SVRG/SAGA-based framework and other stochastic primal-dual methods? A clearer differentiation would help readers understand the specific advantages of your approach.
3.  **Clarity on Theorem 2:** The convergence rate in Theorem 2 depends on a constant `µ`, which is not defined in the main text. Assuming it is the strong monotonicity constant from the appendix, the rate's dependence on `|I|` and `µ` makes it difficult to directly compare with the SVRG rate in Theorem 1. Could you provide a more direct comparison or discussion on the relative performance of the two algorithms as predicted by the theory?
4.  **Assumptions and Monotonicity:** The paper transitions from assumptions (A)-(C) in the main text to the framework of strong monotonicity in the appendix. Could you explicitly clarify in the paper how assumptions (A)-(C) imply the strong monotonicity of the relevant operator used in the proofs?

**Minor Issues:**

*   The notation for the constant `L` appears to be used interchangeably to refer to the "condition number" and the "Lipschitz-continuous gradients" constant. Clarifying this would avoid potential confusion.
*   In Section 2.2, the description of the Forward-Backward (FB) algorithm would benefit from a specific citation.
