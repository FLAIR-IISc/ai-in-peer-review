This paper tackles convex-concave saddle-point problems and shows how to adapt stochastic variance-reduction techniques (e.g., SVRG and SAGA) with proximal updates to this setting. The analysis is carried out through the lens of monotone operators and yields linear convergence guarantees. The authors also introduce an accelerated variant and study non-uniform sampling schemes. The work connects to prior advances on variance-reduction for finite-sum convex minimization (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) and to primal-dual and forward-backward methods for saddle-point problems, extending these lines of work beyond separable objectives.

Novelty/originality: The contributions are substantial. The theoretical development is careful and brings new insights for saddle-point optimization. Among the extensions, the application to non-separable objectives is particularly noteworthy.

Technical quality: While I find the theoretical results compelling, the exposition could be improved. In particular, the link to monotone operators is central to the proofs but is presented as an extension; making this connection clearer earlier would enhance accessibility. On the empirical side, results on only two datasets feel limited; broader experiments would strengthen the paper. For separable cases, a comparison to accelerated primal-dual methods would be informative—for example, the stochastic variant of the Chambolle-Pock algorithm: http://www.jmlr.org/proceedings/papers/v37/zhanga15.pdf. Please add this reference and include a comparison.

Points to clarify:
1) Strength of assumptions A–C, especially (A): How restrictive are these in practice? Do they hold for the saddle-point formulation of SVMs?
2) Relationship to stochastic primal-dual methods: What are the key differences between your approach and stochastic primal-dual schemes (algorithmically and in guarantees)?
3) Theorem 2: Please clarify the constant mu—is this the strong monotonicity parameter referenced in the appendix? How should we compare the rate in Theorem 2 with that of Theorem 1 under the same problem parameters?
4) Monotonicity: The path from assumptions (A)–(C) to strong monotonicity (in the appendix) is hard to follow. Do (A)–(C) themselves imply the needed monotonicity, or are additional conditions required?

Minor issues:
- Clarify the use of L, which appears both as a Lipschitz constant and as a “condition number.” The statement “The quantity L represents the condition number of the problem” conflicts with later references to “the Lipschitz constant L.”
- Forward-Backward algorithm: please add an explicit citation.
