- Paper extends SVRG, SAGA, and Catalyst-accelerated variants to strongly convex–concave finite-sum saddle point problems with individual and factored splits.  
- Provides thorough complexity analysis for different splits and sampling strategies (uniform and nonuniform).  
- Experiments (binary classification) show nonuniform sampling and acceleration are essential to beat competitive batch algorithms.  
- Although extension may appear straightforward, the results are novel and technically non-trivial.  
- For bilinear saddle-point problems, the paper gives a clear, consistent comparison among batch, stochastic, and incremental forward-backward algorithms.  
- Major concern: computing the proximal operator costs O(n + d) per iteration, creating a bottleneck for the incremental methods.  
- Consequently, overall complexity (even accelerated SVRG with nonuniform sampling) may not outperform accelerated batch methods.  
- In the common regime n >> d, factored splits (used in simulations) can yield worse complexity than batch FB, so the theoretical benefit of variance reduction is unclear.  
- Reviewer requests detailed discussion on: (i) whether factored splits are theoretically inferior to individual splits, (ii) when accelerated SVRG outperforms batch FB, and (iii) tightness of the n,d dependence.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with the authors' response; scores remain unchanged.
