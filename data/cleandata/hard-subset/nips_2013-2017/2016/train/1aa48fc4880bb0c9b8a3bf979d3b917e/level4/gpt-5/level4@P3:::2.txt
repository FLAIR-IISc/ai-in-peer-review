Summary
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y), where K is smooth and M admits an efficient proximal operator and is strongly convex–concave. The authors extend variance-reduced finite-sum methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting, showing linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity and Lipschitz-gradient assumptions. A key analytic device is to view the saddle-point problem as finding zeros of a monotone operator. This yields convergence guarantees that also cover a broader class of problems (e.g., variational inequalities), beyond saddle points. The paper highlights two notions of decompositions (by functions or by partial derivatives), emphasizes the importance of non-uniform sampling, and introduces an acceleration scheme via a “catalyst”-style proximal-point regularization that improves dependence on problem constants and is argued to be superior to accelerated batch approaches. Experiments on binary classification tasks with non-separable loss/regularizer demonstrate empirical gains over batch and stochastic baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA for strongly convex finite sums (Le Roux et al., Johnson & Zhang, Defazio et al.), but departs from the standard convex minimization setting by handling general convex–concave saddle-point problems without separability assumptions on loss or regularizer. It connects to primal–dual splitting and accelerated methods (Chambolle–Pock; Rockafellar; Chen & Rockafellar; Nesterov) and contrasts with SPDC and related primal–dual coordinate methods that require separability. The monotone operator perspective relates to classical results (Rockafellar; Bauschke–Combettes) and to recent stochastic forward–backward work; here, however, the analysis does not rely on co-coercivity, which is key in convex minimization but typically fails for saddle points.

Quality
- Theory: The convergence analyses for SVRG/SAGA in the saddle-point/monotone-operator setting appear sound and technically nontrivial. The need to account for two scalings (λ, γ) and to track constants L and L̄(π) is well-motivated. The acceleration via a proximal-point “catalyst” wrapper is clearly explained and leverages linear convergence of the proximal point method. One limitation is that the acceleration proof is given for SVRG but not SAGA; a brief discussion of extending it to SAGA would strengthen the paper.
- Assumptions: The strong convexity–concavity (via M) is restrictive. It would be valuable to (i) clarify whether linear rates still hold when only one side is strongly convex (e.g., primal-strongly-convex or dual-strongly-concave), and (ii) discuss practical regularization strategies that induce the needed strong property without degrading modeling performance. The paper briefly mentions adaptivity as future work; more guidance on tuning when constants are unknown would help.
- Practicality: The results hinge on good non-uniform sampling; computing the necessary probabilities (e.g., via row/column norms for bilinear K) is practical, but the paper should comment on one-time preprocessing costs and on dynamic updates if K changes. SAGA’s storage burden is discussed (O(n + d) for factored splits vs. O(nd) for individual splits), but memory–time trade-offs could be articulated more explicitly. The resampling step required for non-uniform SAGA to preserve the stated rate is noted; experiments use a mixture heuristic—please quantify its effect.
- Experiments: The experiments support the claims, especially the benefits of acceleration and non-uniform sampling. However, the empirical section is relatively narrow. Additional comparisons to strong baselines in the non-separable regime (e.g., accelerated primal–dual splitting variants) and ablations on sampling schemes and mini-batch sizes would improve confidence.

Clarity
Overall the paper is well organized and technically detailed. The presentation of the two decomposition notions (individual vs. factored) and their implications for complexity is useful. Some sections are notation-heavy; adding brief summaries of key constants (L, L̄, λ, γ) near the start of the main theorems and a short “how to set step-sizes in practice” box would aid readability. A few minor typos/grammar issues can be cleaned up (e.g., “does need” vs. “do need,” subject–verb agreement).

Originality
The extension of SVRG/SAGA to general (non-separable) convex–concave saddle-point problems with linear rates and the monotone operator analysis are, to my knowledge, novel and substantive. The treatment of factored vs. individual splits and the tailored non-uniform sampling bounds are also original contributions. The catalyst-based acceleration in this setting is a meaningful addition.

Significance
Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous in ML (regularized risk with non-separable terms, robust optimization, structured losses, certain unsupervised relaxations). Providing the first linearly convergent incremental methods that do not rely on separability and that offer clear complexity gains is significant. The monotone-operator generalization broadens impact beyond ML.

Pros
- First variance-reduced incremental methods with linear rates for general convex–concave saddle-point problems; extends to monotone operators.
- Clear identification of two decomposition paradigms and principled non-uniform sampling with improved constants.
- Acceleration via a simple proximal-point wrapper with favorable complexity.
- Theoretical results are comprehensive, with practical guidance on mini-batching and storage.
- Empirical results corroborate the advantages, particularly under ill-conditioning.

Cons
- Requires strong convexity–concavity; limited discussion of one-sided strong convexity or adaptive strategies when constants are unknown.
- Acceleration is proved for SVRG only; SAGA acceleration is claimed but unproven.
- Experimental scope is modest; more datasets and comparisons (including strong primal–dual baselines) would strengthen the case.
- Practicalities of computing sampling probabilities and SAGA memory costs could be discussed more thoroughly.

Questions and suggestions for authors
1. Can your analysis or a variant handle cases where only the primal (or only the dual) is strongly curved? If not, can you outline a practical restart or regularization scheme to approximate this?
2. Please include a brief procedure for setting step-sizes when L and L̄(π) are unknown, and comment on robustness to mis-specification.
3. Quantify preprocessing cost for non-uniform probabilities and its impact on total runtime; consider reporting wall-clock time including preprocessing.
4. Provide results for the accelerated SAGA variant or clarify obstacles to its proof.
5. Expand experiments with additional datasets and include comparisons to accelerated primal–dual splitting (e.g., Chambolle–Pock with extrapolation) in the non-separable setting.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a substantial and timely contribution with solid theory and promising empirical evidence. Addressing the concerns about assumptions, practical tuning, and experimental breadth would further strengthen the work.
