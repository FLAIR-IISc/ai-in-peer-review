This paper introduces extensions to the stochastic variance reduction methods SVRG and SAGA to address strongly convex-concave saddle-point problems, establishing linear convergence guarantees. The authors demonstrate both theoretically and empirically the benefits of their approach for these challenging problems. A key contribution lies in adapting these algorithms – originally designed for convex optimization – to the saddle-point setting, a task complicated by differences in the underlying analysis. Instead of relying on convex minimization techniques, the authors leverage the theory of monotone operators to prove convergence, showcasing broader applicability to problems like variational inequalities relevant in game theory. The paper also explores two distinct gradient splitting strategies (element-wise and factored) for SVRG and SAGA, and emphasizes the importance of non-uniform sampling for improved performance, validated through both analysis and experimentation. The experimental section, including supplemental results, supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates efficacy on problems featuring non-separable losses and regularizers.

The paper effectively builds upon existing stochastic optimization techniques to tackle a more complex problem structure. It clearly articulates the challenges inherent in extending algorithms designed for convex problems to saddle-point settings, and provides a rigorous analysis based on monotone operator theory, broadening the scope of application beyond purely saddle-point problems. The consideration of different splitting strategies for the gradient operator is also a valuable contribution. The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating performance improvements with non-uniform sampling and on relevant problem instances.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. While the acceleration technique presented in Section 5 builds on prior work [7], the method for adaptively updating the regularization parameters (and consequently the iterate point (x̄, ȳ)) to maximize speed-up remains somewhat unclear. Discussing strategies for adaptive updates would strengthen this section. Furthermore, while the convergence proof is provided for SVRG, it would be beneficial to explicitly state whether the same theoretical guarantees extend to SAGA, even if a full proof is relegated to the appendix. A minor stylistic point: the notation for the smooth function K(.) and the design matrix K could be differentiated to avoid potential confusion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, coupled with the use of monotone operator theory for analysis, is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis, establishing linear convergence rates.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithms address important problems in machine learning involving non-separable losses and regularizers.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods and validate the theoretical results.
*   **Generality:** The analysis extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of clarity regarding adaptive acceleration:** The explanation of how to adaptively update the regularization parameters in the acceleration scheme is insufficient.
*   **Missing SAGA convergence statement:** The applicability of the theoretical results to SAGA isn’t explicitly stated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions. The authors successfully extend powerful stochastic optimization techniques to a challenging class of problems. Addressing the minor points outlined above would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work. I recommend acceptance.
