

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The authors demonstrate that while the algorithmic extension from convex minimization is straightforward, the convergence analysis requires fundamentally different techniques based on monotone operators rather than convexity. This approach not only proves linear convergence for saddle-point problems but also extends to a broader class of problems including variational inequalities. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) establishing the first convergence analysis for stochastic variance reduction in saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory; (2) clarifying the distinction between two types of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) and the necessity of convex-concave splits; (3) demonstrating the critical importance of non-uniform sampling for both theoretical and practical efficiency; and (4) showing how to accelerate these algorithms using the "catalyst" framework.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing an important gap in the literature. While saddle-point problems naturally arise in machine learning through duality (e.g., in supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers, robust optimization, and unsupervised learning relaxations), previous work had not provided linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for these problems. The authors successfully bridge this gap by adapting variance reduction techniques, demonstrating that these methods can be applied to problems where standard convex optimization approaches fail.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution. The monotone operator perspective provides a more general framework that naturally extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities, which is a notable theoretical advancement. The authors also provide clear examples of machine learning applications where their approach is particularly valuable, such as when dealing with non-separable losses (e.g., structured output prediction with AUC surrogates) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., grouped norms with overlapping groups or total variation). The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that non-uniform sampling significantly outperforms uniform sampling and that the accelerated algorithms consistently outperform both batch methods and existing stochastic approaches.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient technical detail to be reproducible. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the proofs follow logically from the monotone operator framework. The authors provide helpful comparisons to existing batch algorithms (forward-backward and accelerated forward-backward) and clarify how their approach differs from previous work.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the sampling probabilities π for general saddle-point problems beyond the bilinear case. While the paper discusses non-uniform sampling for bilinear problems (using row and column norms), it does not specify how these probabilities should be chosen for more general saddle-point problems. This is particularly important since the authors emphasize the critical role of non-uniform sampling for efficiency.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization. While the authors provide theoretical justification for the sampling scheme, a more intuitive explanation would help readers understand this fundamental difference between the two problem classes. This could include a brief comparison of the variance reduction properties in both settings.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are a few minor issues that could be addressed. On line 78, there's a typo where "k" should be "d." Additionally, on line 121, there's an extra "y_{t-1}" term in the second equality. These are easily fixable and don't affect the overall contribution.

The paper's significance is high, as it addresses an important class of problems in machine learning where previous stochastic optimization techniques were not applicable. The linear convergence rates provided are theoretically superior to existing methods, and the experimental results confirm these advantages in practice. The approach also has potential applications beyond machine learning in areas such as game theory and robust optimization.

Strengths:
- First linearly convergent algorithms for saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction
- Theoretical extension to variational inequalities via monotone operator framework
- Clear demonstration of non-uniform sampling's critical importance
- Strong experimental validation across multiple machine learning applications
- Practical acceleration via the catalyst framework

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient detail on sampling probabilities for non-bilinear problems
- Limited discussion on why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle-point problems but not necessarily for convex minimization
- Minor technical typos that could be easily corrected

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that advances both the theory and practice of optimization for saddle-point problems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The main contribution is the theoretical framework that enables these algorithms, and while the algorithms themselves are straightforward extensions, their application to a new class of problems represents a significant advance. The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of the sampling probability selection for general problems and a more intuitive explanation of why non-uniform sampling is so critical for saddle-point problems to further strengthen the paper.
