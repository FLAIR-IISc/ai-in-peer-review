

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors make several significant contributions: (1) they provide a novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, which differs substantially from convex minimization analysis and demonstrates that the algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities; (2) they identify two distinct splitting approaches (function-wise and derivative-wise) that are crucial for practical implementation; (3) they establish that non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency both theoretically and empirically; and (4) they show how to accelerate these methods using a simple extension of the catalyst framework, resulting in algorithms that consistently outperform accelerated batch methods.

The theoretical analysis is technically sound and carefully executed, with thorough proofs of linear convergence that address the unique challenges of saddle-point problems. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with excellent organization that effectively builds on the authors' contributions. The connection to monotone operators significantly broadens the applicability of the results beyond saddle-point problems, which is a major strength.

One concern pertains to the strong convexity-concavity assumption (strong convexity in the primal variable and strong concavity in the dual variable). In standard saddle-point theory, it is typically sufficient to have strong convexity in the primal variable or strong concavity in the dual variable (not necessarily both simultaneously), as this represents the minimal condition for linear convergence. The paper should clarify why both conditions are necessary for their analysis, as this stronger assumption may unnecessarily limit the scope of the results.

The experimental section demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems, with non-uniform sampling significantly outperforming uniform sampling and accelerated methods providing the strongest improvements. Additional experiments on more diverse machine learning problems would further strengthen the significance claim.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a meaningful advance in the field. The technical content is sound, the analysis is thorough, and the results are compelling. With minor clarification of the strong convexity-concavity assumption, this paper would be an excellent fit for the conference.
