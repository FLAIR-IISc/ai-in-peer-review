

The authors introduce the first linearly convergent algorithms for saddle point problems using variance reduction techniques like SVRG and SAGA. A key contribution lies in their novel proof technique, which leverages monotone operator theory to establish convergence—a critical departure from convex minimization analysis, which does not directly apply. This approach not only proves linear convergence for saddle point problems but also extends the analysis to a broader class of problems, including variational inequalities. Consequently, the method enables handling non-separable loss functions and regularizers, which are prevalent in machine learning models. The core innovation is the interpretation of saddle point problems as finding zeros of monotone operators, a perspective that is both elegant and potentially widely applicable. The paper also provides simplified proofs for algorithms like the forward-backward splitting (and its stochastic variant), adding further value.

The proposed algorithms, particularly those employing non-uniform sampling, demonstrate strong empirical performance, outperforming prior methods in most cases. Overall, the work significantly advances the field by establishing a new theoretical framework for saddle point problems. However, since the primary contribution centers on the proof technique (with the algorithms themselves being natural extensions of existing variance reduction methods), the authors might consider including a concise proof sketch in the main paper to enhance accessibility, where space allows.

While the paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, certain aspects require clarification. Specifically:  
1. The paper does not specify the non-uniform sampling distribution \(\pi\) for non-bilinear saddle point problems. Clarification on how \(\pi\) is defined beyond the bilinear case would be valuable.  
2. The step size \(\sigma\) used in experiments is not discussed in terms of its selection rationale. The authors should explain how \(\sigma\) is set and provide intuition for why non-uniform sampling is essential for saddle point problems but less critical in convex minimization contexts. A brief discussion on this distinction would strengthen the paper.

Minor issues:  
1. Line 78: Typo—'k' should be 'd'.  
2. Line 121: Second equality contains an extraneous \(y_{t-1}\) term.  

The work addresses a significant challenge in optimization, and the theoretical insights are promising. With minor clarifications to the technical details and proof exposition, this paper would make a strong contribution to the literature.
