### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems. The problem formulation is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `M` is strongly convex-concave with a simple proximal operator, and `K` is a smooth function whose gradient operator can be decomposed into a sum of many components, `B = sum(B_i)`. This structure is common in machine learning, particularly for problems involving non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization surrogates) or non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation), where standard stochastic methods for convex minimization like SVRG or SAGA do not apply.

The main contributions are:
1.  The extension of SVRG and SAGA algorithms to the saddle-point setting, providing the first large-scale algorithms with provable linear convergence for this problem class.
2.  A novel convergence analysis based on the theory of monotone operators. This is a key technical contribution, as the analysis for SVR methods in convex minimization relies on co-coercivity, a property not held by the gradient operator of saddle-point problems. The provided analysis is more general and also applies to problems like variational inequalities.
3.  A detailed investigation of the problem structure, including different ways to split the gradient operator (functional vs. factored splits) and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling in achieving better theoretical and practical performance.
4.  An acceleration of the proposed SVRG method using an extension of the "Catalyst" framework, which yields an algorithm with a convergence rate that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.

The work is positioned relative to prior art in both stochastic optimization and saddle-point problems. It builds upon the SVRG and SAGA frameworks developed for finite-sum convex minimization. It aims to improve upon batch methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., Forward-Backward), which do not exploit the sum structure, and standard stochastic methods, which exhibit sublinear convergence rates.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality (Technical Soundness)
The paper appears to be technically sound. The central theoretical contribution is the convergence analysis using the framework of monotone operators, which is the correct tool for this problem class and skillfully avoids the pitfalls of naively extending proofs from convex minimization. The proofs for SVRG (Theorem 1), SAGA (Theorem 2), and the accelerated SVRG (Theorem 3) establish linear convergence rates. The derived complexities, summarized in Table 1, clearly show the theoretical advantage of the proposed methods, particularly with non-uniform sampling and acceleration, over existing batch and stochastic algorithms. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed methods converge significantly faster than baselines.

A potential weakness lies in the theoretical rate provided for SAGA in Theorem 2. The convergence rate includes a term `max{3|I|^2/m, ...}` in its denominator, where `|I|` is the number of components. This quadratic dependency on `|I|` is unusual for SAGA-type analyses (which typically have a linear dependency) and may suggest that the analysis is not as tight as it could be, potentially making the theoretical guarantee for SAGA weaker than for SVRG in large-scale settings. Furthermore, the theory for non-uniform SAGA requires a resampling step that is not used in the experiments, a common but noteworthy gap between theory and practice.

#### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and clearly enumerates the paper's contributions. Section 2 effectively defines the problem setting, assumptions, and relevant examples from machine learning. The authors do a good job of explaining the different existing algorithms (batch, stochastic) before introducing their own methods. The inclusion of Table 1, which compares the computational complexities of all discussed algorithms on a bilinear problem, is highly effective for understanding the theoretical improvements. The extension to monotone operators is explained well, clarifying the generality of the analysis.

One minor point of confusion is in the presentation of Algorithm 2 (SAGA). The gradient update step is marked with "(optional)", which is slightly ambiguous. This step is fundamental to SAGA's mechanism. The authors likely intend for the resampling of indices for the update to be the optional part (related to the non-uniform sampling theory), but the current phrasing could be misinterpreted.

#### Originality
The work is highly original. While the algorithms themselves are natural adaptations of SVRG and SAGA, this is the first work to successfully apply them to the general class of decomposable saddle-point problems and provide a rigorous proof of linear convergence. The key novelty is in the analysis. By identifying monotone operator theory as the appropriate framework, the authors provide a non-trivial theoretical contribution that overcomes significant analytical challenges. The analysis of different splitting schemes, the careful derivation of complexity bounds for non-uniform sampling, and the extension of the Catalyst acceleration framework to this new setting are all novel contributions.

#### Significance
The paper addresses a problem of high significance in machine learning and optimization. Many modern ML models, especially in structured prediction and with complex regularizers, fall into the saddle-point formulation where existing linearly convergent stochastic methods are inapplicable. This paper fills an important gap by providing practical, efficient, and theoretically-grounded algorithms for these problems. The results are likely to be used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners. The experiments, although limited to two datasets, convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods on a relevant non-separable classification problem. The generality of the monotone operator framework suggests the techniques may find even broader applications beyond saddle-point problems.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   It provides the first linearly convergent, large-scale stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems.
*   The technical contribution is strong and novel, featuring a sophisticated analysis based on monotone operator theory that is more general than prior work.
*   The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and systematically compares the proposed methods against relevant baselines, both theoretically and empirically.
*   It includes a state-of-the-art acceleration scheme and a thorough treatment of non-uniform sampling, which is key for practical performance.
*   The work is of high significance to the NIPS community, with direct applications in areas like structured prediction, robust optimization, and learning with complex regularizers.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical convergence rate for the SAGA variant appears to have a suboptimal dependency on the number of components (`|I|^2`), which may warrant further investigation.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more extensive, covering a wider variety of saddle-point problems to more fully substantiate the broad claims of applicability.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a substantial and well-executed contribution to the field of large-scale optimization.
