This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, common in machine learning. The authors extend these algorithms to a broader class of problems involving monotone operators, providing convergence analysis and demonstrating advantages over existing batch and stochastic methods. The core idea is adapting existing variance reduction techniques to the saddle-point setting, recognizing the differences from standard convex minimization.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, proposed methods, and theoretical results. The contributions are significant, as it provides the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale saddle-point problems using stochastic variance reduction. The analysis extending to monotone operators adds generality and theoretical depth. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, especially the accelerated variant, showing improvements over existing algorithms. The thorough comparison with batch and stochastic alternatives, and the discussion of different sampling strategies, is particularly valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and monotone operators is a novel contribution, addressing a gap in the literature.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees for the proposed algorithms.  The connection to monotone operator theory is a valuable generalization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivations and contributions are clearly stated. The mathematical notation is generally clear and consistent.
*   **Significance:** Saddle-point problems are prevalent in machine learning, and efficient algorithms for solving them are crucial. The proposed methods have the potential to significantly impact various applications.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The comparison with existing methods is thorough and informative.
*   **Comprehensive Discussion:** The detailed discussion of different split types and the importance of non-uniform sampling are insightful. The extension to the Catalyst framework provides an additional performance boost.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions (A)-(C) are relatively strong. While necessary for the theoretical analysis, their practical limitations are not thoroughly discussed.  The sensitivity of performance to the accuracy of these assumptions would be valuable to explore.
*   **Constant Dependence:** The theoretical convergence rates depend on constants (L, L̄) that may be difficult to estimate in practice. The practical implications of this are not fully addressed.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The practical implementation might require careful tuning of hyperparameters (e.g., step size, sampling probabilities). This aspect could be further elaborated.
*   **Complexity of Catalyst:** The practical benefits of the accelerated variant using the Catalyst framework could be further clarified. The overhead associated with the additional complexity of the algorithm and its impact on practical performance deserve more detailed discussion.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are informative, they are limited to a few datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problems would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The extension to monotone operators is a valuable generalization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions may limit the practical applicability of the algorithms.
*   The dependence on hard-to-estimate constants could be a challenge in practice.
*   The relatively limited scope of the experiments.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The proposed algorithms are novel, theoretically sound, and experimentally validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance.
