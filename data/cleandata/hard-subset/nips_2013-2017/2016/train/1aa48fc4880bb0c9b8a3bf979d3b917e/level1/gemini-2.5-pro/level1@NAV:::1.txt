This paper presents a significant contribution by extending the popular stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, to the general class of convex-concave saddle-point problems. These problems are prevalent in machine learning, particularly for models with non-separable losses or regularizers, robust optimization, and certain unsupervised learning relaxations. While previous works like SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) achieved linear convergence for strongly-convex finite sums, their analysis does not apply here. The authors provide the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this problem class. The core of their theoretical contribution is a novel convergence analysis based on the theory of monotone operators, which is more general than the standard convex optimization analysis and also covers problems like variational inequalities. The paper further introduces an acceleration scheme based on the "catalyst" framework and demonstrates the practical importance of non-uniform sampling.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The work addresses a crucial and previously open problem: how to efficiently solve large-scale saddle-point problems that exhibit a finite-sum structure. This enables the use of powerful variance reduction techniques on a wider and important class of machine learning models.
*   **Originality:** While the algorithmic adaptations are straightforward, the theoretical analysis is highly novel. The use of monotone operator theory is an elegant and powerful choice that correctly handles the saddle-point structure and broadens the applicability of the methods. The proposed acceleration scheme is also a valuable addition.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, providing rigorous convergence proofs for the proposed SVRG and SAGA variants. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, which clearly show the proposed methods outperforming existing batch and stochastic algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the contributions within the existing literature, and provides lucid explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical properties.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

*   The algorithms' step sizes depend on problem constants (e.g., strong convexity-concavity, Lipschitz constants) that may not be known in practice. The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work on adaptive methods.
*   While the experiments are convincing, they are performed on a limited set of problems. A broader empirical evaluation could further strengthen the paper's practical claims.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that advances the state of the art in large-scale optimization.
