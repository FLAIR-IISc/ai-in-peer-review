This paper introduces stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to solve convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The core contribution lies in extending these methods, proving their linear convergence, and identifying key properties of saddle-point formulations that differ from standard convex minimization. The authors also demonstrate acceleration using a catalyst framework. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practically important class of problems, and the theoretical analysis appears sound.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions its work within the context of stochastic gradient methods (SGD), variance reduction techniques (SAG, SVRG, SAGA), and their application to convex optimization. It acknowledges prior work on saddle-point problems and variational inequalities, differentiating itself by providing *linearly convergent* stochastic algorithms specifically tailored for large-scale saddle-point formulations. The connection to monotone operator theory is also appropriately referenced.  The authors highlight gaps in existing literature, such as the lack of scalable methods for non-separable losses and regularizers, making a strong case for the paper's contribution.

**Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The convergence analysis appears rigorous, relying on the well-established framework of monotone operators. The extension of SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point setting is presented clearly and logically.  The authors demonstrate awareness of the subtleties of applying convex minimization analyses to saddle-point problems, justifying the shift to monotone operator theory. The analysis of the two types of splitting (function-level vs. derivative-level) is insightful. The inclusion of the catalyst acceleration is a welcome addition and further strengthens the results.  The discussion of the implications for variational inequalities broadens the applicability of the proposed methods. The analysis seems complete, although more detailed derivations in the appendices would be appreciated.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and contributions. The notation is mostly consistent, and the algorithmic descriptions are easy to follow. However, some sections (particularly those detailing the theoretical analysis) could benefit from increased clarity and more explanatory text. For instance, the jump from assumptions (A)-(C) to the convergence proofs could be smoother. The numerous theorems and their respective appendices can make the flow slightly disjointed. A more cohesive presentation of the theoretical results would enhance readability. The examples in Section 2.1 are helpful for understanding the practical relevance of the work.

**Originality:** The extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, along with the associated convergence analysis, is a significant contribution. While variance reduction methods have been applied to convex optimization extensively, their application to saddle-point problems, especially with the theoretical guarantees provided here, is novel. The identification of the key differences between convex minimization and saddle-point formulations, particularly concerning the splitting of functions and the importance of non-uniform sampling, is original and insightful. The extension to monotone operators further broadens the scope of the work.

**Significance:** The results are important for a wide range of machine learning applications, including those involving non-separable losses, regularizers, and robust optimization. The linear convergence guarantees, combined with the scalability of SVRG and SAGA, make these algorithms particularly attractive for large-scale problems. The empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods are convincing. The potential for acceleration using the catalyst framework further enhances the practical impact of the work. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than previous research, advancing the state-of-the-art in optimization for machine learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with linear convergence guarantees.
*   Clear identification of key differences between convex minimization and saddle-point formulations.
*   Demonstrated practical effectiveness through experiments.
*   Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical sections could benefit from increased clarity and more detailed explanations.
*   The paper relies heavily on assumptions (A)-(C), and a discussion of the robustness of the methods to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
*   The empirical evaluation, while convincing, could be expanded to include a wider range of datasets and problem settings.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of optimization for machine learning. The technical quality is high, the results are original and impactful, and the paper is generally well-written. Despite some minor areas for improvement, I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**
