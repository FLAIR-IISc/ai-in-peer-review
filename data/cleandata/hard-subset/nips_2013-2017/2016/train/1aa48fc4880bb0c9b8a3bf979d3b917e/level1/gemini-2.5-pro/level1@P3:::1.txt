### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces and analyzes extensions of the popular SVRG and SAGA stochastic variance-reduced gradient methods to the setting of convex-concave saddle-point problems. The target problem is `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where `K` is smooth with a gradient operator that can be decomposed into a finite sum, and `M` is a "simple" (proximable) strongly convex-concave function. Such problems are prevalent in machine learning, arising from duality, robust optimization, and structured prediction, especially in cases with non-separable losses or regularizers.

The main contributions are:
1.  The first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for this class of saddle-point problems.
2.  A novel convergence analysis based on the theory of monotone operators. This framework is more general than the typical analysis for convex minimization, does not require properties like co-coercivity (unlike some prior work on SVRG for monotone operators), and demonstrates the applicability of the methods to a wider class of problems, including variational inequalities.
3.  An exploration of saddle-point-specific structural properties, such as different ways to split the gradient operator ("factored" vs. "individual") and the critical role of non-uniform sampling for achieving optimal theoretical rates.
4.  An extension of the "Catalyst" acceleration framework, providing an accelerated algorithm that is theoretically shown to be superior to accelerated full-batch methods.

The work builds directly upon SVRG [2] and SAGA [3] for convex minimization and provides a significant generalization. It positions itself against batch methods for saddle-point problems (e.g., [19, 20]) and standard stochastic methods [22] which only achieve sublinear convergence rates.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and increasingly important problem class in machine learning. While variance reduction has revolutionized large-scale convex minimization, its application to the more general saddle-point setting was an open and important question. This work provides a definitive answer, delivering the first algorithms that can efficiently leverage finite-sum structure to achieve linear convergence. This is a major contribution that could enable the use of more complex, non-separable models in large-scale settings.

2.  **Originality and Technical Depth:** While the algorithmic templates are natural extensions of SVRG and SAGA, the theoretical analysis is highly original and constitutes a significant technical advance. The choice to use the framework of monotone operators is elegant and powerful. It correctly identifies that the saddle-point gradient operator is monotone but not necessarily co-coercive, a crucial distinction that prevented a straightforward application of previous analyses of variance reduction for monotone operators (e.g., [30]). This approach not only yields a correct proof for the saddle-point case but also broadens the applicability of the algorithms. The application and analysis of the Catalyst accelerator in this context is also novel and well-executed.

3.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the literature, and articulates its contributions. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the complexity comparisons in Table 1 are extremely helpful for understanding the landscape of algorithms and the improvements offered. The experiments are well-designed, using relevant non-separable problems to convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) over strong baselines like accelerated forward-backward and standard stochastic methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The proposed methods, particularly in their accelerated form, require knowledge of several problem-dependent constants (strong convexity-concavity `λ`, `γ`; Lipschitz `L`, `L̄`). These are often unknown in practice, and tuning them can be difficult. While the authors rightly note this as a direction for future work in the conclusion, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithms to these parameters or potential heuristics for setting them would enhance the paper's practical value.

2.  **Comparison to Primal-Dual Coordinate Methods:** The paper focuses on comparing its methods to gradient-based batch and stochastic algorithms. However, another important class of methods for structured saddle-point problems is stochastic primal-dual coordinate descent (e.g., Zhang & Xiao, ICML 2014; Zhu & Storkey, ECML PKDD 2015). These methods can also achieve linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity and can exploit the problem structure in a different way (by updating single coordinates). A discussion positioning the proposed variance-reduced methods relative to this line of work would be valuable for providing a more complete picture of the state of the art.

3.  **SAGA Storage Overhead:** For bilinear problems with "individual" splits, SAGA requires storing `|I| = n*d` gradient components, which is prohibitive for large `n` and `d`. The paper mentions this implicitly by focusing on "factored" splits for SAGA in the complexity analysis. It would be beneficial to make this limitation more explicit in the main text and to more clearly contrast the memory-computation trade-offs between SVRG (no storage, but requires periodic full gradient computation) and SAGA (online, but potentially high storage).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Provides the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of finite-sum saddle-point problems, a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical analysis based on monotone operators is novel, general, and technically deep, representing a strong contribution to optimization theory.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written, and the empirical results convincingly validate the theoretical claims, showing substantial improvements over existing methods.
*   **Pro:** The successful extension of the Catalyst acceleration framework further strengthens the contribution, yielding a method with state-of-the-art theoretical guarantees.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The practical implementation requires knowledge of problem constants that may be difficult to obtain.
*   **Con:** The comparison with related work could be expanded to include primal-dual coordinate methods.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to large-scale optimization for machine learning. It elegantly extends the powerful variance reduction paradigm to the general setting of saddle-point problems, supported by a novel and insightful theoretical analysis and strong empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall significance and quality of the work. This paper is a clear accept and is likely to have a high impact on the field.
