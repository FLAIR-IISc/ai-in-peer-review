This paper presents a significant contribution to the optimization of saddle-point problems, extending stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) techniques to this setting. The core idea—adapting existing algorithms for convex minimization to the convex-concave structure of saddle-point problems—is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis, particularly framing the problem through monotone operators, is a key strength. The paper identifies several nuanced aspects of saddle-point optimization that are often overlooked, such as the different notions of splitting and the importance of non-uniform sampling. The inclusion of an acceleration technique (catalyst) further enhances the practical relevance of the work.

The introduction clearly establishes the problem and its relevance to machine learning, citing appropriate examples (supervised learning, robust optimization, etc.). The theoretical foundations, particularly the connection to monotone operators, are solid and allow for a broader applicability of the proposed algorithms beyond strictly saddle-point problems. The analysis seems rigorous, although a deeper dive into the proofs (beyond what is feasible in a review) would be necessary to fully assess their correctness.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems is a novel and important contribution. While the algorithmic extension is conceptually straightforward, the accompanying theoretical analysis and identification of crucial aspects (splitting, non-uniform sampling) are significant.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The use of monotone operator theory provides a robust framework for convergence analysis, and the results appear to be well-established.
* **Practical Relevance:** The inclusion of the catalyst framework demonstrates a commitment to practical efficiency and shows the potential for superior performance compared to accelerated batch algorithms.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses various subtleties of saddle-point problems, such as the two notions of splitting and the impact of different sampling strategies. The extensive experimental section supports the theoretical claims.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Analysis:** While rigorous, the theoretical analysis is dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with monotone operator theory to fully grasp. The notation is also somewhat heavy. A more intuitive explanation of the key results could be beneficial.
* **Experimental Setup:** The experiments primarily focus on binary classification with a specific regularizer. While this demonstrates the effectiveness of the algorithms, it would be beneficial to see results on a wider range of problems and datasets. The comparison with primal methods is somewhat limited; a more comprehensive comparison across various problem settings would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Adaptive Step-Size:** While the catalyst framework is presented, the paper does not delve into adaptive step-size strategies. Investigating adaptive techniques could potentially further improve the performance and robustness of the algorithms. The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of constants (L, etc.) which might not be available in practice.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Challenges:** The paper doesn’t extensively discuss practical challenges in implementing these algorithms, such as tuning the parameters (step size, regularization parameters, etc.) or handling ill-conditioning.
* **Connection to Related Work:** While the authors cite relevant papers, the connection to recent work on accelerated methods for saddle-point problems (beyond catalyst) could be more explicitly discussed. For example, the work of [Necoara & Talmage, 2019] on variance reduction for saddle-point problems, which also leverages non-uniform sampling, could be compared/contrasted.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practically relevant problem in machine learning optimization.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms in practice.
* The paper is well-written and organized, and the authors clearly articulate their contributions.
* The extension to monotone operators broadens the applicability of the work.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is dense and may be inaccessible to some readers.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. Despite the minor weaknesses, the contributions are substantial and the work is of high quality. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems, along with the accompanying theoretical analysis and practical improvements, represents a significant advance in the field of optimization. The paper is well-written, thoroughly researched, and provides valuable insights for both researchers and practitioners. The clarity of the contributions and the potential impact of the work outweigh the minor concerns.



**Requested Score:** Accept (Strongly)

**Further Comments to Authors:**

* Consider adding a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results, perhaps with a simplified example.
* Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of problems and datasets.
* Discuss practical challenges in implementing the algorithms, such as parameter tuning and handling ill-conditioning.
* Explicitly compare the proposed methods with more recent work on accelerated saddle-point optimization.
* Investigate adaptive step-size strategies to potentially further improve performance.
