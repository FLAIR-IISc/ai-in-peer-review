- Studies composite convex-concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with smooth K and proximable strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave M.  
- Main contribution: extend variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) to non-separable saddle-point and general monotone operator settings with linear convergence.  
- Analysis uses monotone-operator framework: forward part B = Σ_i B_i (Lipschitz, not necessarily gradients), backward via resolvent of A = ∂M.  
- Key algorithmic elements: variance-reduced estimators (SVRG/SAGA), two split notions (function-based and factored for bilinear K), non-uniform sampling with smoothness constant L̄(π), and Catalyst-like acceleration.  
- Theoretical rates: linear convergence with step size σ ≈ 1/(L^2 + c L̄^2/m) and explicit epoch length choices.  
- Bilinear K results: relate L and L̄ to operator, Frobenius, and max norms of K, showing improved complexities (e.g., replacing ‖K‖op by ‖K‖F under non-uniform sampling).  
- Generalization: analysis extended to finding zeros of A + Σ_i B_i for maximal strongly monotone A and monotone B (no cocoercivity assumption).  
- Experiments: binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers and separable baseline show accelerated SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling outperform batch and simple stochastic FB; accelerated SVRG best on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Technical soundness: convergence analyses are plausible and internally consistent, relying on monotonicity, Lipschitz B_i, and strong structure in M.  
- Acceleration analysis via proximal point is straightforward and avoids Nesterov machinery.  
- Factored splits and non-uniform sampling handled carefully, with meaningful bounds like L̄ ≤ ‖K‖F/√(λγ).  
- SAGA non-uniform theory requires a post-update resampling step; experiments use a different practical mixture heuristic.  
- Monotone operator extension is sound at high level but may yield conservative step sizes.  
- Clarity: well-organized presentation with explicit definitions and clear description of splits; pseudocode provided.  
- Clarification needed on experimental measurements (optimality gaps/distances), practical choices for m and probabilities, and comparisons to other stochastic primal-dual methods.  
- Originality: novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle-point problems and monotone operators, including Bi not being gradients, and Catalyst-style acceleration adaptation.  
- Significance: provides linearly convergent incremental methods for important non-separable ML problems and practical sampling/split guidance.  
- Strength: first linear-convergence variance-reduced algorithms for general composite saddle-point problems and clean monotone-operator-based analysis.  
- Strength: non-uniform sampling theory and practical factored/individual splits for bilinear K with Catalyst-style acceleration.  
- Weakness: requires knowledge of strong convexity–concavity (λ,γ) in M and no adaptive scheme provided.  
- Weakness: experiments limited (two datasets) with missing comparisons and ablations on sampling and epoch size m.  
- Weakness: theoretical SAGA non-uniform variant differs from empirical implementation, leaving practice without guarantees.  
- Weakness: some claims overstate advantages versus accelerated batch methods; improvements depend on constants/dimensions.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, broadly applicable, solid proofs, useful theoretical insights, and supportive empirical evidence.  
- Rejection arguments: limited experimental validation, practical tuning/resampling issues, and reliance on strong convexity–concavity without adaptive methods.  
- Suggested improvements: add comparisons to SPDC/primal-dual baselines, ablations on m and sampling, clarify distance/gap measurements, give practical guidance on splits/probabilities and SAGA storage/time trade-offs, and discuss/adaptivity for (λ,γ) and step sizes.
