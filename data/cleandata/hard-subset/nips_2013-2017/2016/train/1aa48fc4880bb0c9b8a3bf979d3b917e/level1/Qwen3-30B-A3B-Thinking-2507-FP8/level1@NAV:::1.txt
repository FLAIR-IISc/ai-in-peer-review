

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The authors build upon prior work like SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014) for convex optimization, but make a significant theoretical leap by using monotone operator theory instead of convex minimization analysis. They demonstrate that saddle-point problems allow two distinct types of splits (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) and show that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficiency. The paper also extends these methods to accelerated variants using the "catalyst" framework.

The paper excels in theoretical quality with a rigorous convergence analysis that properly handles the saddle-point structure, avoiding the pitfalls of directly applying convex minimization techniques. The extension to monotone operators (Section 6) is particularly strong, showing the approach applies beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The experimental section (Section 7) provides clear evidence of the method's superiority over batch and stochastic alternatives, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

Strengths include the novel theoretical contribution (extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems), the elegant use of monotone operator theory, and the practical demonstration of the method's effectiveness. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with good connections to related work.

Weaknesses include limited discussion of how to handle cases where strong convexity-concavity constants are unknown (a common issue in practice), and the experimental section could benefit from additional datasets or comparisons to more specialized saddle-point solvers. The connection to specific machine learning applications, while present, could be more explicitly articulated.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical extension to saddle-point problems with provable linear convergence, superior performance demonstrated in experiments, and the extension to monotone operators shows broader applicability beyond the specific problem class. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature.

Arguments against acceptance: The practical limitations regarding unknown strong convexity parameters and limited experimental scope slightly reduce the paper's impact. However, these are minor compared to the substantial theoretical contribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning. The paper is well-written and makes a clear, novel contribution that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
