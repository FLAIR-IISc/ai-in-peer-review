- Paper proposes stochastic variance-reduction (SVR) methods for convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with decomposable gradient operator B=sum(B_i).
- Main contribution 1: Extends SVRG and SAGA to the saddle-point setting, providing the first large-scale algorithms with provable linear convergence for this class.
- Main contribution 2: Develops a novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, overcoming the lack of co-coercivity in saddle-point gradients.
- Main contribution 3: Analyzes problem structure, including functional vs. factored splits and the importance of non-uniform sampling for improved performance.
- Main contribution 4: Accelerates SVRG via an extended Catalyst framework, yielding provably faster rates than accelerated batch methods.
- Technical soundness: Proofs for SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated SVRG establish linear convergence and derived complexities show theoretical advantages (Table 1).
- Empirical support: Experiments (on two datasets) corroborate theoretical claims, showing faster convergence than baselines.
- Weakness (theory): SAGA’s theoretical rate includes a surprising quadratic |I|^2/m term, suggesting the analysis may be loose compared to typical linear dependencies.
- Weakness (theory/experiments): Non-uniform SAGA theory requires a resampling step that was not used in experiments, revealing a theory/practice gap.
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear motivation, definitions, and effective use of Table 1.
- Minor clarity issue: Algorithm 2 (SAGA) labels a gradient update as "(optional)", creating ambiguity about the resampling/update step.
- Originality: First rigorous application of SVRG/SAGA to decomposable saddle-point problems, with the key novelty in monotone-operator-based analysis and splitting/sampling insights.
- Significance: Highly relevant to ML/optimization (structured prediction, complex regularizers), filling an important gap by enabling scalable, linearly convergent stochastic methods.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel technical contribution, strong theory, clear writing, practical acceleration and sampling analysis, and high community relevance.
- Arguments against acceptance: Potentially suboptimal SAGA dependency on |I| and limited experimental diversity to fully substantiate broad applicability.
