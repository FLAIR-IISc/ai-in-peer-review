- Studies composite convex–concave saddle problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with smooth K and (λ,γ)-strongly convex–concave M having an efficient prox.
- Main contributions: extend SVRG and SAGA to this saddle setting with linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity.
- Analysis frames saddle problems as zeros of monotone operators A+B, with A strongly monotone and B Lipschitz, avoiding co-coercivity assumptions.
- Introduces two splitting notions for B: function-based and derivative-based, including factored row/column splits for bilinear K.
- Develops importance/non-uniform sampling schemes that reduce effective smoothness constants L̄(π).
- Proposes acceleration via a proximal-point/Catalyst wrapper that improves conditioning and yields faster outer-loop contraction.
- Extends results beyond saddle points to general monotone inclusions.
- The variance-reduced estimators are standard, but the monotone-operator analysis is technically solid and tailored to saddle problems.
- Provides linear convergence rates for SVRG (per epoch) and SAGA (per iteration) with explicit dependence on L and sampling-dependent L̄(π).
- Assumptions: requires known strong convexity–concavity, B Lipschitz and monotone, and cheap prox for M; limits scope but are standard.
- Sampling and complexity analysis carefully handles factored/elementwise splits, giving tighter bounds in terms of ‖K‖F and coordinate norms.
- Practical issues: SVRG needs periodic full passes; SAGA can have prohibitive memory except for bilinear/factored splits.
- Experiments show gains over batch/naive stochastic methods and highlight importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, but are limited in scope.
- Clarity issues: ambiguous statement about requiring convex-concave term splits vs. derivative-based splits; parameter choices (σ, inner iterations, τ) need clearer guidance.
- Originality lies in unified monotone-operator analysis for variance reduction, saddle-specific splitting treatment, non-uniform sampling, and Catalyst-style acceleration for monotone inclusions.
- Significance: fills gap by providing variance reduction with linear rates for nonseparable composite saddle problems and monotone inclusions; broad potential impact.
- Pros for acceptance: solid theory, extension to monotone inclusions, insightful sampling/splitting strategies, simple effective acceleration, supportive empirical results.
- Cons/concerns: SAGA memory costs, limited experimental baselines/datasets, possibly conservative analysis constants and step sizes, requires known strong convexity parameters, some textual inconsistencies.
- Suggested improvements: add stronger baselines (PDHG/Chambolle–Pock, Mirror-Prox, SPDC), propose SAGA storage reductions (sufficient statistics, sketching), provide adaptive schemes/line-search for parameters, clarify split-related statements, and expand sampling implementation details and practical heuristics.
- Overall recommendation: strong and timely contribution with promising theory and experiments; leans toward acceptance pending improvements on practicality and experimental breadth.
