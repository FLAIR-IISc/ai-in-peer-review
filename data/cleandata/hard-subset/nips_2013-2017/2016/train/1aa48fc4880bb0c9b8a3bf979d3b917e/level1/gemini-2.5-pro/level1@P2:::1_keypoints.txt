- Extends SVRG and SAGA from convex minimization to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Main technical novelty is a convergence analysis using monotone operator theory rather than standard convex potential functions.  
- Analysis shows monotonicity, not co-coercivity, is the key property for convergence in saddle-point settings.  
- Algorithms naturally extend prior variance-reduction templates but are made applicable to variational inequalities as well.  
- Non-uniform sampling is crucial for optimal performance and is analyzed thoroughly.  
- Presents variants for SVRG and SAGA, plus a Catalyst-like acceleration yielding theoretically superior rates to accelerated batch methods.  
- Complexity comparisons (Table 1) comprehensively show theoretical advantages over batch and stochastic competitors.  
- Experimental results on two datasets support theoretical gains, especially for the accelerated method on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Paper is technically strong, complete, and mathematically rigorous.  
- Writing and organization are clear; assumptions and notation are precise; Section 6 effectively connects to broader monotone operator theory.  
- Highly original: first to prove linear convergence of variance-reduced stochastic methods for general composite saddle-point problems.  
- Significant impact: extends powerful stochastic methods to many ML tasks (e.g., AUC, group-sparsity, robust optimization).  
- Con: Step-sizes and parameters depend on Lipschitz and strong convexity/concavity constants that may be hard to estimate in practice.  
- Con: Experimental scope is limited to two datasets with a specific non-separable structure; broader empirical validation would strengthen the paper.  
- Overall recommendation: strengths overwhelmingly outweigh minor weaknesses; strong accept.
