- Addresses convex-concave saddle-point problems with large-scale separable structure common in ML.
- Extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to saddle-point problems, providing first linear convergence for large-scale saddle-point optimization.
- Novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, generalizing applicability to variational inequalities.
- Distinguishes two separability types: function-based and partial-derivative-based.
- Shows non-uniform sampling is important for algorithmic efficiency.
- Proposes an acceleration framework via a modified "catalyst" method.
- Empirical results demonstrate advantages on ML tasks with non-separable losses and regularizers, especially for ill-conditioned problems.
- Technical novelty: significant extension of variance reduction techniques and elegant use of monotone operators.
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis: linear convergence rates, sampling probability discussion, and computational complexity.
- Weakness: presentation is dense and hard to follow; needs more intuition and visual aids.
- Weakness: experimental scope is narrow; additional benchmarks (e.g., game theory, variational inequalities) would strengthen validation.
- Weakness: algorithms require known strong convexity–concavity constants, limiting practical applicability; adaptivity remains future work.
- Weakness: comparisons to recent primal–dual and other saddle-point methods are insufficient.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, impactful methods with strong theory, practical relevance, and useful innovations (acceleration and non-uniform sampling).
- Arguments against acceptance: limited clarity/accessibility and narrow experimental evaluation.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand experiments.
