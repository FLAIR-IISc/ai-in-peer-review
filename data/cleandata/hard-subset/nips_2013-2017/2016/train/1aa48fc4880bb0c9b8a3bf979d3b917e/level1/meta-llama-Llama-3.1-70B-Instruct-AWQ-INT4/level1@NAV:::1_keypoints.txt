- Proposes stochastic variance reduction (SVRG/SAGA) extensions for saddle-point problems, achieving the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.  
- Provides a convergence analysis specific to saddle-point setups that differs from convex minimization analysis.  
- Highlights two notions of problem splitting (by functions or by partial derivatives) and emphasizes the importance of non-uniform sampling.  

Strengths:
- Novel extension of variance-reduction methods to saddle-point problems, advancing ML algorithmic tools.  
- Thorough and rigorous convergence analysis supporting the proposed methods.  
- Comprehensive experiments demonstrating practical effectiveness.  
- Clear and well-organized presentation that aids reader comprehension.  

Weaknesses:
- Assumes known strong convexity–concavity constants, which may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Lacks detailed empirical or theoretical comparison with other existing saddle-point algorithms.  
- Many proofs and technical details are placed in appendices, potentially hindering accessibility.  

Originality:
- Introduces a new application of SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems.  
- Develops a distinct convergence analysis for this problem class.  
- Supports claims with comprehensive experimental validation.  

Significance:
- Makes a meaningful contribution to ML by enabling scalable, linearly convergent saddle-point solvers.  
- Methods are potentially broadly applicable in domains with saddle-point formulations.  
- Results may inspire further algorithmic developments for saddle-point optimization.  

Arguments:
- Pro: Novel methods, strong convergence theory, and extensive experiments support acceptance.  
- Con: Reliance on known strong constants, limited comparisons to prior work, and relegated technical details argue for revisions.  

Overall recommendation:
- Strengths outweigh weaknesses; reviewer recommends acceptance.
