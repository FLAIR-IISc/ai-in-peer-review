This paper extends stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) from finite-sum convex minimization [1–3,24] to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems, providing linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity. The key technical move is to analyze forward–backward splitting in the monotone operator framework [4,8], avoiding co-coercivity and thereby covering general saddle-point and even variational inequality problems [5,6]. The authors discuss two natural decompositions (by functions and by partial derivatives/factored operators), show the importance of non-uniform sampling, and propose an acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst-style outer loop [7] that dominates accelerated batch alternatives. They position the work against batch primal–dual/forward–backward and accelerated variants (e.g., Chambolle–Pock [20]) and stochastic FB [22], and clarify relations to separable-coordinate methods [11,12]. Experiments on non-separable losses/regularizers (e.g., AUC surrogates and grouped/TV-type penalties) corroborate the theory, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

Quality: The theory is careful and, given the operator setup, technically sound: linear rates for SVRG/SAGA with explicit step sizes, complexity bounds reflecting L and the sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π), and a clear proximal-point view for acceleration with favorable trade-offs. Practical aspects (mini-batching, storage for factored vs individual splits, resampling for non-uniform SAGA [25]) are addressed. Some empirical evaluation is limited (two datasets, few baselines beyond batch and primal methods); comparisons to extra-gradient/mirror-prox style methods are missing.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, but notation is dense (Ω, L vs L̄). The discussion of “splits” (function vs factored/partial derivative) could be tightened, and guidance on choosing π, m, and step sizes in practice would help. A brief intuitive explanation of L̄(π) and its computation would improve readability.

Originality: High. This appears to be the first linearly convergent variance-reduced framework for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems, with a novel monotone-operator analysis distinct from prior co-coercive treatments (e.g., SMART [30]). The catalyst-style acceleration in the saddle-point setting is also new and simple.

Significance: Strong. Many ML problems with non-separable structures are now addressable at large scale with provable linear rates. The extension to general monotone inclusions broadens impact.

Pros:
- First variance-reduced, linearly convergent methods for general saddle points; solid theory.
- Monotone operator analysis extends applicability (incl. VIs).
- Non-uniform sampling and factored splits are well-motivated; clear complexity gains.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst.

Cons:
- Limited experimental breadth; missing comparisons to extra-gradient/mirror-prox.
- Requires known strong convexity parameters; no adaptivity.
- Notational heaviness; practical guidance on π, m, and L̄(π) could be expanded.
- Storage/computation trade-offs for individual vs factored splits deserve deeper empirical study.

Overall: A strong, significant contribution well within NeurIPS scope. I recommend acceptance.
