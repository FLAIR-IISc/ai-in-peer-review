

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning through Lagrange or Fenchel duality. The authors recognize that standard convex minimization analysis does not apply to saddle-point problems and instead use monotone operator theory to prove convergence. This is a significant extension from prior work on variance reduction methods (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) which only apply to separable convex minimization problems. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a convergence analysis using monotone operators that extends beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities; (2) identification of two distinct notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) that are crucial for saddle-point problems; (3) demonstration that non-uniform sampling is essential for efficiency; and (4) an acceleration technique via the "catalyst" framework.

The paper is technically sound and well-justified. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful attention to the differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization. The authors properly address the challenges of extending variance reduction methods to this setting, such as the need for convex-concave splits rather than simply convex splits, and the importance of non-uniform sampling. The monotone operator approach is particularly elegant, providing a unified framework that encompasses saddle-point problems and variational inequalities. The experimental results (Section 7) strongly support the theoretical claims, showing significant practical improvements over existing batch and stochastic methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with optimization. The connection to previous work (e.g., SVRG, SAGA, forward-backward methods) is well-explained, and the paper properly situates its contributions within the broader literature. The examples in Section 2.1 (supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, convex relaxation of unsupervised learning) effectively demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.

The originality of this work is strong. While variance reduction methods have been extensively studied for convex minimization, extending them to saddle-point problems is novel. The key insight that the convex minimization analysis doesn't apply to saddle-point problems, and that monotone operators provide a more natural framework, is particularly valuable. The distinction between different split types (factored vs. individual) and the demonstration of why non-uniform sampling is crucial represent important conceptual advances that weren't present in prior work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many important machine learning problems (supervised learning with non-separable losses, robust optimization, certain unsupervised learning formulations) naturally lead to saddle-point problems where existing stochastic methods don't apply. This paper provides the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for these problems. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods significantly outperform existing approaches, both in theory and practice. The acceleration via the catalyst framework provides an additional practical advantage over batch methods, and the connection to monotone operators suggests broader applicability beyond machine learning.

Strengths:
- First extension of variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems
- Rigorous theoretical analysis using monotone operators (beyond saddle-point problems)
- Clear distinction between different split types and the importance of convex-concave splits
- Practical demonstration of non-uniform sampling benefits
- Effective acceleration via catalyst framework
- Strong experimental validation on real machine learning problems

Weaknesses:
- The paper could provide more intuition for why the convex minimization analysis doesn't apply to saddle-point problems (beyond just stating it)
- The connection to the broader literature on monotone operators could be strengthened (e.g., comparing more directly to [28, 29] which also discuss monotone operators)
- The practical implementation details (e.g., how to compute the optimal sampling probabilities in practice) could be elaborated

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is sound, the practical implications are clear, and the results represent a meaningful advance over prior work. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and provides both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms for solving a class of problems that arise frequently in machine learning.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria exceptionally well. It is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a novel contribution (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with practical impact (Significance). The work is clearly relevant to NIPS as it addresses optimization problems that are central to many machine learning applications, and the paper is well-positioned within the recent NIPS literature on optimization methods.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a substantial contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with both theoretical and practical significance. The techniques developed here will likely be adopted in future machine learning algorithms that require solving saddle-point problems.
