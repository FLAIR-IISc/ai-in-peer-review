## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper tackles the important problem of scaling up algorithms for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, common in machine learning (e.g., regularized learning, robust optimization). The authors extend Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) and Stochastic Average Gradient (SAGA) methods to this setting, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale problems of this type. The core idea of adapting existing variance reduction techniques is straightforward, but the analysis and extensions are substantial.

The paper clearly establishes the connection to prior work on stochastic optimization, particularly SVRG [2] and SAGA [3]. It correctly points out the limitations of these methods when directly applied to saddle-point problems, emphasizing the need for a different analytical approach.  The introduction of monotone operators as a unifying framework (building on [5, 6]) is a valuable contribution, allowing for broader applicability beyond standard saddle-point scenarios.  The identification of two types of “splits” (function-wise and partial derivative-wise) and the importance of non-uniform sampling are also insightful. Furthermore, the acceleration via the “catalyst” framework [7] presents a practical improvement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, building on the theory of monotone operators to establish linear convergence.  The authors carefully address the differences between convex minimization and saddle-point analysis.
*   **Originality:**  Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems and proving linear convergence is a novel contribution. The analysis using monotone operators expands the applicability of the algorithms.
*   **Significance:** The proposed methods address a critical bottleneck in scaling up many machine learning algorithms.  The potential for acceleration and the broad applicability to variational inequalities are significant.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized.  The authors provide a clear motivation and explain the key concepts effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Setup:** While experiments are presented, the description feels somewhat limited.  More detail on the datasets, hyperparameter tuning, and comparison to alternative methods (beyond those mentioned) would strengthen the empirical validation.  Specifically, a discussion of the practical challenges in implementing non-uniform sampling could be helpful.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions (A)-(C) are fairly general, but their practical implications are not always fully discussed.  Understanding how sensitive the performance is to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical complexity analysis, while detailed, can be difficult to parse.  A more intuitive explanation of the key factors influencing performance would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Potential for practical impact in a wide range of machine learning applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental validation could be more comprehensive.
*   Theoretical complexity analysis could be simplified for broader understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**  This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the experimental setup and complexity analysis.
