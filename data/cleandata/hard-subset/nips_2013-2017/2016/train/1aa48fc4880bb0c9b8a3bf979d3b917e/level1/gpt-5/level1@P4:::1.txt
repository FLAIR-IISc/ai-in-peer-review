Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth convex-concave and M is proximable and strongly (λ,γ)-convex-concave. The main contribution is to extend variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to general saddle-point and broader monotone operator settings, yielding the first large-scale linearly convergent incremental algorithms for non-separable saddle-point problems. The analysis departs from standard convex minimization proofs by working with monotone operators: the forward (smooth) part is B(x,y) = (∂xK, −∂yK), split as a finite sum B = Σ_i B_i with Lipschitz B_i, possibly not gradients of scalar functions; the backward step uses the resolvent of A = ∂M structured by proxσM.

Key algorithmic elements include: (i) variance-reduced estimators for B using reference points (SVRG) or stored past values (SAGA), (ii) two split notions tailored to saddle structure (function-based and “factored” row/column splits for bilinear K), (iii) non-uniform sampling with tight smoothness constant L̄(π) controlling rates, and (iv) acceleration via a Catalyst-like proximal point outer loop. Theoretically, they prove linear convergence for SVRG and SAGA with step sizes σ ≈ 1/(L^2 + c L̄^2/m), where L is the Lipschitz constant of B under a scaled norm Ω and L̄(π) captures sampling. For bilinear K(x,y)=y^T K x, they relate L and L̄ to operator, Frobenius, and max norms of K and derive complexities showing improvements over batch forward–backward and its acceleration (e.g., replacing ‖K‖op by ‖K‖F under non-uniform sampling). They further generalize the analysis to finding zeros of A + Σ_i B_i with A maximal strongly monotone and B monotone (no cocoercivity), covering variational inequalities. Experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers and a separable baseline demonstrate that (accelerated) SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling outperform batch and simple stochastic FB; accelerated SVRG is most effective on ill-conditioned problems.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Convergence analyses for SVRG and SAGA are plausible and internally consistent, relying on monotonicity and Lipschitz properties of B_i, strong (λ,γ)-convex-concavity in M, and appropriate step sizes. The requirement that σ be inverse proportional to L^2 + L̄^2/m and the epoch length choices are explicit. The proximal point based acceleration analysis is straightforward and avoids Nesterov machinery.
- The handling of factored splits in bilinear problems and non-uniform sampling is careful, with clear bounds L̄ ≤ ‖K‖F/√(λγ) for appropriate probabilities, yielding meaningful complexity separations from batch methods.
- The SAGA non-uniform case correctly notes the need for a post-update resampling step for the theory; the experiments use a practical mixture heuristic, which departs from the analyzed variant.
- The extension to monotone operators is technically sound at a high level (uses resolvent of A and forward steps on B). Not relying on cocoercivity broadens scope compared to related SMART-style analyses, though step sizes may be conservative.

Clarity:
- The paper is well-organized: problem setup, prior batch/stochastic methods, variance-reduced algorithms with pseudocode, sampling/complexity discussion, acceleration, monotone operator extension, and experiments. Definitions (Ω-norm, L, L̄(π)) are explicit. The factored vs individual splits are clearly described for bilinear K.
- A few aspects could be clarified: how optimality gaps or distances are estimated in experiments; practical guidance for choosing m and probabilities; and explicit comparison to closely related stochastic primal-dual methods beyond separable cases.

Originality:
- Extending SVRG/SAGA to general convex-concave composite saddle-point problems with non-separable structures, allowing Bi not to be gradients, and proving linear convergence via monotone operators appears novel circa NIPS 2016. Prior work (SAG/SVRG/SAGA) focused on convex minimization; stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (e.g., SPDC) or ADMM-like methods either require separability or address specific ERM structures. The acceleration via a proximal point outer loop adapted to saddle points is also a meaningful adaptation of Catalyst.

Significance:
- Many ML problems benefit from saddle-point formulations with non-separable losses/regularizers; providing linearly convergent incremental methods with demonstrable improvement over batch methods is valuable. The non-uniform sampling analysis and factored splits provide practical guidance. The monotone operator extension broadens impact to variational inequalities.

Strengths:
- First linear-convergence VR algorithms for general composite saddle-point problems.
- Clean monotone-operator-based analysis not relying on cocoercivity.
- Non-uniform sampling theory with improved constants; practical factored/individual splits for bilinear K.
- Catalyst-style acceleration with simple proximal point view and improved complexities.
- Clear algorithms and complexity summaries; experiments align with theory.

Weaknesses:
- Assumes knowledge of strong convexity–concavity parameters (λ,γ) and requires M to carry the strong structure; adaptivity is left to future work.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope (two datasets), with no comparisons to some contemporary stochastic primal-dual/variance-reduced methods tailored to structured ERM, and limited ablations on sampling and m.
- Theoretical SAGA non-uniform variant requires resampling step that is not used in practice; the empirical variant lacks guarantees.
- Some claims (“always superior to accelerated batch algorithms”) are stronger than what is proven; improvements depend on constants and problem dimensions.
- Extension to general monotone operators is not empirically validated and details are deferred to appendices.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and broadly applicable extension of VR methods to saddle-point/monotone operators with solid linear convergence proofs.
- Meaningful theoretical insights (sampling, splits, acceleration) with practical consequences.
- Empirical results support the theoretical advantages, especially in ill-conditioned and non-separable settings.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental section could be more comprehensive and include stronger baselines and ablations.
- Practical aspects (parameter tuning, resampling, choice of splits) could be elaborated.
- Dependence on strong convexity–concavity in M limits applicability; no adaptive schemes demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to SPDC or other stochastic primal-dual methods on overlapping problem instances, and ablations on mini-batch size and sampling distributions.
- Clarify how distances to optimizer are computed; include primal-dual gap plots.
- Provide practical guidance for selecting splits and probabilities; report storage/time trade-offs for SAGA (individual vs factored).
- Discuss potential adaptive strategies for (λ,γ) and step sizes, or empirical sensitivity.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound, novel, and potentially impactful advance in stochastic variance reduction for saddle-point problems, with a clear analysis and promising empirical evidence.
