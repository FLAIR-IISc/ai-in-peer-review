- Introduces linear-convergent SVRG and SAGA extensions for convex-concave finite-sum saddle-point problems with a proximable strongly convex-concave term.
- Main contribution: first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for this class of saddle-point problems.
- Main contribution: novel convergence analysis using monotone operator theory, avoiding co-coercivity assumptions and broadening applicability.
- Main contribution: analysis of saddle-point-specific structures (factored vs. individual splits) and the importance of non-uniform sampling for optimal rates.
- Main contribution: extends Catalyst acceleration to this setting, providing an accelerated algorithm superior to accelerated full-batch methods.
- Strength: addresses an important open problem, enabling finite-sum variance reduction for non-separable saddle-point models at scale.
- Strength: technically original and deep analysis that correctly models the saddle-point operator’s monotonicity rather than co-coercivity.
- Strength: clear writing, precise theoretical statements, helpful complexity table, and convincing experiments on non-separable problems.
- Weakness: accelerated methods require problem-dependent constants (λ, γ, L, L̄) that are often unknown and hard to tune in practice.
- Weakness: lacks comparison and discussion of stochastic primal-dual coordinate descent methods that also achieve linear rates and exploit structure.
- Weakness: SAGA’s storage can be prohibitive for bilinear individual splits (storing n·d components); memory–computation trade-offs should be made explicit.
- Pro for acceptance: fills a significant gap by providing the first linear-convergence stochastic algorithms for broad finite-sum saddle-point problems.
- Pro for acceptance: novel, general monotone-operator-based theoretical analysis that advances optimization theory.
- Pro for acceptance: strong empirical validation showing the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) outperform baselines.
- Pro for acceptance: successful extension of Catalyst acceleration strengthens theoretical and practical contributions.
- Con for acceptance: practical implementation difficulties due to unknown problem constants.
- Con for acceptance: incomplete comparison with primal-dual coordinate methods limits the completeness of empirical/theoretical positioning.
- Conclusion: overall an excellent, high-impact paper with minor weaknesses that do not undermine its significance; recommended for acceptance.
