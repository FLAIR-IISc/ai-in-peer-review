- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, yielding the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class.
- Uses monotone operator theory rather than convex minimization analysis to obtain the theoretical results.
- Identifies two distinct types of problem splits for saddle-points (function-based vs. partial-derivative-based) and emphasizes their differences.
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for algorithmic efficiency.
- Extends methods to accelerated variants via the "catalyst" framework.
- Provides rigorous convergence analysis that properly handles saddle-point structure and avoids incorrect convex-minimization arguments.
- Generalizes beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities/monotone operators (Section 6).
- Experimental results (Section 7) demonstrate superiority over batch and stochastic alternatives, especially for ill-conditioned problems.
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and connects well to related work.
- Main strength: novel theoretical extension of variance reduction to saddle-point/monotone-operator settings with provable linear convergence.
- Weakness: limited discussion of handling unknown strong convexity–concavity constants in practice.
- Weakness: experimental scope could be broader (more datasets and comparisons to specialized saddle-point solvers).
- Weakness: connections to specific machine-learning applications could be more explicitly articulated.
- Acceptance arguments: significant theoretical advance, empirical superiority, and broader applicability to variational inequalities.
- Rejection arguments: practical limitations (unknown parameters) and limited experiments slightly reduce impact, though considered minor relative to the contribution.
