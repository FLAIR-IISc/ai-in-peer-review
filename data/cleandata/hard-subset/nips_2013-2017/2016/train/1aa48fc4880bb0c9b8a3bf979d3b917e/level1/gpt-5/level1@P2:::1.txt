Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG, SAGA) from convex minimization to composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y) + M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is strongly convex–concave with a tractable proximal operator. The key contributions are: (i) linear-convergence analyses of SVRG/SAGA-type forward–backward splitting in the saddle-point setting using monotone operator theory (rather than cocoercivity arguments used in convex minimization), (ii) two notions of finite-sum structure (splits by functions/partials), including “factored” splits that match the saddle-point structure, (iii) principled non-uniform sampling with refined smoothness constants L̄(π), (iv) an acceleration by a proximal-point/catalyst scheme tailored to saddle points yielding improved complexity, and (v) extension of the analysis beyond saddle points to finding zeros of sums of monotone operators, including variational inequalities.

This work builds on SAG/SVRG/SAGA [1–3,24] and on primal–dual and forward–backward methods [10,19–21]. Relative to stochastic primal–dual coordinate methods such as SPDC and related work [11,12], the proposed algorithms are not restricted to separable losses/regularizers and handle general composite saddle points; to my knowledge this is new. The monotone-operator viewpoint connects to classical results on resolvents and proximal-point methods [8,27] and contrasts with recent variance-reduction analyses in minimization that rely on cocoercivity [6,30]. The acceleration strategy adapts the “catalyst” framework [7] to the saddle-point setting, leveraging the proximal-point contraction for strongly monotone inclusions.

Quality
Technical soundness appears strong. The paper provides clear algorithmic descriptions (SVRG/SAGA variants and the accelerated outer loop) with explicit stepsizes and mini-batch sizes, and proves linear convergence under standard assumptions (strong convex–concavity of M; Lipschitz B = ∑ Bi; B monotone). The move to monotone operators is both natural and necessary, as cocoercivity need not hold in saddle-point problems. The complexity bounds track oracle costs T(B), T(Bi), and Tprox(M), and the refined constants L vs. L̄(π) yield meaningful guidance for non-uniform sampling. The bilinear case is treated in detail with factored vs. elementwise splits and norm inequalities (‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, ‖K‖max), leading to interpretable complexities.

A few caveats:
- SAGA’s non-uniform-sampling guarantee requires a resampling step; the paper notes the practical variant without resampling, but theory does not cover it.
- Some bounds depend on L and L̄; guidance on estimating these or using backtracking/adaptive steps would improve practical robustness.
- The acceleration analysis is for SVRG; SAGA acceleration is claimed plausible but not proved.
- The forward–backward baseline discussion could better situate against extragradient/mirror-prox methods that are classically used for monotone VIs.

Empirically, experiments on two datasets (non-separable AUC surrogate plus non-separable regularizer; and a separable ERM) show consistent gains of SAGA/SVRG over batch and stochastic FB, and clear benefits from acceleration on ill-conditioned regimes. However, the empirical section is limited in breadth (two tasks) and ablations (e.g., sensitivity to sampling, mini-batch size, factored vs. elementwise splits) and omits comparisons with extragradient-type baselines.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized and readable. The monotone-operator framing and the role of λ, γ scaling are explained, and algorithm pseudocode is given. The discussion of sampling probabilities and the associated constants is helpful, especially in the bilinear case. Some notational points could be clarified (e.g., consistent use of L vs. L̄ throughout; centralizing the definition and intuition for the weighted norm Ω; isolating conditions under which factored splits reduce storage). Adding a short “practical recipe” for choosing splits/sampling and stepsizes would benefit practitioners.

Originality
The contribution is original in bringing variance reduction with linear rates to general composite saddle-point problems via monotone-operator analysis, and in exhibiting acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst outer loop with improved complexities. The identification of factored vs. elementwise splits and associated non-uniform sampling in the saddle-point context is novel and practically relevant. The extension to general monotone inclusions (without cocoercivity) broadens the scope beyond prior variance-reduction works.

Significance
The results are significant: many important ML problems are naturally cast as saddle points with non-separable structure (robust optimization, structured losses like AUC, complex proximal regularizers). Having linearly convergent, incremental methods with principled sampling and acceleration can meaningfully reduce training time compared to batch primal–dual methods. The monotone-operator view may catalyze further advances for VIs and games. Limitations include the requirement of strong convex–concavity (or strong monotonicity) and access to the proximal of M; nonetheless this covers many practical models.

Pros for acceptance
- First linear-convergence variance-reduced algorithms for general composite saddle-point problems; solid and general analysis via monotone operators.
- Clear algorithmic design with practically useful non-uniform sampling schemes and factored splits; storage/complexity trade-offs are explicit.
- Acceleration via proximal-point “catalyst” is simple and provides provable improvements; matches intuition from minimization while being technically cleaner here.
- Extension to monotone inclusions broadens impact.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory; accelerated SVRG performs best on ill-conditioned problems.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental section is limited; lacks comparisons to extragradient/mirror-prox and to recent stochastic primal–dual methods beyond the batch baselines.
- Dependence on known strong convexity/concavity and Lipschitz constants; no adaptive stepsize scheme is provided.
- SAGA non-uniform sampling theory requires resampling; practical variant used in experiments is not covered.
- Acceleration analysis only for SVRG; SAGA acceleration left as a conjecture.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons with extragradient/mirror-prox baselines for strongly monotone saddle-point/VIs.
- Provide adaptive or backtracking strategies to avoid reliance on a priori L and L̄, or discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Empirical ablations: uniform vs. non-uniform sampling, mini-batch size, factored vs. elementwise splits, and storage/time trade-offs.
- Clarify the computation of “distance to optimum” in plots; include primal–dual gap curves.
- If feasible, include a proof sketch or theorem for accelerated SAGA, or discuss obstacles.

Overall evaluation
A strong paper: technically solid, novel, and relevant. Despite somewhat limited experiments, the theoretical contributions are substantial and broadly applicable. I recommend acceptance.
