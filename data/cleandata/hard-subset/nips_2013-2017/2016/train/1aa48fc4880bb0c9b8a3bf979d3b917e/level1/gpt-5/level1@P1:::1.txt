Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex-concave saddle-point (SP) problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) where M is proximable and strongly convex-concave and K is smooth, possibly nonseparable. It extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA) from finite-sum convex minimization to this SP setting. The extension is algorithmically natural—replace full gradients of K by variance-reduced stochastic estimates of the operator B(x,y) = (∂xK, -∂yK)—but the analysis is nontrivial because standard convex minimization arguments (e.g., co-coercivity) do not apply. The authors develop a monotone operator view, proving linear convergence without using cocoercivity, and show the approach also covers certain variational inequalities.

They (i) formalize two notions of “splits”: by functions or by partial derivatives (factored row/column sketches for bilinear K), (ii) show splits must preserve convex-concave structure, (iii) derive importance sampling rules and constants L̄(π) that sharpen bounds, (iv) provide storage-efficient SAGA updates for factored splits, and (v) design a simple acceleration via a Catalyst-style proximal-point regularization that improves complexity and is claimed always superior (up to constants) to accelerated batch methods.

The work relates to: variance-reduced methods for finite-sum minimization (SAG/SVRG/SAGA; NIPS’12–’14), primal-dual/forward-backward and accelerated PDHG (Chambolle-Pock; NIPS’16 citations), and primal-dual coordinate methods with linear convergence under separability (SPDC; ICML’15; also stochastic PD coordinate variants). Unlike SPDC/ASPD, which require separable structures, this paper targets nonseparable K or proximable M and provides linear rates with variance reduction in that generality. Stochastic Mirror-Prox and stochastic FB for SPs yield O(1/t) without strong convexity; here strong convexity-concavity in M is crucial for linear convergence.

Quality (correctness and empirical support)
- Theory: The operator-splitting analysis is careful and appropriate for SPs: forward-backward with resolvent of A=∂M and forward steps on B=∇K, with step sizes driven by Lipschitz constants in a weighted norm Ω defined by λ,γ. The convergence statements (SVRG linear rate per epoch; SAGA linear rate per iteration; accelerated proximal-point outer loop with geometric contraction (1+τ)−1) are plausible, and the use of L̄(π) captures importance sampling. The fact that Bi need not be gradients of functions is a meaningful generalization for SPs (e.g., factored row/column sampling for bilinear K).
- Constants/complexities: The run-time bounds reflect T(B), T(Bi), and Tprox(M), with mini-batching to offset proximal costs. For bilinear K, the norm inequalities between ‖K‖op, ‖K‖F, and ‖K‖max are used to compare sampling schemes and show the advantage of importance sampling. The accelerated complexity L̄√T(B) max_i T(Bi) (up to logs) is consistent with Catalyst intuition.
- Experiments: Two datasets (sido, rcv1) with a nonseparable proximal regularizer (OSCAR-like) and a nonseparable AUC surrogate. Results show: importance sampling matters; SAGA/SVRG outperform batch FB/accelerated FB; acceleration helps more for ill-conditioned problems; when separability holds, primal SAGA is still best (as expected) whereas generic accelerated batch is inferior. While limited in scope, the experiments are aligned with the theory.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. The problem setup, operator definitions, and algorithm pseudocode are clear. The discussion of two types of splits and storage for SAGA is helpful. Some parts could benefit from more intuition (e.g., why FB is appropriate for strongly convex-concave SPs vs extragradient; and a brief sketch of why co-coercivity fails in SPs). The dependence of step sizes on L vs L̄ and how to estimate these in practice should be expanded. Practical guidance for computing non-uniform sampling probabilities (and their cost) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- Extending SVRG/SAGA with a monotone operator analysis to SP problems where Bi need not be gradients is novel and technically interesting.
- The “factored” split for bilinear K with O(n+d) storage in SAGA and tailored importance sampling is a useful contribution.
- The Catalyst-style acceleration adapted to SPs via proximal-point interpretation (without Nesterov’s momentum machinery) is elegant and, to my knowledge at the time, new for this general SP/monotone operator setting.

Significance
Saddle-point formulations are ubiquitous (duality, structured losses, robust optimization). Prior linear-convergence stochastic methods largely rely on separability; this work brings variance reduction and acceleration to more general SPs with proximable but nonseparable M and smooth K. The results will likely be adopted for large-scale problems with nonseparable structures and could stimulate further work (e.g., extragradient + variance reduction; adaptive strong convexity). The extension to monotone operators broadens the impact beyond SPs.

Pros
- First linear-convergent variance-reduced methods for general convex-concave SPs (beyond separable cases), with a clean monotone operator analysis.
- Importance sampling design with concrete bounds; practical storage trick for SAGA in factored splits.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point outer loop; theoretically superior complexity to accelerated batch.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory, including ablations on sampling and acceleration.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is modest (two datasets, limited baselines). Including Mirror-Prox/extragradient baselines and more tasks (e.g., robust optimization, VI) would strengthen claims.
- Assumes known strong convexity-concavity parameters (λ,γ) for step sizes; no adaptive tuning is provided.
- Overhead and implementation details for non-uniform sampling probabilities (e.g., computing row/column norms and updating them) are not analyzed empirically.
- Comparison to closely related primal-dual coordinate/variance-reduced methods (e.g., SPDC) could be made more explicit to delineate when each applies and expected performance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a discussion and, if possible, experiments comparing against extragradient (e.g., deterministic Chambolle-Pock; stochastic Mirror-Prox) to position FB+VR versus EG in practice.
- Provide practical guidance for estimating L and L̄(π), and report sensitivity to misspecified step sizes.
- Clarify the resampling requirement in non-uniform SAGA and report empirical impact of omitting it.
- Expand experimental suite to include a VI example and a robust optimization setting; report wall-clock times including probability precomputation.
- Discuss how to add artificial strong convexity-concavity when M is not strongly convex-concave, and the trade-offs.

Overall evaluation
High-quality theoretical contribution with a solid, general framework and promising practical implications. While experiments could be broadened, the paper addresses an important gap and advances the state of the art in SP optimization with variance reduction and acceleration. I lean toward acceptance.
