This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, a common setting in machine learning. The core idea is to adapt these algorithms, which are well-established for convex minimization, to the min-max framework. The authors identify several key challenges and opportunities, including adapting the convergence analysis (using monotone operators), handling different notions of splits, the necessity of convex-concave splits, the importance of non-uniform sampling, and acceleration via a “catalyst” framework.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in stochastic optimization. SVRG [2] and SAGA [3] are foundational algorithms for large-scale convex optimization. Extending these to saddle-point problems is a natural progression, particularly given the prevalence of such problems in machine learning, as highlighted by the examples provided (supervised learning, robust optimization, etc.). The use of monotone operator theory [8, 28] to prove convergence is a clever approach, generalizing the analysis beyond purely saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing batch algorithms for saddle-point problems [20, 22] and acknowledge the connection to related stochastic methods [23, 29, 30].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a valuable contribution.  While the algorithmic extension is relatively straightforward, the theoretical analysis and the identification of key considerations (non-uniform sampling, convex-concave splits) are significant.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The use of monotone operator theory provides a rigorous convergence analysis, and the extensions to variational inequalities broaden the applicability of the results. The analysis appears complete and carefully considered, with clear convergence rate bounds.
* **Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a common problem setting in machine learning and provides practical algorithms with promising empirical performance (demonstrated in Section 7).
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear, and the notation is consistent. The connection to previous work is well-established.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors explore various aspects of the problem, including different split formulations and sampling strategies, and analyze their impact on performance.  The discussion of the “catalyst” acceleration is a particularly strong point.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Setup:** While the experimental results are promising, the choice of datasets and the specific experimental setup could be more thoroughly justified. The authors mention testing losses in Appendix F but don’t provide detailed comparisons to state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks. It's somewhat unclear why the primal SAGA implementation doesn’t outperform the saddle-point SAGA method in some cases.
* **Assumptions:** The assumptions on the functions K and M, particularly the strong convexity-concavity condition (A), might be strong for some applications. The paper acknowledges this and mentions exploring adaptivity in future work, which would be beneficial.  It would be useful to discuss the robustness of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The complexity analysis is detailed, but the dependence on various parameters (L, L̄, T(B), T(Bi)) can be difficult to interpret. A more intuitive understanding of the practical implications of these parameters would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Catalyst Framework:** While the acceleration via the "catalyst" framework is presented, the explanation is somewhat concise. A more detailed explanation of how this acceleration works in the saddle-point setting would improve the paper.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in solving saddle-point problems with stochastic variance reduction methods.
* The algorithms are well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by a rigorous analysis.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* The work addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The strong assumptions on the problem functions may limit the applicability of the algorithms.
* The complexity analysis, while detailed, could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the literature on stochastic optimization and saddle-point problems. The theoretical results are significant, the algorithms are well-designed, and the empirical results are promising. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more detailed explanation of the catalyst acceleration framework.
