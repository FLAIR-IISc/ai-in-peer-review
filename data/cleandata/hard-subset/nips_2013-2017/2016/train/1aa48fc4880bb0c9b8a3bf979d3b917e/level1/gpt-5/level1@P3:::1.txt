Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth and M is (λ,γ)-strongly convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator. The main contributions are extensions of variance-reduced stochastic methods (SVRG and SAGA) to this setting, with linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity. The analysis departs from standard convex minimization by framing saddle-point problems as zeros of monotone operators A+B, where A is strongly monotone (from M) and B is a Lipschitz operator (from K). The authors also:
- articulate two notions of splitting B into components: function-based and derivative-based (including a factored row/column split for bilinear K),
- derive importance sampling schemes that reduce smoothness constants,
- present an acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper,
- extend the analysis beyond saddle points to general monotone inclusions.

This connects to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization (Le Roux et al.; Johnson & Zhang; Defazio et al.), primal-dual methods for separable saddle problems (e.g., SPDC/coordinate schemes), and batch primal–dual splitting (Chambolle–Pock). Stochastic forward–backward for monotone inclusions typically achieves O(1/t) (e.g., Rosasco–Villa–Vũ; Juditsky–Nemirovski mirror-prox), whereas this work shows linear rates via variance reduction under strong convexity–concavity and finite-sum structure. To my knowledge (for NIPS 2016), a variance-reduced analysis for general nonseparable composite saddle-point problems with linear rates was missing; existing linearly convergent primal–dual coordinate methods relied on separability.

Quality:
- Theory: The variance-reduced estimators are standard, but the monotone-operator based analysis is technically solid and appropriately tailored to the saddle-point setting without co-coercivity. The results give linear convergence for SVRG (per epoch) and SAGA (per iteration), with explicit dependences on L and a sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π). The acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper is plausible and clean: the regularized subproblems inherit improved conditioning, and the outer loop enjoys (1−1/(1+τ))^v contraction.
- Assumptions: Strong convexity–concavity is required (and constants known). B must be Lipschitz; the sum B must be monotone (automatic for convex–concave K). The prox of M must be computationally cheap. These are standard but do limit scope.
- Sampling and complexity: The paper carefully develops non-uniform sampling for factored and elementwise splits in bilinear problems, yielding tighter L̄(π) bounds in terms of ‖K‖F and coordinate norms, and provides per-epoch/iteration work bounds that align with practice. The batch FB/accelerated FB baselines are correctly positioned.
- Practicality: The SVRG variant requires periodic full passes to compute B(x̃,ỹ). The SAGA variant may require large memory in the general (non-factored) split, since storing gi∈R^{n+d} per component is prohibitive unless bilinear with factored storage (O(n+d)). The authors acknowledge storage costs and propose factored splits to reduce them, but this limits applicability when K is not bilinear or factorizable.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized. The operator-norm scaling with λ and γ is helpful. Some points could be clearer:
- The statement “the split does need to be done with convex-concave terms” seems at odds with Assumption (C), which explicitly allows derivative-based splits that are not gradients of functions; please clarify.
- The role of the parameter choices (σ, number of inner iterations, and τ in acceleration) could be summarized more intuitively, and a guidance section for practitioners would help.
- The dependence on L vs L̄ in step size/epoch length looks conservative; a brief discussion of tightness would be valuable.

Originality:
Algorithmically, the SVRG/SAGA extensions are natural, but the novelty lies in a unified monotone-operator analysis that does not rely on co-coercivity, the careful treatment of saddle-specific splits (especially factored), the non-uniform sampling analysis, and the catalyst-style acceleration adapted to monotone inclusions. The extension to general monotone inclusions strengthens the contribution beyond saddle-point optimization.

Significance:
The work fills an important gap: variance reduction with linear convergence for nonseparable composite saddle-point problems and general monotone inclusions. The potential impact is broad (robust optimization, variational inequalities, structured prediction). The empirical section suggests clear gains over batch/naive stochastic methods and shows the importance of non-uniform sampling and acceleration, though the experimental scope could be expanded.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid and general theoretical contribution with linear rates under standard strong convexity–concavity.
- Extension to monotone operator inclusions, broadening applicability.
- Insightful handling of sampling and splitting, including factored strategies for bilinear problems.
- Simple and effective acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst.
- Empirical results demonstrate practical benefits and align with theory.

Cons/concerns:
- SAGA memory requirements can be prohibitive outside factored bilinear settings; practical guidance or variants with compressed storage would help.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: few datasets, few baselines; missing comparisons to widely used primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock/PDHG with tuned step sizes, Mirror-Prox, SPDC for applicable cases).
- The analysis constants and prescribed step sizes/epoch lengths may be conservative; no adaptive strategies or backtracking.
- Requires known strong convexity–concavity parameters; no adaptive estimation provided.
- Some clarity issues and small inconsistencies in the text.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines: PDHG (Chambolle–Pock) with optimal over-relaxation; Mirror-Prox; SPDC on problems where separability holds. Include wall-clock and memory comparisons.
- Discuss strategies to reduce SAGA storage (e.g., storing low-dimensional sufficient statistics for factored splits, sketching/compression).
- Provide an adaptive scheme for λ,γ or line-search/backtracking for σ; report sensitivity.
- Clarify the “split does need/does not need convex-concave terms” statement and the precise conditions under which B need not derive from a potential.
- Expand on non-uniform sampling implementation details and costs (precomputing row/column norms, dynamic reweighting).
- Include guidance on choosing m (mini-batch size) and τ in practice, perhaps with heuristics.

Overall:
A strong and timely contribution that advances stochastic variance reduction to a broader and practically relevant class of saddle-point and monotone operator problems, with convincing theory and promising empirical evidence. Addressing the practicality (memory) and baseline breadth would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
