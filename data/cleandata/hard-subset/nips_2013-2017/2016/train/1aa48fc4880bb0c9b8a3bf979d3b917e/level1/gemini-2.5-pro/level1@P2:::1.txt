**Summary**

This paper extends the highly successful stochastic variance reduction methods, SVRG and SAGA, from the domain of convex minimization to the more general class of convex-concave saddle-point problems. Such problems are prevalent in machine learning, arising from non-separable regularizers or losses, robust optimization, and dual formulations. The authors provide the first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for this problem class. While the algorithmic forms are natural extensions of their predecessors, the main contribution lies in a novel convergence analysis. Instead of relying on potential functions common in convex optimization proofs, the authors leverage the framework of monotone operators. This approach is more general, naturally suited to saddle-point problems, and reveals that the algorithms apply even to variational inequalities. The paper further shows that non-uniform sampling is key for optimal performance, explores different ways to decompose the problem, and demonstrates how to achieve acceleration using a Catalyst-like framework, resulting in methods that are theoretically superior to accelerated batch algorithms.

**Review**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound, significant, and well-supported. The core of the paper is the convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA in the saddle-point setting. Moving the analysis to the framework of monotone operators is an elegant and powerful choice. It circumvents the difficulties of applying standard convex analysis and correctly identifies monotonicity as the key property required for convergence, rather than more restrictive properties like co-coercivity, which are not satisfied by saddle-point gradient operators but were assumed in some related prior work. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying mathematical structure.

The work is complete and thorough. The authors present variants for both SVRG and SAGA, analyze the crucial role of non-uniform sampling, and provide an accelerated version. The complexity analysis presented in Table 1 is comprehensive, clearly comparing the proposed methods against a hierarchy of batch and stochastic competitors and demonstrating the theoretical advantages of the new algorithms. The experimental results, though limited to two datasets, convincingly support the theoretical findings, showing a clear performance gain for the proposed methods, especially the accelerated variant on ill-conditioned problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem with concrete machine learning examples where existing fast stochastic methods fail. It effectively positions the work in the context of prior art in both stochastic optimization and saddle-point algorithms. The contributions are explicitly listed and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the assumptions (A-C) are stated precisely. Section 6, which discusses the extension to monotone operators, is particularly insightful as it connects the specific problem to a much broader mathematical context, thereby clarifying the fundamentals of the analysis. While the full proofs are in the appendix, the main body provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to grasp the core ideas.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. While the algorithmic templates are adapted from SVRG and SAGA, the key novelty lies in successfully applying them to a new, important problem class and, most importantly, developing the theoretical machinery to prove their convergence. To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish linear convergence for variance-reduced stochastic methods on general composite saddle-point problems. The use of monotone operator theory for the analysis, specifically without assuming co-coercivity, is a significant departure from previous analyses of SVRG/SAGA and related stochastic operator methods. This new analytical approach is a major contribution in itself. The paper also clearly delineates how its approach differs from prior work on stochastic saddle-point methods (which achieve sublinear rates) and batch methods.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Stochastic variance reduction methods have had a major impact on large-scale convex optimization. By extending these techniques to saddle-point problems, this paper makes them applicable to a much wider range of machine learning tasks, including structured prediction with non-separable losses (e.g., AUC maximization), models with complex group-sparsity or total variation regularizers, and robust optimization. The proposed algorithms demonstrably advance the state-of-the-art, offering substantial speedups over existing methods both in theory and practice. The Catalyst-based acceleration further solidifies this advantage. This paper is likely to become a foundational reference for stochastic optimization in saddle-point settings and will probably inspire considerable follow-up research.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Novelty and Impact:** Provides the first linearly convergent, large-scale stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The convergence analysis based on monotone operators is novel, elegant, and more general than previous analyses for variance-reduced methods.
3.  **Comprehensive Work:** The paper thoroughly treats SVRG, SAGA, non-uniform sampling, and acceleration, providing a complete picture with solid theoretical backing.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed methods offer significant practical speedups over existing batch and stochastic algorithms, as demonstrated by the experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**
1.  **Dependence on Unknown Constants:** The choice of step-size and other parameters depends on Lipschitz and strong convexity-concavity constants, which may be unknown or difficult to estimate in practice. A discussion of adaptivity would have strengthened the paper, although this is a common issue for this class of algorithms.
2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** While illustrative, the experiments are conducted on two datasets with a specific non-separable structure. Demonstrating the efficacy on a wider variety of canonical saddle-point problems from different ML domains could have further broadened the paper's appeal.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical novelty is substantial, and the practical implications are significant. The strengths overwhelmingly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
