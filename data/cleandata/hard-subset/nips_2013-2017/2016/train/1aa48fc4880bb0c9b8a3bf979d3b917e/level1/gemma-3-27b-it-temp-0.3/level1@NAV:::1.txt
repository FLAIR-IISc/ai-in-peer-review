## Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper tackles the important problem of scaling up optimization algorithms for saddle-point problems, common in machine learning, by extending Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods like SVRG and SAGA. The core idea – adapting these methods to the saddle-point setting – is straightforward, but the authors correctly identify several non-trivial challenges and opportunities. They provide a convergence analysis based on monotone operators, demonstrating broader applicability beyond standard saddle-point problems, and highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency. A key contribution is the acceleration of these incremental algorithms using a catalyst framework, achieving performance superior to accelerated batch methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the challenges inherent in extending convex minimization analyses to saddle-point problems. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on stochastic optimization, referencing key papers like [1, 2, 3] and connecting to related work on monotone operator theory [8, 28]. The discussion of different splitting notions (functions vs. partial derivatives) and the necessity of convex-concave splits is insightful. The theoretical results, particularly the convergence analysis using monotone operators, are a significant contribution, broadening the scope of these algorithms. 

However, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the different sampling schemes. While the theoretical advantages of non-uniform sampling are highlighted, a more thorough comparison of its computational cost and sensitivity to parameter tuning would be valuable. The experimental section, while demonstrating improvements over baseline methods, feels somewhat limited.  The choice of datasets and the metrics used could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The connection between the theoretical analysis and the empirical results could also be strengthened.  For example, relating the observed performance gains to the specific properties of the datasets and the chosen regularization parameters would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Extends SVR methods to a challenging and relevant class of problems (saddle-point problems).
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides a rigorous convergence analysis based on monotone operators, extending the applicability of the algorithms.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Demonstrates improvements over existing methods in experiments, and the catalyst acceleration is a valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   **Practical Considerations:**  A deeper discussion of the practical implications of different sampling schemes is needed.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. While the experimental section could be strengthened, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and potential impact of the work justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS.
