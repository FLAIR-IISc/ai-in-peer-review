- Extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to saddle-point problems common in ML.  
- Uses monotone operator theory to prove convergence, covering saddle-point problems and variational inequalities.  
- Identifies two distinct split notions (functions vs. partial derivatives) essential for saddle-point settings.  
- Shows non-uniform sampling is crucial for algorithmic efficiency.  
- Proposes acceleration via the catalyst framework for improved practical performance.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and carefully addresses differences from convex minimization (e.g., need for convex-concave splits).  
- Monotone operator approach provides an elegant, unified framework beyond previous convex-minimization analyses.  
- Experimental results strongly support theory, showing significant improvements, especially on ill-conditioned problems.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and situates contributions within existing literature.  
- Practical examples (non-separable losses, robust optimization, convex relaxations) demonstrate relevance.  
- Original and novel: first extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems with important conceptual insights.  
- Significant impact: enables first large-scale, linearly convergent algorithms for many ML saddle-point problems.  
- Strengths summarized: novelty, rigorous theory, split distinctions, non-uniform sampling benefits, catalyst acceleration, strong experiments.  
- Weaknesses: needs more intuition why convex-minimization analysis fails for saddle points.  
- Weaknesses: connection to broader monotone-operator literature could be strengthened (e.g., compare to [28,29]).  
- Weaknesses: practical implementation details (computing optimal sampling probabilities) could be elaborated.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — substantial, high-quality contribution with both theoretical and practical significance.
