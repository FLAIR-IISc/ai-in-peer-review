

# Review of "Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems"

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG, SAGA) to saddle-point problems, a significant gap in the literature as prior work focused on convex minimization. The authors provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point context, using monotone operator theory rather than convex minimization techniques. They introduce two novel perspectives: (1) the distinction between function-based and partial-derivative-based splits, and (2) the critical role of non-uniform sampling. The paper also demonstrates how to accelerate these algorithms using the "catalyst" framework.

The paper builds on recent work on variance reduction for convex optimization (SAGA, SVRG), but properly recognizes that saddle-point problems require fundamentally different analysis due to their non-convexity in the joint variable space. The authors correctly identify that convex minimization convergence theory does not apply, and instead leverage monotone operator theory to prove linear convergence for their algorithms. This is a significant theoretical contribution that properly contextualizes their work within the broader optimization literature.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the extension to monotone operators (Section 6) significantly broadens the applicability beyond saddle-point problems. The experimental results (Section 7) convincingly demonstrate the superiority of their methods over batch and stochastic alternatives, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The careful treatment of two different split structures (function-based vs. partial derivative-based) is particularly valuable for practitioners.

However, some weaknesses merit consideration. The assumption of strong convexity-concavity (assumption A) is restrictive for many practical applications. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and mention future work on adaptivity, this restriction should be more thoroughly discussed in the context of real-world applications. The paper cites related work on stochastic saddle-point methods (e.g., [11, 12]), but the comparison is not as comprehensive as it could be—particularly regarding how their non-uniform sampling strategy compares to other adaptive sampling approaches.

The paper's significance is high. Saddle-point problems arise naturally in machine learning (robust optimization, structured prediction, non-separable losses), and the authors provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The practical impact is clear: their methods enable efficient optimization for problems where existing techniques fail. The extension to monotone operators further enhances the paper's significance, making the results applicable beyond machine learning to variational inequalities and game theory.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The algorithms are described precisely, and the notation is consistent. The experimental setup is appropriate, and the results support the theoretical claims. The authors correctly acknowledge that their methods require knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants, which is a limitation they plan to address in future work.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a clear and significant contribution. While variance reduction methods have been extensively studied for convex minimization, their extension to saddle-point problems represents a meaningful advance. The key insight—using monotone operator theory instead of convex optimization techniques—is novel and well-justified. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by addressing the fundamental analytical differences between convex minimization and saddle-point problems.

For acceptance considerations:
Pro: Novel theoretical contribution extending variance reduction to saddle-point problems, rigorous convergence analysis using appropriate monotone operator framework, clear experimental validation, significant practical impact for machine learning applications.

Con: Restrictive assumption of strong convexity-concavity (though acknowledged), limited comparison with alternative stochastic saddle-point methods, experiments primarily focused on specific machine learning examples.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The extension of variance reduction techniques to saddle-point problems fills an important gap and has clear practical implications. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results support the claims. While the strong convexity-concavity assumption is a limitation, it is a common issue in optimization papers and the authors acknowledge future work to address it. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art.

I recommend acceptance, as this is a strong theoretical contribution with significant practical implications for a wide range of machine learning problems. The paper is technically sound, well-justified, and makes a clear distinction from previous work. The experimental results provide compelling evidence for the practical advantages of the proposed methods.
