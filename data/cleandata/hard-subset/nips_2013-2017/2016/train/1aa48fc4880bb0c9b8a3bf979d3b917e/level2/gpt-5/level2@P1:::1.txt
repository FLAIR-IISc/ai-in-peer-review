Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper considers composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), with M strongly convex–concave and proximable, and K smooth. The key contribution is to extend stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting, yielding the first incremental, linearly convergent algorithms for non-separable saddle-point problems. The analysis departs from the standard convex minimization arguments (which rely on co-coercivity) and instead uses a monotone operator viewpoint: the saddle-point conditions correspond to finding zeros of A+B, where A is a strongly monotone (set-valued) operator associated with M via its resolvent, and B is the smooth monotone operator induced by K. The paper also:
- formalizes two natural “splits” of B: (i) a sum of components Bi (not necessarily gradients of scalar Ki), and (ii) a “factored” split matching the x/y blocks (useful for storage and sampling),
- advocates non-uniform sampling and provides bounds tied to L̄(π), a sampling-dependent smoothness constant,
- shows a simple acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst scheme that improves the dependence on condition numbers and dominates accelerated batch methods,
- extends the analysis to general monotone inclusions and variational inequalities.

This connects to: SAG/SVRG/SAGA for finite-sum convex minimization (NIPS’12–’14), stochastic and accelerated primal–dual methods (e.g., Chambolle–Pock; stochastic PD coordinate descent such as SPDC), and catalyst acceleration. Prior primal–dual variance reduction either assumes separability or different structural assumptions; to my knowledge, linearly convergent incremental methods for general (non-separable) saddle-point problems were not available. The monotone operator framing also relates to Rockafellar’s proximal point method and more recent splitting schemes.

Quality:
- Theory: The SVRG (Theorem 1) and SAGA (Theorem 2) analyses are technically sound and carefully stated, with explicit step sizes and linear rates. The monotone operator framework clarifies why cocoercivity-based analyses do not apply and how to obtain convergence from strong monotonicity and Lipschitzness alone. The acceleration section leverages the proximal-point viewpoint to obtain a simple, compelling catalyst scheme (Theorem 3) without resorting to Nesterov machinery. The sampling-dependent complexity bounds for bilinear K are well-motivated (using ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max), and the factored vs. individual splits are handled thoughtfully, including storage/time trade-offs.
- Empirics: Experiments on two datasets compare batch FB, accelerated FB, stochastic FB, SAGA/SVRG (uniform vs. non-uniform), and accelerated SVRG. They demonstrate clear gains from variance reduction and from acceleration, especially on ill-conditioned problems, and highlight the importance of non-uniform sampling. The evaluation metric (distance to optimum) matches the theory. However, the empirical scope is limited (two datasets), and comparisons to strong baselines specialized to separable cases (e.g., SPDC) are reported only on one dataset via a primal method; more apples-to-apples comparisons on saddle-point formulations would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is dense but generally well-organized, with assumptions, algorithms, and rates stated precisely, and helpful discussion of sampling and splitting. One phrasing likely contains a typo: in the contributions, “the split does need to be done with convex-concave terms” seems contradicted by Assumption (C) and later discussion; I believe the authors mean “does not need.” A brief “recipe” summarizing when to use factored vs. individual splits and how to pick π in typical ML settings would improve readability.

Originality:
High. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle-point problems with a linear rate under strong convexity–concavity, the non-uniform sampling analysis tailored to the saddle structure, and the catalyst adaptation in the primal–dual/monotone setting appear novel. The monotone operator perspective broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities.

Significance:
High. Many ML problems naturally admit non-separable saddle formulations (structured losses, non-separable regularizers, robust optimization). Providing scalable, linearly convergent incremental methods for these settings is likely to influence practice, similar to how SVRG/SAGA impacted finite-sum convex minimization. The sampling and splitting guidance is also practically useful.

Pros:
- First linearly convergent variance-reduced algorithms for general convex–concave saddle-point problems; clear theoretical guarantees.
- Monotone operator analysis that extends beyond saddle points to variational inequalities.
- Non-uniform sampling and factored/individual splits are well-justified and practically relevant.
- Simple and effective catalyst-style acceleration with clean interpretation as proximal point.
- Experiments substantiate theoretical claims; acceleration and non-uniform sampling matter.

Cons:
- Assumes known strong convexity–concavity constants and a proximable M; no adaptive tuning provided (though discussed as future work).
- Computing/approximating L and L̄(π) may be nontrivial; practical guidance is partial.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires resampling to match theory; experiments use a heuristic mixture—more discussion on the impact would help.
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow and lacks head-to-head comparisons with strong stochastic primal–dual baselines on separable instances or with extragradient-type methods where applicable.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide a practical heuristic for step-size selection without explicit L or L̄(π), and discuss robustness to misspecification?
2. For SAGA with non-uniform sampling, how sensitive is performance to omitting the resampling step? Could you add an ablation?
3. In bilinear problems, how do you recommend choosing between factored vs. individual splits when memory is constrained? Any hybrid schemes?
4. Could you include results against SPDC or PDHG (Chambolle–Pock) on instances where those methods apply, to contextualize gains beyond batch FB?
5. Does your acceleration scheme extend straightforwardly to SAGA, and are there caveats in practice?

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Clarify the suspected typo about whether splits need to be convex–concave.
- Define µ (the strong monotonicity) explicitly where it enters Theorem 2.
- In Section 2.3, please state the constant hidden in O(1/t) and whether it depends on initialization.
- Provide guidance on computing non-uniform probabilities π in general smooth K beyond bilinear cases.
- Release code or pseudocode for the accelerated SVRG variant (Algorithm 3) and sampling implementations.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances scalable optimization for non-separable saddle-point problems, with solid theory and promising empirical evidence. With minor clarifications and broader comparisons, it would be an excellent addition to the conference.
