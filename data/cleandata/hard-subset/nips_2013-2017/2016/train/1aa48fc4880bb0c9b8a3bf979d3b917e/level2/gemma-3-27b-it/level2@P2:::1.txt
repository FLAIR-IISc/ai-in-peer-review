This paper presents extensions of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, common in machine learning. The core idea is to adapt these algorithms, which are successful for convex minimization, to the saddle-point setting. The authors highlight the challenges and opportunities that arise, including the need for a different convergence analysis based on monotone operators, the importance of non-uniform sampling, and the ability to accelerate the algorithms using a catalyst-like framework.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the relevance of saddle-point problems in machine learning and the limitations of existing methods. The contributions are significant: providing the first linear convergence analysis for these algorithms in the saddle-point setting, identifying key properties of saddle-point formulations (splits, convex-concave terms, sampling), and demonstrating acceleration via a catalyst-like approach.  The authors thoroughly compare their work to related algorithms, including batch methods and existing stochastic approaches.  The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly highlighting the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous, utilizing the monotone operator framework appropriately. The extension of SVRG and SAGA is logically sound. The proof sketches mentioned in the appendix are reassuring.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm descriptions are easy to follow. The organization is logical, progressing from problem formulation to algorithm design, analysis, and experimentation.
* **Originality:** Extending SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems *and* providing a rigorous convergence analysis is a novel contribution.  The observation that non-uniform sampling is crucial in this context, and the application of the catalyst framework, further contribute to the originality.  The extension to monotone operators adds significant theoretical breadth.
* **Significance:** The results are important because saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning, and efficient algorithms for solving them are in high demand. The linear convergence guarantees are a major advance, and the practical improvements demonstrated in the experiments suggest that these algorithms will be useful for real-world applications. The broad applicability beyond saddle-points, to variational inequalities, increases the significance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Assumptions:** Assumptions (A)-(C) are stated relatively abstractly.  While mathematically precise, they might be difficult for some readers to immediately grasp in the context of specific applications. Providing more concrete examples illustrating how these assumptions hold in common machine learning scenarios would be helpful.
* **Mini-batch Size Dependence:** The analysis emphasizes the importance of the mini-batch size `m` for the convergence rate, particularly regarding the prox operator computation. The practical implications of choosing `m` and its impact on performance aren’t fully explored in the experiments. A discussion of the trade-offs involved would be beneficial.
* **Catalyst Parameter Tuning:** The explanation of the catalyst acceleration section is somewhat concise. More details on how the parameter `τ` is chosen in practice and its sensitivity to different problem settings could improve understanding.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-designed and demonstrate improvements, they are limited to two datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a broader range of problems, including those with different characteristics (e.g., varying degrees of non-separability, different dimensions) would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and technically sound solution.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable theoretical guarantees.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
* The extension to monotone operators expands the applicability of the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions are somewhat abstract and could benefit from more concrete explanations.
* The practical considerations related to mini-batch size and parameter tuning are not fully explored.
* A broader experimental evaluation would strengthen the conclusions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed with further clarification and experimentation. The strengths—particularly the theoretical rigor and practical relevance—outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above. Specifically, clarifying the assumptions, discussing the practical trade-offs of mini-batch size, and expanding the experimental evaluation would further enhance the paper's impact.
