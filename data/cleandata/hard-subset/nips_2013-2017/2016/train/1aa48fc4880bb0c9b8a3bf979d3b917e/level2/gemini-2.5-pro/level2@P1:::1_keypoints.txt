- Proposes first linearly convergent, large-scale stochastic algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum smooth structure, extending SVRG and SAGA.  
- Main novelty is a convergence analysis based on monotone operator theory rather than co-coercivity, enabling linear rates for saddle-point problems and variational inequalities.  
- Investigates different problem structures (e.g., "factored" splits) and sampling schemes, showing importance of non-uniform sampling for theory and practice.  
- Applies the Catalyst acceleration framework to achieve improved rates, with an accelerated SVRG variant shown superior to accelerated batch methods.  
- Theoretical claims (linear convergence for SVRG/SAGA and improved accelerated rates) are technically sound and supported by proofs.  
- Experimental evaluation is strong, demonstrating effectiveness on non-separable losses/regularizers and fair comparisons to relevant baselines.  
- Paper is very well-written, organized, and clearly motivates the problem; Table 1 effectively summarizes algorithmic complexities.  
- Originality is high: first extension of variance-reduced methods to general composite saddle-point setting with novel analysis and insights.  
- Significance is high: provides scalable, fast-converging algorithms for widely used saddle-point formulations in ML (e.g., TV, overlapping group lasso, AUC).  
- Strength: introduces the first linearly convergent incremental methods for a broad class of saddle-point problems.  
- Strength: monotone-operator-based analysis is elegant, appropriate, and more general than convex-minimization analyses.  
- Strength: provides practical insights on sampling and splitting strategies, backed by theory and experiments.  
- Strength: accelerated SVRG yields state-of-the-art theoretical and empirical performance.  
- Weakness: acceleration analysis is given only for SVRG; claim that it extends to SAGA is unproven and should be either sketched or stated more cautiously.  
- Weakness: non-uniform SAGA requires a theoretical resampling step, but the paper uses a mixed sampling scheme in practice without commenting on its theoretical impact.  
- Weakness: discussion of "factored" splits could better connect to practical considerations like SAGA memory cost (O(n+d) vs. O(nd)).
