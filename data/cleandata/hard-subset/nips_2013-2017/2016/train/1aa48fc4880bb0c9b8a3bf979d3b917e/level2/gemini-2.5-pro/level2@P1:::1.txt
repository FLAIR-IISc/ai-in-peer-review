This paper proposes the first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for convex-concave saddle-point problems where the smooth part of the objective has a finite-sum structure. The authors extend the popular stochastic variance reduction methods SVRG and SAGA to this setting. While the algorithmic extensions are straightforward, the main contributions are a novel convergence analysis based on the theory of monotone operators, an investigation of different problem structures (e.g., "factored" splits) and sampling schemes, and the application of the Catalyst framework for acceleration. This work bridges an important gap, as previous linearly convergent methods were limited to either batch settings or the simpler convex minimization setting.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The key theoretical contribution is the convergence analysis. The authors correctly recognize that standard analyses for SVRG/SAGA in the convex minimization setting, which rely on co-coercivity, do not apply here. Instead, they provide a more general analysis based on monotonicity, which is the correct framework for saddle-point problems and also extends the applicability of their results to other problems like variational inequalities. The theoretical claims, including the linear convergence rates for SVRG and SAGA and the improved rates for the accelerated version, appear correct and are well-supported by the proofs in the appendix. The experimental evaluation is also of high quality, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods on problems with non-separable losses and regularizers, which is the primary motivation for this work. The comparisons to relevant baselines are fair and informative.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and summarizes the paper's contributions. The problem formulation and assumptions are stated precisely. The authors do a good job of situating their work relative to existing batch and stochastic algorithms for saddle-point problems. Table 1, which summarizes the complexities of all considered algorithms, is particularly helpful for understanding the theoretical improvements offered by the proposed methods. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is a nice touch that clarifies the generality of the theoretical analysis.

**Originality:**
The paper's originality is high. To my knowledge, this is the first work to successfully extend variance-reduced stochastic methods like SVRG and SAGA to the general composite saddle-point setting and prove their linear convergence. While the algorithms themselves are adaptations of existing ideas, the analysis is novel and significant. Furthermore, the paper provides original insights specific to the saddle-point structure, such as the distinction between function splits and "factored" splits of the operator, and the critical role of non-uniform sampling in achieving the best theoretical rates and practical performance. The application of the Catalyst framework for acceleration in this context is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:**
The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact on both researchers and practitioners. Saddle-point formulations are becoming increasingly prevalent in machine learning for tackling problems with complex, non-separable regularizers (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso) or loss functions (e.g., AUC maximization, robust optimization). Existing algorithms for these problems were either batch methods, which are too slow for large datasets, or standard stochastic methods with sublinear convergence. This paper provides a new class of algorithms that are scalable and enjoy fast linear convergence, demonstrably advancing the state of the art. These ideas will likely be built upon and used in a wide range of applications.

**Strengths:**
*   **First Linearly Convergent Incremental Methods:** This work is the first to provide linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems.
*   **Novel and General Analysis:** The use of monotone operator theory for the convergence proof is elegant, appropriate, and more general than the standard analysis for convex minimization, extending the results to a wider class of problems.
*   **Practical Insights:** The paper provides valuable insights into the importance of non-uniform sampling and different splitting strategies, which are backed by both theory and experiments.
*   **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The accelerated SVRG variant is shown to be theoretically superior to accelerated batch methods and demonstrates excellent empirical performance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The analysis for the accelerated method is provided for SVRG, with the authors stating that "the same scheme should work for SAGA." While this is plausible, it remains an unproven claim. It would strengthen the paper to either provide a proof sketch for SAGA in the appendix or to state this more cautiously as a direction for future work.
*   The paper mentions that for non-uniform SAGA, a resampling step is technically required for the theory to hold, but a mixed sampling scheme is used in practice. This is a common practical choice, but a brief comment on why this deviation from the theory is acceptable or what its potential impact might be would be helpful for the reader.
*   The discussion on "factored" splits is interesting. It could be slightly improved by more explicitly connecting the choice of split to practical considerations like the memory required for SAGA (O(n+d) for factored vs. O(nd) for individual splits), as this is a key differentiator.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that addresses a significant problem with a novel and technically sound approach. The contributions are substantial, and the paper is well-written and convincing. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
