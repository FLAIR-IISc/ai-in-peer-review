- Proposes first linearly convergent incremental gradient algorithms (SVRG and SAGA extensions) for convex-concave saddle-point problems with finite-sum smooth term K.
- Uses monotone operator theory for a novel convergence analysis, extending applicability beyond standard convex minimization (e.g., to variational inequalities).
- Explores practical algorithmic choices specific to the saddle-point setting, including "factored" gradient splits and the importance of non-uniform sampling.
- Applies the Catalyst acceleration framework to the methods, producing an accelerated algorithm provably superior to accelerated batch methods.
- Significance/originality: Tackles a relevant, previously unaddressed problem class (non-separable losses/regularizers) and is the first to prove linear convergence for variance-reduced methods on saddle-points.
- Quality: Theoretical analysis is rigorous with clear convergence theorems and complexity bounds; experiments support theoretical claims and show strong empirical performance, especially on ill-conditioned problems.
- Clarity: Well-written and organized; problem setup, assumptions, and the flow from batch to stochastic to accelerated methods are clearly presented; monotone-operator connection explained well.
- Weakness — hyperparameter practicality: Algorithms and rates depend on problem constants (L, L̄) that are hard to estimate in practice and may affect performance.
- Weakness — acceleration clarity: Section on Catalyst-based acceleration is high-level and assumes reader familiarity; more intuition on outer/inner loop interactions would help.
- Weakness — SAGA storage cost: Paper should more explicitly state that element-wise SAGA splits require O(nd) storage, which is impractical compared to factored O(n+d) splits.
- Constructive: Evaluate whether non-separable formulations yield empirical benefits (e.g., test accuracy) over separable counterparts to further motivate the approach.
- Constructive: Clarify the two "optional" blocks in Algorithm 2 (SAGA) to distinguish resampling mechanics from core updates and avoid confusion.
- Overall: Technically deep, original, and practically validated work that substantially advances variance-reduction for saddle-point problems; strong accept recommendation.
