This paper addresses the problem of solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, a common occurrence in machine learning, with a focus on leveraging separable structures. The authors extend stochastic variance reduction (SVRG and SAGA) methods to this setting, providing convergence guarantees and demonstrating practical improvements through experiments. The work is well-motivated, and the connection to monotone operator theory is a valuable generalization.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical contributions, particularly the convergence analysis for SVRG and SAGA applied to saddle-point problems. Extending these algorithms to a broader class of problems via the monotone operator framework is a significant result. The identification of non-uniform sampling as crucial for efficiency is also a strong point, backed up by experimental evidence. The acceleration technique based on the “catalyst” framework further enhances the practical utility of the proposed algorithms.  The analysis of different splitting strategies (function vs. partial derivative) and the detailed discussion of the implications for bilinear saddle-point problems are commendable.  The experimental section provides a reasonable evaluation, comparing the proposed algorithms against established methods.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved.  The notation is occasionally dense and could benefit from greater clarity. For example, the use of multiple symbols to represent different norms (Ω, Ω*) feels somewhat cumbersome.  The presentation of the theoretical results, while thorough, is sometimes difficult to follow. More intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs would be helpful. 

A key weakness is the lack of a detailed comparison to other existing saddle-point solvers, particularly those explicitly designed for large-scale problems. While the authors mention some related work, a more comprehensive comparison—including computational complexity analysis—would strengthen the claims of significance.  The discussion of existing stochastic algorithms (Section 2.3) is brief. Providing more context on their limitations specifically concerning saddle-point problems would highlight the novelty of the proposed approach more effectively.

The experiments, while demonstrating improvements, are somewhat limited in scope.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and problem sizes would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.  A more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to hyperparameters, such as the regularization parameter λ, would also be beneficial. The claim that accelerated SVRG is "always superior to accelerated batch algorithms" (in the abstract) feels too strong, given the empirical results and the theoretical caveats; it would be better to qualify this statement.

Several minor points could also be addressed. In Section 2.4, the discussion of sampling probabilities feels a bit disconnected from the main flow. Clarifying the practical implications of different sampling strategies would be valuable.  The connection between the theoretical convergence rates and the observed empirical performance is not fully explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems with proven linear convergence.
*   Generalization to monotone operators, expanding the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   Demonstrated practical improvements through experiments.
*   Clear identification of non-uniform sampling as a key factor for efficiency.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and sometimes difficult-to-follow theoretical presentation.
*   Limited comparison to other state-of-the-art saddle-point solvers.
*   Somewhat limited experimental evaluation.
*   Overly strong claim of superiority of accelerated SVRG.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical results are solid, and the experimental evidence supports the claims of improved performance. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. This reviewer recommends acceptance, contingent upon addressing the suggested revisions, particularly regarding a more thorough comparison to existing saddle-point solvers and a more nuanced discussion of the experimental results. Specifically, expanding the related work section and including a complexity analysis comparing the proposed algorithms to alternative approaches would strengthen the paper considerably. A more careful phrasing of the claim regarding the superiority of the accelerated SVRG is also recommended.
