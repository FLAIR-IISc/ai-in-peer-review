This paper proposes and analyzes stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods, specifically SVRG and SAGA, for solving large-scale convex-concave saddle-point problems. This work extends the highly successful SVR framework, previously established for convex minimization, to a broader and important class of optimization problems common in machine learning. The authors provide the first linear convergence analysis for these methods in the saddle-point setting, leveraging the theory of monotone operators. Key contributions include a unified analysis that extends beyond saddle-point problems, an investigation into different data splitting and sampling strategies, and an effective acceleration scheme based on the catalyst framework.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature. While SVR methods have revolutionized large-scale convex optimization, their application to saddle-point problems was not established. This work provides the first linearly convergent, incremental algorithms for this problem class, representing a substantial advancement over existing batch or standard stochastic methods. The practical relevance is high, given the prevalence of saddle-point formulations in areas like robust optimization, duality-based learning, and structured prediction.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. By framing the problem through the lens of monotone operators, the authors provide a rigorous and generalizable convergence proof that is not limited to saddle-point problems and does not rely on the restrictive co-coercivity assumptions used in related work. This elegant approach enhances the paper's theoretical contribution.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It methodically introduces the problem, details the proposed algorithms, and presents the theoretical results. The experimental section provides compelling empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods, particularly accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling, outperform relevant baselines in practice.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   The primary weakness is the reliance on knowing the strong convexity-concavity constants (λ, γ) to set the step-sizes. While the authors correctly acknowledge this limitation as future work, the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the practical sensitivity to these parameters or potential heuristics for their estimation.
*   The acceleration via the catalyst framework, while effective, adds another layer of parameters. A more detailed practical guide or ablation study on setting τ would be beneficial for practitioners looking to implement the accelerated version.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to large-scale optimization. The combination of strong theory, practical relevance, and solid empirical validation makes it a clear candidate for acceptance. The work is likely to inspire further research into variance reduction techniques for more general operator splitting problems.
