This paper proposes the first linearly convergent, incremental gradient algorithms for a broad class of convex-concave saddle-point problems. Specifically, it extends the popular stochastic variance reduction methods SVRG and SAGA, originally designed for finite-sum convex minimization, to the saddle-point setting `min_x max_y K(x, y) + M(x, y)`, where K is smooth and has a finite-sum structure. This work is well-motivated by machine learning problems that involve non-separable losses or regularizers, which cannot be directly addressed by prior variance-reduced methods but fit naturally into the saddle-point formulation.

The core contributions are:
1.  The extension of SVRG and SAGA algorithms to the saddle-point problem.
2.  A novel convergence analysis based on the theory of monotone operators. This is a significant departure from the potential function-based analyses common for SVRG/SAGA in the convex setting and shows that the proposed algorithms apply to a wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities.
3.  An exploration of practical aspects unique to this setting, such as different ways to split the gradient operator ("factored" splits) and the critical role of non-uniform sampling for achieving state-of-the-art theoretical rates.
4.  The application of the Catalyst framework to accelerate the proposed methods, yielding an algorithm that is provably superior to accelerated batch methods.

This work builds upon the foundations of SVRG [2], SAGA [3], and Catalyst [7]. It significantly advances the state of the art by providing the first stochastic algorithms with linear convergence for this problem class, improving upon batch algorithms that cannot exploit the sum-structure [19, 20] and standard stochastic algorithms that only achieve sublinear rates [22].

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem. Many modern machine learning models, especially in structured prediction or with complex regularizers, lead to non-separable objective functions. By tackling the general saddle-point formulation, this work provides a powerful tool for a wide range of applications. The contributions are highly original; this is the first work to successfully analyze variance reduction methods for saddle-point problems and establish their linear convergence. The use of monotone operator theory for the analysis is particularly elegant and insightful, correctly identifying the core properties needed for convergence without relying on assumptions like co-coercivity which hold for convex minimization but not here.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and appears sound. The derivations lead to clear convergence rate theorems (Theorems 1, 2, and 3) and concrete complexity bounds, which are effectively summarized in Table 1. This table provides an excellent comparison between the proposed methods and existing alternatives, clearly highlighting the theoretical advantages of variance reduction and acceleration. The experimental results convincingly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods (especially accelerated SVRG with non-uniform sampling) outperform existing batch and stochastic baselines in practice, particularly on ill-conditioned problems.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and a concise summary of the contributions. The problem formulation and assumptions are stated precisely. The logical flow from batch to stochastic, then to variance-reduced and accelerated methods, is easy to follow. The connection to the more general framework of monotone operators is explained well in Section 6, which adds significant depth to the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The proposed algorithms and their theoretical rates depend on knowledge of problem-dependent constants like the Lipschitz constants L and L̄. In practice, these can be difficult to compute or estimate, and the performance can be sensitive to their values. A brief discussion on the practical challenges of setting these parameters, or potential avenues for making the algorithms adaptive, would strengthen the paper's practical impact.

*   **Clarity on Acceleration:** The description of the acceleration scheme in Section 5 is quite high-level and relies heavily on the reader's prior knowledge of the Catalyst framework. While space is a constraint, a little more intuition on how the outer loop updates `(x̄, ȳ)` via the proximal-point algorithm interact with the inner-loop SVRG solver would make the section more self-contained and accessible.

*   **SAGA Storage Cost:** The paper correctly notes the storage benefit of SAGA with "factored" splits (O(n+d)). However, it would be beneficial to explicitly state the potential drawback of using "individual" element-wise splits with SAGA, which would require O(nd) storage for the cached operator values, making it impractical for large problems. This would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs.

**Constructive Comments:**

*   The experiments are well-designed to showcase the non-separable setting. It would be interesting to see if these non-separable loss/regularizer formulations provide empirical benefits (e.g., in test accuracy) over their separable counterparts on these datasets, further motivating the need for the proposed specialized solvers.
*   In Algorithm 2 (SAGA), the purpose of the two "optional" blocks could be clarified. The first seems to relate to the resampling strategy needed for the non-uniform sampling theory, while the second block (updating G and g_ik) appears to be a core part of the algorithm. Rephrasing this could avoid confusion.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to large-scale optimization for machine learning. It bridges the gap between the highly successful variance-reduction techniques and the general class of saddle-point problems. The work is technically deep, presents novel analysis, and demonstrates significant practical performance gains. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
