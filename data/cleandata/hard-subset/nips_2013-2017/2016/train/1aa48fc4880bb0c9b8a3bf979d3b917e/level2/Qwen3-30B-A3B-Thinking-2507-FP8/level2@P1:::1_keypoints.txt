- Extends SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems and provides the first convergence analysis for these algorithms in that setting using monotone operator theory.  
- Identifies two distinct notions of splitting for saddle-point problems: splitting in terms of functions versus partial derivatives.  
- Demonstrates the importance and efficiency gains of non-uniform sampling.  
- Shows how to accelerate the proposed algorithms using the catalyst framework.  
- Well-motivated gap-filling work: variance reduction methods previously focused on convex minimization, not saddle-points common via duality.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, proving linear convergence rates (Theorems 1 and 2) versus typical sublinear stochastic rates.  
- Extension to monotone operators broadens applicability beyond saddle-point problems (connection to variational inequalities).  
- Experiments are well-designed and support theoretical claims, showing clear advantages on non-separable losses and regularizers.  
- Empirical comparison with primal methods on a separable dataset (rcv1) highlights the necessity of the saddle-point approach for non-separable settings.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with consistent notation, helpful pseudocode, and reproducibility details (e.g., sampling probabilities).  
- Suggestion: provide more intuition and a brief comparison of proof techniques explaining why monotone operator theory is necessary.  
- Suggestion: discuss how to estimate strong convexity–concavity constants in practice or whether adaptive schemes could be used.  
- Suggestion: include experiments comparing catalyst applied directly to the dual to better isolate benefits of the proposed approach.  
- Strengths summarized: novel convergence analysis, clear conceptual distinction from convex minimization, rigorous theory, strong experiments, and broad mathematical connections.  
- Weaknesses summarized: limited intuition on monotone operator necessity, sparse guidance on estimating constants, and experiments could cover more diverse settings.  
- Recommendation: accept — significant, theoretically sound contribution suitable for NIPS.
