

This paper proposes the first linearly convergent stochastic variance reduction algorithms for saddle-point problems, extending SVRG and SAGA to this class of problems. The authors provide a novel convergence analysis using monotone operators rather than the convex minimization framework, which is crucial since the standard analysis does not apply. They identify key differences between saddle-point and convex minimization problems, including two distinct notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives), the necessity of convex-concave splits, and the importance of non-uniform sampling. The paper also demonstrates how the "catalyst" framework can accelerate their algorithms, leading to methods that outperform accelerated batch algorithms.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear convergence guarantees for both SVRG and SAGA variants. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of different sampling strategies (uniform vs. non-uniform) and demonstrate theoretically and empirically that non-uniform sampling provides superior performance, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The extension of the catalyst framework to saddle-point problems is particularly noteworthy, as it offers a clean way to accelerate the algorithms without requiring complex modifications. The experimental section is well-designed, showing clear advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches on two real-world datasets with non-separable losses and regularizers. The paper's connection to machine learning applications (e.g., robust optimization, structured prediction, and non-separable regularizers) is well-motivated and demonstrates practical relevance.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the requirement to know strong convexity-concavity constants (λ and γ) is a significant limitation, as these constants are often unknown in practice. While the authors mention this will be addressed in future work, it's a notable gap in the current contribution, especially since adaptive methods for convex optimization have already been developed (e.g., [3]). Second, the experimental section, while adequate, is somewhat limited. Only two datasets are used, and the comparison with primal methods on the separable rcv1 dataset is somewhat tangential to the main contribution. More extensive experiments on a wider range of problems would strengthen the empirical claims. Third, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of computing the non-uniform sampling probabilities, which could be prohibitive for large-scale problems. While the paper notes that for bilinear problems these can be computed efficiently, it doesn't discuss the general case.

The paper's placement in the literature is generally good, but it could be improved by more thoroughly discussing prior work on saddle-point problems. While the authors reference some key papers (e.g., [4, 5, 6]), they don't sufficiently compare their approach to existing stochastic methods for saddle-point problems, such as the stochastic forward-backward algorithms mentioned in Section 2.3. A more detailed comparison would help clarify the novelty of their contribution.

The significance of the paper is high. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous in machine learning (e.g., in duality, robust optimization, and structured prediction), and the authors provide the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems. The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, particularly for non-separable problems where traditional stochastic methods cannot be applied. The extension of variance reduction to saddle-point problems opens up new possibilities for solving large-scale machine learning problems that were previously intractable with existing methods.

In conclusion, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending stochastic variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems with rigorous theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The main weaknesses are the dependence on strong convexity-concavity constants and the somewhat limited experimental evaluation. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is both novel and practically significant. The paper would benefit from more thorough experimental comparisons, a deeper discussion of the computational cost of non-uniform sampling, and a clearer articulation of the connection to existing saddle-point optimization literature. Despite these limitations, the paper presents a compelling and well-executed extension of variance reduction methods to a broader class of problems, with clear implications for large-scale machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
