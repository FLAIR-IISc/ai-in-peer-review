- Extends SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave composite saddle-point problems, yielding first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for this class.
- Uses monotone-operator/forward–backward analysis instead of convex minimization, enabling application to variational inequalities.
- Proposes two splitting strategies for the smooth part K (by functions or partial derivatives) and shows non-uniform sampling improves theory and practice.
- Adds a proximal-point/Catalyst-style acceleration that yields complexities dominating accelerated batch methods.
- Experiments on non-separable losses/regularizers show clear empirical gains, especially for ill-conditioned problems and with non-uniform sampling.
- Fills a clear gap: provides linearly convergent stochastic methods for general saddle-point problems.
- Presents a solid monotone-operator-based analysis with linear rates and careful treatment of sampling and mini-batching.
- Offers practical guidance on factored vs. element-wise splits and gives interpretable complexity bounds (‖K‖F, ‖K‖op).
- Acceleration approach is simple, elegant, and implementable.
- Empirical results align well with theoretical predictions; non-uniform sampling and acceleration are impactful.
- Relies on strong convexity–concavity assumptions; adaptivity/robustness to misspecified λ, γ is not addressed.
- Experimental scope is limited (two datasets, few structured tasks) and omits PDHG/Mirror-Prox and recent primal–dual coordinate baselines.
- Lacks prescriptive parameterization details (estimating L, L̄; choosing τ; cost of prox M).
- SAGA storage/time trade-offs for individual splits (O(nd)) limit practicality on large sparse matrices.
- Provides limited ablations on split choices and mini-batch sizing.
- Suggests adding adaptive step-size/regularization (linesearch or diagonal scaling) to reduce reliance on exact constants.
- Recommends broader experiments (structured prediction, robust optimization) and inclusion of PDHG/Mirror-Prox baselines.
- Requests open-source code and concrete guidelines for split selection and sampling probabilities.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled extension with linear rates, clean acceleration, broad applicability to monotone operators and variational inequalities, and strong theory–experiment alignment.
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy assumption burden and limited empirical breadth; practical guidance on parameters and memory not fully resolved.
