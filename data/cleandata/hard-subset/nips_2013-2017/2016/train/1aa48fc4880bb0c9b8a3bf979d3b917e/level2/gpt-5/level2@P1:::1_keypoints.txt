- Problem: solves composite convex–concave saddle-point problems min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) with M strongly convex–concave and proximable, K smooth.
- Main contribution: extends SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle-point problems, yielding the first incremental, linearly convergent algorithms in this setting.
- Analysis technique: uses a monotone-operator viewpoint (zeros of A+B) instead of cocoercivity-based convex-minimization arguments.
- Operator split: formalizes two splits of B — (i) sum of components Bi and (ii) a factored x/y-block split for storage/sampling benefits.
- Sampling: advocates non-uniform sampling and introduces a sampling-dependent smoothness constant L̄(π) with corresponding bounds.
- Acceleration: proposes a simple proximal-point/catalyst acceleration that improves condition-number dependence and outperforms accelerated batch methods.
- Generalization: analysis extends to general monotone inclusions and variational inequalities beyond saddle points.
- Relation to prior work: connects to SAG/SVRG/SAGA, stochastic primal–dual methods, and catalyst; addresses a gap where prior variance-reduction required separability.
- Theoretical quality: SVRG and SAGA analyses are technically sound with explicit step sizes and linear rates; monotone framework clarifies convergence without cocoercivity.
- Sampling-specific bounds: provides well-motivated complexity bounds for bilinear K using norms (‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max) and handles factored vs individual split trade-offs.
- Empirical findings: experiments on two datasets show clear benefits from variance reduction, acceleration, and non-uniform sampling; evaluation uses distance-to-optimum matching theory.
- Empirical limitations: experiments are limited to two datasets and lack comprehensive, apples-to-apples comparisons with strong stochastic primal–dual baselines (e.g., SPDC, PDHG).
- Clarity: paper is dense but well-organized; statements, algorithms, and rates are precise, though a likely typo and a brief practical recipe would help readability.
- Originality: high — novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to non-separable saddle problems, non-uniform sampling analysis, and catalyst adaptation in monotone setting.
- Significance: high — addresses many ML problems with non-separable saddle formulations and provides scalable, linearly convergent methods.
- Pros summary: first linear-rate incremental methods for general saddle points; monotone-operator analysis; practical sampling/splitting guidance; simple catalyst acceleration; supportive experiments.
- Cons summary: requires known strong-convexity constants and proximable M; L and L̄(π) can be hard to compute; SAGA non-uniform sampling requires resampling (experiments use heuristics); limited empirical breadth and baseline comparisons.
- Key questions for authors: practical step-size heuristics without L/L̄(π); sensitivity of SAGA to skipping resampling; guidance for choosing factored vs individual splits under memory limits; comparisons to SPDC/PDHG; whether acceleration extends to SAGA.
- Minor requests: fix the split-phrasing typo, explicitly define µ in theorems, state constants in O(1/t), give guidance for computing π beyond bilinear cases, and release code/pseudocode for accelerated SVRG and sampling. 
- Overall assessment: strong, timely contribution with solid theory and promising empirical evidence; with minor clarifications and broader comparisons it is an excellent paper.
