- Introduces SVRG/SAGA extensions to saddle-point problems, providing first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale settings.  
- Uses monotone operator theory for a general convergence analysis covering variational inequalities.  
- Identifies important distinctions in how to split the problem (function vs. partial derivative).  
- Highlights the theoretical and empirical importance of non-uniform sampling.  
- Demonstrates acceleration via a catalyst framework with practical speedups.  
- Well-motivated: extending convex minimization methods to saddle-point problems is timely and relevant.  
- Theoretical results are rigorous and grounded in monotone operator theory.  
- Experimental results support the theoretical claims and show practical relevance.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a concise introduction of contributions.  
- Addresses a wide range of considerations (splits, sampling, acceleration) thoroughly.  
- Notation is dense and sometimes confusing due to repeated subscripts/superscripts.  
- High mathematical sophistication required; more intuitive explanations would improve accessibility.  
- Experimental details are insufficient: need clearer dataset descriptions, hyperparameter tuning, and baseline comparisons.  
- Paper’s claim about adaptivity to constants is optimistic but lacks concrete methods or results.  
- Connections to other stochastic/primal-dual saddle-point methods could be discussed more deeply.  
- Specific suggestions: motivate Eq. (1) for broader readers and discuss implications of Assumptions (A)-(C).  
- Recommend visual examples for different split notions to improve clarity.  
- Suggest comparing to more recent stochastic optimization algorithms in experiments.  
- Table 1 should include practical implications of the listed complexities.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and impactful work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions on notation, accessibility, and experimental detail.
