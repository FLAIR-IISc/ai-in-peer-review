

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The authors correctly identify that the convex minimization analysis does not apply to saddle-point problems and instead use monotone operator theory for convergence proofs, demonstrating that their algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. The paper's key claims are well-supported through rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates, complexity bounds, and detailed discussion of two distinct notions of splits (functional vs. derivative-based) with non-uniform sampling identified as critical for efficiency.

The paper's strengths include a thorough theoretical foundation, clear exposition of the connections to existing work in convex optimization, and compelling experimental validation on machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers. The experiments demonstrate significant practical advantages over batch methods and uniform sampling variants, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The authors also successfully extend their framework to acceleration via the "catalyst" approach, yielding algorithms superior to accelerated batch methods.

While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it has a notable limitation: it assumes knowledge of strong convexity-concavity constants, which may not be readily available in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion but do not explore adaptivity to these constants, a direction that would strengthen the practical applicability. Additionally, while the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, the experimental section could benefit from more detailed comparisons with specialized saddle-point solvers from the literature.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by bridging the gap between stochastic optimization for convex minimization and saddle-point problems. The theoretical insights are novel and well-justified, and the practical implications for machine learning applications are clear. The authors have addressed all key aspects of the problem with appropriate depth, and the work represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. The paper should be accepted for publication at NIPS as it provides both theoretical novelty and practical value to the community.
