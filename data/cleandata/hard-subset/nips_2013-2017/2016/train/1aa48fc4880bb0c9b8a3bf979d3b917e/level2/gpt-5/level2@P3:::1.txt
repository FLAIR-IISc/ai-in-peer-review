Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex–concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y), where K is smooth convex–concave and M is (λ,γ)-strongly convex–concave with an efficient proximal operator. The central contribution is to extend variance-reduced incremental methods (SVRG, SAGA) from finite-sum minimization to this broader saddle-point class, obtaining linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity. The analysis is not a straightforward port of convex minimization techniques: it leverages monotone operator theory, treating saddle-point problems (and more generally variational inequalities) as finding zeros of a sum of a strongly monotone operator and a finite sum of Lipschitz operators.

Algorithmically, the work:
- Extends SVRG/SAGA to saddle points, including mini-batching and two meaningful ways to split the smooth operator B = (∂xK, −∂yK): (i) by individual functions/partials, and (ii) “factored” splits aligned with the primal/dual structure.
- Analyzes non-uniform sampling schemes and shows they materially improve iteration complexity bounds, with explicit constants for bilinear K via ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max.
- Proposes a simple acceleration via a catalyst-like proximal-point outer loop, yielding complexities that are always at least as good as accelerated batch methods and often better.

Prior stochastic primal–dual or coordinate methods [11,12] achieve linear rates primarily in separable settings; variance-reduced methods [1–3,24] target minimization. The monotone-operator perspective [8,27] and co-coercivity-based analyses [6,30] do not directly cover general non-separable saddle points with the proposed step sizes. This work appears to provide the first linearly convergent incremental methods for general (non-separable) convex–concave saddle points with practical sampling and acceleration.

Quality:
The theoretical development is technically sound and carefully distinguishes conditions needed beyond convex minimization. Linear rates are established for both SVRG and SAGA under strong convexity–concavity, with explicit dependence on L and the sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π). The treatment of factored vs individual splits, and the role of non-uniform sampling, is clear and supported by tight norm inequalities for bilinear K. The catalyst-style acceleration is well-motivated via proximal-point contraction and integrates cleanly with SVRG. Experiments on classification with non-separable loss/regularizer and on a separable baseline support the claims: non-uniform sampling matters; accelerated SVRG dominates on ill-conditioned problems; primal methods that ignore separability are inferior when separability is absent.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, with concrete examples, complexity tables, and explicit pseudocode. The weighted norms Ω and Ω* help keep constants precise, though collecting all definitions and assumptions (A–C) in a single early “Problem setup” box would improve readability. Practical guidance on selecting probabilities, mini-batch size, and how to estimate L, L̄ would benefit practitioners. The distinction between “factored” and “individual” splits is important and is explained well.

Originality:
- Novel extension of SVRG/SAGA to general saddle points (and more broadly to monotone inclusions) without co-coercivity, with linear convergence.
- New analysis of non-uniform sampling and factored splits tailored to saddle-point structure.
- Simple and broadly applicable acceleration via a proximal-point outer loop, with rates that improve over accelerated batch where variance reduction is applicable.

Significance:
Convex–concave saddle-point problems with non-separable losses or regularizers are common in ML (AUC surrogates, structured losses, group/TV/submodular regularization, robust optimization). A linearly convergent, large-scale incremental family that handles non-separable structure fills a notable gap between batch primal–dual methods and separability-dependent stochastic methods. The monotone-operator view broadens impact beyond saddle points.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumption of known strong convexity–concavity parameters (λ, γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄). Practical guidance or adaptive strategies (e.g., backtracking, regularization continuation, heuristics akin to [3]) would strengthen applicability. Essential: at least a brief section outlining how to estimate or safely upper bound these constants and the sensitivity of performance to misspecification.
- SAGA with non-uniform sampling requires a resampling step to maintain the theoretical guarantee. The paper notes a practical workaround; more discussion of the empirical impact of skipping resampling and a small ablation would be useful. Optional: include results comparing “with” vs “without” resampling.
- Experimental scope is limited (two datasets, primarily bilinear K). Including an example where K is smooth but not bilinear (e.g., kernelized or generalized linear saddle formulation) would better validate the general theory. Essential if feasible: one additional non-bilinear experiment.
- Baselines: Mirror-Prox / extragradient methods [20] (batch) and SPDC-like stochastic primal–dual methods [12] are relevant points of comparison; while separability assumptions differ, a brief discussion or, where applicable, empirical comparison would help contextualize gains. Optional: add Mirror-Prox as a batch baseline.
- Memory vs. time trade-offs for SAGA under individual vs factored splits could be quantified more explicitly. Optional: a small study showing wall-clock vs memory footprint for SAGA and SVRG.
- Non-uniform sampling probabilities rely on row/column norms or element magnitudes; discuss the one-time cost to compute these statistics and how to handle streaming or dynamically changing data. Optional: note that probabilities can be approximated or updated periodically at negligible amortized cost.

Pros:
- First linear-rate incremental methods for non-separable convex–concave saddle points.
- Clean monotone-operator analysis, extending applicability beyond saddle points.
- Strong practical insights: factored vs individual splits and non-uniform sampling.
- Simple, effective acceleration via proximal-point/catalyst, with improved complexities.
- Solid empirical validation on meaningful non-separable problems.

Cons:
- Requires knowledge of curvature constants; limited guidance on adaptive tuning.
- Experiments relatively narrow in scope; missing some standard primal–dual baselines.
- SAGA non-uniform sampling requires resampling for theory; practical impact not fully explored.

Recommendation:
Accept. The work makes a substantive and well-supported contribution by bridging variance reduction with general saddle-point problems under a rigorous monotone-operator framework, offering both theoretical advances and practically effective algorithms. Expanding empirical scope and adding guidance on parameter selection would further strengthen the paper.
