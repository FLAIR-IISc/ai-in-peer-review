This paper extends the stochastic variance reduction gradient methods SVRG and SAGA, originally developed for convex minimization of finite sums, to the broader class of convex-concave saddle-point problems. This extension provides the first linearly convergent, large-scale algorithms for structured saddle-point problems common in machine learning, such as those with non-separable losses or regularizers.

The core contributions are threefold. First, the paper provides a novel convergence analysis. Since the standard analysis for convex minimization does not apply, the proofs are developed within the more general framework of monotone operators. This not only establishes linear convergence for the proposed algorithms but also shows their applicability to an even wider class of problems, such as variational inequalities. Second, the work investigates practical and theoretical aspects unique to the saddle-point structure, including different ways to decompose the gradient operator ("factored splits") and the crucial role of non-uniform sampling in achieving superior performance. Third, the paper shows how these new incremental algorithms can be accelerated using an extension of the "Catalyst" framework, leading to a method with theoretical guarantees that are always better than accelerated full-gradient methods. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on binary classification problems with non-separable objectives.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The extension of SVRG/SAGA to this setting is a natural step, but the accompanying theoretical analysis is non-trivial and represents a solid contribution. The choice to use the theory of monotone operators is appropriate and powerful, as it correctly identifies the essential mathematical structure of the problem without relying on stronger assumptions like co-coercivity, which hold for convex minimization but not for general saddle-point problems. The claims of linear convergence and the benefits of acceleration and non-uniform sampling are well-supported by the theoretical analysis provided in the appendices. The experimental results, while focused on specific problems, effectively demonstrate the practical advantage of the proposed methods over existing batch and stochastic baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, and the problem setting and assumptions are formally defined in Section 2. The algorithms are presented in a clean, easy-to-understand format. Table 1 is particularly effective, providing a concise summary of the complexity results and clearly illustrating the theoretical improvements of the proposed methods over prior art. The connection to the more general framework of monotone operators in Section 6 is a valuable addition that helps place the contribution in a broader context.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon the well-known SVRG and SAGA algorithms, it is the first to successfully apply them with a rigorous proof of linear convergence to the general class of composite, decomposable saddle-point problems. The analysis via monotone operators distinguishes this work from previous extensions of variance reduction methods that were restricted to the co-coercive case (i.e., convex minimization). The paper also clearly situates its contributions with respect to existing batch and sub-linearly convergent stochastic methods for saddle-point problems.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to have a substantial impact. Many important problems in machine learning, such as AUC maximization, robust optimization, and training models with structured non-separable penalties (e.g., total variation, overlapping group lasso), can be formulated as saddle-point problems. Before this work, these problems could not benefit from the linear convergence rates of modern variance-reduced stochastic methods. This paper closes that gap, providing practitioners and researchers with a new class of efficient, scalable optimization algorithms. The provided analysis and acceleration technique are general and could inspire further research in this area.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, a few minor revisions could enhance it further.

*   The practical implications of the different "factored" vs. "individual" splits for SAGA could be discussed in more detail, particularly concerning the memory-computation trade-off. Section 4 touches upon this but could be slightly expanded to offer more concrete guidance.
*   The discussion of the Catalyst acceleration framework could benefit from a brief comment on the practical selection of the parameter `τ`. While the optimal theoretical value is given, some heuristic or adaptive guidance would be valuable for practitioners.
*   The experimental section is effective but concise. To improve reproducibility and self-containment, a few more details about the specific AUC surrogate and clustering-inducing regularizer could be moved from the appendix into the main text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad and important class of saddle-point problems.
*   Features a strong, novel, and general theoretical analysis based on monotone operator theory.
*   The contribution is comprehensive, covering SVRG, SAGA, non-uniform sampling, and an acceleration scheme.
*   The work addresses a clear and significant gap in the optimization toolkit for machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The suggestions for improvement are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. This reviewer finds no compelling arguments against acceptance.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem.
