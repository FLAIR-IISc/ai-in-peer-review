Summary: The paper extends stochastic variance-reduced methods (SVRG, SAGA) to convex–concave saddle-point problems with composite structure, providing the first linearly convergent incremental algorithms for this class. The key insight is to analyze the forward–backward scheme via monotone operator theory rather than convex minimization, enabling broader applicability (e.g., variational inequalities). The authors discuss two ways to “split” the smooth part K—by functions or by partial derivatives—and show that non-uniform sampling improves both theory and practice. An acceleration via a proximal-point/catalyst wrapper yields complexities that dominate accelerated batch methods. Experiments on binary classification with non-separable losses/regularizers demonstrate clear gains, especially for ill-conditioned problems and with non-uniform sampling. The work builds on and goes beyond prior NIPS contributions on SAG/SVRG/SAGA and Catalyst, and relates to primal–dual/PDHG and Chambolle–Pock methods while addressing non-separable settings.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a gap: linearly convergent stochastic methods for general saddle-point problems.
- Solid, monotone-operator-based analysis with linear rates; careful treatment of sampling and mini-batching.
- Practical guidance on factored vs. element-wise splits and non-uniform sampling; complexity bounds are interpretable (‖K‖F, ‖K‖op).
- Acceleration via a simple proximal-point outer loop; elegant and implementable.
- Empirical results align with theory; non-uniform sampling and acceleration matter.

Weaknesses:
- Strong convexity–concavity is assumed; adaptivity or robustness to misspecified λ, γ is deferred.
- Experimental scope is limited (two datasets, few structured tasks); comparisons omit Mirror-Prox/PDHG-style baselines and recent primal–dual coordinate methods beyond separable cases.
- Parameterization details (estimating L, L̄; choosing τ; cost of prox M) could be more prescriptive.
- Storage/time trade-offs of SAGA for individual splits (O(nd)) limit practicality on large sparse matrices.
- Limited ablations on splitting choices and mini-batch sizing.

Suggestions:
- Add adaptive step-size/regularization (e.g., linesearch or diagonal scaling) to avoid reliance on exact constants.
- Broaden experiments (structured prediction, robust optimization) and include PDHG/Mirror-Prox baselines.
- Provide open-source code and guidelines for split selection and sampling probabilities.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled extension with linear rates and a clean acceleration.
- General applicability (monotone operators, variational inequalities).
- Strong theoretical-experimental alignment.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Assumption burden and limited empirical breadth.
- Practical guidance on parameters and memory not fully resolved.
