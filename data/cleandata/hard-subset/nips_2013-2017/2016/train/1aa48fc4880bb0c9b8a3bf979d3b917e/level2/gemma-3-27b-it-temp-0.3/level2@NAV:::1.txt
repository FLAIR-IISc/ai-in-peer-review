## Review of “Stochastic Variance Reduction Methods for Saddle-Point Problems”

This paper tackles the important problem of scaling up algorithms for solving convex-concave saddle-point problems, common in machine learning, by extending Stochastic Variance Reduction (SVR) methods like SVRG and SAGA. The core idea – adapting these methods to the saddle-point setting – is sound and potentially impactful, given the limitations of existing approaches for large-scale problems. The authors correctly identify the challenges stemming from the differences between convex minimization and saddle-point analysis, particularly the need to leverage monotone operator theory.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical contributions. Extending the convergence analysis of SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems is a significant achievement, and the use of monotone operator theory provides a more general framework. The identification of the importance of non-uniform sampling and the acceleration via a catalyst-like framework are also valuable additions. The authors clearly articulate the nuances of splitting the objective function in saddle-point problems (function vs. partial derivative splits) and the necessity of convex-concave terms. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling experimental validation. While experiments are presented, they are limited in scope and the comparisons to other methods (particularly primal methods) are not always clear or convincing. The experimental setup, while described, could benefit from more detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and dataset specifics. The claim of superiority over accelerated batch algorithms is supported, but the magnitude of the improvement isn't always dramatic and could be better quantified. 

Furthermore, the paper’s clarity could be improved. The notation is dense, and the connections between the theoretical analysis and the algorithmic descriptions are sometimes difficult to follow. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more thorough discussion of the practical challenges of implementing these algorithms (e.g., tuning the step sizes, handling ill-conditioning) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical extension of SVR methods to saddle-point problems.
*   Sound mathematical analysis based on monotone operator theory.
*   Identification of key considerations for efficient saddle-point optimization (non-uniform sampling, acceleration).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited and somewhat unconvincing experimental validation.
*   Dense notation and occasionally unclear presentation.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of optimization. While the experimental validation is limited, the novelty and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance, *with a strong recommendation for the authors to significantly expand and improve the experimental section and address the clarity issues in a revised version*. The ideas presented are promising and could lead to significant advances in solving large-scale machine learning problems.
