- Central contribution: extends SVRG/SAGA to convex–concave saddle-point problems (min_x max_y K+M) with linear convergence under strong convexity–concavity via a monotone-operator view.
- Analysis novelty: treats saddle points as zeros of a sum of a strongly monotone operator and a finite sum of Lipschitz operators, not a straightforward port from minimization.
- Algorithmic contributions: SVRG/SAGA extensions include mini-batching, two meaningful splits of the smooth operator (individual vs factored), and non-uniform sampling.
- Non-uniform sampling: shown to materially improve iteration complexity with explicit constants for bilinear K using ‖K‖F, ‖K‖op, ‖K‖max.
- Acceleration: simple catalyst-like proximal-point outer loop that yields complexities at least as good as accelerated batch methods and often better.
- Theoretical quality: technically sound proofs, clear conditions beyond convex minimization, linear rates with explicit dependence on L and sampling-dependent smoothness L̄(π).
- Empirical support: experiments (non-separable and separable settings) show non-uniform sampling matters, accelerated SVRG excels on ill-conditioned problems, and primal methods fail when separability is absent.
- Clarity: generally well written with examples, complexity tables, and pseudocode; weighted norms Ω/Ω* keep constants precise.
- Suggested clarity improvements: collect all definitions/assumptions in a single “Problem setup” box and give practical guidance on selecting probabilities, mini-batch size, and estimating L/L̄.
- Originality: first linearly convergent incremental methods for general (non-separable) saddle points and monotone inclusions without relying on co-coercivity.
- Significance: fills a gap for large-scale non-separable saddle problems common in ML (AUC surrogates, structured losses, robust optimization); monotone-operator perspective broadens applicability.
- Weakness — parameter knowledge: requires known strong convexity/concavity (λ,γ) and Lipschitz constants (L, L̄); needs guidance or adaptive strategies for estimating or upper-bounding them.
- Weakness — SAGA resampling: SAGA with non-uniform sampling theoretically requires a resampling step; paper notes a workaround but lacks empirical ablation on skipping resampling.
- Weakness — experimental scope: limited to two datasets and primarily bilinear K; adding a non-bilinear experiment is recommended to validate generality.
- Weakness — baselines: missing comparisons or discussion with Mirror-Prox/extragradient and SPDC-like stochastic primal–dual methods; suggest adding or discussing these baselines.
- Weakness — memory/time trade-offs: need explicit quantification of memory vs time for SAGA under individual vs factored splits and wall-clock benchmarks.
- Weakness — sampling cost: non-uniform sampling probabilities depend on row/column norms or element magnitudes; discuss one-time cost and handling for streaming or dynamic data.
- Pros summary: novel linear-rate incremental methods for non-separable saddle points, clean monotone-operator analysis, practical insights on splits and sampling, and effective acceleration.
- Cons summary: dependence on known curvature constants, relatively narrow experiments, and SAGA theoretical caveat on resampling not fully empirically explored.
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested additions on empirical scope and practical parameter-selection guidance to strengthen applicability.
