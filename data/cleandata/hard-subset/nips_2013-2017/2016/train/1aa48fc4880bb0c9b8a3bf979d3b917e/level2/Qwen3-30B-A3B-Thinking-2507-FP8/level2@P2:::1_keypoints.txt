- Proposes first linearly convergent stochastic variance-reduction algorithms (SVRG and SAGA variants) for saddle-point problems.  
- Introduces a novel convergence analysis using monotone operators instead of convex minimization frameworks.  
- Identifies key differences between saddle-point and convex minimization: two notions of splits (functions vs. partial derivatives), necessity of convex-concave splits, and the importance of non-uniform sampling.  
- Extends the "catalyst" acceleration framework to saddle-point problems, yielding accelerated methods that outperform accelerated batch algorithms.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical convergence guarantees for both SVRG and SAGA variants.  
- Thorough comparison of sampling strategies, showing non-uniform sampling is theoretically and empirically superior, especially for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Experimental results on two real-world datasets demonstrate clear advantages for non-separable losses and regularizers.  
- Strong practical motivation and relevance to ML applications (robust optimization, structured prediction, non-separable regularizers).  
- Major weakness: reliance on known strong convexity–concavity constants (λ and γ), which are often unknown in practice.  
- Limited experimental evaluation: only two datasets and a somewhat tangential comparison with primal methods on rcv1.  
- Does not adequately address computational cost of computing non-uniform sampling probabilities in the general case.  
- Literature placement could be improved with more thorough comparison to existing stochastic saddle-point methods (e.g., stochastic forward–backward algorithms).  
- Overall significance is high; opens variance reduction to large-scale saddle-point problems and is recommended for acceptance.
