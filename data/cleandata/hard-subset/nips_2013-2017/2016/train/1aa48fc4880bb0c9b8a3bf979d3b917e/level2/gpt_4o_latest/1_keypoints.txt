- Extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG/SAGA) to convex-concave saddle-point problems, providing the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale settings.  
- Addresses practical challenges like non-separability of losses/regularizers and the need for non-uniform sampling.  
- Uses the catalyst framework to accelerate methods, yielding algorithms that outperform existing batch and stochastic approaches both theoretically and empirically.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous convergence proofs via monotone operator theory, extending applicability to variational inequalities.  
- Practical relevance: emphasizes non-uniform sampling and efficient proximal computations, with experiments that validate theoretical claims.  
- Clear presentation of main contributions (SVRG/SAGA extension, monotone operator analysis, catalyst acceleration) supported by theory and experiments.  
- Weakness: algorithms require knowing strong convexity–concavity constants, which may be impractical in real-world settings.  
- Weakness: presentation is mathematically dense (especially monotone operator analysis), reducing accessibility for non-expert readers.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of other practical limitations such as extreme high-dimensional scalability and sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning.  
- Arguments for acceptance: substantial theoretical and practical advances, rigorous analysis, empirical validation, and high relevance to the NeurIPS community.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on strong convexity–concavity assumptions limits applicability; dense presentation reduces accessibility.  
- Recommendation: accept, with suggested improvements including more discussion of practical challenges and possible adaptive extensions.
