- Tackles convex-concave saddle-point problems by extending SVRG and SAGA to leverage separable structure.
- Provides convergence guarantees (linear convergence) for these variance-reduced methods in the saddle-point setting.
- Generalizes the approach to monotone operator problems, broadening applicability beyond standard saddle-points.
- Identifies non-uniform sampling as crucial for efficiency, supported by experiments.
- Introduces an acceleration technique based on the catalyst framework to improve practical performance.
- Analyzes different splitting strategies (function vs. partial derivative) and implications for bilinear saddle-point problems.
- Experimental results show practical improvement over established methods but are somewhat limited in scope.
- Notation is dense and sometimes cumbersome (e.g., multiple norm symbols like Ω, Ω*), hurting clarity.
- Theoretical presentation is thorough but can be hard to follow; more intuitive explanations alongside proofs are needed.
- Lacks detailed comparison to other large-scale saddle-point solvers and a computational complexity analysis.
- Section on existing stochastic algorithms (2.3) is brief and should better explain their limitations for saddle-point problems.
- Experimental evaluation should expand to more datasets, problem sizes, and sensitivity analyses (e.g., regularization λ).
- The claim that accelerated SVRG is "always superior" to accelerated batch algorithms is overstated and should be qualified.
- Discussion of sampling probabilities (Section 2.4) feels disconnected; practical implications should be clarified.
- Connection between theoretical convergence rates and empirical performance is not fully explored.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance conditional on addressing comparisons to existing solvers, complexity analysis, expanded experiments, and softer phrasing of superiority claims.
