- Extends SVRG and SAGA to convex-concave saddle-point problems, yielding the first linearly convergent large-scale algorithms for structured saddle-point tasks.  
- Three core contributions: novel convergence analysis via monotone operators; study of saddle-point-specific algorithmic choices (factored splits, non-uniform sampling); Catalyst-based acceleration with superior guarantees.  
- Uses monotone operator framework to prove linear convergence and applicability to broader problems (e.g., variational inequalities).  
- Avoids relying on co-coercivity assumptions required for convex minimization, making the analysis more general and non-trivial.  
- Identifies and analyzes practical aspects unique to saddle points—factored vs. individual splits and the key role of non-uniform sampling.  
- Provides an accelerated incremental method (Catalyst extension) with theoretical guarantees better than accelerated full-gradient methods.  
- Theoretical results are supported by experiments on binary classification with non-separable objectives.  
- High technical quality: solid, nontrivial extension of variance-reduction methods with rigorous proofs.  
- Clear writing and organization; effective presentation (Table 1) and useful connection to monotone operator theory (Section 6).  
- Highly original: first rigorous linear-convergence application of SVRG/SAGA to composite, decomposable saddle-point problems.  
- Significant practical impact: enables faster optimization for ML problems like AUC maximization, robust optimization, and structured non-separable regularizers.  
- Suggest expanding discussion of factored vs. individual SAGA splits, especially memory–computation trade-offs.  
- Suggest providing practical or adaptive guidance for selecting the Catalyst parameter τ.  
- Suggest moving more experimental details (AUC surrogate, clustering regularizer) from appendix to main text for reproducibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, comprehensive, and significant theoretical and practical contributions covering SVRG, SAGA, non-uniform sampling, and acceleration.  
- Arguments against acceptance: none compelling; only minor suggested revisions.  
- Overall assessment: excellent, technically sound, clearly presented work with significant impact.
