Summary
The paper studies convex-concave saddle-point problems of the form min_x max_y K(x,y)+M(x,y) where M is strongly convex-concave with an easy proximal operator and K is smooth. It extends stochastic variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA) to this setting and proves linear convergence under strong convexity-concavity. The analysis departs from standard convex minimization by leveraging monotone operator theory, which also enables an extension to more general variational inequality problems. The paper further (i) formalizes two natural ways to “split” the smooth operator B=[∂_x K, −∂_y K] (by functions or by partial derivatives), (ii) shows the importance of non-uniform sampling and gives tight L̄(π) constants for bilinear problems, (iii) presents storage-efficient “factored” variants for SAGA, and (iv) adapts the Catalyst/proximal-point acceleration to the saddle-point setting, yielding improved complexities. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate clear gains over batch baselines and show the benefits of non-uniform sampling and acceleration.

Relation to prior work
The work is well positioned relative to SAG/SVRG/SAGA for convex minimization and primal-dual/forward-backward methods for saddle points (Chambolle-Pock, Arrow-Hurwicz). Compared to stochastic primal-dual coordinate methods (SPDC, APDCD), which rely on separability, the proposed methods handle non-separable losses/regularizers. The monotone operator perspective connects to Rockafellar’s theory and to generalized forward-backward and SMART-like methods; the authors argue their analysis does not require co-coercivity, unlike some prior stochastic root-finding works. It would be helpful to discuss batch extragradient/Mirror-Prox methods (Korpelevich, Nemirovski) and their linear rates under strong monotonicity as additional baselines; nonetheless, the variance-reduction adaptation appears novel and impactful for large-scale settings.

Quality
- Theory: Theorems 1–3 provide linear convergence rates for SVRG, SAGA, and accelerated SVRG with explicit step-sizes depending on L and L̄(π). The monotone operator analysis is a strong point and convincingly covers both saddle points and VIs. The non-uniform sampling analysis and factored vs. individual splitting for bilinear problems are carefully derived, with informative norm inequalities clarifying when each scheme helps.
- Assumptions: Strong convexity-concavity and access to prox_M are standard in this line of work, and monotonicity/Lipschitz assumptions on B are natural for smooth K. The need to know or bound L and L̄ is acknowledged; the paper notes practical workarounds but could provide a clearer recipe for tuning.
- Algorithms: The SVRG/SAGA variants are straightforward yet thoughtfully adapted (e.g., storage-efficient factored SAGA). The Catalyst adaptation via proximal-point is elegant here and avoids extra Nesterov machinery.
- Experiments: Results are consistent with theory: non-uniform sampling is crucial; accelerated SVRG yields the best performance, especially when ill-conditioned. However, the empirical section is relatively narrow (two datasets), and omits comparison with extragradient/Mirror-Prox and recent primal-dual accelerated methods beyond forward-backward.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The use of weighted norms and operator notation is clear. Two suggestions:
- There appears to be a wording inconsistency: early in Section 1 (point (c)) the text states “the split does need to be done with convex-concave terms,” whereas Assumption (C) emphasizes that Bi need not derive from convex-concave functions (and can be partial-derivative splits). Please clarify.
- Provide a concise “practical tuning” paragraph: how to estimate L or L̄, choose minibatch size m, and set τ for Catalyst without full knowledge of norms.

Originality
High. To my knowledge (circa NIPS 2016), this is the first rigorous linear-convergence variance-reduction framework for general convex-concave saddle-point problems (beyond separable cases), with a clean monotone operator proof and a useful non-uniform sampling story. The extension to monotone inclusions without co-coercivity is also original and broadens scope.

Significance
High. Many important ML problems are better expressed as saddle points and can be non-separable. Bringing variance reduction with linear rates to this regime is likely to influence both practice and follow-up research. The factored SAGA variant and the sampling guidance are practically valuable. The Catalyst-style acceleration provides a principled and effective speedup.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Novel and principled extension of SVRG/SAGA to saddle points and monotone operators with linear rates.
- Insightful analysis of splitting strategies and non-uniform sampling, with tight constants in bilinear settings.
- Simple, implementable algorithms, including storage-efficient factored SAGA.
- Catalyst/proximal-point acceleration adapted to saddle points with clear complexity improvements.
- Empirical results corroborate theory; non-uniform sampling and acceleration matter.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope; lacks comparisons with extragradient/Mirror-Prox and stronger primal-dual baselines.
- Practical guidance on estimating L, L̄, and τ is brief; step-size rules may be conservative.
- For non-uniform SAGA, the need for resampling to match theory vs. the heuristic used in experiments deserves more discussion and ablation.
- Minor clarity issues (contradictory statement about splits).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: batch extragradient/Mirror-Prox (and their accelerated variants when applicable) on the same tasks; discuss their complexities vs. your methods.
- Provide a practical tuning section: how you set L, L̄, m, and τ in experiments; include a sensitivity analysis.
- Expand experiments: more datasets, wall-clock time, effect of factored vs. individual splits, impact of minibatch size, and ablation on resampling in non-uniform SAGA.
- Clarify the split requirement language and unify the presentation of assumptions. Include a brief proof sketch in the main text explaining how monotonicity (without co-coercivity) suffices for your step-size/rate.
- Discuss limitations: reliance on strong convexity-concavity; prospects for adaptivity (as mentioned in the conclusion).

Minor comments
- Check for typos and consistency (e.g., “does need splits” vs. Assumption C).
- Ensure all key references (extragradient/Mirror-Prox) are cited and contrasted.
- Consider adding a brief pseudocode block for the accelerated SVRG outer loop to improve readability.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution with solid theory and promising empirical evidence. With additional baselines and clarifications, it would be an excellent NIPS paper.
