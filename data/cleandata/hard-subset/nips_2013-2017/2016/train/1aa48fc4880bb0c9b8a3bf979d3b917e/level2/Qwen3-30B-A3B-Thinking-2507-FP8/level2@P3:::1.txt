

This paper extends stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, providing the first large-scale linearly convergent algorithms for this class of problems common in machine learning. The work builds upon recent advances in stochastic optimization for convex minimization while addressing unique challenges inherent to saddle-point problems. The authors demonstrate that the standard stochastic variance reduction techniques can be adapted to this setting, but require careful theoretical analysis using monotone operators rather than convex minimization techniques.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a rigorous convergence analysis for saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory, showing that the algorithms extend beyond saddle-point problems to variational inequalities. Second, it identifies two distinct notions of splits (in terms of functions or partial derivatives) that are relevant to saddle-point problems, a distinction that does not exist in convex minimization. Third, it establishes that non-uniform sampling is crucial for efficient implementation, both theoretically and empirically. Finally, it shows how to accelerate these algorithms using a simple extension of the "catalyst" framework, resulting in methods that consistently outperform accelerated batch algorithms.

The theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-justified. The authors carefully establish the necessary conditions for their convergence proofs and provide detailed complexity analysis for various sampling schemes. The paper effectively connects the theoretical results to practical implementation considerations, including the importance of non-uniform sampling strategies for different problem structures. The experiments on machine learning problems with non-separable losses and regularizers (including a structured clustering-inducing regularizer and a non-separable ROC-based loss) provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims, demonstrating significant improvements over existing batch and stochastic methods.

The paper's originality is clear. While stochastic variance reduction methods have been extensively studied for convex minimization, extending them to saddle-point problems requires a fundamentally different theoretical approach. The authors correctly identify that the convex minimization analysis does not apply and develop a novel analysis using monotone operators. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work on saddle-point problems by providing linear convergence guarantees rather than sublinear rates. The connection to variational inequalities and monotone operator theory significantly broadens the applicability of the proposed methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. Saddle-point problems arise in numerous machine learning applications, including robust optimization, supervised learning with non-separable losses or regularizers, and convex relaxations of unsupervised learning problems. The proposed algorithms provide the first large-scale linearly convergent methods for these problems, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The experiments demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where the benefits of variance reduction are most pronounced.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail to allow reproduction of the results. The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical implementation is well-explained, including the importance of different splitting strategies and sampling probabilities. The authors effectively motivate their approach by highlighting the limitations of existing methods for saddle-point problems.

One minor weakness is the limited discussion of practical implementation details for the factored split approach. While the paper mentions that the factored split requires storing O(n+d) elements rather than O(nd) for individual splits, a more detailed explanation of how this storage is managed in practice would be beneficial. Additionally, the paper could more explicitly compare the computational overhead of the proposed algorithms with existing methods for specific problem instances.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, addressing a significant gap in the literature on optimization for machine learning. The technical contributions are substantial, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results support the claims. The work advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning by providing the first linearly convergent stochastic methods for saddle-point problems, with clear theoretical and practical advantages over existing approaches.

This paper should be accepted for NIPS 2016. The contribution is novel, technically sound, and significant for the machine learning community. The authors have made a clear and well-supported contribution to the field of optimization, with important implications for a wide range of machine learning applications. The paper's theoretical insights and practical algorithms represent a meaningful advancement over existing work in this area.
