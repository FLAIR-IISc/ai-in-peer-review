

This paper presents an extension of stochastic variance reduction methods (SVRG and SAGA) to saddle-point problems, which are common in machine learning through duality. The authors make several key contributions: (1) they provide the first convergence analysis for these algorithms on saddle-point problems using monotone operator theory rather than convex minimization techniques; (2) they identify two distinct notions of splitting (in terms of functions versus partial derivatives) that are specific to saddle-point problems; (3) they demonstrate the importance of non-uniform sampling for efficiency; and (4) they show how to accelerate these algorithms using the "catalyst" framework.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature. Saddle-point problems arise naturally in many machine learning contexts (duality, robust optimization, structured prediction), yet prior work on variance reduction methods has primarily focused on convex minimization. The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful attention to the differences between saddle-point problems and convex minimization (e.g., the need for monotone operator theory rather than standard convex analysis). The authors also provide a clear connection to variational inequalities, which broadens the applicability of their results.

The paper excels in its theoretical contributions. The convergence analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) is rigorous and demonstrates linear convergence rates, which is a significant improvement over existing stochastic methods that typically achieve sublinear rates. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work on convex minimization (e.g., SVRG/SAGA for convex problems) and provide valuable insights into the structure of saddle-point problems. The extension to monotone operators in Section 6 is particularly noteworthy, as it demonstrates the broader applicability of their framework beyond saddle-point problems.

The experimental section is well-designed and effectively supports the theoretical claims. The authors compare their methods against batch and stochastic algorithms on two datasets with non-separable losses and regularizers, showing clear advantages for their variance-reduced methods, especially with non-uniform sampling. The results are consistent with the theoretical predictions, and the inclusion of both ill-conditioned and well-conditioned problems strengthens the evaluation. The comparison with primal methods on a separable problem (rcv1 dataset) is particularly convincing for demonstrating the necessity of their approach in non-separable settings.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with good pseudocode for the algorithms. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the connection to existing work (e.g., SVRG, SAGA, monotone operator theory) is well-established. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific implementation choices (e.g., non-uniform sampling probabilities for bilinear problems).

A few specific suggestions for improvement:
1. The paper could provide more intuition about why monotone operator theory is necessary for saddle-point problems rather than simply stating that convex minimization analysis does not apply. A brief comparison of the key differences in the proof techniques would help readers understand the conceptual shift.
2. While the paper mentions that strong convexity-concavity constants need to be known, it would be helpful to include a brief discussion of how these constants might be estimated in practice, or whether adaptivity (as in the convex minimization case) could be incorporated.
3. The experiments could be strengthened by including a comparison with the recent "catalyst" acceleration framework applied directly to the dual problem, to better isolate the benefits of their approach versus standard acceleration techniques.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature by extending variance reduction methods to saddle-point problems, which are ubiquitous in machine learning through duality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are compelling, and the approach is both general and practically useful. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous contributions and demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a theoretically sound contribution that addresses an important problem in machine learning. The results are significant, as they provide the first linearly convergent stochastic algorithms for a broad class of saddle-point problems, with clear theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods. The paper also connects to broader mathematical frameworks (monotone operators, variational inequalities), which adds to its significance.

Strengths:
- First convergence analysis for variance-reduced methods on saddle-point problems
- Clear distinction between saddle-point problems and convex minimization
- Rigorous theoretical analysis using monotone operators
- Strong experimental validation with clear practical advantages
- Connection to broader mathematical frameworks (monotone operators, variational inequalities)

Weaknesses:
- Could provide more intuition about why monotone operator theory is necessary
- Limited discussion of practical estimation of strong convexity-concavity constants
- Experiments could be extended to more diverse problem settings

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The technical content is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are significant. I recommend acceptance. The authors have addressed an important gap in the literature and provided both theoretical and practical advances that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with saddle-point problems in machine learning.
