This paper introduces extensions of stochastic variance reduction (SVR) methods like SVRG and SAGA to saddle-point problems, a common structure in machine learning. The core idea is to adapt these algorithms to handle the min-max nature of the saddle-point formulation, offering the first linearly convergent algorithms for large-scale problems of this type. The authors highlight several key contributions: a convergence analysis based on monotone operators, the identification of crucial split considerations, the importance of non-uniform sampling, and acceleration via a catalyst framework.

The paper is well-motivated. Saddle-point problems are ubiquitous, and extending successful optimization techniques from convex minimization to this setting is a natural and important step. The connection to monotone operator theory provides a more general framework, and the analysis goes beyond simple saddle-point problems, encompassing variational inequalities. The discussion of different split notions (function vs. partial derivative) is insightful. The emphasis on non-uniform sampling, both theoretically and empirically, is commendable. The theoretical results are solid, and the experimental validation supports the claims made.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending SVRG/SAGA to saddle-point problems with linear convergence guarantees is a significant contribution. The application of monotone operator theory is clever and broadens the scope of the work.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous and well-justified. The use of monotone operator theory offers a solid foundation.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The demonstrated acceleration via the catalyst framework and the experimental results suggest practical benefits for machine learning applications.
*   **Clarity (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the contributions, and the different sections are logically structured.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors address a wide range of considerations, including different split notions, sampling strategies, and acceleration techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation:** The notation is sometimes dense and could be simplified. For example, the repeated use of subscripts and superscripts can be confusing.
*   **Accessibility:** While the paper is generally well-written, the level of mathematical sophistication required to fully understand the proofs might be high for some readers. More intuitive explanations could be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Details:** While experiments are presented, more detail about the datasets, hyperparameter tuning, and baseline comparisons would strengthen the results.  Specifically, details on the choice of λ and γ and how they relate to the data, and a more detailed analysis of the performance differences on different datasets would be helpful.
*   **Overly Optimistic Conclusion:** The conclusion states the potential for adaptivity to constants, but doesn’t expand on how. This feels like a promise of future work rather than a concrete result.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While related work is cited, a more in-depth discussion of the connection to existing algorithms for saddle-point problems (beyond the batch methods) would be valuable. For instance, the relationship to other primal-dual methods could be explored.

**Specific Comments:**

*   **Page 2, Eq. (1):** Consider adding a brief explanation of why saddle-point problems are common in machine learning, even for readers familiar with the field.
*   **Page 3, Assumptions (A)-(C):** These assumptions are critical. Consider adding a sentence or two discussing the implications of violating these assumptions.
*   **Page 5:** The discussion of different splits is excellent. Visualizing these splits with simple examples could further enhance clarity.
*   **Section 7:** The experiments are convincing, but a comparison to more recent stochastic optimization algorithms (beyond those mentioned) would be valuable.
*   **Table 1:** The table is helpful, but it would be more informative if it included the practical implications of the different complexities.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of optimization.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified.
*   The experimental results support the claims made.
*   The work has the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The notation is dense and could be simplified.
*   The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental analysis and a broader comparison to existing algorithms.
*   The level of mathematical sophistication required to fully understand the paper might be high for some readers.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths far outweigh them. The work is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically relevant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding notation, accessibility, and experimental details. The authors should focus on simplifying the notation, providing more intuitive explanations, and adding more detail to the experimental section.
