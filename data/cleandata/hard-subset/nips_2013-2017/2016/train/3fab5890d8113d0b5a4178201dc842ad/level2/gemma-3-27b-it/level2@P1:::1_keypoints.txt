- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN that limits reads/writes to a sparse memory subset to improve scalability.  
- Claims asymptotic space/time complexity improvements over NTMs and Memory Networks.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), Omniglot, and bAbI with comparable or improved performance.  
- Reports substantial resource savings (e.g., ~1000x speedup, ~3000x memory reduction).  
- Provides theoretical analysis supporting asymptotic improvements, with implementation details and supplementary material.  
- Shows generality by applying SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).  
- Uses curriculum learning to scale tasks, which strengthens experimental validation.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and organized.  
- Key weakness: sparse ANN implementation and tuning (FLANN vs. LSH) are under-detailed and impact of approximation is not fully explored.  
- Key weakness: limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially sparsity level K and usage measure.  
- Key weakness: insufficient comparison to other sparse or RL-based sparse MANN approaches and claims of asymptotic optimality need stronger contrast.  
- Key weakness: lacks thorough ablation studies isolating contributions of components (sparse reads vs writes, usage measures).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses critical scalability bottleneck, theoretically sound, strong empirical gains, and general applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: needs more ANN details, hyperparameter analysis, comparisons, and ablations.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution that warrants acceptance despite areas for improvement.
