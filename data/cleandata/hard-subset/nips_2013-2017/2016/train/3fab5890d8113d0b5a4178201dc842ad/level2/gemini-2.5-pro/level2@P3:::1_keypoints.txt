- Problem: MANNs suffer from prohibitive O(N) time and O(NT) space complexity per timestep, preventing large-memory or long-sequence applications.  
- Contribution: Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that restricts reads/writes to K sparse memory locations to achieve asymptotic optimality.  
- ANN integration: Uses Approximate Nearest Neighbor structures (e.g., k-d trees, LSH) to find K relevant memory locations in ~O(log N) rather than O(N).  
- Backpropagation innovation: Stores only sparse memory modifications and "rolls back" during BPTT, reducing per-timestep space from O(N) to O(1).  
- Empirical gains: Demonstrates up to three orders-of-magnitude speed and memory improvements over dense models.  
- Performance: Matches or outperforms dense baselines on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning.  
- Scalability: Enables training on hundreds of thousands of timesteps and millions of memory words, previously unattainable for MANNs.  
- Generality: Method applied to Differentiable Neural Computer (Sparse DNC), showing broader applicability.  
- Experimental quality: Thorough evaluation including comparison to canonical NTM and a dense baseline (DAM) across multiple domains.  
- Technical quality: Claims on asymptotic complexity are well-supported and the efficient BPTT implementation is a key technical strength.  
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written with clear motivation, logical architecture description, and effective visualizations (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Originality: Novel integration of sparse operations, ANN search, and sparse BPTT into end-to-end differentiable MANNs.  
- Significance: High impact potential by removing a core scalability barrier and enabling new real-world applications for MANNs.  
- Minor weakness: Lacks detailed analysis of ANN trade-offs (choice of K, ANN hyperparameters, and rebuild costs).  
- Acceptance argument: Reviewer finds no significant reasons against acceptance; implementation complexity of ANN is minor relative to gains.
