

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel differentiable memory architecture for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that achieves asymptotically optimal space and time complexity by constraining reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory. The core claims are: (1) SAM reduces memory overhead by 3,000× and accelerates training by 1,000× versus dense MANNs (e.g., NTM), (2) it maintains comparable learning efficiency on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Omniglot, and Babi QA, and (3) it scales to 100,000+ time steps via curriculum learning.  

The paper provides strong experimental support for its claims. Figure 1 demonstrates the dramatic speed/memory improvements (1,600× faster with k-d trees, 53MB vs. 29GB for 64K memory words). The curriculum learning results (Fig 3) convincingly show SAM’s scalability to sequences >4,000 steps with millions of memory slots, while maintaining generalization to 200,000-step sequences. Omniglot and Babi results (Section 4.4–4.5) confirm SAM’s data efficiency matches or exceeds dense baselines (e.g., SAM outperforms dense MANNs by 0.2 vs. 0.4 errors on Omniglot with 100 characters). The theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) justifies SAM’s asymptotic optimality.  

Strengths include compelling empirical validation, clear technical contributions (sparse write policy, efficient backpropagation), and practical relevance for real-world MANN deployment. The SDNC extension (Supplementary D) further demonstrates generality.  

Key weaknesses are: (1) Incomplete comparison to related work—specifically, the 2015 RL-based sparse NTM (Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015) uses similar sparse access via reinforcement learning but avoids gradients on memory updates; SAM’s differentiable approach should explicitly contrast this trade-off (e.g., gradient variance vs. sparsity). (2) The ANN implementation details (k-d trees vs. LSH) lack ablation studies on K-value sensitivity or long-term balancing costs (Section 3.5). (3) The claim of "1,000× faster" depends on memory size; the paper should clarify this scaling behavior.  

The work is technically sound and addresses a critical bottleneck in MANNs. To strengthen it, the authors should: (a) Compare SAM directly to Zaremba et al.’s RL-NTM in terms of learning stability and gradient quality, (b) Add ablations for K (number of sparse reads) and ANN rebuild frequency, and (c) Clarify the "1,000×" speed claim with scaling curves. These additions would solidify the novelty and completeness. Overall, SAM is a significant step toward scalable MANNs with strong practical impact, warranting acceptance with minor revisions.
