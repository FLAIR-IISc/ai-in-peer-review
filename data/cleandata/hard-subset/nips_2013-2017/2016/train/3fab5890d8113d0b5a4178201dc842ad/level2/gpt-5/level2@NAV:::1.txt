Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable MANN that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to achieve sublinear content-based addressing. It introduces an efficient BPTT scheme that logs sparse updates and reverts them during backprop, yielding O(1) per-step space and O(log N) time for memory access, while retaining NTM-like functionality. Experiments on NTM algorithmic tasks, a large-scale curriculum, bAbI QA (including a sparse DNC variant, SDNC), and Omniglot one-shot classification indicate comparable data efficiency to dense baselines with up to three orders of magnitude speed/memory savings. This builds directly on NTMs, Memory Networks, and the DNC (all well represented in prior NIPS), and contrasts with RL-based discrete addressing for sparsity.

Quality: The claims are clearly stated: asymptotically optimal time/space for content-addressed MANNs, large empirical speed/memory gains, and maintained learning efficacy. Empirical support is substantial and includes wall-clock benchmarks, scaling curricula to millions of memory slots, and competitive bAbI/Omniglot performance. However, statistical rigor is limited (few runs/variances reported), and the impact of sparsification/ANN approximation on gradients and accuracy is only qualitatively assessed. The complexity arguments are relegated to the supplement; a main-text sketch would help.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized with precise descriptions of sparse read/write, memory usage tracking, and ANN maintenance. Some implementation choices (threshold ε, K sensitivity, ANN rebuild policy) could be more systematically explained, and reproducibility may hinge on specialized data structures not fully detailed.

Originality: Embedding an ANN index into a differentiable memory access path and a rollback-based BPTT for sparse updates are novel and practically important. The sparse DNC adaptation strengthens generality.

Significance: If robust, SAM addresses a key bottleneck for MANNs and could enable practical large-memory applications. Current validation is mostly synthetic or small-scale; demonstrations on real-world LM/MT or retrieval-augmented tasks would greatly increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled sparsity mechanism with strong asymptotic guarantees.
- Dramatic empirical efficiency gains without loss of data efficiency.
- Generalizable to other MANNs (SDNC) with strong bAbI results.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited evaluation on large real-world tasks; mostly synthetic benchmarks.
- Insufficient analysis of approximation error, K/ANN sensitivity, and variance across runs.
- Reproducibility risks due to complex indexing/memory management details.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide ablations on K, ε, ANN type/rebuild frequency, and accuracy-speed trade-offs; report mean±std over multiple seeds.
- Quantify approximation error of sparse reads vs. dense soft attention.
- Release code and ANN/MRU data structures; document hardware and fair wall-clock comparisons.
- Add a real-world large-memory experiment (e.g., long-context QA or retrieval-augmented LM) to demonstrate practical utility.
