- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented NN that constrains reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory words.  
- Achieves asymptotically optimal space and time complexity via sparse memory access.  
- Reports massive practical gains: ~1,000× faster and ~3,000× less physical memory than dense models while retaining comparable data efficiency.  
- Theoretical analysis of complexity bounds is sound and well-justified.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering synthetic algorithmic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), bAbI, and Omniglot.  
- Results demonstrate SAM’s scalability advantages while maintaining performance comparable to dense models.  
- Implementation details for sparse memory management and efficient content-based addressing are thorough.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and well-situated in related literature (NTMs, Memory Networks).  
- Curriculum learning approach enables scaling to 100,000+ time steps and memory words, with strong generalization beyond training lengths.  
- Strong empirical performance on bAbI (best reported without supervised memory access) and Omniglot (outperforms dense models).  
- Approach generalizes to other architectures (e.g., Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer).  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and discuss ANN choices (k-d trees vs LSH) and future directions (e.g., Mondrian forests).  
- Minor weakness: insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection for the approximate nearest neighbor index and trade-offs.  
- Minor weakness: lacks a direct comparison with RL-based sparse memory access methods (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever 2015).  
- Overall significance and technical quality are high; the work is novel, practically impactful, and recommended for acceptance.
