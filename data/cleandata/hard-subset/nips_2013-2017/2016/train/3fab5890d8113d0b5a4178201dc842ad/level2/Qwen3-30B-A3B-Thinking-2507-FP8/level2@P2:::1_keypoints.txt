- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrain reads/writes to a sparse subset (K=4–8), use efficient data structures for content-based addressing, and track usage to find unused memory blocks.  
- Claims SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space/time and reports up to 1,000× faster training and 3,000× less memory than dense models while retaining comparable task performance.  
- Main strength is practical scalability improvements demonstrated across multiple tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort, Babi, Omniglot).  
- Speed and memory benchmarks convincingly show benefits for large memory sizes; SDNC generalization attains state-of-the-art on Babi without supervised memory access.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical detail; theoretical proofs are in the supplement.  
- Weakness: the “asymptotically optimal” claim needs stronger justification and a summary of key assumptions in the main text.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of failure cases when relevant memory items are not among the top-K nearest neighbors and how to mitigate this.  
- Weakness: missing empirical comparison to other scaling approaches (e.g., reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing), despite referencing them.  
- Weakness: the “1,000× faster” claim is under-specified—depends on memory size, ANN implementation, and other parameters; conditions should be stated.  
- Weakness: comparison to a dense DAM variant may overstate gains relative to the original NTM.  
- Weakness: trade-offs of sparsity (how varying K affects performance), especially on complex or unpredictable tasks, are not thoroughly explored.  
- Overall: technically sound and valuable contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address theoretical justification and sparse-approach limitations.
