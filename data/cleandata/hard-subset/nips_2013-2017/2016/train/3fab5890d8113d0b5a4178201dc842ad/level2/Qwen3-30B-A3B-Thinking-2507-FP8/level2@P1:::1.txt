

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by constraining reads and writes to sparse subsets of memory. The core innovation leverages approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) data structures to reduce time and space complexity from O(N) to O(log N) per operation, while maintaining end-to-end differentiability. The authors demonstrate SAM's efficiency through extensive experiments, showing 1,000× speedup and 3,000× memory reduction over non-sparse baselines (e.g., NTM), with comparable performance on synthetic tasks, Babi question answering, and Omniglot one-shot learning. The approach generalizes to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), yielding a Sparse DNC (SDNC) that achieves state-of-the-art Babi results without supervised memory access.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the MANN literature. Prior work on scaling MANNs (e.g., [22] using reinforcement learning for discrete addressing) faces higher variance in training, while SAM achieves theoretical optimality (O(1) space/time per step) through sparse access and efficient backpropagation. The authors effectively contextualize SAM against key prior work: NTMs [7], Memory Networks [21], and DNCs [8], clearly articulating how SAM's sparse design addresses the critical scalability bottleneck limiting real-world application of MANNs.

Strengths are numerous. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated: sparse reads (using ANN indices to select top-K similar memory words) and writes (to previously read or least-recently-accessed locations) avoid the linear overhead of dense operations. The theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) rigorously establishes SAM's asymptotic optimality, and the experimental validation is thorough. Key results include: (1) empirical verification of O(1) memory usage per time step (Fig 1b), (2) SAM matching dense baselines on NTM tasks (Fig 2) despite sparsity, (3) successful scaling to 4,000+ sequence lengths on associative recall via curriculum learning (Fig 3), and (4) SDNC achieving 9/10 Babi task success without supervision (Supplementary G). The ablation study (SAM vs. DAM) convincingly isolates the impact of sparsity on learning efficiency.

The writing is generally clear, though some sections could be tightened. The architecture description (Section 3) is precise, but the usage statistic definition (U^(2)_T in Eq 6) merits slightly more intuitive explanation. The supplementary material is comprehensive and well-organized, addressing most technical details not included in the main text. The paper avoids overclaiming; for instance, it acknowledges SAM's performance on Babi is comparable to dense models (not necessarily superior), and notes the NTM's poor performance likely stems from poor memory allocation rather than sparsity.

Minor weaknesses warrant attention. First, the paper states SAM is "asymptotically optimal" but does not explicitly prove the lower bound for the specific problem (e.g., content-based addressing with sparse updates). While Supplementary A likely contains this, a brief statement in the main text would strengthen the claim. Second, the ANN implementation details (k-d trees vs. LSH) could be more directly compared in the main text; Supplementary E describes the setup but lacks a clear rationale for the choice. Third, the Omniglot results (Fig 4) state SAM "outperformed other models" but do not quantify the margin (e.g., "0.2 vs. 0.4 errors"), which would clarify its practical advantage. Finally, while the paper discusses "sparse writes" as a key innovation, the write scheme (Eq 5) could be more explicitly contrasted with alternatives like sparse content-based writes.

The significance is high. MANNs have struggled to scale beyond toy tasks due to memory constraints; SAM directly enables training on problems requiring 100,000s of memory slots and time steps. The 1,000× speedup makes MANNs viable for real-world applications like long-context language modeling. The SDNC's Babi performance (9/10 tasks) without supervision is a notable advancement over prior end-to-end MANNs (which failed at 6 tasks [17]), demonstrating SAM's practical impact.

This work meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written with strong empirical support (Clarity), offers a novel and well-motivated solution to a key scalability problem (Originality), and addresses a significant bottleneck in neural architecture design (Significance). The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable in revision and do not undermine the paper's core contribution.

I recommend acceptance. The paper delivers a compelling, scalable solution to a long-standing challenge in MANNs, with results that are both theoretically rigorous and practically impactful. The authors have clearly demonstrated how to make memory-augmented networks feasible for large-scale applications.
