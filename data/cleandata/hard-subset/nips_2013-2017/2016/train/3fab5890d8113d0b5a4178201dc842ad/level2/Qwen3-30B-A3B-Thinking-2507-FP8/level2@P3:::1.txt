

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network architecture that addresses the scalability limitations of existing models like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks. The key innovation is constraining all memory reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words, which achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity. The authors demonstrate that SAM runs 1,000× faster and uses 3,000× less physical memory than non-sparse models while maintaining comparable data efficiency across multiple tasks.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by solving a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks. The technical analysis of complexity bounds is sound, with clear theoretical justification for the asymptotic optimality claims. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering synthetic algorithmic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. The results convincingly demonstrate SAM's scalability advantages while maintaining performance comparable to dense models. The implementation details for sparse memory management are thorough, including efficient data structures for content-based addressing and approximate nearest neighbor search.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with effective use of figures to illustrate key findings. The motivation for addressing scalability issues is compelling, and the work is positioned clearly within the existing literature on memory-augmented neural networks. The authors provide appropriate citations to related work, including NTMs, Memory Networks, and previous approaches to scaling memory architectures.

A notable strength is the practical demonstration of SAM's capabilities on large-scale tasks. The curriculum learning approach for scaling tasks to 100,000+ time steps and memory words is particularly impressive, with SAM successfully generalizing to sequences far exceeding training lengths. The results on Babi tasks (achieving the best reported result without supervising memory access) and Omniglot (significantly outperforming dense models) demonstrate practical utility beyond synthetic benchmarks.

The paper's approach is generalizable to other memory-augmented architectures, as evidenced by the Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) implementation. The authors thoughtfully address potential limitations, such as the choice between k-d trees and LSH for approximate nearest neighbor search, and suggest promising directions for future work including Mondrian forests.

One area for minor improvement would be to provide more detail about the hyperparameter selection for the approximate nearest neighbor index. While the paper mentions using k-d trees for small word sizes and LSH for large word sizes, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs and how these choices impact performance would enhance reproducibility.

The paper could also benefit from a more direct comparison with reinforcement learning approaches to sparse memory access, such as the work by Zaremba and Sutskever (2015), which also aims to scale NTMs through sparse memory access. While the paper acknowledges this work, a more explicit comparison would strengthen the positioning of SAM within the landscape of scalable memory architectures.

The significance of this work is substantial. By making memory-augmented neural networks practically scalable, SAM enables applications that were previously infeasible due to computational constraints. The demonstrated speedups (1,000× faster, 3,000× less memory) are transformative for real-world applications requiring large-scale memory, such as language modeling and complex reasoning tasks.

The paper's technical quality is high, with careful attention to both theoretical foundations and practical implementation. The experimental results are well-designed and convincingly support the claims. The work advances the state of the art in memory-augmented neural networks by providing a solution that is both theoretically optimal and practically effective.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field with significant theoretical and practical implications. It addresses a critical limitation in memory-augmented neural networks with a clear, well-executed solution that is both novel and broadly applicable. The empirical results are robust, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the work has clear potential for real-world impact. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
