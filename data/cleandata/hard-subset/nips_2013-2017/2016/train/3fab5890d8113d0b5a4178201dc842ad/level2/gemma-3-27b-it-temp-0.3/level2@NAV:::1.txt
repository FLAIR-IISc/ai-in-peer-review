## Review of "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), which, despite their promise for algorithmic learning, suffer from computational and memory bottlenecks as memory size increases. The authors introduce Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme that achieves asymptotic improvements in space and time complexity by restricting read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations. This is a significant contribution as it directly tackles a major limitation hindering the practical application of MANNs.

The core idea of SAM – sparse reads and writes coupled with efficient data structures – is well-motivated and clearly explained. The authors convincingly demonstrate through benchmarks that SAM offers substantial speedups (1000x faster) and memory savings (3000x less physical memory) compared to non-sparse models like the NTM. Importantly, these gains are achieved *without* sacrificing performance on a range of tasks, including synthetic algorithmic problems, Babi reasoning tasks, and one-shot Omniglot character recognition. The extension to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) further highlights the generality of the approach.

A key strength of the paper is its thorough experimental evaluation. The authors not only compare SAM to existing models but also investigate its ability to scale to tasks requiring very long sequences and large memories. The inclusion of results on the Babi tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance without memory access supervision, is particularly noteworthy. The paper also acknowledges limitations, such as the potential for improvement in the ANN implementation, and suggests future research directions.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved in choosing the sparsity level (K). While the authors mention K=4 or K=8, a more systematic exploration of the impact of K on performance and efficiency would be valuable. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates comparable data efficiency, a more direct comparison of sample complexity with other methods would strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant bottleneck in MANN research.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach (SAM) with strong theoretical justification.
*   Demonstrates substantial improvements in speed and memory efficiency through rigorous experimentation.
*   Achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on a variety of tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the impact of sparsity level (K) on performance.
*   Could benefit from a more direct comparison of sample complexity.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM architecture offers a promising solution to the scalability problem, and the thorough experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness. Despite minor areas for improvement, I recommend acceptance with encouragement to further explore the trade-offs associated with sparsity.
