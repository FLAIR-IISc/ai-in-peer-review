This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory-augmented neural network (MANN) architecture designed to address the scalability issues inherent in existing MANNs like NTMs and Memory Networks. The core idea is to limit read and write operations to a sparse subset of the memory, significantly reducing computational and memory overhead. The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic improvements in space and time complexity and show empirical results on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), the Omniglot dataset, and the Babi tasks, demonstrating comparable or improved performance to existing models with drastically reduced resource requirements. They also demonstrate how their approach can be applied to the recently introduced Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a crucial bottleneck in the development of practical MANNs. The problem of scaling MANNs is clearly articulated, and the proposed solution—sparsity—is a logical and promising approach. The technical details appear sound, and the authors provide a good level of detail regarding the implementation and theoretical analysis of SAM (with further details in the supplementary material). The empirical results are compelling, showcasing substantial speedups and memory savings without sacrificing performance. The adaptation of SAM to the DNC is a nice demonstration of the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of curriculum learning for scaling tasks is also a strength.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant Scalability Improvements:** The most significant strength of the paper is the demonstrated improvement in scalability. The reported speedups (1000x faster) and memory reduction (3000x less) are substantial and address a critical limitation of existing MANNs.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide theoretical analysis supporting the asymptotic improvements in space and time complexity.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The results on a diverse set of tasks, including synthetic benchmarks, a one-shot learning task (Omniglot), and a question-answering dataset (Babi), provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of SAM.
*   **Generality:** The application of SAM to the DNC highlights the broader applicability of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **ANN Implementation Details:** While the use of Approximate Nearest Neighbors (ANNs) is a key component of the speedup, the discussion of their implementation and tuning is relatively brief. More detail on the choice of ANN (FLANN vs. LSH) and the parameters used would be beneficial. The impact of ANN approximation on performance is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not extensively explore the sensitivity of SAM to hyperparameters, particularly those related to the sparsity level (K) and the usage measure. A more thorough hyperparameter analysis would strengthen the findings.
*   **Comparison to Other Sparse MANNs:** The introduction mentions reinforcement learning based sparse MANNs [22]. A more detailed comparison to these works, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach, would be valuable. The authors claim their approach is asymptotically optimal, but a comparison to other sparsity-inducing methods would strengthen this claim.
*    **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares SAM to DAM and NTM, more ablation studies investigating the impact of individual components (e.g., sparse reads vs. sparse writes, different usage measures) would provide greater insights into the contribution of each element of SAM.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical bottleneck in MANN research – scalability.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   Demonstrates significant performance improvements with substantial resource savings.
*   The generality of the approach, as shown by the DNC adaptation, is a positive aspect.
*   The results are compelling and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The ANN implementation details could benefit from more elaboration.
*   A more thorough hyperparameter analysis is needed.
*   The comparison to other sparse MANN approaches could be more comprehensive.
*   Ablation studies are lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM architecture effectively addresses the scalability limitations of existing models, enabling the training of MANNs with much larger memories. While there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened (particularly regarding ANN details, hyperparameter analysis, and comparisons to other sparse methods), the compelling results and significant improvements in scalability make this a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper.
