- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable MANN that enforces sparsity in reads/writes to scale to very large memories.  
- Reads use content-based addressing over top-K slots via an ANN index (randomized k-d trees or LSH); writes target previously read slots and an LRU slot.  
- Introduces a rollback-based BPTT that stores and reverts only sparse modifications, giving O(1) per-step memory overhead.  
- Claims asymptotically optimal time O(log N) per step (with ANN) and O(1) per-step space under specific assumptions about addressing and K.  
- Empirically matches or improves data efficiency versus dense NTMs/DAM on algorithmic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort).  
- Trains on curricula to sequence lengths >4k with memory sizes in the millions, showing large wall-clock and memory gains (up to 1600× faster, 3000× less memory).  
- Outperforms dense baselines on Omniglot one-shot classification, likely due to greater capacity.  
- Extends approach to a sparse DNC (SDNC) with strong bAbI results (reported in supplement).  
- Strength: principled, differentiable sparse read/write design that preserves trainability.  
- Strength: solid complexity analysis and practical implementation realizing claimed savings; rollback BPTT is a useful engineering contribution.  
- Strength: extensive and diverse experiments demonstrating scalability and generality, with practical engineering details.  
- Weakness: “asymptotically optimal” claim depends on assumptions (K = O(1), ANN behavior) that are sketched in main text but deferred to supplement.  
- Weakness: ANN/top-K approximations introduce non-differentiabilities; only K slots receive gradients each step and sensitivity to K/ANN recall needs deeper analysis.  
- Weakness: curriculum comparisons use vastly different memory sizes (dense vs SAM), complicating claims about data efficiency or algorithm learning.  
- Weakness: write policy (read interpolation + LRU) is heuristic and may limit tasks needing broad/structured writes; lacks ablation of components and erase policy.  
- Weakness: limited real-world evaluation—Omniglot is promising but language modeling and large QA datasets are missing; bAbI results are supplementary.  
- Quality: technically sound, well-integrated engineering choices, but needs more controlled comparisons and sensitivity analyses (K, ANN type, rebuild frequency, usage threshold).  
- Clarity: generally well-written; recommend moving key optimality assumptions to main text, adding ablation on K/ANN type, and clarifying index rebuild protocol.  
- Originality: high—novel integration of sparse access + ANN into end-to-end differentiable MANNs with practical training and scaling.  
- Significance: high—provides a practical path to orders-of-magnitude larger memories and is likely to influence memory-architecture research.  
- Requests: report sensitivity to K and ANN recall, amortized ANN rebuild overhead, distribution and ablation of write-to-read vs write-to-LRU, robustness to larger word sizes, and capacity-matched curriculum plots.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong practical contribution, impressive efficiency gains, generality across architectures, and solid empirical validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: deferred theoretical details, limited real-world tasks, and missing critical ablations.  
- Overall assessment: impactful, carefully executed work with some missing ablations and clarity on assumptions; reviewer leans toward acceptance pending added sensitivity analyses and clarifications.
