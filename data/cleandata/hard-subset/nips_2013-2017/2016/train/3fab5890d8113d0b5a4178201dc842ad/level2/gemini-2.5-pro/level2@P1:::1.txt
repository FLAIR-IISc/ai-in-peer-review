### Review

**Summary**
This paper addresses a critical scalability problem in Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) such as the Neural Turing Machine (NTM). Standard MANNs employ "soft" attention mechanisms that read from and write to all memory locations at every time step, resulting in time and space complexity that is linear in the memory size (N). This makes it prohibitive to use them with large memories. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an architecture that scales efficiently to very large memories by enforcing sparsity in memory operations.

The core contributions of SAM are:
1.  A sparse read operation that attends to only a small, constant number (K) of the most relevant memory locations. This is achieved by querying an Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) data structure (e.g., k-d trees or LSH) built over the memory content, reducing the read time complexity from O(N) to O(logN).
2.  A sparse write operation that updates either previously read locations or the least recently used (LRU) memory slot, maintaining constant-time updates.
3.  An efficient backpropagation through time (BPTT) algorithm that avoids the O(NT) space complexity of caching the entire memory history. Instead, it stores only the sparse memory modifications at each step (O(T) space) and "rolls back" the memory state during the backward pass.

The authors demonstrate experimentally that SAM achieves orders-of-magnitude improvements in speed (up to 1,600x) and memory usage (up to 3,000x) over dense models. Crucially, this efficiency gain does not come at the cost of learning ability; SAM performs comparably or even better than dense counterparts on a range of tasks, including algorithmic problems, the bAbI question answering dataset, and one-shot learning on Omniglot. The paper also shows that the approach can be generalized to other MANNs by creating a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed architecture is well-motivated, and the claims of asymptotic complexity improvements are well-supported by both theoretical arguments (in the supplement) and extensive empirical benchmarks. The experimental methodology is sound. The authors wisely include a "Dense Access Memory" (DAM) model as a control, allowing for a direct comparison between their sparse access scheme and a dense equivalent, effectively isolating the impact of the sparse approximation. The results are strong and convincing, particularly the curriculum learning experiments that show SAM scaling to sequence lengths and memory sizes far beyond the capabilities of previous models. The work appears complete and carefully executed.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is stated clearly in the introduction, and the proposed architecture is explained in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. Figure 1 provides a powerful and immediate illustration of the practical benefits of the sparse approach. The descriptions of the sparse read, write, and efficient BPTT mechanisms are clear and provide sufficient detail. The overall structure makes the paper's significant contributions readily apparent to the reader.

**Originality**
This work presents a novel and practical solution to a well-known problem in the field. While the idea of using sparsity to scale attention is not entirely new (e.g., RL-based discrete attention), this paper's contribution of an efficient, end-to-end differentiable sparse mechanism is highly original. The integration of classic data structures like ANNs into the forward pass of a neural network for content-based addressing is a creative and effective idea. The "rollback" BPTT method is also a clever engineering contribution that is crucial for the overall space efficiency. This work provides a distinct and compelling alternative to prior approaches for scaling MANNs.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is very high. MANNs hold great promise for tasks requiring long-term memory and algorithmic reasoning, but their practical application has been severely limited by their poor scalability. By providing a solution that dramatically improves computational and memory efficiency by several orders of magnitude, this paper unlocks the potential for these models to be applied to much larger and more complex problems, such as processing entire documents or knowledge bases. The ideas presented are likely to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers working on attention mechanisms and large-scale memory models. The demonstration that this approach also applies to the DNC architecture further highlights its generality and importance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a major, well-defined bottleneck in an important class of models (MANNs).
*   The proposed solution is novel, technically sound, and remarkably effective, providing multiple orders-of-magnitude improvement in speed and memory usage.
*   The experimental validation is thorough, convincing, and covers a diverse set of tasks.
*   The paper is very clearly written and the contributions are easy to understand.
*   The work is of high significance and is likely to have a substantial impact on future research in this area.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The chosen write mechanism (update last-read or LRU) is simpler and potentially less expressive than the content-based writes in the original NTM. While effective on the tested tasks, a discussion of its potential limitations would be beneficial.
*   The reliance on non-differentiable ANN structures for reads means the network cannot learn the distance metric used for retrieval. This is a reasonable trade-off for efficiency, but its implications could be discussed in more detail.

**Detailed comments and suggestions for the authors:**
1.  The trade-offs of the write mechanism could be briefly discussed. The chosen scheme is simple and efficient, but how might it compare to a sparse content-based write on tasks that require updating information that has not been recently read?
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of K, the number of nearest neighbors for the read operation? Is the model's performance sensitive to this hyperparameter? A short comment on this in the experimental section or supplement would be helpful.
3.  The paper states that no gradients are passed through the ANN index. This is a key design choice for efficiency. It would be interesting to add a sentence or two in the discussion about the potential implications of using a fixed, non-learned similarity search, as opposed to the fully learned soft attention in dense models.
4.  In Figure 3, the distinction between "SAM linear" and "SAM ANN" is important. While it is explained, perhaps this distinction could be introduced more explicitly when the model is first described in Section 3 to prepare the reader.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses are very minor and are primarily suggestions for small additions to the discussion. I strongly recommend acceptance.
