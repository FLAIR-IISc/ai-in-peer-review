- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a differentiable MANN with sparse reads/writes and ANN indices for sublinear content addressing.  
- Introduces a rollback-based BPTT that logs sparse updates, achieving O(1) per-step space and O(log N) access time while preserving NTM-like functionality.  
- Evaluated on NTM algorithmic tasks, large curricula, bAbI (and SDNC), and Omniglot, showing comparable data efficiency and up to 1000x speed/memory savings.  
- Builds on NTMs, Memory Networks, and DNC, contrasting with RL-based discrete addressing approaches.  
- Claims clear: asymptotically optimal time/space, large empirical efficiency gains, and retained learning efficacy.  
- Empirical support is substantial (wall-clock benchmarks, millions of slots) but statistical rigor is limited (few runs, few variances reported).  
- Impact of sparsification and ANN approximation on gradients/accuracy is only qualitatively assessed, not quantitatively.  
- Complexity proofs are mainly in the supplement; a main-text sketch would help reader understanding.  
- Paper is well organized with precise method descriptions, but implementation choices (ε, K, ANN rebuild policy) lack systematic explanation.  
- Reproducibility may be hindered by reliance on specialized data structures not fully detailed.  
- Novel contributions: embedding ANN indices in differentiable memory access and rollback-based sparse BPTT; SDNC shows generality.  
- Potentially significant: addresses a key MANN bottleneck and enables large-memory applications if robust; current validation is mostly synthetic/small-scale.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled sparsity with asymptotic guarantees; large empirical efficiency gains without data-efficiency loss; generalizable to other MANNs with strong task results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited real-world evaluations; insufficient analysis of approximation error and hyperparameter sensitivity; reproducibility risks from complex indexing details.  
- Constructive suggestions: add ablations on K/ε/ANN type/rebuild frequency and report mean±std across seeds; quantify approximation error vs dense attention; release code and data structures with hardware details; include a real-world large-memory experiment (e.g., long-context QA or retrieval-augmented LM).
