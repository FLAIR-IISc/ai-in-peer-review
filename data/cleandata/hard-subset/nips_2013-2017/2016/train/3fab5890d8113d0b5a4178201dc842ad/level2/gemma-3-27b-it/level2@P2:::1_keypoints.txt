- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented NN that restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset and uses efficient data structures for content-based addressing.  
- Demonstrates large efficiency gains (reported ~1000x speedup, ~3000x memory reduction) while maintaining comparable task performance.  
- Adapts the sparse access approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer, showing broader applicability.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides enough detail (with supplementary refs) for reproduction.  
- Strong empirical results across synthetic tasks and an Omniglot one-shot learning task, including scaling benchmarks.  
- Claims asymptotic optimality in space/time complexity, lending theoretical support to the method.  
- Thorough comparisons with NTM, DAM, and related models in experiments.  
- Insufficient detail on ANN (FLANN vs LSH) parameter tuning and how ANN accuracy vs speed tradeoffs affect overall performance.  
- Lacks detailed ablation studies to quantify contributions of components (write scheme, usage metric, etc.).  
- Heavy reliance on curriculum learning for scaling experiments; sensitivity to curriculum schedules is not explored.  
- Practical implementation complexity and engineering effort for the claimed O(1) per-step space/runtime are under-discussed.  
- Limited comparison to the very recent literature on sparse memory access beyond classic MANNs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a major scalability bottleneck, provides a compelling and effective solution with strong evidence, and is well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical adoption may be hindered by implementation complexity, limited ablation analysis, and dependence on curriculum learning.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper meriting acceptance with minor revisions addressing ANN implementation details, ablation studies, and practical implementation challenges.
