- Problem: Standard MANNs scale poorly—O(N) per step and O(NT) for BPTT—preventing large-memory and long-term applications.  
- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) constrains memory interactions to a small constant subset per time step.  
- Sparse reads: read only K nearest neighbors via an ANN structure (e.g., k-d tree/LSH) for O(log N) access instead of soft attention over all N.  
- Sparse writes: update a constant number of locations by writing to previously read slots or overwriting the least-recently-used (LRU) slot.  
- Efficient BPTT: store sparse memory modifications and “rollback” to reconstruct past memory states, reducing space to O(T).  
- Empirical results: >1000x speedups and >3000x memory reductions versus dense models while maintaining comparable or better learning performance.  
- Tasks evaluated: algorithmic problems, bAbI QA, Omniglot one-shot learning, and successful adaptation to the DNC.  
- Significance: enables scaling MANNs to millions of slots and very long sequences, opening new application domains.  
- Originality: novel integration of ANN data structures into end-to-end differentiable models and a clever rollback for BPTT.  
- Quality: strong theoretical motivation and extensive empirical validation, including scaling curricula and clear benchmarks.  
- Clarity: well-written, well-organized paper with effective figures and explanations.  
- Weakness — approximation analysis: needs ablation on sensitivity to K and discussion of tasks where distant low-similarity memories matter.  
- Weakness — ANN maintenance: should discuss practical cost and frequency of rebuilding the ANN index and its impact on training time.  
- Weakness — write design: the heuristic LRU/hard-erasure write scheme departs from learned erase vectors and needs justification and discussion of potential risks (e.g., overwriting infrequently accessed but important memories).
