- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable sparse memory architecture achieving asymptotically optimal space/time by restricting reads/writes to a sparse subset.  
- Core claims: ~3,000× memory reduction and ~1,000× training speedup vs dense MANNs; comparable learning efficiency on synthetic tasks, Omniglot, and bAbI QA; scales to 100k+ time steps via curriculum learning.  
- Strong empirical support: Fig.1 shows large speed/memory gains (e.g., 1,600× faster with k-d trees; 53MB vs 29GB for 64K words).  
- Curriculum experiments (Fig.3) demonstrate scalability to >4,000-step sequences and generalization to 200k-step sequences with millions of memory slots.  
- Omniglot and bAbI results indicate SAM matches or exceeds dense baselines in data efficiency (e.g., 0.2 vs 0.4 errors on Omniglot with 100 characters).  
- Theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) justifies SAM’s asymptotic optimality.  
- Strengths: compelling empirical validation, clear technical contributions (sparse write policy, efficient backprop), practical relevance for deploying MANNs.  
- SDNC extension (Supplementary D) shows the approach generalizes beyond the core model.  
- Weakness: incomplete related-work comparison—need explicit contrast to Zaremba & Sutskever (2015) RL-based sparse NTM and trade-offs between differentiable vs RL-based sparsity (gradient variance vs sparsity).  
- Weakness: ANN implementation details lack ablation studies (k-d trees vs LSH), sensitivity to K (number of sparse reads), and long-term balancing/rebuild costs (Section 3.5).  
- Weakness: the "1,000× faster" claim depends on memory size and should be clarified with scaling behavior.  
- Recommended additions: direct comparison to Zaremba et al.’s RL-NTM on learning stability and gradient quality.  
- Recommended additions: ablations for K and ANN rebuild frequency to quantify sensitivity and long-term costs.  
- Recommended additions: provide scaling curves to clarify and qualify the "1,000×" speedup claim.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound, addresses a key MANN bottleneck, significant practical impact—accept with minor revisions.
