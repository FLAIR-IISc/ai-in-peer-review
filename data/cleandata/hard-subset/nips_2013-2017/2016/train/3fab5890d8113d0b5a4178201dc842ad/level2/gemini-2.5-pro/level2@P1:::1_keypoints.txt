- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale MANNs by enforcing sparsity in memory reads/writes.  
- Sparse read attends to a small constant K nearest neighbors via an ANN index, reducing read time from O(N) to O(log N).  
- Sparse write updates either previously read locations or the LRU slot, keeping write updates constant-time.  
- Efficient BPTT stores only sparse memory modifications per step and rolls back during backward pass to avoid O(NT) space.  
- Empirically reports up to 1,600x speed and 3,000x memory improvements over dense models without loss of learning ability.  
- Demonstrates comparable or superior performance on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and one-shot learning on Omniglot.  
- Extends approach to create a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), showing generality.  
- Technical quality is high: asymptotic claims are supported theoretically and validated with thorough experiments, including a dense-control (DAM).  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with effective figures and explanations of core mechanisms.  
- Original contribution: novel end-to-end differentiable sparse mechanism combining ANN data structures and a rollback BPTT.  
- Significance is high: removes a major scalability bottleneck in MANNs and enables application to much larger problems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a key bottleneck, is novel and effective, thoroughly validated, clearly written, and highly impactful.  
- Main concern: the write mechanism (last-read or LRU) is simpler and potentially less expressive than content-based writes.  
- Main concern: use of non-differentiable ANN indices prevents learning the retrieval distance metric; trade-offs should be discussed.  
- Suggest discussing trade-offs of the write scheme vs. sparse content-based writes for cases requiring updates to unseen locations.  
- Suggest reporting sensitivity to K (number of nearest neighbors) and commenting on its selection in experiments or supplement.  
- Suggest adding a short discussion on implications of fixed, non-learned ANN similarity vs. learned soft attention.  
- Suggest introducing the distinction between "SAM linear" and "SAM ANN" earlier in the paper for clarity.
