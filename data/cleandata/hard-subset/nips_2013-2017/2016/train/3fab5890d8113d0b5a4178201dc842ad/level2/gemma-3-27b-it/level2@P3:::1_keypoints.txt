- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN that constrains read/write to a sparse subset of memory locations to improve scalability.  
- Core idea: sparse reads/writes reduce computational and memory overhead compared to dense MANNs.  
- Theoretical analysis claims asymptotic optimality in space and time complexity.  
- Empirical results report large speedups (~1000x) and memory savings (~3000x) versus non-sparse models.  
- Evaluated on synthetic tasks, Omniglot, and bAbI, showing competitive performance and ability to scale to longer sequences.  
- Demonstrates adaptability by integrating SAM concepts into existing architectures like the DNC.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and well-organized with useful supplementary material.  
- Theoretical contributions are a strong point, reinforcing the scalability argument.  
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse tasks adds credibility to claims.  
- Lacks detailed, quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art methods on bAbI despite claiming improvements.  
- Choice of ANN methods (randomized k-d trees, LSH) is only briefly justified; trade-offs and alternatives are underexplored.  
- Impact of ANN choice on accuracy and speedup is not fully analyzed.  
- Explanation of the usage statistic U(2)T is unclear and could use a more intuitive description.  
- Mentions advanced ANN options (Mondrian/LSH forests) but does not explain why they were not investigated.  
- Claim of asymptotic optimality would benefit from more rigorous proofs and explicit assumptions.  
- Effect of sparsity level K on performance and efficiency needs systematic empirical investigation.  
- Paper lacks discussion of potential limitations or scenarios where sparse access could harm performance.  
- Recommendation: qualified accept, contingent on addressing comparative analysis and ANN methodological concerns.
