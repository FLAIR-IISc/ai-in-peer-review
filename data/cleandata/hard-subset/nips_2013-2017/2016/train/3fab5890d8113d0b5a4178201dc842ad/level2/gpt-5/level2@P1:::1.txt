Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales to very large memories by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes. Reads are performed by content-based addressing over only the top-K similar memory slots, obtained via an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), while writes are restricted to previously read locations and the least-recently-used (LRU) location tracked by a usage statistic. The authors also introduce an efficient backpropagation-through-time scheme that stores and reverts only sparse modifications, yielding O(1) per-step memory overhead. Under assumptions about content-based addressing, they argue SAM achieves asymptotically optimal time O(log N) per step (with ANN) and O(1) per-step space.

Empirically, SAM matches or improves data efficiency versus dense NTMs and a dense variant (DAM) on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), trains on curricula to sequence lengths >4k steps with memory sizes in the millions, and shows large wall-clock and physical memory gains (up to 1600× faster and 3000× less memory). On Omniglot one-shot classification, SAM outperforms dense baselines, presumably due to capacity. The approach is extended to a sparse DNC (SDNC) in the supplement, which achieves strong bAbI results without supervised memory access.

The work builds on NTMs (Graves et al.), Memory Networks/End-to-End Memory Networks (Weston; Sukhbaatar), and the DNC, and contrasts with discrete addressing via RL (Zaremba & Sutskever), positioning sparsity and ANN as a scalable alternative while retaining differentiability.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled design for sparse reads/writes that preserves differentiable training.
- Strong complexity analysis and an implementation that realizes the promised savings; the rollback-based BPTT is a practical contribution.
- Extensive empirical evidence across synthetic algorithmic tasks, long-horizon curricula, Omniglot episodes, and bAbI (via SDNC).
- Demonstrated generality: adaptation to DNC; ANN choices discussed; practical engineering details (index maintenance, rebuilds).
- The results are significant: orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime and memory, enabling regimes (e.g., millions of slots, >4k steps) that are infeasible for dense MANNs.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The “asymptotically optimal” claim hinges on specific assumptions (content-based addressing, K = O(1), index behavior); the main text sketches this but defers proof to the supplement. A clearer statement of assumptions and limits in the main paper would help.
- Approximations in ANN and sparse top-K reads introduce non-differentiabilities in the selection stage. While gradients are passed to selected slots, only K slots receive gradient per step; the paper would benefit from deeper analysis/ablations on how K and ANN recall affect convergence and final performance.
- Comparisons on curriculum scaling sometimes use vastly different memory sizes (dense models at 64 slots vs SAM at 2×10^6). While this demonstrates the core point (dense models are infeasible at large N), it complicates conclusions about data efficiency or “learning the algorithm.” A controlled study that holds total memory equal where feasible, or reports performance as a function of N for each method, would be helpful.
- The write policy (interpolate between previous reads and LRU) is a specific heuristic. It might limit tasks that require broad, simultaneous updates or structured writes. An ablation of the two components (write-to-read vs LRU) and the erase policy would clarify necessity.
- Real-world scope is limited. Omniglot one-shot is a good step, but language modeling or large QA corpora would strengthen the case for practical impact. bAbI is synthetic; the strong SDNC result is in the supplement and not fully detailed in the main text.

Quality:
Technically sound overall. The system design aligns with the complexity analysis. The engineering choices (ANN, sparse formats, rollback BPTT) are carefully integrated, and the experiments are diverse. Some experimental comparisons could be more controlled (capacity-matched baselines), and sensitivity analyses (K, ANN type, rebuild frequency, threshold ε for usage) would strengthen the empirical case. It would also help to report ANN recall and its correlation with task performance.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The read/write mechanisms, controller interface, and backprop rollback are clearly described, with useful pointers to the supplement. Suggestions:
- Move the key assumptions for the optimality claim from Supplementary A into the main text.
- Include a small ablation figure on K and ANN type (k-d vs LSH) if space allows.
- Clarify the exact protocol for index rebuilds and their amortized cost during long training.

Originality:
High. While sparse memory access and ANN are known ideas, their integration into an end-to-end differentiable MANN with a workable training recipe, efficient BPTT, and demonstrated scaling is novel and timely. The extension to the DNC further supports generality.

Significance:
High. Scaling MANNs is a central bottleneck. This work provides a practical path to orders-of-magnitude larger memories with competitive learning behavior, enabling tasks beyond reach of dense counterparts. It is likely to influence subsequent research and practice on memory architectures.

Questions and requests to authors:
- Please report sensitivity to K (e.g., K = 2, 4, 8, 16) and to ANN recall (vary tree count/hash count) on at least one task.
- How often are ANN rebuilds triggered in long runs, and what is the measured amortized overhead?
- Can you report the distribution of “write-to-read” vs “write-to-LRU” usage over training, and an ablation removing each component?
- How robust is SAM to higher-dimensional word sizes M? When do k-d trees become inferior to LSH in practice?
- For the curriculum experiments, could you include a plot of generalization error vs sequence length for capacity-matched settings (where possible) or provide a discussion of fairness?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong and practical contribution addressing a key scalability barrier.
- Solid empirical validation and impressive efficiency improvements.
- Method is general and compatible with multiple MANN architectures.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some theoretical claims are deferred; main-text assumptions could be clearer.
- Limited real-world tasks beyond Omniglot; bAbI result for SDNC is supplementary.
- Missing ablations on critical hyperparameters (K, ANN, write policy).

Overall assessment:
An impactful and carefully executed paper that advances the scalability of differentiable memory systems. Despite some missing ablations and limited real-world breadth, the contribution is substantial and timely. I lean toward acceptance and encourage the authors to add brief sensitivity analyses and clarify assumptions in the final version.
