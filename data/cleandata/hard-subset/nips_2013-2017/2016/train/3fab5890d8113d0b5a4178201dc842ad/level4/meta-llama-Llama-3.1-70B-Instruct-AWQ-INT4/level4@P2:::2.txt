The paper proposes a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to improve the scalability of neural networks augmented with external memory. The authors demonstrate that SAM retains the representational power of the original approaches while training efficiently with very large memories. The key innovations of SAM include sparse reads and writes to external memory, which allow for computational and memory-efficient operation.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of scaling memory-augmented neural networks is an important one. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the limitations of existing approaches and how SAM addresses these limitations. The technical contributions of the paper, including efficient backpropagation and approximate nearest neighbor search using sparse access memory, are well-explained and easy to follow.

The experimental results are impressive, showing significant speedups and memory efficiency improvements over existing models. The authors also demonstrate the ability of SAM to learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition.

However, I have some reservations about the technical strength of the paper. While the ideas presented are interesting and well-executed, they are not particularly novel or groundbreaking. The use of sparse matrices and approximate nearest neighbor search is not new, and the application of these techniques to memory-augmented neural networks, while useful, does not seem to require a significant amount of new or innovative thinking.

Furthermore, I am concerned that the paper may not be a good fit for NIPS, which tends to prioritize papers with more significant technical contributions. While the paper is well-written and easy to follow, it may not be substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a top-tier conference like NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel memory access scheme that improves the scalability of neural networks augmented with external memory.
* The experimental results are impressive, showing significant speedups and memory efficiency improvements over existing models.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The technical contributions of the paper, while useful, are not particularly novel or groundbreaking.
* The paper may not be a good fit for NIPS, which tends to prioritize papers with more significant technical contributions.
* The ideas presented, while interesting, do not seem to require a significant amount of new or innovative thinking.

Overall, while the paper is well-written and the experimental results are impressive, I do not think it is technically strong enough for acceptance at NIPS.
