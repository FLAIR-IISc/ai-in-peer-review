This paper proposes a novel architecture called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) for neural networks augmented with external memory, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks (MNs). The authors introduce a sparse read and write scheme that utilizes efficient data structures, resulting in significant improvements in runtime and memory efficiency compared to existing models. The proposed architecture achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, making it an attractive solution for large-scale applications.

The paper relates to previous work on NTMs and MNs, which have shown promise in learning algorithmic solutions to complex tasks. However, these models have been limited by their poor scalability in both space and time as the amount of memory grows. The authors address this limitation by introducing a sparse memory access scheme that retains the representational power of the original approaches while allowing for efficient training with large memories.

The strengths of the paper include its well-motivated introduction, clear explanation of the proposed architecture, and comprehensive evaluation on various tasks. The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory reductions compared to existing models, making it a promising solution for large-scale applications. Additionally, the paper shows that SAM can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition.

The weaknesses of the paper include the lack of a detailed discussion on the reasons behind the drastic performance improvements in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to sequences much longer than those observed in training. While the authors attribute the improvements to the sparse memory access scheme, a more in-depth analysis of the underlying mechanisms would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models in the field.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed architecture and potential avenues for future research.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the organization is logical and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background information on NTMs and MNs, making the paper accessible to readers who may not be familiar with these models. However, some of the technical details, such as the implementation of the sparse memory access scheme, could be clarified further.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel architecture that addresses a significant limitation of existing models. The use of sparse memory access and efficient data structures is a unique contribution that sets the paper apart from previous work in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact on the field of neural networks augmented with memory. The proposed architecture could enable the use of these models in large-scale applications, which could have far-reaching implications for areas such as natural language processing and computer vision.
