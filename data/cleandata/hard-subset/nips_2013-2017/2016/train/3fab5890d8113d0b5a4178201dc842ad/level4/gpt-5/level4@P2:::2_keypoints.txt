- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) does sparse reads/writes to K memory slots, uses ANN (randomized k-d trees/LSH) for top-K reads, and an in-place sparse rollback for BPTT to improve scalability.  
- Claims: Asymptotically optimal time/space relative to memory size while preserving end-to-end training via backpropagation.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Memory Networks, NTMs, and DNC, differing from RL-based discrete addressing by staying mostly differentiable and integrating ANN indices.  
- Strength: Clear motivation addressing MANNs’ linear content lookup and O(NT) BPTT memory bottlenecks.  
- Strength: Practical engineering (sparse rollback + ANN) yields large empirical speed/memory gains (up to ~1000× speed, ~3000× memory).  
- Strength: Demonstrates scalability to very large memories and long sequences (associative recall >4k steps, generalization to 200k) without truncated BPTT.  
- Strength: Broad experiments (synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI, Omniglot) and an SDNC variant with strong bAbI performance without supervised memory access.  
- Strength: Technique is potentially generalizable to other memory architectures.  
- Weakness: Incremental technical novelty—primarily system design/integration of known techniques rather than new theory.  
- Weakness: Differentiability concerns—top-K selection and ANN are non-differentiable and the paper lacks thorough analysis of gradient effects or surrogate-gradient justification.  
- Weakness: Asymptotic optimality claim depends on supplementary assumptions; main text lacks clear statement of conditions/constants and lower-bound comparisons.  
- Weakness: Baseline comparisons may be unfair—SAM often has far larger capacity than dense baselines, conflating architectural gains with capacity advantages.  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of ANN index issues (imbalance, rebuild frequency) and missing ablations on K, index choice, rebuild cadence and their accuracy/speed trade-offs.  
- Weakness: No evaluation on large-scale, real-world tasks (e.g., long-context QA or language modeling) despite such motivations.  
- Quality: Methods and engineering appear sound, but theoretical claims are underdeveloped and empirical reporting/ablations could be clearer for reproducibility.  
- Clarity: Well written overall; core algorithms are clear but critical implementation/theoretical details are deferred to the supplement.  
- Originality: Effective integration of existing ideas; useful but not fundamentally novel; SDNC extension demonstrates generality.  
- Significance: Important scalability advance that could enable longer-sequence work, but immediate impact limited by lack of large-scale task evaluation.  
- Suggested improvements: Add main-text theory on differentiability, expand asymptotic analysis, include ablations (K/index/rebuild), strengthen matched-resource baselines, add a large-scale real-world task, and clarify hardware/software and baseline optimization (release code).  
- Pro-accept arguments: Addresses core MANN scalability, shows large speed/memory gains, strong empirical results including SDNC on bAbI, and broadly applicable technique.  
- Contra-accept arguments: Limited conceptual novelty, incomplete theoretical treatment, insufficient baselines/ablations, and no large-scale demonstrations.  
- Recommendation: Leaning toward rejection in current form; could be acceptable with stronger theory, fairer/broader comparisons, and at least one large-scale application.
