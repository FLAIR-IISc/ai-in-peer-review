- Addresses scalability of MANNs (e.g., NTMs) where read/write costs are O(N) and BPTT space is O(NT), making large memories impractical.
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that restricts reads/writes to a small constant K of memory locations to reduce overhead.
- Sparse reads use Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) structures (k-d trees/LSH) to retrieve K nearest neighbors, lowering read time to O(log N).
- Sparse writes target recently read locations or the least-recently-used slot to maintain sparsity and simple memory management.
- Introduces a “rollback” BPTT that stores only sparse updates per timestep, reducing per-timestep space from O(N) to O(1).
- Evaluated on NTM algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning benchmarks.
- Reports massive empirical gains: up to ~1,600× speedups and ~3,000× memory reductions while matching or exceeding dense baselines.
- Demonstrates scaling to sequence lengths of ~200,000 and millions of memory cells using curriculum training.
- Asymptotic complexity improvements are well-argued and supported in the supplementary material.
- Empirical validation is extensive and rigorous, with strong baselines (NTM, DAM) and convincing scaling experiments.
- Paper is clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Originality is debatable: uses well-known ANN and sparse-update ideas; novelty lies in their integration into an end-to-end differentiable MANN.
- Significance is high: solves a major practical barrier for MANNs and could enable new large-scale applications and research directions.
- Main weakness: limited fundamental algorithmic novelty—primarily engineering and integration of existing techniques.
- Overall recommendation: strong acceptance—practical impact and empirical gains outweigh concerns about novelty.
