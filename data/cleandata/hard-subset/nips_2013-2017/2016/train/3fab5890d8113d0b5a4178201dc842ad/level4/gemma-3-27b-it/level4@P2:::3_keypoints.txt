- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which constrains reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words to scale memory-augmented neural networks.
- Establishes asymptotic optimality of SAM in space and time complexity.
- Shows substantial speedups and reduced memory usage compared to Neural Turing Machines (Section 4.1).
- Demonstrates effectiveness on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) (Section 4.2).
- Applies SAM to a real-world dataset and reports positive results, including Omniglot (Sections 4.4/4.5).
- Finds that sparse reads/writes can improve early-stage learning in some scenarios.
- Provides a clear Torch7 implementation and detailed architectural description in supplementary material.
- Shows generality by adapting the approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) in the supplement.
- Strength: novel, well-motivated solution addressing scalability in memory-augmented networks.
- Strength: solid theoretical justification coupled with strong empirical performance.
- Weakness: limited exploration and discussion of alternative ANN index structures and their performance impact.
- Weakness: lacks deeper analysis of when and why sparsity aids or harms learning.
- Weakness: could provide a more detailed comparison to other memory-scaling approaches (e.g., RL-based addressing).
- Overall assessment: strong, well-executed contribution with compelling results; recommends acceptance despite minor gaps.
