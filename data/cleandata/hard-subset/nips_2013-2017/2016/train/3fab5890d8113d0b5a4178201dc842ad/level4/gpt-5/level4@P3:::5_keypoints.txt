- Proposal: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN enforcing sparse reads/writes and using ANN indices for O(log N) reads and O(1) writes with in-place reversible updates for efficient BPTT.  
- Theoretical claims: Achieves O(log N) time per step and O(1) additional space per time step after O(N) init, with asymptotic lower bounds argued in supplement.  
- Practical variant: Adapts approach to Differentiable Neural Computer to create a sparse DNC (SDNC).  
- Empirical gains: Reports up to ~1000× speedup and ~3000× memory savings vs. dense variants while maintaining or improving task performance.  
- Tasks and scaling: Demonstrates strong results on algorithmic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), bAbI (best unsupervised-access SDNC), and Omniglot one-shot; trains over >4k BPTT steps and generalizes to far longer sequences.  
- Significance: Addresses central MANN bottleneck (compute and memory scaling), enabling training with millions of slots and very long BPTT.  
- Originality: Novel combination of sparse read/write, online ANN in training loop, LRU allocation, and reversible sparse updates.  
- Quality: Coherent system design with plausible complexity analysis and broad empirical evaluation including systems benchmarks.  
- Clarity: Architectural description is generally clear; paper distinguishes SAM from dense baselines and includes useful supplement details.  
- Missing diagnosis: Lacks analysis explaining why sparsity can improve learning and long-range generalization; needs ablations on K, allocation policy, and sparsity thresholds.  
- ANN trade-offs: Does not sufficiently quantify ANN recall, sensitivity to rebuild frequency, or exact vs. approximate read accuracy–speed trade-offs.  
- Fairness of comparisons: Some baselines use far smaller memory (e.g., dense 64 slots vs. SAM 2e6 slots); need comparisons at equivalent compute/memory budgets and ablations varying SAM memory and K.  
- Optimality claims: Asymptotic optimality is compelling but needs concise intuition and spelled-out assumptions in main text rather than only supplement.  
- Practical training details: Long-sequence BPTT experiments lack specifics (optimizer, gradient clipping, initialization, truncation) needed for reproducibility and stability assessment.  
- Clarity/notation issues: Section 3 should introduce data structures and rollback earlier, unify sparse/dense notation, clarify LRU erase behavior, and fix minor typos.  
- Requests for authors: Provide ablations on K, memory size, and exact vs. ANN reads; report ANN recall and rebuild sensitivity; clarify long-BPTT training details; discuss why sparsity aids generalization; summarize optimality assumptions and release code/pseudocode.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Principled, general solution to a core scalability problem with strong empirical and practical systems gains applicable across MANNs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient ablations/diagnostics, potential confounding from different memory capacities in comparisons, and under-quantified ANN stability/online behavior.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — technically sound, original, and practically significant with convincing experiments; minor analysis and clarity additions would further strengthen the paper.
