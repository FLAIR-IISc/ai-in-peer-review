- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable memory architecture that addresses MANN scalability limits.  
- Constrains reads/writes to a small sparse subset (K ≈ 4–8) and uses ANN structures plus a usage tracker for efficient slot allocation.  
- Achieves asymptotic optimality: O(log N) time per timestep, O(N) space initialization, and O(1) space per timestep during training.  
- Shows SAM retains the representational power of dense MANNs while enabling large efficiency gains.  
- Empirical highlights: ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× memory reduction vs. NTM on 64,000-word tasks.  
- Matches dense model data efficiency on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Scales to very long sequences (100,000+ timesteps) and enables previously infeasible training regimes.  
- SDNC variant achieves best reported results on Babi tasks (solves all but one without supervised memory access).  
- Ablation studies and curriculum experiments demonstrate sparsity does not impair learning and can improve performance (e.g., Priority Sort).  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with detailed implementation (CSR for gradients, kd-trees/LSH) and reproducible supplementary material.  
- Original and significant: sparsity-based approach generalizes beyond NTMs and avoids high-variance gradient estimators.  
- Minor weakness: ANN limitations (e.g., k-d tree imbalance during online training) could be discussed more, with explicit tradeoff comparisons (accuracy vs. speed).  
- Overall: technically robust, impactful, and a strong candidate for acceptance.
