This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme designed to address the scalability limitations of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs). The core idea is to retain the representational power of models like the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while achieving significant improvements in space and time complexity during training with large memories. This is a well-motivated problem, as MANNs, despite their promise for learning algorithmic solutions, struggle with real-world applications due to their computational demands. The authors demonstrate asymptotic lower bounds in complexity and report substantial empirical speedups (1,000x faster, 3,000x less memory) compared to non-sparse models. The work is well-explained and demonstrates a strong technical understanding of existing approaches like NTMs [7] and Memory Networks [21].

The paper's strengths are numerous. The technical approach of thresholding memory modifications to a sparse subset, combined with efficient data structures for content-based read operations, is both novel and appears carefully considered. The thorough experimental evaluation, spanning synthetic tasks (inspired by [7]) and one-shot Omniglot character recognition ([16, 12]), convincingly demonstrates the benefits of SAM. Scaling to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and large memories is a significant achievement. The extension to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) [8], resulting in the Sparse DNC (SDNC), further highlights the generality of the proposed approach and achieves state-of-the-art results on the Babi tasks. The detailed analysis of space and time complexity (see Supplementary A) is commendable.

However, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. While the paper clearly states the advantages of SAM, a more in-depth comparison to recent work on memory compression or alternative sparse memory access methods (beyond the DAM variant presented) would be beneficial. The choice of hyperparameters, particularly for the ANN indexes (FLANN and LSH), is briefly mentioned but could benefit from further justification. While the ablation studies included are good, exploring the impact of different sparsity levels (K values) more systematically might provide further insight. Additionally, the discussion around the ANN choices – k-d trees for small word sizes and LSH for large – could be expanded with more detail regarding the transition point and potential performance trade-offs.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** SAM introduces a genuinely novel and effective approach to scaling MANNs.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically rigorous, with clear theoretical justifications and strong empirical validation.
*   **Significance:** The ability to train MANNs with significantly larger memories opens up new possibilities for tackling complex sequential tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
*   **Impact:** The results are impactful and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Alternative Sparse Methods:** The paper could benefit from a more extensive comparison to other relevant techniques.
*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Further justification for the ANN hyperparameter choices would strengthen the analysis.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The technical innovation, thorough evaluation, and clear presentation make it a compelling submission for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
