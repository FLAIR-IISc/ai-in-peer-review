This paper proposes an efficient method for computing attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) using fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations. The authors demonstrate through experiments that this approach does not compromise training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. While the novelty of this work is somewhat limited, as similar ideas have been explored in the context of memory networks (e.g., section 3.3 of http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), the application of these techniques to writable memories is a notable contribution. Despite the lack of a conceptual breakthrough, the practical aspects of implementing this method are worth reporting.

I recommend accepting this paper, but I suggest several revisions to improve its clarity and impact. Firstly, I found it challenging to understand the task descriptions, which are crucial for replicating the results. Given that the code for the tasks from the original NTM paper has not been released and there is no standard version of the tasks, it is essential to provide detailed descriptions of the task construction. I would also appreciate it if the authors could commit to releasing the code for their experiments to facilitate reproducibility.

Secondly, I would like the authors to discuss the limitations and potential failure cases of their approach. The model relies on the argmax (or K-argmax) operation, which is not smooth. It would be interesting to analyze how this affects the model's performance, particularly in comparison to fully smooth models. The authors may want to investigate whether the choice of K=1 or the specific task contributes to the model's success. A more in-depth analysis of the failure cases and the types of tasks or setups that allow their training to work would significantly enhance the paper's value. Furthermore, I suggest removing section 3.6, as it does not add substantial insights to the paper.

Additionally, I recommend that the authors avoid referring to the Omniglot data as "non-synthetic" or "real-world." While Omniglot is a widely used benchmark, it is still a synthetic dataset.

Overall, this paper presents a practical contribution to the development of efficient NTMs, and with the suggested revisions, it has the potential to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's strengths and limitations.
