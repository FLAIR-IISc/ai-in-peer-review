

The authors employ fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine. They present experiments demonstrating that this approach does not compromise training performance and enables training on larger-scale problems. However, the novelty is limited, as evidenced in Section 3.3 of the memory networks paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf); multiple memory network papers already utilize millions of memory items with hashing for scalable lookups. While the practical implementation for writeable memories appears novel—though lacking a conceptual leap—the work's contribution in making this approach functional merits reporting. I recommend acceptance but require revisions:  
1. Reproducibility is challenging due to insufficient task descriptions. Given the absence of released code for original NTM tasks and no standardized task definitions, the paper must provide meticulous task construction details. Additionally, authors should commit to releasing their experimental code.  
2. The paper should analyze failure scenarios and limitations. The model's reliance on non-smooth argmax (or K-argmax) operations—unlike smooth alternatives in prior work (e.g., http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275)—raises questions: Does performance degradation occur due to K=1 (as in MemNN-WSH, http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), task characteristics, or the discrete action assumption? A discussion of where this method succeeds/fails would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, remove Section 3.6 and discontinue describing Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world."
