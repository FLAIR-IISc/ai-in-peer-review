- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme improving efficiency while retaining NTM representational power.  
- Tackles the scalability problem of memory-augmented neural networks in both time and space.  
- Positions the work relative to previous MANN research (NTMs, Memory Networks) with clear comparisons.  
- Proposes technical innovations: sparse read/write operations, efficient data structures, and approximate nearest neighbor indexing.  
- Shows substantial improvements in computational and memory efficiency for large memories.  
- Preserves end-to-end differentiability and the ability to learn algorithmic solutions.  
- Demonstrates generality by adapting SAM to other models, e.g., the Differentiable Neural Computer.  
- Weakness: sparse approximations may fail or be ineffective on certain tasks or domains.  
- Lacks sufficient analysis of trade-offs between sparse access benefits and potential information loss.  
- Could use more experimental results and broader comparisons with other models.  
- Technically sound with thorough time and space complexity analysis and supported claims.  
- Well-written, well-organized, and easy to understand.  
- Original: introduces SAM and a novel combination of sparse access with ANN indexing.  
- Significant potential impact across applications (language modeling, machine translation) and advances MANN research.  
- Reviewer leans toward acceptance for novelty, technical quality, clarity, and impact, but notes concerns about sparse approximations and experimental breadth.
