- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) claiming large computational (1000x) and memory (3000x) gains while preserving representational power.
- Experimental results show strong speed and memory improvements on benchmarks but are not convincing enough for acceptance.
- Core idea—sparse access and ANN for efficiency—builds on established sparse representation and ANN techniques, lacking novelty.
- Efficient backpropagation via sparse gradients and ANN approximation are technically straightforward and borrow from existing methods.
- Paper fails to explain why sparsity does not degrade performance or how SAM generalizes beyond tested benchmarks.
- No analysis of error bounds, sensitivity to sparsity levels, or comparisons to state-of-the-art sparse memory methods.
- Reported speedups and memory reductions lack rigorous baseline comparisons to optimized implementations (e.g., NTM, DNC).
- Unclear whether reported gains come from SAM itself or from experimental simplifications (model size, task complexity); training vs inference conflation.
- Missing ablation studies to isolate SAM’s effect from other variables.
- Theoretical justification is underdeveloped; claims of retained representational power lack formal proof or empirical trade-off curves.
- Contributions are viewed as incremental and derivative, not meeting NIPS standards for novelty and rigor.
- Recommendation: reject; suggest stronger differentiation from prior work, comprehensive ablations, and theoretical justification to improve the paper.
