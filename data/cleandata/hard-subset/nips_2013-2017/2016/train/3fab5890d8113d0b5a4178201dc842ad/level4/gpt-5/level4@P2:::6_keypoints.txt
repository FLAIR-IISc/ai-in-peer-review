- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): differentiable external memory with sparse ANN-based reads and sparse writes using LRU or recent-read targeting.  
- Introduces sparse BPTT with reversible sparse-delta updates to reduce space from O(NT) to O(T).  
- Claims asymptotically optimal time/space under assumptions and demonstrates large empirical speed/memory improvements over dense models.  

- Technical design is sound: ANN sparse reads and LRU writes are simple, effective, and differentiable.  
- Complexity analysis and reversible updates are convincing; main-text should state optimality assumptions.  
- Empirical validation is thorough across speed/memory benchmarks, NTM tasks, long-sequence scaling, bAbI, and Omniglot.  
- Some comparisons may be unfair (matching physical memory vs matching memory slots); include both protocols.  
- Needs additional ablations (sensitivity to K, ANN choice, rebuild frequency, usage threshold).  

- Paper is clear and well organized; equations and control flow are easy to follow.  
- Add a brief primer on memory-augmented neural networks and summarize NTM/DNC write mechanisms for non-experts.  
- Clarify that ANN index has no gradient and explain how index rebuilds interact with training.  
- Unify notation for sparse/dense weights and hyperparameter naming.  

- Original contribution: integrating ANN search into differentiable memory with sparse writes and reversible sparse-delta BPTT.  
- Extending the method to DNC demonstrates generality and practical gains.  

- Addresses a central scalability barrier, enabling millions of slots and thousands of timesteps.  
- Likely to be widely used and extended; advances scalable differentiable memory methods.  

- Strength: elegant, end-to-end differentiable sparse read/write design.  
- Strength: strong asymptotic analysis and practical sparse-delta BPTT engineering.  
- Strength: large empirical speedups (up to 1600×) and memory reductions (thousands×).  
- Strength: broad evaluation across relevant tasks and successful DNC generalization.  

- Compare under both equal memory slots and equal physical memory budgets to separate algorithmic vs engineering gains.  
- Provide ablations for K, ANN type, rebuild schedule, and usage threshold; quantify ANN approximation effects.  
- Move concise optimality-assumption statement from supplement to main text.  
- Clarify handling of ties in LRU, collisions when writes target recent reads, and provide reproducibility details (hyperparameters, code/pseudocode).  

- Can performance vary with K and can K be learned/adapted during training?  
- What is the trade-off between ANN recall and task accuracy; how sensitive are results to index choice and rebuild schedule?  
- Does sparse gradient flow induce bias that harms tasks needing broad credit assignment?  
- Any comparisons to discrete addressing trained with RL under matched budgets?  
- How robust is LRU when tasks require long-term retention of rarely accessed items?  

- Pros: solves an important scalability problem with principled, effective methods and strong empirical evidence.  
- Pros: clear exposition and broadly applicable methodology, including DNC.  
- Cons: some comparisons conflate resource budgets with capacity; more comprehensive ablations needed.  
- Cons: optimality claims depend on assumptions currently in the supplement.  

- Overall recommendation: high-quality, original, significant work; recommend acceptance pending clarifications and additional analyses for fairness and reproducibility.
