The paper "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes" introduces a novel architecture called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) for neural networks augmented with external memory. This work builds upon the Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, which have shown promise in learning algorithmic solutions to complex tasks. However, these models have been limited by their poor scalability in both space and time as the amount of memory grows.

The authors propose an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme, SAM, which retains the representational power of the original approaches while training efficiently with very large memories. The key innovation is the use of sparse reads and writes, which allows for significant improvements in space and time complexity. The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity and show that an implementation runs 1,000 times faster and with 3,000 times less physical memory than non-sparse models.

The paper presents a thorough evaluation of SAM on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition. The results show that SAM learns with comparable data efficiency to existing models and can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by adapting it for models that maintain temporal associations between memories, such as the Differentiable Neural Computer.

The strengths of the paper include its clear and well-organized presentation, thorough evaluation, and significant contributions to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness in practice.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of discussion on the reasons behind the drastic performance improvements in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to sequences that are an order of magnitude longer than those observed in training. While the authors attribute the improvements to the Sparse Memory Access architecture, a more in-depth analysis of the underlying mechanisms would be beneficial.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel architecture that builds upon existing work in the field. The use of sparse reads and writes is a significant innovation that allows for efficient training with large memories. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by adapting it to different models.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to make memory-augmented neural networks practically applicable. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported in the paper could have far-reaching implications for the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel architecture that significantly improves the efficiency and performance of memory-augmented neural networks.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on a range of tasks and demonstrate its effectiveness in practice.
* The paper has the potential to make memory-augmented neural networks practically applicable, which could have far-reaching implications for the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the underlying mechanisms behind the drastic performance improvements in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to long sequences.
* The authors could provide more discussion on the potential limitations and challenges of their approach.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors present a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. With some additional discussion on the underlying mechanisms behind the performance improvements, the paper has the potential to be a groundbreaking work in the field.
