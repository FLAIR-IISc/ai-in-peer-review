- Uses fast approximate-nearest-neighbor structures and arg-top-K attention in a Neural Turing Machine to enable scalable training on larger problems.  
- Experiments show the approach does not compromise training performance and allows larger-scale tasks.  
- Novelty is limited given prior memory-network and hashing work (e.g., Sect. 3.3 of Sukhbaatar et al. 2014) and other large-memory studies.  
- Contribution is notable in applying these scalable lookup techniques to writeable memories, which appears novel.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance but requests revisions.  
- Reproducibility is insufficient: task descriptions lack detail and the authors should provide precise task construction details and release code.  
- Paper fails to adequately analyze limitations: need to characterize when the method fails, dependency on K, and whether observed successes generalize.  
- Argmax/K-argmax discreteness is not smooth; with K=1 the model resembles MemNN-WSH (which underperformed compared to smooth models), so deeper failure-case analysis is required.  
- The model makes discrete decisions during training without explicitly handling discreteness (cf. approaches like arXiv:1511.07275); this should be discussed.  
- Remove section 3.6 and avoid labeling Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world."
