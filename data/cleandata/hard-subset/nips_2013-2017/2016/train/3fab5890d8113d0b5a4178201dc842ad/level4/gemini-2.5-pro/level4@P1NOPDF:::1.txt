This paper proposes scaling Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) by using fast approximate-nearest-neighbor structures and top-K selection to compute attention weights. The experiments demonstrate that this approach does not degrade performance on standard tasks and enables the model to be trained on larger-scale problems.

While the practical engineering aspects of this work are valuable, the core novelty is somewhat limited. The use of hashing methods to scale memory lookups has been explored in several recent memory network papers (e.g., Section 3.3 of Sukhbaatar et al., 2015, "End-To-End Memory Networks"). However, the application of these techniques to a *writable* memory, as in the NTM, appears to be novel. Thus, while the paper may not represent a major conceptual leap, the practical details of its implementation are a worthwhile contribution to the community.

For these reasons, I recommend accepting the paper, but I believe it would be significantly improved if the authors were to address the following points:

1.  **Reproducibility:** I am concerned that it would be very difficult to replicate the results based on the current manuscript. The descriptions of the experimental tasks are particularly lacking in detail. Given that the code for the original NTM paper was never released and there are no standard benchmarks for these tasks, a thorough and precise description of their construction is crucial for reproducibility. To this end, I would also strongly urge the authors to commit to releasing their experimental code upon publication.

2.  **Analysis of Limitations and Failure Modes:** The paper would be substantially strengthened by an analysis of the model's limitations. The authors frequently describe their model as "smooth," yet the core top-K selection operation is non-differentiable. In the read-only setting with K=1, the proposed model is similar to the MemNN-WSH (Weston et al., 2015, "Memory Networks"), which was reported to underperform its fully differentiable counterpart. This raises the question of why the discrete approach succeeds here. Is it due to the choice of K, the nature of the tasks, or some other factor?

    The paper's value would be greatly enhanced by a discussion of when and why this training procedure works. The model makes discrete selections but is trained with standard backpropagation, seemingly without reinforcement learning techniques (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015, "Reinforcement Learning Neural Turing Machines") that are often used for non-differentiable operations. That the authors achieve strong results with this method is a notable contribution; understanding its limitations and providing insight into the conditions under which it succeeds would make the paper much more impactful.

Finally, I have two minor suggestions:
*   Section 3.6 seems tangential to the main contribution and could be removed to improve the paper's focus.
*   The Omniglot dataset, while complex, is not typically considered "real-world" or "non-synthetic." I suggest the authors revise this description to be more precise.
