- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) uses sparse reads/writes and ANN indices to perform content-based addressing in O(log N) time.  
- Write policy: Writes target recently read locations or the least recently used slot; backward pass stores/reverts sparse updates to save memory.  
- Empirical results: Reports up to 1000× speedups and 3000× memory reductions vs dense NTMs while preserving comparable task performance.  
- Tasks: Demonstrated on synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI QA (including SDNC), and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends ANN/top-K ideas from read-only Memory Networks to writeable, end-to-end trainable external memory.  
- Conceptual novelty: Incremental—top-K attention and ANN lookups are established for read-only settings; main contribution is adapting them to differentiable writable memory.  
- Practical advance: Principled sparse read/write design plus efficient BPTT yields substantial time and space reductions in training.  
- Scalability evidence: Strong empirical scaling to large memories and long sequences with generalization beyond training lengths.  
- Breadth: Demonstrations across algorithmic tasks, QA, and one-shot learning suggest general applicability.  
- Potential impact: Technique could be broadly applicable to other MANN architectures and long-context problems.  
- Differentiability concern: Top-K selection and ANN indexing are non-differentiable; paper overstates smoothness and should discuss gradient effects.  
- Reproducibility issues: Task/dataset generation, preprocessing, curricula, and hyperparameters are insufficiently specified; code release recommended.  
- Failure-mode analysis missing: Lacks ablations on K, temperature/sharpness, erase/write policies, and ANN settings to show where sparse selection hurts.  
- Comparative limitations: Prior work shows K=1/hard selection can underperform soft attention; need experiments and discussion of such cases.  
- Positioning/terminology: Calling Omniglot “real-world” is misleading; suggest “benchmark” or “non-synthetic”; some sections (e.g., 3.6) may be auxiliary.  
- Theoretical claims: Asymptotic optimality relies on unstated assumptions; key assumptions should be moved into the main text and clarified.  
- Quality assessment: Method is technically sound with strong engineering, but theoretical and gradient-related claims need clearer qualification.  
- Clarity assessment: High-level description is clear but lacks precise experimental details for replication.  
- Originality assessment: Moderately original—useful practical contribution though built on known techniques.  
- Significance assessment: High practical significance; could remove a major bottleneck for large-memory MANNs.  
- Requested change 1: Fully document dataset generation, curricula, hyperparameters, and commit to releasing code/configs.  
- Requested change 2: Provide ablations varying K, attention temperature, erase/write policies, and ANN settings; report instability/failure cases.  
- Requested change 3: Clarify how non-differentiable top-K/ANN affects gradients, bias, and training stability; include experiments comparing approximate vs exact neighbors if possible.  
- Requested change 4: Move key theoretical assumptions into main text and clarify applicable regimes for asymptotic claims.  
- Requested change 5: Trim/move tangential sections and avoid calling Omniglot “real-world.”  
- Pros for acceptance: Large, demonstrated computational gains; practical and general technique; strong empirical results across tasks and scales.  
- Cons against acceptance: Limited conceptual novelty, insufficient replication details, limited failure analysis, and overstated differentiability/theoretical claims.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on improved reproducibility, clearer discussion of differentiability/limits, and minor presentation fixes.
