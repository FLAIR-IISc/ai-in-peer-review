

This paper proposes a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), within memory-augmented neural network architectures. This research domain, initiated by Neural Turing Machines, remains relatively nascent. SAM enables neural networks to transcend traditional LSTM limitations by constraining all external memory writes and reads to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than an unrestricted memory space. The authors rigorously prove the methodology and its optimality. The reduced memory footprint is expected to yield improved read/write performance, as demonstrated in Section 4.1. Furthermore, Section 4.2 comprehensively analyzes the learning cost of sparsity using standard NTM tasks: copy, associative recall, and priority sorting. The combined cost is lower than NTM for certain tasks (specifically 2 and 3). The inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4 and the use of Torch7 for benchmarking are commendable. The core idea is straightforward, novel, and delivers significant speedups over NTM, which is impressive. The observation that sparse reads/writes facilitated by linear or ANN mechanisms can enhance early-stage learning in specific scenarios (Section 4.2) is particularly intriguing. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should elaborate on the generalization conditions under which SAM improves learning (i.e., *under which cases* learning is enhanced) and explore the potential of integrating diverse ANN architectures with SAM. Overall, this is a well-written paper with clear assumptions, a detailed architecture presented in the appendix, and an engaging contribution.
