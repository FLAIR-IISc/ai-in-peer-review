- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse reads/writes + approximate nearest neighbor search to make memory-augmented networks much more efficient.  
- Reports substantial empirical gains (≈1000× speedup, ≈3000× less memory) while maintaining performance.  
- Demonstrates scalability to very long sequences (100,000s of time steps).  
- Novelty lies in applying hashing/ANN to writeable, end-to-end trainable memory modules.  
- Empirical validation includes synthetic algorithmic tasks and one-shot Omniglot experiments with comparable performance.  
- Replicability concerns: task descriptions lack detail and code is not released—authors should provide precise task definitions and code.  
- Insufficient analysis of failure modes and limitations of the sparse approximation; more failure-case investigation is needed.  
- The non-smooth K-argmax operation needs deeper study (effect on training, influence of K, conditions causing degradation).  
- Lacks rigorous comparison to related discrete-selection models (e.g., MemNN-WSH) and other cited works.  
- The paper should explain why performance remains robust despite non-smoothness and contrast with prior experiences ([19,21]).  
- Calling Omniglot “non-synthetic”/“real-world” is misleading and should be removed.  
- Section 3.6 appears redundant and could be omitted.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on improved reproducibility (code/task detail) and expanded failure/limitation analysis.
