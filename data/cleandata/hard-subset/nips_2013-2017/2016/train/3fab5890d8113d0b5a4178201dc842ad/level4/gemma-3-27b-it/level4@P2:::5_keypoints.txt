- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to improve scalability of memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Claims up to three orders of magnitude runtime and memory efficiency gains over existing approaches.  
- Demonstrates improved curriculum learning performance and extrapolation from length 10k to 200k sequences.  
- Suggests SAM makes memory-augmented networks viable for practical, real-world applications.  
- Clearly identifies bottlenecks in prior work (linear overhead from smooth read/write) and proposes sparsity as an elegant solution.  
- Empirical results show significant speedups and memory savings without performance loss across synthetic benchmarks, bAbI, and one-shot Omniglot.  
- Shows generality by adapting the approach to the Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).  
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly written.  
- Strength: substantial and practical efficiency gains addressing a major limitation.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance on diverse tasks.  
- Strength: scalability to much longer sequences and larger memories.  
- Strength: approach generalizes to other architectures (e.g., DNC).  
- Weakness: limited analysis of why SAM achieves strong generalization to much longer sequences.  
- Weakness: insufficient detail on ANN implementation choices (randomized k-d trees vs. LSH) and their impact.  
- Weakness: needs more detailed comparison to recent memory compression and scaling techniques.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a critical bottleneck with compelling, impactful results.  
- Argument against acceptance: minor gaps in explanation of generalization and ANN implementation details.  
- Overall: high-quality, significant contribution with recommendation for acceptance.
