This paper proposes a method to scale Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) to larger memory sizes by incorporating fast approximate-nearest-neighbor (ANN) structures and arg-top-K operations for computing attention weights. The authors present experiments demonstrating that this approximation does not degrade performance on standard NTM tasks and, importantly, enables training on problems with larger memory requirements than were previously feasible.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its practical application and engineering. While the conceptual novelty is limited, the demonstration of making these techniques work effectively for a *writable* memory system is a valuable contribution worth reporting. The use of hashing and scalable lookups for large memories has been explored previously, particularly in the context of Memory Networks. For instance, Sukhbaatar et al. (2014, http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf) discuss similar ideas in Section 3.3, and subsequent work has successfully applied hashing methods to scale read-only memories to millions of items. However, to my knowledge, this is the first work to successfully apply such an approach to a read-write memory architecture like the NTM.

While I recommend accepting the paper, I believe its contribution could be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points.

**1. Reproducibility:**
As presented, the experimental results would be very difficult to reproduce. The descriptions of the tasks are insufficient. This is a critical issue because, unlike many other domains, there are no "standard" benchmark versions of the NTM tasks, and the code from the original NTM paper was never released. To make this work a useful contribution to the community, it is essential that the authors provide a thorough and precise description of how each task was constructed, including details on data generation, sequence lengths, and any other relevant parameters. Furthermore, I strongly urge the authors to commit to releasing their code for the tasks and the model to ensure the work is verifiable and can be built upon by future research.

**2. Analysis of the Discrete Approximation and Failure Modes:**
The paper would be substantially improved by a more rigorous analysis of the limitations of the proposed method. The authors frequently refer to their model as being smooth, but the argmax (or arg-top-K) operation is fundamentally non-differentiable and thus introduces a discontinuity into the network. This raises several important questions that the paper should address:

*   **Comparison to Prior Work:** In the read-only setting with K=1, the proposed model is conceptually similar to the MemNN-WSH model from Dodge et al. (2015, http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf). That work reported that their discrete, hashing-based model performed worse than its fully differentiable (smooth) counterpart. Why does the discrete approach succeed here? Is it because K>1 is used? Is it something specific to the NTM architecture or the tasks themselves? A discussion of these differences is needed.
*   **Training with Discrete Operations:** The model effectively learns to perform a discrete action (selecting the top-K memory locations) using standard backpropagation. This is a non-trivial result, as such problems are often approached with reinforcement learning techniques (e.g., REINFORCE, as in Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015, http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275) to handle the non-differentiable step. The fact that direct training works is an interesting and valuable finding. However, the paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of *why* this works and an exploration of its limits. Under what conditions does this training paradigm succeed or fail? A discussion of potential failure cases or an ablation study on the factors that enable successful training (e.g., the size of K) would provide deeper insight.

**Minor Suggestions:**
*   I suggest removing Section 3.6, as it does not add substantially to the core contributions of the paper.
*   The Omniglot dataset, while complex, is a curated academic benchmark. I would recommend referring to it as a "challenging character recognition dataset" or similar, rather than "non-synthetic" or "real-world," which could be misleading.
