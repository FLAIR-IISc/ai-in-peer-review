The paper employs fast approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indexing and top-K (arg-top-K) selection to compute attention weights within a Neural Turing Machine. The experiments indicate that these choices do not hinder training and, in fact, enable scaling to substantially larger problems. The degree of novelty is limited relative to prior work (e.g., Memory Networks, sec. 3.3: http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), and there are already several Memory Network papers that use memory with millions of items and hashing-like mechanisms to make retrieval scalable. That said, I am not aware of such tools being applied to writable external memories; even if the conceptual step is modest, the practical engineering required to make this work is worthwhile. I recommend acceptance, contingent on revisions.

1: Reproducibility and task specification. As written, it would be challenging to reproduce the reported results. The task descriptions are particularly sparse; given that the original NTM task code has not been released and there is no universally accepted “standard” formulation, the paper should precisely specify task construction details. I also encourage the authors to commit to releasing the code used in their experiments.

2: Limits and failure modes. Under what conditions does the approach break down? Is it robust across tasks? Although the text emphasizes smoothness, the argmax/top-K operations are not smooth. With K=1 in the read-only setting, the method resembles MemNN-WSH (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), even though that model does not use hashing, and that variant was reported to underperform fully smooth models. Is the gap due to K=1, the task choice, or something else? The paper would be stronger with an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of which tasks/setups permit successful training: the model makes discrete choices yet is trained without explicitly handling this (cf. http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Demonstrating that it nevertheless works is a valuable contribution, but clarifying the boundaries of success would add significant insight. I also suggest removing Section 3.6, and avoiding describing Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
