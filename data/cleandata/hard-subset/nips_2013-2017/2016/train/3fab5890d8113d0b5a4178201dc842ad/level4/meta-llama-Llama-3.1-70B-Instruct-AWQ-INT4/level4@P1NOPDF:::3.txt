This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This research area is relatively recent, dating back to the invention of Neural Turing Machines, which enable neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional LSTM models. The primary contribution of this paper lies in constraining all writes to and reads from external memory to a fixed size, thereby accessing only a subset of memory words, rather than allowing unlimited size. The authors demonstrate the methodology's optimality and show that it can achieve good read and write performance due to the reduced memory size, as presented in section 4.1. However, the authors also investigate the learning costs associated with introducing sparsity in section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks, namely copy, associated recall, and priority sort. Notably, the combined costs are lower than those of NTM for certain tasks (i.e., tasks 2 and 3). I appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in section 4.4, with the benchmark implemented in Torch7. The idea is straightforward and novel, and the significant speedup compared to NTM is impressive. The finding that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear or artificial neural network (ANN), can benefit early-stage learning in some cases (section 4.2) is particularly interesting. To further enhance the paper, it would be helpful if the authors could elaborate on how to generalize SAM to other cases, exploring the conditions under which learning could be improved, or investigating the application of different ANN approaches with SAM. Overall, the paper is well-written, with clear explanations of assumptions and architecture details provided in the appendix, making it an engaging read.
