This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), within a memory-augmented neural network architecture. This research area is relatively recent, following the introduction of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). The proposed augmentation enables neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution is to constrain all reads from and writes to external memory to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than an unbounded set. The authors provide proofs of the methodology and its optimality.

As shown in Section 4.1, this design should yield strong read and write performance due to the reduced effective memory size. The authors also analyze the learning costs introduced by sparsity (Section 4.2) using standard NTM tasks: (1) Copy, (2) Associative Recall, and (3) Priority Sort. The combined cost is lower than that of NTM for some tasks (specifically, tasks 2 and 3). I also appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example (Section 4.4). The benchmarks are implemented in Torch7.

The idea is straightforward and novel, and the speedup relative to NTM is significant and impressive. It is particularly interesting that sparse reads and writes, driven by a linear controller or an ANN, can benefit early-stage learning in some cases (Section 4.2).

It would be very helpful if the authors elaborated on how these early-stage learning benefits might generalize—i.e., under what conditions learning is improved—and evaluated different ANN approaches with SAM. Overall, this is a well-written paper with assumptions stated clearly and the architecture detailed in the appendix. It is an interesting read.
