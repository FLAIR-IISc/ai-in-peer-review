This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This area of research is relatively recent, emerging since the invention of Neural Turing Machines, and this augmentation allows neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional models like LSTMs.

The paper's primary contribution is constraining all read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory words, in contrast to standard approaches that may access the entire memory. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this methodology and its optimality. As demonstrated in Section 4.1, this approach achieves superior read and write performance due to the reduced access size.

The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation. In Section 4.2, they analyze the learning costs of introducing sparsity using standard NTM tasks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The results show that the combined costs for SAM are lower than for the standard NTM on the associative recall and priority sort tasks. The finding that sparse access, guided by a linear controller or an ANN, can benefit early-stage learning in some cases is particularly interesting. The significant speedup over the standard NTM is impressive, and the inclusion of a real-world application in Section 4.4 is a commendable addition to the experimental results.

The paper could be strengthened by further exploring the conditions under which sparse access improves learning. An elaboration on how to generalize these findings would be valuable to the reader. Additionally, an investigation into different controller architectures for SAM could provide further insights.

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper. The core idea is straightforward and novel, the assumptions are clearly stated, and the architecture is detailed in the appendix.
