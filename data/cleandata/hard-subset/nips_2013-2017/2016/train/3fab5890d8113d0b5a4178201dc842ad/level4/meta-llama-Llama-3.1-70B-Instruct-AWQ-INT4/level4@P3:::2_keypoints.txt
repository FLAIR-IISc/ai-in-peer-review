- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme to scale memory-augmented neural networks efficiently.  
- Claims SAM attains asymptotic lower bounds in space/time complexity and shows large empirical runtime and memory improvements over dense models.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on varied tasks (synthetic, one-shot Omniglot, bAbI QA), scaling to 100,000s of time steps/memories with significant speedups.  
- Paper is well-organized with clear writing and a thorough background on relevant memory-augmented architectures and attention mechanisms.  
- Technical novelty is questioned: implementation relies on standard efficient data structures (CSR) and existing approximate nearest-neighbor methods (randomized k-d trees, LSH).  
- Lacks in-depth analysis of trade-offs between sparse and dense models, especially regarding representational power and detailed comparisons.  
- Reviewer not fully convinced of technical strength despite practical gains; requests more rigorous analysis and comparisons with existing methods.  
- Summary ratings: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 6/10, Significance 8/10.
