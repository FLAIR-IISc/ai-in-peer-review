## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please fill in*]

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scalability in neural networks augmented with external memory. A significant limitation of many existing memory-augmented neural network architectures is their poor scaling in both time and space as the memory size increases. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme, and demonstrate that it achieves comparable representational power to existing approaches while offering substantial improvements in training efficiency – specifically, a reported 1000x speedup and 3000x reduction in memory consumption.

Despite the relevance of the problem and the promising empirical results, I do not believe this paper currently reaches the bar for acceptance to NIPS. While the experiments presented are interesting, the core technical contributions – efficient backpropagation through SAM and the approximation of nearest neighbor lookups within the sparse access structure – appear relatively straightforward extensions of existing techniques. 

The approach to enabling backpropagation through the sparse access mechanism, while functional, lacks significant novelty. The paper describes a technique for maintaining gradient flow, but it doesn’t present a fundamentally new or insightful solution to the challenges inherent in differentiable memory access. Similarly, the nearest neighbor approximation method, based on [cite relevant prior work if any, otherwise state “established techniques”], appears to be a direct application of known strategies adapted to the specifics of SAM. The paper would benefit greatly from a more detailed discussion of *why* these techniques work particularly well within the SAM framework, or what novel insights were required to successfully apply them.

My concerns are not with the *implementation* of these techniques, but with their foundational novelty. The paper positions SAM as a significant advance, but the technical elements underpinning it feel incremental.  A stronger justification of the technical contributions, perhaps highlighting a nuanced theoretical analysis or a previously unaddressed challenge overcome by the approach, would be necessary for me to reconsider. 

Specifically, I would like to see the authors address the following points:

* **Comparison to alternative sparsity patterns:** The paper focuses on a specific form of sparsity. How does this chosen sparsity pattern compare to other potential sparsity strategies (e.g., random sparsity, structured sparsity) in terms of performance, memory usage, and computational cost? A more comprehensive ablation study would clarify the benefits of the chosen approach.
* **Theoretical analysis of representational power:** The authors claim SAM retains representational power. While the experimental results are suggestive, a more formal analysis of the theoretical capacity of SAM would strengthen this claim. How does the representational power of SAM scale with memory size and sparsity level?
* **Computational complexity:** While speedups are reported, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of SAM compared to existing methods would be valuable.  Specifically, how does the cost of the sparse access mechanism scale with memory size and the dimensionality of the memory vectors?
* **Impact of hyperparameters:** The performance of SAM likely depends on several hyperparameters, such as the sparsity level and the parameters of the nearest neighbor approximation. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the results to variations in these hyperparameters is needed.



In summary, while the practical benefits of SAM—improved speed and reduced memory consumption—are undeniable, the technical contributions, as presented, do not currently represent a substantial advance warranting acceptance to NIPS.  I encourage the authors to revise the paper to better articulate the novelty of their approach and address the concerns outlined above.
