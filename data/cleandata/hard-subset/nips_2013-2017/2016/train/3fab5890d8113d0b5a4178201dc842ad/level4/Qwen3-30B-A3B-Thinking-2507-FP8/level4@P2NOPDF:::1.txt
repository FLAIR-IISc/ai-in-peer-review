

The authors propose leveraging fast approximate-nearest-neighbors (ANN) structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM), demonstrating that this approach maintains training performance while enabling scalability to larger problems. While the core technique builds directly on established methods in memory networks—such as hashing for scalable lookups in large memories (e.g., Weston et al., 2014, Section 3.3; and subsequent works)—the application to *writeable* memories represents a meaningful practical contribution. To our knowledge, no prior work has adapted these tools for dynamic memory writing, making the implementation details valuable for the community. However, the conceptual novelty is limited, and the paper requires significant revisions before acceptance.  

The primary concern is replicability. The paper lacks sufficient detail on task construction, particularly given the absence of standardized task implementations (unlike the original NTM paper, whose code remains unreleased). Without precise descriptions of how tasks were generated and validated, replication is highly challenging. The authors must provide exhaustive task specifications and commit to releasing all experimental code.  

Second, the paper fails to address critical limitations of the approach. The use of arg-top-K (especially K=1) introduces non-smooth discrete operations, contrasting with fully differentiable models (e.g., Graves et al., 2016). This is a significant oversight: the MemNN-WSH model (Wang et al., 2015), which similarly uses argmax without smoothing, underperformed relative to smooth alternatives. The authors’ model succeeds despite this, but they do not analyze *why* or *under what conditions* it works. Crucially, they omit:  
- A systematic study of failure cases (e.g., tasks where arg-top-K degrades performance).  
- Discussion of task properties enabling success (e.g., whether discrete actions align with problem structure).  
- Comparison to smoothed baselines on identical tasks.  
This omission weakens the contribution, as the paper’s value hinges on understanding *when* this approach is viable. The claim that the model is "smooth" is misleading given the discrete operation; the authors should clarify this point or remove the characterization.  

Additionally, Section 3.6 (discussing the "smoothness" of the model) is redundant and should be removed. Finally, labeling Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world" is inaccurate—Omniglot is synthetically generated (Larochelle et al., 2013)—and undermines the paper’s credibility.  

In summary, the practical implementation of ANN for writeable memories is a useful contribution, but the paper must address replication barriers, clarify the role of discrete operations, and eliminate misleading claims. Acceptance is contingent on:  
1. **Detailed task specifications** (including data generation protocols) and **code release commitment**.  
2. **Analysis of failure modes** and task-specific conditions for success, contrasting with smoothed baselines.  
3. **Removal of Section 3.6** and correction of the Omniglot description.  

With these revisions, the paper would provide a valuable, actionable framework for scalable NTMs. As written, it lacks the rigor required for publication. I recommend **acceptance pending major revisions**.
