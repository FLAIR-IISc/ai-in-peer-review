- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which constrains reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words to improve MANN scalability.
- Provides theoretical justification for SAM’s optimality and empirically validates the approach.
- Demonstrates significant speedups and memory improvements compared to the original Neural Turing Machine.
- Shows scalable performance on larger problems and strong empirical results on standard tasks.
- Evaluated on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) with comparable or improved learning efficiency.
- Observes that sparse reads/writes can benefit early-stage learning in some cases.
- Includes a real-world experiment on the Omniglot dataset, showing practical applicability.
- Implementation is based on Torch7.
- Paper is well-written and clearly presents assumptions, architecture, and results (supplementary material provides details).
- Strong acceptance arguments: novelty, technical soundness, significance, clarity, and empirical strength.
- Main critique: lacks a comprehensive investigation of the conditions under which sparsity helps or hurts learning.
- ANN discussion is brief; deeper comparison of alternative ANN methods (e.g., Mondrian forests, LSH forests) would strengthen the work.
- Suggests future work to analyze when sparsity improves learning and to explore different ANN approaches.
