- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable memory architecture that drastically reduces compute and memory while preserving representational power.  
- Achieves asymptotic optimality: O(N) init, O(1) per-timestep space, and O(log N) per read/write by restricting accesses to sparse subsets and using ANN.  
- Demonstrates massive empirical gains (e.g., ~1,000–1,600× speedup, ~3,000× less memory) on 1M-word memories versus dense baselines.  
- Maintains near-identical data efficiency to dense MANNs on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and performs well on Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Scales to very long sequences (100k+ steps) and achieves state-of-the-art on bAbI QA (SDNC solves 8/9 tasks) without supervised memory access.  
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous: proofs of SAM’s asymptotic optimality and alignment between theory and experiments (Supplementary A).  
- Key technical strengths: theoretically sound sparse write using least-recently-accessed slots, efficient BPTT via sparse state tracking, and extensive ablation studies.  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with precise math, detailed experimental setup, and reproducible algorithmic descriptions (sparse read/write, ANN integration, BPTT).  
- Novelty: first NIPS-era work to combine sparse, ANN-accelerated reads/writes with end-to-end differentiability and optimal complexity for MANNs.  
- Extension to Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) shows generality beyond the core SAM idea.  
- High significance: enables practical scaling of MANNs to real-world tasks (language, long-horizon reasoning) and sets complexity benchmarks future models must match.  
- Weakness: limited evaluation on non-synthetic large-scale language datasets (e.g., WikiText) to demonstrate broader real-world impact.  
- Weakness: ANN implementation trade-offs (k-d trees vs. LSH) and their scaling/error behaviors are not deeply analyzed or quantified.  
- Weakness: least-recently-accessed write policy may struggle with highly repetitive access patterns; acknowledged but not resolved.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — technically solid, novel, well-validated, and highly impactful despite minor empirical and analysis gaps.
