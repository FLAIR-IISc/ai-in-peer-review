- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), constraining reads/writes to a fixed-size sparse subset to improve scalability of memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Achieves O(log N) time complexity and O(1) space per time step via approximate nearest neighbors (ANNs), contrasting with O(N) costs of dense MANNs.  
- Theoretically justified: formal asymptotic analysis shows SAM’s optimality (Supplementary A).  
- Demonstrates massive empirical gains: ~1,000× runtime speedups and ~3,000× memory reductions versus Neural Turing Machines.  
- Matches or exceeds dense baselines on synthetic NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and real tasks (Omniglot, Babi QA).  
- Extends to a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), showing generalizability beyond the core architecture.  
- Shows SAM enables training on very long sequences (curriculum to 200,000 steps) and tasks requiring 100,000s of steps where dense models fail.  
- Novel insight: sparsity can improve learning efficiency on some tasks (e.g., priority sort), a counterintuitive benefit.  
- Strong experimental validation: comprehensive benchmarks, ablations (ANN choices k-d trees vs LSH), and reproducible implementation details.  
- Clear presentation and well-structured architecture descriptions; helpful supplementary figures for reproducibility.  
- Minor clarity suggestion: expand discussion of ANN trade-offs and reference recent ANN advances (e.g., Mondrian forests).  
- Main limitation: focus on content-based addressing — may not directly generalize to location-based or non-content MANN variants.  
- Limited exploration of sparsity in non-sequential tasks, though reasonable given the paper’s sequence focus.  
- Overall recommendation: substantial, well-executed, and practically significant contribution — Accept.
