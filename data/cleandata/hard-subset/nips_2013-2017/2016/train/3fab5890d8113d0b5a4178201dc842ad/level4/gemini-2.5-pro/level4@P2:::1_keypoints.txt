- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a memory-augmented NN that restricts reads/writes to a small constant K locations.
- Uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) structures (k-d trees/LSH) to find read locations, reducing read time from O(N) to O(log N).
- Write mechanism updates recently read locations or the least-recently used slot to maintain sparsity.
- Reports dramatic efficiency gains (e.g., >1000x speedup, ~3000x memory reduction) enabling much larger memories.
- Empirical results: matches or outperforms dense MANNs on algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot; scales to sequences >100k.
- Practical novelty: successful integration of ANN/hash lookups into a writable, end-to-end training-compatible memory (incl. BPTT rollback).
- Major weakness: reproducibility concerns—insufficient task generation details and lack of released code.
- Major weakness: insufficient analysis of the non-differentiable top-K/ANN selection and when the straight-through approximation may fail.
- Minor weakness: overstates Omniglot as "real-world" given the synthetic nature of its one-shot evaluation setup.
- Recommendation: accept conditional on improving reproducibility and providing a deeper discussion/analysis of the selection approximation.
