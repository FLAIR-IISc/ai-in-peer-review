Summary:
The paper addresses the scalability challenges of differentiable neural memory by proposing Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme that uses sparse reads/writes and approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) lookups. The authors claim that SAM preserves the representational capacity of dense memory access while achieving large computational and memory savings, reporting up to 1000x faster training and 3000x lower memory usage. The main technical components are an efficient backpropagation scheme compatible with sparse access and an ANN-based addressing mechanism. While the motivation is strong and the empirical results are promising, I find the technical contributions incremental and not sufficiently deep to warrant acceptance.

Strengths:
- Timely and important problem: scaling neural memory in space and time is a key bottleneck for memory-augmented models.
- Practicality: sparse access plus ANN is a natural and useful combination that could be adopted widely.
- Empirical promise: the reported speed and memory improvements, if measured fairly, would be impactful.
- Simplicity: the method appears modular and potentially easy to integrate into existing architectures.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: using ANN for memory addressing and restricting backpropagation to a sparse subset are known ideas in large-scale retrieval and attention. The paper appears to combine established techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new theory or algorithms.
- Technical depth: the treatment of “efficient backpropagation” through sparse access is described as straightforward. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous analysis of gradient correctness, bias introduced by sparsification, and conditions under which representational equivalence to dense access holds.
- Claims vs. evidence: the claim of “retaining representational power” is strong but not well substantiated. Formal results, ablations, or proofs clarifying what is preserved (e.g., universal approximation under certain sparsity schedules, bounds on approximation error due to ANN) are missing.
- Experimental scope and rigor: while results look interesting, it is unclear whether comparisons are end-to-end and fair. For example:
  - Are speedups measured including index construction/updates and data transfer overheads?
  - How do results vary with memory size, sparsity level (k), and ANN parameters?
  - Are there strong baselines using optimized dense attention or block-sparse kernels?
  - Do results hold across multiple tasks and datasets, not just synthetic or favorable regimes?
- Clarity on implementation details: the method depends critically on how the memory is organized, updated, and indexed. The paper should be clearer about:
  - How writes are handled (e.g., collisions, eviction, consistency with the index).
  - How often the ANN index is rebuilt/updated and its amortized cost.
  - Differentiation through the top-k selection and whether any straight-through or surrogate gradients are used.
  - The exact asymptotic and practical complexity of forward and backward passes, including constants.

Detailed comments and questions:
- Representational power: Please formalize what “retains representational power” means. Is there a bound on the discrepancy between dense and sparse access as a function of k and ANN recall? Are there tasks for which sparse access provably fails where dense access would succeed?
- Gradient flow: How are gradients to non-selected memory locations treated? Is there any mechanism to mitigate bias from top-k gating (e.g., softening, stochastic selection, or exploration)?
- ANN choice and tuning: Which ANN method is used, and how sensitive are results to its hyperparameters and recall/precision trade-offs? Is there a regime where ANN cost dominates and erodes speedups?
- Scalability and stability: How do training stability and final accuracy vary with larger memories and longer sequences? Provide curves showing accuracy vs. latency/memory trade-offs as k varies.
- Baselines: Include comparisons with strong alternatives such as:
  - Dense attention with optimized kernels or locality-aware sparsity patterns.
  - Other memory-augmented networks (e.g., differentiable neural computers, key-value memories) using recent engineering improvements.
  - Retrieval-augmented models that leverage external indices but incorporate learned gating.
- Measurement details: Report wall-clock times on standardized hardware, batch sizes, and precision settings; include profiling breakdowns (index lookup, memory gather/scatter, compute) to substantiate the claimed 1000x/3000x figures.
- Reproducibility: Release code and configurations, and document seeds and variance across runs.

Overall assessment:
The paper’s motivation and empirical direction are compelling, and the approach is practical. However, the core technical contributions—sparse backpropagation and ANN-based addressing—are relatively straightforward and not developed with the level of theoretical or methodological novelty expected for acceptance. The strong efficiency claims need more careful qualification and broader, more rigorous empirical validation. Without stronger theoretical grounding, clearer algorithmic details, and more comprehensive experiments and baselines, I cannot recommend acceptance.

Recommendation: Reject.

Confidence: Moderate.
