The paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) architecture for neural networks augmented with memory, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks. This allows for remarkable run-time and memory efficiency improvements (three orders of magnitude) compared to existing neural networks augmented with memory. This architecture also seems to allow for remarkable improvements in performance in the setting of curriculum learning and in the ability to generalize to sequences that are an order of magnitude longer than those observed in training (from length of up to 10,000 in training to sequences of length of 200,000 in testing). This is potentially a groundbreaking paper.

We have an actively developing field of neural networks augmented with memory, starting with Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. So far, we have observed very interesting research and experimental results in that area, but they did not look like they were at a level where they would be practically applicable. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported in the present paper are likely to mean that memory-augmented neural networks are now ready to be used in practice. That is likely to have far-reaching implications.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its benefits. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating significant improvements in run-time and memory efficiency compared to existing models. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the performance of SAM on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance technical detail with high-level intuition. The authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its benefits, making it accessible to a wide range of readers. The paper also includes a thorough analysis of the performance of SAM on various tasks, demonstrating its ability to generalize to new tasks and settings.

However, one potential weakness of the paper is the lack of discussion on the reasons for the drastic performance improvements in curriculum learning and the ability to predict sequences with length so far beyond the training range. While the authors attribute the improvements to the SAM architecture, it would be helpful to provide more insight into the underlying mechanisms that drive these improvements. For example, how does the sparse memory access architecture contribute to the improved performance in curriculum learning? What are the implications of these findings for future research in neural networks augmented with memory?

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its benefits. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating significant improvements in run-time and memory efficiency compared to existing models. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of neural networks augmented with memory and has the potential to have far-reaching implications.
