This paper tackles the well-known scalability problem of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme that uses sparse reads and writes to reduce computational and memory overhead. They claim that SAM preserves the representational power of prior approaches while training far more efficiently—up to 1000× faster and 3000× more memory-efficient than dense baselines.

Strengths:
- The problem addressed is important: existing MANNs scale poorly in both space and time as memory size grows.
- The proposed approach is simple and appealing—enforcing sparsity in memory access—and the empirical speed and memory gains are substantial.
- The experimental results are interesting and suggest that sparse approximations can be effective in practice.

Weaknesses:
- In my view, the paper is not technically strong enough for acceptance. The main technical components—efficient backpropagation through time via sparse memory updates, and the use of approximate nearest neighbor search for content-based addressing—are quite straightforward.
- Given this, the novelty and depth of the contribution appear limited despite the promising empirical results.

Overall assessment:
While the empirical findings are encouraging and the application of sparse access is practical, I do not find sufficient technical novelty or depth. Therefore, despite the interesting experiments, I am not in favor of acceptance.
