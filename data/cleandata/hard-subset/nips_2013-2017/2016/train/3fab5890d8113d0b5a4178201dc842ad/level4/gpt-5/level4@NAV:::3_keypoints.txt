- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a new memory access mechanism for memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Builds on Neural Turing Machines and extends capabilities beyond traditional LSTMs.  
- Main contribution: restricts all reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words instead of an unbounded set.  
- Provides theoretical proofs for the methodology and its optimality.  
- Empirically achieves strong read/write performance due to reduced active memory size (Sec. 4.1).  
- Evaluates learning costs of sparsity on standard NTM tasks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort (Sec. 4.2).  
- Combined computational/learning costs are lower than NTM for some tasks (associative recall and priority sort).  
- Includes a real-world example (Sec. 4.4) and implements benchmarks in Torch7.  
- Idea is straightforward and novel, with substantial and impressive speedup over NTM.  
- Notable finding: sparse reads/writes can improve early-stage learning in some cases.  
- Suggests authors should clarify conditions for generalizing these gains and applying SAM with other ANN approaches.  
- Overall assessment: well-written, clear assumptions, architecture details in appendix, and engaging to read.
