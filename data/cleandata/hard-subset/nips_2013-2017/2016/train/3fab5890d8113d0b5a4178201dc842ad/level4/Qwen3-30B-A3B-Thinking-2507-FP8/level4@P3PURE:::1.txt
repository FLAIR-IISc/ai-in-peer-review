

Review:  
The authors employ fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and argmax-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM). Their experiments demonstrate that this approach does not degrade training performance and enables training on larger-scale problems. However, the novelty is limited; as noted in Section 3.3 of the Memory Networks paper (arXiv:1410.3916), multiple memory network works have since leveraged millions of memory items and hashing for scalable lookups. While the conceptual leap is modest, the practical implementation for *writable* memories—where such techniques have not been previously applied—represents a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance, but the paper requires two key revisions.  

First, replication would be challenging due to insufficient task descriptions. Given the absence of standardized task implementations (e.g., the original NTM code remains unreleased), the paper must provide meticulous details on task construction. Additionally, the authors should commit to releasing their experimental code.  

Second, the paper lacks analysis of failure modes. The authors frequently characterize their model as "smooth," yet argmax-top-K operations are inherently non-smooth. With $K=1$, their read-only model resembles MemNN-WSH (arXiv:1503.08895), which underperformed relative to fully smooth alternatives (e.g., Graves et al., arXiv:1511.07275). It remains unclear whether the $K=1$ choice, task characteristics, or discrete actions (unlike the smooth gradient-based training in Graves et al.) drive the observed results. A deeper discussion of conditions where this method succeeds or fails—particularly given the model’s discrete action space—would significantly strengthen the contribution.  

I further recommend removing Section 3.6 and avoiding the term "non-synthetic" for the Omniglot dataset, as it is misleading and misrepresents the data’s nature.
