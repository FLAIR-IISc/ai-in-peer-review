Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network architecture that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes to external memory. SAM uses content-based addressing with approximate nearest neighbor search to select only K memory locations per read (and similarly constrains writes), leading to sublinear time per step in memory size and constant space per step during training via an efficient rollback scheme for BPTT. Empirically, the authors report speedups of up to three orders of magnitude and similarly dramatic reductions in physical memory usage compared to dense Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), while maintaining comparable data efficiency on standard synthetic tasks. The model also scales to very long sequences and very large memories, and, under a curriculum, generalizes from training sequences up to length 10,000 to test sequences as long as 200,000. The approach is situated within the line of work on memory-augmented neural networks, including NTMs and Memory Networks, and the authors further show how to adapt their sparsity scheme to more complex architectures (e.g., Differentiable Neural Computers).

Strengths:
- Substantial algorithmic and systems contribution: sparse reads/writes with ANN-based content addressing and constant-space BPTT lead to dramatic runtime and memory savings (up to ~1000× faster and ~3000× less memory than dense baselines).
- Strong empirical results: comparable or better learning efficiency on canonical synthetic tasks; compelling scaling via curriculum to very long sequences; impressive extrapolation from sequences of 10k at training to 200k at test.
- Practicality: by removing the prohibitive space and time costs of dense memory access, this work plausibly moves memory-augmented models from interesting demos toward practical applicability.
- Breadth: the sparse-access design appears general enough to retrofit onto related architectures.

Weaknesses / points for clarification:
- The paper does not discuss why the sparse-access design yields such large performance gains under curriculum training and such strong extrapolation far beyond the training range. Runtime and memory efficiency alone do not explain these effects; there seems to be an additional inductive bias at play. My intuition is that sparsity here may play a role analogous to that of sparse autoencoders—encouraging more discrete, modular, or compositional internal algorithms—but the paper does not provide analysis or even hypotheses. A discussion (even preliminary) or an ablation dissecting which aspects of sparsity drive the observed gains would substantially strengthen the work.

Quality:
- The paper appears technically sound, with clear asymptotic analyses supporting the claimed time and space complexities and careful engineering for efficient BPTT. The experimental evidence is broad and compelling. The main gap is the lack of analysis explaining the striking curriculum and extrapolation gains, which would help readers understand when and why SAM provides benefits beyond compute savings.

Clarity:
- Overall clearly written and well organized, with problem setup, architectural choices, and results presented coherently. The paper would benefit from a dedicated discussion of the mechanisms behind the improved learning under curriculum and long-range generalization.

Originality:
- The combination of end-to-end differentiable sparse access (both read and write), ANN-based addressing, and constant-space BPTT is novel and addresses a central bottleneck in prior MANNs. Adapting the approach to related architectures further underscores its originality and generality.

Significance:
- High. If reproducible and robust, the reported efficiency and scaling gains materially change the practical landscape for memory-augmented models and open the door to real-world applications. The strong out-of-distribution sequence generalization is particularly noteworthy.

Pros for acceptance:
- Large, well-documented speed and memory improvements.
- Strong results on synthetic tasks, curriculum scaling, and long-range generalization.
- General approach likely applicable across MANN variants.
- Moves the field toward practical deployment.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing analysis of why sparsity improves learning dynamics and generalization beyond computational savings.

Recommendation:
- I lean strongly toward acceptance. This is potentially a groundbreaking contribution that makes memory-augmented neural networks substantially more practical. I encourage the authors to add discussion (and, if possible, ablations) on why sparse access improves curriculum learning and extrapolation, even if the analysis is preliminary.
