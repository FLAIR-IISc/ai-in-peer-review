- Core contribution: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory using ANN to scale MANNs.
- Complexity gains: Reduces access time from linear to sub-linear (logarithmic) and reduces per-timestep space from linear to constant via sparse backpropagation.
- Relation to prior work: Positions SAM as addressing scalability limits of dense-attention MANNs like NTM and Memory Networks.
- Significance: Solves a major bottleneck enabling training on much larger memory sizes and longer sequences than previously feasible.
- Empirical validation: Strong benchmarks showing orders-of-magnitude speed/memory improvements and matching or outperforming dense models on algorithmic tasks.
- Practical scalability: Demonstrates ability to scale to sequences of hundreds of thousands of steps, a notable practical achievement.
- Generality: Shows sparse-access approach can be adapted to other architectures (e.g., DNC/SDNC) and achieves SOTA on bAbI without supervision.
- Originality concern: Core idea of using hashing/ANN for content-based attention has prior use in read-only Memory Networks; novelty needs clearer positioning.
- Reproducibility issue: Paper lacks sufficient detail on synthetic task data generation and does not provide code, hindering replication.
- Non-differentiability analysis missing: Top-K discrete selection is non-differentiable; paper lacks discussion of why gradients through selected cells suffice and where this might fail.
- Sensitivity and failure modes: No analysis of sensitivity to K or potential failure cases of the hard-selection approximation versus RL-based discrete access.
- Terminology refinement: Describing Omniglot as "real world" or "non-synthetic" is misleading; more precise language is recommended.
- Suggested fixes: Provide detailed dataset generation procedures and release code, analyze the discrete top-K approximation (sensitivity to K, limitations, comparison to RL), and clarify dataset terminology.
