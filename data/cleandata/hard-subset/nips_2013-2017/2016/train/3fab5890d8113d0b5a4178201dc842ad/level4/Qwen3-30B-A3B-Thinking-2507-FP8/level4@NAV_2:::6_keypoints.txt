Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN that restricts reads/writes to sparse subsets and uses approximate nearest-neighbor data structures for content addressing.

Achieves asymptotic optimality in space/time, enabling training with 100k+ memory slots and reducing physical memory by ~3,000× and runtime by ~1,000× versus dense baselines.

Maintains or improves performance on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) and real tasks (Omniglot one-shot, bAbI QA), showing sparse access preserves representational power.

Provides rigorous technical contribution with asymptotic complexity analysis, theoretical justification for sparse approximation, and thorough empirical validation.

Implementation details (LRU-like memory management, efficient backprop through sparse updates) are well-designed and convincingly support claims.

Comprehensive comparisons and ablation studies against dense variants (DAM), NTM, LSTM demonstrate sparsity does not harm learning efficiency.

Writing is clear and well-organized; figures and supplementary materials effectively support reproducibility.

Minor clarity issue: background could briefly define the write operation’s purpose for non-specialist readers.

Minor methodological concern: Figure 4’s “same physical memory vs same memory size” comparison may be misleading; identical memory-size comparisons would better isolate algorithmic effects.

Original and significant: resolves a long-standing MANN scalability bottleneck distinct from prior RL or discrete-addressing approaches (e.g., NTM, DNC).

High practical impact: enables previously intractable tasks (very long sequences, millions of slots) and advances deployability of MANNs in industry.

Strengths summarized: asymptotic optimality, demonstrated large-scale scalability, generality to other MANNs, solid theoretical/experimental justification, polished presentation.

Conclusion: technically sound, significant, and well-presented work; strongly recommended for acceptance.
