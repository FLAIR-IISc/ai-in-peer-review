The authors leverage fast approximate nearest neighbor search and arg-top-K operations to efficiently compute attention weights within a Neural Turing Machine framework. Their experiments demonstrate that this approach maintains training performance and enables scaling to larger problem instances. While the core idea builds upon existing work – notably, section 3.3 of the Memory Networks paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf) and subsequent memory network research employing large-scale memories and hashing for efficient lookups – its application to *writable* memories appears novel. Even if the work doesn’t represent a substantial conceptual advance, the practical challenges of implementation are valuable to the community. Therefore, I recommend acceptance with revisions.

First, reproducibility is a significant concern. The current descriptions, particularly of the experimental tasks, are insufficient. Given the lack of publicly available code for the original NTM tasks and the absence of a standardized implementation, a detailed description of task construction is essential. I also strongly encourage the authors to release their experimental code.

Second, a more thorough analysis of the method’s limitations is needed. The authors frequently characterize their model as “smooth,” yet acknowledge the discrete nature of the argmax (or K-argmax) operation. With $K=1$, the model closely resembles the MemNN-WSH model described in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf (which did not utilize hashing), and that model exhibited inferior performance compared to fully smooth alternatives. Is this difference attributable to $K=1$, the specific tasks employed, or other factors?  A discussion of failure cases and an investigation into the conditions under which training succeeds would significantly enhance the paper’s contribution. The model performs a discrete action, but training doesn’t explicitly account for this (unlike the approach in http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). The authors’ success in this regard is noteworthy, but understanding the boundaries of its applicability is crucial.  I recommend removing section 3.6, and suggest revising the description of the Omniglot dataset to avoid characterizing it as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
