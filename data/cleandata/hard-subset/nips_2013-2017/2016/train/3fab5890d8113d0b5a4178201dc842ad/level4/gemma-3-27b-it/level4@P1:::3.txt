This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This area, pioneered by Neural Turing Machines, aims to overcome the limitations of traditional recurrent models like LSTMs by decoupling memory capacity from model parameters. The key contribution of this work is constraining both reads and writes to external memory to a sparse subset of memory words, rather than operating on the entire memory. The authors demonstrate the theoretical optimality of their approach and empirically validate it, showing significant performance improvements in section 4.1. They further evaluate the learning dynamics introduced by this sparsity using standard NTM tasks – copy, associative recall, and priority sort – in section 4.2, demonstrating comparable or even improved learning efficiency for some tasks. The inclusion of a real-world example using the Omniglot dataset (section 4.5) is also a strong point. The implementation is based on Torch7. 

The core idea is straightforward and novel, with impressive speedups demonstrated compared to the original NTM. The observation that sparse reads and writes can *benefit* early-stage learning in certain cases (section 4.2) is particularly interesting. It would be valuable for future work to explore under what conditions this improvement occurs and whether different approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) approaches could further enhance SAM's performance. Expanding the analysis of ANN choices, specifically investigating alternatives like Mondrian forests or LSH forests (mentioned in the discussion), could provide further insights.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that clearly presents its assumptions, architecture (detailed in the supplementary material), and results. It is an engaging and promising contribution to the field. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a novel and effective sparse memory access scheme.
*   **Technical Soundness:**  Provides theoretical justification for the approach and supports claims with thorough experimental evaluation.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks—scalability—and demonstrates significant speed and memory improvements.
*   **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-organized and clearly explains the methodology.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Shows comparable or improved performance on established tasks and demonstrates scaling to larger problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   While the paper demonstrates improved learning in some cases with sparsity, a more comprehensive investigation of the conditions under which sparsity aids or hinders learning would be beneficial.
*   The discussion of ANN approaches is somewhat brief; a more detailed comparison and exploration of alternatives could strengthen the work.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims based on both theoretical analysis and experimental results. It presents a complete piece of work, honestly evaluating both strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized, though some sections (e.g., the detailed description of the ANN implementations) could benefit from further clarification.

**Originality:** The proposed approach is a novel contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks, offering a significant improvement in scalability.

**Significance:** The results are important, as they address a key limitation of MANNs and open up the possibility of applying these models to more complex and real-world problems.
