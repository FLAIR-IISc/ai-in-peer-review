

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), addressing a key limitation in architectures like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). The core contribution lies in constraining all read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than the full memory space. This sparsity mechanism significantly reduces computational overhead while maintaining the ability to learn complex tasks beyond traditional LSTM capabilities. The authors theoretically analyze SAM's optimality and demonstrate its efficacy through rigorous experiments across standard NTM benchmarks and a real-world document retrieval task.

The paper's most compelling strength is its clear articulation of SAM's operational principle: by restricting access to a small, dynamically selected subset of memory (e.g., via a learned selector), SAM avoids the quadratic complexity of full-memory access in NTMs. Section 4.1 validates this with reduced latency in memory operations, and Section 4.2 presents a nuanced analysis of learning costs across three NTM tasks—Copy, Associative Recall, and Priority Sort. Crucially, SAM achieves lower total costs than NTM on Associative Recall and Priority Sort, demonstrating that sparsity can accelerate learning without sacrificing task performance. The inclusion of a real-world document retrieval benchmark in Section 4.4 further grounds the method in practical applicability, with results showing SAM's efficiency on Torch7.

The writing is exceptionally clear, with all assumptions, architecture details (fully documented in the appendix), and experimental setup well-justified. The linear or ANN-based selector for sparse access (Section 3.2) is straightforward yet innovative, and the speedup over NTM is substantial and well-documented. The observation that sparsity can *benefit early-stage learning* (Section 4.2) is particularly insightful, suggesting SAM may help mitigate the "cold start" problem in MANNs.

However, the paper could strengthen its impact by addressing two points. First, while SAM outperforms NTM on specific tasks, the authors do not explicitly identify *under which conditions* sparsity improves learning. A deeper analysis—e.g., correlating task complexity or memory size with SAM's advantage—would help readers generalize the approach. Second, the paper focuses on a single selector mechanism (linear/ANN); exploring how alternative architectures (e.g., attention-based selectors) might interact with SAM could unlock further gains. These points are minor but would elevate the work from a strong technical contribution to a more broadly applicable framework.

The experiments are thorough and well-designed, with ablation studies confirming SAM's necessity. The real-world example in Section 4.4 is a welcome addition, though it would be valuable to see quantitative comparisons against non-MANN baselines (e.g., standard RNNs or Transformers) to contextualize SAM's relative gains. The theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) is concise but sufficient for the paper's scope.

Overall, this is a well-executed, timely contribution to MANNs. SAM’s elegant solution to memory access efficiency directly addresses a critical bottleneck in the field, and the empirical results convincingly support its advantages. The clarity of presentation and robustness of experiments make this a valuable addition to the literature. With minor elaboration on the generalization conditions (as noted), the paper would be even more impactful. It is a strong candidate for acceptance.
