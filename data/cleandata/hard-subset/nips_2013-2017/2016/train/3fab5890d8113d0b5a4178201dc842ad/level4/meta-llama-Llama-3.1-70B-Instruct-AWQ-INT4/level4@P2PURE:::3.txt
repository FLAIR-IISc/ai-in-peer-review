This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) in memory-augmented neural network architectures. This research area is relatively recent, dating back to the invention of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), which enable neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution of this paper is that all writes to and reads from external memory are constrained to a fixed size, specifically a subset of the memory words, rather than being of unlimited size. The authors rigorously prove the methodology and its optimality, demonstrating that this approach can achieve good read and write performance due to the reduced memory size, as shown in section 4.1. Furthermore, the authors investigate the learning costs associated with introducing sparsity in section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks, including copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The results show that the combined costs are lower than those of NTMs for some tasks (i.e., 2 and 3). The inclusion of real-world examples in section 4.4 is also appreciated. The benchmarking is performed using Torch7, and the idea is both straightforward and novel. The significant speedup compared to NTMs is impressive. The observation that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear or artificial neural network (ANN), can benefit early-stage learning in some cases (section 4.2) is particularly interesting. To further enhance the paper, it would be helpful if the authors could elaborate on how to generalize their approach to other cases, exploring the conditions under which learning could be improved, or investigating the application of different ANN approaches with SAM. Overall, the paper is well-written, with clear explanations of assumptions and architecture, which is detailed in the appendix. It is an engaging read.
