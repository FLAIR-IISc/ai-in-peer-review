This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs). This work contributes to the burgeoning field of MANNs, an area that has gained significant traction since the introduction of the Neural Turing Machine (NTM). Such architectures aim to overcome the limitations of traditional recurrent models like LSTMs by equipping them with an external memory component.

The primary contribution of this paper is the introduction of a sparsity constraint on memory operations. Unlike the NTM, which employs dense attention over the entire memory bank for every read and write, SAM restricts these operations to a small, fixed-size subset of memory locations. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approach and analyze its properties. This sparse access scheme is well-motivated, as it intuitively leads to significant improvements in computational performance by reducing the scope of each memory operation. The experimental results in Section 4.1 empirically validate this, demonstrating substantial and impressive speedups over the standard NTM architecture.

Beyond computational efficiency, the authors astutely investigate the impact of this sparsity on learning dynamics. In Section 4.2, they evaluate SAM on several standard algorithmic tasks (copy, associative recall, and priority sort) commonly used to benchmark MANNs. The results indicate that for the associative recall and priority sort tasks, the combined learning and computational costs for SAM are lower than those for the standard NTM. A particularly interesting finding is that the inductive bias introduced by sparse access can benefit learning, especially in the early stages of training. This constraint appears to act as a form of regularization, potentially simplifying the credit assignment problem for the controller.

A significant strength of the paper is its application of SAM to a real-world task in Section 4.4, which demonstrates the practical viability of the proposed method beyond synthetic benchmarks. The experiments, implemented in Torch7, are thorough and well-documented.

While the paper presents compelling results, it could be further strengthened by a more in-depth discussion on the conditions under which this sparse access benefits learning. For instance, a characterization of the problem types or data structures where SAM is most likely to outperform dense attention models would be highly valuable to the community. Furthermore, exploring the interplay between SAM and different controller architectures (e.g., more complex ANNs for selecting the memory subset) could be a fruitful direction for future research and would provide deeper insight into the method's potential.

In summary, this is a well-written and well-structured paper presenting a conceptually simple yet novel and effective idea. The assumptions are clearly stated, the experimental evaluation is convincing, and the inclusion of detailed architectural information in the appendix is commendable. This paper is a valuable and interesting contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks.
