

The paper introduces the Sparse Access Memory architecture for neural networks augmented with memory, such as Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. This approach achieves significant runtime and memory efficiency improvements—three orders of magnitude—compared to existing memory-augmented neural networks. Additionally, the architecture enables substantial performance gains in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to sequences up to 200,000 in length, far exceeding the training sequences of up to 10,000. These results represent a significant advancement in the field.  

The field of memory-augmented neural networks, initiated by Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, has seen promising research but has not yet reached practical applicability. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements demonstrated here suggest that memory-augmented networks are now practically viable, with potentially far-reaching implications.  

The sole omission is a discussion of the mechanisms underlying the substantial improvements in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to sequences orders of magnitude longer than seen in training. Runtime and memory efficiency alone cannot explain these gains; other factors must be at play. The authors should address whether the Sparse Access Memory architecture functions analogously to sparse auto-encoders in other contexts—this perspective would be valuable to readers, even if preliminary.
