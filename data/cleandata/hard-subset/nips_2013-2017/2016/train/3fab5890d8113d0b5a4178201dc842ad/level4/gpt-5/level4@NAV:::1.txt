The authors employ fast approximate nearest neighbor structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine. Their experiments indicate that this does not harm training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger-scale problems. The novelty here is limited; see, for example, section 3.3 of the memory networks paper http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf. There are now several memory-network papers that handle memories with millions of items and use hashing to make lookups scalable. However, I have not seen these tools applied to writeable memories, and even without a substantial conceptual advance, the practical engineering required to make this work is worth reporting. I recommend acceptance, but with revisions.

1: Reproducibility is a concern. It would be challenging to replicate these results from the current descriptions. The task descriptions are especially thin; since the original NTM task code has not been released and there is no standard version of these tasks, it is essential that the paper provide precise, careful descriptions of how the tasks are constructed. I also ask the authors to commit to releasing the code for their experiments.

2: Failure modes and boundaries. When does this approach fail? Does it always work? The authors often describe their model as smooth, yet the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth. With K=1 in the read-only setting, their model closely resembles MemNN-WSH in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf (though that model does not use hashing), and that model was reported to perform worse than fully smooth alternatives. Is this due to K=1? The task choice? The paper would be stronger with an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of which tasks/setups enable successful training: the model makes discrete decisions, and training does not account for this (in contrast to http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Showing that training works under these conditions is a useful contribution, but clarifying the limits would be better. I also suggest removing section 3.6, and avoiding describing Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
