Addresses scalability bottleneck in MANNs by introducing Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to avoid dense read/write costs.  
SAM uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) structures for sparse content-based reads and a sparse write scheme updating read or least-recently-used slots.  
Introduces an efficient backpropagation-through-time that tracks only sparse memory modifications to avoid huge caching costs.  
Significance: enables MANNs to scale to massive memories and very long time horizons, opening applications like long-form QA and large-scale language modeling.  
Demonstrates applicability and generality by integrating SAM ideas with existing models such as the Differentiable Neural Computer.  
Technical quality: sound complexity analysis with well-argued asymptotic optimality.  
Empirical evidence: dramatic speed and memory improvements shown in benchmarks (orders of magnitude).  
Empirical evidence: synthetic-task experiments show sparse approximation does not harm—and can improve—learning and data efficiency.  
Empirical evidence: curriculum scaling experiments push sequence lengths far beyond dense models’ capabilities.  
Originality: novel integration of ANN data structures into differentiable memory and a clever rollback BPTT implementation.  
Clarity: paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly motivated with effective figures.  
Weakness: write mechanism is heuristic (recently read or LRU) and may be less flexible than content-based writes; potential failure modes not deeply explored.  
Weakness: ANN introduces extra hyperparameters and engineering choices (k-d tree vs LSH, rebuild frequency) whose sensitivity isn’t fully discussed.  
Overall: significant, technically sound, and empirically powerful contribution with minor limitations.
