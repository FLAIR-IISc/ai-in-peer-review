This paper presents a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing a sparse read and write scheme, dubbed Sparse Access Memory (SAM). The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory reductions while maintaining comparable performance to dense models on various tasks.

The main idea of SAM is to constrain reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words, allowing for efficient computation and memory usage. The authors also introduce a sparse memory management scheme to track memory usage and find unused blocks for recording new information.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its components. The experimental results are impressive, showing that SAM can scale to tasks requiring large amounts of memory and long sequence lengths.

However, I do have some concerns and suggestions for improvement.

Firstly, while the authors provide a good overview of the SAM architecture, the descriptions of the tasks and experiments could be more detailed. For example, the construction of the tasks and the hyperparameter settings are not clearly described. I would like to see more information on how the tasks were created and how the hyperparameters were chosen.

Secondly, the authors should commit to releasing the code for their experiments to facilitate replication and further research.

Thirdly, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the failure cases and the limitations of the SAM approach. The authors note that the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth, but they do not provide a detailed discussion of how this affects the performance of the model. I would like to see more analysis on what kinds of tasks or setups allow the SAM training to work and what are the limits of the approach.

Lastly, I suggest removing section 3.6, as it seems unnecessary and does not add much value to the paper. Additionally, I would recommend not referring to the Omniglot data as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as it is a well-known benchmark dataset.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but I would like to see some changes to address the above concerns.
