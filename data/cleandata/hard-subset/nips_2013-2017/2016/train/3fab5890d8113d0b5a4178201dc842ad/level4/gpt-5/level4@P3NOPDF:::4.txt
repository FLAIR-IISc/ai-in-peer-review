Summary
The paper proposes an end-to-end differentiable memory access mechanism that maintains the representational capacity of the original Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while training far more efficiently with very large memories. The problem—scaling neural architectures with external memory—is timely and important. The authors present clear motivation, a coherent design, and a well-articulated solution that advances the practicality of differentiable memory systems.

Strengths
- Significance: Efficiently scaling memory access is central to tasks requiring long-term dependencies, algorithmic reasoning, and large-scale retrieval. The potential impact is substantial.
- Novelty and technical depth: The paper introduces several original ideas that are both elegant and technically sound. The approach preserves the expressive power of NTMs while addressing the core bottleneck of memory size.
- Clarity: The exposition is concise and well structured. The key concepts, design choices, and high-level intuition are clearly communicated, making the work accessible without sacrificing rigor.
- Practicality: By emphasizing training efficiency with large memories, the work moves differentiable memory mechanisms closer to real-world applicability.

Areas for Improvement and Questions
- Computational complexity: Please provide a more explicit analysis of the time and space complexity per read and write as a function of memory size. Clear asymptotics and concrete wall-clock benchmarks would strengthen the scalability claims.
- Comparisons and ablations: It would be helpful to see thorough empirical comparisons against strong baselines (NTM variants, DNC, Memory Networks, or other sparse/differentiable access schemes), especially at scales where NTMs typically fail. Ablation studies isolating each novel component would clarify which design choices are most critical.
- Stability and optimization: Training large, sharp attention distributions over memory can be unstable. Discuss the role of temperature/softmax sharpness, normalization, and any regularizers used to control gradient variance or address collapse/interference.
- Representational equivalence: The claim of preserving NTM-level representational power is central. A more formal statement (e.g., conditions under which the proposed access scheme is equivalent) and a succinct proof sketch or citation would make this claim more convincing.
- Read/write semantics: Clarify how the method handles write conflicts, memory reuse, and long-horizon credit assignment. Do sparse updates or gating mechanisms introduce bias or hinder credit propagation?
- Generalization: If possible, evaluate or discuss how performance scales as memory grows by orders of magnitude, and whether the method generalizes to out-of-distribution memory sizes without retuning.
- Practical guidance: Provide hyperparameter guidelines, initialization strategies, and implementation details that matter at scale (e.g., batching strategies, numerical stability tricks). Pseudocode for the memory access routine would aid reproducibility.

Minor Suggestions
- Improve notation consistency and explicitly define all operators used in the access mechanism.
- Include a brief discussion of limitations and settings where the approach may underperform (e.g., extremely sparse, non-differentiable access patterns).

Overall Assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that addresses a central bottleneck in differentiable memory architectures. The approach is innovative, well motivated, and clearly presented, with high potential impact for scaling neural models that rely on large external memories. With expanded empirical comparisons, clearer complexity analysis, and more formal support for the representational claims, the paper would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
