This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) in memory-augmented neural network architecture. The research area of memory-augmented neural networks is fairly recent, with the invention of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) being a notable example. This augmentation allows neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models.

The primary contribution of this paper is the introduction of a sparse memory access scheme, where all writes to and reads from external memory are constrained to a fixed size subset of the memory words, rather than an unlimited size. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the methodology and its optimality, demonstrating that it achieves good read and write performance due to reduced memory size.

The paper also investigates the learning costs of introducing sparsity in several standard NTM tasks, including copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The results show that the combined costs are lower than those of the NTM for some tasks, indicating that the sparse memory access scheme can be beneficial in certain cases.

One of the strengths of the paper is its inclusion of real-world examples, such as the Babi tasks, which test various aspects of simple reasoning. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with other models and an analysis of the effects of sparsity on learning.

The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and architecture. The assumptions are laid out clearly, and the appendix provides additional details about the architecture.

The idea of using sparse reads and writes is novel and interesting, and the results show significant speedup compared to the NTM. The fact that sparse reads and writes can actually benefit early-stage learning in some cases is also noteworthy.

One potential area for improvement is the generalization of the proposed method to other cases. The authors could elaborate on the conditions under which the learning could be improved and explore different approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) approaches with SAM. This would be helpful for the audience and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is novel and interesting. The results show significant speedup compared to the NTM, and the inclusion of real-world examples adds to the paper's value.
