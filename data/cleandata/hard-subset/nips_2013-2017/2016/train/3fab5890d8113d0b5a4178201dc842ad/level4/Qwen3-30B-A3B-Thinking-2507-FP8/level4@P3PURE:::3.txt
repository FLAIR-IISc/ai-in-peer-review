

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks. Building upon the recent Neural Turing Machine (NTM) framework, SAM constrains all write and read operations to a fixed-size subset of the external memory, rather than enabling unlimited access. The authors prove the methodology's optimality and demonstrate its performance benefits in Section 4.1, where reduced memory access size leads to improved read/write efficiency. The paper further evaluates SAM using standard NTM tasks (copy, associated call, and priority sort) in Section 4.2, showing that the combined computational cost is lower than NTM for tasks 2 and 3. The authors also present a compelling real-world example in Section 4.4, benchmarked on Torch7. The core idea is straightforward yet novel, with significant speedups over NTM, and the observation that sparse access via linear or ANN mechanisms can benefit early-stage learning (Section 4.2) is particularly insightful. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should elaborate on the specific conditions under which sparsity improves learning performance and explore generalizations to other task domains or alternative ANN approaches with SAM. Overall, the paper is well-written, with all assumptions clearly stated and the architecture detailed in the appendix. It presents a compelling advancement in memory-augmented network design.
