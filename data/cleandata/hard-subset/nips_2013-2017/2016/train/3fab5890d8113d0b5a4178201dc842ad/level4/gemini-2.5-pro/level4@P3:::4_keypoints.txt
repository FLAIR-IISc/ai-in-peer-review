- Identifies MANNs' scalability bottleneck: dense read/write cause O(N) time and O(NT) BPTT space.
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) achieving asymptotic time/space optimality while remaining end-to-end differentiable.
- Sparse reads: read a constant K memory slots using ANN data structures, reducing search from O(N) to ~O(log N).
- Sparse writes: restrict writes to a few locations (recent reads + LRU) to localize updates and cut computation.
- Efficient BPTT: store only sparse modifications and roll them back during backprop, lowering memory from O(NT) to O(T)+O(N).
- Method generalizes to other MANNs (e.g., DNC).
- Strong theoretical analysis supporting the claims (Supplementary A).
- Empirical benchmarks show orders-of-magnitude improvements (e.g., ~1,000x speed, ~3,000x less memory).
- Comprehensive experiments on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning.
- Sparse approximation does not hurt performance and sometimes improves learning compared to dense models.
- Demonstrates scaling to sequences up to 200,000 steps—beyond prior dense MANN capabilities.
- Careful comparisons to appropriate baselines, including a dense ablation (DAM).
- Minor weakness: ANN index introduces extra complexity and hyperparameters (e.g., rebuild timing) with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and provides enough detail for reproduction.
- Highly original: novel integration of ANN/sparse attention into differentiable MANNs and a clever rollback BPTT.
- High significance: enables training MANNs with millions of slots and very long sequences, likely to drive follow-up work.
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a critical problem, technically elegant, massive empirical gains, broad evaluation, high impact, excellent presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: none reported.
