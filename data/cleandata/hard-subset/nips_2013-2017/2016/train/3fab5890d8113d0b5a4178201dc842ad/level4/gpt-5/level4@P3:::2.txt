Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes to achieve sublinear time and constant per-step space with respect to memory size. The key ideas are: (1) top-K sparse content-based reads implemented via an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), reducing read complexity to O(log N); (2) sparse writes restricted to previously read locations and to the least recently used slot, tracked via a lightweight usage data structure; and (3) an efficient backpropagation-through-time (BPTT) scheme that stores and reverts only the sparse per-step memory modifications, yielding O(1) per-step space. The paper claims asymptotically optimal space and time under reasonable assumptions (details in the supplement). The approach is demonstrated on classic NTM tasks, long-horizon curricula, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning. The authors also sketch how to adapt the method to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), reporting large speedups and strong bAbI performance without supervision of memory access.

This work sits squarely in the line of memory-augmented models such as Neural Turing Machines (Graves et al., 2014), Memory Networks and End-to-End Memory Networks (Weston et al., Sukhbaatar et al.), and the Differentiable Neural Computer (Graves et al., 2016). It targets a well-known bottleneck of content-based attention over large memories: dense O(N) reads/writes and O(NT) memory for BPTT. Related attempts at scalability include reinforcement learning–based discrete addressing (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015). SAM retains (mostly) differentiable training while achieving sparsity through engineering choices and data structures.

Strengths:
- Significance: The scaling problem for MANNs is important, and the proposed combination of sparse reads/writes, efficient memory management, and reversible BPTT addresses a practical barrier to using large memories. The reported empirical speedups (up to three orders of magnitude) and drastic reductions in physical memory usage are compelling.
- Empirical scope: Experiments cover algorithmic tasks, long-sequence curricula (thousands of steps without truncation), bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning. Sparse models achieve comparable or better data efficiency than dense baselines in several settings and demonstrate strong scalability.
- Practicality: The reversible BPTT scheme with sparse deltas is simple and generally useful. The memory allocation policy (update recent reads, overwrite least recently used) is intuitive and effective.
- Generality: The adaptation to DNC suggests the approach can extend to other MANNs.

Weaknesses:
- Quality/novelty: The main technical components—top-K sparse attention via ANN, LRU-style allocation, and reversible sparse updates for BPTT—are straightforward and largely engineering-oriented. The theoretical “asymptotically optimal” claim is relegated to the supplement and depends on assumptions that are not clearly stated or justified in the main paper.
- Differentiability and approximation: The use of an external ANN index introduces a non-differentiable approximation in the addressing mechanism. The paper provides limited analysis of how this affects gradient bias/variance and learning dynamics, beyond empirical results. A deeper ablation (varying K, ANN types/parameters, and exact vs approximate search) would strengthen the case.
- Fairness and scope of comparisons: In several comparisons, dense baselines use very small memories (e.g., 64 slots) while SAM uses up to millions, chosen to match physical memory usage over a fixed sequence length. This makes capacity and complexity confounded; it would be helpful to present accuracy vs. wall-clock vs. memory curves across a range of capacities for all methods. Comparisons to discrete-addressing MANNs or other sparse attention variants are missing.
- Clarity and completeness: Key mechanisms (usage data structure, rollback algorithm, complexity assumptions for optimality) are described briefly and pushed to the supplement. Readers would benefit from more detail or pseudocode in the main text. The conditions under which kd-trees vs. LSH are used, their rebuild schedules, and their effect on accuracy and speed deserve more discussion.
- Significance beyond synthetic tasks: While Omniglot is a useful benchmark, it would be valuable to see results on real-world sequence modeling (e.g., language modeling or machine translation) where large-memory MANNs are often motivated.

Quality:
- The experimental results convincingly demonstrate speed and memory reductions and show that the sparse approximations can learn effectively on several tasks. However, some claims (optimal complexity; effect of approximate addressing on learning) need stronger theoretical or empirical support in the main paper.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, but central algorithmic and theoretical elements are too condensed or deferred. Explicitly stating the assumptions behind the optimality claim, providing pseudocode for the rollback mechanism, and detailing the ANN integration would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The contribution is primarily in the integration of known ideas—sparse top-K addressing with ANN, LRU allocation, and reversible updates—into a coherent, efficient MANN. While not highly novel theoretically, it is a meaningful systems contribution.

Significance:
- If broadly adopted, the approach could enable MANNs to scale to much larger contexts. The demonstrated speedups and long-horizon training are practically important.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a central scalability bottleneck with a simple, effective design.
- Strong empirical speed/memory benefits with competitive or improved accuracy.
- Demonstrates very long sequence training without truncation.
- Potentially general to multiple MANN architectures (e.g., DNC).

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited technical novelty; heavy reliance on engineering and well-known data structures.
- Optimality claim insufficiently justified in the main paper.
- Approximate, non-differentiable addressing not analyzed theoretically; ablations limited.
- Comparisons could be more balanced; real-world applications limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Bring the key theoretical assumptions and proofs (or at least clear statements) from the supplement into the main text; quantify constants in complexity where possible.
- Provide ablations on K, ANN type/parameters, and exact vs approximate search to quantify accuracy–speed trade-offs and the impact on gradients.
- Include comparisons to discrete-addressing MANNs and/or other sparse attention baselines.
- Present fairness-controlled plots (e.g., performance vs. wall-clock vs. memory footprint) across a range of capacities for all models.
- Add algorithmic details (pseudocode) for the rollback and usage-tracking mechanisms in the main paper.
- Explore at least one real-world sequence modeling task to demonstrate broader impact.

Recommendation:
Weak reject. The paper targets an important problem and shows impressive empirical efficiency, but the technical novelty is modest and several claims and comparisons need stronger support. Strengthening the theoretical justification and broadening/clarifying the empirical analysis would make this a strong submission.
