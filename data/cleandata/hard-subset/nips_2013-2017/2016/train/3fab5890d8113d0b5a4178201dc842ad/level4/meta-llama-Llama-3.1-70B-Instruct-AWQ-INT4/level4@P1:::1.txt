This paper proposes a novel neural memory architecture, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), that scales more efficiently than previous memory-augmented neural networks. The authors introduce a sparse read and write scheme, leveraging efficient data structures, to reduce the computational and memory overhead. The key innovation is the use of fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations for computing attention weights, allowing the model to train on larger problems without degrading performance.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of SAM on various tasks, including algorithmic problems, question answering, and one-shot learning on the Omniglot dataset. The results show that SAM achieves comparable or better performance than dense models, while significantly reducing computational and memory costs. The authors also provide an analysis of the time and space complexity of SAM, showing that it is asymptotically optimal.

While the paper presents a practical contribution to the field, the novelty of the work is somewhat limited. Similar ideas have been explored in previous memory network papers, which use memories with millions of items and hashing methods to make lookups scalable. However, the application of these techniques to writable memories is a new and valuable contribution.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following changes:

1. Provide more detailed descriptions of the tasks and experiments to facilitate replication. The authors should commit to releasing the code for their experiments to ensure reproducibility.
2. Analyze and discuss the limitations of the proposed approach, including failure cases and the impact of the argmax (or K-argmax) operation on the model's performance. The authors should explore the conditions under which their model works well and those under which it may fail.
3. Remove section 3.6, as it is not essential to the main contribution of the paper.
4. Avoid referring to the Omniglot dataset as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as it is a standard benchmark dataset in the field.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but with the above revisions to improve its clarity, reproducibility, and impact.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a practical solution to the scalability issue in memory-augmented neural networks.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of their proposed architecture.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM on various tasks.

Weaknesses:

* The novelty of the work is somewhat limited, as similar ideas have been explored in previous memory network papers.
* The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations and failure cases of the proposed approach.
* The authors could improve the clarity and reproducibility of their experiments by providing more detailed descriptions and releasing their code.

Acceptance: Yes, with revisions.
