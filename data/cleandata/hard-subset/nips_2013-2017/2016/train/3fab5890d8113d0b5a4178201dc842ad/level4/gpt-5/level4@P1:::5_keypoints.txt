- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse reads/writes with ANN-based content addressing selecting K locations, yielding sublinear time and constant per-step training space via rollback BPTT.  
- Reports dramatic runtime and memory improvements: up to ~1000× faster and ~3000× less memory than dense NTMs while preserving data efficiency.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical scaling: handles very long sequences and large memories; curriculum training generalizes from length 10k to 200k.  
- Sparse-access scheme can be retrofitted to related architectures (e.g., DNC), indicating breadth and practicality.  
- Major algorithmic/systems contribution: combines sparse reads/writes, ANN addressing, and constant-space BPTT for substantial efficiency gains.  
- Empirical results are broad and compelling on canonical synthetic tasks and extrapolation benchmarks.  
- Practical significance: removes prohibitive compute/memory costs, moving MANNs toward real-world applicability.  
- Key weakness: no analysis of why sparsity improves curriculum learning and long-range extrapolation beyond computational savings.  
- Reviewer intuition: sparsity may provide an inductive bias (e.g., more discrete/modular algorithms), but paper lacks hypotheses or ablations to test this.  
- Suggestion: add discussion and/or ablations dissecting which sparsity aspects drive observed learning and generalization gains.  
- Technical quality: appears sound with clear asymptotic analysis and careful engineering for efficient BPTT.  
- Clarity: well written and organized; would benefit from a dedicated discussion of mechanisms behind improved learning/generalization.  
- Originality: novel combination of end-to-end differentiable sparse access, ANN addressing, and constant-space BPTT, plus adaptability to other MANNs.  
- Significance: high — reproducible results could materially change practical use of memory-augmented models.  
- Recommendation: reviewer leans strongly toward acceptance, urging authors to include discussion/ablations on sparsity’s role in curriculum/extrapolation.
