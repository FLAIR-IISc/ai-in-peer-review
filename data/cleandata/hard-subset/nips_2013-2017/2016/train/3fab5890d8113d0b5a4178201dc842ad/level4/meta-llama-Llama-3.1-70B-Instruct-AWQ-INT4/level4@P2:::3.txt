This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) in memory augmented neural network architecture, a research area that has gained significant attention since the invention of Neural Turing Machines. The SAM architecture allows neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional LSTM models by decoupling the memory capacity from the number of model parameters. The primary contribution of this paper is the introduction of a sparse memory access scheme, where all writes to and reads from external memory are constrained to a fixed-size subset of the memory words, rather than an unlimited size.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the methodology and its optimality, demonstrating that the SAM architecture achieves good read and write performance due to the reduced memory size. The experimental results in Section 4.1 show significant speedups compared to the Neural Turing Machine (NTM), with the SAM architecture being over 100 times faster when the memory contains one million words.

The authors also investigate the learning costs of introducing sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks, including copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The results show that the combined costs are lower than the NTM for some tasks, and the authors observe that sparse reads and writes can actually benefit early-stage learning in some cases. This finding is particularly interesting, and the authors' discussion on the potential benefits of sparse memory access is well-supported by the experimental results.

The inclusion of real-world examples in Section 4.4, such as the Babi tasks and the Omniglot dataset, demonstrates the applicability of the SAM architecture to practical problems. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of the architecture, including a comparison with other models and an analysis of the effects of sparsity on learning.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the methodology and results. The architecture is well-documented in the appendix, and the assumptions are clearly laid out. The use of Torch7 as the benchmarking framework is also a positive aspect of the paper.

One potential area for improvement is the discussion on the generalization of the SAM architecture to other cases. The authors mention that the approach can be applied to many differentiable memory architectures, but a more detailed analysis of the conditions under which the learning could be improved would be beneficial. Additionally, an exploration of different approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) approaches with SAM could provide further insights into the potential benefits and limitations of the architecture.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The introduction of the SAM architecture and the demonstration of its benefits in terms of speed and learning efficiency make this a valuable addition to the literature.
