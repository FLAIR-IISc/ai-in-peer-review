The authors present a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks, which have been shown to be promising for applications such as language modeling and machine translation. However, these models typically scale poorly in both space and time as the amount of memory grows, limiting their applicability to real-world domains.

The proposed method, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), introduces an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme that retains the representational power of the original approaches while training efficiently with very large memories. The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity and show significant improvements in empirical runtime and memory overhead over non-sparse models.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. The authors provide a thorough background on memory-augmented neural networks, including attention and content-based addressing, memory networks, and neural Turing machines. The introduction of SAM is well-motivated, and the technical details are clearly explained.

The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results show that SAM can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories, achieving significant speedups over dense models.

However, I have some reservations about the technical strength of the paper. While the idea of sparse memory access is interesting, the implementation details seem relatively straightforward. The use of efficient data structures, such as Compressed Sparse Rows (CSR), and approximate nearest neighbor search algorithms, such as randomized k-d trees and locality-sensitive hashing, are not particularly novel.

Moreover, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the trade-offs between the sparse and dense models. While the authors show that SAM can achieve significant speedups, they do not provide a detailed comparison of the representational power of the two models.

Overall, I think the paper is well-written and provides an interesting contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. However, I am not entirely convinced of its technical strength, and I would like to see more rigorous analysis and comparison with existing methods.
