- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), constraining reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words.  
- Proves the methodology and its optimality, showing improved read/write performance by reducing active memory (sec 4.1).  
- Addresses scalability limits of prior smooth-access models (NTM, Memory Networks) by introducing sparse memory access to avoid linear per-step overhead.  
- Demonstrates significant speedup over Neural Turing Machine while maintaining comparable task performance.  
- Evaluated on synthetic tasks and real-world datasets (e.g., Omniglot) with thorough experiments.  
- Finds sparsity can lower learning costs and sometimes benefits early-stage learning.  
- Paper is well-written with clear explanations, assumptions, and appendix detailing the architecture.  
- Strength: Novel and interesting idea of sparse reads and writes in memory-augmented networks.  
- Strength: Significant empirical speedup relative to NTM.  
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation across tasks and datasets.  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of the conditions under which sparsity improves learning costs.  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of different ANN approaches integrated with SAM.  
- Suggestions: Provide more analysis on when sparsity helps, explore different ANN methods with SAM, and apply SAM to more real-world tasks/datasets.  
- Recommendation: Accept — valuable contribution; reviewer rating 9/10.
