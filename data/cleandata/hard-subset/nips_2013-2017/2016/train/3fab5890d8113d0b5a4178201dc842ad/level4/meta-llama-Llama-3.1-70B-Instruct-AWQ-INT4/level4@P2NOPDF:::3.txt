This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) for memory-augmented neural network architectures, a research area that has gained significant attention since the invention of Neural Turing Machines. This innovation enables neural networks to learn beyond the limitations of traditional LSTM models. The primary contribution of this paper is that all writes to and reads from external memory are constrained to a fixed size, specifically a subset of the memory words, rather than being of unlimited size. The authors rigorously prove the methodology and its optimality, demonstrating that it can achieve improved read and write performance due to the reduced memory size, as shown in section 4.1.

Moreover, the authors assess the learning costs of introducing sparsity in section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks, including copy, associated recall, and priority sort. Notably, the combined costs are lower than those of NTM for certain tasks (i.e., associated recall and priority sort). The inclusion of real-world examples in section 4.4 is also appreciated, with the benchmark being implemented using Torch7. The idea is straightforward, yet novel, and the achieved speedup is significant compared to NTM, which is impressive. The finding that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear or artificial neural network (ANN), can benefit early-stage learning in some cases is particularly interesting (section 4.2).

To further enhance the paper, it would be beneficial if the authors could elaborate on how to generalize the approach to other cases, exploring the conditions under which learning could be improved. Additionally, investigating the application of different ANN approaches with SAM would be valuable for the audience. Overall, the paper is well-written, with clear explanations of assumptions and architecture details provided in the appendix. The text is engaging and easy to follow, making it an interesting read.

Minor suggestions for improvement include providing more details on the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning, as well as discussing potential limitations and future directions for the proposed SAM approach. Nevertheless, the paper presents a compelling contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks.
