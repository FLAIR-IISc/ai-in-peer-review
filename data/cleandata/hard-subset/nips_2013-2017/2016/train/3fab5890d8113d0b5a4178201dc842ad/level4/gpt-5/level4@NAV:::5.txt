The paper presents a Sparse Access Memory architecture for memory-augmented neural networks, including Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. It delivers striking improvements in run-time and memory efficiency—by roughly three orders of magnitude—relative to existing memory-augmented neural models.

The architecture also appears to produce notable gains under curriculum learning and supports generalization to sequences far longer than those encountered during training (from training lengths up to 10,000 to test sequences as long as 200,000). This could be a landmark contribution.

Memory-augmented neural networks, beginning with Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, constitute a rapidly advancing area. While prior work has produced intriguing ideas and experiments, it has not yet seemed ready for practical deployment. The substantial efficiency and performance advances reported here suggest these models may now be viable in practice, with potentially wide-ranging impact.

What I find missing is an analysis of why curriculum learning benefits so dramatically and why the model can extrapolate to sequence lengths so far beyond the training range. Pure gains in computational and memory efficiency do not seem sufficient to explain this, implying an additional mechanism at work. My tentative intuition is that the Sparse Memory Access architecture may play a role analogous to that of sparse autoencoders in other contexts; readers would value the authors’ perspective on this point, even if only preliminary.
