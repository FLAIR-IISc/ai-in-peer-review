- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which constrains read/write to a fixed-size subset of memory locations.
- Provides a theoretical justification for SAM and argues for its potential optimality.
- Empirical results (Sec. 4.1) show improved read/write performance from reduced memory access scope.
- Evaluated on standard NTM tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort); combined cost (access + learning) is not always lower than NTM.
- SAM shows clear advantages on Associative Recall and Priority Sort (Sec. 4.2).
- Includes a real-world example (Sec. 4.4), extending evaluation beyond synthetic NTM benchmarks.
- Idea is straightforward, novel, and yields substantial speedups compared to NTM.
- Observes that sparse reads/writes—especially with a linear layer or ANN—can benefit early-stage learning in some cases.
- Paper needs more discussion on the generalizability of the learning benefits and under what conditions they occur.
- Recommends further study of SAM’s interaction with different ANN architectures for addressing and weighting.
- Well-written, clear assumptions and architectural details in the appendix; Torch7 implementation sufficient for reproducibility; recommendation: accept.
