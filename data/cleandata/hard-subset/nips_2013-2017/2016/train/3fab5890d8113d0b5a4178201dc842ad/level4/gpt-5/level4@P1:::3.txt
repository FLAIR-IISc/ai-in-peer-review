Summary
The paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network architecture that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes to an external memory. Unlike LSTMs, whose parameter count grows quadratically with memory size, and unlike standard Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks that operate with dense, linear-time memory access, SAM uses a fixed, small number of memory slots per step, independent of total memory size. Content-based reads are made efficient via approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indices (randomized k-d trees or LSH), giving O(log N) access time; writes target previously read locations or the least-recently used slot. The authors also design an efficient backpropagation scheme that stores only sparse updates and rolls back memory states, yielding O(1) space per time step and O(T) total for BPTT. They claim asymptotic optimality under reasonable assumptions and demonstrate large empirical speed and memory gains over NTM. SAM matches or outperforms dense baselines on canonical NTM tasks, scales via curriculum to very long sequences and very large memories, performs competitively on bAbI, and achieves strong one-shot learning on Omniglot. The approach extends to a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).

Relation to prior work
SAM builds directly on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling memory size from parameters and by using content-based addressing, but introduces sparse reads/writes and ANN indices to scale training. It also relates to reinforcement learning approaches that discretize addressing (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever), but remains fully differentiable. The SDNC connection shows generality across MANN designs.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clear, end-to-end differentiable sparse memory access mechanism with an efficient BPTT rollback implementation. The use of ANN indices inside the network for content-based addressing is practical and well motivated.
- Theoretical and systems claims: Asymptotic analysis argues O(log N) per-step time and O(1) per-step space; the implementation demonstrates dramatic speedups and memory savings (orders of magnitude vs. NTM).
- Empirical validation: 
  - Benchmarks (Sec. 4.1) show up to ~1000–1600× speedups and drastic reductions in physical memory use.
  - On standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), SAM learns with comparable or better data efficiency; notably better on associative recall and priority sort.
  - Curriculum scaling to millions of memory slots and >4000 time steps without truncated BPTT; generalization to sequences much longer than seen during training.
  - bAbI results are competitive without supervised memory retrieval; the SDNC variant achieves the best reported unsupervised result the authors are aware of.
  - Real-world data: strong one-shot performance on Omniglot (Sec. 4.5), outperforming dense models, plausibly due to larger memory capacity.
- Clarity: Assumptions are stated, architecture details and data structures are described, and appendices are substantive.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Analysis of sparsity’s learning effects: Sec. 4.2 notes that sparse reads/writes (with linear or ANN retrieval) can benefit early-stage learning in some tasks. It would help to articulate conditions under which sparsity helps or hurts (e.g., task structure, memory interference, K sensitivity), and to include ablations over K and the threshold.
- ANN choices and trade-offs: While both k-d trees and LSH are tried, a more systematic comparison (e.g., accuracy vs. speed, stability under online updates, effects of rebuild frequency) would guide practitioners. Additional ANN methods (e.g., LSH forests, online balanced trees) could be informative.
- Real-world scope: Beyond Omniglot and bAbI, evaluation on larger-scale, truly real-world sequence tasks (e.g., language modeling or machine translation, as highlighted in the intro) would strengthen claims of practical impact.
- Memory management policy: The least-recently-used write scheme is simple and effective, but further analysis of its behavior under heavy reuse or rapidly changing contexts, and comparison with alternative usage measures, would clarify robustness.
- Reproducibility details: While Torch7 is noted, more explicit reporting of hyperparameter sensitivities (e.g., threshold for “non-negligible” access, rebuild cadence for ANN) would improve reproducibility.

Assessment against review criteria
- Quality: Methodologically sound with both theoretical complexity arguments and strong empirical evidence. The BPTT rollback mechanism and integration of ANN indices are carefully implemented. Some analyses (sensitivity to K/ANN choice) could be expanded.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized; assumptions and architectural choices are clear, and the appendices are helpful. Minor nit: ensure consistent section references (real-world data is Sec. 4.5).
- Originality: Novel combination of sparse memory access, ANN-based content addressing, and efficient backward pass within an end-to-end differentiable MANN. Extension to SDNC underscores generality.
- Significance: Addresses a key bottleneck in scaling MANNs; results suggest practical feasibility for very large memories and long sequences. Likely to influence future MANN design and applications.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong conceptual and practical advance in scalable differentiable memory.
- Impressive speed/memory gains without sacrificing performance; sometimes improved learning.
- Comprehensive experiments, including synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot.
- Generalizable approach (applies to DNC).

Cons against acceptance
- Limited analysis of when/why sparsity improves learning and of sensitivity to hyperparameters (K, thresholds).
- Incomplete empirical exploration of ANN design choices in the fully online setting.
- Lack of large-scale real-world sequence tasks beyond Omniglot.

Suggestions for improvement
- Elaborate on theoretical or empirical conditions where sparse access benefits optimization and generalization; include ablations on K and thresholds.
- Provide a deeper evaluation of alternative ANN approaches and their online behavior within SAM.
- Add experiments on larger, real-world sequence tasks (e.g., LM/MT) to showcase applicability.
- Clarify section references and include more implementation details for reproducibility.

Overall, this is a well-executed, novel, and impactful paper with clear writing and strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
