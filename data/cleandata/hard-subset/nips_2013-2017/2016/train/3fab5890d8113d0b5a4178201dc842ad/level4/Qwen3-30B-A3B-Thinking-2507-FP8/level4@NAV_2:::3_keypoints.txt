- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrains reads/writes to a fixed-size sparse subset using ANN indexing, achieving O(N) init, O(1) per-timestep space, and O(log N) per operation time.  
- Demonstrates major empirical gains: ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× memory reduction over NTM.  
- Maintains near-identical data efficiency on standard NTM tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort).  
- Scales to very long sequences (100,000+ timesteps).  
- Achieves strong downstream results: SDNC solves 20/21 bAbI tasks without memory-access supervision and outperforms dense MANNs on Omniglot.  
- Original and generalizable approach, distinct from RL-based scaling methods with high-variance gradients.  
- Clear exposition: detailed architecture, proofs, implementation choices (e.g., usage-based memory management), and structured experiments.  
- Weakness: superficial analysis of why sparsity improves learning for tasks like Associative Recall and Priority Sort; lacks deeper theoretical conditions.  
- Weakness: ANN evaluation limited—no comparison to alternative ANN structures (e.g., Mondrian forests) that may handle online imbalances better.  
- Recommendation: technically rigorous, transformative scalability gains with strong empirical validation; overall accept.
