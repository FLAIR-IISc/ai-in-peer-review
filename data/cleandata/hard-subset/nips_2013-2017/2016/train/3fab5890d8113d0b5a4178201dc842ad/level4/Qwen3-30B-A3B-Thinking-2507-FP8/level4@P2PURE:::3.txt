

This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), for memory-augmented neural network architectures. While this research area is relatively recent, following the introduction of Neural Turing Machines, SAM enables neural networks to surpass the limitations of traditional LSTM models. The primary contribution is that all write and read operations to external memory are constrained to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than the full memory. The authors prove the methodology and its optimality. As demonstrated in Section 4.1, this sparsity is expected to yield improved read and write performance due to the reduced memory footprint. The authors further analyze the learning costs introduced by sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks: 1) Copy, 2) Associate Call, and 3) Priority Sort. The combined costs for tasks 2 and 3 are lower than those of NTM. The inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4 is commendable, and the benchmark employs Torch7. The core idea is straightforward and novel, with significant speedups relative to NTM, which is impressive. The observation that sparse read/write operations—implemented via linear or ANN approaches—can benefit early-stage learning in specific cases (Section 4.2) is particularly interesting. For future work, it would be valuable for the authors to elaborate on how to generalize these findings (i.e., under which conditions sparse access improves learning) or explore the integration of SAM with alternative ANN approaches. Overall, the paper is well-written, with all assumptions clearly articulated and the architecture detailed in the appendix. It presents an engaging and insightful contribution.
