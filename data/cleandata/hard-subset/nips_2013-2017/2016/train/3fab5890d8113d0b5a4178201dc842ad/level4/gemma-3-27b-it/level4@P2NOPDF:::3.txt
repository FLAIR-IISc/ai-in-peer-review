This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks. This is a relatively new and exciting research area, originating with the development of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), which aim to extend the capabilities of traditional recurrent models like LSTMs by allowing networks to learn with external memory. The key contribution of this work is constraining both read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory locations, rather than allowing access to the entire memory bank. The authors provide a theoretical justification for their approach and demonstrate its potential optimality.

As shown in section 4.1, limiting memory access in this way plausibly leads to improved read and write performance due to the reduced search space. However, the paper also carefully considers the potential learning costs associated with this sparsity, evaluating the method on standard NTM tasks – copy, Associative Recall, and Priority Sort (section 4.2). The results indicate that, for some tasks (specifically Associative Recall and Priority Sort), the overall computational cost – considering both memory access and learning – is lower than that of standard NTMs. 

I particularly appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in section 4.4, which demonstrates the applicability of SAM beyond synthetic benchmarks. The implementation utilizes Torch7, and the experimental results reveal a significant speedup compared to NTMs, which is a notable achievement. The observation that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear layer or an artificial neural network, can *benefit* early-stage learning in certain scenarios (as detailed in section 4.2) is particularly interesting.

To further strengthen the paper, I would encourage the authors to explore the generalizability of these benefits. Specifically, investigating under what conditions SAM consistently improves learning, or exploring the performance of SAM with different ANN architectures for memory addressing, would be highly valuable to the research community.  

Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper. The assumptions are explicitly stated, and the architectural details are readily available in the appendix. It is an engaging and promising contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks.
