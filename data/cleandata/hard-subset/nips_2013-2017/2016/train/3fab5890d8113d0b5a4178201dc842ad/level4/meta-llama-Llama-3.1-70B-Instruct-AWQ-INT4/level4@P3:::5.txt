This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) architecture, a novel neural memory architecture designed to scale memory-augmented neural networks efficiently. The authors propose an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme that retains the representational power of original approaches while training efficiently with very large memories. The key innovation lies in the sparse read and write operations, which allow for significant reductions in computational and memory overhead.

The paper relates to previous work on Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, which have shown promise in learning algorithmic solutions to complex tasks. However, these models suffer from scalability issues, with computational overhead increasing linearly with the number of memories stored. The authors address this limitation by introducing a sparse memory management scheme that tracks memory usage and finds unused blocks of memory for recording new information.

The proposed architecture, SAM, achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, making it an attractive solution for scaling memory-augmented neural networks. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of SAM through a series of experiments on synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results show that SAM achieves comparable data efficiency to existing models while scaling to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories.

The paper's strengths lie in its innovative architecture, thorough analysis of time and space complexity, and impressive experimental results. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work, making it easy to follow their arguments. The use of approximate nearest neighbor indexes, such as FLANN's randomized k-d tree implementation and locality sensitive hashing, is a nice touch, allowing for fast inspection of nearby points in O(logN) time.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of discussion on the reasons behind the drastic performance improvements in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to sequences much longer than those observed in training. While the authors attribute the improvements to the sparse memory access architecture, a more in-depth analysis of this phenomenon would be beneficial. Additionally, the authors could provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the model to these choices.

Overall, this is a well-written and well-structured paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of neural networks augmented with memory. The authors' work has the potential to make a substantial impact on the development of more efficient and scalable memory-augmented neural networks.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and innovative architecture for scaling memory-augmented neural networks.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of time and space complexity, demonstrating the efficacy of their approach.
* The experimental results are impressive, showing significant improvements in run-time and memory efficiency.
* The paper is well-written and well-structured, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the reasons behind the drastic performance improvements in curriculum learning and generalization to longer sequences.
* The authors could provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the model to these choices.
