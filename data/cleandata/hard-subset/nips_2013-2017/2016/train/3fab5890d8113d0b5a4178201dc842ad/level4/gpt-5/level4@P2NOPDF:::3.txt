This paper proposes a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), for memory-augmented neural networks. Building on the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) line of work, the approach enables networks to operate beyond the limits of standard LSTMs. The key contribution is to restrict reads and writes to a fixed-size subset of memory locations, rather than performing dense operations over the entire memory. The authors provide theoretical justification and optimality results for this design.

Section 4.1 shows that constraining access to a small subset yields strong read/write performance due to reduced memory operations. The authors also account for the potential learning costs introduced by sparsity in Section 4.2, using standard NTM benchmarks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort. On some tasks (associative recall and priority sort), the combined costs are lower than those of the NTM, indicating that sparsity can be advantageous. I also appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4. Experiments are implemented in Torch7. Overall, the idea is straightforward and genuinely novel, with significant speedups relative to NTM—an impressive result.

An especially interesting finding is that sparse reads and writes, whether driven by a linear controller or a neural network, can in some cases improve early-stage learning (Section 4.2). It would strengthen the paper to analyze when and why sparsity helps (i.e., delineate the conditions under which learning is improved) and to evaluate SAM with a broader range of controllers or architectures to test generality.

The paper is clearly written, with assumptions explicitly stated and architectural details provided in the appendix. It is engaging and easy to follow.
