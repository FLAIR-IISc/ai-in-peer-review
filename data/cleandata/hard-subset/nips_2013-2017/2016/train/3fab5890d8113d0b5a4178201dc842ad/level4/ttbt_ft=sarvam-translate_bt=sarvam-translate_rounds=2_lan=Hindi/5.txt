This paper presents a sparse access memory architecture for memory-enhanced neural nets, similar to neural Turing machines and memory networks. This allows for significant run-time and memory efficiency improvements (in terms of scale) compared to existing neural nets. This architecture also allows for significant performance improvements in course learning settings and the ability to generalize sequences that are longer in terms of scale than those observed in training (ranging from lengths of up to 10,000 in training to lengths of up to 200,000 in testing). This is potentially an unprecedented paper. We have an actively developing field of memory-enhanced neural networks, starting with neural Turing machines and memory networks. So far, we have seen very interesting research and experimental results in that area, but they have not appeared at a level where they could be practically applied. The significant efficiency and performance improvements mentioned in the current paper are likely to mean that memory-enhanced neural networks are now ready to be used in practice. This is likely to have far-reaching implications. The current paper lacks a discussion on the reasons for significant performance improvements in course learning alone (and also for significant improvements in the ability to predict sequences with lengths beyond the training limit). Certainly, run-time and memory efficiency cannot be responsible for this, so something else is happening (my initial impression is that it is likely that the sparse memory access architecture plays a role similar to that played by sparse auto-encoders in other situations here; certainly, the reader will want to know the authors' thoughts on this, even if those thoughts are preliminary).
