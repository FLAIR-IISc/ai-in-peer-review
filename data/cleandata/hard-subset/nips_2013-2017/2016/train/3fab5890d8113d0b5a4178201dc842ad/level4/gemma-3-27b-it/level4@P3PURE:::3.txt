This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This research area has emerged recently with the development of Neural Turing Machines, enabling neural networks to potentially surpass the limitations of traditional LSTM models. The core contribution of this work is constraining both read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of external memory words, rather than allowing access to the entire memory space. The authors provide a proof of methodology and demonstrate its optimality. This constrained access plausibly leads to improved read and write performance due to the reduced memory footprint, as evidenced in section 4.1. The authors also consider the learning costs associated with introducing sparsity, evaluating SAM on standard Neural Turing Machine tasks – copy, associative recall, and priority sort – in section 4.2. The combined computational cost is lower than that of NTM for some tasks (specifically, associative recall and priority sort). I particularly appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in section 4.4. The implementation is based on Torch7, and the reported speedup relative to NTM is significant and impressive. The observation that sparse reads and writes, mediated by a linear layer or ANN, can benefit early-stage learning in certain cases is noteworthy (section 4.2). It would be valuable for the authors to elaborate on generalizing these benefits—under what conditions can learning be improved with SAM?—or to explore the performance of SAM when integrated with alternative ANN architectures. Overall, this is a well-written paper with clearly stated assumptions and a detailed architectural description in the appendix. It is a compelling and interesting read.
