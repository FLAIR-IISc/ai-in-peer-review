This paper introduces an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme that retains the representational power of original Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), but is significantly more efficient when training with very large memories. The approach, termed Sparse Access Memory (SAM), presents several novel concepts exhibiting technical sophistication. The problem addressed – the scalability of memory-augmented neural networks – is well-motivated and effectively attacked. Scaling memory systems for neural networks has substantial potential impact, and this paper introduces the problem and its solution in a clear, concise, and well-explained manner.

The authors correctly identify the limitations of recurrent neural networks like LSTMs regarding long-term memory and position SAM within the context of Memory Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) – a class of models addressing this limitation by decoupling memory capacity from model parameters. The core contribution lies in addressing the computational overhead associated with smooth read and write operations in MANNs, particularly the linear complexity with respect to memory size and the memory duplication required for backpropagation through time (BPTT). SAM tackles these issues by thresholding memory modifications to a sparse subset, alongside the use of efficient data structures for content-based read operations.

The paper demonstrates asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity for SAM, and empirical results showcase significant speedups (1,000x faster) and reduced memory consumption (3,000x less physical memory) compared to non-sparse models. Furthermore, SAM maintains comparable data efficiency to existing models on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition, while successfully scaling to tasks demanding 100,000s of time steps and large memory capacities. The adaptation of SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), achieving substantial performance gains and improved results on the Babi tasks, highlights the generality of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, with a clear theoretical justification for its efficiency gains. The claim of asymptotic optimality is compelling.
*   **Originality:** The introduction of sparse read and write operations within a differentiable memory architecture is a novel contribution. Adapting the approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer further demonstrates its versatility.
*   **Significance:** Scaling memory-augmented neural networks is a critical challenge, and the demonstrated speedups and memory reductions are highly significant. The ability to handle larger sequences and memory sizes opens up new possibilities for applying these models to more complex real-world problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The technical details are presented with sufficient clarity, and the motivations for design choices are well-explained. The inclusion of Supplementary Material is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **ANN Implementation Details:** While the use of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes is presented as a performance optimization, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the specific ANN implementations used (FLANN and LSH) and a more thorough comparison of their trade-offs. The choice of k-d trees for small word sizes and LSH for large word sizes could be further justified.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The effect of hyperparameters, particularly the discount factor in the usage statistic, could be explored more thoroughly. How sensitive is the performance to these choices, and are there any guidelines for setting them effectively?
*   **Comparison with Alternative Scaling Approaches:** The paper briefly mentions reinforcement learning-based approaches for scaling NTMs. A more detailed comparison of SAM with these alternative approaches, including a discussion of their respective advantages and disadvantages, would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound solution with significant performance gains.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results on a variety of tasks, including both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The approach is generalizable to other differentiable memory architectures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion of ANN implementations could be more detailed.
*   A more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   The comparison with alternative scaling approaches is somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM architecture offers significant advantages in terms of scalability and efficiency, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are a few areas where the paper could be improved, the overall quality and potential impact of the work are high. I recommend acceptance.
