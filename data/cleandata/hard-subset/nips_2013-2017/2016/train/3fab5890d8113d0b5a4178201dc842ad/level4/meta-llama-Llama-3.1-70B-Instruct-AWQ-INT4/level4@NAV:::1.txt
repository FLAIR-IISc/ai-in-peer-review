The authors propose the use of fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine. Their experiments demonstrate that this approach does not compromise training performance and even enables training on larger problems. While the novelty of this work is somewhat limited, as similar concepts have been explored in memory networks (e.g., http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), the application of these techniques to writable memories is a notable contribution. Although the paper may not present a groundbreaking idea, its practical aspects are worth reporting. I recommend accepting the paper, pending some revisions.

To enhance the paper's value, I suggest the following changes:

1. The authors should provide more detailed descriptions of the tasks used in their experiments, as the original NTM paper's code is not publicly available, and there is no standard version of the tasks. I also recommend that the authors commit to releasing their experiment code to facilitate replication.

2. It would be beneficial to discuss the limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed model. The authors often describe their model as smooth, but the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth. A comparison with the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf) and an analysis of the impact of K=1 on the model's performance would be insightful. Additionally, the authors should consider discussing the types of tasks and setups that allow their training to work, as their model involves discrete actions trained without considering certain aspects (unlike http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Understanding the limits of their approach would significantly enhance the paper's contribution.

Furthermore, I suggest removing section 3.6 and avoiding the description of the Omniglot data as "non-synthetic" or "real-world."
