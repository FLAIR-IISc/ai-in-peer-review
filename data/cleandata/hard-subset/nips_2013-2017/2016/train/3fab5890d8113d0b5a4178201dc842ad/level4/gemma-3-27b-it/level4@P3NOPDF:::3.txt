This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This is a rapidly developing field, spurred by the introduction of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), which aim to extend the capabilities of traditional recurrent models like LSTMs by enabling them to learn with external memory. The key contribution of this work lies in constraining both read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory locations, rather than allowing access to the entire memory space. The authors provide a theoretical justification for their methodology and demonstrate its potential optimality. 

The empirical results presented in Section 4.1 suggest that SAM can achieve improved read and write performance due to this reduced memory access scope. Furthermore, the authors thoughtfully consider the learning costs associated with introducing sparsity, evaluating SAM on standard NTM tasks – Copy, Associative Recall, and Priority Sort (Section 4.2). While the combined cost (memory access + learning) is not universally lower than NTM, SAM demonstrates advantages on certain tasks (Associative Recall and Priority Sort). The inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4, applying SAM to a task beyond the standard NTM benchmarks, is a valuable addition. 

The idea behind SAM is both straightforward and novel. The reported speedups compared to NTM are substantial and represent a significant achievement. Notably, the observation that sparse reads and writes, particularly when facilitated by a linear layer or ANN, can *benefit* early-stage learning in some scenarios (Section 4.2) is particularly interesting. This challenges the intuitive assumption that restricting access necessarily hinders learning and opens up avenues for further investigation.

However, the paper would benefit from further discussion regarding the generalizability of this beneficial learning effect. Exploring *under what conditions* SAM improves learning, or investigating its interaction with different ANN architectures used for addressing and weighting, would significantly strengthen the work and provide more concrete guidance for future research.  Specifically, the authors could explore if certain network structures are particularly well-suited to leverage the properties of SAM.

Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper. The assumptions are explicitly stated, and the architectural details are conveniently provided in the appendix. The paper is engaging and presents a promising new direction for memory-augmented neural networks. The implementation details (using Torch7) are sufficient for reproducibility. I recommend acceptance.
