- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for MANNs that constrains reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words.  
- Core contribution: sparsity in memory access reduces computational overhead while preserving ability to learn tasks beyond LSTMs.  
- Theoretical analysis argues SAM’s optimality for sparse accesses (Section 3.1).  
- Empirical results on standard NTM benchmarks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) validate SAM’s learning and efficiency.  
- SAM achieves lower total learning cost than NTM on Associative Recall and Priority Sort, showing sparsity can accelerate learning.  
- Demonstrates substantial latency/speedup improvements over full-memory NTMs (Section 4.1).  
- Real-world document retrieval experiment (Section 4.4) grounds practical applicability and shows efficiency on Torch7.  
- Writing and presentation are clear, with architecture and experimental details well-documented in the appendix.  
- Selector mechanism (linear/ANN) is simple and effective, and sparsity appears to aid early-stage learning ("cold start").  
- Ablation studies support SAM’s necessity and experimental rigor is strong.  
- Weakness: paper does not specify conditions or task characteristics under which sparsity yields improvements.  
- Weakness: only a single selector family (linear/ANN) is explored; alternatives like attention-based selectors are not evaluated.  
- Weakness: real-world results lack quantitative comparisons to non-MANN baselines (e.g., standard RNNs or Transformers).  
- Overall assessment: timely, well-executed contribution to MANNs with minor extensions suggested to increase generality; strong candidate for acceptance.
