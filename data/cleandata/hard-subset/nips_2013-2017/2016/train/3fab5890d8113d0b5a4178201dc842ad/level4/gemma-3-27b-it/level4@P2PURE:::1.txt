The authors leverage fast approximate nearest neighbor search and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights within a Neural Turing Machine. Their experiments demonstrate that this approach does not diminish training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. While the core novelty of this work is limited—as similar techniques are employed in memory networks (e.g., section 3.3 of http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf) and several subsequent papers utilize hashing for scalable memory lookups with millions of items—the application to writeable memories appears less common. Even without a substantial conceptual advancement, the practical challenges of implementation are worthwhile to document. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, contingent on several revisions. First, reproducibility is a concern. The current descriptions are insufficient to reliably replicate the results, particularly regarding task construction. Given the lack of publicly available code for the original NTM tasks and the absence of a standardized implementation, a detailed explanation of task construction is crucial. The authors should also commit to releasing their experimental code. Second, a more thorough analysis of the model’s limitations is needed. While the authors characterize their model as smooth, the argmax (or K-argmax) operation inherently introduces discontinuity. In the read-only case with $K=1$, the model closely resembles MemNN-WSH (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), which reportedly underperformed fully smooth models. Determining whether this stems from $K=1$, the specific tasks, or other factors would significantly enhance the paper’s value. A discussion of failure cases and the conditions under which their training succeeds would be highly beneficial, as their model performs a discrete action yet is trained without explicit consideration for this aspect (in contrast to http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Demonstrating successful results is a positive contribution, but understanding the boundaries of its applicability is more valuable. Finally, I recommend removing section 3.6 and avoiding the characterization of the Omniglot dataset as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
