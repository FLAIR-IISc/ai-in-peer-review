- Problem: Dense read/write in MANNs causes O(N) time per step and O(N*T) BPTT space, preventing large memories or long sequences.  
- Contribution: Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to enforce sparsity in memory access and updates for scalability.  
- Sparse reads: Uses ANN indices (e.g., randomized k-d trees, LSH) to retrieve K nearest memory words in O(log N) and read over only those K.  
- Sparse writes: Restricts writes to a constant number of slots (previously read locations or LRU slot).  
- Efficient BPTT: Logs only O(1) sparse changes and reverts them during backprop, reducing per-step space to O(1).  
- Empirical validation: Tested on NTM algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning with comparable or better performance.  
- Efficiency results: Reports up to 1,600x speedup and 3,000x less physical memory, scaling to sequences of hundreds of thousands.  
- Theoretical quality: Asymptotic complexity claims are well-supported and represent a significant advance.  
- Experimental quality: Extensive, convincing benchmarks and appropriate baselines (including dense DAM and NTM).  
- Clarity: Paper is well-structured and clear with helpful supplemental diagrams, but could be more self-contained for non-experts (e.g., explain soft read/write intuition).  
- Originality: Novel integration of ANN indices into an end-to-end differentiable memory system to enforce sparsity.  
- Significance: Substantially addresses a major bottleneck, enabling large-scale MANN applications and generalizable to DNC (SDNC).  
- Minor critique: Comparison by constraining physical memory conflates learning capacity with efficiency; equal-N comparisons would better isolate sparsity effects.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses critical bottleneck, novel and principled approach, optimal asymptotics, massive empirical gains, generalizable impact.  
- Argument against acceptance: Only a minor experimental comparison issue—lack of direct equal-memory-size baselines to fully isolate effects of sparsity.
