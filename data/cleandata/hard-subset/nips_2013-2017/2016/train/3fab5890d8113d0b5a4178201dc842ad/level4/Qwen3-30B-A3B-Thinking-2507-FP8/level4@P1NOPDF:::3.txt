

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks. While the field of neural network architectures with external memory is relatively recent—following the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) paradigm—SAM offers a significant refinement by constraining all write and read operations to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than the full memory space. This design choice is theoretically justified and proven optimal by the authors. The authors demonstrate that this sparsity constraint leads to improved read and write performance (Section 4.1), as the reduced memory footprint minimizes access overhead.

The paper further evaluates the learning costs of sparsity through rigorous experiments on standard NTM benchmark tasks: Copy, Associative Recall, and Priority Sort (Section 4.2). Notably, SAM achieves lower combined costs than NTM for Associative Recall and Priority Sort, indicating that sparsity can be beneficial for specific learning scenarios. The observation that sparse read/write operations, implemented via linear or ANN-based mechanisms, can accelerate early-stage learning in certain cases (Section 4.2) is particularly intriguing and merits deeper exploration. The authors are encouraged to elaborate on the conditions under which this sparsity-induced learning benefit occurs and how it might generalize across diverse architectures or tasks.

The inclusion of a real-world application example in Section 4.4 is a strong point, providing concrete context for SAM's utility. The benchmarking is conducted using Torch7, and the paper is well-structured with all assumptions clearly stated and the architecture detailed in the appendix. The core idea is straightforward yet novel, and the reported speedup over NTM is substantial and impressive.

While the technical contributions are solid and the presentation is clear, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the generalizability of the observed learning benefits. Specifically, the authors should discuss *under what conditions* sparse access improves learning performance and explore how different ANN approaches might interact with SAM. This would significantly enhance the paper's impact and provide valuable guidance for future work.

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound contribution that advances the field of memory-augmented networks. The SAM scheme is elegantly conceived, its advantages are rigorously demonstrated, and the paper is engaging to read. It represents a meaningful step towards more efficient and scalable memory-augmented architectures.
