- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme that constrains reads/writes to sparse subsets to achieve asymptotic space/time efficiency.  
- Reports empirical gains: up to 1,000× speedup and 3,000× reduced memory vs dense baselines while maintaining comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks and Omniglot.  
- Demonstrates scalability to 100,000+ time steps and memory slots; a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) achieves state-of-the-art on bAbI without supervised memory access.  
- Significance: directly addresses a key scalability bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks, enabling real-world applicability.  
- Clarity: paper is well-structured with clear exposition and comparisons to dense variants (DAM) and prior models (NTM, LSTM).  
- Empirical quality: thorough evaluations (synthetic tasks, curriculum learning, bAbI, Omniglot) showing consistent efficiency gains without performance loss.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical justification and rigorous error bounds for the sparse approximation and claimed asymptotic optimality.  
- Weakness: novelty is limited—sparse addressing and ANN integration appear incremental relative to prior content-based addressing, LSH, and RL-based sparse methods.  
- Weakness: efficient backpropagation section omits critical implementation details for tracking sparse modifications during BPTT, hindering reproducibility.  
- Pro-acceptance: provides a practical, scalable path for MANNs with robust empirical speed and memory trade-offs.  
- Con-acceptance: theoretical gaps and limited differentiation from recent work weaken the paper’s suitability for top-tier acceptance.  
- Overall: valuable for practitioners but lacks the theoretical depth and conceptual novelty expected for NIPS acceptance.
