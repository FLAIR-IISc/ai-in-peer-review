This paper proposes using fast approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) methods and top-K selection to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM). The authors present experiments demonstrating that this approach does not degrade performance and enables training on larger-scale problems.

While the engineering and empirical validation are a valuable contribution, the conceptual novelty of this work is somewhat limited. The use of hashing-based methods for scalable memory lookups has been explored in several Memory Network papers (e.g., http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf, Sec 3.3). However, the application of these techniques to a *writeable* memory, as in the NTM, appears to be novel, and the practical aspects of making this work are certainly worth reporting.

Therefore, I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the following revisions:

1.  **Reproducibility:** The paper currently lacks sufficient detail to allow for the replication of its results. The descriptions of the experimental tasks are particularly sparse. Given that the code for the original NTM paper was never released and no standard benchmarks exist for these tasks, it is crucial that the authors provide a precise and thorough description of how the tasks were constructed. I also strongly encourage the authors to commit to releasing their experimental code.

2.  **Analysis of Limitations and Failure Modes:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of when this approach might fail. The authors often describe their model as "smooth," yet the core argmax (or K-argmax) operation is non-differentiable. For instance, in a read-only setting with K=1, the model is conceptually similar to the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), which was reported to underperform its fully differentiable counterpart. The authors should discuss why their method succeeds here. Is it due to the value of K, the nature of the tasks, or another factor?

    Furthermore, the model is trained to make discrete actions without employing techniques like REINFORCE (e.g., http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275) that are designed for such non-differentiable operations. That the authors achieve good results with this more direct training method is an interesting contribution. However, the paper would be more impactful if it analyzed the boundary conditions for this success. What kinds of tasks and model setups are amenable to this training strategy, and where does it break down?

Finally, I have two minor suggestions:
*   The authors could consider removing Section 3.6.
*   The authors should refrain from describing the Omniglot dataset as "non-synthetic" or "real-world."
