

The authors employ fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine. They demonstrate that this approach does not degrade training performance and enables training on larger-scale problems. However, the novelty is limited; similar techniques appear in Section 3.3 of the memory networks paper (arXiv:1410.3916), where multiple memory network papers utilize millions of memory items with hashing for scalable lookups. While the practical implementation for writeable memories is novel—despite the absence of a conceptual leap—the authors should emphasize these implementation details. I recommend acceptance pending revisions.  

1. **Replicability**: Replicating the results would be challenging due to insufficient task descriptions. Given the absence of released code for the original NTM tasks and no standardized task definitions, the paper must provide meticulous details on task construction. The authors should commit to releasing their experiment code.  

2. **Failure Analysis**: The model’s reliance on non-smooth argmax (or K-argmax) operations warrants deeper analysis. At $K=1$, the read-only variant resembles MemNN-WSH (arXiv:1503.08895), which underperformed compared to fully smooth models. Is the success due to $K=1$, task characteristics, or other factors? The paper should include failure-case analysis and discuss task-specific conditions enabling success. The model makes discrete actions without incorporating smoothing (unlike arXiv:1511.07275), and understanding these limits would strengthen the contribution.  

3. **Revisions**: Remove Section 3.6. Additionally, avoid mischaracterizing Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world."
