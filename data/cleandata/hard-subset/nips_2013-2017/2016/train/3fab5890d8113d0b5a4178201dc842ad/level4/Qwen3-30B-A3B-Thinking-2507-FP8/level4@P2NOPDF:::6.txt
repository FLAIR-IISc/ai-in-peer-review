

This paper introduces a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) variant featuring sparse read and write operations to address a critical limitation hindering NTMs' real-world applicability: their excessive memory demands and computational overhead during backpropagation. The authors propose a solution that maintains competitive performance relative to state-of-the-art methods while significantly reducing resource requirements. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the core contributions accessible.

To enhance accessibility for a broader audience, a brief overview of memory-augmented neural networks in the background section would be beneficial. While the technical details are clear to specialists, readers unfamiliar with the field may struggle to grasp the conceptual distinction between the proposed write operator and conventional memory mechanisms. Clarifying this—such as explaining how the sparse write operation selectively updates memory locations versus full overwrites—would strengthen the paper's self-containment without adding undue length.

Regarding Figure 4's comparison, the authors justify their approach by matching physical memory sizes across methods, which is a valid and reasonable constraint for practical deployment scenarios. However, the comparison could be further enriched by also evaluating performance under identical *memory capacities* (e.g., same number of memory slots or storage units), rather than solely fixed physical memory. This would provide deeper insight into how the proposed architecture leverages memory efficiency to achieve performance gains, independent of hardware constraints. While the current comparison is defensible, the alternative perspective would more directly highlight the algorithm's intrinsic advantages in memory utilization.

The results are compelling: the method achieves performance close to theoretical bounds while operating with substantially reduced memory overhead. This demonstrates a significant step toward making NTMs viable for resource-constrained applications. The authors' focus on practical feasibility—addressing the gap between theoretical promise and real-world deployment—is particularly noteworthy and aligns well with current trends in efficient deep learning.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed sparse access mechanism effectively mitigates a key barrier to NTM adoption, and the experimental validation is thorough. Minor enhancements to the background and comparison methodology would elevate the work further, but these do not detract from the core innovation. The results are robust, the writing is clear, and the approach shows strong potential for real-world impact. I recommend acceptance.
