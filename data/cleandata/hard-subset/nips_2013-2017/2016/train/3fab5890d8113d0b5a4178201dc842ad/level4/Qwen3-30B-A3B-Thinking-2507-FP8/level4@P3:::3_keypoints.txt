- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme that restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset (typically K=4–8) for MANNs.  
- Achieves asymptotic optimality in time and space by using sparse memory operations and ANN indexes while remaining end-to-end differentiable.  
- Reports dramatic empirical efficiency gains: ~1,000× faster runtime and ~3,000× less physical memory than dense MANNs.  
- Maintains comparable learning performance to dense models on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and real tasks (Omniglot).  
- Demonstrates scalability to previously infeasible settings: memories up to ~10^6 words and sequences exceeding 200,000 time steps.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) supporting SAM’s asymptotic optimality.  
- Empirically validates claims with comprehensive benchmarks and comparisons to dense baselines (DAM, NTM) and standard models (LSTM).  
- Shows sparse approximations can sometimes improve learning (e.g., priority sort), suggesting benefits of sparsity in early learning stages.  
- Presents a sparse DNC variant (SDNC) that attains state-of-the-art results on Babi without supervised memory access.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with precise mathematical formulations and useful figures (e.g., memory/speed benchmarks).  
- Supplementary materials include implementation details, hyperparameters, and extra experiments supporting reproducibility.  
- Original contribution distinct from prior RL-based sparse access approaches by providing a differentiable, low-variance solution.  
- Limitation: limited theoretical exploration of conditions under which sparsity might impair learning.  
- Limitation: lacks a systematic comparison of alternative ANN implementations (k-d trees vs. LSH) and clearer articulation of when sparsity improves learning.
