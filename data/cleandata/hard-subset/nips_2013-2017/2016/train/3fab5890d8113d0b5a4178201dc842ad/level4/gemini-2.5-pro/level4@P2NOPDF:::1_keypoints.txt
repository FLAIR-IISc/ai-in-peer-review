- Proposes scaling NTMs using fast ANN structures and arg-top-K attention to enable larger writable memories.  
- Experiments show the ANN/arg-top-K approximation does not degrade performance and enables training on larger-memory tasks.  
- Primary contribution is practical/engineering: making scalable lookups work for a writable memory system.  
- Conceptual novelty is limited; similar hashing ideas exist for read-only memories (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al. 2014).  
- Claimed novelty: first successful application of hashing/ANN to a read-write NTM-style architecture.  
- Recommendation: accept, but several issues must be addressed to strengthen the paper.  
- Reproducibility concern: task descriptions are insufficient; must provide precise task construction details (data generation, sequence lengths, parameters) and release code.  
- Discrete approximation concern: argmax/arg-top-K is non-differentiable and introduces discontinuities that need deeper analysis of limitations and failure modes.  
- Need direct comparison/discussion vs prior discrete hashing work (e.g., MemNN-WSH) to explain why the discrete approach succeeds here.  
- Need analysis of why training with discrete selection via backprop works, including exploration of limits and ablations (e.g., effect of K) and potential failure conditions.  
- Minor suggestions: remove Section 3.6 as nonessential, and rephrase Omniglot description to avoid implying "real-world" or "non-synthetic."
