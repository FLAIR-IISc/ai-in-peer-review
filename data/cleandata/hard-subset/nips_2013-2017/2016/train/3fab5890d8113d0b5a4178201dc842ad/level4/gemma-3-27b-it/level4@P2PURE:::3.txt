This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This is a relatively new research area, originating with the development of Neural Turing Machines, and aims to extend the capabilities of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution of this work is constraining both read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of the external memory, rather than allowing access to the entire memory space. The authors demonstrate the methodology and provide a proof of its optimality. The reduced memory access size plausibly leads to improved read and write performance, as evidenced in section 4.1. They also consider the learning costs associated with this sparsity in section 4.2, evaluating performance on standard Neural Turing Machine tasks – copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The overall cost is lower than that of a standard NTM for certain tasks (specifically, associative recall and priority sort). I appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in section 4.4 and note that the implementation utilizes Torch7. The idea is both straightforward and novel, and the observed speedup compared to NTM is significant and impressive. The finding that sparse reads and writes, implemented using a linear layer or ANN, can actually *benefit* early-stage learning in some scenarios (section 4.2) is particularly interesting. To further strengthen the paper, the authors could elaborate on how to generalize these benefits – under what conditions might learning be improved, or how might different ANN architectures interact with SAM? Overall, this is a well-written paper with clearly stated assumptions and a detailed architectural description provided in the appendix, making it an engaging read.
