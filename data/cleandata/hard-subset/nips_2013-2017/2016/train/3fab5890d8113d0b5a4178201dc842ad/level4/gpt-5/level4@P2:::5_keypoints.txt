- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) replaces dense read/write in memory-augmented nets with sparse top-K reads and targeted sparse writes using ANN indices and LRU-like slots.
- Backprop: Introduces reversible sparse update recording for efficient BPTT, yielding O(1) per-step memory and O(log N) time with ANN.
- Complexity/empirics: Demonstrates 100–1600× speedups and large memory savings versus dense NTMs while keeping comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks.
- Scalability: Trained via curriculum to 10k steps and generalizes to 200k, enabling very long-sequence learning.
- Benchmarks: Strong performance on Omniglot one-shot classification and near–state-of-the-art on bAbI without supervised memory access; adapts to SDNC temporal links.
- Literature grounding: Situates work within MANN literature (NTM, DNC, Memory Networks) and contrasts with RL-based discrete addressing.
- Significance: Addresses core bottleneck in scaling external memory, offering practical gains to move beyond toy problems.
- Originality: Novel end-to-end differentiable sparse read/write plus efficient BPTT and principled integration of ANN indices.
- Quality: Solid engineering, clear asymptotic analysis, and compelling empirical evidence across tasks.
- Generality: Approach applies beyond NTMs (e.g., DNC/SDNC) and likely relevant to other memory architectures.
- Missing analysis: Paper lacks mechanistic explanation for why sparsity improves learning and strong generalization.
- Curriculum fairness: Comparisons may be confounded by memory capacity differences (dense limited to 64 slots vs SAM using millions).
- Sensitivity/ablations: Limited exploration of hyperparameters (K, usage thresholds) and ANN choices (k-d tree vs LSH), and impact of exact vs approximate neighbors.
- Differentiability/stability: Top-K and index maintenance introduce non-smoothness; more discussion of optimization stability, variance, and training tricks needed.
- Real-world evaluation: Needs larger, non-synthetic benchmarks (e.g., long-context language tasks) to support practical applicability claims.
- Theoretical clarity: Optimality claims depend on supplementary assumptions; main text should state assumptions and intuition clearly.
- Clarity improvements: Expand intuition for write policy, usage metrics, ANN maintenance strategy, and provide algorithmic sketches for reproducibility.
- Suggested experiments: Ablations on K/thresholds/ANN, diagnostics on sparsity effects (interference, gradient norms), sensitivity across seeds, and a matched-capacity baseline.
- Practical guidance: Request for ANN maintenance details (rebuild frequency, costs) and implementation tips.
- Pros for acceptance: Highly impactful, scalable approach with large empirical gains and broad applicability.
- Cons against acceptance: Insufficient analysis of sparsity benefits, confounded curriculum comparisons, and limited real-world evaluation breadth.
- Overall: Potentially breakthrough contribution; leans toward acceptance but would be stronger with additional analyses and ablations.
