Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), inspired by Neural Turing Machines (NTM), Memory Networks, and the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC). SAM enforces sparsity by restricting reads and writes to a fixed number K of memory locations per step. Reads use content-based addressing with top-K selection implemented via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices (k-d trees or LSH); writes update either recently read locations or the least-recently-used slot via a simple usage-based policy. The authors also introduce an efficient backpropagation-through-time strategy that stores only sparse memory diffs, yielding O(1) per-step memory and O(log N) per-step time with ANN queries (O(N) initialization). They argue this is asymptotically optimal under content-based addressing.

Empirically, SAM achieves large speed and memory gains over dense NTMs: up to ∼1000–1600× faster and ∼3000× less memory at large N. On standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), SAM matches or surpasses dense baselines (NTM and a dense variant, DAM) in data efficiency; in some cases sparseness appears to help early learning. Using a curriculum, SAM scales to sequences of thousands of steps with memories in the millions, and generalizes to much longer sequences at test time. The approach extends to a sparse DNC (SDNC) that attains strong performance on the bAbI QA tasks without memory supervision. On Omniglot one-shot classification, SAM benefits from larger memory and outperforms dense baselines in the reported setup. The implementation is in Torch7.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple mechanism: top-K sparse reads with ANN and sparse writes via read-then-LRU, enabling both forward and backward sparsity.
- Strong theoretical and systems story: asymptotic complexity analysis and a practical BPTT scheme that rolls back sparse updates.
- Substantial empirical gains in speed and memory footprint, enabling experiments at scales infeasible for dense MANNs.
- Broad evaluation: classic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling, bAbI (including SDNC), and a real dataset (Omniglot).
- Evidence that sparsity does not harm—and may sometimes help—data efficiency.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions behind the “asymptotically optimal” claims should be stated more prominently in the main text, with discussion of constant factors and regimes where dense methods can be competitive.
- The ANN component is treated as a fixed, non-differentiable index. More analysis of its impact on optimization (e.g., recall vs. convergence, stability under online updates, rebuild frequency) would strengthen the work. Ablations across k-d trees vs. LSH, K values, distance metrics, and index parameters would be helpful.
- The sparsity thresholding/top-K selection introduces non-smoothness. Please clarify whether straight-through or other gradient tricks are used, and analyze sensitivity to K and to the usage threshold ε in the LRU policy.
- Comparisons: DAM and NTM are appropriate, but additional baselines (e.g., discrete-addressing RL NTMs, recent memory networks without supervision) would situate the contribution more fully. Also ensure matched parameter counts and fair memory budgets are clearly reported.
- Real-world evaluation: Omniglot is a reasonable testbed, but broader domains (e.g., language modeling or machine translation) would better demonstrate applicability. At minimum, discuss expected behavior and practical integration issues in such settings.
- For SDNC, provide more details in the main paper (not only supplementary) on how temporal linkage is sparsified and how this affects performance and complexity.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound, with a coherent design for sparse reads/writes and a careful BPTT implementation. Claims are supported by complexity arguments and extensive experiments. Some theoretical assertions (optimality) rely on assumptions that merit clearer exposition.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with helpful supplementary material and architectural details. Minor suggestions: correct small inconsistencies (e.g., mapping of sections to tasks/datasets), and expand the discussion on when sparsity helps learning and the trade-offs of different ANN choices.

Originality:
- The work presents a novel, practical synthesis: enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes, integrating ANN into end-to-end training, and enabling efficient backward passes via sparse diffs. The SDNC adaptation further shows generality.

Significance:
- The contribution is significant: it removes a major barrier to scaling MANNs, enabling training with very large memories and long sequences. Results on synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot indicate broad utility, and the systems insights are likely to be adopted by others.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Elegant, general approach to scalable differentiable memory with strong theoretical motivation.
- Orders-of-magnitude improvements in speed and memory.
- Comparable or better learning performance despite sparsity; scales to very long sequences.
- Versatile: applicable to DNC and likely other MANNs.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited analysis of ANN design choices and their effect on optimization and accuracy.
- Optimality claims depend on assumptions that deserve clearer treatment and empirical validation across more domains.
- Real-world evaluation breadth is limited; stronger evidence beyond Omniglot and bAbI would increase impact.

Overall assessment:
A well-executed and timely contribution that advances the scalability of memory-augmented neural networks. With additional analysis of ANN trade-offs and a clearer discussion of theoretical assumptions, this work merits acceptance.
