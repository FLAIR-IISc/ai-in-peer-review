- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): replace dense soft attention with sparse reads via ANN (top-K) and sparse writes (recently read or LRU) to enable sub-linear time and constant space per timestep.
- Shows SAM is orders of magnitude faster and more memory-efficient than dense models, scaling to massive memories and sequences (e.g., >100k time steps) without degrading—and sometimes improving—learning performance.
- Strength: strong practical contribution that addresses major computational and memory bottlenecks in memory-augmented neural networks.
- Strength: novel application of hashing/ANN methods to writable, end-to-end trained memory (unlike prior read-only Memory Networks).
- Strength: convincing experimental evidence and curriculum learning benchmarks demonstrating extreme scaling.
- Weakness: limited discussion of novelty relative to prior scalable Memory Network and hashing/ANN literature; needs clearer positioning.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—task specifications (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) are underspecified and code/experimental setup should be released.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of the discrete top-K selection and gradient propagation; the straight-through approximation needs justification and limitation analysis.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to discrete-action or RL-based methods and explanation for why hard-attention succeeds here despite prior reports of worse performance for hard attention.
- Minor point: characterizing Omniglot as "real-world" is overstated given its controlled episodic benchmarking nature.
- Recommendation: accept, but strongly urge authors to address reproducibility and provide a deeper analysis of the sparse/discrete approximation.
