The paper introduces a Sparse Access Memory architecture for neural networks augmented with external memory, such as Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. It delivers remarkable gains in run-time and memory efficiency—on the order of three magnitudes—compared to existing memory-augmented models. It also appears to yield substantial performance improvements in curriculum learning and enables generalization to sequences an order of magnitude longer than those seen during training (from lengths up to 10,000 in training to sequences of 200,000 at test time). This is potentially a groundbreaking contribution.

Neural networks augmented with memory, beginning with Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, form a rapidly developing field. Until now, the research and experimental results have been intriguing but did not appear ready for practical deployment. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported here suggest that memory-augmented neural networks may now be practical, with far-reaching implications.

The main omission in the current paper is a discussion of why curriculum learning performance improves so dramatically and why the model generalizes so far beyond the training range. Run-time and memory efficiency alone cannot fully explain these effects, so additional factors must be at play. My preliminary intuition is that the Sparse Access Memory mechanism may play a role analogous to that of sparse autoencoders in other contexts. Readers would benefit from the authors’ thoughts on this point, even if only preliminary.
