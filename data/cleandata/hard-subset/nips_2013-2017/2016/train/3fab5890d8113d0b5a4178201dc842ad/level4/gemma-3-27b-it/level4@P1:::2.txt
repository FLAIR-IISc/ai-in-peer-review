Neural networks augmented with external memory have shown promise for learning algorithmic solutions to complex tasks, but their scalability in both space and time remains a significant limitation. The authors address this challenge with a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM), demonstrating that it preserves representational power while enabling efficient training—achieving speedups of 1,000x and memory reductions of 3,000x compared to non-sparse models. While the experimental results are encouraging, I find the paper's technical contributions to be incremental and, therefore, do not recommend acceptance at this time.

The paper begins by contextualizing SAM within the broader landscape of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), correctly identifying the quadratic parameter growth of LSTMs as a motivation for decoupling memory capacity from model size, citing key works like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) [7] and Memory Networks [21]. The introduction clearly articulates the problem of computational overhead during training, stemming from smooth read and write operations and the need for backpropagation through time (BPTT), and effectively highlights the potential benefits of a sparse memory access scheme. 

The core technical contributions – efficient backpropagation and approximate nearest neighbor search utilizing sparse access memory – are well-explained. The idea of restricting writes and reads to a sparse subset of memory locations is intuitive, and the authors appropriately draw connections to existing techniques. The description of the custom data structures for sparse memory management and the strategies for efficient backpropagation are clear and concise. However, these techniques, while effectively implemented, feel relatively straightforward and do not represent a substantial theoretical breakthrough. The use of approximate nearest neighbor search, while important for scalability, is a well-established technique [15, 3] and its application here, using existing libraries like FLANN, doesn’t introduce novel algorithmic contributions. 

The empirical evaluation is a strength of the paper. The authors demonstrate the speed and memory benefits of SAM convincingly, with results showing significant improvements over both the NTM and a dense baseline (DAM). Performance on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) is comparable to or better than existing approaches, and the scaling experiments to larger sequence lengths are particularly noteworthy. The application to the Babi tasks and Omniglot character recognition further strengthens the evaluation and demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. However, while the performance is solid, the gains are not dramatic and the results do not fundamentally shift our understanding of the capabilities of MANNs.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated significant speed and memory improvements compared to existing MANNs.
*   Clear and well-written explanation of the SAM architecture and its components.
*   Comprehensive empirical evaluation on a variety of tasks, including scaling experiments.
*   Successful adaptation of SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer [8].

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core technical contributions – sparse access and efficient backpropagation – are incremental and build upon established techniques.
*   The paper lacks a substantial theoretical novelty.
*   The performance gains, while notable, are not transformative.
*   The use of existing ANN libraries limits the novelty of the approximate nearest neighbor implementation.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a practical and well-engineered improvement to memory-augmented neural networks. However, the technical contributions are not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like NIPS. The authors have built a useful system, but it doesn't represent a paradigm shift in the field. I recommend rejection.
