Most proposed neural network models augmented with memory exhibit poor scaling in both space and time as the memory size increases. The authors present a memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory, demonstrating that it maintains representational power while enabling efficient training – achieving speedups of 1000x and 3000x improvements in memory efficiency. However, I do not believe the paper is technically strong enough for acceptance. While efficient backpropagation and approximate nearest neighbor search using sparse access memory are the core technical contributions, they appear relatively straightforward. Therefore, despite the interesting experimental results, I do not recommend acceptance.

The primary motivation of enabling larger memory networks is sound, and the practical limitations of existing approaches are well articulated. The core idea of sparsely accessing memory locations based on content is intuitive and potentially impactful. However, the technical novelty feels incremental. The backpropagation through the sparse access mechanism relies on standard techniques, and the adaptation of approximate nearest neighbor search doesn’t introduce substantial algorithmic breakthroughs. The paper lacks a deeper exploration of *why* this specific sparse access pattern is so effective – is it the particular hashing function, the sparsity level, or some other factor? A more thorough ablation study investigating these components would strengthen the argument.

The experiments are a strong point of the paper. The authors demonstrate performance on several tasks, including image classification and language modeling. The reported speedups and memory savings are significant and clearly presented. However, the comparisons to existing memory networks are not always comprehensive. For example, it would be helpful to compare against more recent and optimized implementations of Neural Turing Machines or Differentiable Neural Computers. Furthermore, the choice of baselines feels somewhat limited; exploring the impact of different memory sizes within each baseline model would offer a more nuanced comparison. 

I found the presentation of the sparse access mechanism clear and the accompanying figures helpful. However, the notation could be improved for consistency. For instance, the meaning of certain symbols is not always immediately obvious and requires careful backtracking through the text. A more consistent use of mathematical notation would enhance readability. 

My major concern remains the lack of significant technical innovation. The paper presents a practical and potentially useful optimization, but it doesn’t push the boundaries of the field in a substantial way. While the empirical results are promising, they do not, on their own, justify acceptance to NeurIPS. I would encourage the authors to consider a more in-depth theoretical analysis and a more exhaustive experimental evaluation to bolster the technical contribution.
