- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): memory-augmented network with sparse read/write and ANN indexing achieving asymptotic optimality.  
- Addresses MANN scalability gap: reduces O(N) time/space overhead that limits practical use.  
- Key innovations: sparse writes constrained to K slots (K-argmax) and efficient content-based reads via ANN.  
- Efficient implementation: sparse data structures (CSR), ANN indexing, and optimized BPTT.  
- Theoretical guarantees: O(log N) time and O(1) space per step.  
- Empirical gains: ~1,000× faster training and ~3,000× less memory vs. dense baselines.  
- Maintains performance across tasks: copy, associative recall, priority sort, bAbI, Omniglot one-shot.  
- Ablation study: sparse approximations do not degrade learning; SDNC adaption extends method to temporal tasks.  
- Clarity: architecture and experimental setup are clearly described and replicable in principle.  
- Originality: novel sparse write mechanism and usage-based slot management, avoiding RL-based addressing.  
- Significance: enables training on very large memories/long sequences and achieves SOTA bAbI results.  
- Weakness — reproducibility: insufficient detail on synthetic task generation and curriculum parameters.  
- Weakness — failure analysis: lacks discussion of limitations and sensitivity to non-smooth K-argmax (K=1 vs K>1).  
- Weakness — terminology: calling Omniglot "non-synthetic" is misleading; suggest "natural" or "real-world."  
- Weakness — redundancy: Section 3.6 on smoothness is tangential and should be removed.  
- Overall verdict: strong, practical contribution; accept with minor revisions for reproducibility and failure-mode analysis.
