This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks. This area, originating with Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), aims to overcome the limitations of traditional recurrent models like LSTMs by decoupling memory capacity from the number of model parameters, enabling networks to learn algorithmic solutions. The key contribution of this work is constraining both read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory words, rather than operating on the entire memory. The authors demonstrate the theoretical optimality of this approach and, crucially, show significant performance improvements in Section 4.1. They evaluate the impact of this sparsity on learning efficiency using established NTM tasks – copy, associative recall, and priority sort – in Section 4.2, and showcase its applicability to a real-world dataset in Section 4.4. 

The idea is straightforward, novel, and well-motivated. The reported speedups over NTMs are substantial and impressive. The finding that sparse reads and writes can *improve* early-stage learning in certain scenarios (Section 4.2) is particularly interesting. The implementation, based on Torch7, is clearly described, and the architecture is well-detailed in the supplementary material.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The SAM approach provides a compelling solution to the scalability issues of memory-augmented neural networks.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors establish the asymptotic optimality of their method in space and time complexity.
*   **Empirical Results:** The performance gains in both speed and memory usage (Section 4.1) are significant. The results on standard NTM tasks and the Omniglot dataset (Section 4.5) demonstrate the efficacy of the approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.
*   **Generality:** Demonstrating adaptability to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) in the supplementary material highlights the broader applicability of the core idea.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **ANN Exploration:** While the use of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes is a practical optimization, the discussion of different ANN approaches could be expanded. Exploring alternative ANN structures (e.g., Mondrian forests, LSH forests) and their impact on performance would be valuable.
*   **Generalization of Benefits:** The paper notes that sparsity can improve learning in some cases. A deeper investigation into *why* this occurs and under what conditions it is most effective would strengthen the work. A more systematic analysis of scenarios where sparse access hinders learning could also provide insights.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper references relevant work, a more detailed comparison to alternative approaches for scaling memory networks (e.g., reinforcement learning based addressing schemes [21]) would be beneficial.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of memory-augmented neural networks – scalability.
*   The proposed solution (SAM) is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
*   The results demonstrate substantial improvements in both speed and memory usage.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The exploration of alternative ANN approaches is limited.
*   The analysis of the conditions under which sparse access improves learning could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The SAM approach offers a promising solution to the scalability problem, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**.
