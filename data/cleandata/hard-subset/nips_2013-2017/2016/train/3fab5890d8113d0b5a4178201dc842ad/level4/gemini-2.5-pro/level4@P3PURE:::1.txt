This paper proposes using fast approximate-nearest-neighbor (ANN) search and top-K selection to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine, with the goal of improving scalability. The authors present experiments demonstrating that this approximation does not degrade performance on standard tasks and, in fact, enables training on problems with larger memory sizes.

The core novelty of this work is limited. The idea of using hashing-based methods for scalable lookups in large memories has been explored previously in the context of Memory Networks (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2014, Section 3.3), with several follow-up works demonstrating scalability to millions of items. However, the application of these techniques to *writeable* memories, as done here for NTMs, is less common. While this may not represent a major conceptual leap, the practical contributions and empirical validation of making such a system work are valuable and worth reporting to the community.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1.  **Reproducibility:** The experimental results would be very difficult to reproduce from the paper in its current form. The descriptions of the tasks are particularly insufficient. Given that the original NTM task code was never released and no standard benchmarks exist, it is crucial for the authors to provide a detailed and precise description of their task construction and parameters. To fully address this concern, I strongly urge the authors to commit to releasing their experimental code upon publication.

2.  **Analysis of Limitations and Failure Modes:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the proposed method's limitations. The authors often describe their model as smooth, yet the arg-top-K operation is non-differentiable and introduces a hard decision point. This raises a critical question: why does training succeed despite this?

    In the read-only setting with K=1, this model is conceptually similar to the MemNN-WSH model (Bordes et al., 2015), which was reported to underperform its fully differentiable counterpart. What allows the authors' model to work well? Is it the use of K > 1, the specific nature of the tasks, or another factor?

    The model effectively learns to perform a discrete action (selecting memory locations) but is trained with standard backpropagation, seemingly without requiring reinforcement learning techniques to handle the non-differentiable step (in contrast to, e.g., Mnih & Gregor, 2015, arXiv:1511.07275). The fact that this approach is successful is an interesting contribution in itself. However, to make this contribution more impactful, the paper should include a discussion of the conditions under which this training regime is expected to work and where it might fail. An analysis of failure cases or an ablation study exploring the sensitivity to K would be highly valuable.

Finally, I have two minor suggestions:
*   I suggest the authors remove Section 3.6.
*   The Omniglot dataset, while valuable, is not typically considered "non-synthetic" or "real-world" in the same vein as natural images; I suggest the authors remove this characterization.
