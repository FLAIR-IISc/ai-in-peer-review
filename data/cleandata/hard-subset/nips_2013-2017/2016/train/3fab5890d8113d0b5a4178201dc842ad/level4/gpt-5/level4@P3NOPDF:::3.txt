Summary
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks that constrains both reads and writes to a fixed-size subset of memory locations. By enforcing sparsity in the attention over memory, SAM aims to retain the expressivity of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) while achieving substantially improved computational efficiency. The paper presents the methodology and an optimality argument for the sparse access mechanism, demonstrates significant speedups (Section 4.1), and analyzes the learning costs introduced by sparsity (Section 4.2) on standard NTM benchmarks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort. It also includes a real-world example (Section 4.4), with an implementation in Torch7.

Strengths
- Clear and timely contribution: Memory-augmented neural networks are an active area since NTMs, and this work addresses one of the key barriers to scaling—computational cost—via a principled sparsity mechanism.
- Methodological rigor: The constrained read/write scheme is well motivated, and the paper provides an optimality analysis of the access strategy.
- Empirical validation: On standard NTM tasks, SAM matches or improves upon NTM performance while substantially reducing compute, and the combined training cost is lower than NTM on associative recall and priority sort (Section 4.2).
- Practical significance: The reported speedups are impressive and make memory-augmented models more tractable for longer sequences and larger memories.
- Real-world relevance: Inclusion of a real-world task (Section 4.4) strengthens the case that SAM’s benefits extend beyond toy tasks.
- Presentation: The paper is generally well written, with assumptions clearly stated and architectural details provided in the appendix.

Weaknesses and questions
- Characterization of when sparsity helps: Section 4.2 suggests that sparse reads/writes (via linear search or approximate nearest neighbor indexing) can benefit early-stage learning in some cases. It would help to more systematically characterize when and why sparsity improves learning (e.g., by acting as an inductive bias or regularizer), and when it might hinder performance by limiting credit assignment or context aggregation.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: The fixed-size read/write budget (k) is central to the approach. How sensitive are results to k, memory size, and addressing noise? Is there an adaptive strategy for k that balances accuracy and speed during training?
- Fair comparison and overheads: While Section 4.1 shows speedups, it would be useful to disentangle the cost of maintaining the sparse data structures/indexing from the core model computation, and to clarify hardware settings to ensure fair wall-clock comparisons with dense baselines.
- Scope of baselines: The paper focuses primarily on NTM comparisons. Including results against other contemporary memory-augmented models (e.g., DNC or related sparse attention schemes) would strengthen claims of novelty and efficiency.
- Real-world task details: More detail on the real-world setting in Section 4.4 (dataset characteristics, sequence lengths, error modes) would make the case study more informative and reproducible.
- Robustness across retrieval backends: Since the implementation can use either linear scan or ANN backends for sparse access, it would be helpful to compare different ANN methods (e.g., LSH vs graph-based indices) in terms of accuracy/speed trade-offs and training stability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clearer analysis of the regimes where SAM’s sparsity is most beneficial: task characteristics (e.g., local vs global dependencies), memory size, sequence length, and noise levels. A simple taxonomy or set of guidelines for choosing k would be very valuable to practitioners.
- Add ablations varying k, memory size, and the sparsity pattern (e.g., number of reads vs writes) to quantify accuracy/speed trade-offs and identify failure modes.
- Report early-vs-late training dynamics to support the claim that sparse access benefits early learning, and discuss whether the advantage persists or diminishes as training progresses.
- Compare multiple retrieval backends (linear vs several ANN indices) and report their impact on convergence, final accuracy, and throughput. If possible, include GPU-friendly variants.
- Expand baseline coverage to include stronger or more recent memory architectures and sparse attention models, and consider reporting both step-time and end-to-end wall-clock metrics for parity.
- Enrich the real-world evaluation with additional metrics (e.g., memory utilization, error breakdowns) and include more implementation details (hardware, batch sizes) to facilitate replication.

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent terminology (e.g., “associative recall” rather than “associated call”).
- A brief practical recipe (in the appendix) for setting k and choosing an ANN backend would improve usability.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and relevant paper that introduces a simple, effective, and novel sparse access mechanism for memory-augmented neural networks. The theoretical framing and empirical results convincingly demonstrate substantial efficiency gains, with competitive or better accuracy on standard tasks and encouraging real-world evidence. With additional analysis on when sparsity helps, thorough ablations, and broader baselines, SAM could become a strong reference approach for scalable differentiable memory. I recommend acceptance.
