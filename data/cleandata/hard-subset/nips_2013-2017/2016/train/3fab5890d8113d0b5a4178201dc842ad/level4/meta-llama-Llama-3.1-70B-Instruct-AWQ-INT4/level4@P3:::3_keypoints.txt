- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrains reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words.  
- Provides theoretical analysis and argues optimality, showing improved read/write performance and reduced memory costs.  
- Evaluated on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), with lower combined learning costs than NTM on some tasks.  
- Demonstrates significant runtime speedup over NTM and that sparsity can aid early-stage learning in some cases.  
- Includes real-world evaluations (bAbI tasks) and comparisons to other models, analyzing effects of sparsity on learning.  
- Paper is well-written with clear methodology, explicit assumptions, and detailed appendix.  
- Sparse reads/writes are a novel and interesting idea, though building on existing memory-augmented NN work.  
- Technically sound: claims supported by theory and experiments (Quality 9/10).  
- Clear presentation and explanations (Clarity 9/10).  
- Originality is strong but somewhat incremental (Originality 8/10).  
- Significant contribution due to practical speedups and empirical demonstration (Significance 9/10).  
- Needs broader analysis of when sparsity improves learning and generalization to other cases.  
- Recommends exploring different approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) approaches to integrate with SAM.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel scheme, solid analysis, speedups, and real-world examples.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient comprehensive analysis of learning conditions and ANN variants.
