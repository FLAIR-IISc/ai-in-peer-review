- Summary: Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that enforces K-sparse reads/writes, uses ANN indices for O(log N) addressing, and a rollback BPTT for O(1) per-step space, achieving large empirical speed/memory gains over dense NTMs.

- Key novelties: integrates approximate nearest-neighbor indices into differentiable content addressing and introduces a constant-space rollback scheme for sparse in-place updates.

- Strength: Clear and practical scalability claims with O(log N) time and O(1) per-step space under reasonable assumptions, backed by engineering.

- Strength: Thoughtful design of sparse reads (top-K approximate retrieval) and sparse writes (write-to-read or least-recently-used with an efficient tracker).

- Strength: Strong empirical evidence — huge speed/memory savings, comparable learning to dense models, successful BPTT over >4000 steps, good bAbI and Omniglot results.

- Strength: Generality — method extends to other MANNs (e.g., DNC) with demonstrated speedups.

- Strength: Relevant and timely for scaling memory-augmented neural networks in language and sequential reasoning.

- Weakness: Fairness of comparisons — some plots match physical memory rather than logical slots; matched-logical-memory comparisons are needed to isolate algorithmic gains.

- Weakness: Missing ANN specifics — need index parameters, rebalancing frequency, and empirical recall/precision to assess speed/accuracy trade-offs.

- Weakness: Lack of ablations — sensitivity analyses on K and ANN type (k-d tree vs LSH) are absent.

- Weakness: Optimality assumptions unclear — lower-bound arguments rely on content-addressing assumptions that should be restated in the main text.

- Weakness: Write policy not fully justified — brief comparison to sparse content-based writing and DNC allocation, and sensitivity to usage-thresholds, is needed.

- Weakness: Task scope limited — mostly synthetic tasks; a real large-scale sequential task (e.g., language modeling or long-context QA) would better demonstrate end-to-end applicability.

- Quality: Technical approach is sound and well implemented, though some methodological details (ANN recall, sensitivity) could be expanded and code/pseudocode would aid reproducibility.

- Clarity: Paper is generally clear but would benefit from a concise primer on MANN read/write semantics and a high-level explanation of the rollback BPTT trick.

- Clarity (cont.): Main-text summary of complexity assumptions and lower-bound intuition should be added.

- Clarity (cont.): Clarify and justify when comparisons match physical vs logical memory and include hyperparameter tables for ANN indices and K with ANN recall during training.

- Originality: While sparse memory access is not entirely new, combining top-K sparse reads/writes, in-graph ANN indexing, and constant-space rollback is novel and impactful.

- Significance: Substantially advances scalability of differentiable memory architectures, enabling very large memories and long-sequence training.

- Pros for acceptance: Strong motivation, theoretical and practical speed/space improvements, broad experimental evaluation, and general applicability to multiple architectures.

- Cons against acceptance: Limited ablations on ANN choices and K, write-policy analysis lacking, and experiments are primarily synthetic rather than large-scale real-world.

- Recommendations: Add a MANN primer, report ANN configurations and recall, include ablations for K and ANN types, present matched-logical-memory comparisons, summarize optimality assumptions, and release code/pseudocode for rollback and usage tracking.

- Overall evaluation: Strong, timely, and well-executed contribution that merits acceptance.
