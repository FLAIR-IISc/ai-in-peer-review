This paper introduces a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) with sparse read and write access, addressing a major limitation that hinders the practical application of such models. Specifically, conventional NTMs require prohibitively large physical memory and computational resources for backpropagation. The proposed architecture aims to solve this scalability issue while achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art models. The paper is well-structured and clearly written.

To improve the paper's self-containment and accessibility for readers not deeply familiar with this subfield, the authors could consider adding a brief background on memory-augmented neural networks. For instance, a clearer explanation of the memory mechanism would be beneficial, particularly how the 'write' operator functions in contrast to conventional computer memory. While this is clear to experts in the area, it may be a point of confusion for a broader audience.

Furthermore, the experimental comparison presented in Figure 4, while justifiable, may not be the most informative. The authors compare models by constraining them to the same amount of *physical* memory. This is a valid approach for evaluating performance under a fixed resource budget. However, a comparison using the same *logical* memory size (i.e., the same number of memory slots) might offer a clearer insight into the intrinsic performance advantages of the proposed sparse access mechanism, independent of its implementation efficiency.

Despite these minor suggestions, the results are impressive. It is particularly interesting to see the proposed algorithm perform so well while operating very close to theoretical bounds.
