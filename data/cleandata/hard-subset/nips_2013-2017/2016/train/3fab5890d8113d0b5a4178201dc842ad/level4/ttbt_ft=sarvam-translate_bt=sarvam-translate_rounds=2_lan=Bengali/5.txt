This paper introduces a sparse access memory architecture for neural networks that is enhanced by memory, such as neural Turing machines and memory networks. This allows for significant run-time and memory efficiency improvements (three-dimensional scaling) compared to existing memory-enhanced neural networks. This architecture appears to significantly improve performance in curriculum learning and the ability to generalize to sequences one dimension longer than observed sequences in training (up to 10,000 sequences in training versus up to 200,000 sequences for testing). This is potentially a groundbreaking paper. We have an actively developing field of memory-enhanced neural networks, starting with neural Turing machines and memory networks. So far, we have seen very interesting research and experimental results in that field, but they do not seem to be at a practical application level. The extensive efficiency and performance improvements mentioned in the current paper likely mean that memory-enhanced neural networks are now ready for practical use. This could have far-reaching implications. What I miss in the current paper is the reason for the extensive performance improvement in curriculum learning (and the reason for the extensive improvement in the ability to predict sequences of lengths beyond the training limit). Of course, run-time and memory efficiency cannot be responsible for this, so something else is happening (my initial impression is that sparse auto- encoders play a similar role here as they do in other situations; of course, readers will want to know the authors' opinions on this matter, even if those opinions are preliminary).
