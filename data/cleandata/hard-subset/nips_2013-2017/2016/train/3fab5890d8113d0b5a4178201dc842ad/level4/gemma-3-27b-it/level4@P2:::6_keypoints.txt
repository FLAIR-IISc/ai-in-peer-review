- Addresses MANN scalability: identifies poor space/time scaling of memory-augmented neural networks as a critical limitation.  
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a differentiable sparse memory access scheme that preserves representational power while enabling efficient training.  
- Large efficiency gains: reports up to 1,000x speedups and 3,000x memory reductions versus non-sparse models.  
- Theoretical support: provides asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity to justify SAM.  
- Competitive empirical performance: matches existing models on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot while scaling to large memories and long horizons.  
- Generality: successfully adapts SAM to a Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), showing broad applicability.  
- Clear presentation: paper is well-written, organized, and accessible.  
- Weak background coverage: needs clearer explanation of core MANN concepts (e.g., write operator and relation to conventional memories) for less familiar readers.  
- Figure 4 comparison choice: using equal physical memory rather than equal memory size may obscure SAM’s memory-efficiency benefits; fixed-memory comparisons are suggested.  
- ANN discussion limited: would benefit from a deeper treatment of approximate nearest neighbor data-structure trade-offs and limitations in training contexts.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with strong theoretical and empirical evidence and substantial practical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: impact of ANN choice not thoroughly investigated and Figure 4 comparison could be clearer.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to background, Figure 4, and ANN discussion.
