The paper addresses a key limitation of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) – their poor scaling in space and time with increasing memory size – which hinders their application to real-world problems. It introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable memory access scheme designed to retain the representational power of existing approaches while enabling efficient training with large memories. The core idea is to threshold memory modifications to a sparse subset, coupled with efficient data structures for content-based reads, achieving asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity. The authors demonstrate significant speedups (1,000x) and memory reduction (3,000x) compared to non-sparse models. SAM achieves comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot recognition, and scales to tasks requiring substantial memory and sequence lengths. They also extend their approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), showing significant improvements in speed and performance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions SAM within the context of prior work like NTMs [7] and Memory Networks [21]. The technical details are presented in a logical manner, and the experimental results are comprehensive. The comparison to existing models (NTM, DAM) is valuable, and the scaling experiments are particularly compelling. The inclusion of results on the Babi tasks and Omniglot demonstrates the versatility of the approach. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical problem:** The scaling issues of MANNs are a significant barrier to their wider adoption, and this work provides a compelling solution.
*   **Strong theoretical grounding:** The claim of achieving asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity is a significant contribution.
*   **Impressive empirical results:** The speedups and memory reductions observed are substantial, and the performance on various tasks is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Generality:** The successful adaptation to the DNC [8] demonstrates the broader applicability of the approach.
*   **Thorough experimentation:** The comprehensive set of experiments, including scaling experiments and comparisons to multiple baselines, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of SAM.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Background could be more self-contained:** While the paper is generally clear, a more detailed explanation of the write operator and the overall functionality of memory-augmented networks in the background section would benefit readers less familiar with the field.  Specifically, elaborating on the ideal function of the write operator compared to conventional memory operations would enhance understanding.
*   **Fairness of comparison in Figure 4:**  Comparing models based on equal *physical* memory usage, rather than equal memory size, could be misleading. While justifiable in the context of limited resources, presenting results with the same memory size (even if physical memory differs) would offer a more direct comparison of performance potential.
*   **ANN implementation details:** While the paper mentions using approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices, further details on the specific ANN algorithms used (e.g., FLANN, LSH forests) and their parameter settings would be helpful. A discussion of the trade-offs between ANN accuracy and speed would also be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of memory-augmented neural networks.
*   The proposed solution (SAM) is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The results demonstrate substantial improvements in speed, memory usage, and scalability.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The approach has the potential to enable the development of more powerful and practical MANNs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The background section could be more accessible to readers unfamiliar with the field.
*   The comparison in Figure 4 may not be the most informative way to present the results.
*   More details on the ANN implementation would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed approach (SAM) is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the results demonstrate substantial improvements in scalability and efficiency.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
