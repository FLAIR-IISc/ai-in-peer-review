- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrain reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory to retain NTM expressivity while improving computational efficiency.
- Paper provides methodology, an optimality argument for sparse access, significant speedups, learning-cost analysis on standard NTM benchmarks, a real-world example, and a Torch7 implementation.

Strengths
- Timely contribution addressing a key scalability bottleneck for memory-augmented networks via principled sparsity.
- Constrained read/write scheme is well motivated and supported by an optimality analysis.
- Empirical results show SAM matches or improves NTM performance while substantially reducing compute.
- Combined training cost lower than NTM on associative recall and priority sort benchmarks.
- Reported speedups make memory-augmented models more tractable for longer sequences and larger memories.
- Real-world task inclusion strengthens practical relevance.
- Paper is well written with clear assumptions and architectural details in the appendix.

Weaknesses and questions
- Lacks systematic characterization of when sparsity helps vs when it hinders learning (inductive bias vs credit assignment/context aggregation issues).
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (fixed k), memory size, and addressing noise is unclear; no adaptive strategy for k proposed.
- Need to disentangle costs of sparse data structures/indexing from core model computation and clarify hardware for fair wall-clock comparisons.
- Baselines limited largely to NTMs; comparison to DNCs or other sparse-attention models is missing.
- Real-world task lacks dataset characteristics, sequence lengths, and error-mode details for reproducibility.
- No thorough comparison of retrieval backends (linear scan vs various ANN methods) in accuracy/speed/training stability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide clearer analysis/taxonomy of regimes where SAM is most beneficial (task types, memory/sequence size, noise).
- Add ablations varying k, memory size, and sparsity patterns (reads vs writes) to quantify trade-offs and failure modes.
- Report early-vs-late training dynamics to validate claimed early-learning benefits and whether they persist.
- Compare multiple retrieval backends (LSH, graph indices, GPU-friendly variants) and report impacts on convergence, accuracy, and throughput.
- Expand baselines to recent memory architectures and sparse attention models and report both step-time and end-to-end wall-clock metrics.
- Enrich real-world evaluation with memory utilization, error breakdowns, and implementation/hardware/batch-size details for replication.

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent terminology (e.g., “associative recall”).
- Add a practical recipe in the appendix for choosing k and selecting an ANN backend.

Overall assessment
- Well-executed, novel, and effective sparse access mechanism with strong theoretical framing and empirical efficiency gains; recommend acceptance with suggested additional analyses and broader baselines.
