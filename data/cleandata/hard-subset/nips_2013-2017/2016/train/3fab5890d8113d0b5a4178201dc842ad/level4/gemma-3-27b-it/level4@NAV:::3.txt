This paper presents Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks—a relatively new field originating with Neural Turing Machines. This augmentation enables neural networks to surpass the limitations of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution lies in restricting all read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of the external memory, unlike the unlimited scope of previous approaches. The authors demonstrate the methodology and its optimality, with results in Section 4.1 suggesting potentially strong read and write performance due to this reduced memory footprint. They further analyze the learning costs associated with this sparsity, evaluating SAM on standard Neural Turing Machine tasks—copy, associative recall, and priority sort (Section 4.2)—showing combined costs lower than those of NTM for certain tasks. The inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4 is a valuable addition. Implemented in Torch7, the approach is both conceptually simple and innovative, achieving impressive speedups compared to NTM. The observation that sparse reads and writes, mediated by a linear layer or ANN, can *improve* early-stage learning in some scenarios (Section 4.2) is particularly intriguing. To further benefit the research community, the authors could explore the generalizability of these learning improvements—identifying conditions under which SAM consistently enhances learning—or investigate the performance of SAM with alternative ANN architectures. Overall, this is a well-written and clear paper, presenting all assumptions explicitly and providing architectural details in the appendix. It represents a compelling and interesting contribution to the field.
