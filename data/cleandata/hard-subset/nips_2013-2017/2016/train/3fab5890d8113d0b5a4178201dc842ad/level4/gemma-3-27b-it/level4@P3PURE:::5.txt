The paper introduces a Sparse Access Memory architecture for neural networks augmented with memory, such as Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. This architecture achieves remarkable run-time and memory efficiency improvements—three orders of magnitude—compared to existing memory-augmented neural networks. Furthermore, it appears to enable substantial performance gains in curriculum learning settings and significantly improves generalization to sequences an order of magnitude longer than those seen during training (extrapolating from training sequences of up to 10,000 to testing sequences of length 200,000). This is a potentially groundbreaking contribution. The field of neural networks augmented with memory, originating with Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, has seen interesting research and experimental results, but these have not yet reached a level of practical applicability. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported here suggest that memory-augmented neural networks may now be ready for practical use, with potentially far-reaching implications. My primary concern is the lack of discussion regarding the reasons for these drastic improvements, particularly in curriculum learning and the ability to predict sequences far exceeding the training length. While run-time and memory efficiency undoubtedly contribute, they cannot fully explain these gains, suggesting other factors are at play. My initial intuition is that the Sparse Memory Access architecture may play a role analogous to that of Sparse Auto-encoders in other contexts; readers would benefit from the authors’ thoughts on this, even if preliminary.
