- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme using sparse reads/writes to scale MANNs in space and time.  
- SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space/time and demonstrates significant training speedups.  
- Core idea: constrain reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory words while retaining gradient-based learning.  
- Evaluated on synthetic algorithmic tasks and one-shot Omniglot, scaling to hundreds of thousands of time steps/memories.  
- Paper is well-written with clear explanations and convincing experimental results.  
- Practical contribution: making scalable lookups work for writable memories is valuable.  
- Novelty concern: use of approximate nearest neighbors and top-K attention is not new and mirrors prior memory-network/hashing work.  
- Reproducibility concern: task descriptions are insufficient and replication is difficult; requests code release.  
- Requests analysis of failure cases and discussion of which tasks/setups allow training to succeed, especially regarding non-smooth K-argmax behavior and K=1 effects.  
- Suggests removing Section 3.6 and not calling Omniglot "non-synthetic" or "real-world."  
- Strengths summarized: novel scalable method, convincing experiments, clear writing.  
- Weaknesses summarized: limited novelty, poor task descriptions, lack of failure-case analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to address reproducibility, analysis, and suggested edits.
