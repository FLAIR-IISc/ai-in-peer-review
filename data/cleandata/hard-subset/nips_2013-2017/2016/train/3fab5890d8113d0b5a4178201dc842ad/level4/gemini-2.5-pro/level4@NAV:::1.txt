This paper presents a method for computing attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine using fast approximate nearest-neighbor structures and arg-top-K operations. The authors provide experimental results showing that this approach maintains training performance while allowing the model to scale to larger problems.

The conceptual novelty of this work is somewhat limited. The use of hashing-based methods for scalable memory lookups has been explored previously in memory networks (e.g., Section 3.3 in Weston et al., 2014), and several subsequent papers have demonstrated memory architectures with millions of items using similar techniques. However, the application of these tools to *writeable* memories appears novel. While this may not be a major conceptual leap, the practical aspects of implementing this successfully are a valuable contribution worth reporting.

For these reasons, I recommend the paper for acceptance, but I would like to see the following changes addressed.

1.  The paper in its current form would be difficult to reproduce. The descriptions of the experimental tasks are particularly lacking. Given that the code for the original NTM paper has not been made public and no standard versions of these tasks exist, it is crucial for the authors to provide meticulous descriptions of their task construction. I would also strongly request that the authors commit to releasing their experimental code.

2.  A discussion of the method's limitations is needed. Under what conditions will this approach fail? The paper frequently refers to the model as "smooth," yet the core argmax (or K-argmax) operation is non-differentiable. With K=1 in a read-only context, this model is very similar to the MemNN-WSH (Sukhbaatar et al., 2015), which was reported to be less effective than fully smooth models. Is the success here attributable to the value of K, the nature of the tasks, or another factor? This paper would be more impactful if it included an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of the setups that enable its training to succeed. The model performs a discrete action and is trained without explicitly accounting for this (in contrast to methods like those in Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015). That the authors achieve good results this way is an interesting contribution, but the paper would be stronger if it helped the reader understand the limits of the approach.

Additionally, I suggest the authors remove Section 3.6. I also recommend that they avoid describing the Omniglot dataset as "non-synthetic" or "real-world."
