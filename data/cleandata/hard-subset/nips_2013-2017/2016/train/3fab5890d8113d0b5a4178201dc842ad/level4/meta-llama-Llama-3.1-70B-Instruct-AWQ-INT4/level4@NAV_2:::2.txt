This paper proposes a novel memory access scheme, called Sparse Access Memory (SAM), for neural networks augmented with external memory. The authors demonstrate that SAM retains the representational power of the original approaches while training efficiently with very large memories. They show that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory efficiency compared to non-sparse models.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of scaling memory-augmented neural networks is an important one. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the background and related work, including a discussion of attention and content-based addressing, Memory Networks, and Neural Turing Machines.

The proposed SAM architecture is well-described, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of the sparse read and write operations, as well as the controller and efficient backpropagation through time. The use of approximate nearest neighbor indexing is also a key contribution of the paper.

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating that SAM can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition. The authors also show that SAM can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories.

However, I have some concerns regarding the technical strength of the paper. While the experiments are interesting, the main technical contributions, namely efficient backpropagation and approximation nearest neighbor using sparse access memory, seem quite straightforward. The authors do not provide a rigorous analysis of the time and space complexity of their approach, and the theoretical guarantees are limited.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. However, I am not convinced that the paper is technically strong enough for acceptance.
