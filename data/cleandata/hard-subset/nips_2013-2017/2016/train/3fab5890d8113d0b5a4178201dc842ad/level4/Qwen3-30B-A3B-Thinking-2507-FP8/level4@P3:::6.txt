

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an efficient memory-augmented neural network (MANN) architecture that addresses a critical scalability limitation of existing models like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks. The authors demonstrate that by constraining memory reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words and using efficient data structures for content-based addressing, SAM achieves asymptotic optimal time and space complexity (O(log N) per time step for reads and O(1) space per time step), while maintaining comparable learning performance to dense variants. The paper makes a strong case for SAM's significance by showing it runs 1,000× faster and uses 3,000× less physical memory than non-sparse models, while successfully scaling to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories. This work directly addresses a fundamental limitation of previous MANNs that has hindered their application in real-world settings, as noted in prior NIPS work on NTMs [7] and Memory Networks [21].

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide thorough theoretical analysis of SAM's complexity (Supplementary A) and conduct extensive experiments across multiple benchmarks: synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), the Babi question-answering tasks, and the Omniglot one-shot learning dataset. The results consistently demonstrate that SAM maintains near-identical data efficiency to dense models while offering dramatic improvements in computational and memory efficiency. For instance, SAM achieves the best reported results on Babi tasks without supervising memory access, and significantly outperforms other models on Omniglot when scaled to large memory capacities. The authors also successfully demonstrate a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), showing the approach's generality.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The background section effectively contextualizes SAM within the landscape of MANNs, clearly explaining content-based addressing and the limitations of existing approaches. The technical exposition of SAM's sparse read/write operations and efficient backpropagation through time is particularly well-structured. The authors thoughtfully address potential concerns about the impact of sparsity on learning performance, showing that SAM actually learns more effectively than dense models on certain tasks. The experimental setup is transparent, with detailed hyperparameter specifications provided in supplementary materials.

Regarding originality, SAM presents a novel and elegant solution to a well-known problem in MANNs. While prior work has explored sparse memory access (e.g., [22] using reinforcement learning for discrete addressing), SAM's approach is fundamentally different in its use of approximate nearest neighbor search within a differentiable framework, achieving optimal complexity while maintaining end-to-end differentiability. The authors properly contextualize their contribution against prior NIPS work, noting how SAM improves upon previous approaches in both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance.

The significance of this work is substantial. By enabling the use of extremely large memories in MANNs, SAM opens the door to applications requiring long-term memory storage that were previously computationally infeasible. The paper demonstrates this through successful scaling to tasks with memory sizes in the millions and sequence lengths exceeding 40,000 steps. The authors also provide compelling evidence of SAM's generalizability by adapting it to the Differentiable Neural Computer. This work represents a meaningful step toward making memory-augmented networks practical for real-world applications.

The main weakness of the paper is the lack of discussion regarding the potential limitations of the sparse approximation. While the authors demonstrate that SAM performs well on the tested tasks, it would be valuable to explore whether there are specific problem domains where the sparse approximation might fail. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how SAM compares to alternative approaches for scaling memory systems, such as the reinforcement learning-based method in [22].

The paper's comparison in Figure 4 (Omniglot results) is fair and well-justified. The authors appropriately compare models using the same physical memory constraints, which is a more realistic scenario than comparing models with the same memory size but different physical requirements. This is a thoughtful consideration of practical resource limitations.

In summary, this is a high-quality contribution to the field. The paper demonstrates a significant technical advance with clear theoretical and empirical validation, addresses an important practical limitation of memory-augmented networks, and provides a framework that can be applied to multiple existing architectures. The work is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a compelling case for the significance of sparse memory access in MANNs.

Strengths:
- Solves a fundamental scalability problem in MANNs with asymptotically optimal complexity
- Demonstrates dramatic speed and memory improvements (1,000× faster, 3,000× less memory)
- Maintains comparable learning performance to dense models across multiple benchmarks
- Shows generalizability through adaptation to Differentiable Neural Computer
- Provides thorough theoretical analysis and experimental validation

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of potential limitations of sparse approximation
- Could provide more detailed comparison with alternative scaling approaches

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks, addressing a critical practical limitation while maintaining performance. The technical soundness, clarity, and significance of the work align strongly with NIPS standards.
