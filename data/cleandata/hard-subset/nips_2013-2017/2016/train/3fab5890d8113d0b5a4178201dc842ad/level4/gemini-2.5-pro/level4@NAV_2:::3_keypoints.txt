- Addresses a major scalability bottleneck in MANNs: dense read/write operations that scale linearly with memory size.  
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), restricting reads/writes to a small constant subset of memory per timestep.  
- Uses ANN indices for content-based reads and a usage-tracking mechanism for writes to enable sparse access.  
- Achieves dramatic empirical efficiency gains—up to three orders of magnitude in speed and memory—making MANNs feasible with millions of slots.  
- Technically sound with clear architecture description and asymptotic complexity analysis (Supplementary A).  
- Thorough experimental evaluation across synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning.  
- Novel integration of sparse updates and ANN data structures into an end-to-end differentiable memory system, with extension to DNC.  
- Unexpected finding that sparsity can match or improve learning and data efficiency, suggesting sparsity may act as a useful bias/regularizer.  
- Lacks analysis or intuition explaining why sparse updates sometimes improve learning (regularization, optimization effects, etc.).  
- Relies on standard off-the-shelf ANN structures (k-d trees, LSH) not tailored for dynamic online memory—needs more discussion of online ANN alternatives and challenges.  
- Overall: well-written, significant, original contribution that convincingly solves a key scalability issue and is likely to be influential.
