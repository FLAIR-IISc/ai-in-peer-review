This paper presents a novel memory access scheme, known as Sparse Access Memory (SAM), designed for memory-augmented neural network architectures. Building upon the recent concept of Neural Turing Machines, this work enables neural networks to transcend the limitations of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution lies in the constraint of all writes to and reads from external memory to a fixed size, effectively limiting the memory words to a subset rather than an unlimited size. The authors rigorously prove the methodology and demonstrate its optimality, showcasing potential improvements in read and write performance due to reduced memory size, as evident in section 4.1. Furthermore, the authors assess the learning costs associated with introducing sparsity in section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks, including copy, associated call, and priority sort. Notably, the combined costs are lower than those of NTM for certain tasks (i.e., 2 and 3). The inclusion of real-world examples in section 4.4, benchmarked using Torch7, adds significant value to the paper. The concept is both straightforward and innovative, with substantial speedup compared to NTM. The finding that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear or ANN approach, can benefit early-stage learning in specific cases (section 4.2) is particularly intriguing. To further enhance the paper, the authors may consider elaborating on how to generalize SAM to other cases or exploring different ANN approaches that can leverage SAM, providing valuable insights for the audience. Overall, the paper is well-written, with clear assumptions and a detailed architecture presented in the appendix, making for an engaging read.
