This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), within a memory-augmented neural network architecture. This research area is relatively new, originating with the invention of Neural Turing Machines, and aims to extend the capabilities of neural networks beyond the limitations of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution of this paper is constraining all reads and writes to external memory to operate on a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than allowing access to the entire memory space. The authors provide a proof of the methodology and demonstrate its optimality. This constrained access plausibly leads to improved read and write performance due to the reduced effective memory size, as shown in section 4.1. However, the authors also carefully consider the learning costs associated with introducing this sparsity, evaluating the approach using standard Neural Turing Machine tasks: 1. copy, 2. Associative Recall, and 3. Priority Sort. The overall cost—considering both learning and access—is lower than that of a standard NTM for some tasks (specifically, tasks 2 and 3). 

I particularly appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in section 4.4. The implementation is based on Torch7, and the speedup achieved compared to NTM is significant and impressive. The observation that these sparse reads and writes, implemented using either a linear layer or an ANN, can actually *benefit* early-stage learning in certain cases (section 4.2) is very interesting. 

To further strengthen the paper, it would be helpful if the authors could elaborate on how to generalize these benefits. Exploring under what conditions learning could be further improved, or investigating the performance of SAM when used with different ANN architectures, would be valuable for the broader research community. 

Overall, this is a well-written paper that clearly lays out all assumptions and provides a detailed architectural description in the appendix. It is an interesting and worthwhile contribution to the field.
