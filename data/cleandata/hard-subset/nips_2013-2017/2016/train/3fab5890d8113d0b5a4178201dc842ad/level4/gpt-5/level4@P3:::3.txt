Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales read and write operations to very large external memories by enforcing sparsity. Instead of dense, soft attention over all N memory slots at each step—as in Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and related MANNs—SAM restricts reads and writes to K slots (K ≪ N), chosen via content-based addressing using approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indices, and manages writes via a simple policy that updates previously read locations and overwrites the least recently used (LRU) slot. The authors also introduce an efficient backpropagation-through-time (BPTT) scheme that stores only sparse memory modifications and rolls them back during the backward pass, yielding O(1) memory per timestep.

Relative to NTMs (Graves et al., 2014) and Memory Networks (Weston et al., Sukhbaatar et al.), SAM maintains end-to-end trainability while achieving asymptotically optimal time and space with respect to memory size under reasonable assumptions. The approach is also shown to extend to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) by forming a sparse variant (SDNC). Empirically, SAM attains large speed and memory savings and competitive or improved task performance on NTM synthetic tasks, curriculum-scaled long sequences, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot classification.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple mechanism to induce sparsity in reads and writes, coupled with practical data structures (kd-trees/LSH) for content-based addressing.
- Strong asymptotic complexity: O(log N) time per step (with ANN) and O(1) memory per timestep for BPTT, with convincing system-level engineering (sparse updates with rollback).
- Large empirical gains: up to ~1000× faster and ~3000× less physical memory than dense baselines for large N; sublinear scaling with ANN.
- Competitive or improved learning compared to dense models on NTM tasks; shows that sparsity can aid early training on some tasks.
- Demonstrates scalability via curriculum to sequences with thousands of steps and million-scale memories; generalization to much longer sequences than seen in training.
- Broad applicability: adaptation to DNC (SDNC) with strong bAbI results (near state of the art without supervised memory retrieval).

Weaknesses and questions:
- End-to-end differentiability: The read operation uses top-K selection via an ANN index; this introduces non-differentiability in the routing step. While the authors state there are no gradients through the ANN, it would help to more carefully justify why training remains stable and effective, and discuss potential gradient bias introduced by hard selection.
- Experimental fairness and scope: Some scaling comparisons equate physical memory usage rather than memory capacity (e.g., dense models with 64 slots vs SAM with ~2M slots). This is reasonable for demonstrating scalability, but it would be useful to also report accuracy at matched capacity where feasible (or matched performance vs compute curves).
- Sensitivity/ablation: Limited analysis of how K, the LRU threshold, erase policy, and choice of ANN (kd-tree vs LSH) affect performance, stability, and speed. Ablations on K and write policies (e.g., content-based sparse writes vs LRU) would strengthen the empirical story.
- Practicality of online ANN: Maintaining a balanced kd-tree online is known to be tricky; the paper rebuilds periodically. More details on rebuild frequency, overhead, and interaction with GPU training would be valuable. Similarly, a brief discussion of high-dimensional regimes where kd-trees degrade and LSH parameters are critical would help practitioners.
- Theoretical claims: The “asymptotically optimal” statement hinges on assumptions about content-based addressing and constant K; clarifying the assumptions and constants would make the claim more precise.
- Real-world tasks: Beyond Omniglot and bAbI, evaluation on larger-scale natural language or sequential tasks (e.g., long-context language modeling or real document QA) would better demonstrate practical impact.

Quality:
Technically solid with a strong systems contribution. The complexity analysis, sparse rollback BPTT, and ANN integration are thoughtfully designed. Empirical evidence spans synthetic and semi-real benchmarks, with substantial speed/memory profiling. Some aspects (effect of non-differentiable top-K, ANN overhead details) could be analyzed more rigorously.

Clarity:
Overall well-written and organized, with the main ideas clearly presented and helpful figures. Some important algorithmic details are relegated to the supplement (e.g., data structures for usage tracking, rollback mechanics); a concise pseudocode or schematic in the main text would improve readability. Clarify the differentiability story and assumptions behind complexity optimality. Minor typos exist but do not impede comprehension.

Originality:
The combination of sparse read/write with efficient ANN-based content addressing and reversible sparse BPTT for MANNs is novel and timely. Related ideas exist (e.g., discrete RL-based addressing, memory networks with soft attention), but this work offers a distinct, practical path to scaling differentiable memory.

Significance:
High. Efficient large-memory access is a core bottleneck for MANNs, and this paper provides a principled, implementable solution with strong empirical evidence. The approach is likely to influence future architectures and applications needing long-term memory.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a short main-text description (or pseudocode) of the rollback BPTT mechanism and memory usage tracking, including computational overhead in practice.
- Expand ablations on K, erase/overwrite policy, and ANN choice; report sensitivity and trade-offs between speed and accuracy.
- Discuss the impact of non-differentiable top-K selection (e.g., comparisons with soft top-K or straight-through estimators), and clarify why training remains effective without gradients through the index.
- Report ANN maintenance overhead (insertion/deletion/rebuild costs) and training device (CPU/GPU) details; note how to integrate with modern deep learning frameworks.
- Where possible, include matched-capacity comparisons or performance vs compute/memory trade-off curves.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important scalability limitation of MANNs with a simple, effective method.
- Strong theoretical and systems contribution with clear complexity benefits.
- Extensive empirical results showing large speed/memory gains and competitive accuracy.
- Demonstrated generality (SDNC) and practical utility on diverse tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited analysis of the effects and potential pitfalls of non-differentiable sparse selection.
- Some comparisons favor SAM’s capacity; fewer results on large real-world datasets.
- ANN maintenance details and ablations could be expanded for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper makes a substantial and well-validated contribution to scaling differentiable memory, with clear potential impact on research and practice. The suggested clarifications and ablations would further strengthen an already strong submission.
