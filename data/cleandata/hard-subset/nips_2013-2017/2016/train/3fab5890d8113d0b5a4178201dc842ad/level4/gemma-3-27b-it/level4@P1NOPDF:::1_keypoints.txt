- Method: uses fast approximate nearest neighbors and arg-top-K to compute attention in a Neural Turing Machine.  
- Empirical result: shows no training degradation and enables scaling to larger problems.  
- Novelty: incrementally novel—similar techniques exist for memory networks, but application to writable memories is less explored.  
- Practical contribution: implementation challenges are worth reporting.  
- Recommendation: accept conditional on revisions.  
- Reproducibility: task descriptions are insufficient; replication would be difficult—authors should provide detailed task construction and release their code.  
- Limitations: paper lacks analysis of when the approach fails or succeeds and whether performance is consistent.  
- Smoothness issue: authors claim smoothness despite using non-smooth argmax/K-argmax; this mismatch needs discussion.  
- K=1 comparison: with K=1 the model resembles MemNN-WSH (which performed worse than smooth models); need to determine if differences stem from K=1 or task choices.  
- Discrete-action training: model makes discrete selections but is trained without explicit methods for discreteness—this requires justification or analysis.  
- Editorial fixes: remove section 3.6.  
- Dataset wording: do not characterize Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
