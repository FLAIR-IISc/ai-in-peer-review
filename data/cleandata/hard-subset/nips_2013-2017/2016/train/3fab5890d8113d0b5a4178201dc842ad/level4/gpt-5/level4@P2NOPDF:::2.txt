This paper targets the poor time and space scalability of memory-augmented neural networks as memory size grows. The authors propose a memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), intended to preserve the representational power of prior models while enabling efficient training. They report substantial gains—up to 1000× faster training and 3000× lower memory usage compared to dense alternatives.

While the problem is important and the empirical results are appealing, I do not find the technical contributions sufficiently strong for acceptance. The core components—efficient backpropagation through sparse operations and the use of approximate nearest-neighbor lookups within SAM—are relatively straightforward and do not, in my view, constitute a deep or novel advance. The manuscript does not convincingly demonstrate conceptual innovation beyond these practical optimizations, nor does it provide theoretical insights that would elevate the contribution.

In summary, despite the interesting experiments and promising speed and memory savings, the paper’s technical novelty and depth appear limited. I am not in favor of acceptance.
