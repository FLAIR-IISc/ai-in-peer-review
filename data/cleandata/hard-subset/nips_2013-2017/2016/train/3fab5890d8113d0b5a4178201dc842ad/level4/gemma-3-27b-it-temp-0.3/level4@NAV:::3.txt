This paper presents Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a new memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks – a rapidly developing field originating with Neural Turing Machines. This augmentation enables neural networks to surpass the limitations of conventional LSTM models. The key contribution lies in restricting both read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of external memory words, rather than allowing access to the entire memory space. The authors demonstrate the methodology and establish its optimality. The reduced memory footprint, as detailed in section 4.1, suggests potential for improved read and write performance. Importantly, the work also analyzes the learning costs associated with this sparsity (section 4.2) using established Neural Turing Machine tasks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The overall cost is shown to be lower than that of standard NTMs for certain tasks (specifically, associative recall and priority sort). The inclusion of a real-world example in section 4.4 further strengthens the paper. Implemented in Torch7, the proposed approach achieves significant speedups compared to NTM, a particularly impressive result. The observation that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear layer or ANN, can positively influence early-stage learning in specific scenarios (section 4.2) is a noteworthy finding. Expanding on the generalizability of these learning benefits – identifying conditions under which learning is improved, or exploring SAM’s compatibility with alternative ANN architectures – would be valuable for the broader research community. Overall, this is a clearly written and well-structured paper, with all assumptions explicitly stated and the architecture thoroughly documented in the appendix. It represents a compelling and interesting contribution to the field.
