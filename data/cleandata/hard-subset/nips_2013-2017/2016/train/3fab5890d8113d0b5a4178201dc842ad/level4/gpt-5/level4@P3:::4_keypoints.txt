- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a differentiable MANN with K-sparse ANN reads, sparse targeted writes, and sparse rollback for efficient BPTT, achieving asymptotically optimal complexity claims.
- Key ideas: approximate nearest-neighbor K-sparse reads (O(log N)), sparse writes to read locations + LRU (O(1)), and rollback storing only sparse updates (O(1) per step).
- Retains representational power of prior MANNs while replacing dense attention with sparse operations and in-network ANN structures.
- Empirically shows large runtime/memory savings (≈1000× faster, ≈3000× less memory vs dense NTM) and comparable learning on synthetic NTM tasks, scalability to thousands of steps, good Omniglot one-shot results, and strong bAbI performance with SDNC.
- Strength: coherent, well-motivated redesign enforcing sparsity while preserving differentiability; elegant sparse rollback for efficient BPTT.
- Strength: clear asymptotic complexity analysis (O(log N) time, O(1) space per step) with arguments for optimality.
- Strength: strong engineering and empirical validation across timing, memory benchmarks, and multiple learning tasks.
- Strength: general framework extendable beyond NTM (e.g., DNC-style temporal associations).
- Weakness: practical performance and guarantees depend on specific ANN choices and rebuild policies; needs deeper analysis of accuracy–speed trade-offs.
- Weakness: heuristic write policy (read+LRU) is ad hoc; more ablations needed versus alternative sparse write strategies.
- Weakness: limited task scope—no large-scale real-world sequence evaluations (e.g., language modeling, MT).
- Weakness: fairness concerns in curricula (sparse models use much larger memories); need ablations on K, reads vs writes sparsity, and ANN vs exact search.
- Weakness: some theoretical and implementation details are relegated to the supplement, reducing accessibility.
- Quality: technically sound, carefully implemented, claims supported by benchmarks, but generality to large real-world tasks is unproven.
- Clarity: generally clear and organized; would benefit from a complexity table, pseudocode for core ops, and expanded ANN maintenance discussion.
- Originality: high—novel use of in-network ANN for differentiable K-sparse content addressing with efficient BPTT; extension to SDNC shows generality.
- Significance: potentially high if applicable broadly; current empirical scope promising but needs larger-scale validation.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to K and adaptive K methods; ANN rebuild frequency and impact vs exact NN; learnable sparse write keys vs LRU; effects on gradient/credit assignment; episode memory management.
- Suggestions: add ablations on K/write policy/ANN/rebuild frequency, complexity vs accuracy plots, at least one large real-world sequence task, and release code/data structures.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, scalable technical contribution with clear empirical efficiency gains and general applicability.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on engineering choices without exhaustive analysis and limited large-scale real-world evaluation.
- Overall: impressive, timely contribution addressing a central MANN bottleneck; merits strong consideration despite missing ablations and broader evaluations.
