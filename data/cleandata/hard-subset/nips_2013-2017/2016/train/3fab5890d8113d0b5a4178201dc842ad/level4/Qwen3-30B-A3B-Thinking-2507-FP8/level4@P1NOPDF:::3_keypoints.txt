- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which constrains all read/write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory words.  
- Provides theoretical justification and claims optimality for the sparsity constraint.  
- Sparsity reduces memory footprint and improves read/write performance (Section 4.1).  
- Empirical evaluation on standard NTM benchmarks: Copy, Associative Recall, and Priority Sort (Section 4.2).  
- SAM achieves lower combined learning costs than NTM for Associative Recall and Priority Sort.  
- Sparse read/write implementations (linear or ANN-based) can accelerate early-stage learning in some cases.  
- Authors are encouraged to clarify when sparsity-induced learning benefits occur and how broadly they generalize.  
- Includes a real-world application example (Section 4.4) demonstrating practical utility.  
- Benchmarking performed in Torch7; architecture and assumptions are well-documented in the appendix.  
- Reported speedup over NTM is substantial and impressive.  
- Main weakness: limited discussion of generalizability and how different ANN approaches interact with SAM.  
- Overall assessment: well-written, technically sound, novel contribution advancing scalable memory-augmented architectures.
