- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural architecture improving efficiency while maintaining performance.  
- Addresses scalability limits of existing MANNs by reducing linear time and memory overhead as memory size grows.  
- Proposes a sparse read/write scheme with O(log N) time per step and O(1) space per step, achieving asymptotic optimality.  
- Theoretical justification provided (Supplement A) is thorough and convincing.  
- Empirical results show SAM is ~1,000× faster and uses ~3,000× less physical memory than dense models.  
- Maintains comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi, and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Demonstrates scalability to tasks with 100,000+ time steps and memories.  
- Shows strong generalization, handling sequences 20× longer than seen during training and trained via curriculum to 40× longer sequences.  
- Extends SAM to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) achieving best reported results on Babi without supervised memory access.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-motivated, and technically accessible.  
- Contribution is novel and significant, making MANNs viable for large-scale, real-world applications.  
- Evaluation is comprehensive and results are consistently compelling.  
- Pro-acceptance: asymptotic optimality, massive efficiency gains, preserved benchmark performance, strong generalization, and architecture generality.  
- Con-acceptance: lacks in-depth explanation for why SAM enables superior sequence generalization beyond efficiency.  
- Con-acceptance: insufficient comparison with other recent scalable memory approaches (e.g., RL-based discrete addressing).  
- Con-acceptance: connection to sparse representation theory (e.g., sparse autoencoders) is noted but underdeveloped.
