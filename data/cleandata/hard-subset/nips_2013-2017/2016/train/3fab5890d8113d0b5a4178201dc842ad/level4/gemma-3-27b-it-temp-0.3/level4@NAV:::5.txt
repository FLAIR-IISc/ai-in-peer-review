This paper presents a Sparse Access Memory architecture designed for memory-augmented neural networks, including Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. The results demonstrate substantial gains in both runtime and memory efficiency – improvements of three orders of magnitude compared to existing approaches. Notably, the architecture also exhibits significant performance enhancements in curriculum learning scenarios and a remarkable ability to generalize to sequences substantially longer than those encountered during training (successfully extrapolating from training sequences up to length 10,000 to testing sequences of length 200,000). This work represents a potentially significant advance in the field. Neural networks with external memory have been an active area of research since the introduction of Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, yielding interesting results but lacking the practical applicability demonstrated here. The reported efficiency and performance improvements suggest that memory-augmented networks are now viable for real-world applications, with potentially broad implications. My primary concern is the lack of discussion regarding the mechanisms driving the observed improvements in curriculum learning and long-sequence generalization. While increased efficiency contributes, it doesn’t fully explain these gains. I suspect the Sparse Memory Access architecture may be playing a key role, potentially analogous to the function of Sparse Auto-encoders in other contexts. Exploring this intuition, even preliminarily, would be valuable for readers.
