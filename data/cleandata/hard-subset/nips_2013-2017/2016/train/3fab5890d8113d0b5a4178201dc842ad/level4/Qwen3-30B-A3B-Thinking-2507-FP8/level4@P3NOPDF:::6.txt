

The paper introduces a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) variant with sparse read/write access, addressing a critical limitation of conventional NTMs: their prohibitive memory requirements and computational overhead during backpropagation, which hinder practical deployment. The proposed architecture achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods while significantly reducing memory demands, making it a compelling contribution to memory-augmented neural networks. The writing is clear, well-structured, and accessible, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution.

To enhance the paper’s accessibility for a broader audience, the background section would benefit from a concise, self-contained explanation of memory-augmented neural networks. Specifically, a brief definition of the write operator’s functional role—distinguishing it from conventional memory systems (e.g., how it enables dynamic, content-based updates versus fixed addressing)—would aid non-specialist readers. While experts may grasp this implicitly, such a clarification would strengthen the paper’s foundation without adding significant length.

The comparison in Figure 4 warrants careful consideration. The authors compare models under identical *physical memory* constraints (e.g., same RAM allocation), which is a valid engineering perspective. However, this approach conflates two distinct variables: the *memory structure* (e.g., sparse vs. dense) and the *physical resource* (e.g., 1GB RAM). A more informative comparison would fix the *memory capacity* (e.g., 1,000 memory slots) across all methods, then evaluate performance under that constraint. This would isolate the impact of the sparse access mechanism itself, revealing how effectively the proposed method utilizes a given memory size—a more direct reflection of real-world resource limitations where memory capacity (not physical footprint) is typically the bottleneck. While the current comparison is defensible for hardware-aware scenarios, the alternative would provide clearer insights into the algorithm’s efficiency gains.

That said, the paper’s core contribution is notable. The method’s performance near theoretical bounds—achieved with minimal memory overhead—demonstrates significant progress toward practical NTM deployment. The sparse access design elegantly balances expressiveness and efficiency, and the empirical results (particularly in Figure 4’s performance metrics) convincingly support this claim. The authors’ focus on bridging theory and practice is well-executed.

Minor suggestions for refinement:  
- Clarify the "memory" vs. "physical memory" distinction early in the methodology (e.g., "We compare models under identical memory slot capacity, not identical physical storage").  
- Consider a short example in the background illustrating the write operator’s operation (e.g., "Unlike a standard array, the write operator updates a subset of memory locations based on content, enabling adaptive storage").  
- Ensure consistent terminology: "Neural Turing Machine" (NTM), not "MTN" or "Neural Turting Machine."

In summary, this work addresses a well-motivated problem with a technically sound solution. The paper is strong in its core argument and results, and the proposed refinements would elevate its clarity and impact without altering the contribution. The authors successfully demonstrate that sparse access can make NTMs viable for real-world applications, a significant step forward. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
