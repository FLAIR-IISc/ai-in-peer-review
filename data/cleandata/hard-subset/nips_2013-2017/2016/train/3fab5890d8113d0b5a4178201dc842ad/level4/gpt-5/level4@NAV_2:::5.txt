The paper proposes a Sparse Access Memory (SAM) architecture for memory-augmented neural networks (e.g., Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks) that delivers striking runtime and memory savings—up to three orders of magnitude—relative to existing dense-access approaches. The same design also appears to yield substantial gains under curriculum training and strong extrapolation to sequence lengths far beyond those encountered during training (trained on sequences up to 10,000 and evaluated successfully on sequences of length 200,000). This could be a landmark contribution. The field of memory-augmented neural networks, initiated by NTMs and Memory Networks, has produced compelling research and experiments, but practical applicability has remained limited. The dramatic efficiency and performance gains reported here suggest these models may now be ready for real-world use, with broad implications. The primary missing element is a discussion of why curriculum performance and long-range extrapolation improve so dramatically. Pure runtime and memory efficiency cannot by themselves explain these effects, so additional mechanisms must be contributing (my tentative intuition is that sparse access may play a role analogous to sparse autoencoders, improving representation and credit assignment). Even a preliminary analysis from the authors on this point would be valuable.
