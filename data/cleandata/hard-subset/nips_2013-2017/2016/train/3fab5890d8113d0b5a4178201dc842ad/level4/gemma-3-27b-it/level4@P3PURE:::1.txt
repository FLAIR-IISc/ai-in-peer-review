The authors leverage fast approximate nearest neighbor search and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights within a Neural Turing Machine. Their experiments demonstrate that this approach does not degrade training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problem instances. While the novelty of this work is incremental – as evidenced by related approaches in memory networks, such as section 3.3 of Weston et al. (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf) – where memories with millions of items and hashing techniques are employed for scalable lookups, the application to *writable* memories appears less common. Even without a significant conceptual leap, the practical considerations of implementing this approach are valuable and warrant reporting. Therefore, I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on several revisions.

First, replicating the results presented would be challenging given the current level of detail. The task descriptions, in particular, are lacking. Considering the unavailability of code from the original NTM paper and the absence of a standardized task implementation, a thorough description of task construction is crucial. I also strongly encourage the authors to commit to releasing their experimental code.

Second, a critical analysis of the limitations of this method is needed. The authors frequently characterize their model as smooth, yet acknowledge the non-smooth nature of the argmax (or K-argmax) operation. With $K=1$, the model closely resembles the MemNN-WSH architecture described in Sukhbaatar et al. (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf) – albeit without hashing – which was previously reported to perform worse than fully smooth alternatives. Is this performance difference attributable to $K=1$, the specific tasks used, or other factors? This paper would benefit significantly from an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of the task characteristics and settings under which this training approach succeeds. Their model inherently makes discrete action choices, and they train without explicitly addressing this (in contrast to Graves et al., http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). The authors’ successful results are a contribution, but understanding the limitations would be even more impactful. 

Finally, I suggest removing section 3.6. Furthermore, the characterization of the Omniglot dataset as “non-synthetic” or “real-world” is inaccurate and should be revised.
