The authors replace standard attention computations in a Neural Turing Machine with fast approximate-nearest-neighbor structures and arg-top-K operations. Their experiments indicate no degradation in training performance and, in fact, enable training on larger problems. The novelty here is limited; see, for example, section 3.3 of the memory networks paper http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf. Several memory-network papers now use memories with millions of items and hashing to scale lookups. On the other hand, I have not seen such techniques applied to writable memories; even if the conceptual step is modest, the practical engineering to make it work is worth reporting. I recommend acceptance, but with the following revisions:

1. Reproducibility: It would be difficult to replicate the results based on the current descriptions. The task specifications are especially under-described. Since the code for the original NTM tasks has not been released and there is no standardized version of these tasks, the paper must provide careful, detailed descriptions of how the tasks are constructed. I also ask the authors to commit to releasing the code for their experiments.

2. Limits and failure cases: When does this approach fail? Does it always work? The paper frequently describes the model as smooth, yet the argmax (or K-argmax) step is not smooth. With K=1 in the read-only setting, the model is quite similar to MemNN-WSH in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf (though that model does not use hashing), and that variant was reported to perform worse than the fully smooth model. Is this due to K=1? The particular tasks? The paper would be more valuable with an analysis of failure modes and a discussion of which task types and training setups allow learning to succeed. The model makes a discrete action, and training proceeds without accounting for this (in contrast to http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Demonstrating that it works under these conditions is a worthwhile contribution, but the limits should be clarified. I also suggest removing section 3.6 and avoiding calling the Omniglot data “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
