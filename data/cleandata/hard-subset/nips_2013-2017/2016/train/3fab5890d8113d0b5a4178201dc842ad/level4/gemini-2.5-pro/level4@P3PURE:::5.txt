This paper introduces a Sparse Access Memory architecture for memory-augmented neural networks, such as Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. The proposed architecture achieves remarkable improvements in run-time and memory efficiency—reportedly by three orders of magnitude—compared to existing models in this class. The architecture also demonstrates significant performance gains, particularly in curriculum learning settings and in its ability to generalize to sequences an order of magnitude longer than those seen during training (e.g., training on sequences up to length 10,000 and testing on lengths up to 200,000).

This is a potentially groundbreaking paper. The field of memory-augmented neural networks, initiated by work on Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, is an active area of research. While prior work has produced interesting results, practical applicability has been limited. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported here could signify that these models are now ready for practical use, which would have far-reaching implications.

One aspect missing from the paper is a discussion of the underlying reasons for the drastic performance improvements, specifically in curriculum learning and long-sequence generalization. The gains in run-time and memory efficiency do not in themselves account for these performance benefits, suggesting another mechanism is at play. My preliminary intuition is that the Sparse Access Memory architecture may be playing a role analogous to that of sparse auto-encoders in other contexts. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide their own thoughts on this phenomenon, even if their hypotheses are preliminary.
