The authors propose a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks by introducing sparse reads and writes, which they term Sparse Access Memory (SAM). This method allows for efficient training with large external memories, achieving significant speedups and reductions in memory usage. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of SAM on various tasks, including synthetic and natural language processing tasks, and shows that it can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models.

The main idea of SAM is to constrain reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words, allowing for efficient computation and memory usage. The authors achieve this by using approximate nearest neighbor search and arg-top-K operations for computing attention weights. The paper also introduces a sparse memory management scheme that tracks memory usage and finds unused blocks of memory for recording new information.

The authors evaluate SAM on various tasks, including algorithmic tasks, question answering, and one-shot Omniglot character recognition. They show that SAM achieves significant speedups and reductions in memory usage compared to dense models, while maintaining comparable performance. The paper also demonstrates the ability of SAM to scale to tasks requiring large amounts of memory and long sequence lengths.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its advantages. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM on various tasks. However, there are some limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed.

One concern is the lack of detailed descriptions of the tasks and the experimental setup. The authors should provide more information about the construction of the tasks, the hyperparameters used, and the training procedure. This would facilitate replication of the results and allow for a more thorough understanding of the proposed method.

Another concern is the potential limitations of the proposed method. The authors note that the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth, which may lead to difficulties in training. The authors should provide more analysis of failure cases and discuss the types of tasks or setups that may not be suitable for their method.

Additionally, the authors should consider removing Section 3.6, as it appears to be unnecessary and may distract from the main contributions of the paper. Furthermore, the authors should avoid referring to the Omniglot data as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as this may be misleading.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and effective approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
