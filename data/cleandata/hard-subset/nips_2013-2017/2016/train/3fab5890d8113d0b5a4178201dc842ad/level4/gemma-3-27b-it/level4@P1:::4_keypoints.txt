- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory scheme that preserves NTM representational power while being far more efficient for very large memories.  
- SAM addresses scalability bottlenecks in Memory Augmented Neural Networks by thresholding memory modifications to a sparse subset and using efficient data structures for content-based reads.  
- The paper provides theoretical asymptotic lower bounds for SAM’s time and space complexity and demonstrates empirical speedups and memory reductions.  
- Empirical results show up to ~1,000x speedups and ~3,000x less physical memory compared to dense models, with comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks and Omniglot.  
- SAM scales to tasks requiring hundreds of thousands of time steps and large memory capacities.  
- The method generalizes to other architectures (e.g., Sparse DNC), improving performance on tasks like bAbI.  
- Technical soundness: clear theoretical justification and a compelling claim of asymptotic optimality.  
- Originality: novel introduction of sparse read/write operations in differentiable memory and adaptation to DNC.  
- Significance: substantial practical impact by enabling much larger sequence and memory usage in MANNs.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and supplemented with helpful material.  
- Weakness: ANN implementation details (FLANN, LSH, k-d trees) lack in-depth discussion and trade-off analysis.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., discount factor for usage statistic) is under-explored and lacks practical guidelines.  
- Weakness: Limited comparison with alternative scaling approaches such as reinforcement-learning-based methods.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a critical bottleneck and provides a novel, technically sound solution with strong empirical evidence.  
- Argument for acceptance: approach is generalizable across differentiable memory architectures.  
- Argument against acceptance: insufficient detail on ANN choices and trade-offs.  
- Argument against acceptance: insufficient exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with high potential impact and convincing results; recommended for acceptance.
