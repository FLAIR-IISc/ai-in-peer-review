- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for MANNs that limits reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory locations.
- Contrasts SAM with dense attention models like the NTM and provides theoretical justification and optimality analysis.
- Novel and clear idea: enforcing sparse memory access is straightforward, well-motivated, and well-defined.
- Significant practical speedups in read/write operations compared to dense-access baselines (Section 4.1).
- Thorough empirical evaluation on NTM benchmarks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) showing lower learning cost for SAM on several tasks.
- Demonstrates practical utility with a real-world application (Section 4.4).
- Insight: sparsity implemented via a linear layer or small ANN can benefit early-stage learning, acting as an inductive bias (Section 4.2).
- Suggests deeper analysis of why and when the sparse constraint aids learning to assess generalization of the benefit.
- Recommends exploring alternative access architectures beyond the tested linear/simple ANN approaches.
- Overall assessment: well-written, methodologically sound, assumptions clear, detailed appendix, and a valuable contribution to memory-augmented neural networks.
