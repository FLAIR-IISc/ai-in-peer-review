- Problem: Standard MANNs require read/write over entire memory each step, causing O(NT) time/space and prohibitive training costs for large N or long T.  
- Core idea: Make memory access sparse to achieve asymptotic improvements in time and space.  
- Sparse reads: Retrieve only K nearest neighbors using an ANN (k-d tree/LSH), reducing read complexity from O(N) to ~O(log N).  
- Sparse writes: Restrict writes to a constant number of locations (recently read or LRU), keeping updates constant-time.  
- Efficient BPTT: Store only sparse modifications per step and "roll back" during backward pass to reduce per-step space from O(N) to O(1).  
- Empirical gains: Up to three orders of magnitude faster and more memory-efficient than dense models while maintaining comparable learning performance.  
- Task performance: Competitive on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and one-shot Omniglot; can scale to sequences up to 200,000 steps via curriculum learning.  
- Generality: Approach extends to other MANNs (demonstrated on the DNC).  
- Significance/originality: Addresses a crucial scalability bottleneck and novelly integrates non-differentiable ANN structures into end-to-end models.  
- Technical quality: Sound theory, clear complexity analysis, and thorough experimental validation.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, with precise explanations and effective figures/tables.  
- Weakness — ANN analysis: Needs deeper discussion of online index dynamics, rebuild overhead, imbalance, and approximation error impacts on learning.  
- Weakness — write flexibility: The simple write heuristic may lack flexibility compared to content-interpolating writes; potential limitations should be discussed or alternatives explored.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: More analysis needed on sensitivity to K, ε, and ANN index parameters and their trade-offs.
