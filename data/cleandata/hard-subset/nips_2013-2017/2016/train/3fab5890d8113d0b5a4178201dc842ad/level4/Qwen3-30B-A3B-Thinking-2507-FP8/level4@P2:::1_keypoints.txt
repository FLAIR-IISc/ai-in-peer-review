- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse read/write for memory-augmented networks using ANN and arg-top-K.
- Claims substantial efficiency gains and scaling: asymptotic lower bounds, ~1,000–1,600× speedups and ~3,000× less memory than dense models.
- Key novelty: applies sparse access techniques to writeable memory (extending prior read-only sparse work).
- Solid technical execution with experiments on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi, and Omniglot one-shot classification.
- Theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) supports empirical complexity and scaling claims.
- Demonstrates scalability to very long sequences and large memories (4,000+ sequence length, millions of memory slots) and strong Babi results without supervising memory access.
- Clarity is generally good, but synthetic task construction is under-specified and Omniglot is misleadingly labeled as "non-synthetic/real-world."
- Originality is moderate: idea not entirely novel, but meaningful practical extension to writable memories within a differentiable framework.
- Major strengths: huge computational efficiency, successful sparse writes, scalability, competitive benchmark performance, and theoretical backing.
- Major weaknesses: insufficient replication details, limited analysis of failure cases/limitations, misleading Omniglot terminology.
- Methodological concern: paper does not adequately explain why non-smooth arg-top-K discrete approximation works well compared to related work.
- Comparative analysis weakness: needs clearer direct comparison to related models (e.g., Differentiable Neural Computer).
- Recommendations: provide detailed synthetic task specs, release code, fix Omniglot terminology, analyze failure cases, address arg-top-K non-smoothness and related work, and remove duplicate/irrelevant Section 3.6.
- Overall judgment: valuable practical contribution with modest conceptual novelty; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
