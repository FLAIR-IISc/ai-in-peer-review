The paper introduces a Sparse Access Memory architecture for neural networks augmented with memory, such as Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. This architecture achieves remarkable improvements in both runtime and memory efficiency – three orders of magnitude better than existing memory-augmented neural networks. Furthermore, it appears to significantly enhance performance in curriculum learning scenarios and substantially improves generalization to sequences an order of magnitude longer than those seen during training (extending from training sequences of up to 10,000 to testing sequences of length 200,000). This is a potentially groundbreaking contribution.

The field of neural networks augmented with memory, initiated by Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, has been rapidly developing. While previous research has yielded interesting results, practical applicability has remained a significant challenge. The dramatic efficiency and performance gains reported in this paper suggest that memory-augmented neural networks may now be ready for real-world use, which could have far-reaching implications. 

My primary question regarding this work concerns the underlying reasons for the substantial performance improvements observed in curriculum learning, and the ability to predict sequences far exceeding the training sequence lengths. Runtime and memory efficiency improvements alone cannot fully explain these gains, suggesting that other factors are at play. My initial intuition is that the Sparse Memory Access architecture may be functioning similarly to Sparse Auto-encoders in other contexts. The authors’ insights on this matter would be valuable, even if preliminary. I believe addressing this point would further strengthen an already compelling paper.
