- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel sparse memory access scheme for neural networks with external memory.  
- Well-motivated problem: scaling memory-augmented neural networks is important.  
- Provides clear background and related work on attention, Memory Networks, and Neural Turing Machines.  
- SAM architecture and sparse read/write operations are well-described, including controller and efficient BPTT.  
- Uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexing as a key component for sparse access.  
- Experiments show comparable data efficiency to existing models on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot.  
- Demonstrates scalability to very large memories and tasks with hundreds of thousands of time steps.  
- Main technical contributions (efficient backpropagation and ANN-based sparse access) appear straightforward.  
- Lacks rigorous analysis of time and space complexity and provides limited theoretical guarantees.  
- Paper is well-written and easy to follow.  
- Originality is moderate: significant proposal but builds on existing memory-augmented ideas.  
- Significance is high due to potential impact on large-scale memory-augmented models.  
- Quality: technically sound but not sufficiently strong in theoretical contribution.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel memory scheme, strong empirical results, clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: contributions seem incremental/straightforward, missing rigorous complexity analysis, limited theoretical advance.
