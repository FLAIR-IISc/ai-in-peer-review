- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable sparse memory access scheme to scale memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs).
- Motivated by MANNs’ real-world scalability limitations; aims to retain representational power while improving training efficiency.
- Shows asymptotic lower bounds in complexity and reports large empirical speedups (≈1,000x faster, ≈3,000x less memory) versus dense models.
- Technical approach: threshold memory modifications to a sparse subset and use efficient data structures for content-based reads.
- Well-explained paper with strong grounding in prior work (NTM, Memory Networks) and solid theoretical understanding.
- Thorough experimental evaluation on synthetic algorithmic tasks and one-shot Omniglot, demonstrating practical benefits.
- Scales to tasks with hundreds of thousands of time steps and large memories, a notable achievement.
- Extends approach to Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and achieves state-of-the-art results on bAbI tasks.
- Detailed analysis of space and time complexity (Supplementary A) is a strength.
- Novelty, technical soundness, significance, clarity, and potential impact argued as strong reasons for acceptance.
- Weakness: limited comparison to other memory compression or alternative sparse access methods beyond the DAM variant.
- Weakness: ANN index hyperparameter choices (FLANN, LSH) are briefly justified and need deeper explanation.
- Weakness: ablations are good but systematic exploration of different sparsity levels (K values) is lacking.
- Weakness: discussion of ANN choice trade-offs (k-d trees vs LSH), transition point, and performance trade-offs could be expanded.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance.
