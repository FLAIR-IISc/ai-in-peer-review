The authors propose an efficient approach to computing attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) by leveraging fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations. They demonstrate through experiments that this method does not compromise training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. While the novelty of this work is somewhat limited, as similar ideas have been explored in memory networks (e.g., section 3.3 of http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), the application of these techniques to writable memories is a notable contribution. Although the conceptual leap in this paper may not be substantial, the practical aspects of implementing this approach are worth reporting.

I recommend accepting the paper, but I suggest several revisions. Firstly, I find that the descriptions of the tasks and experiments are insufficient for replication. Given that the original NTM paper's code has not been released and there is no standard version of the tasks, it is crucial that the authors provide detailed descriptions of the task construction. Furthermore, I request that the authors commit to releasing the code for their experiments to facilitate reproducibility.

Secondly, it is essential to discuss the limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed approach. The authors often describe their model as smooth, but the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is inherently non-smooth. With K=1, in the read-only setting, their model bears similarities to the MemNN-WSH in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf, which was reported to perform less well than the fully smooth model. It is unclear whether the suboptimal performance is due to the choice of K=1 or the task itself. I believe that an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of the tasks and setups that allow their training to work would significantly enhance the paper's value. Additionally, the authors should consider the implications of training a model that makes discrete actions without accounting for this aspect, as opposed to the approach in http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275.

Lastly, I suggest removing section 3.6, as it does not contribute substantially to the paper. Moreover, I recommend avoiding the terms "non-synthetic" or "real-world" to describe the Omniglot data, as they may be misleading.
