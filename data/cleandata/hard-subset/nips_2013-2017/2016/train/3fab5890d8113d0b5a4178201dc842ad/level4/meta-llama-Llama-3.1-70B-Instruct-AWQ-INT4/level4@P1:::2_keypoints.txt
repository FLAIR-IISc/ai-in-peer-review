- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for neural nets with external memory that retains representational power while improving efficiency.
- Claims SAM is asymptotically optimal in space/time and reports implementation speedups (≈1000×) and memory reductions (≈3000×) versus non-sparse models.
- Evaluated on synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot, and bAbI QA, showing comparable data efficiency and scaling to hundreds of thousands of time steps/memories.
- Demonstrates generality by adapting SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer.
- Main technical contributions are efficient backpropagation and approximate nearest-neighbor using sparse access.
- Reviewer finds those technical contributions relatively straightforward and not particularly novel.
- Experiments are interesting but considered insufficient to compensate for limited technical depth.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for expert reproduction.
- Theoretical analysis is limited, which weakens technical soundness despite empirical support.
- Originality is mixed: SAM is a novel proposal, but sparse access ideas are not entirely new and related work could be better situated.
- Results are significant for large-scale applications, but the paper lacks deeper analysis of implications and potential applications.
- Overall recommendation: not strong enough for acceptance due to simplicity of contributions and lack of technical depth.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, large efficiency gains, clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: contributions are straightforward, limited analysis, and insufficient contextualization in prior work.
