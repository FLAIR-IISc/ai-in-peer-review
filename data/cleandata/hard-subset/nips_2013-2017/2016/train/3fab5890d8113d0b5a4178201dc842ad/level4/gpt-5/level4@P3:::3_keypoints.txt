- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) uses content-based ANN top-K reads and sparse writes (update read locations + overwrite LRU) to scale MANNs, plus sparse rollback BPTT with O(1) memory per timestep.
- Relation to prior work: Maintains end-to-end trainability like NTMs/DNCs while achieving asymptotic scaling and extends to a sparse DNC (SDNC).
- Strength: Simple, clear mechanism to induce sparsity with practical ANN data structures (kd-tree / LSH).
- Strength: Strong asymptotic complexity claims—O(log N) time per step with ANN and O(1) per-timestep memory for BPTT.
- Strength: Large empirical gains—up to ~1000× faster and ~3000× less physical memory than dense baselines for large N.
- Strength: Competitive or improved learning on NTM tasks and sometimes faster early training due to sparsity.
- Strength: Demonstrates scalability to thousands of steps and million-scale memories, generalizing to much longer sequences than trained on.
- Strength: Broad applicability shown by SDNC and near-state-of-the-art bAbI results without supervised memory retrieval.
- Weakness: Non-differentiability from ANN top-K selection—needs stronger justification of training stability and potential gradient bias.
- Weakness: Some comparisons match physical memory rather than memory capacity; matched-capacity or performance-vs-compute curves are missing.
- Weakness: Limited ablation/sensitivity analysis on K, LRU threshold, erase policy, and choice of ANN (kd-tree vs LSH).
- Weakness: Practicality concerns for online ANN maintenance—rebuild frequency, overhead, and GPU integration are under-detailed.
- Weakness: “Asymptotically optimal” claim depends on assumptions about content addressing and constant K; constants and assumptions should be clarified.
- Weakness: Evaluation limited on large-scale real-world sequential tasks (e.g., long-context language modeling or document QA).
- Quality: Technically solid systems contribution with thoughtful complexity analysis and profiling, but some analyses (non-diff top-K, ANN overhead) need more rigor.
- Clarity: Well-written overall, but key algorithmic details (usage tracking, rollback mechanics) are in the supplement—add concise pseudocode to main text and clarify differentiability/assumptions.
- Originality: Novel and timely combination of sparse read/write, ANN-based addressing, and reversible sparse BPTT distinct from prior discrete or soft-attention approaches.
- Significance: High—addresses a core scalability bottleneck for MANNs and is likely to influence future architectures and applications.
- Suggestions: Add main-text pseudocode for rollback BPTT, expand ablations (K, write policies, ANN), discuss non-differentiable alternatives (soft top-K, STE), and report ANN maintenance overhead and device details.
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses important scalability problem, strong theoretical and systems contributions, extensive empirical speed/memory gains, and demonstrated generality.
- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient analysis of non-differentiable selection effects, some capacity-favoring comparisons, and limited ANN maintenance/ablation detail.
- Overall recommendation: Accept, pending suggested clarifications and additional ablations for robustness and reproducibility.
