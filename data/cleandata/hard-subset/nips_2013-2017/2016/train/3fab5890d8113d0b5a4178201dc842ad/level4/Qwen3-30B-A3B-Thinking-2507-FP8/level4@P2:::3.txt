

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that significantly improves scalability while retaining representational power. Building directly on foundational NIPS work like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs; Graves et al., NIPS 2014) and Memory Networks (Sukhbaatar et al., NIPS 2015), SAM addresses a critical bottleneck in MANNs: linear computational and memory overhead with increasing memory size. The authors constrain all reads and writes to a fixed-size sparse subset of memory words (e.g., K=4–8), leveraging efficient data structures like approximate nearest neighbors (ANNs) to achieve O(log N) time complexity and O(1) space per time step. This contrasts with dense MANNs like NTMs, which incur O(N) time/space costs per step due to full-memory operations. The paper demonstrates SAM’s theoretical optimality (Supplementary A), empirical speedups of 1,000× in runtime and 3,000× reduction in memory usage versus NTMs (Fig. 1b), and successful learning on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and real-world applications (Omniglot, Babi QA).

**Originality and Significance (Strengths):** SAM offers a clean, principled solution to a well-known scalability challenge in MANNs, distinct from prior approaches like reinforcement learning-based addressing (Zaremba & Sutskever, NIPS 2015), which relies on high-variance gradients. The work is highly significant: it enables training on tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps (e.g., curriculum learning to sequence lengths of 200,000) and real-world datasets (Omniglot), where dense MANNs fail due to memory constraints. Crucially, SAM achieves this *without sacrificing learning performance*—it matches or exceeds dense baselines on NTM tasks (Fig. 2) and sets a new state-of-the-art on Babi tasks (SDNC variant solves all but one task without supervised memory access). The insight that sparsity *improves* learning efficiency on certain tasks (e.g., priority sort) is particularly novel and counterintuitive.

**Quality (Strengths):** The technical contribution is rigorously validated. The paper provides: (1) Formal asymptotic analysis of SAM’s optimality (Supplementary A); (2) Comprehensive benchmarks across synthetic and real tasks (4.1–4.5), including ablation studies on ANN choices (k-d trees vs. LSH); (3) Reproducible implementation details (e.g., memory usage measurements, curriculum scaling); and (4) A generalizable extension to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) in Supplementary D. The experimental design effectively isolates the impact of sparsity, showing SAM’s speed/memory gains do not compromise data efficiency (4.2) or generalization (4.3, 4.5). The ablation of learning costs (4.2) is especially compelling, demonstrating SAM’s practical viability.

**Clarity (Strengths and Minor Weaknesses):** The paper is exceptionally clear. The architecture (Section 3) is logically structured, with precise definitions (e.g., sparse reads/writes, usage statistics), and the connection to prior work (Section 2) is well-motivated. Supplementary materials (e.g., Fig. 5, 6, A) greatly aid reproducibility. A minor suggestion: The discussion of ANN trade-offs (k-d trees vs. LSH) in Section 3.5 could briefly reference recent advancements (e.g., Mondrian forests; Lakshminarayanan et al., NIPS 2014) to contextualize future work. However, this is not critical to the core contribution.

**Weaknesses (Mitigated):** The most notable limitation is the focus on content-based addressing, which may not generalize to all MANN variants (e.g., those using location-based addressing). However, the authors convincingly argue SAM’s approach is broadly applicable (Section 5) and demonstrate SDNC as proof-of-concept. The paper does not explore sparsity in non-sequential tasks, but this is reasonable given the MANN focus on sequence learning. No major technical gaps are evident.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Original solution to a critical scalability bottleneck; rigorous theoretical and empirical validation; significant speed/memory gains without performance loss; extends to real-world tasks (Babi, Omniglot); clear, reproducible, and well-motivated.  
*Con:* Limited exploration of sparsity in non-content-based MANNs; ANN implementation details could be expanded (though Supplementary D covers SDNC adequately).

**Overall Assessment:** This work makes a substantial, well-executed contribution to MANN research. It directly addresses a key barrier to real-world MANN adoption (scalability) with a simple yet powerful technique, validated across diverse tasks. The results are compelling, the writing is clear, and the contribution is distinct from prior NIPS work. SAM’s efficiency gains (1,000× speedup, 3,000× memory reduction) enable applications previously infeasible with dense MANNs. It advances the state of the art demonstrably and is highly relevant to NIPS’ focus on scalable, practical machine learning. **Recommendation: Accept.**
