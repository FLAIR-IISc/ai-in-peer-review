

The paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that achieves asymptotic optimality in space and time complexity by constraining reads/writes to sparse subsets of memory. The authors demonstrate SAM’s efficiency through empirical results showing 1,000× speedup and 3,000× reduced memory usage compared to dense baselines (e.g., NTM), while maintaining comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) and Omniglot one-shot classification. SAM’s scalability enables training on tasks requiring 100,000+ time steps and memory slots, with a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) achieving state-of-the-art results on Babi tasks without supervised memory access.

Strengths:  
- **Significance**: Addresses a critical scalability bottleneck in MANNs (noted in prior NIPS work like NTM [7] and DNC [8]), enabling real-world applicability.  
- **Clarity**: Well-structured exposition of the sparse access mechanism, with clear experimental comparisons to dense variants (DAM) and prior models (NTM, LSTM).  
- **Quality**: Empirical results are thorough (synthetic tasks, curriculum learning, Babi, Omniglot) and demonstrate consistent gains in efficiency without performance degradation.  

Weaknesses:  
- **Quality**: The core technical contribution (sparse reads/writes with ANN indexing) lacks theoretical justification for the approximation error bounds. The paper claims asymptotic optimality (Supplementary A) but fails to rigorously prove that sparse approximations preserve representational power for all tasks (e.g., the 3000× memory reduction relies on empirical validation without formal guarantees).  
- **Originality**: While the approach is practical, the sparse addressing scheme (previously-read/least-recently-accessed) and ANN integration build incrementally on existing techniques (e.g., content-based addressing in NTM [7], LSH in [15]), with insufficient differentiation from related NIPS work (e.g., RL-based sparse addressing in [22]).  
- **Clarity**: The efficient backpropagation section (3.4) omits critical details on how sparse modifications are tracked during BPTT, making reproducibility challenging.  

Pro-acceptance arguments:  
- Demonstrates a viable path to scalable MANNs for real-world applications (e.g., processing long documents).  
- Empirical results are robust across multiple tasks and scales, with compelling speed/memory trade-offs.  

Con-acceptance arguments:  
- Theoretical gaps undermine claims of "asymptotic optimality"; the paper relies on empirical evidence without rigorous error analysis.  
- Limited novelty relative to recent NIPS contributions (e.g., DNC [8], RL-based scaling [22]), with the sparse mechanism being a straightforward adaptation rather than a novel paradigm.  

The paper’s technical contribution is valuable for practitioners but lacks the theoretical depth or conceptual novelty expected for NIPS acceptance. While the results are impressive, the absence of formal guarantees for the approximation error and insufficient differentiation from prior work weaken its scientific contribution.
