The paper proposes a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) for neural networks augmented with external memory. The authors demonstrate that SAM retains the representational power of traditional memory-augmented neural networks while achieving significant improvements in computational and memory efficiency. Specifically, they show that SAM is asymptotically optimal in space and time complexity, and their implementation runs 1000 times faster and with 3000 times less physical memory than non-sparse models.

The authors evaluate SAM on a range of tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results show that SAM achieves comparable data efficiency to existing models and can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by adapting it to the Differentiable Neural Computer.

The main technical contributions of the paper are the efficient backpropagation and approximation nearest neighbor using sparse access memory. However, I found these contributions to be relatively straightforward and not particularly novel. The experiments, while interesting, do not compensate for the lack of technical depth in the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach and its advantages. However, the technical soundness of the paper is somewhat compromised by the simplicity of the main contributions. The claims are well-supported by experimental results, but the theoretical analysis is limited.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to memory-augmented neural networks. However, the idea of using sparse access memory is not entirely new, and the authors could have done a better job of situating their work in the context of existing research.

In terms of significance, the results are important and demonstrate the potential of SAM for large-scale applications. However, the paper could have benefited from a more detailed analysis of the implications of the results and the potential applications of SAM.

Overall, while the paper is well-written and the results are interesting, I do not think it is technically strong enough for acceptance. The main contributions are relatively straightforward, and the paper could have benefited from more technical depth and analysis.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to memory-augmented neural networks.
* The results demonstrate significant improvements in computational and memory efficiency.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The main technical contributions are relatively straightforward and not particularly novel.
* The paper lacks technical depth and analysis.
* The authors could have done a better job of situating their work in the context of existing research.
