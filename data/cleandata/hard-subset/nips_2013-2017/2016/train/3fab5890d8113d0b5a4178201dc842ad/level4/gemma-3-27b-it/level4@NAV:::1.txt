The authors leverage fast approximate nearest neighbor search and arg-top-K operations to efficiently compute attention weights within a Neural Turing Machine framework. Their experiments demonstrate that this approach maintains training performance and enables scaling to larger problem instances. While the core idea isn’t entirely novel – similar techniques have been applied in memory networks for scalable lookups, as seen in, for example, the Memory Networks paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf) and subsequent work utilizing memories with millions of items – its application to *writable* memories appears less common. Even if the conceptual contribution is incremental, the practical implementation and demonstrated success warrant publication. I recommend acceptance, contingent on several revisions.

First, reproducibility is a significant concern. The current descriptions of both the model and the experimental tasks are insufficient for replication. Given the lack of publicly available code for the original NTM tasks and the absence of a standardized implementation, detailed task construction descriptions are crucial. The authors should also commit to releasing their experimental code.

Second, a critical analysis of the method’s limitations is needed. The authors frequently characterize their model as smooth, yet the underlying argmax (or K-argmax) operation is inherently discrete.  Their $K=1$ case closely resembles the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), which reportedly performed worse than fully smooth alternatives. Investigating whether this discrepancy stems from the specific choice of $K=1$, the nature of the tasks, or other factors would greatly enhance the paper’s value.  A discussion of failure cases, and a more thorough understanding of the conditions under which their training succeeds – particularly considering the discrete action selection and the lack of explicit consideration for this during training (in contrast to, e.g., http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275) – would significantly strengthen the contribution.  The observed results are promising, but defining the boundaries of applicability is essential. 

Finally, I recommend removing section 3.6. Additionally, the characterization of the Omniglot dataset as "non-synthetic" or "real-world" should be reconsidered.
