- Problem: existing MANNs scale O(N) per time step in time and memory, making large memories and long sequences impractical.
- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) architecture to enable scalable memory-augmented networks.
- SAM design — reads: constrain reads to K slots using an ANN index (e.g., kd-tree/LSH) to reduce search from O(N) to O(log N).
- SAM design — writes: sparsify writes by targeting previously-read locations or the least-recently-used (LRU) slot.
- Efficient BPTT: store only sparse O(K) memory modifications and roll back during backward pass to avoid O(NT) space.
- Evaluation: compared SAM to dense SAM (DAM) and original NTM on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning.
- Empirical gains: up to ~1,600x speedup and ~3,000x memory savings while maintaining comparable or better performance.
- Scaling demonstration: can train via curriculum on extremely long sequences (e.g., 200,000 steps) unattainable by dense models.
- Strength — significance: addresses a critical practical bottleneck for MANNs and enables much larger-scale applications.
- Strength — empirical validation: thorough, diverse tasks and clear benchmarks demonstrating the approach’s effectiveness.
- Strength — clarity: well-written paper with clear explanation of sparse operations and backpropagation strategy.
- Weakness — novelty: core ideas (sparsity, ANN indices) are established techniques; contribution is mainly engineering/system integration.
- Weakness — write heuristic: writing to read locations or LRU is a simple heuristic lacking theoretical justification or detailed ablation/comparison.
- Acceptance case: substantial, empirically-verified improvements and novel scaling demonstrations justify acceptance.
- Rejection case: limited conceptual novelty might argue the work is a systems-level optimization rather than a fundamental new model.
