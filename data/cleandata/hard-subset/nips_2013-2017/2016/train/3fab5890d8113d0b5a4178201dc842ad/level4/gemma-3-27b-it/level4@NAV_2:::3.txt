This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks. This research area, originating with Neural Turing Machines [7], addresses the limitations of traditional recurrent networks like LSTMs [11] by decoupling memory capacity from model parameters. The core contribution of this work is constraining both reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words, rather than operating on the entire memory, thereby improving scalability. The authors demonstrate the theoretical optimality of their approach and present empirical results in Section 4.1 indicating substantial performance gains due to reduced memory requirements. They then evaluate the learning costs associated with this sparsity through experiments on standard NTM tasks – copy, associative recall, and priority sort – demonstrating comparable or improved learning efficiency (Section 4.2). Importantly, the authors also showcase the applicability of SAM to real-world data with an Omniglot benchmark (Section 4.5). The work builds upon established memory network architectures [21, 17] and proposes an extension to the Differentiable Neural Computer [8] (Supplementary D). The idea is straightforward and novel, achieving significant speedups over the NTM, which is a compelling result. The observation that sparse reads and writes can *benefit* early-stage learning in certain tasks (Section 4.2) is particularly interesting. While the paper thoroughly investigates various aspects of SAM, further exploration of the conditions under which sparsity enhances learning, or a comparative analysis of different Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) approaches within the SAM framework, could strengthen the work. Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper with comprehensive supporting details in the supplementary materials. It is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** SAM presents a novel approach to addressing the scalability limitations of memory-augmented neural networks.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide theoretical justification for the efficiency of SAM and demonstrate its practical benefits through rigorous experimentation.
*   **Significance:** The reported speedups and memory savings are substantial and could enable the application of memory-augmented networks to more complex, real-world problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   While the paper demonstrates strong performance on a range of tasks, further analysis of the conditions under which sparse access is most beneficial would be valuable.
*   A more comprehensive comparison of different ANN implementations within SAM might be insightful.
