This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This is a relatively recent area of research, following the introduction of Neural Turing Machines, which enable neural networks to learn capabilities beyond the limits of traditional models like LSTMs.

The primary contribution of this work is a mechanism that constrains all read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory words, in contrast to standard models that can address the entire memory space. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this methodology and its optimality. As demonstrated in Section 4.1, this approach achieves significant improvements in read and write performance due to the reduced scope of each memory access.

The authors also conduct a thorough empirical evaluation. In Section 4.2, they analyze the learning trade-offs of introducing sparsity using standard NTM benchmark tasks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The results show that the combined computational and learning costs for SAM are lower than those for the NTM on the associative recall and priority sort tasks. A notable strength of the paper is the inclusion of a real-world application in Section 4.4, with experiments benchmarked using Torch7.

The core idea is both straightforward and novel, and the resulting speedup compared to the NTM is impressive. A particularly interesting finding, discussed in Section 4.2, is that constraining reads and writes with a sparse mechanism (implemented via a linear layer or an ANN) can benefit learning in the early stages of training for some tasks.

The paper would be strengthened if the authors could further elaborate on the conditions under which this learning benefit occurs. An analysis of how to generalize this finding—for instance, by identifying the characteristics of tasks where sparsity provides a helpful inductive bias—would be valuable for the community. Additionally, exploring the integration of SAM with different neural network architectures could further demonstrate its utility.

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper. The assumptions are clearly stated, the proposed architecture is detailed in the appendix, and the experimental results convincingly support the authors' claims.
