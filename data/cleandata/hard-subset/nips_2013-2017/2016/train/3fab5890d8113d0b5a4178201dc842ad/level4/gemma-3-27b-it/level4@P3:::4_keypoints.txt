- Addresses scalability bottleneck of memory-augmented neural networks in space and time as memory size grows.  
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): end-to-end differentiable memory access with sparse reads/writes and efficient data structures.  
- Core idea of restricting memory modifications to a sparse subset is well-motivated and preserves representational power.  
- Technically sound with claimed asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.  
- Builds on prior MANN work (LSTM, NTM, Memory Networks) and maintains end-to-end differentiability versus discrete-addressing RL methods.  
- Strong empirical results: large speedups (up to 1,000x) and memory reductions (up to 3,000x) over dense models.  
- Maintains comparable data efficiency and task performance (synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI, Omniglot).  
- Demonstrates scalability to tasks with hundreds of thousands of time steps.  
- Generality shown by extending SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and supplemented with helpful implementation details.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of ANN (approximate nearest neighbor) algorithm choices and their trade-offs/limitations.  
- Weakness: insufficient investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., usage discount factor).  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison to other recent scaling approaches beyond RL-based addressing.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution likely worthy of acceptance.  
- Reviewer confidence: 9/10.
