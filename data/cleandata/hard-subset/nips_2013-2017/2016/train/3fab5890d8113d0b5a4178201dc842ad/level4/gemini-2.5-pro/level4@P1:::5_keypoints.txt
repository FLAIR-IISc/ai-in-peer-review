- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale memory-augmented neural networks by restricting accesses to a sparse subset of memory locations.
- Addresses the core bottleneck that standard MANNs have read/write costs linear in memory size, limiting large-scale use.
- Uses content-based reads via efficient approximate nearest-neighbor search and writes to recently read or LRU locations to enable sparse access.
- Achieves sub-linear time complexity for memory access and constant space complexity per time step.
- Primary contribution is a practical, scalable method for MANNs that is implementable and efficient.
- Empirical results show up to three orders of magnitude speed and memory improvements over dense models.
- Sparse approximation does not harm learning and sometimes improves performance on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning.
- Demonstrates exceptional generalization via curriculum learning, scaling from training on 10k-step sequences to successful testing on 200k-step sequences.
- Suggests the sparse mechanism may act as implicit regularization, potentially explaining improved learning dynamics and OOD generalization.
- Recommends the paper add a discussion (even speculative) analyzing why sparsity improves learning and generalization.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical scalability problem, introduces a novel and effective architecture, and promises high impact.
- Argument against acceptance: lacks deeper analysis of the mechanisms behind the observed performance gains (minor and fixable).
