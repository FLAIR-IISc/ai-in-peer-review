- Uses fast approximate nearest-neighbor structures + top-K argmax for attention in an NTM, enabling training on larger problems without harming performance.
- Novelty is limited given prior memory-network and hashing work, but applying these tools to writable memories and the engineering is worthwhile.
- Overall recommendation: accept, conditional on requested revisions.

- Reproducibility: paper as written is hard to reproduce; task descriptions must be precise and self-contained.
- Provide full experimental details: data generation, sequence lengths, distributions, noise models, train/val/test splits, curricula, stopping criteria, optimization settings, and hyperparameters.
- Commit to releasing the code for experiments.

- Limits and failure modes: clarify where the approach fails and whether it always works, especially given the non-smooth argmax/K-argmax operations.
- Explain relationship to MemNN-WSH and whether underperformance is due to K=1, task specifics, or other factors.
- Analyze failure cases and describe task/training properties that enable successful learning despite discreteness.

- Ablations requested: vary K (including K=1 vs larger), read-only vs read–write, and compare approximate indexing vs exact attention for performance and stability.
- Discuss sensitivity to memory size, hashing collisions, and noise.

- Presentation fixes: remove Section 3.6 and avoid labeling Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”

- Summary: empirical results are encouraging and the work is practically important; acceptance is recommended contingent on stronger task specs, code release, and analysis of limitations.
