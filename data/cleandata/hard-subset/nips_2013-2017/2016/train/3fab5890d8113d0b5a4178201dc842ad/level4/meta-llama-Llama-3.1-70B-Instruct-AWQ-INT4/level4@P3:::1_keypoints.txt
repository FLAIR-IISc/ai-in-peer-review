- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a sparse read/write scheme to scale memory-augmented neural networks with large speed and memory gains.  
- SAM constrains reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory words for efficient computation and storage.  
- Introduces a sparse memory management scheme to track usage and reclaim unused blocks.  
- Paper is well-written with a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and components.  
- Experimental results show SAM matches dense-model performance while scaling to long sequences and large memory.  
- Lacks detailed descriptions of task construction and hyperparameter settings, hindering reproducibility.  
- Authors should commit to releasing code to facilitate replication and follow-up research.  
- Paper needs deeper analysis of failure cases and limitations, especially effects of non-smooth argmax/K-argmax operations.  
- More discussion required on which tasks/setups allow SAM training to succeed and where it fails.  
- Recommend removing section 3.6 as it appears unnecessary and adds little value.  
- Avoid labeling Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world,” since it’s a benchmark dataset.  
- Novel contribution to scaling MANNs, though sparse read/write ideas are not entirely new.  
- Results are significant for efficient, scalable models with potential applications in NLP and vision.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with requested revisions to experimental detail, code release, and deeper analysis.
