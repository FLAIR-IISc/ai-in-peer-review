This paper introduces an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme that has the representational power of an original Neural Turing Machine (NTM), but is much more efficient with training with very large memory. The authors present a novel neural memory architecture, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which addresses the limitations of existing memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing sparse reads and writes to external memory.

The paper is well-motivated, as the authors clearly explain the need for scaling memory systems for neural networks. They demonstrate that existing MANNs, such as NTMs and Memory Networks, have limited success in real-world applications due to their poor scalability. The authors propose SAM as a solution to this problem, which retains the representational power of the original approaches while allowing for efficient training with very large memories.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant. The authors introduce a sparse read operation that uses an approximate nearest neighbor data structure to calculate the read weights in O(logN) time. They also propose a sparse write operation that uses a simple scheme to write to previously read locations or the least recently accessed location. The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, and show that an implementation runs 1,000 times faster and with 3,000 times less physical memory than non-sparse models.

The paper presents several experiments to evaluate the performance of SAM. The authors compare SAM with its dense variant, DAM, and other established models, such as the NTM and LSTM, on several tasks, including algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. The results show that SAM is able to learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models and can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories.

The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by adapting SAM for models that maintain temporal associations between memories, such as the Differentiable Neural Computer. They show that the Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) is over 400 times faster than the canonical dense variant for a memory size of 2,000 slots and achieves the best reported result in the Babi tasks without supervising the memory access.
