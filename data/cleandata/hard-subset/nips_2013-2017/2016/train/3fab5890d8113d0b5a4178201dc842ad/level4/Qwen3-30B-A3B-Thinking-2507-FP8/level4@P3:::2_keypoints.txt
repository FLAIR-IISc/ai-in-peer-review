- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory architecture that limits read/write to a sparse subset and uses ANN indexing to achieve scalable MANNs.  
- Claims asymptotic optimality: O(log N) time per step and O(1) space per step while retaining representational power.  
- Maintains end-to-end differentiability enabling gradient-based training without performance loss.  
- Core technical novelty is the sparse access scheme plus efficient sparse BPTT via sparse modification tracking.  
- Theoretical grounding: SAM’s asymptotic complexity and BPTT optimization are well-motivated and derived (Supplementary A, Section 3.4).  
- Empirical rigor: extensive experiments on synthetic tasks, Babi QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning validate scalability and performance.  
- Demonstrates dramatic practical gains: training with 100k+ words at ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× lower memory than dense models.  
- SDNC variant achieves state-of-the-art on Babi (19/20 tasks) without supervised memory access.  
- Weakness: “asymptotic optimality” claims need nuance — ANN approximation errors and their impact are not quantified.  
- Weakness: ANN implementation choices (k-d trees vs LSH) and hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., rebuild frequency) lack comparative analysis and reproducibility details.  
- Overall originality/significance: novel, non-trivial extension of MANNs making long-range, large-memory learning practical and impactful.  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong technical and empirical contribution with minor addressable gaps in theory and implementation details.
