## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission

This paper investigates the use of fast approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search and arg-top-K operations to scale Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) to larger problems. The authors demonstrate that employing these techniques for attention weight computation does not degrade training performance and, in fact, enables training on more complex tasks. While the core novelty of leveraging ANN structures for memory access is incremental – as evidenced by related work in memory networks (e.g., Weston et al., 2014) which have successfully used hashing for scalable lookups in memories containing millions of items – its application to *writable* memories is a valuable contribution. The practical challenges of implementing this approach within an NTM framework are noteworthy and warrant reporting, leading me to recommend acceptance with revisions.

However, several aspects of the paper require improvement to enhance reproducibility and deepen the understanding of the proposed method.

**Reproducibility and Clarity (Major Concern):** The paper currently lacks the detail necessary for straightforward replication. The task descriptions are particularly insufficient. Given the lack of publicly available code from the original NTM paper, and the absence of a standardized implementation of these tasks, a thorough description of task construction is crucial. I strongly urge the authors to commit to releasing their experimental code alongside the final version of the paper. This will significantly benefit the research community and facilitate further investigation.

**Analysis of Limitations & Failure Modes:** The authors frequently characterize their model as “smooth,” yet acknowledge the discrete nature of the argmax (or K-argmax) operation. This raises important questions about potential limitations.  With K=1, the model closely resembles the MemNN-WSH (Weston et al., 2015), a model reported to perform less effectively than fully differentiable alternatives. Is the performance difference attributed to K=1, the specific tasks employed, or another factor? A more thorough analysis of failure cases is needed. Specifically, I would like to see a discussion of the types of tasks or setups where this method consistently succeeds, and where it struggles. The model's reliance on a discrete action selection process, trained without explicit consideration of differentiability (as addressed in Graves et al., 2015), is a critical aspect. Understanding the boundaries of its applicability would substantially increase the paper's value.  Successfully achieving results *despite* this challenge is a contribution, but defining the limits of that success is even more impactful.

**Specific Suggestions:** 

* **Remove Section 3.6:** This section does not substantially contribute to the core argument of the paper and can be removed to streamline the presentation.
* **Re-evaluate Terminology:**  The authors should refrain from describing the Omniglot dataset as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.” Omniglot is a widely-used dataset, but its characteristics are distinct from naturally occurring, uncurated data.
* **Clarify ANN Implementation:** Providing more details on the specific ANN library or implementation used (e.g., LSH, HNSW) would be helpful. Details on parameter tuning for the ANN search (e.g., number of hash tables, connection parameters) could also improve reproducibility.
* **Further Elaboration on K > 1:** The effect of increasing K on performance and the smoothness of the attention mechanism should be discussed in more detail.



**References:**

* Graves, A., et al. (2015). *Neural Turing Machines*. arXiv:1510.02195.
* Weston, J., Chopra, S., & Bordes, A. (2014). *Memory Networks*. arXiv:1410.3916.
* Weston, J., Chopra, S., & Bordes, A. (2015). *Key-Value Memory Networks*. arXiv:1503.08895.



In conclusion, this paper presents a pragmatic and potentially impactful approach to scaling NTMs. Addressing the concerns regarding reproducibility, limitations, and clarity will significantly strengthen the manuscript and enhance its contribution to the field. I believe that with these revisions, this work is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.
