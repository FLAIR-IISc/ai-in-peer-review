Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) architecture that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes to an external memory. SAM uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices to perform content-based reads in O(log N) time (with K nonzeros), writes only to a constant number of locations (recently read and least-recently-used), and introduces an efficient backpropagation-through-time scheme that stores and reverts sparse in-place memory updates rather than full memory snapshots. Under these design choices, SAM achieves O(log N) time per step and O(1) additional space per time step (after O(N) initialization), with matching asymptotic lower bounds argued in the supplement. The approach is presented as a drop-in alternative to dense-access MANNs such as the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) and Memory Networks, and is also adapted to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), yielding a sparse DNC (SDNC).

Empirically, SAM achieves large wall-clock and memory savings (up to ~1000× faster and ~3000× less memory than dense variants) while maintaining or improving data efficiency on NTM-style algorithmic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), scaling to very long sequences via curriculum (BPTT over >4,000 steps; training up to 10k steps and generalizing to 200k), competitive performance on bAbI (with SDNC achieving the best reported unsupervised-access results), and strong one-shot classification on Omniglot, where large memory capacity appears to help. This builds on prior work that decoupled parameters from memory capacity (NTM, Memory Networks) and complements reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing (e.g., RL-NTM), while addressing the practical bottlenecks that have limited MANNs’ applicability.

Strengths:
- Significance: The work directly tackles the central limitation of MANNs—computational and memory scaling—and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude gains without sacrificing differentiability, enabling training with memories in the millions of slots and very long untruncated BPTT. This substantially broadens the practical scope of MANNs.
- Originality: The combination of sparse reads/writes, online ANN within the training loop, least-recently-used allocation, and an in-place reversible memory-update scheme for efficient BPTT is novel and broadly applicable (including to DNC).
- Quality: The technical contributions are well-motivated, and the complexity analysis and system design are coherent. The empirical section spans synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot, and includes both accuracy and systems benchmarks. The generalization to sequences far beyond the training regime is compelling.
- Clarity: The architectural description is generally clear, and the paper is careful to delineate SAM from its dense comparator (DAM) and from prior MANNs. The supplement appears to contain useful implementation details.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Analysis of why sparsity can improve learning: The paper reports that sparse access can surpass dense baselines on some tasks and markedly improves long-range generalization, but offers little diagnosis. A discussion or ablation (e.g., effect of K, read/write allocation policy, sparsity thresholds) would strengthen claims and guide practitioners.
- Trade-offs with approximate search: The use of ANNs (k-d trees, LSH) introduces approximate neighbors and index maintenance. The paper could quantify recall of the ANN queries, sensitivity to rebuild frequency, and accuracy–speed trade-offs (exact vs. approximate reads) across tasks.
- Fairness and ablations: Some comparisons (e.g., Omniglot) pit dense models with 64 slots against SAM with 2e6 slots. While the memory budget parity is argued via physical RAM usage, reporting results for dense models at their maximal feasible memory under the same compute budget, and ablations varying SAM’s memory size and K, would clarify where gains arise (capacity vs. sparsity vs. ANN).
- Optimality claims: The asymptotic optimality in time and space is compelling; a concise intuition in the main text (with assumptions spelled out) would help readers who may not consult the supplement.
- Practical training details: The long-sequence experiments (e.g., BPTT over thousands of steps) would benefit from more specifics in the main text (optimizer, gradient clipping, initialization, truncation if any) to aid reproducibility and to assess stability.
- Clarity/notation: Section 3 could be streamlined by introducing the data structures and rollback mechanism earlier, unifying the notation for sparse vs. dense weights (w vs. w̃), and clarifying the erase behavior for least-recently-used slots. Minor typos (e.g., equation formatting around Mt−1) should be corrected.

Quality:
- Technically sound with clear design choices and plausible complexity analysis; empirical claims are supported across diverse tasks. Additional ablations on K, memory size, and ANN recall would further solidify conclusions.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written. Suggestions: move a brief summary of Supplementary A (complexity and rollback) and the ANN maintenance choices into the main text; provide a small schematic contrasting dense vs. sparse backprop state caching.

Originality:
- High. The integration of sparse differentiable access with in-graph ANN, LRU allocation, and reversible updates is a meaningful contribution beyond existing MANNs and discrete-addressing approaches.

Significance:
- High. The work removes a major barrier to scaling MANNs and demonstrates strong performance on algorithmic, reasoning, and one-shot tasks. The SDNC result on bAbI further underscores generality.

Requests for authors (actionable):
- Provide ablations: effect of K (e.g., K=1,4,8,16), memory size, and exact vs. ANN reads on accuracy, speed, and memory.
- Report ANN query recall and sensitivity to rebuild cadence; discuss worst-case behaviors and online imbalance.
- Clarify training details for long BPTT runs (optimizer, gradient clipping, regularization).
- Add discussion on why sparsity may aid generalization and data efficiency (e.g., reduced interference, inductive bias akin to sparse coding/attention).
- Where space permits, summarize the optimality assumptions in the main text. Consider releasing code or pseudo-code for the rollback mechanism and memory manager.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a core scalability bottleneck with a principled, general solution.
- Strong empirical evidence with large practical gains and competitive accuracy.
- Applicable to multiple MANN architectures (NTM, DNC).
- Enables new regimes (millions of memory slots, >10k-step sequences).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited ablation/diagnostic analysis; unclear which components are most responsible for gains.
- Some comparisons may be confounded by differing memory capacities.
- ANN trade-offs and stability under online updates are not deeply quantified.

Overall recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper presents a technically sound, original, and practically significant advance that materially improves the scalability of differentiable memory systems, with convincing experiments and clear potential impact. With minor additions to analysis and clarity, this would be a standout NIPS contribution.
