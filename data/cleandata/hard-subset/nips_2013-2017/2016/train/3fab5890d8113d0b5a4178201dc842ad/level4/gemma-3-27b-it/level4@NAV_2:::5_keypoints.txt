- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel scalable memory-augmented neural network architecture.  
- Reports runtime and memory efficiency improvements up to three orders of magnitude vs. prior approaches.  
- Demonstrates strong performance gains in curriculum learning and remarkable extrapolation to sequences far longer than training (10k → 200k).  
- Claims potential to make memory-augmented networks practical for real-world applications.  
- Key open question: causes of improved generalization with curriculum learning are not fully explained.  
- Technical soundness: provides clear theoretical justification for asymptotic space/time complexity (Supplementary A).  
- Empirical results are consistent and strong across synthetic NTM tasks, Babi, and one-shot Omniglot.  
- Introduces Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), showing generality of the sparse-access idea on Babi tasks.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with helpful diagrams and thorough supplementary implementation details.  
- Authors evaluate SAM against strong baselines and prior state-of-the-art models.  
- Originality: combines sparse read/write operations with efficient data structures, applying sparsity to memory-augmented networks novelly.  
- Significance: high — enables training with very large memories and long-sequence generalization, relevant to language modeling, translation, and long-term reasoning.  
- Practical reproducibility concern: code not yet released (promised), which would aid replication.  
- Arguments for acceptance: large efficiency gains, strong empirical evidence, novel scalable approach, broad potential impact, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of analysis into why curriculum/generalization gains occur and temporary unavailability of code.
