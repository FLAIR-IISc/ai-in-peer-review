The authors leverage fast approximate nearest neighbor structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights within a Neural Turing Machine. They demonstrate through experiments that this approach does not degrade training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. While the novelty of this work is incremental – as similar techniques are employed in memory networks (e.g., section 3.3 of http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), and several recent memory network papers now utilize memories with millions of items and hashing for scalable lookups – I haven’t encountered these tools applied to *writable* memories. Even if this paper doesn’t represent a significant conceptual advance, the practical challenges of implementation are certainly worthy of reporting. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, contingent on several revisions.

First, replicating the results presented would be difficult given the current level of detail in the paper. The task descriptions, in particular, are lacking. Considering the original NTM code remains unavailable and no standard version of these tasks exists, a careful and comprehensive description of their construction is crucial. I also request that the authors commit to releasing their experimental code.

Second, it’s important to understand the limitations of this approach. Under what conditions does it fail, and does it consistently perform well? The authors frequently describe their model as being smooth, yet acknowledge the non-smooth nature of the argmax (or K-argmax) operation. With $K=1$, in a read-only setting, their model closely resembles the MemNN-WSH model described in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf (although that model doesn't employ hashing). That model was reported to perform worse than its fully smooth counterpart. Is this difference attributable to $K=1$? Or to the specific tasks used? This paper would be significantly strengthened by an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of the types of tasks or configurations that facilitate successful training. Their model makes a discrete action selection, yet they train it without explicitly accounting for this (unlike the work in http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). The fact that they achieve results with this approach is a valuable contribution, but defining its limitations is equally important.

Finally, I suggest removing section 3.6. Additionally, I advise against characterizing the Omniglot dataset as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
