- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrains reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words to improve scalability.  
- Provides theoretical justification for SAM’s optimality and efficiency.  
- Empirical results (Section 4.1) show substantial performance gains and reduced memory requirements.  
- Experiments on NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) demonstrate comparable or improved learning efficiency (Section 4.2).  
- Sparse reads/writes can benefit early-stage learning in certain tasks.  
- Demonstrates real-world applicability via an Omniglot benchmark (Section 4.5).  
- Builds on established memory architectures and proposes an extension to the Differentiable Neural Computer (Supplementary D).  
- Achieves significant speedups over the Neural Turing Machine; method is straightforward and novel.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and supported by comprehensive supplementary material.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, technical soundness (theory + experiments), significant speed/memory improvements, and clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: needs deeper analysis of when sparsity is most beneficial and a more comprehensive comparison of ANN implementations within SAM.  
- Suggestion: further exploration of conditions under which sparsity enhances learning and comparative ANN studies would strengthen the contribution.
