This paper presents a new memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), within a memory-augmented neural network architecture. This line of work builds on the recent emergence of Neural Turing Machines, extending neural networks beyond the limitations of traditional LSTMs. The key contribution is to constrain all reads and writes to a fixed-size sparse subset of memory words rather than operating over the entire memory. The authors argue for the method’s optimality and provide supporting analysis. As shown in Section 4.1, this design yields strong read/write efficiency due to reduced computational and memory overhead. The paper also examines the learning implications of inducing sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks: 1) Copy, 2) Associative Recall, and 3) Priority Sort, finding that the overall cost is lower than NTM on some tasks (notably 2 and 3). I also appreciate that the authors include a real-world example in Section 4.4. The implementation uses Torch7. The idea is straightforward yet novel, and the speedups over NTM are striking. It is particularly interesting that sparse reads and writes using either a linear search or an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index can sometimes improve early-stage learning (Section 4.2). If the authors could further elaborate on when and why such improvements occur, and assess how different ANN choices interact with SAM, it would be very helpful to readers. Overall, the paper is clearly written, with assumptions stated explicitly and architectural details placed in the appendix. It is engaging and informative.
