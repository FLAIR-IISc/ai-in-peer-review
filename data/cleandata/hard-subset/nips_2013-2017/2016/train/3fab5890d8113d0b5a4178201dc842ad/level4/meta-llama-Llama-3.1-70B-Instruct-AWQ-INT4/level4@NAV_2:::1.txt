This paper proposes an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme, called Sparse Access Memory (SAM), that scales memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) efficiently. The authors address a significant limitation of MANNs, which is their poor scalability in both space and time as the amount of memory grows. By using sparse reads and writes, SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, and demonstrates significant speedups during training.

The main idea of SAM is to constrain all writes to and reads from external memory to a sparse subset of the memory words. This allows for computational and memory-efficient operation, while retaining end-to-end gradient-based optimization. The authors evaluate SAM on a range of synthetic tasks, including algorithmic tasks, and one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and demonstrate that it can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed method. The experimental results are also convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of SAM in scaling MANNs efficiently.

However, I do have some concerns regarding the novelty of the work. As the authors mention, the use of approximate nearest-neighbor structures and arg-top-K operations for computing attention weights is not new. In fact, similar techniques have been used in memory network papers that use memories with millions of items and hashing methods to make the lookups scalable. Nevertheless, the practical aspects of making it work for writeable memories are worth reporting.

I would like to see some changes to the paper. Firstly, I think it would be quite difficult to replicate the results of this paper from the descriptions in the paper. Especially the descriptions of the tasks are lacking; considering the code for the tasks from the original NTM paper have not been released, and there is no "standard" version of the tasks, it is crucial that the paper give careful descriptions of the construction of the tasks. I would also ask that the authors commit to releasing the code for their experiments.

Secondly, I would like to see some analysis of failure cases and some discussion of what kinds of tasks/setups allow their training to work. The authors often discuss their model as being smooth, but note that the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth. With $K=1$, in the read-only setting, their models are much like the MemNN-WSH in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf (although that model does not use hashing), and that model was reported to work less well than the fully smooth model. Is it because of the $K=1$? Because of the task? I think this paper would be more valuable if there was some analysis of failure cases and some discussion of what kinds of tasks/setups allow their training to work.

Finally, I would suggest that the authors remove section 3.6, and I would also suggest the authors not call the Omniglot data "non-synthetic" or "real-world".

Strengths:
- The paper proposes a novel method for scaling MANNs efficiently.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of SAM.
- The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed method.

Weaknesses:
- The novelty of the work is thin, as similar techniques have been used in memory network papers.
- The descriptions of the tasks are lacking, making it difficult to replicate the results.
- The authors do not provide an analysis of failure cases and what kinds of tasks/setups allow their training to work.

Arguments For Acceptance:

- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of SAM.
- The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed method.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

Recommendation:
I recommend accepting the paper, but with revisions that address the concerns mentioned above.
