- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse reads via top-K ANN and sparse writes (previously read + LRU) enabling O(log N) queries and constant-time updates.
- Introduces sparse in-place rollback for BPTT, giving O(1) space per step and O(N) initialization for training with large external memories.
- Claims asymptotic optimality under content-based access and shows extension to Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).
- Empirically trains much faster and with far lower memory than dense NTMs, matching or improving data efficiency on standard tasks.
- Scales via curriculum to sequences with thousands of steps and millions of memory slots, with strong bAbI and improved one-shot Omniglot performance.
- Core idea and sparse write mechanism are well-motivated; engineering to enable end-to-end training at scale is the main contribution.
- Experimental suite is broad and demonstrates large speed/memory savings without apparent loss in data efficiency.
- Reproducibility is a concern: many crucial implementation details (task generators, curricula, ANN settings, rebuild frequencies, memory structures) are terse or in the supplement.
- The “end-to-end differentiable” claim is misleading: top-K selection is discrete and gradients flow only through chosen entries, an approximation rather than strict differentiability.
- Paper clarity is generally good but needs expanded descriptions of task construction, training curricula, ANN configurations, and memory management to enable replication.
- Originality: builds on prior sparse retrieval and hashing work, but novelty lies in making sparse writes trainable and providing an efficient general BPTT strategy for large memories.
- Significance: methods could enable MANNs for much larger problems; reported 100–1600x speedups and much lower memory footprint are compelling.
- Suggest releasing code and detailed experimental settings (generators, preprocessing, hyperparameters, ANN index parameters, rebuild cadence) to improve reproducibility.
- Suggest ablations and failure-mode analysis: vary K, compare exact vs ANN search, test ANN recall effects, and assess sensitivity to usage threshold and allocation (LRU vs discounted).
- Ask for clarification on optimization implications of non-differentiable top-K, boundary-case effects, and contrast to discrete-attention training methods.
- Recommend tempering language around “end-to-end differentiable” and calling Omniglot “real-world”; emphasize explicit comparisons to prior MemNN/hash-based large-memory work.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong practical system advance, clear path to scaling writeable memories, broad applicability including DNC, and convincing experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: novelty is largely engineering, insufficient experimental detail and code, and limited analysis of sensitivity to K and ANN approximations.
- Recommendation: Accept conditional on clarifying experimental details and, ideally, releasing code to aid replication.
