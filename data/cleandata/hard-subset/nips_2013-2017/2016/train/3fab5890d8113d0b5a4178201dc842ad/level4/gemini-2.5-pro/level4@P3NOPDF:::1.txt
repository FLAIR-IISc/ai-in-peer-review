This paper proposes a method to scale Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) to larger memory sizes by replacing the standard attention mechanism with a more efficient, approximate alternative. The core technique involves using fast approximate-nearest-neighbor (ANN) structures to first identify a small subset of candidate memory locations, followed by an arg-top-K operation to select the final locations for read and write operations. The authors present experiments demonstrating that this approach allows NTMs to be trained on larger problems without a degradation in performance.

While the use of hashing and ANN for scalable memory lookups is not entirely new (see, for instance, Section 3.3 of Sukhbaatar et al., 2014, and subsequent work on memory networks with millions of items), this paper makes a valuable contribution by successfully applying these techniques to the more complex case of a *writeable* memory. Even if the conceptual leap is modest, the practical aspects of implementing this for a dynamic memory structure like an NTM are non-trivial, and the empirical results are worth reporting to the community. For these reasons, I recommend accepting the paper, provided the authors address the following significant concerns.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Reproducibility:** A primary weakness of the paper in its current form is the difficulty of reproducing the presented results. The descriptions of the experimental tasks are particularly lacking. Given that the code for the original NTM paper was never released and there are no standard benchmarks for these tasks, it is crucial for the authors to provide meticulous descriptions of their task construction, data generation, and evaluation protocols. To address this critical issue, I strongly urge the authors to commit to releasing the source code for their experiments and task environments upon publication.

2.  **Analysis of Limitations and Failure Cases:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the limitations and failure modes of the proposed method. The current presentation suggests the method is a universally applicable improvement, but this is unlikely to be the case.
    *   The authors frequently describe their model as "smooth," which is misleading since the arg-top-K selection is a discrete, non-differentiable operation. This distinction is crucial and should be addressed directly.
    *   When might this approach fail? For instance, with K=1 in a read-only setting, the proposed model is conceptually similar to the MemNN-WSH model (Weston et al., 2015), which was reported to underperform its fully differentiable counterpart. The authors should discuss why their approach succeeds here. Is it due to the choice of K>1, the specific nature of the NTM tasks, or some other factor?
    *   The model is trained with a discrete action selection without explicitly accounting for the non-differentiability through, for example, reinforcement learning techniques (e.g., Grefenstette et al., 2015). The fact that this direct approach works is an interesting and valuable finding, but it warrants a deeper investigation into the conditions under which it is effective. The paper would be more impactful if it included an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of what kinds of tasks, memory access patterns, or hyperparameter settings allow this training procedure to succeed.

**Minor Comments:**

*   I suggest that the authors remove Section 3.6, as its contribution to the main thesis of the paper seems marginal.
*   The Omniglot dataset, while a standard benchmark, is not typically considered a "real-world" or "non-synthetic" dataset in the same vein as natural images. I would suggest using more precise terminology to describe it to avoid overstating its complexity.
