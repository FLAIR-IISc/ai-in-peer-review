This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This line of work is relatively recent, following the introduction of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). Such augmentation enables neural networks to go beyond the capabilities of standard LSTMs.

The key contribution is to constrain all reads from and writes to the external memory to a fixed-size subset of memory locations, rather than addressing the entire memory. The authors provide theoretical justification for the method and offer optimality results. Consequently, one would expect improved read and write performance due to the reduced active memory footprint, which is borne out by the results in Section 4.1.

The authors also evaluate the learning costs introduced by sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks: (1) Copy, (2) Associative Recall, and (3) Priority Sort. The combined costs are lower than those of the NTM for some tasks (namely 2 and 3). I particularly appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4. The benchmarks are implemented in Torch7. The idea is straightforward yet novel, and the speedups over the NTM are significant and impressive.

It is especially interesting that sparse reads and writes, whether driven by a linear controller or an ANN, can in some cases benefit early-stage learning (Section 4.2). It would be helpful if the authors elaborated on when and why sparsity aids learning, and discussed how SAM might generalize to other settings or controllers (e.g., which ANN approaches pair best with SAM and under what conditions learning can be improved).

Overall, the paper is well written; the assumptions are clearly stated, and the architectural details are provided in the appendix. It is an interesting and timely contribution.
