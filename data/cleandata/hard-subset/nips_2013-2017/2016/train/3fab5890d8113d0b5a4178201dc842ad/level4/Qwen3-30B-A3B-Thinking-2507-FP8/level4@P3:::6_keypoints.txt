- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that constrains reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory words for scalability.  
- Achieves asymptotically optimal time/space complexity (O(log N) per read, O(1) space per time step) using efficient data structures for content-based addressing.  
- Demonstrates large practical gains: up to 1,000× faster and 3,000× less physical memory than non-sparse models.  
- Scales to very large problems (hundreds of thousands to millions of memory slots and sequence lengths >40,000).  
- Maintains near-identical data efficiency and learning performance compared to dense MANNs across benchmarks.  
- Extensive theoretical analysis of complexity provided (Supplementary A).  
- Strong experimental validation on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on Babi without supervising memory access and outperforms others on Omniglot at large memory capacities.  
- Demonstrates generality by developing a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).  
- Clear, well-structured exposition, with effective background, technical details, and transparent experimental setup/hyperparameters.  
- Novel approach: uses approximate nearest neighbor search within a differentiable framework, distinct from prior RL-based sparse addressing.  
- Significant practical impact by enabling large-memory MANN applications previously infeasible.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of potential failure modes or domains where the sparse approximation might degrade performance.  
- Weakness: could compare more thoroughly with alternative scaling approaches (e.g., reinforcement-learning discrete addressing).  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, significant, well-executed contribution aligning with NIPS standards.
