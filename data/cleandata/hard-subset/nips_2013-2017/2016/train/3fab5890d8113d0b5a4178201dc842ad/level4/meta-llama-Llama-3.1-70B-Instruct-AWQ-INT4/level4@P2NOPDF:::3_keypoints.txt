- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks.
- Main contribution: constrains all reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words rather than unlimited-size operations.
- Provides rigorous proofs of the methodology and claims of optimality (section 4.1).
- Demonstrates improved read/write performance due to reduced memory size (section 4.1).
- Evaluates learning costs of sparsity on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) (section 4.2).
- Finds combined learning costs lower than NTM on some tasks (associative recall and priority sort).
- Shows that sparse reads/writes, via linear/ANN controllers, can aid early-stage learning in some cases (section 4.2).
- Includes real-world examples and a Torch7 benchmark implementation (section 4.4).
- Achieves significant speedup compared to Neural Turing Machines; idea is simple yet novel.
- Suggests expanding discussion on generalizing SAM and conditions where learning improves.
- Recommends investigating different ANN/controller approaches combined with SAM.
- Minor requests: more experimental setup and hyperparameter details, and discussion of limitations and future directions.
- Overall assessment: well-written, clear assumptions/architecture (appendix), and a compelling contribution to the field.
