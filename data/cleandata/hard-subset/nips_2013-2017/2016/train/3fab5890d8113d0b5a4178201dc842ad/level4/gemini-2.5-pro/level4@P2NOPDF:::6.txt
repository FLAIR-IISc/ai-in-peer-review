This paper presents a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) with sparse read and write access, designed to overcome a significant limitation that has hindered the practical application of such models. Specifically, standard NTMs often require prohibitively large physical memories and computational resources for backpropagation. The proposed architecture aims to resolve this issue while achieving performance comparable to current state-of-the-art methods.

The paper is well-structured and the core ideas are communicated clearly. The work addresses a highly relevant problem, and the results are compelling. The following points are offered as suggestions for further improvement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making the proposed architecture and its motivation accessible to the reader.
*   **Problem Significance:** The research directly confronts a major bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks. By reducing the memory and computational footprint required for training, this work represents an important step toward making NTMs more viable for real-world applications.
*   **Performance:** The experimental results are impressive. It is particularly interesting to see the algorithm performing so well while operating close to theoretical bounds, which highlights the efficiency of the proposed approach.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Improving Self-Containment:** To enhance the paper's accessibility for a broader audience, the background section could be strengthened with a brief introduction to memory-augmented neural networks. For readers not already expert in this subfield, a clearer explanation of the NTM's memory mechanism would be beneficial. For instance, explicitly describing the intended function of the write operator and how it differs from memory access in conventional computer systems would help ground the contributions of the paper for non-specialists.
*   **Experimental Comparison in Figure 4:** The comparison presented in Figure 4, while well-justified, may not present a complete picture. The current approach compares models by allocating them the same amount of physical memory. This is a valid and practical method for evaluating performance under a fixed resource constraint. However, to more directly assess the algorithmic benefits of the sparse access mechanism, an additional comparison could be valuable. It would be highly informative to see how the models compare when configured with the same number of memory *locations*, even if this results in different physical memory requirements. Such an analysis would help disentangle the performance gains stemming from the novel architecture itself versus those arising from differences in memory size.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method is elegant and the results are promising. Minor revisions to improve the paper's accessibility and supplement the experimental analysis would further strengthen the submission.
