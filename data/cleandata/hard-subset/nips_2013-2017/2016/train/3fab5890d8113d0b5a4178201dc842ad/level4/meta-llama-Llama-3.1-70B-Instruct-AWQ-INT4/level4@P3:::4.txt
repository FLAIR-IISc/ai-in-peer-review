This paper introduces an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which retains the representational power of the original Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while training efficiently with very large memories. The authors tackle the problem of scaling memory systems for neural networks, a pressing research direction with significant potential impact. The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the limitations of existing memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) and the need for a more efficient approach.

The proposed SAM model introduces two key innovations: sparse reads and writes to external memory, and a sparse memory management scheme. The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, making it optimal for large memory sizes. The experimental results show that SAM outperforms existing models, including the NTM and Memory Networks, in terms of speed and memory efficiency. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components. The use of approximate nearest neighbor indexes, such as randomized k-d trees and locality sensitive hashing, is a notable contribution. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of SAM, which is essential for understanding the scalability of the model.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance technical depth with clarity and concision. The paper also includes a detailed analysis of the experimental results, which helps to demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM.
