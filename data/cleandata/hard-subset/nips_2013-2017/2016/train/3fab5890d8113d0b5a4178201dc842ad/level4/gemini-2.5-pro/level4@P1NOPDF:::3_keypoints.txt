- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks.  
- SAM constrains reads/writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words rather than the full memory.  
- Provides theoretical justification and optimality for the sparse access mechanism.  
- Demonstrates significant read/write performance improvements due to reduced access scope (Sec. 4.1).  
- Thorough empirical evaluation on NTM benchmarks (copy, associative recall, priority sort).  
- Shows lower combined computational and learning costs for SAM vs. NTM on associative recall and priority sort (Sec. 4.2).  
- Includes a real-world application and Torch7 benchmark experiments (Sec. 4.4).  
- Key empirical finding: sparsity in reads/writes can improve early-stage learning for some tasks.  
- Suggests need for analysis of when sparsity helps (task characteristics) and exploration with different architectures.  
- Overall: well-written, clear assumptions, detailed appendix, and experimental results that support the claims.
