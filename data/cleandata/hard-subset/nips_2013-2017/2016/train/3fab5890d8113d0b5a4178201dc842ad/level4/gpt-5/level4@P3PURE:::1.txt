This paper proposes to use fast approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) structures and top-K argmax (arg-top-K) operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM). The experiments suggest that these approximations do not degrade training performance and, in fact, enable training on larger problems.

Novelty and relation to prior work: The novelty is thin. See, for example, Section 3.3 of the Memory Networks paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf); several subsequent memory-network papers already use memories with millions of items and hashing/ANN methods to make lookups scalable. That said, I have not seen these tools applied to writeable memories. Even if the conceptual step is modest, the practical engineering required to make this work is valuable and worth reporting.

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on revisions.

Major revision requests:
1) Reproducibility and task specification. It would be difficult to replicate the results as currently described. The task descriptions are particularly under-specified. Since the original NTM task code has not been released and there is no standard version of these tasks, the paper must provide precise, complete descriptions of how each task is constructed. I also ask that the authors commit to releasing the code for their experiments.

2) Limits and failure modes. Where will this fail? Does it always work? The paper frequently describes the model as “smooth,” yet the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth. With K=1 in the read-only setting, the model is very similar to MemNN-WSH (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), although that model does not use hashing; that variant was reported to work less well than fully smooth alternatives. Is the issue K=1? The task choice? The paper would be more valuable with an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of which tasks/setups allow training to succeed. The model makes discrete actions but is trained without accounting for this discreteness (in contrast to http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Demonstrating that this can work is a useful contribution, but clarifying the limits would strengthen the paper.

Additional suggestions:
- Remove Section 3.6.
- Avoid calling the Omniglot data “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
