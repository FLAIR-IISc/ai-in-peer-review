- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a sparse read/write scheme for MANNs that constrains operations to a subset of memory locations.  
- Claims theoretical optimality in space and time complexity for SAM.  
- Reports empirical efficiency gains up to 1,000x speedup and 3,000x memory reduction.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and Omniglot shows comparable or superior performance to existing methods.  
- Demonstrates generality by adapting SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with clear motivation and technical exposition.  
- Strength: substantial practical efficiency gains addressing MANN scalability.  
- Strength: strong theoretical justification for the approach.  
- Strength: diverse and convincing empirical results.  
- Strength: broad applicability beyond the specific SAM architecture.  
- Weakness: limited exploration and discussion of trade-offs between linear-index and ANN-based nearest neighbor implementations.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on curriculum learning for scaling experiments without sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to other sparsity techniques in neural networks (e.g., pruning, regularization).  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a critical MANN limitation with solid theory and practical impact.  
- Argument against acceptance: dependence on curriculum learning and insufficient ANN trade-off analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with minor areas for improvement; recommend acceptance.
