The authors propose the use of fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations for computing attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM). They demonstrate through experiments that this approach does not compromise training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. Although the novelty of this work is limited, as evident in Section 3.3 of the memory networks paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), where similar methods have been employed for scalable memory lookups, the application of these tools to writable memories is a new contribution. Despite the lack of a conceptual breakthrough in this paper, the practical aspects of implementing this approach are noteworthy and worth reporting.

I recommend accepting this paper, but I suggest the authors make several revisions. Firstly, I find that the descriptions of the tasks and experiments are insufficiently detailed, making it challenging to replicate the results. Given that the code for the tasks from the original NTM paper is not publicly available and there is no standard version of the tasks, it is essential that the paper provides thorough descriptions of the task construction. I also request that the authors commit to releasing the code for their experiments to facilitate reproducibility.

Secondly, the authors should discuss the limitations and potential failure cases of their model. While they often describe their model as smooth, they acknowledge that the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth. The authors should investigate whether their model's performance is affected by the value of K, the task, or other factors. For instance, with K=1 in the read-only setting, their model bears resemblance to the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), which was reported to perform less well than the fully smooth model. Understanding the limits of their model and the conditions under which it works would significantly enhance the value of this paper.

Additionally, I suggest the authors remove Section 3.6, as it does not contribute substantially to the paper. Furthermore, I recommend that the authors avoid referring to the Omniglot data as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as this terminology may be misleading.

Overall, while this paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach, it would benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of its limitations and potential failure cases.
