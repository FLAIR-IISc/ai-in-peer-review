- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) uses top-K reads and prev-read+LRU writes with ANN indices for O(log N) content addressing and sparse BPTT rollback for O(1) space per step.  
- Asymptotics/extension: Under reasonable assumptions SAM is asymptotically optimal for content-addressable external memory and can be adapted to the DNC (SDNC).  
- Empirical gains: Reported 100–1600× speedups and 3000× memory savings vs dense NTMs, with comparable or better learning on algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot; scales to thousands of steps and millions of slots.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on NTMs/Memory Networks, removes dense attention/write bottleneck, preserves differentiable training (vs RL-NTM), and leverages ANN methods (FLANN/LSH).  
- Strength: Principled sparse access scheme with rigorous analysis, practical BPTT engineering, strong empirical support, and generality to other MANNs.  
- Strength: Paper is well organized and readable; complexity claims are empirically substantiated.  
- Weakness: Background could be clearer—add a brief, self-contained primer on MANNs and clarify erase/add write semantics for non-experts.  
- Weakness: Comparison fairness—current experiments equalize physical memory; should also compare models with equal numbers of memory slots.  
- Weakness: Lacks ablation/sensitivity studies on K, exact vs approximate NN, and ANN rebuild frequency to quantify approximation effects.  
- Weakness: Write policy limits—LRU-based writes can cause overwriting when many relevant items are active; discuss sparse content-based write alternatives.  
- Weakness: Reproducibility—key hyperparameters and implementation choices (K, thresholds, ANN settings, word size) are in the supplement and should be in the main text.  
- Quality: Technical approach is sound, complexity analysis plausible, and implementation details are carefully considered; some empirical choices could be broadened.  
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-structured; would benefit from a concise MANN primer and clearer explanation of write operator semantics.  
- Originality: Novel combination of sparse read/write, ANN-based content addressing, and constant-space BPTT rollback; extension to SDNC increases novelty.  
- Significance: Meaningfully advances scalability of MANNs, enabling large memories and long sequences while retaining accuracy—important for sequence modeling and one-shot learning.  
- Pros for acceptance: Addresses central scalability bottleneck, strong theoretical+empirical support, large efficiency gains, competitive task performance, and extensibility.  
- Cons for acceptance: Needs more ablations on ANN approximations and K, include equal-slot baselines, and improve background accessibility.  
- Overall: A well-executed, impactful paper that substantially improves MANN scalability; with minor clarifications and expanded comparisons it would be a strong NIPS contribution.
