This paper introduces a variant of the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) that utilizes sparse read and write operations. The primary motivation is to address a key limitation of standard NTMs: their requirement for large physical memory and computationally expensive dense operations for backpropagation, which hinders their practical application. The proposed architecture aims to mitigate these issues while maintaining performance comparable to state-of-the-art models.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the proposed architecture and its motivation easy to understand. The core contribution—enabling sparse memory access to make NTMs more efficient—is a significant and practical step towards making these models more scalable.

While the paper is clear for experts in the field, its accessibility could be improved for a broader audience. Including a brief background section on Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) or a more detailed explanation of the memory access mechanisms would make the paper more self-contained. For instance, an explicit explanation of how the "write" operator in an NTM differs from a write to conventional computer memory would be beneficial for readers less familiar with this specific area. This would help clarify the unique challenges and innovations of this line of work.

A more significant point concerns the experimental comparison in Figure 4. The authors chose to normalize models by their total physical memory footprint. This is a valid and justifiable approach for comparing performance under a fixed resource constraint, which is a practical and relevant scenario. However, a potentially more informative comparison would be to use the same *logical* memory size (i.e., the same number of memory slots) for all models. Such a comparison would provide a clearer insight into how the different architectures' performance scales with the size of the problem space, independent of their implementation's physical memory efficiency. This would more directly highlight the performance benefits of the proposed architectural changes.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of memory-augmented networks by introducing a more scalable and efficient NTM architecture. Despite the minor suggestions for improving the paper's self-containedness and the framing of one experimental comparison, the work is solid. It is particularly impressive to see the model achieve performance so close to theoretical bounds while addressing a critical practical limitation of its predecessors.
