- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a sparse memory-access scheme that removes a key scalability bottleneck in MANNs.  
- Addresses the O(N) per-step computational/memory overhead of NTMs/Memory Networks by restricting reads/writes to a fixed-size sparse subset.  
- Achieves asymptotic optimality: O(log N) time per access and O(N) initialization with O(1) per-step space.  
- Provides a formal theoretical optimality proof (Supplementary A).  
- Empirical gains: up to 1,000× faster and 3,000× less memory than dense NTMs on large-scale tasks (Section 4.1).  
- Maintains near-identical learning efficiency to dense models on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort).  
- Generalizes to real benchmarks (Omniglot one-shot) and scales to sequences requiring 100,000+ time steps.  
- Extends to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and achieves state-of-the-art on the Babi tasks without supervised memory access.  
- Strength — Originality & significance: conceptually simple yet transformative solution enabling practical MANN deployment.  
- Strength — Quality: rigorous theory, well-designed experiments, thorough ablations, and reproducible implementation details.  
- Strength — Clarity: clear explanations, diagrams, and transparent experimental setup.  
- Weakness — Limited analysis of why sparsity sometimes improves learning; lacks mechanistic/ broader analysis.  
- Weakness — ANN implementation trade-offs (k-d trees vs LSH) are pragmatic but not deeply evaluated; impact of imbalance not fully explored.  
- Recommendation: strong, high-impact contribution; minor analytical/implementation gaps but overall recommends acceptance.
