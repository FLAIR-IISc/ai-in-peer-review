Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that enables end-to-end differentiable, content-based access with sparse reads and writes. The key idea is to restrict memory interactions to a small, fixed number of locations per step (top-K for reads; previously read locations and the least recently used location for writes), and to use efficient data structures (approximate nearest-neighbor indices via randomized k-d trees or LSH) to implement content-based addressing in O(log N) time. The authors also introduce an efficient backpropagation-through-time scheme that stores only sparse memory diffs and rolls back modifications during the backward pass, yielding O(1) space per time step. Under reasonable assumptions, SAM achieves asymptotically optimal time and space complexity for content-addressable external memory. They further show how to adapt the approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).

Empirically, SAM is reported to be 100–1600× faster than dense NTMs and uses 3000× less physical memory at scale, while learning comparably or better on standard NTM algorithmic tasks. With a curriculum, SAM handles sequences of thousands of steps and memory sizes in the millions, and generalizes well beyond training lengths. On bAbI tasks, SAM/SDNC achieve near state-of-the-art without supervision of memory access, and on Omniglot one-shot classification SAM outperforms dense baselines, presumably due to its larger capacity.

Relation to prior work
SAM builds directly on Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks by decoupling parameter count from memory capacity, but addresses the key scalability bottleneck of dense attention and dense writes. Compared to reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing (e.g., RL-NTM), SAM retains differentiable training with low-variance gradients. The paper also connects to approximate nearest-neighbor search methods (FLANN, LSH) for content-based addressing.

Strengths
- Clear and impactful contribution: a principled sparse memory access scheme with rigorous asymptotic analysis and practical engineering for BPTT.
- Strong empirical evidence: large speed and memory savings with comparable or improved learning on synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot.
- Generality: the sparse approach extends to DNC and likely other MANNs.
- Well organized and readable; the architectural choices are motivated and the complexity claims are substantiated empirically.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Background clarity: Although the paper targets readers familiar with MANNs, a brief, self-contained explanation of memory-augmented networks would help non-experts. In particular, clarifying what the erase/add write operator is intended to accomplish (vs. conventional, discrete memory writes) would improve accessibility.
- Fairness of comparisons (Figure 4): Matching models by equal physical memory usage is a reasonable way to compare under fixed resource constraints. However, a complementary comparison using the same number of memory slots across models (rather than equalizing physical memory) would be more informative about task performance per memory capacity, independent of implementation efficiency.
- Approximation analysis: While the paper shows that sparse reads/writes and ANN indexing do not hurt learning on the reported tasks, additional ablations (e.g., sensitivity to K, exact vs. approximate nearest neighbors, rebuild frequency of the ANN) would strengthen the claim that sparsity/ANN rarely degrades performance.
- Write policy: The “previously read or least recently used” write scheme is simple and effective, but a brief discussion of its limitations (e.g., potential overwriting due to suboptimal LRU when many relevant items are active) and when a sparse content-based write might help would add nuance.
- Reproducibility: Many training details are relegated to the supplement. Consolidating the key hyperparameters and implementation choices (e.g., K, thresholds, ANN settings, memory word size) in the main text would improve reproducibility.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and well justified. The complexity analysis is plausible, and the implementation details (sparse diffs for BPTT, ANN integration) are carefully considered. Claims are supported by both asymptotic reasoning and empirical benchmarks across multiple domains. Some empirical choices (e.g., comparison setup for memory size) could be broadened, but the current results already convincingly support the main claims.

Clarity
The paper is well structured and generally easy to follow. Adding a concise primer on MANNs and a clearer explanation of the write operator’s semantics would make it more self-contained for a broader NIPS audience.

Originality
The combination of sparse read/write mechanisms, efficient ANN-based content addressing inside the network, and the constant-space BPTT rollback is novel and practically significant. The extension to SDNC further demonstrates originality and generality.

Significance
Scaling MANNs is an important problem with clear implications for sequence modeling, reasoning, and one-shot learning. The presented approach advances the state of the art in both efficiency and practical applicability, enabling training with very large memories and long sequences while retaining competitive accuracy.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a central scalability bottleneck with a clean, general solution.
- Strong theoretical and empirical support, with large speed/memory gains.
- Competitive or superior performance on standard tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot.
- Extensible to other architectures (e.g., DNC).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited discussion/ablation on the effects of ANN approximations and K.
- Comparison protocol could include equal-memory-slot baselines in addition to equal-physical-memory baselines.
- Background could be more self-contained for non-experts.

Overall
A well-executed and impactful paper that substantially improves the scalability of memory-augmented neural networks while preserving accuracy. With minor clarifications and expanded comparisons, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
