- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel scheme restricting external memory reads/writes to a fixed-size sparse subset.  
- Theoretically establishes SAM’s optimality for sparse memory access.  
- Empirically demonstrates practical efficacy and significant speedups over Neural Turing Machines (NTMs).  
- Section 4.1 shows sparsity reduces memory access overhead, enabling efficient read/write performance.  
- Section 4.2 rigorously evaluates learning cost on standard NTM benchmarks (copy, associative recall, priority sorting).  
- SAM achieves lower combined costs than NTM on associative recall and priority sorting, indicating task-specific advantages.  
- Sparse read/write operations (linear or ANN-based) can improve early-stage learning, suggesting sparsity as an inductive bias.  
- Real-world application in Section 4.4 strengthens relevance and Torch7 implementation ensures reproducibility.  
- The paper is well-structured with clear assumptions, thorough experiments, and detailed architecture in the appendix.  
- All claims are supported by evidence and empirical results do not overclaim SAM’s benefits.  
- Suggests need for analysis of when sparsity helps (task characteristics, model scale) and how alternate ANN architectures interact with SAM.  
- Recommends more explicit discussion of trade-offs (memory footprint reduction vs potential loss of expressiveness in complex tasks).
