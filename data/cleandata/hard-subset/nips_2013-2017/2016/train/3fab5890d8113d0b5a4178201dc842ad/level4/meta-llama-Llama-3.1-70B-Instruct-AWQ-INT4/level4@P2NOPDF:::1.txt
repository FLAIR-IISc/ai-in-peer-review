The authors propose an efficient method for computing attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM) using fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations. They demonstrate through experiments that this approach does not compromise training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. While the novelty of this work is somewhat limited, as similar concepts have been explored in memory network papers (e.g., section 3.3 of http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), the application of these techniques to writable memories is a notable contribution. Although the paper may not present a groundbreaking idea, its practical aspects and experimental results make it worth reporting. I recommend accepting the paper, but I suggest the following changes:

1. To facilitate replication, the paper should provide more detailed descriptions of the tasks, especially considering that the original NTM paper's code has not been released and there is no standard version of the tasks. The authors should also commit to releasing their experiment code to ensure transparency and reproducibility.

2. The authors should discuss the limitations and potential failure cases of their model. Specifically, they often describe their model as smooth, but the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is not smooth. It would be valuable to analyze how this affects the model's performance, particularly in comparison to fully smooth models. For instance, the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf) uses a similar approach but without hashing and was reported to perform less well. The authors should investigate whether the differences in performance are due to the choice of K, the task, or other factors. Furthermore, they should consider the implications of training their model with discrete actions without accounting for this discreteness (unlike http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Understanding the limits of their approach would significantly enhance the paper's contribution.

Additionally, I suggest removing section 3.6, as it does not add substantial value to the paper. The authors should also avoid referring to the Omniglot data as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as these terms may be misleading.
