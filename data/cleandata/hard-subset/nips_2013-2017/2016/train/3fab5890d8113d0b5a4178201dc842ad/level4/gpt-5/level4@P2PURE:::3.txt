This paper presents a novel memory-access scheme, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), within a memory-augmented neural network architecture. This area has developed recently following the introduction of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). Such augmentation enables neural networks to learn beyond the capabilities of traditional LSTM models. The key contribution is to constrain all reads from and writes to the external memory to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than allowing unbounded access. The authors provide proofs supporting the method and its optimality.

This design should yield strong read/write efficiency due to the reduced active memory footprint, as shown in Section 4.1. The authors also quantify the learning costs introduced by sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks: (1) Copy, (2) Associative Recall, and (3) Priority Sort. The combined costs are lower than those of the NTM for some tasks (i.e., 2 and 3). I also appreciate the inclusion of a real-world example in Section 4.4. The benchmarks use Torch7. The idea is straightforward and novel, and the speedups over the NTM are substantial and impressive. It is particularly interesting that sparse reads and writes performed by a linear model or an ANN can, in some cases, benefit early-stage learning (Section 4.2).

If the authors could elaborate on when such early-learning improvements are likely to occur (i.e., under what conditions sparsity helps), or evaluate SAM with different ANN approaches to assess generality, it would be very helpful for readers. Overall, the paper is well written, with assumptions laid out clearly and the architecture detailed in the appendix. It is an engaging read.
