This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural network architectures. This work builds upon the relatively recent area of research pioneered by Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), which augment neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional LSTM models.

The primary contribution of this paper is a mechanism that constrains all reads from and writes to the external memory to a fixed-size subset of memory words, in contrast to the dense, full-memory access used by standard NTMs. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this methodology and its optimality. As shown in Section 4.1, this approach achieves superior read and write performance due to the reduced number of memory locations accessed.

Beyond computational performance, the authors evaluate the impact of sparsity on learning convergence using standard NTM tasks: copy, associative recall, and priority sort (Section 4.2). For associative recall and priority sort, the combined learning cost is lower for SAM than for the NTM baseline. A strength of the paper is the inclusion of a real-world task in Section 4.4, with benchmarks conducted using Torch7.

The core idea is straightforward yet novel, and the resulting speedup over the NTM is significant and impressive. A particularly interesting finding is that the sparse access mechanism, controlled by a linear layer or an ANN, can benefit early-stage learning in some cases (Section 4.2). The paper could be strengthened if the authors elaborated on this finding by analyzing the conditions under which this learning improvement occurs. Furthermore, an exploration of different controller architectures combined with SAM would be a valuable addition.

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper. The assumptions are laid out clearly, architectural details are provided in the appendix, and the work presents a compelling and well-evaluated contribution to the field.
