This paper investigates the application of fast approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search and arg-top-K operations to improve the scalability of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). The authors demonstrate that using these techniques for attention weight computation does not negatively impact training performance and, importantly, enables training on larger and more complex problems. While the core idea of leveraging approximate lookups in memory networks is not entirely novel – as demonstrated in prior work such as the Memory Networks paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf) and subsequent memory network research utilizing hashing for scalability with millions of items – its application to *writable* memories appears less common. Therefore, despite a limited conceptual leap, the practical challenges overcome and reported in this work are valuable. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the following points.

First, reproducibility is a significant concern. The current descriptions provided are insufficient to reliably replicate the results. Specifically, the task definitions lack crucial detail. Given the unavailability of code from the original NTM paper and the absence of a standardized implementation for these tasks, a comprehensive and precise description of their construction is essential. Furthermore, I strongly encourage the authors to release their experimental code to facilitate reproducibility and further research.

Second, a critical analysis of the model’s limitations is needed. The authors frequently emphasize the “smoothness” of their model, yet acknowledge the inherent discontinuity introduced by the argmax (or K-argmax) operation. When K=1, the model bears resemblance to the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), which reportedly performed worse than its fully smooth counterpart. Is the observed success here attributable to the use of K>1, the specific tasks employed, or other factors? A deeper investigation into potential failure cases, and a discussion of the task characteristics and experimental setups conducive to successful training, would significantly enhance the paper’s contribution. The model fundamentally makes discrete decisions, yet the training procedure does not appear to explicitly account for this (in contrast to, for example, http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275). Understanding the conditions under which this approach *works* – and crucially, where it *fails* – is of paramount importance. Identifying the limits of the method will greatly increase its practical value.

Finally, I suggest removing section 3.6, as its contribution appears marginal. Additionally, the characterization of the Omniglot dataset as “non-synthetic” or “real-world” is misleading and should be revised.
