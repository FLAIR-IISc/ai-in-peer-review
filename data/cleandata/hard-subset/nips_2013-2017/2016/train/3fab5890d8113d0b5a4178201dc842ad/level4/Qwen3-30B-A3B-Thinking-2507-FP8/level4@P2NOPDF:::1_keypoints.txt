- Proposal: use fast ANN structures + arg-top-K for attention in NTMs to maintain training performance and scale to larger problems.  
- Builds on established memory-network methods; applying these tools to writeable memories is a meaningful practical implementation contribution.  
- Conceptual novelty is limited; paper needs significant revisions before acceptance.  
- Primary replicability concern: insufficient detail on task construction and data generation; replication is highly challenging.  
- Authors must provide exhaustive task specifications and commit to releasing all experimental code.  
- Major methodological concern: arg-top-K (especially K=1) introduces non-smooth discrete operations versus fully differentiable models.  
- Prior work (e.g., MemNN-WSH) shows argmax can underperform smoothed alternatives; the paper does not analyze why their model succeeds.  
- Missing systematic study of failure cases where arg-top-K degrades performance.  
- Missing discussion of task properties that enable success (e.g., alignment of discrete actions with problem structure).  
- Missing empirical comparison to smoothed/differentiable baselines on identical tasks.  
- Claiming the model is "smooth" is misleading given discrete ops; clarify or remove that characterization.  
- Section 3.6 (on smoothness) is redundant and should be removed.  
- Incorrectly labeling Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world"; this must be corrected.  
- Acceptance contingent on: (1) detailed task specs + code release, (2) analysis of failure modes and comparisons to smoothed baselines, (3) removal of Section 3.6 and correction of Omniglot.  
- Overall: practical implementation is useful and actionable with revisions; as written it lacks rigor—recommend acceptance pending major revisions.
