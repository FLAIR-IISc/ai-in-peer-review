This paper introduces the Sparse Access Memory architecture for memory-augmented neural networks, including Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. The authors demonstrate that this approach yields substantial run-time and memory efficiency gains—by three orders of magnitude—over existing models. Furthermore, the architecture enables remarkable performance improvements in curriculum learning and allows for generalization to sequences an order of magnitude longer than those observed during training (from lengths of 10,000 in training to 200,000 in testing).

This is a potentially groundbreaking paper. While the field of memory-augmented neural networks has produced interesting research, practical applicability has remained a challenge. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported here suggest that these models may now be ready for practical deployment, which could have far-reaching implications.

However, the paper is missing a discussion of the underlying reasons for the drastic performance improvements, particularly in curriculum learning and long-sequence generalization. The efficiency gains alone cannot fully account for these advances in capability. It seems plausible that the Sparse Access Memory architecture plays a role analogous to that of sparse auto-encoders in other contexts. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors shared their perspective on this phenomenon, even if their analysis is preliminary.
