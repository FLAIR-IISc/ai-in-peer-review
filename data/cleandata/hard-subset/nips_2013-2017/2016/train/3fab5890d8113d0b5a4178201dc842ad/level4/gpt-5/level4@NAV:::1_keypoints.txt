- Method: uses fast approximate nearest-neighbor structures and arg-top-K to compute attention in a Neural Turing Machine, enabling larger-scale training without harming performance.  
- Novelty: limited compared to prior memory-network hashing work, but applying these tools to writeable memories and the engineering required is a useful contribution.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions.  
- Reproducibility concern: task descriptions are too thin; paper must provide precise task construction details to allow replication.  
- Code release: reviewer asks authors to commit to releasing experiment code.  
- Failure modes: paper lacks analysis of when the approach fails and whether it always works.  
- Non-smoothness issue: argmax/K-argmax operations introduce non-smooth/discrete decisions that may impede training and need examination.  
- K=1 case: with K=1 the model resembles prior MemNN-WSH work that performed worse than smooth alternatives—authors should analyze whether K, task choice, or other factors explain performance.  
- Limits and setup: provide experiments/discussion clarifying which tasks and setups enable successful training under discrete decisions and when training breaks.  
- Presentation edits: remove section 3.6 and avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”
