This paper presents a new memory access mechanism, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), within a memory-augmented neural network architecture. This area of research is relatively recent, following the introduction of Neural Turing Machines. The augmentation enables neural networks to learn beyond the capabilities of traditional LSTM models. The paper’s main contribution is to constrain all reads from and writes to external memory to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than an unbounded set. The authors provide proofs for the methodology and its optimality. As shown in Section 4.1, this design achieves strong read and write performance due to the reduced active memory size. The authors also account for the learning costs introduced by sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks: 1) copy, 2) Associated Call, and 3) priority sort. The combined costs are lower than those of the NTM for some tasks (i.e., 2 and 3). I also appreciate that the authors include a real-world example in Section 4.4. The benchmarks are implemented in Torch7. The idea is straightforward and novel. The speedup over the NTM is substantial and impressive. It is particularly interesting that sparse reads and writes by a linear model or an ANN can benefit early-stage learning in some cases (Section 4.2). If the authors could elaborate on how to generalize these gains to other settings (i.e., under what conditions learning is improved), or apply SAM with different ANN approaches, it would be very helpful for the audience. Overall, this is a well-written paper with assumptions clearly stated and the architecture provided in the appendix. It is engaging to read.
