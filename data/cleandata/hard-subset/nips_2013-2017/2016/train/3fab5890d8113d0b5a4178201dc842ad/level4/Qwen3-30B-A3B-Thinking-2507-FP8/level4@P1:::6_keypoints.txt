- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a new MANN architecture that addresses scalability limits of NTMs.  
- Achieves asymptotically optimal complexity: O(log N) time per step and O(1) space per step.  
- Maintains performance comparable to dense MANNs on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort).  
- Matches or improves results on Omniglot one-shot classification and Babi QA tasks.  
- Reduces memory usage by ~3,000× and speeds training by ~1,000× for large memories (e.g., 64,000 words).  
- Generalizes to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) that attains state-of-the-art on Babi without supervised memory access.  
- Technical exposition is clear and well-structured, with effective background coverage of MANN literature.  
- Sparse reads/writes, approximate nearest neighbors, and LRU allocation are convincingly argued as enabling optimal scaling.  
- Theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) supports the claimed complexity bounds.  
- Experimental design is rigorous (e.g., curriculum learning) and empirically validated across benchmarks.  
- Figure 4’s comparison by physical memory constraints is justified and shows SAM outperforming dense models under equal hardware.  
- Minor suggestion: clarify the functional purpose of the memory write operation (e.g., erase/add updates) for non-specialist readers.  
- Overall assessment: significant, original contribution with strong theoretical and empirical support; strong candidate for acceptance.
