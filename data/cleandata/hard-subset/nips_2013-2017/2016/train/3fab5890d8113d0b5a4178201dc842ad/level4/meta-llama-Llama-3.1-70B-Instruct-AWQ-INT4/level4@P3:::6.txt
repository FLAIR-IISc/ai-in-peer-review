This paper presents a novel architecture, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which addresses the scalability issues of traditional Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks. The authors propose a sparse read and write scheme that significantly reduces the computational and memory overhead of MANNs, making them more suitable for real-world applications.

The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its components. The background section provides a good overview of the existing MANNs, including NTMs and Memory Networks, and highlights the limitations of these models. However, a brief description of the memory-augmented neural networks and an explanation of the memory operations (e.g., the write operator) would make the paper more self-contained and accessible to readers who are not experts in the field.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM through various experiments, including speed and memory benchmarks, learning with sparse memory access, and scaling with a curriculum. The results show that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory reductions compared to traditional MANNs, while maintaining comparable performance. The authors also investigate the learning cost of inducing sparsity and the effect of using approximate nearest neighbor indexes within the network.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of MANNs, and the proposed architecture has the potential to overcome the scalability issues that have limited the adoption of these models in real-world applications.
