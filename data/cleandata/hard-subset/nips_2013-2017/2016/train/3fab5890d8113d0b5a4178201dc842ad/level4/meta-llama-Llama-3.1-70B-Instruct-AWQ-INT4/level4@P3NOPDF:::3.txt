This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) in memory-augmented neural network architectures. This research area is fairly recent, originating with the invention of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs), which allow neural networks to learn beyond the limits of traditional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models. The primary contribution of this paper is that all writes to and reads from external memory are constrained to a fixed size, specifically a subset of the memory words, rather than an unlimited size.

The authors demonstrate the methodology and its optimality, and it is conceivable that this approach will achieve good read and write performance due to the reduced memory size, as shown in Section 4.1. However, the authors also investigate the learning costs of introducing sparsity in Section 4.2 using standard NTM tasks, namely: 1) copy, 2) associative recall, and 3) priority sort. The combined costs are lower than those of NTM for some tasks (i.e., 2 and 3). Additionally, the authors include a real-world example in Section 4.4, which is a valuable addition to the paper.

The benchmarking is performed using Torch7, and the idea is both straightforward and novel. The speedup achieved by SAM is significant compared to NTM, which is impressive. Furthermore, the fact that sparse reads and writes, facilitated by a linear or artificial neural network (ANN), can actually benefit early-stage learning in some cases is very interesting, as discussed in Section 4.2. To further enhance the paper, it would be helpful if the authors could elaborate on how to generalize SAM to other cases, including those where learning could be improved, or by exploring different ANN approaches with SAM.

Overall, the paper is well-written, with all assumptions clearly laid out and the architecture detailed in the appendix. The writing is engaging, making the paper interesting to read.
