

The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that addresses the critical scalability limitations of existing approaches like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic optimality in space and time complexity (O(log N) per operation, O(1) space per timestep) while maintaining representational power. Key innovations include sparse read/write operations constrained to K=4–8 memory words per timestep, an efficient approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index for content-based addressing, and a memory management scheme tracking least-recently-used slots. Empirically, SAM achieves 1,000× speedup and 3,000× reduced memory usage over dense models (e.g., 53MB vs. 29GB for 64K memory words), with comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Omniglot one-shot learning, and Babi question answering. The approach is further generalized to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), which sets a new state-of-the-art on Babi tasks without supervised memory access.

**Strengths:**  
The technical contribution is significant and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the scalability bottleneck in MANNs (O(N) time/space per timestep) and provide a rigorous solution with asymptotic guarantees (Supplementary A). The empirical validation is thorough: SAM’s speed/memory gains are demonstrated across multiple datasets and task scales (e.g., 4,000+ sequence length on Associative Recall), and the ablation studies (Fig. 2) convincingly show sparse access does not impair learning. The implementation details—particularly the ANN integration (k-d trees/LSH), efficient backpropagation via sparse state tracking (Section 3.4), and usage-based memory management (Section 3.2)—are sound and well-justified. The SDNC extension (Supplementary D) further demonstrates generality, achieving the best Babi results without supervision. The paper also situates itself well within NIPS work (e.g., NTM [7], Memory Networks [21]) while clearly differentiating its sparse approach from prior methods like reinforcement learning-based addressers [22].

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary limitation lies in the *depth* of theoretical analysis. While the asymptotic claims are supported, the paper lacks a formal proof that sparse reads/writes preserve the *algorithmic expressiveness* of dense MANNs (e.g., whether SAM can learn all NTM-learnable algorithms). The ANN implementation (Section 3.5) is pragmatic but could elaborate on trade-offs: k-d trees become imbalanced during online training (as noted in Section 5), and LSH’s dependence on word dimensionality (e.g., "small vs. large word sizes") needs clearer guidelines for practitioners. Additionally, the Omniglot results (Section 4.5) show SAM’s superiority but do not explicitly compare against the strongest non-MANN baselines (e.g., recent meta-learning models [16]), which slightly weakens the significance claim for real-world applicability. The "surprisingly better" learning on Priority Sort (Fig. 2) warrants deeper investigation—why does sparsity aid certain tasks?

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong technical contribution that directly addresses a critical scalability challenge in MANNs, with compelling empirical results and clear path to real-world deployment. The paper’s strengths—novel solution, rigorous validation, and broad applicability—outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely implementation-focused and improvable in revision. It advances the state of the art in memory-augmented learning (e.g., SDNC’s Babi results surpass prior unsupervised work [17]) and aligns with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, well-founded methods. While the technical approach is conceptually simple, its effectiveness and generality make it valuable. I recommend **weak acceptance** with minor revisions: (1) clarify the expressiveness argument in Supplementary A, (2) add a brief discussion of ANN trade-offs for practitioners, and (3) briefly contextualize Omniglot results against non-MANN baselines. The paper’s scalability claims are transformative for MANNs and deserve publication at NIPS.
