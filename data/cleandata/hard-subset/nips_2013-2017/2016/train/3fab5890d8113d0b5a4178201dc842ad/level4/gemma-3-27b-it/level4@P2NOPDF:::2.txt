Current neural network models incorporating external memory often suffer from significant scalability issues, with both computational cost and memory requirements increasing rapidly as memory size grows. This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme, and demonstrates its ability to maintain representational capacity while achieving substantial improvements in training efficiency – reportedly 1000x faster and 3000x more memory efficient. 

However, despite these promising empirical results, I do not believe the paper, in its current form, reaches the technical bar for acceptance at NeurIPS. The core technical contributions – efficient backpropagation through SAM and an approximate nearest neighbor search within the sparse memory – appear relatively straightforward. While the experimental validation is interesting, the novelty and depth of the technical advancements do not, in my assessment, sufficiently justify publication.

Specifically, the proposed backpropagation method relies on established techniques adapted to the SAM structure. The authors do not introduce a fundamentally new gradient-based learning mechanism. Similarly, the approximate nearest neighbor search, while functional, utilizes existing algorithms and doesn't present a significant algorithmic breakthrough in the context of approximate nearest neighbor search itself. 

The reported speed and memory efficiency gains are significant and represent a worthwhile engineering achievement. However, a strong NeurIPS paper should offer more than just efficient implementation of known techniques. It should present novel algorithmic insights or a deeper theoretical understanding. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of *why* SAM facilitates such substantial efficiency gains, potentially through a theoretical analysis of the sparsity pattern or a comparison to alternative memory access methods beyond those currently presented. 

The experimental section demonstrates the method’s performance on a limited set of tasks. While the results are positive, the paper lacks a broader evaluation against a wider range of memory-augmented neural network architectures and tasks. A more comprehensive comparative analysis would better establish the benefits and limitations of SAM. Further investigation into the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameter choices, particularly those related to the sparsity pattern, would also be valuable.
