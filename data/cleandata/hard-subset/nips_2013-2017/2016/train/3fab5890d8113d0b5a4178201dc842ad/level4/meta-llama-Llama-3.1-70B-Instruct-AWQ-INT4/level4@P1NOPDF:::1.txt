The authors propose the use of fast approximate-nearest-neighbors structures and arg-top-K operations to compute attention weights in a Neural Turing Machine (NTM). They demonstrate through experiments that this approach does not compromise training performance and, in fact, enables training on larger problems. While the novelty of this work is somewhat limited, as similar concepts have been explored in memory networks (e.g., section 3.3 of http://arxiv.org/pdf/1410.3916.pdf), the application of these techniques to writable memories is a notable contribution. Moreover, the practical considerations of implementing these methods are valuable and worth reporting.

I recommend accepting the paper, but I suggest several revisions to strengthen the manuscript. Firstly, I find that the descriptions of the tasks and experiments are insufficiently detailed, making it challenging to replicate the results. Given that the code for the original NTM paper has not been released and there is no standard version of the tasks, it is crucial that the authors provide thorough descriptions of the task construction. I also request that the authors commit to releasing the code for their experiments to facilitate reproducibility.

Secondly, it is essential to discuss the limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed approach. The authors often describe their model as smooth, but the argmax (or K-argmax) operation is inherently non-smooth. With K=1, in the read-only setting, their model bears similarities to the MemNN-WSH model (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.08895.pdf), which was reported to perform less well than the fully smooth model. Is this due to the choice of K=1 or the specific task? I believe that the paper would benefit from an analysis of failure cases and a discussion of the types of tasks or setups that allow their training method to work. The model's discrete actions are trained without considering the underlying dynamics (unlike http://arxiv.org/abs/1511.07275), and understanding the limits of this approach is crucial. Furthermore, I suggest removing section 3.6, as it does not contribute significantly to the paper's core message. Additionally, I recommend avoiding the terms "non-synthetic" and "real-world" when referring to the Omniglot data, as they may be misleading.
