## Review of Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in the development of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs): their poor scalability in both space and time as memory size grows. The authors present Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme designed to retain the representational power of MANNs like the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while dramatically improving training efficiency with large memories. The core idea – restricting memory modifications to a sparse subset and utilizing efficient data structures – is technically sound and well-motivated. 

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in recurrent neural networks (LSTMs [11], NTMs [7], Memory Networks [21]) which have sought to decouple memory capacity from model parameters.  While these prior approaches demonstrated the potential of external memory for learning algorithmic solutions, their computational and memory overhead limited their applicability to complex, real-world problems.  SAM directly tackles this limitation, and importantly, the authors demonstrate that their approach achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.  This work also connects to recent efforts in scaling NTMs, such as those employing reinforcement learning for discrete addressing [22], but distinguishes itself by maintaining end-to-end differentiability.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed SAM architecture and its optimizations (sparse reads/writes, efficient data structures, clever backpropagation strategy) are well-reasoned and appear technically robust. The claimed asymptotic complexity improvements are a significant contribution.
* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the scalability problems of existing MANNs and convincingly argues for the importance of addressing them. The concrete examples illustrating the memory overhead (e.g., 30MiB for 64 memories, 29GiB for 64,000) are highly effective.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling. The paper demonstrates significant speedups (1,000x faster, 3,000x less memory) compared to non-sparse models.  Crucially, these improvements are *not* achieved at the expense of performance; SAM maintains comparable data efficiency on various tasks (synthetic NTM tasks [7], Babi [20], Omniglot [16]). The scaling to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps is particularly impressive.
* **Generality:** The authors showcase the adaptability of their approach by extending it to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) [8], further demonstrating its broader applicability.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The inclusion of Supplementary Material is helpful for understanding implementation details and additional experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **ANN Choices:** While the use of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) data structures is sensible for efficiency, the paper doesn’t delve deeply into the trade-offs between different ANN algorithms (FLANN vs. LSH) or their impact on performance. More analysis of ANN performance and potential limitations would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions tuning parameters (e.g., the discount factor in the usage definition), but a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity could strengthen the findings.
* **Limited Comparison to Other Scaling Approaches:** While the comparison to the original NTM and dense DAM is valuable, including a comparison to more recent or alternative scaling techniques (beyond reinforcement learning mentioned in the discussion) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in the field of memory-augmented neural networks.
* The proposed SAM architecture is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates strong empirical performance.
* The results show substantial improvements in scalability without sacrificing accuracy.
* The approach is generalizable and applicable to other memory architectures.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* While the empirical results are strong, the analysis of the ANN component could be more in-depth.
* Further investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity would be helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors have successfully addressed a critical scalability issue with a novel and technically sound approach. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the potential of SAM to enable the development of MANNs capable of handling more complex, real-world problems. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.

**Confidence:** 9/10
