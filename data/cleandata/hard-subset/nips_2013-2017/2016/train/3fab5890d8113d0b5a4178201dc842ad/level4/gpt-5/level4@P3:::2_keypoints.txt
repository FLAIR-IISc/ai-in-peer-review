- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) enforces sparsity in reads/writes and uses reversible sparse BPTT to achieve sublinear read time and constant per-step space with respect to memory size.  
- Core mechanisms: top-K sparse content-based reads via ANN (randomized kd-trees or LSH), sparse writes to read locations + LRU slot, and sparse-delta rollback for BPTT.  
- Claim: asymptotically optimal space/time under assumptions detailed in the supplement.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on NTM, Memory Networks, DNC; targets the O(N) attention bottleneck; related to discrete-addressing approaches.  
- Strength — significance: addresses a central MANN scaling bottleneck with up to three orders of magnitude empirical speedups and large memory savings.  
- Strength — empirical scope: evaluated on algorithmic tasks, long-horizon curricula, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning with good scalability and data efficiency.  
- Strength — practicality: reversible BPTT with sparse deltas and the LRU-based allocation policy are simple and effective.  
- Strength — generality: approach can be adapted to DNC and likely other MANNs.  
- Weakness — novelty: main components (ANN top-K, LRU allocation, reversible sparse updates) are largely engineering integrations rather than theoretical innovations.  
- Weakness — differentiability: external ANN index is non-differentiable and the paper lacks analysis of its effect on gradient bias/variance and learning dynamics.  
- Weakness — limited ablations: insufficient experiments varying K, ANN types/parameters, and exact vs approximate search to quantify trade-offs.  
- Weakness — fairness of comparisons: dense baselines use small memories while SAM uses much larger ones, confounding capacity with runtime/memory comparisons.  
- Weakness — missing baselines: no comparisons to discrete-addressing MANNs or other sparse attention variants.  
- Weakness — clarity/completeness: key mechanisms (usage data structure, rollback, complexity assumptions) are brief and deferred to the supplement; pseudocode and details are lacking.  
- Weakness — ANN details: unclear conditions for kd-tree vs LSH, rebuild schedules, and their impact on accuracy/speed.  
- Weakness — real-world validation: limited demonstration on real-world sequence tasks beyond Omniglot.  
- Quality: experiments convincingly show speed/memory benefits and learning ability, but theoretical optimality and the impact of approximation need stronger support.  
- Clarity: paper is well written overall but central algorithmic/theoretical elements are too condensed; moving assumptions and pseudocode into the main text would help reproducibility.  
- Originality: primarily a systems contribution integrating known techniques rather than introducing new theory.  
- Significance: if adopted, could enable scaling MANNs to much larger contexts and enable long-horizon training.  
- Pros for acceptance: solves a key scalability issue, shows strong empirical efficiency, enables very long sequence training, and generalizes to other MANNs.  
- Cons for acceptance: modest technical novelty, insufficient justification of optimality, unanalyzed non-differentiable approximations, and unbalanced comparisons.  
- Suggested improvements — theory: bring main theoretical assumptions/proofs or clear statements into the main text and quantify constants.  
- Suggested improvements — experiments: add ablations on K and ANN parameters and compare exact vs approximate search to show accuracy–speed trade-offs.  
- Suggested improvements — baselines/plots: include comparisons to discrete-addressing and other sparse attention baselines and present performance vs wall-clock vs memory across capacities.  
- Suggested improvements — details: provide pseudocode for rollback and usage-tracking and detail kd-tree vs LSH choices and rebuild strategies.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — important practical results but limited novelty and insufficient theoretical/empirical support for several claims.
