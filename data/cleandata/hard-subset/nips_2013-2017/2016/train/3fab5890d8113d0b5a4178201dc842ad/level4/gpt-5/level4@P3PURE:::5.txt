This paper introduces a Sparse Access Memory (SAM) architecture for memory-augmented neural networks, including Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks. The approach delivers striking run-time and memory efficiency improvements—on the order of three orders of magnitude—relative to existing memory-augmented models. The architecture also appears to yield substantial gains in curriculum learning and in generalization to sequences that are an order of magnitude longer than those seen during training (trained on sequences up to length 10,000 and tested on sequences of length 200,000). This is potentially a groundbreaking contribution.

The field of neural networks augmented with external memory—launched by Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks—has progressed rapidly with many intriguing ideas and results. However, prior systems did not yet seem practically viable. The drastic efficiency and performance improvements reported here strongly suggest that memory-augmented neural networks may now be ready for practical deployment, with likely far-reaching implications.

The primary missing piece is a discussion of why the observed performance gains occur in curriculum learning and in generalization so far beyond the training range. Run-time and memory efficiency alone cannot account for these effects, so there must be an additional mechanism at play. My preliminary intuition is that the Sparse Access Memory architecture may play a role analogous to that of sparse autoencoders in other settings. Even a preliminary account of the authors’ hypotheses would be valuable to readers.
