- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme that constrains reads/writes to a sparse subset to improve scalability of memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Claims SAM retains representational power of prior MANNs while achieving asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.  
- Core technical approach combines sparse memory management with efficient data structures for content-based reads and efficient backpropagation.  
- Empirical results show SAM trains with very large memories, providing significant speedups and memory reductions versus non-sparse models.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, with technical components explained and experiments presented clearly.  
- Technical soundness appears solid; approximate nearest neighbor via sparse access and backpropagation methods seem well-executed.  
- Main concern: perceived lack of technical novelty—components are viewed as straightforward extensions of existing ideas.  
- Authors do not convincingly argue why these sparse techniques were not explored previously.  
- Experiments, while interesting, do not fully explain why SAM scales so efficiently or thoroughly analyze trade-offs with other models.  
- Comparisons to models like NTM and Memory Networks are not entirely convincing or detailed.  
- Recommendation leans against acceptance: technical contribution deemed insufficient without stronger novelty and deeper comparative analysis.  
- Reviewer scores: Quality 6/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 4/10, Significance 7/10.
