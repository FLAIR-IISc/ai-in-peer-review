## Review of "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) – a class of models promising for algorithmic learning but hampered by computational and memory bottlenecks as memory size increases. The authors introduce Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable scheme that significantly improves training efficiency with large memories while retaining the representational power of models like the Neural Turing Machine (NTM). 

SAM achieves this through sparse read and write operations, limiting modifications to a constant number of memory locations per timestep and employing efficient data structures for content-based addressing. The paper clearly articulates the problem, building upon prior work in recurrent networks (LSTMs), MANNs (NTMs, Memory Networks), and differentiable memory access. It effectively positions SAM within this landscape, highlighting its advantages over existing approaches. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by adapting it to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).

The strengths of this work are numerous. The proposed SAM architecture is technically sound, with a compelling argument for its asymptotic space and time complexity. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating significant speedups (1000x faster, 3000x less memory) compared to non-sparse models, and comparable performance on a range of tasks including synthetic algorithmic problems, Babi reasoning, and one-shot Omniglot recognition. Scaling to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps is a particularly impressive achievement. The clarity of the paper is also commendable; the concepts are explained concisely and well, and the architecture is illustrated effectively. 

While the paper is strong, a minor weakness lies in the relatively limited comparison to other recent scaling approaches beyond the NTM. Briefly discussing alternative strategies, such as those employing discrete addressing policies via reinforcement learning (Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015), would further contextualize the contribution. Additionally, while the use of approximate nearest neighbor search is mentioned, a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different ANN implementations on performance would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a high-impact problem in scaling neural networks.
*   Introduces a novel and technically strong solution (SAM) with clear theoretical justification.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements in efficiency and scalability.
*   Paper is well-written, clear, and effectively motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Comparison to alternative scaling methods beyond NTM could be more comprehensive.
*   A more detailed analysis of the impact of different ANN implementations would be valuable.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM architecture offers a promising path towards building more scalable and efficient MANNs, and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
