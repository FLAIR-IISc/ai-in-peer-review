This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks. The authors build upon recent work like Neural Turing Machines, enabling learning beyond traditional LSTM limits. The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of a sparse read and write scheme that constrains reads and writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words rather than unlimited memory. The authors provide theoretical justification and claim optimality of the methodology.

The paper is well-written, and the assumptions and architecture details are clearly explained in the appendix. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through various experiments, including speed and memory benchmarks, learning with sparse memory access, and scaling with a curriculum. The results show that SAM achieves significant speedups compared to the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while maintaining comparable data efficiency and performance.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to scale to large external memories, achieving improvements in empirical run-time and memory overhead by up to three orders of magnitude over vanilla NTMs. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by describing how to construct a sparse version of the recently published Differentiable Neural Computer.

The paper has some limitations, however. The authors acknowledge that the idea is straightforward, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of when sparsity improves learning. Additionally, the authors do not explore other ANN integrations with SAM, which could be an interesting direction for future research.

Overall, I believe that this paper is a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors' approach to sparse memory access is novel and yields significant speedups compared to previous models. The paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough and well-executed.

Arguments for acceptance:
