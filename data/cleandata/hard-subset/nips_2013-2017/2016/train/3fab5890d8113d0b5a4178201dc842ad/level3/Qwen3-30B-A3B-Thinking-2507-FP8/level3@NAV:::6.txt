

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by constraining read/write operations to sparse subsets of memory. SAM achieves asymptotic optimality in space (O(1) per timestep) and time (O(log N)) complexity, demonstrating 1,000× speedup and 3,000× reduced physical memory usage over non-sparse models while maintaining near-state-of-the-art performance on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi question answering, and Omniglot one-shot learning. The work builds directly on Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Differentiable Neural Computers (DNCs), addressing their critical scalability limitations through efficient sparse operations and approximate nearest-neighbor indexing.

**Strengths:** The technical contribution is strong and well-validated. The complexity analysis is rigorous, and experimental results convincingly demonstrate SAM’s scalability (e.g., handling 100,000+ time steps with 1M memory slots). The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with thorough ablation studies showing SAM’s data efficiency rivals dense models. The SDNC variant achieves the best reported Babi results without memory access supervision, highlighting practical significance.

**Weaknesses:** Two minor but important issues require attention. First, the background on MANNs (e.g., distinctions between NTM, Memory Networks, DNC) is insufficient for readers unfamiliar with the field; a brief overview would improve self-containment. Second, Figure 4’s comparison of physical memory sizes is misleading—logical memory capacity (e.g., 64K words) is the more meaningful metric for scalability claims, as physical memory scales with implementation, not model capability. The authors should clarify this in revision.

**Overall:** SAM makes a significant, practical contribution to MANNs, solving a major bottleneck in the field. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear progress toward deployable memory-augmented systems. While the Figure 4 comparison warrants correction, the paper’s core innovation and results are compelling. It advances the state of the art in scalability without sacrificing performance, making it highly significant for both theory and practice. The minor clarity improvements suggested would further strengthen an already strong submission.
