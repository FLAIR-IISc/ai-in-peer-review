- Proposal: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) using sparse reads and writes to scale MANNs with improved asymptotic space/time complexity.  
- Novelty claim: Applies fast approximate nearest neighbors and arg-top-K attention to writable memory in NTMs.  
- Strength — technical clarity: Paper is well-organized and clearly explains the SAM architecture and components.  
- Strength — efficiency: Sparse reads/writes significantly reduce computational and memory overhead of MANNs.  
- Strength — experiments: Comprehensive evaluation on synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot, and bAbI demonstrating SAM’s effectiveness.  
- Strength — related work: Detailed discussion comparing SAM to Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines.  
- Weakness — novelty: Contribution is somewhat thin because prior work already uses large memories with hashing for scalable lookups.  
- Weakness — failure analysis: Lacks detailed analysis of failure cases and limits of SAM across tasks and setups.  
- Weakness — non-smooth ops: Does not thoroughly discuss non-smoothness of argmax/K-argmax (e.g., K=1) or effects on training and comparison to smooth models.  
- Weakness — discrete actions: Does not address implications/limits of training with discrete actions without accounting for discreteness.  
- Reproducibility: Needs more details on task construction and experimental setup for replicability.  
- Suggestion: Analyze and report when SAM fails and under what conditions it degrades.  
- Suggestion: Discuss and empirically compare effects of argmax/K-argmax non-smoothness versus fully smooth alternatives and MemNN-WSH.  
- Suggestion: Discuss training implications of discrete actions and related prior work that handles discreteness.  
- Clarity score: 9/10 — generally clear, but section 3.6 could be removed or merged for better flow.  
- Quality score: 8/10 — well-written and explained, but needs deeper limitation analysis.  
- Originality score: 7/10 — meaningful contribution but limited novelty relative to prior scalable-memory work.  
- Significance score: 8/10 — useful potential for enabling efficient large-problem training, contingent on further analysis.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Significant contribution to MANNs, clear exposition, and thorough experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited novelty, insufficient analysis of limitations/failure modes, and inadequate discussion of argmax discreteness effects.
