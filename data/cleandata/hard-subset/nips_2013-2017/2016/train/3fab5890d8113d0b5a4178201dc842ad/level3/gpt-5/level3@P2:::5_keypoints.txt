- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), using top-K ANN reads and sparse writes (previous reads + LRU) with sparse BPTT rollback to scale memory-augmented networks.  
- SAM achieves asymptotically better time/space complexity versus dense MANNs while retaining end-to-end gradient training; authors also propose SDNC (sparse DNC).  
- Empirically matches or exceeds dense MANNs on synthetic algorithmic tasks, scales to millions of slots, generalizes from ~10k training to ~200k inference, and performs well on bAbI and Omniglot.  
- Major strength: substantial, well-demonstrated scalability (up to ~1000× runtime and ~3000× memory savings) with both theoretical bounds and wall-clock measurements.  
- Practical recipe: combine top-K ANN reads, sparse writes to read+LRU slots, and a sparse-delta BPTT rollback to enable very long untruncated sequences.  
- Strong empirical coverage across tasks (NTM tasks, curriculum scaling, long-sequence generalization, bAbI without supervision, Omniglot).  
- SDNC extension shows generality and yields strong bAbI results without supervised memory retrieval.  
- Concern: top-K selection via ANN is non-differentiable, inducing biased gradients relative to dense soft attention; paper needs explicit discussion and quantification of this approximation.  
- The “asymptotically optimal” claim relies on assumptions (constant K, amortized ANN costs, rebuilds); these assumptions and core arguments should be summarized in the main text.  
- Limited evaluation on large real-world tasks; benefits are mainly shown on synthetic/small-scale benchmarks and need testing on high-throughput real tasks.  
- Comparison breadth is limited: should compare to other sparsity techniques (low-temperature attention, sparsemax/top-k relaxations, Gumbel-top-k) and RL-based addressing to isolate effects.  
- Engineering and empirical methodology are strong, but the gradient approximation's implications (subgradient behavior, stability at selection boundaries) need more rigorous treatment.  
- Experiments include clear speed/memory benchmarks and useful ablations; further ablations on K, write policy, and thresholds are recommended.  
- Complexity analysis is solid but largely relegated to the supplement; stronger placement in main text would help.  
- Clarity is good overall; clarify gradient flow when top-K changes, ANN maintenance/rebuild policy, and sparse delta memory formats.  
- Originality: novel and practical integration of sparse read/write, rollback BPTT, and in-graph ANN addressing; LRU write allocation is an effective inductive bias.  
- Significance: high — SAM moves MANNs toward practical large-scale, long-horizon use and could have substantial impact if validated on bigger real tasks.  
- Sparsity helps generalization by promoting lookup-like algorithms, reducing interference and capacity dilution, stabilizing addressing across scales, and simplifying optimization/credit assignment.  
- Suggested experiments: analyze effect of K/temperature/ANN approximation, compare with exact top-K, test differentiable sparse relaxations, and ablate write policies and interference metrics.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong, practical scalability gains, competitive accuracy, architectural generality, and clear path for future work.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient analysis of gradient approximation from non-differentiable selection and limited large-scale real-world evaluations.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept; reviewer confidence: high.
