- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrain reads/writes to a sparse subset and use ANN indices to overcome dense MANN O(N) bottleneck, claiming O(1) per-step cost and large empirical speed/memory gains.  
- Empirical claims: up to 1,000× speedup and 3,000× memory reduction versus dense baselines on synthetic tasks, Babi QA, and Omniglot.  
- Extends to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) that outperforms prior end-to-end MANNs on Babi tasks.  
- Strength: thorough, convincing experimental validation with scalability to 100k+ memory slots and near-identical data efficiency to dense models.  
- Strength: memory-usage metric empirically supports the O(1) space claim.  
- Strength: efficient backpropagation scheme using sparse rollbacks is technically sound and well-justified.  
- Strength: clear ablation studies (e.g., k-d trees vs. LSH) and practical evaluation on Omniglot showing benefits of larger memory.  
- Weakness: core technical contribution lacks novelty—sparse memory access and sparse addressing have prior art (Zaremba & Sutskever 2015; Graves et al. 2014).  
- Weakness: sparse-write (LRU overwrite) is a minor variant of existing memory-management strategies and ANN integration is standard.  
- Weakness: no theoretical barrier or rigorous optimality proof beyond asymptotic claims; novelty is mainly combining known components.  
- Weakness: limited significance—does not demonstrate new capabilities unattainable by dense MANNs, and gains on real tasks (Babi, Omniglot) are marginal.  
- Weakness: lacks large-scale real-world experiments (e.g., language modeling or translation) to substantiate claimed applicability.  
- Comparison: builds directly on NTM and RL-NTM; sparse access is an extension of content-based addressing and overlaps with LRU-based memory management.  
- Recommendation: Reject—technically solid and well-engineered but insufficiently novel for NIPS; scalability is mainly an engineering advance rather than a new scientific insight.
