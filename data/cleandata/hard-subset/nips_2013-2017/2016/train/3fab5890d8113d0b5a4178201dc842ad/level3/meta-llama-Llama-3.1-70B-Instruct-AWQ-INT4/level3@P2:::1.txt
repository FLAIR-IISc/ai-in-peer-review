This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a neural memory architecture that enables efficient training of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) with large external memories. The authors propose a sparse read and write scheme, which reduces the computational and memory overhead of traditional MANNs, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks. The key innovation is the use of efficient data structures, including approximate nearest neighbor indexes, to enable fast and scalable memory access.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the limitations of traditional MANNs and the benefits of their proposed approach. The technical contributions are sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of their method. The experimental results are also impressive, demonstrating significant speedups and memory reductions compared to traditional MANNs.

However, the reviewer notes that the novelty of the paper is somewhat thin, as related work, such as Memory Networks, already uses large memories with hashing for scalable lookups. Nevertheless, the practical contribution of applying these tools to writeable memories is less explored, and the engineering and practical results are worth reporting.

The reviewer recommends acceptance but requests revisions and clarifications. Specifically, the task descriptions are insufficient, and the authors must provide careful task construction details and release experiment code to ensure replicability. Additionally, the authors should analyze failure cases and limits, discussing when the method fails, and under what task or setup conditions.

The reviewer also notes that the non-smoothness of the argmax/K-argmax operation may have significant effects, particularly when K=1. The authors should discuss these effects and compare their method to fully smooth models and MemNN-WSH behavior.

Furthermore, the reviewer suggests that the authors discuss the implications and limits of training with discrete actions without accounting for discreteness, unlike some prior work.

Finally, the reviewer suggests removing section 3.6, as it appears to be unnecessary, and not referring to Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as this may be misleading.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of MANNs, and with revisions, it has the potential to be a strong and impactful paper.
