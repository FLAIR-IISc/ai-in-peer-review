This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel neural memory architecture that enables efficient training of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) with large external memories. The authors propose a sparse read and write scheme, leveraging fast approximate nearest-neighbors and arg-top-K for attention, to reduce the computational and memory overhead of traditional MANNs.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing MANNs, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks. The proposed SAM architecture is described in detail, including the sparse read and write operations, the controller, and the efficient backpropagation through time. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of SAM, demonstrating its asymptotic optimality.

The experimental results are impressive, showing that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory reductions compared to dense MANNs, while maintaining comparable performance on a range of synthetic and natural tasks. The authors also demonstrate the scalability of SAM by training it on tasks with large external memories and long sequences.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient details to enable replication of the results. However, there are some areas that require clarification and improvement. For example, the task descriptions are insufficient, and the authors should provide more details on the task construction and the experiment setup. Additionally, the authors should analyze the failure cases and limits of SAM, discussing when the method fails and under what conditions.

The novelty of the paper is somewhat thin, as related work, such as Memory Networks, already uses large memories with hashing for scalable lookups. However, the practical contribution of applying these tools to writable memories is less explored, and the engineering and practical results are worth reporting.

The reviewer recommends acceptance but requests revisions and clarifications. Specifically, the authors should:

* Provide more details on the task construction and experiment setup.
* Analyze the failure cases and limits of SAM.
* Discuss the implications and limits of using discrete actions without accounting for discreteness.
* Remove Section 3.6, which is not essential to the paper.
* Avoid referring to Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world," as these terms are misleading.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of MANNs, and the proposed SAM architecture has the potential to enable efficient training of large-scale MANNs. With some revisions and clarifications, the paper can be even stronger and more impactful.
