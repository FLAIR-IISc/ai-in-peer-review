- Addresses critical scalability bottleneck of MANNs/NTM: dense read/write each step causes linear time and prohibitive memory for BPTT.
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that limits interactions to a constant-sized subset of memory slots to enable scaling.
- Uses Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) indexing for reads to retrieve K most similar memory words in O(log N) time.
- Writes are restricted to previously read locations or the least-recently-used slot, reducing write overhead.
- Introduces a backpropagation scheme that rolls back sparse memory changes instead of caching full memory states, cutting per-timestep space to O(1).
- Claims theoretical and empirical asymptotic improvements (time O(log N), space O(1)) with sound supplementary analysis.
- Empirical results across algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot show dramatic speed/memory gains and competitive or improved learning, including training on sequences up to 200k steps.
- Integrates off-the-shelf data structures (ANN) into end-to-end differentiable models, a novel and practical contribution enabling millions of memory slots.
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and figures effectively communicate results.
- Could be more self-contained for non-experts by explaining NTM write mechanism (erase/add matrices) in greater detail.
- Experimental comparisons conflate sparsity benefits with larger logical memory capacity; should include comparisons at equal logical memory sizes.
- The write mechanism (read-or-LRU) is simple and effective but less flexible than content-addressable writes; potential task limitations should be discussed.
- Overall a strong, elegant, and practical advance that meaningfully improves scalability of MANNs, with minor issues in framing and accessibility.
