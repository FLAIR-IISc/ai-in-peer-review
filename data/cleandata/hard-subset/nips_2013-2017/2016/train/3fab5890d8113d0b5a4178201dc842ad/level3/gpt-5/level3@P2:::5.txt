Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes to an external memory. The key idea is to (i) restrict reads to the top-K content-addressed locations found via an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (e.g., randomized k-d trees or LSH), and (ii) restrict writes to a sparse combination of previously read locations and the least-recently used slot (LRU-style allocation). The authors also propose an efficient backpropagation-through-time (BPTT) “rollback” mechanism that stores only the sparse deltas at each step, avoiding O(NT) memory use.

Relative to Neural Turing Machines (NTM) and Memory Networks, SAM preserves end-to-end gradient-based training while achieving asymptotically better time and space complexity per step with respect to the memory size N. The approach is complementary to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) and the authors provide a sparse DNC variant (SDNC). Compared to reinforcement-learning-based sparse addressing (e.g., RL-NTM), SAM avoids high-variance gradient estimators by using a “soft” but sparse attention.

Empirically, SAM matches or exceeds dense MANNs on standard synthetic algorithmic tasks, scales to millions of memory slots, generalizes from training on sequences up to ~10k to inference on ~200k, achieves strong results on bAbI (particularly with SDNC, without supervision of memory retrieval), and performs well on Omniglot one-shot classification with long episodes.

Strengths
- Substantial and well-demonstrated scalability: up to ~1000× runtime and ~3000× memory savings versus dense MANNs, with clear asymptotic bounds and convincing wall-clock data.
- A practical, general recipe: top-K reads via ANN, sparse writes to read and LRU locations, and a BPTT rollback scheme that makes long untruncated sequences feasible.
- Strong empirical coverage: classic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling, very long-sequence generalization, bAbI without access supervision, and Omniglot episodes.
- Useful extension to DNC (SDNC) with strong bAbI results, suggesting generality beyond one architecture.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Differentiability of top-K selection: The read is “sparse-soft” over the selected K items, but the selection itself is non-differentiable and implemented via an ANN index. This induces a biased gradient relative to dense soft attention (especially near index changes). The paper should discuss this approximation more explicitly, characterize stability (e.g., with varying K or temperature), and, if possible, quantify any degradation vs. exact top-K computed from the dense similarity.
- Optimality claims hinge on assumptions: The “asymptotically optimal” statement depends on constant K, amortized ANN costs, and occasional index rebuilds. Bringing a succinct version of the assumptions and core argument from the supplement into the main text would strengthen the claim.
- Limited evaluation on large real-world tasks: Results are compelling but still mostly synthetic or small-scale (bAbI, Omniglot). Demonstrating benefits on a realistic high-throughput task (e.g., long-context language modeling or large QA without supervision) would further cement significance.
- Comparison breadth: It would be helpful to include baselines that pursue sparsity by other means (e.g., low-temperature dense attention, sparsemax/top-k softmax relaxations, Gumbel-top-k) or RL-based discrete addressing, to disentangle the effect of the specific write policy (read+LRU) from sparsity per se.

Quality
- Soundness: The engineering is strong and the empirical evidence is thorough for the stated domains. The gradient approximation via top-K selection is a reasonable heuristic, but a more explicit treatment of its implications (subgradient behavior, stability near selection boundaries) would improve rigor.
- Experiments: Clear speed/memory benchmarks; ablations on SAM vs. dense (DAM) and exact vs. ANN are appreciated. Additional ablations on K, write policy, and usage thresholds would be valuable.
- Theory: The complexity analysis appears solid but is mostly in the supplement.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organizes the system cleanly (read, write, controller, BPTT). Consider clarifying: (i) how gradients flow through the read weights when the top-K set changes, (ii) ANN maintenance costs and rebuild policy, and (iii) exact memory formats used for sparse deltas.

Originality
- The integration of sparse read/write, efficient BPTT rollback, and in-graph ANN for content addressing is a novel and practical combination. The LRU write allocation is a simple, effective inductive bias that pairs well with sparse reads.

Significance
- High. SAM makes a credible step toward making MANNs practical at large scale and long horizons, a long-standing barrier in the field. If borne out on larger real-world tasks, the impact would be substantial.

Why do sparsity and SAM help curriculum/long-range generalization?
- Inductive bias toward lookup-like algorithms: Top-K reads and LRU writes reduce interference and encourage discrete, key–value behaviors, making learned solutions more algorithmic and length-invariant.
- Reduced crosstalk and capacity dilution: Sparse writes limit overwriting and promote locality of updates; analogous to sparse autoencoders, sparsity yields more selective representations and less catastrophic interference.
- Stable addressing across scales: With large memories and consistent K, the controller’s addressing policy need not change qualitatively as sequences lengthen, easing extrapolation under curriculum.
- Optimization benefits: Sparser gradients and fewer competing memory targets can simplify credit assignment across very long BPTT windows.

Suggestions for the authors
- Provide analysis/experiments quantifying the effect of K, temperature, and ANN approximation on stability and generalization; compare with exact top-K where feasible.
- Explore differentiable sparse selection relaxations (e.g., k-sparsemax, Gumbel-top-k) as alternatives to hard selection.
- Ablate the write policy: previous-reads vs. LRU vs. content-based sparse writes; report interference metrics.
- Demonstrate performance on a larger real-world task with long contexts (e.g., long-form language modeling or document QA).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, practical contribution that materially advances scalability of MANNs.
- Clear empirical speed/memory wins with competitive or improved accuracy.
- Broad applicability (NTM, DNC) and open path to further research.

Cons for acceptance
- Gradient approximation via non-differentiable selection not fully analyzed.
- Limited evaluation on large-scale real-world datasets.

Recommendation: Strong accept.
Confidence: High.
