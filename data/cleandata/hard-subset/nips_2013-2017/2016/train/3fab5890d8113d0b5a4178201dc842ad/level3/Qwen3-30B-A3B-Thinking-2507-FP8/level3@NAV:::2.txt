

The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that claims to achieve 1,000× faster training and 3,000× reduced memory usage by constraining reads/writes to sparse subsets and using approximate nearest neighbors (ANN). It positions SAM as a scalable solution to MANNs’ linear space/time complexity issues, demonstrating improvements on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall) and Omniglot classification, with a Sparse DNC variant achieving state-of-the-art Babi results.  

**Strengths**: The experimental validation is thorough, with compelling scalability results (e.g., 1M memory words in sublinear time) and strong performance on benchmark tasks. The implementation details for sparse backpropagation (tracking only modified memory slots) and ANN integration (k-d trees/LSH) are clearly presented. The empirical claims of speed/memory efficiency are well-supported by Figure 1 and Supplementary E.  

**Weaknesses**: The core technical contribution lacks novelty. The sparse read/write mechanism—retaining only top-K nearest neighbors and using a least-recently-used policy for writes—is a direct adaptation of established systems techniques (e.g., cache management) and closely parallels Zaremba et al.’s reinforcement learning-based sparse addressing (NIPS 2015). The ANN integration (FLANN/LSH) is standard in ML for nearest-neighbor search, not a novel contribution. Crucially, the paper fails to demonstrate *why* these techniques, when applied to MANNs, represent a *new* theoretical or algorithmic advance beyond prior work. The claim of "asymptotic optimality" (Supplementary A) is unsubstantiated without rigorous complexity analysis comparing to all relevant baselines.  

**Originality/Significance**: While the results are impressive, the work is incremental. It combines existing techniques (sparse approximations, ANN) without introducing a fundamentally new approach. This is not a novel combination but an application of known methods to a specific problem. Prior NIPS work (e.g., Zaremba 2015, Graves et al. 2016) already explored sparse memory access, and SAM does not significantly advance the state of the art beyond those. The significance is limited to empirical scaling gains, not methodological innovation.  

**Verdict**: The paper is technically sound with strong experiments but fails to meet NIPS’s originality threshold. The technical components are straightforward adaptations, not novel contributions. While the results are interesting, they do not outweigh the lack of novelty. **Reject**.
