This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) designed to address scalability issues related to both space and time complexity. The core idea is to limit read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, significantly reducing computational overhead and memory requirements without sacrificing representational power. The authors demonstrate substantial speedups and memory savings compared to standard NTM implementations, while achieving comparable performance on several benchmark tasks, including synthetic algorithmic problems, Babi reasoning tasks, and one-shot Omniglot character recognition. They also showcase the adaptability of their approach by applying it to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).

The paper builds upon a growing body of work in memory-augmented neural networks, including Neural Turing Machines [7] and Memory Networks [21]. These models aim to overcome the limitations of recurrent neural networks like LSTMs [11] by decoupling memory capacity from the number of model parameters. While promising, existing MANNs have been hindered by their computational cost, particularly when dealing with large memories. Recent NIPS conferences have featured numerous contributions in this space, focusing on improving memory access mechanisms and addressing scalability challenges. This work directly addresses a critical bottleneck in the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** The scalability of MANNs is a major obstacle to their wider adoption. SAM offers a compelling solution by dramatically reducing computational and memory demands.
* **Strong empirical results:** The paper presents convincing evidence that SAM achieves substantial speedups (1,000x faster, 3,000x less memory) while maintaining competitive performance on a range of tasks. The scaling experiments, particularly with the curriculum learning approach, are impressive.
* **Clear and well-structured:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical details are presented in a clear and accessible manner, and the figures and tables are informative. The supplementary material further enhances clarity.
* **Generality of the approach:** The successful adaptation of SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer demonstrates its broader applicability beyond the NTM framework.
* **Performance near theoretical bounds:** The authors convincingly argue that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity and demonstrate results that align with these theoretical expectations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited background:** While the paper mentions memory-augmented neural networks, a slightly more comprehensive background section, briefly outlining the key concepts and challenges in the field, would make it more self-contained for readers less familiar with the area. A more detailed explanation of the write operator (Equation 3) and the associated weight matrices could also be beneficial.
* **Fairness of comparison (Figure 4):** The comparison in Figure 4, which matches physical memory sizes, raises a valid concern. While a reasonable choice, comparing models using the same *logical* memory size, rather than physical memory, might provide a more insightful comparison of algorithmic efficiency. This would isolate the benefits of the sparse access mechanism from potential differences in memory allocation strategies.
* **Lack of ablation studies:** While the authors present results with different ANN implementations, further ablation studies investigating the contribution of each component of SAM (e.g., sparse reads vs. sparse writes, different usage definitions) could provide a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM algorithm effectively addresses the scalability challenges that have hampered the practical application of MANNs. The strong empirical results, clear writing, and demonstrated generality of the approach make this a valuable addition to the NIPS program.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Solves a critical problem hindering the progress of MANNs.
* Demonstrates substantial improvements in speed and memory efficiency.
* Achieves competitive performance on a variety of tasks.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Demonstrates a novel and effective algorithmic approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Minor limitations in background information and comparison methodology.
* Lack of extensive ablation studies.

**Recommendation:**

Strongly Accept. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work is novel, technically sound, and likely to have a significant impact on the field.
