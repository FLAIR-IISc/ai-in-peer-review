This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that restricts reads and writes to a fixed, small subset of memory locations. Building on Neural Turing Machines and Memory Networks, and connecting to the Differentiable Neural Computer via a sparse variant (SDNC), the authors combine sparse addressing with approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search to achieve O(log N) access and O(1) per-step memory during BPTT via reversible sparse updates. The main claim is asymptotic optimality under content-based addressing, supported by a theoretical argument, and substantial empirical speed/memory gains. On standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), SAM matches or improves data efficiency; on large-scale curricula it trains over sequences of thousands of steps; it performs competitively on bAbI and strongly on Omniglot one-shot episodes. Benchmarks use Torch7 and report up to 1000× speed and 3000× memory reductions over dense NTMs.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and carefully engineered (sparse reads/writes, usage-based allocation, reversible memory for BPTT). The empirical evaluation is extensive and aligns with the claims. However, the “optimality” result would benefit from a clearer theorem statement and explicit assumptions (e.g., model of content-based access, lower bounds) in the main text. The impact of ANN approximation on gradients, stability, and task performance is not fully quantified.

Clarity: The paper is well written, with assumptions clearly stated and architectural details available in the appendix. Consider surfacing key algorithmic choices (usage metric, K, ANN type) and providing a compact pseudocode summary in the main text.

Originality: Constraining access to a constant-size subset with end-to-end differentiability and efficient BPTT rollback is a simple yet novel combination that advances scalable MANNs beyond prior NTMs, Memory Networks, and RL-based discrete addressing.

Significance: The speed/memory gains are substantial and likely to broaden the applicability of MANNs. The evidence that sparsity can improve early-stage learning on some tasks is intriguing.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel idea with strong theoretical motivation and practical engineering.
- Compelling speed/memory improvements and ability to scale to very large memories.
- Broad evaluation across synthetic, QA (bAbI), and one-shot learning (Omniglot).
- Generalizable to other MANNs (SDNC).

Cons for acceptance:
- Optimality claim needs a precise, centralized statement and discussion of assumptions.
- Limited analysis of when/why sparsity aids learning; sensitivity to K, ANN choice, and thresholding.
- Comparisons sometimes mix memory sizes; include ablations controlling for capacity.

Suggestions: Elaborate on regimes where sparsity helps or hurts learning (task structure, noise, curriculum), explore alternative ANN integrations (e.g., LSH forests, online-balanced trees), and provide ablations on K and usage heuristics. Releasing code would enhance reproducibility.
