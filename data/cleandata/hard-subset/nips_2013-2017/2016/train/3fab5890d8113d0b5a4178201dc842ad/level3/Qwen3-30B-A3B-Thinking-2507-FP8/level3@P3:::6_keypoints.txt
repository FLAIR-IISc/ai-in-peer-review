- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a method that scales memory-augmented neural networks by performing sparse reads/writes to external memory.  
- Restricts access to a small subset of memory words (K=4–8) and uses efficient data structures for content-based addressing.  
- Achieves asymptotically optimal complexity: O(log N) time for reads and O(1) space per time step (theoretical analysis provided).  
- Implements approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexing to support scalable content-based addressing.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), Babi reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Scales to very large settings (100k+ time steps, up to 1M words), with reported ~1,000× faster training and ~3,000× less memory than dense models.  
- Provides thorough ablation studies and comparisons to dense variants (DAM), NTM, and LSTM, showing comparable or superior performance.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-structured, and clearly written with precise mathematical formulations and systematic validation.  
- Original contribution: achieves differentiable sparsity for MANNs (contrast to RL-based discrete addressing) and introduces a Sparse DNC (SDNC) variant.  
- Significant practical impact: removes a major scalability bottleneck in MANNs and enables real-world large-memory applications.  
- Strengths: solves scalability issue, strong theory+empirics, clear presentation, and applicability across multiple tasks.  
- Weaknesses: Figure 4 comparison may be unfair (physical vs logical memory matching), background on MANNs could be expanded, and trade-offs between ANN implementations (k-d trees vs LSH) are not deeply discussed.  
- Recommendation: Accept — substantial, well-supported contribution with clear potential for broader application.
