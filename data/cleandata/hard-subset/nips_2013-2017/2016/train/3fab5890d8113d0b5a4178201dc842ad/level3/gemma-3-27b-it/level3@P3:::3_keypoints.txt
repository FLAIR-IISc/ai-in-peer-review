- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), restricting reads/writes to a subset of memory to improve MANN scalability.  
- Uses efficient data structures and approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search to reduce computation and memory overhead.  
- Provides theoretical justification showing SAM is optimal in space and time complexity.  
- Empirically validates SAM on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and on the real-world Omniglot dataset.  
- Demonstrates comparable or improved performance to NTMs, especially with much larger memories.  
- Reports substantial practical speedups (e.g., ~1000x faster) and large memory savings (~3000x less memory).  
- Shows generality by applying SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).  
- Novelty: sparse memory access and integrating ANN techniques within neural memory is a creative and effective idea.  
- Presentation: paper is well-written, clearly organized, with helpful appendix, figures, and tables.  
- Limited analysis of when and why sparsity benefits learning; more investigation into conditions for improvement is needed.  
- ANN integration is primarily for speed; deeper integration or joint learning of ANN parameters is not explored.  
- Results show sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., discount factor); a fuller sensitivity analysis is lacking.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a key scalability problem, is novel/theoretically sound, empirically strong, and well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete exploration of sparsity’s theoretical impact on learning dynamics and limited analysis of its benefits.  
- Overall: strong, valuable contribution that convincingly improves MANN scalability; recommendation for acceptance.
