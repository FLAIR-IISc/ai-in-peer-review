- Paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse read/write to memory plus approximate nearest neighbors (ANN) to achieve claimed 1,000× faster training and 3,000× less memory.  
- Demonstrates scalability (e.g., 1M memory words in sublinear time) and strong empirical results on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall), Omniglot, and a Sparse DNC achieving SOTA on bAbI.  
- Experimental validation is thorough with clear implementation details for sparse backpropagation (tracking only modified slots) and ANN integration (k-d trees/LSH, FLANN).  
- Empirical efficiency and speed/memory claims are well-supported by Figure 1 and Supplementary E.  
- Core technical contribution lacks novelty; sparse top-K reads/writes and LRU-style writes are adaptations of established systems/cache techniques.  
- SAM closely parallels prior sparse-addressing MANN work (e.g., Zaremba et al. NIPS 2015) rather than offering a new algorithmic insight.  
- ANN integration is standard practice (FLANN/LSH) and not a novel methodological contribution.  
- Claim of "asymptotic optimality" (Supplementary A) is unsubstantiated without rigorous complexity comparisons to relevant baselines.  
- Overall significance is incremental: impressive empirical scaling but no fundamental methodological advance over prior work.  
- Verdict: technically sound with strong experiments but fails NIPS originality threshold — recommended reject.
