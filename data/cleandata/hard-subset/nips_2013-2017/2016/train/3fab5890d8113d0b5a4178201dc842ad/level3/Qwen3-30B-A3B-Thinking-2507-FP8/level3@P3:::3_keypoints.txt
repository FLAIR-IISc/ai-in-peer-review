- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), limiting reads/writes to a fixed-size subset to scale MANNs while preserving differentiability.
- Provides theoretical justification that SAM is asymptotically optimal in time and space complexity.
- Shows up to 1,000× faster training and 3,000× less physical memory than standard NTM implementations.
- Demonstrates SAM maintains comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort).
- Validates real-world applicability with Omniglot one-shot classification and state-of-the-art results on Babi QA without supervised memory access.
- Section 4.1 benchmarks highlight dramatic speed and memory advantages.
- Section 4.2 finds sparse models sometimes learn more effectively than dense models (associative recall, priority sort).
- Section 4.3 curriculum experiments scale to tasks with 100,000+ time steps and memory words, infeasible for dense MANNs.
- Strength — Quality: technically sound, strong theory, thorough experiments, and careful baseline comparisons.
- Strength — Clarity: well-written with comprehensive appendix and clear figures enabling reproducibility.
- Strength — Originality: novel sparse access approach that advances scaling of MANNs beyond prior NTM work.
- Strength — Significance: addresses a critical scalability bottleneck with clear practical impact for sequence learning tasks.
- Weakness: Lacks deeper analysis explaining why sparsity improves learning on certain tasks.
- Weakness: Limited discussion on when to choose different ANN implementations (k-d trees vs LSH) and guidance for practitioners.
- Weakness: Mentions exploring other ANN integrations with SAM but does not detail potential future directions.
- Recommendation: Strong accept — strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; important, well-executed contribution.
