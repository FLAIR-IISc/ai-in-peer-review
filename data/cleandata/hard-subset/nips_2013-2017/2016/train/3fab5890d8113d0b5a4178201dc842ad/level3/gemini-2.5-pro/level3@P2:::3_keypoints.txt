- Addresses MANN scalability by introducing Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to avoid O(N) per-step memory access.
- SAM reads/writes only K words per timestep; uses an ANN index for content reads to reduce search to O(log N).
- Writes target previously read locations or the least-recently-used slot to maintain sparsity.
- Introduces an efficient backpropagation "rollback" that reverts sparse updates instead of caching full memory.
- Empirical results show massive speedups and memory reductions (e.g., 1000x speed, 3000x memory) while matching or exceeding dense models.
- Demonstrates training on very long sequences (up to 200,000 steps) and strong performance across algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot.
- Theoretical asymptotic optimality in time and space is well-supported and validated experimentally.
- Compares against NTM and a dense variant (DAM) to isolate effects of sparse access; experiments are comprehensive and convincing.
- Paper is technically sound, carefully designed, and includes a thoughtful architecture and implementation (e.g., sparse rollback).
- Exceptionally clear writing and organization; key details and proofs are relegated to the appendix for readability.
- Highly original: novel integration of ANN indices into end-to-end differentiable memory systems to enforce sparsity.
- Significant impact: makes MANNs practical for large-scale applications and can generalize to other architectures like DNC.
- Interesting empirical finding that sparsity can improve learning on some tasks; calls for deeper analysis of this effect.
- Explores k-d trees and LSH for ANN; suggests investigating other online-friendly indices (e.g., Mondrian forests) to avoid rebalancing costs.
- Strong case for acceptance: addresses a critical bottleneck, offers an elegant principled solution, and provides robust empirical validation.
- No major objections; only minor recommendations to analyze why sparsity helps learning and broaden discussion of ANN choices.
