This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme designed to improve the efficiency of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs). The authors claim that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory efficiency compared to existing models, while retaining the representational power of the original approaches.

The main idea of SAM is to constrain all writes to and reads from external memory to a sparse subset of the memory words. This is achieved through a combination of sparse read and write operations, as well as an efficient memory management scheme that tracks memory usage and finds unused blocks of memory for recording new information.

The authors provide an extensive evaluation of SAM on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, question answering, and one-shot learning. The results show that SAM achieves comparable performance to existing models, while significantly reducing the computational and memory overhead.

However, upon reviewing the paper, I found the technical contribution to be insufficient for acceptance. The main technical components, such as efficient backpropagation and approximate nearest neighbor via sparse access, are viewed as straightforward and not particularly novel. While the experiments are interesting, they do not outweigh the perceived lack of technical novelty.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the SAM architecture and its components, as well as a detailed evaluation of its performance. However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the theoretical implications of SAM and its potential limitations.

In terms of originality, the paper builds upon existing work on MANNs and introduces a novel memory access scheme. However, the idea of using sparse access to improve efficiency is not entirely new, and the authors could have provided more context and comparison to existing work in this area.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of neural networks, namely the scalability of MANNs. The results show that SAM can achieve significant speedups and memory efficiency, making it a promising approach for large-scale applications.

Overall, while the paper is well-written and provides interesting results, I believe that the technical contribution is insufficient for acceptance. The authors could benefit from providing more context and comparison to existing work, as well as a more in-depth analysis of the theoretical implications of SAM.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of neural networks.
* The results show that SAM can achieve significant speedups and memory efficiency.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The technical contribution is insufficient.
* The main technical components are viewed as straightforward and not particularly novel.
* The paper could benefit from more context and comparison to existing work.
