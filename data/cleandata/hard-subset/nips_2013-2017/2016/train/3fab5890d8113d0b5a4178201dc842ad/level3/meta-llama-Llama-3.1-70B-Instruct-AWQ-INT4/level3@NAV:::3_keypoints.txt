- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a sparse read/write scheme for memory-augmented neural networks that limits operations to a fixed-size subset of memory words.
- Builds on prior work (NTM, DNC) to enable learning beyond LSTM limits using external memory.
- Provides theoretical justification and claims optimality of the sparse access methodology.
- Paper is well-written with clear assumptions and detailed architecture descriptions in the appendix.
- Experiments include speed and memory benchmarks, learning with sparse access, and curriculum scaling.
- Empirical results show significant speedups over the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while maintaining comparable data efficiency and performance.
- Demonstrates scalability to large external memories with up to three orders of magnitude improvement in runtime and memory overhead versus vanilla NTMs.
- Shows generality by outlining how to construct a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).
- Main limitation: idea is straightforward and lacks detailed analysis of when sparsity improves learning.
- Additional limitation: does not explore integrations with other approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) methods or broader ANN variants.
- Suggests the paper could benefit from more experiments to further validate effectiveness in varied settings.
- Overall assessment: significant, technically sound contribution with strong clarity, originality, and significance (scores mostly 9/10).
