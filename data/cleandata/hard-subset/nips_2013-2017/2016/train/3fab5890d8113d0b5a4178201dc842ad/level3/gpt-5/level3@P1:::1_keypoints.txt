- Paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), enforcing sparsity in reads/writes and using ANN indices for sublinear content addressing.  
- Introduces a rollback scheme storing sparse diffs for O(1) per-step BPTT space and O(log N) access time with kd-trees/LSH.  
- Empirically matches dense NTMs/DNC-like models on synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot one-shot while enabling much larger memories and longer sequences.  
- Presents a sparse DNC variant (SDNC) that performs strongly on bAbI without supervised memory access.  
- Builds on NTMs/DNCs and Memory Networks, connecting to prior hashed/candidate-restricted attention work but extends to scalable writes and end-to-end training.  
- Engineering contribution: integration of ANN structures inside the network to enable practical scaling while retaining content-based semantics.  
- Technical design is careful; time/space analysis and rollback approach are convincing with major wall-clock and memory savings reported.  
- Several performance claims depend on implementation choices (ANN type, rebuild policy, K, ε); deeper ablations and failure-mode analysis are needed.  
- Theoretical “optimal” claims require clearer assumptions and scope to strengthen validity.  
- Paper is overall clear, but some descriptions (task generation, ANN parameters, usage-tracking) are too terse for reproduction.  
- Distinction between dense (DAM) and sparse (SAM) is clear, but choices like K, ε, and kd-tree vs LSH need more justification.  
- Novelty is incremental: top-K attention, ANN retrieval, and sparse writes are known individually; novelty lies in making them work together in a writable differentiable memory.  
- Significant practical impact: scales MANNs to millions of slots and long time horizons; SDNC bAbI result notable.  
- Strengths: principled sparsification, practical ANN integration and bookkeeping, O(1) BPTT memory via rollback, strong benchmarks, DNC applicability.  
- Weaknesses: limited conceptual novelty, under-discussed non-smoothness of top-K selection, sparse ablations, replicability concerns, and limited failure-mode analysis.  
- Clarification requested on discreteness/smoothness: effects of arg top-K on gradients, stability vs dense softmax, and comparison to differentiable sparse alternatives (e.g., sparsemax).  
- Request direct comparison to MemNN-WSH/candidate-restricted attention and explanation of softmax within top-K and handling of ANN-induced bias.  
- Ask for ablations on K, ε, ANN type, rebuild frequency, usage/overwrite policy, and breakdown of speedup sources (reads, writes, rollback, ANN vs scan, CPU/GPU).  
- Request analysis of failure modes and regimes where SAM/SDNC underperform, with specific bAbI task breakdown and causes.  
- Reproducibility requests: full task construction, seeds, curricula, model/optimizer/hyperparameters, ANN libraries/settings, rebuild policies, training budgets, and release of core code.  
- Ask to state assumptions behind “asymptotically optimal” time/space bounds and clarify covered model classes.  
- Editing suggestions: consider removing Section 3.6 and avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong practical scalability contribution, careful systems design, clear analysis, and impressive empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited conceptual novelty, insufficient discussion of discrete top-K consequences, and reproducibility/ablation gaps.  
- Recommendation: Accept pending revisions to address smoothness/discreteness, ablations, failure analyses, and reproducibility details.
