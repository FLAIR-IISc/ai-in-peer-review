- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) using sparse read/write and approximate nearest neighbor search to scale memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Claims large computational gains: up to ~1000x speedups and ~3000x memory reduction vs. dense MANNs.  
- Demonstrates comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot, and adapts SAM to DNC for improved speed/performance.  
- Practical engineering contribution: combines sparse operations with efficient data structures to enable training with much larger memories and longer sequences.  
- Generalizable approach: authors successfully apply SAM ideas beyond the proposed architecture (e.g., to DNC).  
- Empirical results are strong, suggesting sparse approximations do not significantly hurt learning and can help.  
- Explanations of SAM architecture and optimizations are relatively clear and well presented.  
- Limited novelty: components (sparsity, ANN) are known; main contribution is their combination within differentiable, writable MANNs.  
- Replicability concerns: experimental details (Babi, Omniglot) are insufficient and code release is necessary for reproduction.  
- Lacks analysis of failure cases and limitations — should describe when SAM fails or performs poorly.  
- Discreteness concerns: argmax/arg-top-K introduce non-smooth behavior (especially K=1) and need discussion/comparison to fully smooth models.  
- Section 3.6 (ANN implementations) feels tangential and should be condensed or removed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important scalability problem with strong empirical and engineering results and demonstrated generality.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, insufficient experimental detail, and missing analysis of limitations.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions — provide detailed task descriptions and code, discuss limitations/discrete operations, condense Section 3.6, and avoid calling Omniglot "real-world."
