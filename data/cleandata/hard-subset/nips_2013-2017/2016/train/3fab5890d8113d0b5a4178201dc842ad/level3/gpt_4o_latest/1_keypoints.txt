- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a scalable neural memory using ANN structures and sparse read/write to handle millions of slots and thousands of steps while matching dense-model performance.
- Scalability and efficiency: proves asymptotic space/time improvements with empirical up to 1,600x speedups and 3,000x memory savings vs NTMs.
- Practical contribution: applies sparse approximations and ANN lookups to writable memory—an impactful, well-engineered adaptation of existing techniques.
- Empirical validation: thorough experiments (synthetic tasks, curriculum learning, Babi, Omniglot) consistently support scalability and performance claims.
- Limited novelty: core techniques (sparsity, ANN lookups) are incremental and have prior exploration in memory networks.
- Insufficient task descriptions: experimental setups and dataset details are not described clearly enough.
- Missing failure-case analysis: lacks in-depth discussion of limitations and effects of discrete actions or sparsity thresholds on training/performance.
- Mislabeling data: calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world” is misleading given its controlled/artificial nature.
- Recommendation: accept—meaningful contribution with strong empirical evidence.
- Suggested improvements: expand task/dataset descriptions, release code for reproducibility, analyze failure cases/limitations, and consider removing low-value Section 3.6.
