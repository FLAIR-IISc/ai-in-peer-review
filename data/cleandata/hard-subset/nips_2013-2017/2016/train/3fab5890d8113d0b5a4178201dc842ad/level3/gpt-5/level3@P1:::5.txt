This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that performs sparse reads and writes to an external memory while remaining trainable end-to-end with backpropagation through time. The core idea is to restrict both reading and writing to a constant number K of memory slots per time step, using approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search for content-based reads and a simple, effective write policy that targets previously read locations and the least recently used slot. Together with an efficient “rollback” scheme for BPTT that stores only sparse deltas rather than full memories, SAM achieves O(log N) time for reads, O(1) time and space per time step for writes and gradient storage, and O(N) initialization, with N the number of memory slots. The authors further adapt the approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), yielding an SDNC that is dramatically faster than the dense baseline.

Relative to prior work: NTMs (Graves et al., 2014) and DNCs (Graves et al., 2016) provide powerful content-addressable, differentiable memory but scale linearly in N per step and require storing full memories for BPTT, which is prohibitive. Memory Networks (Weston et al., Sukhbaatar et al.) use content reads without writes or with limited writes, and often rely on supervision for support retrieval. RL-based discrete addressing (Zaremba & Sutskever) achieves sparsity but at the cost of high-variance optimization. SAM retains differentiability and content-based access while attaining optimal asymptotics under reasonable assumptions, using standard ANN structures (randomized k-d trees, LSH) inside the network as a non-parameterized index.

Empirically, SAM is up to 1000x faster and 3000x more memory-efficient than dense NTMs at large N, matches or exceeds dense variants (NTM, a dense approximation DAM) on classic NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), scales through an exponentially expanding curriculum to very long sequences and large memories (millions of slots), generalizes far beyond training lengths (trained up to ~10k, tested at ~200k), attains strong bAbI results (SDNC solves all but one task without supervision), and performs best among compared MANNs on an Omniglot one-shot classification curriculum.

Quality
- Soundness: The algorithmic components are well-motivated and carefully engineered: top-K sparsification of attention, LRU-based memory allocation, approximate KNN for content reads, and sparse rollback for BPTT. The asymptotic claims are plausible and supported by measured scaling; formal lower bounds rely on assumptions about content-addressed memory that are reasonable but could be more explicitly stated in the main text.
- Empirics: Benchmarks are compelling, with large margins in wall-clock and memory footprint, and competitive accuracy on synthetic and semi-natural tasks. Some ablations are missing: sensitivity to K, to the choice/quality of ANN (recall vs speed trade-off), and to the LRU write policy versus alternatives (e.g., pure content-based sparse writes).
- Reproducibility: The paper describes the necessary components, but details (hyperparameters, ANN tuning, curriculum schedule) are largely in the supplement. Reporting ANN query recall and collision statistics would further clarify the accuracy/runtime trade-off during training.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The read/write equations and controller interface are easy to follow. The description of the sparse BPTT rollback is a strength; a short algorithmic box in the main text would help. To aid practitioners, include a brief recipe for choosing K, the threshold epsilon for usage, and when to rebuild the ANN index.

Originality
- The building blocks (sparse attention, ANN, LRU memory management) are known, but integrating them into an end-to-end differentiable MANN with optimal asymptotics and a practical sparse BPTT scheme is novel and impactful. The SDNC adaptation further supports generality.

Significance
- High. The results substantially advance the practical scalability of differentiable memory, enabling tasks with hundreds of thousands of steps and very large memories. This is likely to unlock broader application (e.g., long-context reasoning, retrieval-augmented generation) and stimulate follow-on work.

Why does SAM help curriculum learning and long-range generalization, beyond efficiency?
- Reduced interference via sparse reads/writes: Dense content attention diffuses information and gradients across all slots, causing interference and overwriting that grow with N and T. Top-K attention and LRU allocation enforce locality and orthogonality of memories, akin to sparse autoencoders encouraging selective, non-overlapping codes. This structural sparsity regularizes the controller to form discrete, algorithm-like read–write routines that are length-invariant.
- Cleaner credit assignment: With only K slots touched per step, gradients propagate along a narrow path through time and memory, reducing variance relative to dense attentional “smearing.” This yields more stable learning under curricula and supports extrapolation to longer sequences.
- Pointer-based computation: Writing back to previously read locations and using LRU for new allocation induces pointer-chasing dynamics rather than global content blending. Such pointer-algorithms naturally generalize with sequence length because the access pattern depends on state transitions, not absolute time.
- ANN-induced locality bias: Approximate KNN restricts reads to the most similar candidates, implicitly enforcing a winner-take-most behavior that curbs spurious matches as memory grows. This combats the “softmax denominator” effect in dense attention where the tail of irrelevant slots collectively corrupts reads.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- ANN non-differentiability: While the index is treated as a fixed data structure, the top-K selection is non-smooth. Empirically it works; still, reporting sensitivity to ANN recall and comparing exact vs approximate KNN would strengthen the case.
- Fairness of comparisons: Some accuracy comparisons pit SAM with millions of slots against dense baselines with 64 slots (to match physical memory). This demonstrates scalability, but it would help to also compare at matched effective memory sizes to disentangle algorithmic benefits from sheer capacity.
- Limited real-world evaluations: bAbI and Omniglot are helpful but small. Even a small-scale language modeling or QA benchmark with long contexts (without supervision) would broaden the impact.
- Analysis of errors and capacity: Provide measurements of collision rates in memory, utilization histograms, and how often LRU overwrites useful content.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, principled advance in scalable differentiable memory with clear asymptotic and empirical gains.
- Broad applicability; adaptation to DNC shows generality.
- Convincing demonstrations of long-horizon training and remarkable length generalization.
- Clear presentation and useful engineering insights (sparse rollback, memory management).

Cons against acceptance
- Limited ablations on K, ANN quality, and write-policy choices.
- Few real-world benchmarks; reliance on synthetic tasks.
- Theoretical bounds and assumptions largely deferred to the supplement.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to K and to the ANN recall (e.g., 70% vs 95%)? Does higher ANN accuracy materially affect learning outcomes?
- Have you tried purely content-based sparse writes or hybrid write policies, and how do they compare to the LRU+previously-read scheme?
- Can you report memory utilization and overwrite statistics during training to validate the LRU mechanism?
- How does SAM perform when trained with truncated BPTT versus full BPTT at long horizons?
