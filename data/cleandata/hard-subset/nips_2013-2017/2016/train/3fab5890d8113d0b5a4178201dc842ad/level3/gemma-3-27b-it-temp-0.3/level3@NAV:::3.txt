## Review of “Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes”

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme designed to address the scalability limitations of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). Building on the established framework of differentiable MANNs, SAM constrains read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, significantly reducing computational and memory overhead. This work directly tackles a critical bottleneck in the field, as MANNs, while promising for complex sequential tasks, have struggled with practical application due to their scaling issues. 

The authors provide a strong theoretical justification for SAM, claiming optimality in space and time complexity. Empirically, they demonstrate substantial speedups (up to 1000x) and reduced memory consumption (3000x) compared to NTMs. Evaluation on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) shows comparable or improved performance, particularly for associative recall and priority sort where combined costs are lower. The inclusion of a real-world example using the Omniglot dataset and the availability of Torch7 implementations further strengthens the work. Notably, the authors also demonstrate the adaptability of SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), showcasing its broader applicability.

The core idea of sparsity is straightforward yet impactful, and the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the architectural details are thoroughly documented in the appendix. The authors are careful to evaluate both the benefits and potential drawbacks of their approach. The use of approximate nearest neighbor search to further optimize performance is a clever addition. 

However, while the results are promising, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *when* sparsity is most advantageous. The observation that sparse reads/writes can improve early-stage learning is intriguing, but further investigation into the underlying mechanisms and task characteristics that drive this effect would be valuable. Additionally, exploring other ANN integrations beyond the DNC, and perhaps investigating different ANN architectures tailored specifically for SAM, could further broaden the impact of this work. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective approach to scaling MANNs.
*   Strong theoretical justification and empirical validation.
*   Clear writing and thorough documentation.
*   Significant speedups and memory savings compared to existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of conditions under which sparsity is most beneficial.
*   Potential for further exploration of ANN integrations.

**Overall:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM scheme is a compelling solution to a critical scalability problem, and the results are both theoretically sound and empirically supported. I recommend acceptance.
