This paper introduces an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which retains the representational power of the original Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while allowing for efficient training with very large memories. The authors propose a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks by introducing sparsity in the read and write operations, making it possible to train models with millions of memory words.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem is effectively addressed. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method, including the sparse read and write operations, the memory management scheme, and the efficient backpropagation through time algorithm. The experimental results demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant speedups and memory savings compared to the original NTM and other baseline models.

One of the strengths of the paper is its technical soundness. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of the proposed method, showing that it achieves asymptotic lower bounds. The experimental results are also well-supported, with a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on a range of synthetic and natural tasks.

Another strength of the paper is its originality. The proposed method is a novel combination of familiar techniques, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it differs from previous contributions. The paper also provides a thorough review of related work, including the NTM and Memory Networks.

The significance of the paper is also high. The proposed method has the potential to impact a wide range of applications, including language modeling and machine translation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on several tasks, including one-shot Omniglot character recognition and question answering on the Babi tasks.

One potential weakness of the paper is the limited evaluation of the proposed method on real-world tasks. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on several synthetic and natural tasks, it would be beneficial to see more extensive evaluation on real-world tasks.

Another potential weakness is the lack of comparison to other state-of-the-art methods. While the authors compare the proposed method to the original NTM and other baseline models, it would be beneficial to see a comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, such as the Differentiable Neural Computer.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method. The technical soundness, originality, and significance of the paper make it a strong contribution to the field.
