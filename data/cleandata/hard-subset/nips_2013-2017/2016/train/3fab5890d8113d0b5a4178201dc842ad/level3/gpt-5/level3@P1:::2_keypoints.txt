- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): replace dense reads/writes with top-K sparse access and an ANN index, plus sparse change logs to rollback for BPTT.  
- Reads use top-K nearest memory slots via ANN; writes target previously read slots or an LRU slot determined by usage.  
- Claims O(log N) per-step time (via ANN) and O(1) BPTT memory per step (via sparse updates), retaining end-to-end differentiability.  
- Empirical results show large wall-clock and memory savings, scaling to millions of slots and thousands of steps without truncated BPTT.  
- Achieves competitive or better accuracy on synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI (with SDNC), and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Introduces an SDNC variant and details engineering contributions (sparse rollback, ANN integration) useful to practitioners.  
- Components (top-K attention, LRU allocation, ANN, change logs) are standard; novelty is their integrated systemization rather than a new algorithmic idea.  
- Theoretical claims (e.g., O(log N)) rely on assumptions not fully discussed, such as ANN dimensionality dependence, rebuild costs, and hidden constants.  
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analyses: K choice, ANN recall vs runtime, write policy effects, and bias from ignoring gradients outside top-K are underexplored.  
- Analysis of ANN approximation effects is thin: frequency of missed neighbors and accuracy impact are not quantified.  
- Write policy (previously read + LRU) is plausible but not sufficiently compared to learned content-based writes.  
- Memory accounting omits ANN index overhead and rebuild costs; total memory usage reporting is incomplete.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; descriptions of sparse read/write and rollback are easy to follow.  
- Suggested clarity improvements: include concise forward/backward algorithms, specify when writes are additive vs overwrite, and list K and ANN parameters per task.  
- Originality is limited: the work is a well-engineered combination of known techniques rather than a conceptual advance.  
- Significance is practical if robust, as SAM can make MANNs scalable, but evidence on demanding real-world tasks is lacking.  
- Key suggested experiments: ablate K and ANN recall, compare k-d trees vs LSH, test write policies, analyze sparse-gradient effects, and report end-to-end speed/memory including ANN maintenance.  
- Recommend releasing code to aid adoption and reproducibility.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses a key scalability bottleneck, shows strong empirical speed/memory gains, and maintains competitive accuracy.  
- Cons against acceptance: incremental technical novelty, insufficient analysis of approximation biases and hyperparameter sensitivity, and limited real-world validation.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject—practical and well-executed but needs stronger empirical ablations or theoretical analysis to justify acceptance.
