- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse content-selected reads/writes with ANN indexing and reversible in-place BPTT to achieve O(log N) access and O(1) per-step memory.  
- Addresses space/time bottlenecks of dense attention and dense backpropagation in prior MANNs (NTM, DNC, Memory Networks).  
- Technical contribution is sound with clear complexity analysis and credible implementation (sparse updates, reversible BPTT, ANN indices).  
- Experiments cover NTM tasks, long-horizon curricula, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning, showing up to 1000× speedups and 3000× less physical memory.  
- Algorithm empirically operates close to its theoretical complexity bounds and scales to millions of slots.  
- Concern: Figure 4 comparisons may be unfair—matching physical memory can hide differences in logical/addressable capacity.  
- Concern: Missing ablation study on K (sparsity level) and different ANN backends (k-d tree vs LSH) to clarify accuracy/runtime trade-offs.  
- Concern: No gradients flow through ANN selection; brief discussion of stability and variance is needed.  
- Clarity: Paper is well structured but would benefit from a concise background on MANNs and an earlier, clearer exposition of the write operator and memory management.  
- Originality: Novel synthesis of sparse reads/writes, ANN-backed content addressing, and reversible sparse BPTT distinct from dense and RL-based approaches.  
- Significance: High — improves scalability enabling very large-memory applications and competitive or superior results at scale.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical advance on MANN scalability, solid theory, convincing empirical gains, diverse tasks, and reproducible exposition.  
- Cons for acceptance: fairness of some comparisons, limited ablations on sparsity/ANN choices, and minor missing background/clarifications.  
- Overall recommendation: Reviewer leans toward acceptance.
