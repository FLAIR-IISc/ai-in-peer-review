This paper presents a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a new neural memory architecture that enables efficient training with large external memories. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in run-time and memory efficiency, as well as strong performance gains in curriculum learning and long-range generalization.

The main contribution of this work is the development of SAM, which achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity. SAM is designed to retain the representational power of original MANNs while allowing for efficient training with large memories. The authors show that SAM can be trained with very large memories, achieving a 1000x speedup and 3000x reduction in physical memory usage compared to non-sparse models.

The paper also presents an extensive evaluation of SAM on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results demonstrate that SAM can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models and can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories.

One of the key strengths of this paper is its ability to balance theoretical analysis with empirical evaluation. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of SAM, demonstrating its asymptotic optimality. At the same time, they present extensive experimental results that demonstrate the practical benefits of SAM.

The paper also raises interesting questions about the role of sparsity in neural networks. The authors speculate that the sparse approximations used in SAM may be beneficial for learning, analogous to the role of sparse autoencoders. This is an intriguing area for future research, and the authors' discussion of this topic adds depth and context to the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to scaling MANNs, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by adapting it to the recently introduced Differentiable Neural Computer.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the field. The authors provide sufficient background information on MANNs and the challenges of scaling them, making the paper accessible to a broader audience.
