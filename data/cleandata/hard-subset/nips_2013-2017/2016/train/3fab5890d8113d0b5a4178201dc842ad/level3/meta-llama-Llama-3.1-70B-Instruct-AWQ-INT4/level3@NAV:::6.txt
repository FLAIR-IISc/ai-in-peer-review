This paper proposes a novel Neural Turing Machine (NTM) variant, dubbed Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which tackles the scalability issue of traditional NTMs by introducing sparse read and write operations. The authors claim that SAM achieves state-of-the-art performance while reducing the huge physical memory and backpropagation operations required by traditional NTMs.

The paper is well-structured and easy to read, making it accessible to a broad audience. The authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its components, including the sparse read and write operations, the controller, and the efficient backpropagation through time.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to demonstrate the scalability of SAM through various experiments. The authors show that SAM can handle large external memories with significant improvements in empirical runtime and memory overhead compared to traditional NTMs. Additionally, SAM achieves comparable data efficiency and performance to existing models on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors could provide a brief background on memory-augmented neural networks to make the paper more self-contained. Secondly, the comparison in Figure 4 may be unfair, as matching physical memory sizes is one choice, but comparing using the same logical memory (not the same physical memory) would be more informative.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to addressing the scalability issue of NTMs, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by applying it to other models, such as the Differentiable Neural Computer.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and the results are important for the development of scalable memory-augmented neural networks. The paper also provides unique data and conclusions on existing data, which advances the state of the art in the field.

In conclusion, this paper is a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks, and its results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on related problems. The paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and it is a strong candidate for acceptance.
