- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for MANNs claiming large speedups and memory efficiency while preserving representational power.
- SAM restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory using sparse read/write ops and a memory-management scheme that finds unused blocks.
- Empirically evaluated on synthetic tasks, QA, and one-shot learning, showing comparable task performance with much lower compute/memory overhead.
- Reviewer finds the core technical ideas (efficient backpropagation, approximate nearest neighbor via sparse access) straightforward and not sufficiently novel.
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, with thorough architectural and experimental descriptions.
- Lacks deeper theoretical analysis and discussion of potential limitations of SAM.
- Originality limited: builds on prior MANN work and sparse access ideas that are not entirely new; needs more contextual comparison to existing methods.
- Significance: addresses important scalability problem for MANNs and shows promising practical speed/memory gains for large-scale use.
- Overall recommendation: interesting results but insufficient technical contribution for acceptance without more novelty, context, and theoretical insight.
- Suggested improvements: provide stronger comparisons to prior work and more in-depth theoretical/limitation analysis.
