- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrains reads/writes to K=4–8 words and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexing for content-based addressing.  
- Achieves asymptotic efficiency: O(log N) time and O(1) space per step, yielding ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× memory reduction vs NTMs on large memories.  
- Retains near-identical data efficiency on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) and real benchmarks (Omniglot, bAbI).  
- Demonstrates unprecedented generalization and scaling: curriculum learning to million-word memories and sequences 20× longer than training (10k→200k steps).  
- Extends to Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), achieving state-of-the-art bAbI results without memory access supervision.  
- Empirical rigor: comprehensive benchmarks, curriculum experiments, and ablation studies convincingly validate claims.  
- Originality: novel application of sparse content-based addressing with ANN in MANNs, distinct from prior sparse attention or RL-based approaches.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: architecture, dense variant comparisons, and supplementary material are well-explained and detailed.  
- Theoretical gap: lacks rigorous analysis explaining why sparsity improves curriculum learning and long-range generalization.  
- Scope limitation: does not explore sparse variants of other MANN families (e.g., Memory Networks) despite claiming generality.  
- Limited discussion of failure modes, such as performance on ultra-short sequences.  
- Overall verdict: landmark, high-impact contribution that makes MANNs practically viable — recommend accept.
