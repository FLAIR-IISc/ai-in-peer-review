This paper proposes an extension to the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) architecture, dubbed Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which addresses the scalability issues inherent in the original NTM design. The NTM, a type of memory-augmented neural network (MANN), has shown promise in learning algorithmic solutions to complex tasks, but its smooth read and write operations incur significant computational and memory overhead, limiting its applicability to real-world domains. The authors of this paper introduce a sparse read and write scheme, which reduces the memory and computational requirements of the NTM, making it more practical for large-scale applications.

The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed SAM architecture and its components. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of their approach, demonstrating that it achieves asymptotic lower bounds in both space and time complexity. The experimental results are also impressive, showing that SAM can run up to 1,000 times faster and with 3,000 times less physical memory than non-sparse models, while maintaining comparable performance on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition.

The paper builds upon previous work on NTMs and MANNs, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from existing methods. The use of sparse reads and writes is a novel contribution, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in reducing the computational and memory requirements of the NTM.

This could potentially bias the results in favor of the SAM model.
