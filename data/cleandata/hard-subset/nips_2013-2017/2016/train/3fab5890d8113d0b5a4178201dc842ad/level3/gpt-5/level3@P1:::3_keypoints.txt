- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): differentiable MANN enforcing sparsity in reads/writes via top-K content reads and restricted writes.  
- Replaces dense content addressing with approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) top-K retrieval and writes only to previously read slots or LRU slot.  
- Introduces reversible in-place memory updates for BPTT, achieving O(1) space per time step and O(log N) time per access.  
- Generalizes approach to DNC, producing a sparse DNC (SDNC).  
- Claims SAM preserves representational power of NTMs/DNCs while delivering asymptotically optimal time/space under content-addressable assumptions.  
- Empirically shows large scaling gains: up to ~1600× faster and ~3000× less memory than NTM in large-N regimes.  
- Learns standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) with comparable or better data efficiency than dense variants.  
- Scales to sequences >4k steps and million-word memories via curriculum training.  
- Achieves strong bAbI performance (especially SDNC) without supervising retrieval and performs well on Omniglot one-shot classification using larger capacity.  
- Engineering contributions highlighted: sparse top-K reads, simple write policy (previously read + LRU), and reversible BPTT rollback scheme.  
- Complexity claims are well-argued and the O(1) per-step memory via rollback is noted as a practical insight.  
- Experiments include careful runtime and memory profiling showing convincing scaling benefits.  
- Reviewer requests analyses quantifying gradient bias from hard top-K and ANN approximations.  
- Reviewer requests ablations over K, ANN type (k-d tree vs LSH), rebuild cadence, and write-policy components (LRU vs previously read, usage threshold ε).  
- Reviewer asks for reporting variance across random seeds and separating wall-clock vs algorithmic efficiency (FLOPs/step).  
- Paper clarity is praised; minor requests: main-text ablation table, clarify conditions for "optimal in time and space", and discuss potential failure modes (ANN imbalance, high-dim similarity issues).  
- Originality: integration of end-to-end differentiability, provable sparsity, and ANN-backed content addressing in a trainable MANN is novel and useful.  
- Significance: addresses key bottleneck for memory-based models by enabling million-word memories and thousands of steps without truncation; impact would grow with more real-world tasks.  
- Specific strengths: simple sparse write policy, effective ANN integration with practical rebuild strategy, reversible updates for O(1) BPTT memory, SDNC generalization, comprehensive runtime/memory benchmarks.  
- Specific weaknesses/questions: limited analysis of why sparsity aids early learning; sensitivity of performance to ANN recall/precision and word dimensionality.  
- Fairness concerns: comparisons sometimes conflate capacity — sparse models use much larger memory than dense baselines; request capacity-controlled comparisons and compute-budget reporting.  
- Missing ablations: K, ε, write gates (αt, τ), erasing LRU vs additive updates.  
- Real-world evaluation gap: beyond Omniglot, needs at least one large-scale text task (e.g., language modeling or MT) to strengthen claims.  
- Suggested improvements: add K/ANN ablations and index-recall correlations, analyze gradient bias from hard top-K and compare to soft variants, explore more ANN structures and word-dim effects, report per-seed stats and FLOPs, include qualitative retrieval/allocation analyses.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong practical advance in scaling differentiable memory, large measured gains, generality to multiple MANNs, clear writing and reproducible supplement — reviewer leans toward acceptance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited large-scale real-world evaluations, incomplete analysis of approximation effects and limited ablations, and capacity-conflating comparisons.
