- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to constrain reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory, improving MANN scalability.  
- Novel application of sparsity to memory access in MANNs, addressing a critical limitation.  
- Demonstrates large practical gains: up to 1000x speedups and 3000x memory savings versus dense models.  
- Provides theoretical justification, claiming asymptotic optimality in space and time complexity.  
- Empirical validation shows comparable or superior performance on synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI, and one-shot Omniglot.  
- Compelling scaling experiments using curriculum learning.  
- Shows generality by adapting SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).  
- Paper is well-structured, clear, and situates SAM appropriately within related work.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of why sparsity sometimes improves learning; lacks deeper inspection of learned representations and attention.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of ANN implementation trade-offs (k-d trees vs LSH) and omission of newer ANN methods like Mondrian forests.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to key hyperparameters (e.g., sparsity level K, usage parameter) not discussed in main text.  
- Overall assessment: strong, technically sound contribution with significant empirical and theoretical support.  
- Recommendation: accept; main counterpoints are limited sparsity analysis and ANN implementation detail gaps.
