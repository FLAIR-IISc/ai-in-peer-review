This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that constrains both reads and writes to a small, content-selected subset of memory slots and uses approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indexing to achieve O(log N) access and O(1) per-step memory during BPTT via reversible in-place updates. Relative to Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and related Memory Networks and the Differentiable Neural Computer (all widely discussed at NIPS), SAM addresses the long-standing space/time bottlenecks of smooth dense attention and dense backpropagation through external memory. The paper situates itself well against prior discrete addressing approaches (e.g., RL-based NTMs) by retaining end-to-end differentiability and argues near-optimal asymptotic complexity for content-addressed MANNs.

Quality: The technical contribution is sound and carefully evaluated. The complexity analysis is clear, and the implementation details (sparse updates, reversible BPTT, use of ANN indices) are credible. Experiments cover classic NTM tasks, long-horizon curricula, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning. Reported gains (up to 1000× faster, 3000× less physical memory) are compelling, and the algorithm appears to operate close to the theoretical complexity bounds. Concerns: (1) The comparison in Figure 4 seems potentially unfair; matching physical memory budgets can obscure differences in logical capacity. A comparison at equal logical memory (same number of addressable slots) would be more informative in addition to the physical-memory-controlled setting. (2) An ablation on K (sparsity level) and ANN backends (k-d tree vs LSH) would clarify accuracy/runtime trade-offs and robustness. (3) As no gradients flow through the ANN selection, a brief discussion of stability/variance would help.

Clarity: The paper is well structured and easy to read. To make it more self-contained, consider a concise background on MANNs and a clearer, earlier exposition of the write operator and memory management scheme.

Originality: Combining sparse reads/writes, efficient ANN-backed content addressing, and reversible sparse BPTT is a novel and practical synthesis distinct from prior dense MANNs and RL-based discrete addressing.

Significance: High. Addressing scalability unlocks applications requiring very large memories; results are competitive with state of the art and often superior at scale.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, practical advance on a core limitation of MANNs
- Solid theory with convincing empirical speed/memory gains
- Competitive performance on diverse tasks; scales to millions of slots
- Clear, reproducible exposition

Cons for acceptance:
- Fairness of some comparisons (logical vs physical memory)
- Limited ablations on sparsity and ANN choices
- Minor missing background/clarifications

Overall: I lean toward acceptance.
