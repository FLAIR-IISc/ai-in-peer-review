Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that preserves the representational power of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) while scaling to very large memories via end-to-end differentiable sparse reads and writes. Reads are made sparse by top-K content-based addressing supported by an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), yielding O(log N) access time. Writes are also sparse: the controller updates previously read locations and allocates new content to the least recently accessed slot via a simple usage-based policy. Crucially, the authors design an efficient backprop-through-time (BPTT) mechanism that records and reverts only sparse deltas, achieving O(1) space per time step and O(T log N) time for sequences of length T. Empirically, SAM is 100–1600x faster than dense NTMs and uses up to 3000x less physical memory at large N, learns comparably on standard NTM tasks, scales via curriculum to millions of memory slots and thousands of time steps, performs strongly on Omniglot one-shot classification, and adapts to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) to produce an SDNC variant with strong bAbI results.

Relative to prior work, SAM advances the NTM/MemNets line [Graves et al. 2014; Weston et al. 2014–2016] by addressing a major bottleneck: dense content-based attention scales linearly in memory. Unlike reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing for NTMs [Zaremba & Sutskever 2015], SAM keeps training fully differentiable while touching only a sparse subset per step. It complements DNC [Graves et al. 2016] by providing a sparse, efficient analogue. The integration of ANN within the training loop and an LRU-like allocator for differentiable controllers are technically novel and pragmatic.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated problem: dense attention in MANNs is prohibitive for realistic memory sizes; scaling such systems is high-impact.
- Technically strong contributions: sparse read/write scheme; ANN-backed content lookup; constant-space BPTT via in-place deltas and rollback; simple, effective memory management (LRU-based allocation/erase).
- Theoretical and empirical support: asymptotic complexity claims and convincing wall-clock and memory benchmarks; consistent learning performance across synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot.
- Generality: method adapts to DNC, yielding significant speedups with competitive accuracy.
- Clarity: the paper is well organized, with concise equations and implementation details (with further material in the supplement).

Weaknesses:
- Differentiability caveat: the top-K selection and ANN index introduce nondifferentiable components; gradients do not flow through the selection. Although the authors argue end-to-end training is preserved (gradients pass to selected memory contents), a more formal analysis of approximation bias and its effect on convergence would strengthen the claims.
- Limited ablations: little systematic study of K, choice of ANN (k-d tree vs LSH), rebuild frequency, or write-policy variants on accuracy/speed trade-offs.
- Benchmarks skew synthetic: beyond Omniglot and bAbI, there are no large-scale real-world sequence tasks (e.g., language modeling or long-context QA) to demonstrate practical gains.
- Comparisons: while NTM/DAM baselines are appropriate, a broader set of scalable memory baselines (e.g., key-value memory networks, cache-based or pointer mechanisms) would help contextualize significance.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound with careful engineering. Claims about asymptotic complexity are reasonable (full proofs in the supplement), and empirical results substantiate dramatic gains in time and space without observable loss in learning efficacy. The evaluation spans classic NTM tasks, longer curricula, bAbI, and Omniglot, though deeper ablations and harder real-world tasks would improve robustness. The honesty about the ANN being a fixed, nongradient component is appreciated; a discussion of failure modes is limited.

Clarity:
Very well written and organized. Key design choices and their motivations are clearly presented; equations are concise; implementation details for backprop rollback and memory management are lucid. Suggestions: move a minimal complexity proof sketch and a compact ablation table from the supplement into the main text.

Originality:
The combination of sparse reads/writes, constant-space BPTT via reversible updates, and in-network ANN for content addressing is novel and technically elegant. The memory allocator (LRU with erase) is simple yet effective. While sparse attention ideas exist broadly, this paper provides a concrete, differentiable, end-to-end scalable instantiation for MANNs.

Significance:
High. Enabling MANNs to scale to millions of slots and 100k+ steps addresses a central bottleneck for algorithmic reasoning, one-shot learning, and long-context applications. The approach is general enough to influence subsequent architectures and practical systems.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide ablations on K, ANN type, rebuild schedule, and write policy; quantify the accuracy–speed trade-off.
- Analyze the effect of nondifferentiable selection on optimization (e.g., stability, variance of gradients, sensitivity to noise).
- Evaluate on at least one larger-scale real dataset requiring long memory (e.g., document-level QA, long-context language modeling) to demonstrate practical impact.
- Release code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.
- Discuss interactions with truncated BPTT and catastrophic forgetting under the LRU policy.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled, efficient solution.
- Strong technical contributions with clear complexity improvements.
- Comprehensive experiments showing large speed/memory gains and competitive accuracy.
- Generalizable framework (e.g., SDNC) likely to influence future work.
- Clear, concise presentation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Nondifferentiable top-K/ANN selection not theoretically analyzed for convergence/approximation error.
- Limited ablations and scarce real-world, large-scale tasks beyond Omniglot/bAbI.
- Comparisons could include additional scalable memory baselines.

Recommendation: Accept (high confidence). This is a well-executed, impactful contribution that substantially advances scalable memory-augmented neural networks. Confidence: 0.8.
