This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs). The authors build on recent work such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, enabling learning beyond traditional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) limits. The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of a sparse read and write scheme, where reads and writes are constrained to a fixed-size subset of memory words rather than unlimited memory. This approach is theoretically justified and claimed to be optimal in terms of space and time complexity.

The authors provide a clear and well-written explanation of the SAM architecture, including the sparse read and write operations, the controller, and the efficient backpropagation through time. The paper also includes a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, including speed and memory benchmarks, learning costs, and performance on various tasks such as copy, associative recall, and priority sort.

The results show that SAM achieves significant speedups compared to NTMs, with improvements in empirical run-time and memory overhead by up to three orders of magnitude. The authors also demonstrate that SAM can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models on a range of synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition.

One of the strengths of this paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed approach, including the theoretical justification and the empirical evaluation. The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the results and their implications.

Another strength of this paper is its originality. The introduction of a sparse read and write scheme for MANNs is a novel contribution, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. The paper also explores the application of SAM to various tasks, including real-world examples, which demonstrates the potential of the proposed approach.

However, there are some limitations to this paper. One of the limitations is that the authors do not provide a thorough discussion of when sparsity improves learning. While the authors demonstrate that sparse reads and writes can benefit early-stage learning in some cases, they do not provide a clear explanation of the underlying mechanisms. Additionally, the authors do not explore other ANN integrations with SAM, which could be an interesting direction for future research.

Overall, this paper is well-written, clear, and original. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, and the results demonstrate the potential of SAM for MANNs. However, there are some limitations to the paper, and the authors could benefit from providing a more thorough discussion of the underlying mechanisms and exploring other ANN integrations with SAM.
