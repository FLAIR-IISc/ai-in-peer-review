Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a well-known bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), such as NTMs [7], Memory Networks [21, 17], and the DNC [8]: time and space scale linearly with the number of memory slots due to dense content-based addressing and dense writes, and BPTT requires caching full memory states. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which restricts both reads and writes to a constant number K of memory locations and uses an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to implement top-K content-based reads in O(log N). Writes are limited to a small set (previously read locations plus a least-recently-accessed slot), and BPTT stores and reverts only sparse deltas rather than full memory snapshots. They argue this yields O(log N) per-step compute and O(1) per-step memory, asymptotically optimal under content-based addressing (proofs deferred to Supplement A). Experiments show large wall-clock and peak-memory savings versus dense NTMs/DAM, comparable learning on standard NTM tasks, scaling to very long sequences via curriculum, competitive bAbI results (with an SDNC variant), and improved one-shot Omniglot performance due to larger memories.

Relative to prior NIPS work, the paper situates itself against dense NTMs [7], end-to-end Memory Networks [17], and the DNC [8], and contrasts to RL-based discrete addressing for NTMs [22]. The core idea of focusing attention on top-K memory slots and using LRU-like allocation connects to known practices in attention sparsification and memory management, but here they are packaged into a practical, differentiable MANN with explicit engineering for efficiency.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The sparse read/write scheme and rollback-style BPTT are plausible and consistent with sparse linear algebra and standard AD techniques. However, the claim of “end-to-end differentiability” sits somewhat uncomfortably with a non-differentiable top-K selection/ANN. Gradients are passed only through the selected K rows; selection itself is not learned (no gradient to ANN candidates). This introduces a biased gradient estimator whose effect is not analyzed. The asymptotic optimality claims depend on assumptions about content-based addressing and constant K that are not discussed in the main text.
- Empirical support: The speed and memory benchmarks are compelling and align with the complexity discussion. Synthetic NTM tasks and curriculum scaling are thorough. bAbI and Omniglot results are encouraging. That said, ablations are limited: there is little sensitivity analysis for K, the threshold ε, or the choice of write policy (previous read vs LRU). The impact of ANN recall quality on learning is not quantified (e.g., recall@K vs task performance).
- Experimental fairness: Implementational details drive large speedups; the fairness of comparisons (e.g., highly optimized SAM vs a reference NTM) could be clarified, including codebases, hardware, and hyperparameter parity.
- The SDNC adaptation further demonstrates generality but is incremental.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The main bottlenecks and engineering choices are clearly motivated. Important details (complexity proofs, data structures for usage tracking, training details) are relegated to the supplement; bringing key assumptions and limitations into the main text would improve accessibility. A clearer treatment of the gradient implications of the ANN/top-K truncation would help.

Originality
The contribution is predominantly a consolidation of straightforward ideas:
- Top-K sparsification of content-based attention using ANN indices.
- Sparse writes via previously read locations plus LRU-style allocation/erase.
- Storing sparse deltas to enable memory rollback during BPTT.
These components are individually well known; the novelty lies in integrating them into a practical MANN and showing strong scaling. While useful, the conceptual advance over prior work is limited.

Significance
Practically, enabling MANNs with very large external memories and long sequences is important. The empirical evidence suggests SAM can make such models tractable, which could matter for language modeling, QA, and one-shot learning. However, the theoretical and algorithmic novelty is modest, and the lack of analysis of gradient bias and robustness to ANN errors weakens the scientific contribution.

Pros
- Clear identification of a genuine scalability bottleneck in MANNs.
- Simple, effective sparse access design with strong engineering.
- Dramatic empirical improvements in time/memory; ability to train with very large memories and long sequences.
- Competitive results on bAbI (with SDNC) and Omniglot; good scaling via curriculum.
- Approach appears broadly applicable to other MANNs (e.g., DNC).

Cons
- Technical novelty is limited; main components (ANN-based top-K, LRU allocation, sparse rollback) are straightforward and largely engineering choices.
- “End-to-end differentiable” claim is qualified: top-K/ANN selection is non-differentiable; gradient bias not analyzed.
- Insufficient ablations: sensitivity to K/ε, robustness to ANN recall errors, effect of different write policies.
- Asymptotic optimality depends on assumptions not clearly stated in the main text.
- Benchmark fairness and reproducibility details need strengthening.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an analysis or empirical study of the gradient bias introduced by top-K truncation and ANN candidate sets (e.g., varying ANN recall, assessing training stability and final performance).
- Add ablations for K, ε, write policy (prev-read vs content-based vs LRU), and frequency of ANN rebuilds; report compute–accuracy trade-offs.
- Clarify implementation parity in benchmarks; consider releasing code or giving detailed reproducibility information.
- Move key assumptions behind the optimality claims into the main text and outline the proof sketch.
- Discuss alternatives to ANN (e.g., differentiable approximate top-K, learned gating) and when they might be preferable.

Overall recommendation
While the experimental results are interesting and the engineering is solid, the paper’s main ideas are relatively straightforward and incremental, and the theoretical claims are not fully substantiated in the main text. In my view, the technical contribution is insufficient for NIPS acceptance at this time. I encourage the authors to strengthen the analytical aspects and ablations and clarify limitations; the work could have substantial practical impact with a firmer scientific footing.
