- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a neural memory architecture enabling efficient training with large external memories.  
- SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity while retaining MANN representational power.  
- Demonstrates massive practical gains: ~1000x speedup and ~3000x reduction in physical memory versus dense models.  
- Extensive empirical evaluation across synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot, and bAbI question answering.  
- Shows SAM scales to very long-range tasks (100,000s of time steps) with comparable data efficiency to existing models.  
- Balances theoretical analysis (time/space complexity, asymptotic optimality) with strong empirical results.  
- Suggests sparse approximations may aid learning, drawing analogy to sparse autoencoders and prompting future research.  
- Adapts SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer, demonstrating approach generality.  
- Paper is well-written and organized, with sufficient background for readers familiar with MANNs.  
- Technically sound with thorough analysis and well-presented experimental results.  
- Original contribution to scaling MANNs, addressing an important practical bottleneck.  
- Significant impact due to efficiency and performance improvements demonstrated.  
- Potential weaknesses: may be too specialized for general readers and the sparsity-learning link needs further investigation.
