This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs). The authors build upon recent work, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, enabling learning beyond traditional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) limits. The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of a sparse read and write scheme, which constrains reads and writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words rather than unlimited memory.

The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach and claim optimality of the methodology. They also present empirical results that demonstrate improved read/write performance due to reduced memory size. In addition, they evaluate the learning costs of introducing sparsity using standard NTM tasks, such as copy, associative recall, and priority sort. The results show that the combined costs are lower than NTM for some tasks, and that sparse reads/writes via linear models or approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes can benefit early-stage learning in some cases.

The paper also includes a real-world example and benchmarks implemented in Torch7. The idea is straightforward, novel, and yields significant speedups compared to NTM. The authors suggest that their approach can be applied to many differentiable memory architectures, such as Memory Networks.
