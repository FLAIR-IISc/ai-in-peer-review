This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory-augmented neural network that scales to very large external memories by making reads and writes sparse. Instead of dense content-based attention over all N memory slots (as in NTMs and Memory Networks), SAM performs K-sparse reads via approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search and K-sparse writes to either recently read locations or the least-recently-used slot. A key engineering contribution is an in-place, reversible write-log that enables backpropagation through time without storing full memory snapshots, yielding O(1) space per step and O(log N) time per step (for fixed K) while retaining differentiability of the overall model. The paper also shows how to adapt the approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), yielding SDNC. Empirically, SAM achieves orders-of-magnitude speed and memory improvements over dense NTMs, with comparable or better data efficiency on synthetic algorithmic tasks, strong performance on Omniglot one-shot classification, and competitive results on the bAbI QA tasks without supervision of memory access.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well placed within the line of memory-augmented models, including Memory Networks [Weston et al.; Sukhbaatar et al.] and Neural Turing Machines [Graves et al.], and follows the DNC [Graves et al.] in maintaining temporal associations. Unlike reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing for NTMs [Zaremba & Sutskever], SAM preserves gradient-based training while enforcing sparsity through top-K selection and LRU-style allocation. The use of ANN (k-d trees and LSH) inside the training loop is a notable systems contribution enabling sublinear read time, and the reversible write-log is an elegant BPTT solution.

Quality: Technically sound and carefully executed. The complexity analysis is plausible: sparse reads/writes combined with efficient data structures support O(log N) compute and O(1) per-step memory during training. The backprop “rollback” mechanism is simple and correct given that only a constant number of locations are modified per step. The write policy (interpolating between updating previously read slots and reallocating the least recently used slot) is a reasonable, effective heuristic for allocation without explicit supervision. The empirical evaluation is strong: (1) clear speed/memory benchmarks against NTM and a dense approximation (DAM); (2) learning curves on standard NTM tasks indicating that sparsity does not harm—and sometimes helps—data efficiency; (3) curriculum-based scaling to millions of memory slots and thousands of untruncated BPTT steps; (4) results on bAbI (with SDNC) competitive with the best non-supervised memory-access approaches; and (5) meaningful gains on Omniglot episodes with long sequences enabled by larger memory.

Potential technical concerns and limitations:
- The top-K selection for reads is non-differentiable with respect to indices; gradients flow only through the selected slots and not through the selection process or ANN parameters. While this is common in sparse attention, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the bias this introduces and any stability issues; an ablation varying K would help quantify the tradeoff.
- ANN search is approximate; there is no analysis of how neighbor recall quality affects convergence or final accuracy. Although the results are convincing, a sensitivity study (varying ANN parameters and rebuild frequency) would strengthen the claims.
- The allocation heuristic is LRU-like and task-agnostic. It works well empirically, but comparisons with alternative usage measures (the discounted-usage DAM variant is briefly discussed) and with learned allocation policies would provide a fuller picture.
- Reproducibility and portability: the approach depends on specialized data structures and integration with ANN libraries. More implementation detail on synchronizing the ANN index with in-graph memory, and performance on standard accelerators (GPU/TPU), would be valuable.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The background succinctly recalls content-based addressing and prior MANNs; the architecture section specifies read, write, and controller computation with the necessary equations; complexity and BPTT-rollback are explained cleanly; and implementation details are appropriately deferred to the supplement. Figures on scaling and learning are informative. Suggestions for improving clarity: add an ablation for K, report ANN recall and rebuild cadence, and include a small schematic of the sparse write/erase flow in the main text.

Originality: High. Combining end-to-end differentiable sparse access with ANN-driven content retrieval, plus an in-place reversible write-log for BPTT, is a novel and technically strong approach distinct from both dense attention in NTMs/Memory Networks and RL-based discrete addressing. The extension to SDNC further demonstrates generality.

Significance: High. Efficient scaling of neural memory is a central bottleneck for long-context reasoning and retrieval-heavy tasks. Demonstrating 100–1600× speedups and 3000× memory savings while maintaining performance makes this work impactful for the community. The ability to train with very long sequences and very large memories broadens the applicability of MANNs to more realistic settings.

Pros:
- Strong, well-motivated problem with a compelling, principled solution.
- Clear asymptotic improvements in time and space; practical wall-clock and memory gains.
- Solid empirical coverage across synthetic algorithms, QA (bAbI), and one-shot learning (Omniglot).
- General method applicable beyond NTM; SDNC results are impressive.
- Clear writing and thorough supplements.

Cons:
- Sparse top-K selection and ANN dependence introduce approximation/bias not theoretically characterized.
- Limited ablations on K, ANN quality, and allocation strategies.
- Engineering complexity may hinder adoption without more implementation guidance.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive is performance to K? Is there a regime where increasing K ceases to help?
- What is the impact of ANN recall on task performance? Do you monitor or adapt ANN parameters during training?
- Can you report GPU utilization and end-to-end throughput vs. dense baselines on identical hardware?
- Does the LRU allocation ever cause premature overwriting for tasks requiring very long-term retention, and could a learned allocation gate help?

Recommendation: Strong accept. The paper offers a substantial advance in scalable differentiable memory with convincing theory, systems design, and experiments. Even with some missing ablations and theoretical analysis of sparse selection, the contribution is timely, original, and impactful for NIPS.
