

This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network architecture that uses approximate nearest-neighbor search and arg-top-K operations to achieve efficient memory access in neural Turing machines. The authors demonstrate significant computational and memory efficiency gains (up to 1,000x speedup and 3,000x less memory usage) while maintaining performance on synthetic tasks and Omniglot classification.

The paper makes a valuable practical contribution by applying these techniques to writeable memories, which is less explored than read-only applications. While the computational benefits are compelling, the novelty is somewhat limited as related work (e.g., Memory Networks section 3.3 and other papers) has already explored scalable memory access using hashing techniques. The authors' approach represents a pragmatic engineering solution rather than a fundamental theoretical advance.

Strengths include strong empirical results showing SAM's scalability to 100,000+ memory slots, successful application to Babi tasks, and clear demonstration of computational advantages. The paper's clarity is generally good, though the task descriptions lack sufficient detail for full replicability, and the authors must provide careful task construction details and release experiment code.

Weaknesses include insufficient discussion of the non-smoothness of argmax/K-argmax operations (particularly for K=1) and their effects on training dynamics compared to fully smooth models. The authors should analyze failure cases and limits: when does the method fail, does it always work, and under what conditions? The claim that Omniglot is "non-synthetic" or "real-world" is misleading and should be revised. The training procedure makes discrete actions without accounting for discreteness, a limitation that requires discussion.

I recommend acceptance with revisions to address these concerns, particularly the replicability issues, failure case analysis, and discussion of the discrete optimization challenges. The practical contribution is significant, but the paper must better contextualize its novelty relative to existing literature and provide more thorough theoretical discussion. The suggested removal of section 3.6 is appropriate. This work advances the engineering of memory-augmented networks, making them more applicable to larger-scale problems, though it doesn't fundamentally advance the underlying theory.
