Summary
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes. The core idea is to: (1) restrict attention to an arg-top-K set of memory slots per read (using fast approximate nearest-neighbor search to find the K most similar items), and (2) write only to a small set of locations (previously read locations and a least-recently-used slot). Combined with a lightweight usage-based memory management scheme and a rollback strategy for backpropagation, SAM achieves O(log N) time per step and O(1) space per step (beyond initialization), enabling training with very large memories. Experiments on synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot classification show large speed/memory savings with comparable or better accuracy than dense baselines; the approach also scales via curriculum to very long sequences. The paper additionally sketches a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on NTMs and Memory Networks by replacing fully smooth, dense addressing with sparse, approximate content-based access. The use of hashing/ANN to scale memory lookup has precedent in the Memory Networks literature (e.g., End-to-End MemNNs Section 3.3 and MemNN variants that preselect memories with hashing), where large memories are handled by candidate reduction. The novelty here lies less in the idea of scalable lookup and more in applying fast ANN and sparse top-K to a writeable, differentiable memory with careful engineering (sparse writes, backprop rollback, usage-based allocation). RL-NTM approaches that learn discrete access policies also relate, but trade efficiency for stochastic optimization.

Strengths
- Strong, practical contribution: a clean, effective recipe for scaling writeable external memory with compelling asymptotic and empirical speed/space gains (up to 1000× runtime and 3000× memory improvements).
- Clear engineering: sparse writes, usage-based allocation, and reversible in-place memory updates for efficient BPTT are well-motivated and practically useful.
- Broad applicability: the approach can be adapted to other MANNs (e.g., DNC).
- Empirics indicate minimal accuracy loss vs dense models and improved scaling via curriculum.

Weaknesses and requested revisions
- Thin novelty relative to MemNN hashing/candidate reduction and other large-memory schemes. Please better differentiate conceptually and empirically from MemNN-WSH/hashed variants and RL-NTM.
- Non-smoothness of arg-top-K: The K-argmax selection is discrete; gradients do not flow to excluded slots. Please:
  - Analyze effects of K (e.g., K=1 vs 4/8): stability, convergence, gradient starvation, and final performance. Include comparisons to fully smooth dense attention (softmax over all slots) and to MemNN hashing where a softmax is applied within a candidate set.
  - Discuss whether training-time discreteness introduces bias or brittleness (e.g., small perturbations switching membership at the margin).
  - Clarify whether you tried straight-through estimators, soft top-k relaxations, or annealing, and why you settled on the current choice.
- Discrete actions without accounting for discreteness: Unlike RL-NTM or approaches that include a learning signal for discrete decisions, SAM treats the top-K gating as fixed. Please discuss implications and limits (e.g., inability to optimize which candidates are included), and under what regimes this is acceptable or harmful.
- Replicability concerns: Task descriptions and training details are insufficient to reproduce results. Please provide:
  - Full task generators (copy/associative recall/priority sort/bAbI/Omniglot), exact curricula schedules, sequence lengths, noise/variations.
  - Complete hyperparameters and optimizer settings; normalization of memory words and queries; thresholds (epsilon for usage, sparsity cutoff), gating parameterizations; initialization; gradient clipping; BPTT length.
  - ANN details: index type per experiment (k-d tree vs LSH), hyperparameters (number of trees/hashes, table sizes, distance metrics), rebuild frequency/criteria, and cost breakdown.
  - Release training and evaluation code.
- Failure modes and limits: Please characterize when SAM fails or degrades, including:
  - Tasks requiring blended attention over many slots (where sparsity hurts).
  - Noisy queries or non-metric similarities; rapidly shifting memory distributions that destabilize ANN indices; high-dimensional regimes where k-d trees degrade and LSH collisions rise.
  - Interference from writing to previously read slots; limits of least-recently-used allocation; catastrophic overwrites; long-horizon credit assignment with rollback.
  - Sensitivity to K and memory size; robustness under truncated BPTT.
- Suggest removing Section 3.6 (or moving to the supplement). It reads as tangential and detracts from the core contribution.
- Terminology: Avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world.” Prefer “natural image benchmark” or simply “Omniglot one-shot classification.”

Quality
The engineering is solid and the complexity claims are supported by both analysis and compelling speed/memory measurements. The learning results are encouraging, but the lack of ablations on K, exact vs approximate search, index choice, and detailed task specifications weakens the empirical support. The “end-to-end differentiable” claim should more carefully acknowledge the discrete top-K gate.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. However, key implementation and task-generation details are missing for reproduction, and the ANN configuration is under-specified. Please add the requested details and expand the discussion of discrete gating effects.

Originality
Conceptual novelty is modest: scalable lookup via hashing/ANN and sparse attention has prior art, especially in MemNNs. The main novelty is the integration into a writeable MANN with efficient backprop and memory management. That integration is nontrivial and valuable.

Significance
High practical significance. Many will use these ideas to train MANNs at previously infeasible scales. Even if conceptual novelty is limited, the demonstrated scaling and clean recipe will likely influence subsequent work.

Pros for acceptance
- Orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains with comparable accuracy.
- Clear, broadly reusable engineering contributions (sparse writes, rollback BPTT, usage-based allocation).
- Demonstrated scaling to very long sequences and large memories.
- Adaptability to other MANNs (e.g., DNC).

Cons against acceptance
- Limited novelty versus MemNN hashing and RL-NTM lines.
- Insufficient analysis of discrete top-K effects and ablations over K and ANN choices.
- Reproducibility gaps (task generators, curricula, ANN hyperparameters, code).

Recommendation
Accept, conditional on revisions. The practical contribution is strong and timely; with the requested clarifications, ablations (especially on K and ANN), discussion of discrete gating, failure analysis, removal of Section 3.6, careful terminology for Omniglot, and released code/task generators, the paper will be a valuable resource for the community.
