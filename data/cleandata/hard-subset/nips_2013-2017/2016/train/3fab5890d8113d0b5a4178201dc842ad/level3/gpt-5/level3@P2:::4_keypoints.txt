- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): end-to-end differentiable MANN with ANN-based top-K reads, sparse writes to previous reads or LRU, usage tracking, and rollback BPTT yielding O(1) per-step memory and O(log N) time (O(N) init).
- Builds on NTMs and Memory Networks but avoids O(N) per-step costs and remains fully differentiable (unlike RL-based discrete addressing).
- SDNC adapts sparse access to DNC-style temporal links, demonstrating generality and large speedups.
- Empirical results: matches/improves dense models on classic NTM tasks, scales to thousands–tens of thousands, generalizes to 200k steps, strong bAbI (SDNC solves all but one), improved Omniglot one-shot.
- Reports substantial wall-clock speedups (100–1600×) and memory savings (~3000×).
- Technical soundness: sparse read/write, LRU management, and rollback BPTT are well-motivated and implemented; complexity claims justified under standard assumptions.
- Evidence: experiments cover synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot with appropriate comparisons (NTM, LSTM, DAM); scaling and generalization tests are convincing.
- Caveat: use of approximate NN is nondifferentiable and only partially characterized; needs ablations on ANN type/quality vs. performance.
- Caveat: write scheme (previous-reads + LRU) limits content-based writes; requires ablations (e.g., sparse content-based writes) and sensitivity analysis over K, ε, and gating.
- Caveat: some baselines could be stronger/more closely matched (e.g., Memory Networks variants, other memory-augmented meta-learners).
- Caveat: practical engineering details (GPU/CPU placement for ANN, rebuild amortization) are underexplained for reproducibility.
- Clarity: paper is clearly written and organized; supplement holds derivations, but brief in-text pseudocode for rollback and memory manager would aid reproduction.
- Originality: novel combination of sparse differentiable read/writes, ANN integration, O(1)-per-step BPTT via sparse diffs/rollback, and DNC-like extension.
- Significance: addresses a key bottleneck in scaling MANNs, enabling long-context modeling and training on much larger memories/sequences.
- Pros: conceptually clean design, principled complexity improvements, efficient BPTT, ANN integration, strong empirical speed/memory gains, and demonstrated generality.
- Cons: limited ablations and robustness analysis, baseline/matching gaps, missing engineering details, and limited real-world evaluation beyond synthetic/Omniglot.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to K and ε; amortized cost and impact of ANN rebuilds; experiments with sparse content-based writes; device placement (CPU vs GPU) for ANN and throughput impact; bAbI version/training details and seed averaging.
- Recommendation: Strong accept.
