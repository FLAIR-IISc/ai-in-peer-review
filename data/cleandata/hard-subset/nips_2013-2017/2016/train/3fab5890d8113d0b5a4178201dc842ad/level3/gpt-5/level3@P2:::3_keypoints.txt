- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparsifies reads/writes to K slots and uses ANN indices for sublinear content addressing.  
- Achieves O(log N) time per read via ANN and O(1) per-step space for BPTT using reversible sparse updates.  
- Writes update previously read locations or the least recently used slot, yielding a differentiable LRU-like allocation.  
- Extends sparsification approach to Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).  
- Empirically matches or outperforms dense baselines on synthetic NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort).  
- Demonstrates large speed and memory improvements (up to ~1000x speed, ~3000x memory savings) and scales to millions of slots and long sequences.  
- Performs competitively on bAbI (best with SDNC) and shows strong one-shot learning on Omniglot.  
- Sparse read/write formulation, reversible updates, and ANN indexing are well-motivated and technically sound.  
- Non-differentiability of ANN top-K selection handled by fixing indices; introduces biased gradients but works in practice.  
- Empirical evaluation is strong and engineering is convincing, though a few ablations are missing.  
- Limited analysis of when sparsity improves or harms data efficiency; more explanation needed.  
- Reliance on kd-trees/LSH may degrade with high-dimensional memory words; dimensionality/constant effects not fully quantified.  
- Simple write policy (previously read + LRU) is effective but may fail on tasks needing content-determined new writes.  
- Fairness concern: sparse models use much larger N in scaled curricula; needs discussion of accuracy–resource tradeoffs.  
- Clarity is good; suggest moving complexity assumptions/proof sketch to main text and adding intuition/visualization for write policy.  
- Recommend summarizing ANN configuration (kd-tree vs. LSH thresholds, rebuild frequency) and empirical overhead in main text.  
- Originality: novel combination of sparse reads/writes, ANN-based access, and O(1) BPTT memory; deterministic alternative to RL-based sparse addressing.  
- Significance: addresses key MANN scalability bottleneck with practical relevance for language modeling, QA, and one-shot learning.  
- Pros: simple idea, strong theory motivation, reversible O(1) memory, demonstrated scalability, broad applicability.  
- Cons: limited analysis of sparsity effects, ANN and dimensionality details in supplement, few ablations, possible write-policy restrictions.  
- Requested ablations/questions: sensitivity to K, sparse reads vs. writes, usage metric choices (discounted sum vs. LRU).  
- Requested experiments: effect of word dimensionality on kd-tree vs. LSH (wall-clock and accuracy), bound on approximation error ||r − r̃||.  
- Asked about failure cases of the write scheme and potential hybrid content-based sparse writes or continuous sparse relaxations (sparsemax/top-k).  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: High.
