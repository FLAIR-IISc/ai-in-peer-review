- Identifies a key scalability problem in MANNs: dense read/write and BPTT cost scale linearly with memory, making large memories and long sequences impractical.
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which restricts reads/writes to a small constant subset using ANN data structures for K-nearest neighbors.
- Achieves time complexity reduction (search from O(N) to O(log N)) and space reduction for BPTT (per-timestep from O(N) to O(1)) by tracking only sparse memory changes.
- Provides theoretical analysis demonstrating near-optimal asymptotic time and space complexity.
- Empirical results show massive practical gains (up to ~1,600x speedup and ~3,000x memory savings) and enable scaling to very long sequences (e.g., 200k steps).
- SAM matches or outperforms dense models on benchmark tasks (algorithmic tasks, bAbI, Omniglot) while enabling much larger logical memories.
- A key experimental weakness: comparisons sometimes confound sparsity benefits with vastly larger logical memory sizes, making it unclear if gains come from sparsity or memory capacity.
- Suggests a more controlled comparison to disentangle the effect of sparse approximation from increased memory capacity.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured; figures effectively illustrate gains.
- Minor clarity suggestion: expand background on the standard dense NTM write (outer-product add/erase) for broader accessibility.
- The approach is original, combining differentiable memory with ANN data structures and a sparse BPTT algorithm in a novel way.
- The work is highly significant, removing a major barrier to applying MANNs to real-world, large-scale sequence problems.
- Strong arguments for acceptance: addresses an important bottleneck, technically sound, empirically compelling, original, and likely to have major impact.
- Overall conclusion: excellent paper with a minor experimental concern, strongly recommended for acceptance.
