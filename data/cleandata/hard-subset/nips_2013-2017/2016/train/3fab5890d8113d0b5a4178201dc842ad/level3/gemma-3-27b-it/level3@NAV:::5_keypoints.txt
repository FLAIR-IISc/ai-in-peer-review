- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme that restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory locations.  
- Core idea: reduce computational and memory costs while retaining representational power of dense MANNs.  
- Reports speedups up to three orders of magnitude and large memory-efficiency gains.  
- Demonstrates strong performance on algorithmic tasks, curriculum learning, and long-sequence generalization.  
- Achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time, addressing MANNs’ quadratic complexity bottleneck.  
- Extends SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer, showing generalizability of the approach.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, with convincing comparisons to baselines like NTM and LSTM.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized with a clear explanation of SAM’s architecture and benefits.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of why SAM improves curriculum learning and generalization—mechanistic explanation lacking.  
- Authors’ speculation linking effects to sparse autoencoders is unsubstantiated and needs deeper investigation.  
- Implementation relies on custom data structures and is complex, potentially hindering reproducibility and adoption.  
- Recommendation: strong paper merits acceptance with minor revisions to probe mechanisms and discuss implementation/simplification.
