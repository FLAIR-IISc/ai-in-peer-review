## Review of “Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes”

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme designed to address the scalability limitations of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs). Building upon the recent surge of interest in MANNs – stemming from their potential to surpass the algorithmic limitations of LSTMs – the authors tackle the core issue of computational and memory overhead associated with large memory sizes. The key innovation lies in restricting read and write operations to a fixed-size subset of memory locations, thereby reducing complexity while theoretically maintaining representational power. 

The paper presents a strong argument for the optimality of SAM, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. Demonstrating significant speedups (up to 1000x) and reduced memory usage (3000x) compared to NTMs is a compelling contribution. Evaluations on standard NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) reveal comparable, and in some cases improved, learning efficiency. The inclusion of a real-world application (Omniglot character recognition) and publicly available Torch7 implementation further strengthens the work. Notably, the authors successfully extend SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), showcasing its broader applicability. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The idea is conceptually straightforward yet impactful, and the writing is clear and well-organized. The authors are thorough in explaining the architecture and providing relevant details in the appendix. The theoretical justification adds weight to the empirical findings. However, a key weakness lies in the limited exploration of *when* sparsity truly enhances learning. While improved performance is observed on some tasks, the underlying reasons aren’t fully elucidated. Further investigation into the interplay between sparsity level, task complexity, and controller network architecture would be valuable. Additionally, the paper could benefit from exploring more diverse ANN integrations with SAM beyond the SDNC example. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and impactful approach to scaling MANNs.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance gains.
*   Solid theoretical grounding and clear presentation.
*   Broad applicability demonstrated via SDNC implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited analysis of conditions under which sparsity improves learning.
*   Potential for further exploration of ANN integrations with SAM.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a well-written, technically sound, and original contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. It addresses a critical challenge in scaling these models and offers a promising solution. Despite minor areas for improvement, the paper represents a significant step forward and is **strongly recommended for acceptance**.
