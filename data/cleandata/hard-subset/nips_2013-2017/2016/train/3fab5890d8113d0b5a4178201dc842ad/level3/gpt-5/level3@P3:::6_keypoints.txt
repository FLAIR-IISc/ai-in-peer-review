- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): K-sparse reads/writes with ANN-based content addressing for O(log N) time and sparse rollback BPTT for O(1) per-step space.
- Writes combine previously-read locations and an LRU slot to achieve constant-time, constant-space updates.
- Positioned against NTMs, Memory Networks, DNC, and RL-based discrete addressing, arguing for lower-variance end-to-end differentiability.
- Empirical claims: up to ~1000× speedup and ~3000× less physical memory than dense models, while maintaining data efficiency.
- Demonstrates strong scaling (thousands of steps, millions of slots), competitive bAbI performance, and improved one-shot Omniglot; extends to Sparse DNC (SDNC).
- Technical soundness: complexity claims are well argued and engineering choices (ANN, LRU, CSR, rollback) are appropriate; ANN index is non-differentiable and acknowledged.
- Empirical support: benchmarks convincingly show benefits in the large-N, long-T regime and synthetic tasks indicate sparsity does not harm data efficiency.
- Caveat: top-K reads introduce biased gradients vs dense soft attention; paper lacks analysis of approximation error vs K or ANN recall and impact on training stability.
- Caveat: amortized vs worst-case costs of ANN maintenance and periodic rebuilds are not fully accounted for.
- Clarity: paper is well structured and main mechanisms are explained clearly with good intuition.
- Suggestion: add a brief MANN background and explicit write-operator explanation (figure/pseudocode) in the main text.
- Suggestion: explicitly state how top-K pruning affects read distributions/gradients and clarify the “non-negligible” access threshold used.
- Originality: novel integration of K-sparse read/write, efficient sparse backprop bookkeeping, and in-network ANN addressing; SDNC shows generality.
- Significance: addresses a central scalability barrier in MANNs, achieving near-optimal asymptotics relevant to long-context and retrieval-augmented models.
- Strength: principled design that attains strong asymptotic and practical gains with careful systems engineering.
- Strength: broad empirical evaluation including large-T curricula and MANN baselines.
- Strength: achieves state-of-the-art bAbI results (unsupervised) and scales to regimes infeasible for dense MANNs.
- Weakness: Figure 4 (Omniglot) comparison may be unfair—matching physical memory gives SAM vastly larger logical capacity; needs equal-logical-memory baselines or fixed-accuracy comparisons.
- Weakness: limited ablations on K, ANN type/recall, and pruning impact on gradient bias/variance and stability.
- Weakness: write policy alternatives and sensitivity to interpolation/write gates and thresholds are not thoroughly explored.
- Weakness: periodic ANN rebuild costs and worst-case latency implications need clearer exposition and measurement.
- Question: how sensitive are results to K (e.g., 2,4,8) and to ANN choice (k-d tree vs LSH) across tasks?
- Question: can authors report accuracy vs compute trade-offs at fixed logical memory sizes to complement physical-memory-matched comparisons?
- Question: what is the empirical overhead and frequency of ANN rebuilds at scale, and do imbalanced trees affect training?
- Question: have there been failure modes where LRU overwrites still-useful content, and would a learned usage gate help?
- Suggestions for improvement: add MANN background, controlled Omniglot comparisons at equal logical memory, ablations on K/ANN/write policy, report ANN recall, and clarify amortized vs worst-case ANN costs.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Pros for acceptance: strong, well-motivated solution with theoretical and empirical backing, O(1) per-step space and O(log N) time, broadly applicable to multiple memory architectures.
- Cons against acceptance: some uncontrolled comparisons, limited analysis of approximation error and sensitivity to hyperparameters, and insufficient ablation of write-policy choices.
