Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales Neural Turing Machine–style read/write access by enforcing sparsity and using efficient approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) data structures. Reads are restricted to top-K content-based matches (via ANN search such as randomized k-d trees or LSH), and writes are applied sparsely to previously read locations and a least-recently-used (LRU) slot. The authors also describe an efficient backprop-through-time scheme that stores only sparse deltas, yielding O(1) memory per time step and O(log N) time per access, with asymptotic optimality claims under reasonable assumptions. Empirically, SAM matches or improves the data efficiency of dense NTMs on synthetic tasks, scales via curriculum to very long sequences and large memories (millions of slots), performs competitively on bAbI (with a sparse DNC variant achieving strong results), and achieves good one-shot classification performance on Omniglot.

The work is closely related to Memory Networks and End-to-End Memory Networks that use content-based attention over large memories; prior MemNN work has used hashing and candidate selection (e.g., windowed hashing, WSH) to scale softmax attention, and Zaremba & Sutskever explored RL-based discrete addressing for NTMs. The key practical contribution here is to bring efficient candidate selection and sparse operations to fully writeable, differentiable external memory with end-to-end training and to demonstrate large empirical speed/memory gains.

Strengths
- Clear and practical scaling story: sublinear-time reads, constant-time writes, and O(1) memory per time step with an in-place rollback during BPTT.
- Strong empirical results: up to three orders of magnitude speed/memory improvements; comparable or better learning dynamics than dense NTMs on classic synthetic tasks; scaling to very long sequences; competitive bAbI results without supervised memory pointers; good Omniglot performance.
- Generality: the sparse recipe extends to DNC-like temporal link memories (SDNC) and plausibly to other MANNs.
- Engineering contribution is substantial and timely; the paper surfaces concrete, reproducible systems ideas (ANN indices, sparse data structures, LRU usage).

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Novelty is incremental: using ANN and top-K/argmax selection to scale attention has precedent in MemNNs (e.g., hashing-based candidate selection). The main novelty is applying these tools to learnable writeable memories and making them work efficiently; this should be more explicitly contextualized relative to MemNN-WSH and RL-NTM.
- Discreteness and non-smoothness: the top-K selection and ANN introduce non-differentiabilities; gradients do not pass through the index or the selection. Please discuss implications (e.g., potential training bias/instability, what happens at K=1), and compare to fully smooth baselines and to MemNN-WSH behaviors. Contrast with prior work that models discreteness explicitly (e.g., REINFORCE in RL-NTM) and explain when your straight-through selection suffices or fails.
- Limits/failure modes: the paper focuses on successes; please analyze when SAM fails. For which tasks, sequence lengths, or memory loads do approximate selection errors accumulate? How sensitive are results to K, the usage threshold, LRU policy, and ANN hyperparameters (tree depth, number of hashes, rebuild schedule)?
- Asymptotic optimality claims: clarify the assumptions (e.g., fixed K, bounded controller size, properties of the index) and provide intuition on when these assumptions break (e.g., high-dimensional keys degrading ANN performance).
- Replicability: task generation details (especially curricula), hyperparameters, ANN settings, and data-structure implementation specifics are insufficient for exact reproduction. Please provide full task construction details and release code.
- Wording: avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world”; it is a standardized benchmark with synthetic episode construction.
- Editorial: consider removing Section 3.6; it adds little relative to the core contributions.

Quality
The technical design is sound and well-engineered. The empirical evaluation is broad and includes relevant baselines. However, the paper would benefit from: (a) ablations on K, exact vs. ANN search, and write policies; (b) explicit analysis of approximation error vs. performance; (c) clearer discussion of gradient flow given discrete selection. Claims of “end-to-end differentiability” should note that the index and top-K are fixed, non-differentiable components.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful figures. Some implementation details are deferred to the supplement; several are essential for reproduction (ANN parameters, rebuild frequency, usage threshold, sparse formats, curriculum schedule). Tighten the exposition of the asymptotic analysis and state assumptions up front. Please also explicitly relate to MemNN-WSH and RL-NTM in the main text.

Originality
Conceptually incremental relative to prior attention scaling via hashing/ANN and discrete addressing via RL, but novel in its comprehensive application to writeable differentiable memory with a carefully engineered training pipeline (sparse BPTT, LRU allocation) and extensive empirical validation.

Significance
High practical significance: the methods enable training MANNs with very large memories and long sequences on standard hardware. This should be useful to researchers and practitioners working on memory-intensive tasks (reasoning, long-context modeling). The demonstrated extension to DNC further increases impact.

Specific questions and revision requests
- Provide ablations on K (including K=1) and sensitivity to ANN choice/parameters; contrast with exact KNN.
- Analyze failure cases and regimes where approximation hurts (e.g., high dimensionality, adversarial collisions).
- Clarify the “end-to-end differentiable” claim and discuss the effect of non-differentiable selection; compare with RL-based discrete addressing.
- Expand task construction and curriculum details; release code.
- Temper “non-synthetic/real-world” phrasing for Omniglot.
- If present, remove or condense Section 3.6.

Pros
- Strong scaling with compelling engineering.
- Broad and convincing experiments.
- General method applicable to multiple MANNs.

Cons
- Thin conceptual novelty relative to prior attention hashing/ANN.
- Limited analysis of approximation-induced failure modes and discreteness.
- Reproducibility hinges on unreleased details/code.

Recommendation
Accept (with minor revisions). This is a solid, practically important contribution that advances the usability of memory-augmented models at scale.

Confidence
High.
