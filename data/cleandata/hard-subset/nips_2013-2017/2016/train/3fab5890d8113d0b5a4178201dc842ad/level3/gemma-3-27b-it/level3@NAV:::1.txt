## Review of "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) to handle larger, more complex tasks. The authors introduce Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a method employing fast approximate nearest-neighbors and arg-top-K operations for attention in MANNs, aiming to significantly reduce computational and memory overhead during training. The core idea – limiting read/write operations to a sparse subset of memory – is a pragmatic approach to tackling the scalability issue inherent in traditional MANNs like NTMs and Memory Networks. 

The paper appropriately situates its work within the existing literature, referencing key works in memory networks (e.g., [21, 17]) and NTMs [7]. While the use of hashing for scalable lookups in large memories isn't entirely novel (as noted in the related work of Memory Networks), the specific application to writeable memories and the resulting engineering achievements are a valuable contribution. The reported speedups (1000x faster, 3000x less memory) are substantial and demonstrate the practical impact of SAM. The experimental results on both synthetic tasks and Omniglot are promising, showing comparable data efficiency with existing models while scaling to much larger problem sizes.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of depth in several areas. While the benefits of SAM are demonstrated, a more thorough analysis of failure cases is needed. Under what task characteristics or setup conditions does the method *not* work well? The authors should investigate the limitations of their approach. Additionally, the use of argmax/K-argmax introduces a non-smoothness that requires discussion. A comparison to fully smooth models and a more detailed explanation of how SAM's behavior relates to MemNN-WSH would strengthen the analysis. The paper also sidesteps the issue of training discrete actions without accounting for discreteness, which should be addressed. Section 3.6 feels extraneous and could be removed to streamline the presentation. Finally, characterizing Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world" is misleading; it's a curated dataset and lacks the complexity of truly real-world data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant practical contribution: substantial speed and memory improvements.
*   Solid experimental validation across various tasks.
*   Addresses a critical bottleneck in MANN research – scalability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty in the core idea of sparse memory access.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure modes and limitations.
*   Replicability concerns due to inadequate task descriptions.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with revisions. The authors have presented a valuable engineering contribution with promising results. However, addressing the points raised regarding failure analysis, non-smoothness, and replicability is crucial to elevate this work from a practical demonstration to a more robust scientific contribution. Crucially, the authors *must* provide detailed task construction instructions and release their code to ensure reproducibility.
