This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel neural memory architecture that addresses the scalability issues of traditional memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs). The authors propose an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme that retains the representational power of the original Neural Turing Machine (NTM) while allowing for efficient training with very large memories.

The paper relates to previous work on MANNs, such as NTMs and Memory Networks, which have shown promise in learning algorithmic solutions to complex tasks. However, these models suffer from poor scalability in both space and time as the amount of memory grows. The authors' proposal, SAM, aims to address this limitation by introducing sparse reads and writes to the external memory, which reduces the computational and memory overhead.

The technical soundness of the paper is evident in the authors' thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of their proposed scheme. They demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, making it optimal for large-scale memory applications. The authors also provide a detailed description of their architecture, including the sparse read and write operations, the controller, and the efficient backpropagation through time.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively conveys the problem and solution. The authors provide a thorough background on MANNs, NTMs, and Memory Networks, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with the field. The experimental results are also well-presented, with clear comparisons to previous models and a detailed analysis of the performance benefits of SAM.

One of the strengths of the paper is its originality. The authors propose several technically strong concepts, including the sparse access memory scheme, the efficient data structures for content-based read operations, and the sparse memory management scheme. These contributions have the potential to significantly impact the field of neural networks and machine learning.

The significance of the paper is also evident in its potential applications. Scaling memory systems for neural networks is a high-impact research direction, with potential applications in natural language processing, computer vision, and other areas. The authors' proposal has the potential to enable the training of neural networks with very large memories, which could lead to significant breakthroughs in these fields.

In terms of weaknesses, one potential limitation of the paper is the reliance on approximate nearest neighbor indexes, which may not always provide optimal results. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a thorough analysis of the trade-offs involved.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel and technically strong concept that addresses a significant limitation of traditional MANNs.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of their proposed scheme, demonstrating its optimality for large-scale memory applications.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively conveys the problem and solution.
* The experimental results are well-presented, with clear comparisons to previous models and a detailed analysis of the performance benefits of SAM.
* The paper has significant potential impact on the field of neural networks and machine learning.

Arguments against acceptance:
