This paper addresses the critical scalability problem in memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), such as the Neural Turing Machine (Graves et al., NIPS 2014). The authors propose a new architecture, Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which constrains the differentiable read and write operations to a small, sparse subset of the memory at each timestep. By leveraging efficient data structures like approximate nearest neighbor indices, SAM aims to reduce the prohibitive computational and physical memory costs associated with dense memory access, which scale linearly with memory size and hinder practical applications.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and important bottleneck for a promising class of models. Enabling MANNs to operate efficiently with very large external memories is a significant step forward and could unlock their use in complex, real-world domains like processing entire books or knowledge bases.
*   **Originality & Quality:** The approach of integrating sparse operations and ANN data structures into an end-to-end differentiable framework is novel and technically sound. The authors provide a strong theoretical motivation, showing that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in speed and memory usage while achieving performance comparable to, or even exceeding, dense models on benchmark tasks. The ability to scale to sequences in the hundreds of thousands is a powerful demonstration of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making a complex architecture relatively easy to understand.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   **Clarity:** While generally clear, the paper could be more self-contained. The introduction assumes significant familiarity with MANNs. Adding a brief background section on memory-augmented networks or a clearer initial explanation of the dense read/write operators would improve accessibility for a broader audience.
*   **Quality:** My main concern lies with the experimental comparison in the curriculum scaling experiments (Figure 3). The authors compare SAM with a massive logical memory (2 million words) against dense models with a small logical memory (64 words) by matching their total *physical* memory footprint. While this highlights SAM's memory efficiency, it may not be an entirely fair comparison of learning capability. It would be more informative to also include a comparison where both sparse and dense models have the same *logical* memory size to better isolate the performance impact of the sparse approximation itself.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to experimental framing and presentation, which do not detract from the core importance of the work.
