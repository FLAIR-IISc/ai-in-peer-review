This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by employing sparse read and write operations combined with efficient data structures, specifically approximate nearest neighbor search. The core idea is to reduce the computational and memory overhead associated with traditional MANNs like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, enabling training with significantly larger memories and longer sequences. The authors demonstrate substantial speedups (1000x faster, 3000x less memory) compared to non-sparse models, achieving comparable data efficiency on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition. They also adapt their approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), resulting in a faster and more performant variant.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in memory-augmented neural networks, notably NTMs [7] and Memory Networks [21]. It addresses the well-known scalability issues inherent in these models, where the computational cost and memory requirements grow linearly with the memory size. While techniques like locality-sensitive hashing have been used in information retrieval and related fields for scalable lookups, applying them within the differentiable framework of MANNs, especially to *writeable* memories, is a significant contribution.  Recent work such as [17] demonstrates effective memory networks, however, they don’t address the scalability problem to the degree that SAM does. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck associated with backpropagation through time (BPTT) and provide a solution by tracking sparse modifications to the memory.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Speed and Memory Improvements:** The reported speedups and memory reduction are substantial and address a critical limitation of MANNs. This makes scaling to more complex problems realistically feasible.
* **Practical Contribution:** The combination of sparse operations and efficient data structures provides a tangible engineering advance, demonstrating that large-scale MANNs can be trained efficiently.
* **Generalizability:**  The ability to adapt the SAM approach to the DNC highlights the broader applicability of the technique beyond the specific architecture introduced.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance on synthetic tasks and Omniglot demonstrates that the sparse approximations do not significantly degrade learning and, in some cases, may even improve it.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper presents a relatively clear explanation of the SAM architecture and the optimizations employed.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:**  The individual components (sparse operations, approximate nearest neighbor search) are not entirely novel. The core contribution lies in the *combination* of these techniques within a differentiable MANN and demonstrating the resulting scalability. While impactful, the incremental nature of the innovation should be acknowledged more explicitly.
* **Replicability Concerns:** The descriptions of the experimental tasks, particularly the Babi tasks and Omniglot setup, lack sufficient detail for straightforward replication.  Providing clear task construction details and, crucially, releasing the experimental code is essential.
* **Analysis of Failure Cases:**  The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of SAM.  Under what conditions does the method fail? Are there specific task characteristics or parameter settings that lead to poor performance?  Understanding these limitations is crucial for assessing the generalizability and robustness of the approach.
* **Discreteness and Smoothness:** The use of argmax or arg-top-K operations introduces a degree of discreteness into the model. The authors should discuss the potential effects of this discreteness, particularly in the case of K=1, and compare the performance to fully smooth models or the behavior of MemNN-WSH.  The training process makes discrete actions without explicitly accounting for that discreteness, a point worth discussing with reference to related work.
* **Section 3.6 (ANN):** This section feels somewhat tacked on and could be removed or significantly condensed. The specific ANN implementations (FLANN, LSH) feel less central to the core contribution of SAM.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in MANNs – scalability – with a well-motivated and effective approach.
* The empirical results demonstrate substantial improvements in both speed and memory usage without sacrificing performance.
* The adaptation to the DNC highlights the generalizability of the proposed technique.
* The work represents a solid engineering contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the individual components is limited.
* The lack of detail regarding experimental setup hinders replicability.
* The absence of analysis of failure cases and limitations weakens the overall assessment of the approach.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on major revisions. The authors should address the replicability concerns by providing detailed task descriptions and releasing their code. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of SAM, including an analysis of failure cases and the implications of the discrete operations, is essential.  I also recommend removing or condensing Section 3.6.  Finally, the authors should avoid characterizing Omniglot as "real-world"; it’s a curated dataset for meta-learning and not representative of naturally occurring data.  Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
