- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): end-to-end differentiable memory with K-sparse reads/writes using ANN for sublinear access.
- Key engineering innovation: in-place reversible write-log enabling BPTT with O(1) space per step and O(log N) time per step (for fixed K).
- Extends approach to Differentiable Neural Computer, yielding SDNC.
- Uses ANN data structures (k-d trees, LSH) inside training loop to achieve sublinear read time.
- Empirically shows large speed and memory improvements vs dense NTMs and DAM, with comparable or better data efficiency on synthetic tasks.
- Demonstrates scalability: millions of memory slots and thousands of untruncated BPTT steps via curriculum training.
- Strong results on Omniglot one-shot classification and competitive bAbI QA performance without supervised memory access.
- Well situated relative to prior work (Memory Networks, NTM, DNC); distinct from RL-based discrete addressing by preserving gradient training.
- Complexity analysis and rollback mechanism are plausible, simple, and technically sound given few per-step modifications.
- Write allocation policy (interpolate between updating read slots and LRU reallocation) is a reasonable, effective heuristic.
- Main concern: top-K selection is non-differentiable w.r.t. indices and may introduce bias; paper lacks discussion and ablation on K.
- Main concern: ANN is approximate; no sensitivity analysis of how neighbor-recall quality affects learning or final accuracy.
- Main concern: LRU-like allocation is task-agnostic; comparisons with alternative or learned allocation strategies are limited.
- Reproducibility/portability concern: relies on specialized data structures and ANN libraries; more implementation details and accelerator performance needed.
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized, but would benefit from ablation on K, reporting ANN recall/rebuild cadence, and a schematic of sparse write/erase flow.
- Originality: high — novel combination of sparse differentiable access, ANN retrieval, and reversible write-log.
- Significance: high — enables training with very long sequences and very large memories with large practical speed/memory gains.
- Recommendation: strong accept, despite missing ablations and theoretical characterization of sparse selection.
