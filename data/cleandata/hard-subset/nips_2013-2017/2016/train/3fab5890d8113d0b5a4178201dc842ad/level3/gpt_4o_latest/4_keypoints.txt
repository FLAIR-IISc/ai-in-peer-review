- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN using sparse read/write and efficient data structures to scale memory access.
- Claims SAM matches NTM representational power while achieving asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.
- Empirical results show up to 1,600x speedup and 3,000x memory reduction vs dense models on benchmarks.
- Problem is well-motivated: scaling memory is critical for language modeling and one-shot learning.
- Clear articulation of existing MANN limitations (computational and memory overheads) and SAM as a solution.
- Technical contributions are original and rigorous, notably sparse memory management and efficient BPTT.
- Use of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) methods for efficient memory access is a notable technical strength.
- Paper is well-written, organized, and supported by useful supplementary materials.
- SAM is distinguished from prior work and demonstrated to scale to millions of memory slots and thousands of time steps.
- Weakness: applicability to complex, real-world datasets is underexplored.
- Weakness: reliance on ANN methods (k-d trees, LSH) may cause issues like imbalanced structures during training and reduced generalizability.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a timely problem, offers a novel efficient solution, and provides strong theoretical and empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: needs more diverse real-world experiments and may be limited by specific ANN dependencies.
- Overall recommendation: strong scientific contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address noted weaknesses.
