- Paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale memory-augmented neural networks via sparse reads and writes.
- Well-motivated explanation of scalability limitations in existing MANNs (NTMs, Memory Networks).
- Claims SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.
- Reports large empirical improvements (≈1000x speedup, ≈3000x memory reduction) over dense models.
- Technical contribution: sparse read/write scheme enabling efficient computation and memory use.
- Technical contribution: memory management scheme that tracks usage and reuses unused memory blocks.
- Reviewer views core technical components (efficient backpropagation, approximate nearest neighbor via sparse access) as straightforward.
- Reviewer judges the technical novelty as insufficient for acceptance.
- Experiments demonstrate SAM on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot, and scaling to 100,000s of steps/memories.
- Reviewer finds experimental results interesting but not surprising and lacking explanation for why SAM succeeds.
- Paper is well-written, clear, and well-organized.
- Paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the sparse read/write and memory management mechanisms.
- Originality is acknowledged but judged not particularly surprising or clearly superior to prior methods.
- Significance is potentially high due to scalability, but perceived limited because results and novelty are not convincing.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel scalability approach, empirical demonstrations across tasks, potential field impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient technical novelty, straightforward components, unimpressive/marginal experiments, unclear justification for results.
- Overall recommendation: reject, with suggestion to add deeper technical detail and stronger/challenging experiments to justify contribution.
