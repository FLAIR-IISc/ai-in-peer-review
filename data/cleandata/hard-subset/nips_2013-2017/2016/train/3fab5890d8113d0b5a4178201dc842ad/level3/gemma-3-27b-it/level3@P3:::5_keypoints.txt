- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory to cut compute and memory costs without losing representational power.
- Reports dramatic empirical gains: up to 1000x speedups and 3000x memory reductions, plus ability to generalize to sequences up to 200,000 steps.
- Demonstrates improved curriculum learning and achieves state-of-the-art results on bAbI after adapting SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).
- Positions SAM as an end-to-end differentiable alternative to discrete/RL-based sparse addressing, addressing the quadratic bottleneck of prior MANNs.
- Provides a theoretical claim of asymptotic optimality in space and time (details in the supplement).
- Experimental validation spans synthetic tasks, Omniglot, and bAbI, offering strong empirical support.
- Shows generality by successfully integrating SAM into the DNC architecture.
- Paper is well written with useful supplementary material and implementation notes.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of why SAM improves generalization and curriculum learning; the causal mechanism is not well explored.
- Weakness: Insufficient sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters (sparsity level K and ANN indexing parameters).
- Weakness: Lacks direct comparative evaluation against other sparse memory methods (e.g., RL-based addressing) and discussion of trade-offs.
- Weakness: Missing detailed explanation of ANN choices (FLANN vs. LSH) and their impact on performance.
- Argument for acceptance: Addresses a critical scalability bottleneck, offers a novel, theoretically motivated method with broad applicability and strong empirical gains.
- Argument against acceptance: Needs deeper analysis of generalization mechanisms and more thorough hyperparameter and comparative evaluations.
- Overall assessment: Strong, significant contribution to memory-augmented neural networks that merits acceptance, pending additional analysis and comparisons.
