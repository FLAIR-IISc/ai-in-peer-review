## Review of "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes"

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) – their poor scalability due to the computational and memory overhead of dense read/write operations. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel approach that leverages sparse read and write operations alongside efficient data structures to significantly reduce these costs, achieving asymptotic improvements in space and time complexity. This work builds upon the foundation laid by Neural Turing Machines [Graves et al., 2014] and Memory Networks [Weston et al., 2014], aiming to make MANNs practical for more complex, real-world tasks. The problem of scaling MANNs has been a recurring theme in NIPS and related conferences, and this paper presents a compelling solution. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. Technically, the approach appears sound, with a clear explanation of how sparsity is introduced and maintained during both forward and backward passes. The experimental results are promising; SAM demonstrably achieves substantial speedups and memory reductions compared to dense models (NTM and DAM), while maintaining comparable performance on synthetic tasks and even surpassing them in some cases (priority sort). Importantly, the authors show SAM can scale to tasks requiring large memories and long sequences, which addresses the core motivation of the work. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, facilitating understanding of the proposed method. The extension to the Differentiable Neural Computer [Graves et al., 2016] further highlights the generality of the approach.

However, there is a point of concern regarding the experimental setup. Specifically, the comparison in Figure 4, where physical memory sizes are matched, may be an unfair comparison.  While a valid choice, comparing using the same *logical* memory capacity (holding the number of memory slots constant) across all models would arguably provide a more insightful evaluation of the efficiency gains achieved by SAM.  Additionally, while the paper mentions the use of Approximate Nearest Neighbor search, a more detailed discussion of the chosen ANN implementation and its impact on performance would be beneficial. A brief background on MANNs in the introduction, while not critical, would also enhance the paper's self-containedness for readers less familiar with the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant improvement in scalability for MANNs.
*   Strong experimental validation demonstrating comparable or better performance to existing approaches.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a novel and technically sound contribution.
*   Addresses a crucial bottleneck in the development of practical MANNs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Potential unfairness in the comparison setup in Figure 4.
*   Limited discussion of the ANN implementation. 



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks, offering a practical solution to a longstanding scalability issue. The strengths of the work outweigh the minor concerns, and the results are interesting and align well with theoretical expectations.
