- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale memory-augmented neural networks via sparse read/write while keeping end-to-end differentiability.  
- Addresses the linear time/memory bottleneck of prior MANNs (e.g., NTMs, Memory Networks).  
- Achieves asymptotic optimality: O(log N) time, O(N) initialization, O(1) per-step space, with up to 1,000× faster training and 3,000× less memory vs. dense implementations.  
- Provides thorough theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) supporting the sparse access scheme.  
- Empirically validates SAM across synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Maintains comparable data efficiency to dense models and scales to 100,000+ time steps and memory slots—first practical large-scale MANN implementation.  
- Generalizes to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and achieves state-of-the-art results on bAbI without supervised memory access.  
- Experimental quality is excellent with rigorous validation, ablation studies, and analysis of sparse approximation effects.  
- Paper clarity is outstanding, with clear descriptions of sparse read/write mechanisms and efficient backpropagation.  
- Originality is strong: differentiable, end-to-end sparse access is a novel technical contribution compared to prior RL-based sparse approaches.  
- Significance is high, addressing a key bottleneck for real-world large-scale memory usage in neural models.  
- Minor limitation: reliance on approximate nearest neighbor methods (k-d trees/LSH) and limited exploration of alternative ANN techniques (noted as future work).  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept.
