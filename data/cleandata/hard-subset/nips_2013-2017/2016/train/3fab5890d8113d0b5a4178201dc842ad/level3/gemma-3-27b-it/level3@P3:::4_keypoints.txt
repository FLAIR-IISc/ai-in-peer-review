- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an efficient sparse memory access scheme for MANNs that preserves NTM-like representational power while reducing overhead.
- Shows massive empirical gains: ~1,000x speedups and ~3,000x reduction in physical memory, maintaining data efficiency and scaling to 100,000s of time steps.
- Demonstrates applicability to Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), indicating generality beyond the specific model.
- Directly addresses the scalability bottleneck in memory-augmented networks, building on prior NTM/Memory Network/DNC work.
- Uses gradient-based sparsity and ANN search to avoid RL’s high-variance solutions for scaling memory access.
- Technical strengths: well-motivated design, solid theoretical asymptotic bounds, and detailed efficient sparse read/write and backpropagation schemes.
- Paper clarity: exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and accompanied by reproducible experimental details and supplementary material.
- Originality: novel differentiable sparse memory access and integration of approximate nearest neighbor search into training.
- Significance: enables training of much larger MANNs with practical speed/memory improvements; validated on synthetic and real tasks (e.g., Omniglot).
- Weakness: performance depends on choice and behavior of ANN implementations (e.g., k-d trees vs. LSH), requiring more analysis of trade-offs.
- Weakness: limited discussion in the main text on sensitivity to key hyperparameters (sparsity K, discount factor), details relegated to supplement.
- Weakness: comparisons to other recent memory-augmented architectures are somewhat limited; broader baselines would strengthen claims.
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a critical problem with strong theory, clear presentation, and substantial empirical improvements.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on efficient ANN implementations and relatively narrow comparative evaluation.
- Overall assessment: strong, high-impact contribution; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
