- Addresses MANNs’ scalability bottleneck by reducing dense O(NT) memory access to sparse accesses.
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), using ANN data structures for content reads and sparse write heuristics (read-or-LRU).
- Reduces time complexity to O(log N) and per-timestep space to O(1) by tracking only sparse updates.
- Significant impact: enables practical large-scale sequence tasks with reported >1,000x speedups and >3,000x memory reductions.
- Technical novelty: integrates ANN indices into differentiable content addressing and introduces efficient backprop by reverting sparse updates.
- Empirical validation: clear speed/memory benchmarks demonstrating asymptotic advantages over dense models.
- Empirical validation: matches or improves learning on standard NTM tasks and curriculum learning, enabling >4,000-step training where dense models fail.
- Empirical validation: remarkable generalization from 10,000-step training to 200,000-step sequences, indicating learned algorithmic behavior.
- Empirical validation: strong performance on bAbI reasoning and one-shot Omniglot perception tasks.
- Weakness: lacks deeper analysis of why sparse updates improve curriculum learning and long-sequence generalization (possible regularization effects).
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical ANN trade-offs—hyperparameter sensitivity, index imbalance, and rebuilding/maintenance overhead.
- Clarity: well-written, well-organized paper with informative figures supporting claims.
- Overall: significant, technically sound, and influential contribution; recommends accept.
