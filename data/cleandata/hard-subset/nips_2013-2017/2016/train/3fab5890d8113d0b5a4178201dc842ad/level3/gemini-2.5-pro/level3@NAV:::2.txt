This paper addresses the critical problem of scalability in Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs). Models like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks have shown promise but are hindered by computational and memory costs that scale linearly with the size of the external memory, making them impractical for large-scale problems. The authors introduce a Sparse Access Memory (SAM) model that constrains memory reads and writes to a small, constant-sized subset of memory slots. Reads are made efficient by using an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index to find the most relevant memory locations in logarithmic time, while writes target recently accessed or least-used slots. This approach allows the model to achieve impressive asymptotic complexity and, as the authors demonstrate, massive empirical gains in efficiency.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles a highly significant and practical bottleneck for an important class of models. The claimed improvements—up to 1000× in speed and 3000× in memory efficiency—are substantial and could enable new applications of MANNs.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling. The authors demonstrate on a range of tasks (algorithmic, question answering, one-shot learning) that the sparse approximation does not harm, and in some cases even helps, learning performance. The ability to scale tasks via curriculum learning to sequence lengths in the hundreds of thousands is a particularly strong result.
*   The paper is clear, well-motivated, and the proposed architecture is described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**
*   My main concern is the limited technical novelty of the core contributions. The approach is primarily an effective combination of existing techniques rather than a new fundamental concept. The two key components are:
    1.  Using ANN search to approximate a dense similarity search (i.e., the softmax attention). This is a standard and widely-used technique for accelerating retrieval in large databases.
    2.  An efficient backpropagation scheme that tracks sparse updates. This is a clever but straightforward engineering solution for systems with sparse modifications.
*   While the application of these ideas to MANNs is novel and the engineering is well-executed, the conceptual leap feels insufficient for a top-tier publication. The work reads more as a very strong systems paper demonstrating how to scale an existing model class, rather than introducing a new algorithmic or theoretical advance.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The empirical results are outstanding and address a major open problem. The demonstrated ability to train with massive memories is a significant practical contribution that other researchers will likely build upon.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The core technical ideas are not new. The contribution lies in the application and system engineering, which, while impressive, may lack the fundamental novelty expected at this conference.

In my view, while the experimental results are interesting and the engineering is solid, the lack of sufficient technical novelty is a significant weakness that prevents me from recommending acceptance.
