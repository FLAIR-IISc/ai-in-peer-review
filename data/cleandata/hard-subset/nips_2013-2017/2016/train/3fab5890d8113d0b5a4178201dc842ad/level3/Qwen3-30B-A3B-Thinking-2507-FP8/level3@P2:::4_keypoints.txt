- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), enforcing sparse read/write to scale MANNs while preserving differentiability.  
- Key innovations: restrict access to K (4–8) words via ANN and use usage tracking to overwrite stale slots.  
- Achieves asymptotically optimal complexity: O(1) space per step and O(log N) time per operation.  
- Uses ANN data structures (k-d trees/LSH) to enable efficient, differentiable sparse addressing.  
- Theoretical proofs of asymptotic optimality provided (Supplementary A).  
- Empirical speed/memory gains: ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× memory reduction vs. dense models (Fig.1).  
- Matches or outperforms dense MANNs on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort).  
- SDNC variant attains state-of-the-art on bAbI tasks without memory access supervision.  
- Demonstrates practical scalability: training with 100k+ memory words and generalization to 200k-sequence/2M slots.  
- Strong experimental validation includes ablation studies and curriculum scalability tests.  
- Paper is very clear, well-organized, and includes reproducibility details (data structures for O(1) backprop).  
- Original contribution: differentiable ANN-based sparse access within MANNs, outperforming prior RL-based sparse methods.  
- Significant impact: enables MANNs on real-world, large-scale sequence tasks (e.g., Wikipedia-length).  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of why sparsity sometimes improves learning (e.g., Priority Sort generalization).  
- Weakness: Lacks systematic comparison of ANN variants (runtime trade-offs across word dimensions).  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, original, significant, and well-validated contribution.
