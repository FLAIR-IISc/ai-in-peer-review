- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): differentiable memory with sparse top-K reads and targeted writes to scale sublinearly and keep O(1) BPTT space.
- Core techniques: ANN-based approximate top-K content lookup, writes to read locations + LRU slot, and rollback of sparse deltas for efficient backprop.
- Claims optimal asymptotics for content-based addressing with O(log N) per-step time and O(1) per-step space; reports large measured speed/memory gains (~1000× faster, ~3000× less memory vs dense NTMs).
- Empirical results: matches or exceeds dense baselines on synthetic algorithmic tasks, scales to millions of slots and long sequences, generalizes to longer sequences.
- Adaptation to DNC (SDNC) achieves near state-of-the-art on bAbI without supervised memory traces.
- On Omniglot one-shot classification, SAM outperforms dense MANNs when allowed much larger external memories.
- Strength: clear, focused contribution with a simple, well-motivated LRU+read-based write policy addressing O(N) bottlenecks.
- Strength: strong systems engineering (sparse data structures, kd-trees/LSH integration, in-place delta logging/rollback).
- Strength: theoretical and empirical alignment showing sparse gradients do not harm learning and can aid early learning.
- Strength: broad experimental suite demonstrating scalability and generalization across tasks and architectures.
- Strength: high relevance and potential impact for scaling MANNs to long-context problems.
- Weakness: fairness of comparisons—matching physical memory allows SAM much larger logical memory, conflating systems and algorithmic gains.
- Weakness: limited ablations (sensitivity to K, ANN type, rebuild frequency, thresholds, write policies) leave robustness unclear.
- Weakness: non-differentiable components (fixed ANN, hard top-K/LRU) may introduce bias; impact on convergence not fully discussed.
- Weakness: clarity could improve with a tighter MANN background, clearer write operator exposition, rollback data structure details, and hyperparameter tables.
- Weakness: limited “real-world” evaluation; missing large-scale tasks like language modeling or translation to strengthen significance.
- Quality: technical approach is sound, engineering matches complexity analysis, but some methodological choices complicate attribution of gains.
- Originality: novel combination of sparse differentiable access, in-network ANN lookup, and O(1)-space BPTT rollback.
- Significance: high—enables orders-of-magnitude scaling of differentiable memory and long-sequence training.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to K/ANN/LRU and failure modes; results at fixed logical memory sizes; cost/scaling of ANN rebuilds; comparisons with content-based sparse writes.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled, implementable solution to a central scalability bottleneck with strong empirical/asymptotic evidence and broad applicability.
- Arguments against acceptance: some comparisons may bias results toward SAM and ablations/real-world evaluations are limited.
- Overall: solid, well-executed paper recommended for acceptance with suggested clarifications and controlled comparisons.
