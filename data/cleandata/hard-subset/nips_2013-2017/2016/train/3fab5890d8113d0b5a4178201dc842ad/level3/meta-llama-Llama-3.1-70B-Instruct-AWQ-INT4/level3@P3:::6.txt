This paper proposes a novel neural memory architecture called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that addresses the scalability issue of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing sparse read and write operations. The authors claim that SAM achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art models while reducing the huge physical memory and backpropagation operations that make MANNs impractical.

The paper is well-structured and easy to read, making it accessible to a broad audience. The authors provide a clear background on MANNs, including Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, and explain the limitations of these models in terms of scalability. They then introduce the SAM architecture, which uses sparse read and write operations to reduce the computational and memory overhead.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM through a series of experiments on synthetic and natural tasks, including algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. The results show that SAM achieves significant speedups and memory reductions compared to dense models, while maintaining comparable performance.

The paper also explores the use of approximate nearest neighbor indexes within the network to reduce the forward pass of training to sublinear time. The authors discuss the trade-offs between different indexing approaches and suggest that there may be room for improvement in this area.

One potential concern with the paper is the comparison in Figure 4, which may be unfair since it matches physical memory sizes rather than logical memory sizes. A more informative comparison would be to compare the models using the same logical memory size.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and well-executed approach to scaling MANNs. The results are promising, and the algorithm performs close to theoretical bounds. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work, making it easy to follow and understand.
