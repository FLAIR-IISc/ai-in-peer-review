- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale memory-augmented neural networks using approximate nearest neighbors and sparse read/write to reduce compute and memory overhead.  
- Shows significant speedups and reduced memory vs standard NTM, with comparable performance on synthetic and one-shot tasks and scaling to very long sequences.  
- Builds on prior MANN and hashing/ANN work; novelty mainly in applying these techniques to writeable memories and engineering a differentiable implementation.  
- Practical contribution: enables training MANNs with large memories, opening up more complex problems.  
- Soundness: theoretical time/space claims seem reasonable and are supported by empirical results.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with helpful supplementary implementation details and experiments.  
- Demonstrated scalability: successful curriculum learning to scale tasks to tens of thousands of time steps.  
- Limited novelty: core ideas (approximate NN and sparse access) are not entirely new; main contribution is application/engineering.  
- Replicability concerns: task descriptions, hyperparameters, and preprocessing are insufficient; code release is essential.  
- Lack of failure-case analysis: missing systematic investigation of when SAM fails, comparative limits vs dense models, and task characteristics that affect success.  
- Non-smoothness of argmax/K-argmax: discrete selection introduces non-smoothness and potential gradient issues that are not sufficiently discussed; behavior for K=1 needs clarification.  
- Discrete actions & training: paper does not explicitly address implications of making discrete memory-location selections during training.  
- Section 3.6 (usage statistic definition) seems tangential and could be removed for conciseness.  
- Omniglot characterization is misleadingly labeled as "non-synthetic" or "real-world"; it is a curated character dataset and should be described more accurately.  
- Arguments for acceptance: substantial practical gains, well-motivated approach, and strong empirical results across diverse tasks.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, lack of detailed descriptions/code, and missing analysis of limitations and failure modes.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with revisions—authors must add detailed task descriptions, release code, analyze failure cases, discuss argmax/K-argmax non-smoothness, remove/trim Sec. 3.6, and correct Omniglot characterization.
