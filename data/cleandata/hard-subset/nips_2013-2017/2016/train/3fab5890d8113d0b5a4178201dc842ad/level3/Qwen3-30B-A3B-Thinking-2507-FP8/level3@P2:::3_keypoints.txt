- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel sparse read/write scheme for memory-augmented neural networks.
- Identifies linear computation and memory overhead in existing MANNs as a critical scalability bottleneck.
- SAM restricts reads and writes to a fixed-size sparse subset of memory words instead of the entire memory.
- Provides theoretical justification for SAM’s asymptotic optimality in time and space (Supplementary A).
- Demonstrates empirical speedups (up to ~1,000×) and memory reductions (up to ~3,000×) for large memories.
- Evaluation covers synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot classification.
- Empirical results show SAM achieves comparable or better learning performance than dense alternatives while being far more efficient.
- Authors analyze when sparsity benefits learning (Section 4.2) and note that benefits vary by task.
- Paper is technically sound with strong theoretical support and comprehensive experiments.
- Writing, organization, and mathematical notation are clear; supplementary materials enable reproducibility.
- SAM is original: an end-to-end differentiable sparse MANN with theoretical optimality guarantees, distinct from prior sparse or RL-based approaches.
- Significance: makes MANNs practical for real-world tasks by scaling to hundreds of thousands of memory slots.
- Strengths: novel idea, strong theory, compelling empirical evidence, thorough implementation details.
- Weaknesses: needs deeper analysis of when/why sparsity helps, more exploration of ANN implementations (k-d trees vs. LSH), and stronger proofs in Supplementary A.
- Pro-acceptance: addresses key scalability issue, backed by theory and large efficiency gains, with comprehensive evaluation and clear writing.
- Con-acceptance: limited analysis of sparsity effects, some theoretical claims require fuller support, and alternative ANN choices not fully explored.
- Overall recommendation by reviewer: accept.
