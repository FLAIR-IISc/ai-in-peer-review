- Problem: MANNs (e.g., NTMs, Memory Networks) face severe scalability limits due to dense read/write and BPTT memory duplication.
- Contribution: Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that uses sparse reads/writes to reduce space and time complexity.
- Core idea: Limit modifications to a sparse subset of memory and use efficient data structures for content-based reads.
- Theoretical claim: Achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.
- Empirical results: Reports up to 1,000x speedup and 3,000x reduction in physical memory versus dense models.
- Evaluation: Thorough experiments on synthetic algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning, and one-shot Omniglot, scaling to 100Ks time steps.
- Adaptability: SAM is adaptable to Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and yields state-of-the-art Babi results without supervised memory access.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes helpful supplementary implementation details.
- Weakness — background: Needs a more comprehensive introduction to the broader MANN landscape and intuitive explanation of the write operator.
- Weakness — comparisons: Figure 4 compares physical memory usage; comparing logical memory size could provide additional, possibly different, insights.
- Weakness — ANN details: Limited exploration of ANN (approximate nearest neighbor) parameter trade-offs between accuracy and speed.
- Novelty concern: Sparsity concept isn’t entirely novel, though its specific application and gains for MANNs are significant.
- For acceptance: Addresses a critical limitation, is novel and well-motivated, with strong theoretical and empirical support and clear presentation.
- Against acceptance: Minor issues include limited ANN exploration and the generality of sparsity as an idea.
- Overall assessment: Strong paper making a significant, well-supported contribution to scalable MANNs.
- Recommendation: Strongly Accept.
