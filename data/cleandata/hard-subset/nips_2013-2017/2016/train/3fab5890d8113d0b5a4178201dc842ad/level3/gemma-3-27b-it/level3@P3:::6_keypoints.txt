- Proposal: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a sparse read/write scheme for NTMs that limits operations to a subset of memory locations to improve scalability.
- Core claim: SAM reduces time and space complexity without sacrificing representational power.
- Empirical results: Demonstrates large speedups and memory savings (reported ~1,000x faster, ~3,000x less memory) while maintaining competitive performance on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and one-shot Omniglot.
- Generality: Shows SAM can be adapted to other architectures, including the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC).
- Theoretical alignment: Authors argue and provide evidence that SAM approaches asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.
- Contribution to field: Directly addresses the major scalability bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, well-structured, and supplemented with useful figures, tables, and appendices.
- Strength of experiments: Scaling experiments (including curriculum learning) are particularly convincing.
- Weakness — background: Background section is somewhat limited; more context on MANNs and clearer explanation of the write operator (Eq.3) and weight matrices are needed.
- Weakness — comparisons: Figure 4’s fairness is questionable because it matches physical memory sizes rather than logical memory capacity, which may conflate algorithmic benefits with allocation differences.
- Weakness — ablations: Lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate contributions of components (e.g., sparse reads vs. writes, usage definitions).
- Overall assessment: High-quality, novel, and impactful work that substantially advances practical scalability of MANNs.
- Arguments for acceptance: Solves a critical problem, shows major efficiency gains, retains competitive performance, is clearly presented, and is broadly applicable.
- Arguments against acceptance: Minor issues in background, comparison methodology, and insufficient ablation analysis.
- Recommendation: Strongly accept.
