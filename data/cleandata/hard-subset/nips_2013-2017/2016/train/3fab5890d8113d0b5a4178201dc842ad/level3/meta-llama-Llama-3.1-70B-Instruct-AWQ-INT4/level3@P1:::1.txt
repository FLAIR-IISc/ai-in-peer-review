The paper "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes" proposes a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing sparse reads and writes. The authors claim that their approach, called Sparse Access Memory (SAM), achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, making it more efficient than traditional MANNs.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its components. The use of sparse reads and writes is a significant innovation, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in reducing the computational and memory overhead of MANNs.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. The introduction of sparse reads and writes in MANNs, which enables efficient training on large problems without degrading performance.
2. The use of fast approximate nearest-neighbors and arg-top-K for attention in an NTM, which is a novel application of these tools to writeable memories.

The paper's strengths are:

1. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the SAM architecture and its components, including the sparse read and write operations, the controller, and the efficient backpropagation through time.
2. The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM in various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks.
3. The authors provide a detailed discussion of the related work, including Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines, and highlight the novelty of their approach.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

1. The novelty of the paper is somewhat thin, as related work (e.g., Memory Networks section 3.3) and other papers already use large memories with hashing for scalable lookups.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the failure cases and limits of the SAM approach. The authors should provide more information on when the method fails, and under what task/setup conditions.
3. The non-smoothness of argmax/K-argmax is not thoroughly discussed, and the authors should provide more information on the effects of this non-smoothness (e.g., K=1) and comparison to fully smooth models and MemNN-WSH behavior.
4. The paper does not discuss the implications and limits of training with discrete actions without accounting for discreteness, unlike some prior work.

To address these concerns, the authors should:

1. Provide more details on the task construction and experiment setup to ensure replicability.
2. Analyze the failure cases and limits of the SAM approach and provide more information on when the method fails and under what conditions.
3. Discuss the effects of the non-smoothness of argmax/K-argmax and compare it to fully smooth models and MemNN-WSH behavior.
4. Discuss the implications and limits of training with discrete actions without accounting for discreteness.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of MANNs, and the authors' approach has the potential to enable efficient training on large problems. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and discussion of the limitations and failure cases of the SAM approach.
