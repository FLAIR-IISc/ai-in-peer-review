- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): uses ANN-based arg-top-K reads and sparse locality-driven writes for scalable, writable memory with O(log N) reads and O(1) step space.  
- Main novelty: applying sparse ANN/top-K to both reads and writes in an end-to-end trained, writeable memory with a practical implementation.  
- Empirical claim: matches or exceeds dense NTMs/DNCs on synthetic tasks, bAbI, and one-shot Omniglot while giving orders-of-magnitude speed and memory savings.  
- Engineering and complexity analysis are strong; scaling experiments are impressive.  
- Major concern: key claims rely on non-smooth operations (top-K/argmax, ANN) and need qualification and empirical analysis of optimization effects.  
- Clarity issues: manuscript omits critical experimental details, hindering reproducibility; request removal of Section 3.6 and avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”  
- Originality: conceptual novelty is limited since hashing/ANN for sparse attention is established for read-only memories; applying it to writable memories is useful but incremental.  
- Significance: high practical impact; techniques likely to be widely adopted for large-memory training.  
- Replicability: task descriptions and full experimental settings are insufficient; request full hyperparameters, ANN index settings (type, rebuild policy, distance metric), K strategy, and code release.  
- Requested analyses: study optimization effects of arg-top-K (incl. K=1) vs. smooth dense attention and MemNN-WSH.  
- Requested analyses: discuss discreteness—implications of discrete selections without RL-style handling, and consider straight-through or relaxation baselines.  
- Requested analyses: characterize failure modes across K, memory size, sequence length, and task structure.  
- Requested analyses: add baselines vs. large-memory read-only hashing methods and smooth sparse approximations.  
- Pros: solid engineering, strong speed/space gains, demonstrated scalability, competitive task performance.  
- Cons: limited novelty, incomplete reproducibility and ablations, insufficient discussion of non-smoothness and discrete decisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept with revisions.
