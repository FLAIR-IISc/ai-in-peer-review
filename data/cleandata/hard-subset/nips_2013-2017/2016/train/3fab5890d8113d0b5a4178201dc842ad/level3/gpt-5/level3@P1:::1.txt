This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes, combined with approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indices to support sublinear-time content-based addressing. The authors show a backward-in-time “rollback” scheme that stores only sparse diffs, yielding O(1) per-step space during BPTT and O(log N) time per access (with kd-trees or LSH). Empirically, SAM matches dense NTMs/DNC-like models on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot one-shot, while enabling orders-of-magnitude larger memories and much longer sequences. The paper also outlines a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), which performs strongly on bAbI without supervised memory access.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds directly on NTMs/DNCs for differentiable addressing and on Memory Networks for content-based retrieval, connecting to large-scale MemNN systems that used hashing/subsampling to restrict soft attention to a candidate set (e.g., Hill et al., “Goldilocks,” and related MemNN-WSH variants). Unlike those retrieval-only models, SAM supports write operations at scale with end-to-end training; unlike RL-based discrete addressing (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, RL-NTM), SAM stays within (piecewise) differentiable training. The use of ANN structures inside the network is an engineering contribution that enables practical scaling while retaining content-based addressing semantics.

Quality: The technical design is careful and well-motivated. The time/space analysis and the in-place rollback for BPTT are convincing, and the empirical benchmarks show large wall-clock and memory savings (up to ~1000x and ~3000x) without obvious loss in data efficiency. That said, several claims hinge on implementation choices (ANN type, rebuild policy, K, threshold ε), and the paper would benefit from a deeper ablation and failure-mode analysis. The theoretical “optimal” claims depend on assumptions about content-addressable models; clarifying these assumptions and the scope of optimality would strengthen the paper.

Clarity: Overall clear and readable, with useful figures. A few descriptions (task generation, ANN parameters, usage-tracking data structure) are too terse for reproduction. The distinction between DAM (dense) and SAM (sparse) is clear, but some design choices (e.g., K selection, ε threshold, kd-tree vs LSH regime) need more justification.

Originality: The main ideas—top-K attention, ANN-based retrieval, and sparse writes—are individually known, and hashed/candidate-restricted attention has precedent in MemNNs. The novelty lies in making these tools work in a writeable, end-to-end differentiable memory with efficient BPTT, and demonstrating that this yields stable training and strong performance at previously impractical scales. This is a solid engineering/architectural contribution, albeit with relatively thin conceptual novelty.

Significance: The ability to scale MANNs to millions of slots and tens/hundreds of thousands of steps is valuable and likely to impact subsequent research. The SDNC result on bAbI without supervision is also noteworthy. The approach seems general enough to retrofit to other MANNs.

Strengths
- Clear, principled sparsification of reads and writes with strong asymptotic and empirical benefits.
- Practical ANN integration with careful bookkeeping (synchronization, rebuild policies) and O(1) per-step BPTT memory via rollback.
- Strong runtime/memory benchmarks and competitive performance across tasks.
- Application to DNC shows generality.

Weaknesses/concerns
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior hashed/candidate attention in MemNNs; the conceptual step is modest.
- Non-smoothness of top-K selection is under-discussed; end-to-end “differentiable” claim glosses over index-set discreteness.
- Limited ablations: sensitivity to K, ANN choice, threshold ε, usage heuristic, and erase/add gating not fully explored.
- Replicability: insufficient detail for exact task construction and ANN/hyperparameter settings; code not provided.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and limits (where and why SAM fails).

Requests for clarification and revision
- Discreteness/smoothness: Please discuss the implications of arg top-K masking for gradients (especially K=1). What are the observed effects on stability vs dense softmax models? How does SAM compare to “sparse-soft” alternatives (e.g., sparsemax) that yield sparse attention while remaining fully differentiable on support?
- Candidate restriction vs MemNN hashing: Add a direct comparison and discussion of differences/similarities to MemNN-WSH/candidate-restricted attention (e.g., Goldilocks Sec. 3.x). In particular, do you softmax within the top-K set? How is bias introduced by ANN errors handled during training?
- Ablations and sensitivity: Provide results varying K, ε, ANN type (kd-tree vs LSH), rebuild frequency, and the usage/overwrite policy. Include a breakdown of speedups from (i) sparse reads, (ii) sparse writes, (iii) rollback, (iv) ANN vs linear scan, and CPU vs GPU effects.
- Failure modes: Identify tasks where SAM/SDNC underperform or fail (which bAbI tasks?), analyze why (e.g., temporal links vs content reads, ANN miss rates, overwrite policy), and describe regimes where SAM is not appropriate.
- Reproducibility: Include detailed task construction (distributions, seeds, curriculum schedules), model sizes, optimizer settings (e.g., RMSProp params, clipping), memory sizes, ANN libraries and parameters, rebuild policies, and exact training budgets. Please release code for the core SAM ops and experiments.
- Theoretical claims: State the assumptions behind “asymptotically optimal” time/space bounds for content addressing and clarify what classes of models are covered.
- Editing suggestions: Consider removing Section 3.6 (it reads as incidental/standard and distracts from the core contributions). Also, avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world”; “non-synthetic benchmark” is more accurate.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong practical contribution that materially advances the scalability of MANNs.
- Careful systems design with clear analysis and impressive empirical speed/memory gains.
- Competitive or superior task performance with orders-of-magnitude larger memories.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited relative to prior candidate/hashing approaches.
- Insufficient discussion of the consequences of discrete top-K selection and limited ablations.
- Reproducibility concerns without additional details/code.

Recommendation: Accept, pending revisions addressing smoothness/discreteness discussion, ablations, failure analyses, and reproducibility details. This work will be useful to the community and is appropriate for NIPS.
