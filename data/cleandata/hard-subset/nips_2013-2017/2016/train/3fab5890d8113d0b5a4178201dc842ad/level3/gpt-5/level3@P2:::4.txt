Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory-augmented neural network that scales to very large external memories by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes. SAM performs content-based reads via an approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) index to select the top-K memory slots and performs sparse writes to either previously read locations or the least-recently-used location, supported by a usage-tracking memory management scheme. It further describes an efficient backpropagation-through-time strategy that rolls memory back by reverting only the sparse updates, yielding O(1) space per time step and O(log N) time per step (under standard assumptions about the ANN structure), with O(N) initialization. The paper also outlines how the approach can be adapted to DNC-like temporal linkages (SDNC), demonstrating large speedups.

The work builds on NTMs and Memory Networks, both of which decouple memory capacity from parameter count but incur O(N) read/write costs and O(NT) training memory. Unlike RL-based discrete addressing (e.g., RL-NTM), SAM maintains full differentiability. Compared to end-to-end Memory Networks, SAM supports online writing and long-range temporal credit assignment with efficient BPTT. The SDNC variant relates directly to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), showing the generality of the sparse access idea.

Empirically, SAM matches or improves the data efficiency of dense models (NTM, a dense variant “DAM”) on classic NTM tasks, scales via curriculum to sequence lengths in the thousands to tens of thousands, generalizes to sequences longer than trained (up to 200k steps), attains strong results on bAbI without supervision of memory retrieval (SDNC solves all but one task), and achieves improved one-shot performance on Omniglot under a scalable curriculum. The reported wall-clock speedups (100–1600×) and memory savings (~3000×) are substantial.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The sparse read/write design, LRU-based memory management, and rollback BPTT are well-motivated and carefully implemented. The complexity claims are justified under standard assumptions (balanced ANN, constant K) and supported by benchmarks showing sublinear scaling with ANN and constant per-step memory.
- Evidence: Experiments span synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot. The comparisons to NTM, LSTM, and a dense variant (DAM) are appropriate. The scaling experiments and generalization tests are convincing. The SDNC adaptation strengthens the claim of generality.
- Caveats:
  - The use of approximate NN introduces nondifferentiable, approximate selection; while gradients do not flow through the ANN, the induced approximation error is only partially characterized. More detailed ablations of ANN type/quality vs performance would help.
  - The sparse write scheme (previous reads + LRU) is intuitive and efficient, but limits direct content-based writes; the trade-offs would benefit from ablations (e.g., allowing sparse content-based writes) and a sensitivity analysis of K, ε in usage, and gating.
  - Some baselines could be expanded: e.g., stronger Memory Networks variants on Omniglot and more direct comparison to contemporary memory-augmented meta-learners (Santoro et al.) under matched protocols.
  - Practicality details (GPU/CPU hybridization for ANN, rebuild overhead amortization) could be elaborated for reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The main ideas are explained concisely, with sufficient intuition and concrete algorithms. Derivations and complexity arguments are mostly deferred to the supplement, which is acceptable, though a brief in-text algorithmic summary/pseudocode of the rollback mechanism and memory manager would aid reproduction. Figures convincingly illustrate scaling. Hyperparameters are said to be in the supplement.

Originality
The combination of:
- sparse, end-to-end differentiable read/writes,
- integrating ANN structures into the forward pass of a MANN,
- an O(1)-per-step BPTT via in-place sparse diffs and rollback,
- and extension to DNC-like temporal links
is, to my knowledge, novel at NIPS 2016. Related discrete-addressing approaches rely on RL and have different optimization challenges. The paper clearly differentiates from prior NTMs, Memory Networks, and DNC.

Significance
Scaling MANNs is an important problem with impact on long-context modeling (e.g., language, reasoning). The asymptotic improvements and empirical speed/memory savings remove a key bottleneck and enable training on sequences and memories previously impractical. The results on bAbI (without supervision) and Omniglot are competitive, and the demonstrated generalization to much longer sequences is compelling. This work is likely to be adopted and extended by others.

Pros
- Conceptually clean sparse read/write design with principled complexity improvements.
- Efficient BPTT rollback leading to O(1) per-step training memory.
- Integration of ANN into differentiable memory access and demonstration of sublinear query time.
- Strong empirical speedups (up to 1600×) and memory savings (~3000×).
- Good performance across synthetic tasks, bAbI (notably SDNC), and Omniglot; strong scaling and generalization.
- Clear presentation; generality shown by SDNC.

Cons
- Limited ablation on key design choices (K, write scheme variants, ε for usage) and robustness to ANN approximation quality.
- Some baseline gaps or mismatches (e.g., matched-capacity comparisons, broader Memory Networks variants on Omniglot).
- Practical engineering details (device placement for ANN, rebuild frequency amortization) not fully discussed.
- Real-world evaluation beyond synthetic and Omniglot is limited.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to K and to the threshold ε in usage tracking? Is there a degradation regime?
2. What is the amortized cost of periodically rebuilding the ANN, and how does it affect end-to-end training speed for very large N?
3. Have you tried sparse content-based writes (top-K near a write-key) and, if so, how do they compare to the previous-reads+LRU scheme?
4. Can you provide more details on device placement (CPU vs GPU) for ANN operations and their impact on throughput?
5. On bAbI, which version and training set size (1k vs 10k) were used, and how many hops? Are results averaged over seeds?

Recommendation
Accept (strong). This paper presents a technically solid, original, and impactful approach to scaling differentiable memory with clear theoretical and empirical benefits. With minor additional ablations and clarifications, it would be an excellent NIPS contribution.
