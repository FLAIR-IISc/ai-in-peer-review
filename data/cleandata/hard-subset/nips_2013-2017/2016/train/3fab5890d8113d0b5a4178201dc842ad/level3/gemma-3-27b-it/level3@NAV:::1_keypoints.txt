- Paper addresses scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs) via Sparse Access Memory (SAM) using approximate nearest-neighbors and top-K attention to reduce compute/memory.
- Core idea: limit read/write to a sparse subset of memory to make writable memories scalable.
- Engineering contribution is valuable despite related prior use of hashing for lookups in Memory Networks.
- Reported empirical gains are substantial (e.g., ~1000x speedups, ~3000x less memory).
- Experiments on synthetic tasks and Omniglot show comparable data efficiency while scaling to larger problems.
- Related work is cited appropriately, but novelty of sparse lookup concept is limited.
- Paper lacks thorough analysis of failure cases and conditions where SAM fails.
- Use of argmax/K-argmax introduces non-smoothness that is not sufficiently discussed or compared to fully smooth models.
- Training with discrete actions is not properly addressed—discreteness concerns are overlooked.
- Comparison and relation to MemNN-WSH need more detailed explanation.
- Section 3.6 appears extraneous and could be removed to streamline the paper.
- Characterizing Omniglot as "non-synthetic" or "real-world" is misleading; it's a curated dataset with limited real-world complexity.
- Replicability concerns: task descriptions are inadequate and authors must release code and detailed instructions.
- Recommendation: accept with revisions contingent on addressing failure analysis, non-smoothness/discreteness issues, and reproducibility requirements.
