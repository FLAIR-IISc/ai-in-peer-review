This paper tackles the well-known scaling limitations of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), such as NTMs and Memory Networks, whose dense content-based reads/writes and BPTT lead to linear-in-memory time per step and O(NT) space. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which restricts reads and writes to a constant K locations via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search for content-based reads and a simple policy (previous reads plus least-recently-used) for writes. They further implement an efficient BPTT scheme by logging sparse updates instead of storing full memory snapshots. Empirically, they report large gains (≈1000× faster training, ≈3000× lower memory) with comparable data efficiency on NTM-style synthetic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning. This work is related to Memory Networks (Sukhbaatar et al., NIPS 2015), NTMs (Graves et al.), and discrete-addressing RL-NTMs (Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015), and draws on classical ANN and sparse linear algebra techniques (e.g., kd-trees, LSH).

Strengths:
- Addresses a consequential bottleneck for MANNs with a simple, practical design; the engineering is careful and the speed/memory benchmarks are compelling.
- Broad empirical evaluation, including long-horizon curricula and an adaptation to DNC-style temporal linking.
- Clear articulation of the operations, complexity targets, and design choices; supplementary details are useful.

Weaknesses:
- Technical novelty appears limited. The core components—top-K sparse reads via ANN, LRU-style allocation for sparse writes, and BPTT via reversible sparse updates—are straightforward applications of existing tools rather than new algorithms or analyses.
- Claims of “retaining representational power” and asymptotic optimality are not supported by formal guarantees; results are empirical and hinge on ANN approximations and small K.
- Missing comparisons to other scalable memory/attention variants (e.g., discrete addressing via RL, alternative sparse indexing schemes), and limited ablations on K, ANN recall, rebuild frequency, or write policies.
- Some reproducibility concerns: performance may depend sensitively on ANN configuration and constants hidden in O(log N).

Quality: Sound implementation and experiments, but theory and comparative analysis are thin. Clarity: Generally well written; moving some key algorithmic and complexity assumptions from the supplement to the main paper would help. Originality: Incremental. Significance: Practical impact could be high if widely adopted, but the scientific contribution is modest.

Pros for acceptance:
- Important problem; impressive empirical speed/memory gains.
- Demonstrates training at scales previously infeasible.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited technical novelty and formal backing.
- Insufficient comparative and ablation studies to isolate what drives success.

Overall, interesting engineering with strong experiments, but not sufficiently novel for NIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form.
