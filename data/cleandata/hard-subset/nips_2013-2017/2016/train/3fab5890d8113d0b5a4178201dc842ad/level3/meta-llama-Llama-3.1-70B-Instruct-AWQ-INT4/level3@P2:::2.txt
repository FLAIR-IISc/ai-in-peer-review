This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that aims to improve their scalability. The authors argue that traditional MANNs, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, suffer from poor scaling due to their smooth read and write operations, which incur linear computational overhead on the number of memories stored per time step of training. To address this issue, SAM proposes a sparse read and write scheme that uses efficient data structures within the network.

The paper relates to previous work on MANNs, including NTMs and Memory Networks, which have been successful in various tasks such as language modeling and machine translation. However, these models have limited applicability to real-world domains due to their poor scaling. The authors claim that SAM retains the representational power of these models while being much more efficient, achieving asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.

The main technical components of SAM are the efficient backpropagation through time and the approximate nearest neighbor search via sparse access. However, the reviewer finds these components to be straightforward and not particularly novel. The efficient backpropagation is achieved by tracking the sparse modifications made to the memory at each time step and applying them in-place to compute the memory state. The approximate nearest neighbor search is performed using an efficient data structure, such as a k-d tree or locality-sensitive hashing.

The paper presents several experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM. The authors compare SAM with its dense variant, DAM, and other established models, such as NTMs and LSTMs, on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results show that SAM achieves comparable performance to the dense models while being much faster and more memory-efficient.

However, the reviewer finds the technical contribution of the paper to be insufficient for acceptance. While the experiments are interesting and demonstrate the potential of SAM, they do not outweigh the perceived lack of technical novelty. The reviewer believes that the paper would benefit from more in-depth analysis and comparison with other related work, such as the Differentiable Neural Computer.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its components, and the experiments are well-designed and executed. However, the reviewer finds the claims of the authors to be somewhat exaggerated, particularly with regards to the novelty and impact of the technical contributions.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the SAM architecture and its components, and the experiments are well-designed and executed. However, the reviewer finds some of the notation and terminology to be unclear, particularly with regards to the usage of certain variables and functions.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a new memory access scheme for MANNs, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the reviewer finds the technical components of SAM to be not particularly novel, and the paper could benefit from more in-depth analysis and comparison with other related work.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates the potential of SAM to improve the scalability of MANNs, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the reviewer finds the impact of the paper to be somewhat limited, particularly with regards to the novelty and impact of the technical contributions.
