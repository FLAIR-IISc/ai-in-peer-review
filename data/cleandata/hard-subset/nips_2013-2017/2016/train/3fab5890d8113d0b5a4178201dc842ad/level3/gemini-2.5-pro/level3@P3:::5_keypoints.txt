- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to make Memory-Augmented Neural Networks scalable by replacing dense read/write with sparse operations.  
- Uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) structures to retrieve K relevant memory slots, reducing read complexity from O(N) to O(log N).  
- Proposes a write scheme that updates recently read locations or least-recently-used slots to keep writes sparse.  
- Presents an efficient backpropagation method that tracks only sparse memory modifications, reducing per-step space complexity from O(N) to O(1).  
- Demonstrates large empirical speedups and memory savings (up to ~1000x speed, ~3000x memory) through rigorous benchmarks (Figure 1).  
- Validated across tasks: synthetic algorithmic problems, reasoning (bAbI), and one-shot learning (Omniglot), matching or exceeding dense baselines.  
- Extends approach to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), showing generality of techniques.  
- Highly original integration of ANN indices into end-to-end differentiable MANN read/write heads and sparse backpropagation (“rolling back”) is a key novelty.  
- Claims high significance: removes the main barrier to practical MANNs and enables training on much longer sequences and larger memories.  
- Paper is clear and well-written, with helpful figures and supplementary material for reproducibility.  
- Striking generalization: SAM generalizes to sequences far longer than training and excels in curriculum learning, but the paper lacks deep analysis of why.  
- Hypothesis offered: sparsity may act as regularization, forcing sharp queries and algorithmic learning rather than brittle pattern-matching.  
- Suggestion: add deeper analysis of how sparsity drives generalization and how ANN index dynamics interact with non-stationary memory during training.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid, empirically impressive, minor weaknesses are discussion gaps; recommendation = Strong Accept.
