This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory-augmented neural network that performs sparse reads and writes while retaining the representational power of the Neural Turing Machine (NTM). The key ideas are: (i) top-K content-based reads implemented via an in-network approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), (ii) sparse writes targeted to previously read locations and the least recently used (LRU) slot, and (iii) reversible memory updates to enable O(1) per-step space during BPTT. Relative to prior work—NTM (Graves et al.), Memory Networks (Weston et al.; Sukhbaatar et al.), DNC (Graves et al.), and RL-based discrete addressing (Zaremba & Sutskever)—SAM preserves differentiability while achieving asymptotically optimal time/space for content-based access. Experiments show up to 1000× speed and 3000× memory savings, comparable data efficiency on classic NTM tasks, scaling to millions of memory slots and >100k steps, strong bAbI results with a sparse DNC variant, and improved one-shot performance on Omniglot.

Quality: The technical contributions are sound and well supported by complexity analysis and empirical benchmarks. The reversible-memory BPTT and sparse memory management are carefully engineered. Some claims (e.g., optimality) hinge on assumptions deferred to the supplement; a brief main-text statement of conditions would strengthen the paper. Additional ablations (e.g., sensitivity to K, ANN recall, and write policy) would improve rigor.

Clarity: Exceptionally clear and concise. The problem is well-motivated; algorithms, data structures, and training setup are well explained. Minor suggestion: centralize ANN hyperparameters and rebuild schedules in the main text.

Originality: High. While sparsity and ANN are known tools, integrating them into a fully differentiable MANN with reversible BPTT and demonstrating generalization to DNC is novel and technically strong.

Significance: High. Efficiently scaling differentiable memory is impactful for long-context sequence modeling and algorithmic reasoning. The results are likely to be adopted and extended.

Pros:
- Elegant, end-to-end differentiable sparse access retaining NTM expressivity.
- Strong asymptotic and practical gains (time/space), demonstrated at scale.
- Clear, well-written presentation with broad applicability (NTM, DNC).
- Compelling results on curricula and one-shot Omniglot; strong bAbI performance.

Cons:
- Limited real-world large-scale tasks (e.g., language modeling, MT).
- ANN nondifferentiability and hyperparameter sensitivity not deeply analyzed.
- Fairness of comparisons when memory sizes differ could be discussed more.
- Write policy design (prev-read/LRU) vs alternatives not ablated.

Overall: Clear accept.
