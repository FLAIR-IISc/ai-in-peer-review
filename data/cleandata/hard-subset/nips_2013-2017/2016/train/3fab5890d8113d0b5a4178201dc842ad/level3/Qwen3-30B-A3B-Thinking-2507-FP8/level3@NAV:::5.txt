

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that achieves dramatic efficiency gains while maintaining strong performance. SAM constrains reads and writes to a sparse subset of memory words (K=4–8), leveraging approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) structures for sublinear-time content-based addressing. The authors demonstrate ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× memory reduction over vanilla NTMs (e.g., 53MB vs. 29GB for 64K memory words), enabling training on sequences up to 200,000 steps—far exceeding prior work (max 10,000 steps). SAM maintains near-identical data efficiency on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort) and achieves state-of-the-art results on Babi tasks (solving all but one) and Omniglot one-shot learning, with superior scaling via curriculum learning.

**Strengths:** The paper excels in **Significance** and **Quality**. The efficiency gains are rigorously validated with comprehensive benchmarks, addressing a critical bottleneck in MANNs. The scalability results (e.g., 200,000-step generalization) are compelling and directly demonstrate practical applicability, a long-standing challenge in the field. The technical execution is sound, with clear theoretical analysis of time/space complexity (optimal O(log N) and O(1) per step) and thorough ablation studies. **Originality** is strong: SAM’s sparse access scheme is a novel, principled approach distinct from prior methods (e.g., reinforcement learning-based sparse addressing [Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015]), and the adaptation to SDNC shows generality.

**Weaknesses:** The primary gap is **lack of theoretical insight** into *why* SAM enables such remarkable generalization. While efficiency is well-demonstrated, the paper fails to address the supervisor’s key request: explaining how sparsity drives superior curriculum learning and long-range generalization beyond mere computational gains. The speculation about "sparse autoencoders" is superficial—no analysis links sparsity to representation learning or inductive bias. This weakens the claim of "far-reaching impact," as the mechanism remains opaque. Minor clarity issues include sparse implementation details (e.g., ANN rebuild frequency) and inconsistent notation (e.g., "SAM linear" vs. "SAM ANN" in results).

**Verdict:** This is a high-quality, impactful contribution with exceptional empirical rigor. The missing theoretical explanation for generalization gains is the sole critical weakness. If addressed, this would be a strong accept. As-is, it merits **accept** with minor revisions (specifically, adding a dedicated section analyzing *why* sparsity improves generalization, e.g., via representation sparsity or reduced overfitting). The work significantly advances MANNs toward real-world use, making it a valuable addition to NIPS.
