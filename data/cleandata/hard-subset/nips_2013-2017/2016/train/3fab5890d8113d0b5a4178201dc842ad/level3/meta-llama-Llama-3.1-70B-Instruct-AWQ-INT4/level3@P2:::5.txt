This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory architecture designed to scale memory-augmented neural networks efficiently. The authors propose a sparse read and write scheme that leverages efficient data structures to reduce the computational and memory overhead associated with traditional memory-augmented models. The paper demonstrates significant speedups and memory efficiency gains over existing models, such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, while maintaining comparable performance on various tasks.

The authors provide a thorough background on memory-augmented neural networks, including NTMs and Memory Networks, and discuss the limitations of these models in terms of scalability. They then introduce the SAM architecture, which uses a sparse read operation and a novel write scheme to update the memory. The paper also discusses the use of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes to further improve the efficiency of the model.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM in terms of speed and memory efficiency. The authors show that SAM achieves significant speedups over NTMs and Memory Networks, while maintaining comparable performance on various tasks, including copy, associative recall, and priority sort. They also demonstrate the ability of SAM to generalize to sequences much longer than those seen during training, with impressive results on the Omniglot dataset.

The paper makes several contributions to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. Firstly, it introduces a novel memory architecture that is designed to scale efficiently. Secondly, it demonstrates the effectiveness of sparse read and write schemes in reducing the computational and memory overhead associated with traditional memory-augmented models. Finally, it shows that SAM can be used to achieve state-of-the-art performance on various tasks, while maintaining significant speedups and memory efficiency gains over existing models.
