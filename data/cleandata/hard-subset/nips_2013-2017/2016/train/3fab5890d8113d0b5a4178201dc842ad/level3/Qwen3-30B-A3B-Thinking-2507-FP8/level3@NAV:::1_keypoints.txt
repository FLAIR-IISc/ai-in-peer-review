- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a memory-augmented NN using approximate nearest-neighbor search + arg-top-K for efficient memory access.  
- Reports large efficiency gains (up to 1,000× speedup, 3,000× less memory) while maintaining performance on synthetic tasks and Omniglot.  
- Valuable practical contribution by applying scalable access to writable memories, an underexplored area compared to read-only memory work.  
- Novelty is limited: related hashing/scalable memory access work already exists, so this is more an engineering/practical solution than a theoretical advance.  
- Strong empirical results: scales to 100,000+ memory slots and performs well on Babi tasks.  
- Computational advantages are clearly demonstrated and compelling.  
- Paper clarity is generally good, but task descriptions lack sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- Authors must release experiment code and provide detailed task construction for replicability.  
- Insufficient discussion of non-smoothness of argmax/K-argmax (especially K=1) and impacts on training dynamics versus smooth models.  
- Paper lacks analysis of failure cases and limits: when and why the method fails or conditions under which it breaks.  
- Claim that Omniglot is “non-synthetic”/“real-world” is misleading and should be revised.  
- Training uses discrete actions without addressing discreteness in optimization—this limitation requires discussion.  
- Recommend acceptance with revisions to address replicability, failure-case analysis, discrete optimization issues, and contextualizing novelty.  
- Suggest removing section 3.6; emphasize engineering impact but clarify that it does not fundamentally advance underlying theory.
