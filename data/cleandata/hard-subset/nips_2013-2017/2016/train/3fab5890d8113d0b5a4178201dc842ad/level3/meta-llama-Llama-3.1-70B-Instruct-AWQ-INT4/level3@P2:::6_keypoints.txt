- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an extension to Neural Turing Machines that uses sparse read/write to improve scalability.  
- Motivates SAM by noting NTMs’ smooth read/write incur large computational and memory overhead, limiting real-world applicability.  
- Introduces a sparse read/write scheme that reduces memory and computation, making NTMs practical for large-scale applications.  
- Paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of SAM architecture and components.  
- Provides thorough time and space complexity analysis, claiming asymptotic lower bounds in both.  
- Experimental results show SAM can be up to 1,000× faster and use 3,000× less physical memory while maintaining comparable performance.  
- Validated on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot character recognition with strong empirical performance.  
- Builds on prior NTM/MANN work and clearly differentiates the proposed approach; sparse reads/writes are a novel contribution.  
- Potential concern: Figure 4 comparison may be unfair because authors match physical memory rather than logical memory between models.  
- Authors’ detailed experimental setup and methodology help mitigate some concerns about fairness.  
- Paper is technically sound and the algorithm performs close to theoretical bounds.  
- Significance: results have important implications for scaling memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and effective approach, impressive empirical speed/memory gains, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: questionable fairness of comparison in Figure 4 and need for clearer explanation of memory/write operator and MANN background.  
- Recommendations: add brief MANN background, clarify memory and write operator, and compare SAM to non-sparse models using the same logical memory.
