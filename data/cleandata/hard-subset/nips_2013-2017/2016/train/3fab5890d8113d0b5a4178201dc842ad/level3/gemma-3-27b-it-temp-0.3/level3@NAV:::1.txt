## Review of “Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes”

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) to larger problems, hampered by the computational and memory overhead of dense read/write operations. The authors propose Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a method leveraging approximate nearest neighbors and sparse operations to significantly reduce this overhead while maintaining performance. The core idea – thresholding memory modifications and using efficient data structures for content-based reads – is a sensible approach to tackling the scalability issue. The reported speedups (1000x faster, 3000x less memory) are substantial and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed technique. The experiments on synthetic tasks and Omniglot are encouraging, showing comparable or even improved data efficiency compared to existing models.

However, the novelty of the approach feels somewhat incremental. The use of hashing and sparse representations for efficient memory lookup is not entirely new; related work, particularly Memory Networks (section 3.3) and other papers utilizing large memories with hashing, already explores similar concepts. While applying these techniques specifically to *writable* memories like NTMs is a valuable contribution, the paper doesn’t fully articulate how SAM significantly advances beyond existing approaches. 

The practical contribution is strong. The engineering effort to implement and optimize SAM, and the resulting performance gains, are noteworthy and represent a valuable addition to the field. However, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed task descriptions, hindering replicability. The authors *must* provide more specifics on task construction and release their experimental code. 

Further analysis is needed regarding failure cases and limitations. When does SAM fail to deliver performance gains? Does it consistently work well, or are there specific task characteristics or setup conditions where it struggles? The non-smoothness introduced by the argmax/K-argmax operations also warrants further discussion. A comparison to fully smooth models and the behavior of MemNN-WSH would be insightful. Finally, the authors should address the implications of training with discrete actions without accounting for discreteness, as some prior work has done. Section 3.6 feels extraneous and should be removed. Lastly, referring to Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world” is misleading and should be revised.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant practical speedup and memory reduction.
*   Demonstrated performance on a range of tasks.
*   Addresses a critical bottleneck in MANN research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty.
*   Replicability concerns due to insufficient task details.
*   Lack of analysis of failure cases and limitations.



**Recommendation:** Accept with revisions. The paper presents a valuable engineering contribution, but requires clarification and further analysis to strengthen its scientific rigor and impact.
