- Addresses MANN scalability: dense content-based addressing and BPTT scale poorly; proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) limiting reads/writes to K and using ANN for top-K, plus sparse deltas for BPTT.  
- Claims SAM yields O(log N) per-step compute and O(1) per-step memory, with proofs deferred to the supplement.  
- Builds on NTMs, Memory Networks, DNC, and RL-based discrete addressing; integrates known attention sparsification and LRU-like allocation into a practical differentiable MANN.  
- Sparse read/write and rollback-style BPTT are technically plausible, but the non-differentiable top-K/ANN selection produces biased gradients that are neither analyzed nor discussed in the main text.  
- Asymptotic optimality claims depend on unstated assumptions (e.g., constant K and content-addressing constraints) not presented in the main paper.  
- Strong empirical speed and peak-memory improvements; successful scaling to long sequences and larger memories, with good results on synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI (SDNC), and one-shot Omniglot.  
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analysis: little investigation of K, threshold ε, write policy choices, or the impact of ANN recall quality on learning.  
- Experimental comparisons may be unfairly favorable to SAM due to implementation differences; need clarity on codebases, hardware, and hyperparameter parity.  
- SDNC adaptation shows generality but is considered incremental rather than novel.  
- Paper is generally clear and well written, but key assumptions, complexity proofs, and gradient implications are relegated to the supplement and should be moved into the main text.  
- Originality is modest: main components (ANN top-K, sparse writes, sparse rollback) are known; novelty lies in their integration and engineering for scale.  
- Practical significance is high (enables very large memories and long sequences), but theoretical and algorithmic novelty is limited and robustness to ANN errors is unexamined.  
- Pros: clear problem identification, simple effective design, dramatic empirical gains, competitive task performance, and broad applicability to other MANNs.  
- Cons: limited technical novelty, unaddressed gradient bias from non-differentiable selection, insufficient ablations, unclear optimality assumptions, and reproducibility/fairness issues.  
- Suggested improvements: analyze/measure gradient bias and ANN recall effects, add ablations for K/ε/write policies and ANN rebuild frequency, clarify benchmark parity and release code, move optimality assumptions/proof sketch to main text, and discuss differentiable alternatives to ANN.  
- Overall recommendation: solid engineering and useful empirical results but incremental and lacking theoretical/analytical support—insufficient for NIPS acceptance without stronger analysis and ablations.
