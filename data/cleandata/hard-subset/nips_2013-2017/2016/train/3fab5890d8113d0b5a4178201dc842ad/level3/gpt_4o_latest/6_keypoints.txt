- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): a MANN with sparse read/write and efficient data structures that attains better space/time asymptotics and scales to large memories and long sequences.  
- Achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art on synthetic tasks, Omniglot one-shot, and bAbi while substantially reducing memory and compute overhead.  
- Strength — Technical contribution: introduces sparsity in memory access with solid theoretical and practical impact on scaling NTMs.  
- Strength — Efficiency gains: reports orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime and memory using ANN data structures.  
- Strength — Empirical validation: robust experiments across synthetic benchmarks, curriculum learning, and real-world datasets; generalizes to longer sequences.  
- Strength — Clarity: well-structured, clearly written, with supplementary material aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness — Accessibility: lacks a concise introduction to MANNs and their challenges for non-expert readers.  
- Weakness — Write operator: the write mechanism is under-described and needs clearer comparison to conventional NTMs.  
- Weakness — Comparison bias: experimental comparisons may be biased by using physical memory rather than normalized memory size across models.  
- Weakness — Limited alternatives: discussion of alternative approaches (e.g., RL for discrete addressing) is brief and could be expanded.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a critical scalability problem with strong theoretical and empirical support and offers a novel, impactful solution.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited background/detail and potential experimental bias may weaken accessibility and some claims.  
- Recommendation: accept, subject to revisions addressing background, write-operator detail, fairer comparisons, and broader discussion of alternatives.
