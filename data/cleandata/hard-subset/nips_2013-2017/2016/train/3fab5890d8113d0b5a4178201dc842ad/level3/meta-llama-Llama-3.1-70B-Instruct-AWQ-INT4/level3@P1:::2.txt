This paper introduces a novel memory access scheme called Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that addresses the scalability issues of memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs). The authors claim that SAM retains the representational power of the original approaches while being much more efficient, achieving asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.

The key idea behind SAM is to constrain all writes to and reads from external memory to a sparse subset of the memory words. This is achieved through a combination of sparse memory management and efficient data structures for content-based read operations. The authors demonstrate that SAM can be trained efficiently with very large memories, achieving significant speedups and memory reductions compared to non-sparse models.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The technical components of the paper, including the efficient backpropagation and approximate nearest neighbor via sparse access, are well-explained and appear to be sound. The experiments are also interesting and demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM in various tasks.

However, I have some reservations about the technical novelty of the paper. The main technical components of SAM, while well-executed, seem to be relatively straightforward extensions of existing ideas. The use of sparse memory management and efficient data structures for content-based read operations is not particularly surprising, and the authors do not provide a compelling argument for why these techniques were not explored before.

Furthermore, while the experiments are interesting, they do not entirely outweigh the perceived lack of technical novelty. The authors demonstrate that SAM can be trained efficiently with very large memories, but they do not provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. The comparison to other models, such as the Neural Turing Machine (NTM) and Memory Networks, is also not entirely convincing, as the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between these models.

Overall, while the paper is well-written and the experiments are interesting, I believe that the technical contribution is insufficient for acceptance. The authors need to provide a more compelling argument for the technical novelty of their approach and a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between SAM and other models.
