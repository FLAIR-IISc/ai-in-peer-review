Summary and relation to prior work: This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that replaces dense attention with fast approximate nearest-neighbors (ANN) and an arg-top-K attention/read, combined with sparse, locality-driven writes (to recently read or least-used slots). This yields O(log N) time for reads and O(1) space per step while retaining a writeable memory, enabling training on very large memories and long sequences. Empirically, SAM matches or exceeds dense NTMs/DNCs on synthetic NTM tasks, bAbI, and a one-shot Omniglot setup, and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed and memory savings. The approach is closely related to prior Memory Networks and variants that use hashing/ANN for scalable lookups (e.g., MemNN Section 3.3; MemNN with hashing/WSH), and to work on discrete addressing for NTMs via RL. The main novelty is applying sparse ANN/top-K to both reads and writes in an end-to-end trained, writeable memory and delivering a practical, efficient implementation.

Quality: The engineering is solid and the complexity analysis is persuasive. Results are strong and the scaling experiments are impressive. However, several claims hinge on non-smooth operations (top-K/argmax, ANN) and need careful qualification and empirical analysis.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally clear, but key experimental details are missing, hindering reproducibility. Please remove Section 3.6, and avoid describing Omniglot as “non-synthetic” or “real-world.”

Originality: Conceptual novelty is thin, as sparse attention via hashing/ANN is established for large read-only memories; applying these tools to writeable memories at scale is less explored and practically valuable.

Significance: High practical significance. Many will adopt these techniques for large-memory training.

Replicability: Task descriptions are insufficient. Please provide precise task construction protocols, full hyperparameters, ANN index settings (type, rebuild policy, distance metrics), K selection strategy, and release code.

Requested analyses and clarifications:
- Non-smoothness: Analyze effects of arg-top-K (including K=1) on optimization and gradients; compare against fully smooth dense attention and MemNN-WSH behavior.
- Discreteness: Training makes discrete selections without accounting for discreteness (unlike RL-based NTMs). Discuss implications, potential bias/variance trade-offs, and limits; consider straight-through or relaxation baselines.
- Failure modes: When does the method fail? Characterize regimes of K, memory size, sequence length, and task structure where performance degrades.
- Baselines: Include comparisons to large-memory read-only hashing baselines and to smooth sparse approximations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong engineering contribution with compelling speed/space wins.
- Demonstrated scalability to millions of slots and very long BPTT.
- Competitive task performance; practical impact likely high.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to prior hashed/ANN memory access.
- Incomplete reproducibility and missing ablations.
- Insufficient discussion of non-smoothness and discrete decisions.

Recommendation: Accept with revisions.
