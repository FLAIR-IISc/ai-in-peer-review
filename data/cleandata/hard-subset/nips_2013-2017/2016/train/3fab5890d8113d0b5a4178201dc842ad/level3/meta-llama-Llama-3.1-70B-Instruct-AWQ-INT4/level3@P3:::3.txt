This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that enables efficient training with large external memories. The key contribution of SAM is to constrain reads and writes to a fixed-size subset of memory words, rather than the entire memory, which significantly reduces the computational and memory overhead.

The authors build on recent work such as Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, and provide a thorough theoretical justification for their approach. They also demonstrate the optimality of SAM in terms of space and time complexity. The empirical results show that SAM achieves significant speedups compared to NTMs, while maintaining comparable data efficiency and performance on a range of synthetic and natural tasks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The architecture details are well-explained in the appendix, and the paper includes a thorough evaluation of the proposed method. The use of an approximate nearest neighbor index within the network is a nice touch, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between different indexing methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to scale to large external memories and long sequence lengths. The authors demonstrate that SAM can be used to train models with millions of memory words, and that the model can generalize to sequences that far exceed those observed during training.

The paper also includes a real-world example, where the authors apply SAM to the Omniglot dataset. The results show that SAM can learn to rapidly associate novel characters with correct labels, and that the model can generalize to sequences that are significantly longer than those observed during training.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of MANNs, and demonstrates the potential of SAM for scaling memory-augmented neural networks to large external memories and long sequence lengths.
