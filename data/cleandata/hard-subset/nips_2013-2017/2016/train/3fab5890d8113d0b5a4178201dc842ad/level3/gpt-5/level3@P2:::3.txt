Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) architecture that constrains both reads and writes to a fixed, small number K of memory slots and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) data structures to enable sublinear-time content-based addressing. In contrast to Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and related MANNs (Memory Networks; Differentiable Neural Computer, DNC), which perform dense soft attention over all N memory slots and incur O(N) per step time and O(NT) space for backpropagation, SAM achieves O(log N) time per step (for content-based reads via ANN) and O(1) space per step by sparsifying both reads and writes and by recording sparse in-place memory updates that can be rolled back during BPTT. Writes are directed to either previously read locations (to update relevant content) or the least recently used location (for allocation), yielding a simple, differentiable LRU-like scheme. The paper further shows how to adapt the sparsification approach to the DNC (SDNC).

Empirically, SAM matches or improves data efficiency versus dense baselines (NTM, a dense variant DAM) on standard NTM synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), demonstrates dramatic training-time and memory-usage gains (up to 1000x speed, 3000x memory savings), scales to millions of memory slots and thousands of time steps via curriculum learning, performs competitively on bAbI without memory supervision (best with SDNC), and achieves strong one-shot learning on Omniglot. The work builds directly on prior NIPS/related work on MANNs [Graves et al. 2014/2016; Sukhbaatar et al. 2015], while proposing a simple and effective sparsification and indexing layer for scalable content-based access.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The sparse read/write formulation, the O(1) per-step space via reversible sparse updates, and the use of ANN indices to approximate top-K content reads are well-motivated. The optimality claims (asymptotic lower bounds) are plausible given the content-based addressing model; it would help to make the assumptions and constants explicit from Supplementary A in the main text. The non-differentiability of index selection (top-K via ANN) is handled by treating the index as fixed; gradients flow only to selected slots. While this introduces a biased gradient estimator, experiments suggest it suffices in practice.
- Empirical evaluation: Strong engineering and convincing benchmarks. Comparisons on synthetic NTM tasks, large-scale curricula, bAbI, and Omniglot are appropriate. The speed/memory benchmarks are compelling and directly tied to the stated goals. A few ablations are missing (see below).
- Potential weaknesses/limitations:
  - The conditions under which sparsity improves data efficiency are only briefly observed (e.g., priority sort, associative recall). Analysis of when/why sparsity helps would be valuable.
  - The reliance on kd-trees/LSH for O(log N) lookups can degrade with high dimensional word sizes; the paper mentions switching between kd-trees and LSH but does not quantify the effect of dimensionality or constants.
  - The write policy (previously read + LRU) is simple and effective, but a discussion of failure modes versus content-based writes would help (e.g., tasks requiring writing to new addresses determined purely by content).
  - Fairness of some comparisons: for scaled curricula, sparse models use vastly larger N than dense baselines (to match physical memory limits). While this is the point (scalability), a brief discussion of the accuracy–resource tradeoff would clarify interpretability.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core ideas, algorithms, and controller interface are presented concisely; practical details (efficient BPTT rollbacks, usage statistics, ANN maintenance) are described and detailed in the supplement. Figures support the scalability claims. Suggestions:
- Move key complexity assumptions and a brief sketch of the optimality proof from the supplement into the main text.
- Provide more intuition and examples for the write policy and usage metric, perhaps with a small visualization of memory allocation over time.
- Summarize ANN configuration (kd-tree vs. LSH thresholds, rebuild frequency) and its empirical overhead in the main text.

Originality
The idea of enforcing sparsity in memory access is simple but impactful. Prior work has explored discrete/sparse addressing via RL (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever 2015), but SAM offers a deterministic, end-to-end differentiable alternative with efficient data structures and a reversible training scheme. The combination of sparse reads/writes, ANN-based content access inside the network, and O(1) per-step memory for BPTT is, to my knowledge, novel in this form, and the adaptation to DNC (SDNC) further demonstrates generality.

Significance
The contribution is significant: it addresses a primary bottleneck of MANNs and opens the door to training with very large memories and very long sequences. The speed/space improvements are substantial and practically relevant for language modeling, QA, and one-shot learning. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended by the community (e.g., alternative indices, sparsity schedules, different controllers).

Pros
- Clear, simple idea with substantial engineering rigor.
- Strong theoretical motivation and complexity improvements; elegant O(1) per-step memory via reversible updates.
- Demonstrated scalability to millions of slots and thousands of steps; large speed and memory savings.
- Comparable or better learning efficiency on standard synthetic tasks; strong results on bAbI (especially SDNC) and Omniglot.
- General approach applicable to other MANNs (NTM, DNC, Memory Networks).

Cons
- Limited analysis of when sparsity helps/hurts learning beyond empirical observations.
- ANN details and dimensionality/constant-factor effects are mostly in the supplement; limited ablations (e.g., varying K, sparse read vs. write contributions).
- Write policy may be restrictive for some tasks; lack of negative results or characterization of failure modes.
- Some comparisons involve different N (by design); include a brief resource–accuracy tradeoff discussion.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report ablations for K (number of read slots), sparse reads only vs. sparse writes only, and the choice of usage metric (discounted sum vs. LRU)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- How does dimensionality of memory words affect kd-tree vs. LSH performance and final accuracy? Please provide wall-clock vs. dimension plots and accuracy deltas due to ANN approximation.
- Can you quantify the approximation error between dense and sparse reads (e.g., bound on ||r − r̃|| in terms of tail mass of attention) in typical training regimes?
- Are there tasks where the “previously read + LRU” write scheme fails compared to content-based writes? Could hybrid content-based sparse writes be viable?
- Would alternatives like sparsemax/top-k continuous relaxations help stabilize gradients while retaining sparsity?

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper provides a principled and practical solution to a central scalability problem for differentiable memory, with clear algorithmic contributions, compelling empirical results, and broad applicability.
