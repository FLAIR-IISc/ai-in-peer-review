This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural architecture that enforces K-sparse reads and writes and leverages approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indices to achieve O(log N) access and O(1) per-step memory during training, with a reversible sparse BPTT scheme that stores only incremental updates. SAM retains differentiability and is shown to be 10^3× faster and 10^3× more memory-efficient than dense NTMs, while matching or surpassing accuracy on classic NTM tasks, scaling through long curricula, generalizing from ~10k to ~200k time steps, performing well on Omniglot one-shot, and adapting to DNC (SDNC) for strong bAbI results. The work sits squarely in the active MANN literature (NTM, Memory Networks, DNC), addressing their core scalability bottlenecks; it contrasts with discrete/RL addressing approaches that trade efficiency for high-variance gradients.

Quality: The asymptotic analysis, systems design (ANN integration, LRU-like allocation, reversible BPTT), and empirical evaluations are solid and compelling. However, key ablations are missing: sensitivity to K and ANN recall, the impact of the write policy (previously read vs least-recently-used), and fairness of capacity-matched baselines. Some comparisons conflate architectural advances with much larger external memory.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Suggestions: specify ANN rebuild cadence and hyperparameters, report K and word-size choices per task, and provide pseudocode for the rollback/backprop logic. Public code would aid reproducibility given the engineering complexity.

Originality: High. The specific combination of top-K differentiable access, in-network ANN, and reversible sparse BPTT is novel and practically meaningful.

Significance: High. The reported three-order-of-magnitude gains plausibly make MANNs practical and invite broader adoption.

On why SAM improves curriculum and long-range generalization: Efficiency alone is unlikely sufficient. The K-sparse reads reduce gradient dilution and interference, promoting stable algorithmic learning; the LRU-style allocation enforces episodic, low-overlap storage; together these act like a sparsity prior akin to sparse autoencoders, encouraging disentangled, parsimonious memory use. I recommend measuring retrieval overlap/interference, varying K, swapping dense vs sparse reads at equal capacity, and comparing LRU vs alternative allocation to substantiate this hypothesis.

Pros for acceptance:
- Dramatic, well-demonstrated speed/memory gains; scalable reversible BPTT
- Strong performance on curricula, long-range generalization, Omniglot, and bAbI (SDNC)
- Clear path to practical MANN deployment; broadly applicable design

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing ablations and theoretical analysis of generalization gains
- Capacity-mismatch in some baselines; limited robustness analysis to ANN/K
- Reproducibility risk without code/engineering details

Overall: Clear accept, contingent on adding ablations and releasing implementation details.
