- Tackles scalability bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks caused by dense soft attention with O(N) compute and O(NT) BPTT space.

- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse K-nearest read/write to only K slots and sparse writes to read locations plus least-recently-used slot.

- Integrates approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) data structures (k-d trees, LSH) to make K-NN search O(log N).

- Introduces an efficient BPTT implementation that stores only sparse modifications, reducing per-step space from O(N) to O(1).

- Benchmarks show massive speedups (up to 1,600x) and memory reductions (up to 3,000x) compared to dense models.

- Empirical results across algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and one-shot Omniglot show no performance degradation and sometimes improved learning.

- Demonstrates scalability to previously infeasible problems (e.g., associative recall over 200k time steps) via curriculum training.

- Strength: addresses a highly important practical problem and enables large-memory applications like document-level understanding.

- Strength: thorough, well-designed experiments convincingly illustrating practical benefits and scalability.

- Strength: well-written and clearly organized presentation of motivation, architecture, and results.

- Weakness: limited technical novelty—core ideas are straightforward applications of existing techniques (top-K selection, ANN, sparse rollback).

- Weakness: contribution framed more as strong systems/engineering integration than a fundamental ML algorithmic advance.

- For acceptance: practical importance, outstanding empirical gains, and potential to enable new research directions.

- Against acceptance: insufficient conceptual novelty for a venue prioritizing fundamental algorithmic contributions.
