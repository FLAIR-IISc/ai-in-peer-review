- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): differentiable MANN with sparse reads/writes (top-K) and ANN-based content addressing, trainable end-to-end via BPTT.
- Key mechanisms: top-K sparsification, ANN (randomized k-d trees/LSH) for reads, LRU + previously-read write allocation, and sparse rollback for BPTT.
- Complexity claims: O(log N) reads, O(1) writes and gradient storage per step, O(N) initialization, enabling very large memories.
- Adapts approach to DNC (SDNC), achieving dramatic speedups over dense baselines.
- Compared to prior work: retains differentiability and content addressing while achieving optimal asymptotics versus NTMs/DNCs and avoids RL high-variance discrete addressing.
- Empirical gains: up to 1000x faster and 3000x more memory-efficient than dense NTMs at large N.
- Task performance: matches/exceeds dense variants on copy, associative recall, priority sort; strong bAbI results (all but one task) and best on Omniglot one-shot curriculum.
- Scalability/generalization: trains on ~10k steps and generalizes to ~200k steps, scales to millions of slots via expanding curriculum.
- Soundness: components are well-motivated and engineered; asymptotic claims plausible and empirically supported though formal assumptions could be clearer.
- Empirics critique: compelling benchmarks but missing ablations on sensitivity to K, ANN recall vs speed tradeoff, and write-policy alternatives.
- Reproducibility: necessary components described but key details (hyperparameters, ANN tuning, curriculum) mainly in supplement; ANN recall/collision stats not reported.
- Clarity: generally clear and organized; sparse BPTT description strong but could benefit from a main-text algorithm box and practical recipes (K, epsilon, rebuild timing).
- Originality: uses known building blocks but novel, impactful integration into an end-to-end differentiable MANN with practical sparse BPTT and asymptotic gains.
- Significance: high — substantially advances scalable differentiable memory, enabling long-context tasks and prompting follow-on work.
- Why SAM aids curriculum/long-range generalization: reduced interference via sparse reads/writes enforcing locality and orthogonality.
- Why SAM aids learning: cleaner credit assignment due to narrow gradient paths and pointer-based computation that generalizes by access patterns.
- ANN effect: approximate KNN induces locality bias/winner-take-most behavior that mitigates softmax tail corruption as memory grows.
- Weakness: ANN/top-K selection is non-differentiable; sensitivity to ANN recall should be reported and compared to exact KNN.
- Weakness: comparisons can conflate scalability with capacity—need matched effective-memory baselines to isolate algorithmic gains.
- Weakness: limited real-world evaluations; additional long-context language/QA benchmarks would broaden impact.
- Weakness: lacking analysis of memory collisions, utilization histograms, and LRU overwrite frequency.
- Pros for acceptance: principled, scalable advance with strong empirical and engineering contributions and generality via SDNC adaptation.
- Cons against acceptance: missing ablations (K, ANN quality, write policy), few real-world benchmarks, and theoretical bounds/assumptions deferred to supplement.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to K and ANN recall (e.g., 70% vs 95%), comparisons of pure content-based or hybrid write policies versus LRU+previous-read, memory utilization/overwrite stats during training, and effects of truncated BPTT versus full BPTT at long horizons.
