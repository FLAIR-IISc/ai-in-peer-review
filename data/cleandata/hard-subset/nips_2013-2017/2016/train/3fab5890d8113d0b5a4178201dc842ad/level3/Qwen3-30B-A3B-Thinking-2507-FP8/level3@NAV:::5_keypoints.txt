- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): constrains reads/writes to K=4–8 memory words and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) structures for sublinear content-based addressing.  
- Delivers dramatic efficiency gains: ~1,000× speedup and ~3,000× memory reduction vs. vanilla NTMs (e.g., 53MB vs. 29GB for 64K words).  
- Enables training on much longer sequences (up to 200,000 steps), far exceeding prior limits (~10,000 steps).  
- Maintains near-identical data efficiency on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort).  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on Babi (all but one task) and Omniglot one-shot learning, and scales well with curriculum learning.  
- Strong technical execution: clear theoretical time/space complexity (optimal O(log N) and O(1) per step) and comprehensive ablation studies.  
- Original contribution: a principled sparse access scheme distinct from prior methods and generalizable (e.g., to SDNC).  
- Major weakness: no theoretical explanation for why sparsity produces superior generalization and curriculum learning—speculation is superficial.  
- Minor clarity issues: missing implementation details (e.g., ANN rebuild frequency) and inconsistent notation ("SAM linear" vs. "SAM ANN").  
- Verdict: high-quality, impactful work; recommend accept with minor revisions to analyze why sparsity improves generalization.
