- Summary: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale MANNs by restricting reads/writes to a sparse subset, using ANN for O(log N) reads and sparse tracking for O(1) per-step space, claiming large empirical speed/memory gains.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on NTM, Memory Networks, DNC and related sparse-access attempts, applying standard ANN and sparse-write ideas to MANNs.

- Strength - Quality: Presents thorough empirical evaluation across synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot, showing scalability to very large memories with strong empirical speed/memory improvements.

- Strength - Clarity: Paper is well-written and organized with accessible technical descriptions and supplementary implementation details.

- Strength - Significance: Scalability gains could enable real-world MANN applications previously infeasible, training on 100k+ timesteps.

- Weakness - Originality: Core components (sparse access, ANN indexing, least-recently-used writes) are seen as straightforward, standard techniques lacking novel algorithmic insight.

- Weakness - Quality (theory): Claims of asymptotic optimality lack formal proofs or clear theoretical justification and no connection to lower bounds is established.

- Weakness - Significance: Perceived limited novelty reduces impact; empirical gains are impressive but the work may be incremental rather than a substantial advance.

- For acceptance: Demonstrates practical scalability, strong empirical results, clear presentation, and generalizability to other MANN architectures.

- Against acceptance: Uses mostly standard techniques, lacks theoretical backing for key claims, and insufficient technical novelty for NIPS.

- Overall assessment: Practical and well-evaluated contribution with limited technical novelty; authors should strengthen theoretical or algorithmic contributions.

- Recommendation: Reject.
