- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a scalable NTM variant using sparse read/write and efficient data structures to reduce compute and memory.  
- Core idea: leverage sparse operations + approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search to lower computational complexity for memory access.  
- Demonstrates large efficiency gains (reported ~1,000x speedup, ~3,000x memory reduction) while preserving task performance.  
- Evaluated on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot one-shot learning with comparable results to dense NTMs.  
- Builds on prior MANN work (NTMs, Memory Networks) and applies known ANN techniques specifically to writable memory access.  
- Extends the approach to a sparse Differentiable Neural Computer variant (SDNC), showing broader applicability.  
- Strength: substantial practical speedups and memory savings addressing a key MANN bottleneck.  
- Strength: maintains comparable performance to dense baselines despite sparsity.  
- Strength: strong engineering contribution and practical implementation/value.  
- Strength: generalizable method applicable to multiple MANN architectures.  
- Strength: comprehensive experimental evaluation across tasks and scales.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—core techniques (sparsity, ANN) are not entirely new; novelty is in their combination/application.  
- Weakness: experimental task descriptions lack detail, hindering reproducibility (missing params, splits, procedures).  
- Weakness: use of argmax/K-argmax introduces discreteness not discussed; potential effects on gradients and learning are unclear.  
- Weakness: no detailed analysis of failure modes or limitations—conditions where SAM fails are not explored.  
- Weakness: Section 3.6 (ANN structure) appears redundant with Section 3.5.  
- Overall assessment: solid, practical contribution warranting acceptance with revisions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses scalability, shows large efficiency gains, preserves performance, and offers a practical widely applicable solution.  
- Arguments for rejection: limited novelty, insufficient reproducibility details, and missing failure-mode analysis.  
- Recommended revisions: provide full reproducibility details and release code.  
- Recommended revisions: discuss discreteness (argmax/K-argmax), compare to fully smooth models, and study effect of varying K.  
- Recommended revisions: analyze failure cases and limitations, clarifying conditions of poor performance.  
- Recommended revisions: remove the redundant Section 3.6.  
- Recommended revisions: avoid calling Omniglot “non-synthetic” or “real-world”; clarify dataset terminology.
