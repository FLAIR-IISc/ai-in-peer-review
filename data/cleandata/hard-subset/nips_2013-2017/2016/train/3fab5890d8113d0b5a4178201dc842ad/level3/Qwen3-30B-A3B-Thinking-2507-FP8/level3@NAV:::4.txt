

This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by implementing sparse read and write operations while maintaining end-to-end differentiability. The authors address a critical limitation of existing MANNs like Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and Memory Networks, which suffer from linear computational and memory complexity as memory size grows. SAM achieves asymptotic optimality in space and time complexity (O(log N) time, O(N) initialization, O(1) per step space), demonstrating up to 1,000× faster training and 3,000× reduced memory usage compared to dense implementations on large-scale tasks.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field. The sparse access scheme is technically sound, with thorough theoretical analysis (Supplementary A) and compelling empirical validation across multiple benchmarks: synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. Notably, SAM maintains comparable data efficiency to dense models while scaling to 100,000+ time steps and memory slots—demonstrating the first practical implementation of large-scale MANNs. The authors also show how SAM generalizes to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), achieving state-of-the-art results on Babi tasks without supervising memory access.

Quality is excellent: experiments rigorously validate claims, with appropriate ablation studies and careful analysis of sparse approximation effects. Clarity is outstanding—the paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the sparse read/write mechanisms and efficient backpropagation techniques. Originality is strong: while prior work explored sparse memory access via reinforcement learning (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015), SAM's differentiable, end-to-end approach represents a fundamentally new technical contribution. Significance is high, addressing a critical bottleneck in deploying MANNs for real-world applications requiring large-scale memory.

The paper's only minor limitation is the reliance on approximate nearest neighbor techniques (k-d trees/LSH), though the authors acknowledge potential improvements. Overall, this work makes a substantial contribution to the field of neural memory systems, with clear theoretical and practical impact. 

Pro: Novel technical contribution addressing a high-impact problem; strong empirical validation; excellent clarity; significant scalability improvements.  
Con: Limited exploration of alternative ANN methods (though acknowledged as future work).  
Acceptance recommendation: Strong accept.
