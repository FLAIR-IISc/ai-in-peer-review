Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) that enforces sparsity in reads and writes. Building on Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and related content-addressable architectures (e.g., Memory Networks, and contemporaneous DNC), the core idea is to constrain read and write operations to a fixed-size subset (K) of memory words, independent of the total memory size N. SAM uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to retrieve top-K memory locations in O(log N) time, and a simple memory management policy (write to previously read locations or the least recently accessed slot). The authors provide theoretical arguments for asymptotically optimal time and space complexity under content-based addressing, and an efficient backprop-through-time scheme via in-place sparse update logging and rollback.

Empirically, SAM achieves substantial speed and memory savings over dense NTMs/DAMs (Section 4.1), learns comparably or better on classic NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort; Section 4.2), scales to very long sequences via curriculum, performs competitively on the bAbI suite (including a sparse DNC variant in the supplement), and demonstrates utility on one-shot Omniglot classification (Section 4.4/4.5). Benchmarks are implemented in Torch7.

Quality
Technical soundness: The complexity analysis and implementation details (sparse tensors, ANN-based reads, LRU-inspired allocation, and BPTT rollback) are thoughtfully designed and justified. The claim of asymptotic optimality is framed under reasonable assumptions for content-based addressing and appears credible; it would benefit from a succinct statement of the exact assumptions and constants in the main text (currently in the supplement).

Empirics are persuasive: Section 4.1 shows >1000× wall-clock speedups and drastic memory reductions (up to 3000×) versus NTM, with O(1) per-step memory cost confirmed. Section 4.2 carefully studies the “learning cost” of sparsity: SAM matches dense baselines on copy and outperforms on associative recall and priority sort, suggesting that sparse reads/writes (via either linear search or ANN) can reduce interference and aid early-stage learning. The scaling experiments and generalization to sequences far beyond training lengths are compelling and directly enabled by the reduced complexity. Results on bAbI (notably with SDNC) and Omniglot strengthen the case that sparsity does not impair learning and can improve practical performance.

Areas to strengthen:
- Ablations on K (number of accessed slots), choice of ANN (kd-tree vs LSH), and ANN recall/latency trade-offs would clarify robustness and when sparsity helps or hurts. Section 4.2 hints at benefits but does not diagnose conditions (e.g., memory size, task structure, interference) under which sparsity improves optimization.
- Fairness and details of runtime comparisons (hardware, batch sizes, controller size, use of exact vs approximate search, rebuild frequency for ANN) should be exhaustively documented in the main text.
- Analyze the effect of non-differentiable ANN components on gradient quality; while the index is side data-structure, retrieval approximations may influence training dynamics. A quantitative comparison of exact top-K vs ANN-approximated top-K on learning curves would help.

Clarity
The paper is well written, with clear assumptions, architecture descriptions, and a helpful appendix containing derivations and implementation details. The high-level narrative—replace dense content addressing with top-K operations implemented efficiently—is straightforward and easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Bring a concise statement of the “optimality” result and its assumptions from the supplement into the main paper.
- Provide pseudo-code for the write policy (previously read vs least-recently-used), usage counters, and rollback BPTT to aid reproducibility.
- Summarize hyperparameters (K, thresholds, ANN settings, rebuild schedule) in a compact table in the main text.
- State code availability (the Torch7 implementation is referenced; releasing it would greatly aid adoption).

Originality
SAM’s core contribution—explicitly enforcing sparsity in reads and writes with a fixed-K budget and integrating ANN search inside a differentiable MANN—is a novel and elegant combination. While related to prior memory systems (NTM, Memory Networks, DNC) and discrete addressing via RL, this paper provides a distinct, practical route to scalability with theoretical and empirical backing. The efficient rollback trick for BPTT with sparse updates is also a useful engineering contribution.

Significance
The results are important: SAM makes content-addressed external memory viable at scales (10^6+ slots, 10^5+ time steps) that are otherwise intractable, with 10^3× speedups and large memory savings, while preserving or improving learning efficiency on standard MANN tasks and realistic benchmarks. The approach is general (applied to DNC in the supplement) and likely to influence future scalable memory architectures. The empirical finding that sparsity can improve early-stage learning on some tasks further increases its practical appeal.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel idea that is simple to implement and widely applicable to MANNs.
- Strong theoretical motivation with plausibly optimal asymptotics.
- Convincing empirical evidence: huge runtime/memory gains; maintained or improved accuracy; real-world task (Omniglot) and bAbI results.
- Thorough engineering: ANN integration, memory management, and efficient BPTT.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited ablations on K, ANN variants, and impact of approximate retrieval on learning.
- “Optimality” claims depend on assumptions relegated to the supplement; more main-text precision would help.
- Write policy is heuristic (LRU-like); potential pathological behaviors and trade-offs (e.g., catastrophic overwriting) are not deeply analyzed.

Suggestions for the authors
- Elaborate on when and why sparsity improves learning: analyze interference, gradient signal-to-noise, and task regimes (e.g., associative vs sequence copying). Provide ablations varying K and memory size.
- Explore and report on additional ANN integrations (e.g., LSH forests, more online-balanced trees) and their effect on speed/accuracy; characterize recall/latency trade-offs.
- Include controlled comparisons of exact top-K vs ANN-approximate top-K during training and inference.
- Report sensitivity to the write policy (previously read vs least-recently-used mix) and to the threshold used in the usage counter.
- Release code and scripts to facilitate replication of Section 4.1 and 4.2.

Overall assessment
A well written, technically solid, and impactful paper. The idea is straightforward yet novel, the implementation details are clear (with architecture specifics in the appendix), and the empirical speedups and scaling results over NTM are compelling. With minor clarifications and additional ablations on sparsity and ANN choices, this would be a strong accept.
