- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) using sparse reads/writes and efficient data structures to scale memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Demonstrates substantial speedups and memory savings versus dense MANNs and NTMs.  
- Shows competitive performance on synthetic algorithmic tasks, Babi tasks, and one-shot Omniglot, including longer-sequence scaling experiments.  
- Adapts SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), indicating generality of the approach.  
- Clear, well-written presentation with thorough experimental evaluation and benchmarking.  
- Motivation is strong: addresses the linear overhead and backpropagation memory duplication bottlenecks in MANNs.  
- Core techniques (sparse updates, approximate nearest neighbor search) are largely existing methods applied to MANNs rather than novel algorithms.  
- Claim of asymptotic optimality lacks rigorous theoretical analysis and only receives a high-level sketch in the supplement.  
- Experimental gains are chiefly efficiency improvements; they do not substantially advance state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks.  
- No deep analysis of why SAM works (e.g., interpretation of learned sparsity patterns) to explain empirical success.  
- Paper reads more like an engineering/optimization effort than a fundamental research contribution.  
- Recommendation: weak reject — technically sound and useful but insufficient novelty/impact for NIPS; better suited to a more applied venue.
