This paper, "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes", proposes a novel neural memory architecture called Sparse Access Memory (SAM). The authors address the problem of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), which are limited by their smooth read and write operations that incur linear computational overhead on the number of memories stored per time step.

The authors introduce two innovations in SAM: (1) sparse reads and writes, which constrain memory access to a sparse subset of memory words, and (2) a sparse memory management scheme that tracks memory usage and finds unused blocks of memory for recording new information. This allows SAM to achieve asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.

The paper presents experimental results on various tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results show that SAM achieves significant speedups during training, with a 1000x faster runtime and 3000x less physical memory usage compared to non-sparse models. Additionally, SAM learns with comparable data efficiency to existing models and can scale to tasks requiring 100,000s of time steps and memories.

Strengths of the paper:

* The authors propose a novel and efficient neural memory architecture that addresses the scalability limitations of MANNs.
* The paper presents thorough experimental results on various tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of SAM.
* The authors provide a detailed analysis of the space and time complexity of SAM, showing that it achieves asymptotic lower bounds.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The paper assumes that the sparse read and write operations do not impair learning, but this assumption may not always hold true.
* The authors do not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art MANNs, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of SAM.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the trade-offs between sparsity and performance.
