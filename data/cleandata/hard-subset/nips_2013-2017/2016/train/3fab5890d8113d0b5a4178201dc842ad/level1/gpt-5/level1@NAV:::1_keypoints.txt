- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a differentiable memory network enforcing sparse reads/writes and using ANN indices for sublinear memory access.
- Introduces K-sparse content reads via ANN search (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to avoid dense addressing.
- Uses K-sparse writes to read locations plus an LRU slot, with an in-place rollback scheme for efficient BPTT.
- Extends the approach to Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), demonstrating generality beyond NTMs.
- Shows large empirical speedups and memory savings (100–1600x speed, up to 3000x memory reduction) and scalability to 100k+ steps.
- Achieves comparable learning on synthetic tasks, strong bAbI results without memory supervision, and good Omniglot one-shot performance.
- Technical contribution is sensible and carefully implemented; asymptotic O(log N) time and O(1) per-step memory claims are plausible but rely on ANN quality and constant K.
- Write policy (read-following + LRU) and rollback mechanism are simple, effective, and useful engineering contributions.
- Empirical validation is broad, but lacks some baselines (e.g., RL-based sparse addressing, Zaremba & Sutskever) and more ablations.
- Clarity is good; suggests adding a concise complexity table, guidance for selecting K/ANN parameters, and clarifying index rebuild impact on amortized complexity.
- Original combination of differentiable memory, ANN-based sparse addressing, and principled sparse BPTT is novel.
- Significance is high due to scalability improvements and long-horizon BPTT capability.
- Cons: limited analysis of trade-offs from approximate/non-differentiable ANN components.
- Cons: simple write policy may struggle on tasks needing distributed or content-based writes.
- Cons: missing comparisons to alternative sparse/discrete addressing methods and limited ablations on K and ANN choices.
