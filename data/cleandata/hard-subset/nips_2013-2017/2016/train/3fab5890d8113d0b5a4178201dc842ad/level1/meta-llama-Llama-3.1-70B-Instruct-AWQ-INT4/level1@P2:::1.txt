This paper presents a novel approach to scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing a sparse read and write scheme, called Sparse Access Memory (SAM). The authors demonstrate that SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity, and show that it can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models on a range of tasks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the technical details of the proposed approach. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work, highlighting the limitations of existing MANNs and the potential benefits of scaling them to larger memories.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant. The authors propose a novel sparse read and write scheme that allows for efficient computation and memory usage, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a range of tasks. The use of approximate nearest neighbor indexes, such as randomized k-d trees and locality sensitive hashing, is also a key innovation that enables the model to scale to large memories.

The experimental results are impressive, showing that SAM can learn with comparable data efficiency to existing models on a range of tasks, including synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot character recognition, and question answering on the Babi tasks. The results also demonstrate the scalability of the approach, showing that SAM can be trained on tasks that require storing a large amount of information over long sequences.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the technical details of the proposed approach, making it easy to understand the key innovations and how they are implemented. The paper also includes a number of useful figures and tables that help to illustrate the key concepts and results.

Another strength of the paper is its thoroughness. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the space and time complexity of the proposed approach, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a range of tasks. They also provide a number of useful comparisons to existing models, which helps to put the results into context.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed approach. While the authors do discuss some of the potential limitations of the approach, such as the need for careful tuning of the hyperparameters, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs involved in using a sparse read and write scheme.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of MANNs, and demonstrates a novel and effective approach to scaling these models to larger memories. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough analysis of the technical details of the proposed approach.
