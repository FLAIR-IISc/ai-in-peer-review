- Paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse reads/writes + ANN data structures to scale MANNs with asymptotic bounds (O(log N) per op, O(N) init space, O(1) per time step).

- Theoretical contribution: rigorous analysis showing SAM achieves asymptotic optimality (Supplementary A).

- Empirical evaluation: comprehensive experiments on synthetic tasks, Babi, and Omniglot with detailed baselines (NTM, DAM).

- Practical gains: SAM achieves ~1,000× runtime speedup and ~3,000× reduction in physical memory vs. dense models while maintaining data efficiency.

- Implementation: well-justified engineering (sparse matrix formats, efficient backprop) and reproducible experimental setup.

- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and figures effectively illustrate computational and learning advantages.

- Originality: novel end-to-end differentiable sparsity for MANNs that generalizes to other architectures (e.g., Sparse DNC) and improves on prior scalability efforts.

- Significance: addresses a fundamental MANN limitation, enabling use with 100,000s of time steps/memory slots and improving real-world applicability.

- Reported results: state-of-the-art performance on Babi (SDNC) without supervised memory access and improved Omniglot one-shot classification with larger memory.

- Weakness — sparsity trade-offs: paper lacks systematic analysis of sparsity parameter K (e.g., K=4 or 8) and potential performance degradation at higher sparsity.

- Weakness — ANN choices: k-d tree vs. LSH implementations are described but not quantitatively compared for efficiency/accuracy trade-offs.

- Recommendation: overall high-quality, technically sound, novel, and significant work; recommended for acceptance with minor suggestions to analyze K and ANN trade-offs further.
