This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), an end-to-end differentiable memory-augmented neural network that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices to achieve sublinear access in memory size. The approach extends the line of work on Neural Turing Machines (Graves et al.) and Memory Networks (Weston et al.; Sukhbaatar et al.) by targeting the key bottleneck—linear-time, linear-space memory access and dense backpropagation through time (BPTT). SAM introduces two main ideas: (i) K-sparse content reads via ANN search (randomized k-d trees or LSH) and (ii) K-sparse writes to previously read locations plus the least recently used (LRU) location, combined with an in-place “rollback” scheme for efficient BPTT. The authors also show how the method can be adapted to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), yielding SDNC. Empirically, SAM achieves orders-of-magnitude wall-clock and memory savings over dense NTMs, learns comparably on canonical synthetic tasks, scales to 100k+ steps, performs well on Omniglot one-shot classification, and attains strong bAbI results without memory supervision.

Quality: The technical contribution is sensible and implemented carefully. The asymptotic claims (O(log N) time per step, O(1) per-step memory) are plausible given the data structures, though these rely on assumptions (e.g., ANN quality, constant K) and details are mostly in the supplement. The write policy (read-following + LRU) is simple and effective, and the backward “rollback” mechanism is a practical engineering contribution. Empirical validation is broad, though some baselines (e.g., RL-based sparse addressing, Zaremba & Sutskever) are missing.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. Suggestions: add a concise complexity table in the main text; provide more guidance on selecting K and ANN parameters; clarify the impact of index rebuilds on amortized complexity.

Originality: Combining differentiable memory with ANN-based sparse content addressing and a principled sparse BPTT mechanism is novel relative to prior NTMs/Memory Networks. The SDNC extension strengthens the claim of generality.

Significance: The scalability advance is substantial and relevant to many sequence and memory-intensive tasks. The demonstrated 100–1600x speedups and 3000x memory reductions are compelling, and the ability to BPTT over thousands of steps is important.

Pros (for acceptance):
- Clear, practical path to scaling differentiable memory with strong empirical gains
- General technique applicable beyond NTMs (e.g., DNC)
- Solid results on synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot; impressive long-horizon generalization
- Careful systems contributions (rollback, sparse updates)

Cons (against acceptance):
- Limited analysis of trade-offs from approximate indexing and non-differentiable ANN components
- Simple write policy may underperform on tasks requiring distributed or content-based writes
- Missing comparisons to alternative sparse/discrete addressing methods; limited ablations on K and ANN choice

Overall, a strong, well-motivated, and impactful contribution that fits squarely within NIPS.
