- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural architecture using sparse reads/writes to improve scalability.  
- Introduces sparse read and write operations plus an efficient memory management scheme.  
- Uses approximate nearest neighbor indexes (randomized k-d trees, LSH) to reduce computational overhead.  
- Claims significant space and time complexity improvements and training speedups over dense MANNs.  
- Provides a detailed time and space complexity analysis demonstrating efficiency and scalability.  
- Evaluates SAM on synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot, and bAbI question-answering with comprehensive experiments.  
- Presentation is clear and well-organized, making the architecture and components easy to follow.  
- Original implementation and analysis of sparsity in memory access represent a meaningful contribution.  
- Lacks a detailed analysis of trade-offs between sparse and dense versions, including potential drawbacks of sparsity.  
- Needs more comprehensive comparisons to related work (e.g., Differentiable Neural Computer) and broader discussion of limitations.  
- Some experiments are insufficiently explained or analyzed in depth.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, significant, and well-presented (Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 9/10).
