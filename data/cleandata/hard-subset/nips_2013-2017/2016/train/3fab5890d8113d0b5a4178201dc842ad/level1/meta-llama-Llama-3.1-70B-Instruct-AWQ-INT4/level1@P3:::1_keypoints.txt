- Summary: Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented NN with sparse read/write and an efficient content-based read data structure, evaluated on synthetic tasks, one-shot Omniglot, and bAbI.
- Related work: Builds on NTMs and Memory Networks, addressing their compute/memory overhead and citing RL-based discrete addressing as related scalable approaches.
- Strength — Scalability: SAM enables training MANNs with much larger memories while maintaining time and space efficiency.
- Strength — Efficient memory access: Sparse read/write plus an efficient data structure yields fast, efficient content-based memory operations.
- Strength — Flexibility: Applicable across tasks including synthetic benchmarks, one-shot Omniglot, and bAbI question answering.
- Strength — Empirical performance: Reports state-of-the-art results on several evaluated tasks.
- Weakness — Sparse approximation analysis: Lacks in-depth analysis of trade-offs between sparse and dense memory models.
- Weakness — Comparative baselines: Does not sufficiently compare to other scalable memory architectures (e.g., RL-based methods).
- Weakness — Controller details: Limited discussion/analysis of the controller (uses a simple one-layer LSTM) and its impact on performance.
- Quality: Technically sound with theoretical justification and thorough experimental evaluation supporting the claims.
- Clarity: Well-written and organized, with clear explanations, figures, and tables.
- Originality: Offers a novel approach to scaling MANNs via sparse approximations and efficient data structures.
- Significance: Important contribution for scalable MANNs, with potential broad applicability and insights on sparse vs. dense trade-offs.
- Pro acceptance: Novel solution to scalability, strong empirical gains, and applicability across tasks argue for acceptance.
- Con acceptance: Missing comprehensive comparisons, deeper sparse/dense analysis, and more controller investigation argue for revision.
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance, with suggestions to address the noted weaknesses in a future revision.
