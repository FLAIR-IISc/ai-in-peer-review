- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), which scales memory-augmented neural networks by restricting reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory locations.  
- Motivated by MANNs’ scalability bottleneck where compute and memory grow linearly with memory size.  
- Proposes a sparse read/write scheme plus efficient data structures (CSR) and approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search for asymptotic space/time improvements.  
- Reports large empirical gains: ~1000x speedups and ~3000x memory reductions versus dense models with comparable data efficiency.  
- Validated on synthetic tasks and one-shot Omniglot; also adapted successfully to a sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).  
- Situates work within MANN literature (NTMs, Memory Networks) and contrasts with common compression techniques like pruning/quantization.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and problem definition addressing a critical MANN limitation.  
- Strength: Technically sound method with justified asymptotic complexity improvements.  
- Strength: Empirical validation is comprehensive, including curriculum scaling and comparisons to NTMs/DAM.  
- Strength: Generality shown by adapting SAM to the Differentiable Neural Computer.  
- Strength: Paper is generally well written and concepts are clearly explained.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., sparsity level K and usage parameters).  
- Weakness: Sparse discussion of ANN implementation details (FLANN/LSH configurations) and lack of ablation on ANN choice.  
- Weakness: Limited comparisons to other scaling approaches (e.g., discrete addressing via reinforcement learning).  
- Weakness: Little investigation of cases where sparsity might harm learning (negative transfer on complex tasks).  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on supplementary material for important architectural and experimental details.  
- For acceptance: Addresses a key scalability bottleneck with strong technical and empirical contributions and demonstrated generality.  
- Against acceptance: Concerns about robustness due to hyperparameter/ANN choices and incomplete comparisons; reliance on supplements.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor revisions to expand hyperparameter discussion and briefly compare alternative scaling methods.
