- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to improve MANN scalability by restricting reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory with efficient data structures.
- Problem is well-motivated: existing MANNs suffer from linear memory access overhead limiting scalability.
- Positions work clearly within MANN literature and cites relevant prior work.
- Technically sound: sparse operations, ANNs, and compressed sparse representations are well-justified; efficient backpropagation is a notable contribution.
- Claims asymptotic space/time improvements (details in supplement) that are compelling.
- Reports substantial empirical gains: up to ~1000x speedups and ~3000x memory savings versus standard NTM implementations.
- Maintains competitive performance and data efficiency on synthetic tasks and the Babi QA dataset, including long-sequence tasks.
- Demonstrates generality by adapting SAM to a sparse DNC (SDNC).
- Paper is generally well-written with clear explanations, figures, and tables.
- Novelty concern: sparsity itself is not new and prior work has explored sparse addressing; differentiation from prior sparse approaches could be clearer.
- ANN implementation choices (e.g., FLANN, LSH) and their tuning are under-explained; impact of ANN quality is not thoroughly analyzed.
- Curriculum learning details (thresholds, schedules) are insufficiently specified, hurting reproducibility.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for theoretical analysis, backprop implementation, and experimental setup limits in-paper evaluation.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a key MANN bottleneck, technically solid, large empirical improvements, generalizable approach, and competitive performance.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty of sparsity idea, important details pushed to supplement, and ANN implementation specifics lacking depth.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — contributions and empirical results outweigh the relatively minor weaknesses.
