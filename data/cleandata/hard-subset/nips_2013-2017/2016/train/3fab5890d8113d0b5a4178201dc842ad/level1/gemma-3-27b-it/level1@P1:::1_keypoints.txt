- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN that restricts reads/writes to a sparse memory subset to improve scalability.
- Claims theoretical space and time complexity improvements and empirical speedups (up to 1000x) with much less memory (up to 3000x).
- Validated on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbi reasoning tasks, and one-shot Omniglot, showing scalability to large memories and long sequences.
- Extends the sparse access idea to a sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), demonstrating generality.
- Builds on NTM and Memory Networks literature and targets the BPTT/smooth read-write computational bottleneck.
- Differentiates from RL-based scaling approaches by remaining gradient-based; cites relevant attention, LSTM, and ANN prior work.
- Major strength: dramatic reduction in computational cost and memory footprint, enabling larger-scale MANN applications.
- Major strength: theoretical analysis supports asymptotic optimality in space and time.
- Major strength: comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse tasks, including curriculum scaling experiments.
- Major strength: successful adaptation to DNC shows broader applicability of the sparse access scheme.
- Major strength: clear, well-written exposition with useful supplementary material.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially sparsity level (K) and usage-statistic parameters.
- Weakness: brief description of the Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) implementation; lacks choice rationale and configuration details (e.g., FLANN vs. LSH).
- Weakness: limited comparisons to more recent memory-augmented architectures or alternative scaling techniques.
- Weakness: limited ablation study isolating contributions of sparse read vs. sparse write and different usage definitions.
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a critical scalability bottleneck in MANNs with substantial empirical and theoretical gains.
- Argument for acceptance: offers significant efficiency improvements without apparent performance loss and demonstrates generality.
- Argument for acceptance: strong combination of theory and experiments supports the approach.
- Argument against acceptance: robustness concerns due to missing hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
- Argument against acceptance: reproducibility concerns from incomplete ANN implementation details.
- Argument against acceptance: unclear impact on truly complex, real-world applications remains to be demonstrated.
- Overall assessment: significant contribution recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address hyperparameter sensitivity and ANN implementation details.
