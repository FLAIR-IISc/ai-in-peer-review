- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale MANNs by restricting reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory locations.  
- Core idea: reduce computation and memory overhead of dense memory access while retaining representational power.  
- Demonstrates large speedups (up to 1000x) and memory reductions (up to 3000x) versus standard NTMs.  
- Shows comparable or improved performance on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and one-shot Omniglot classification.  
- Extends approach to create a sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), demonstrating generalizability.  
- Builds on existing MANN literature and leverages ANN search techniques for efficient reads.  
- Theoretical justification: argues asymptotic optimality in space and time (details in supplement).  
- Strong empirical validation across diverse tasks and scaling via curriculum learning.  
- Writing and organization are clear and logical.  
- Weakness: insufficient details and ablation on ANN index parameter choices (e.g., FLANN/LSH settings).  
- Weakness: limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and performance variation.  
- Weakness: lacks direct empirical comparison with reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing methods.  
- Weakness: impact of using different usage definitions U(1) vs U(2) is not fully explored.  
- Weakness: the asymptotic optimality claim is somewhat overstated and should be qualified.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a key bottleneck, shows compelling empirical gains, generalizable design, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reproducibility concerns from missing ANN/hyperparameter analysis, insufficient comparisons to alternatives, and optimistic optimality claims.  
- Overall assessment: strong, practical contribution deserving acceptance (Rating: Accept).
