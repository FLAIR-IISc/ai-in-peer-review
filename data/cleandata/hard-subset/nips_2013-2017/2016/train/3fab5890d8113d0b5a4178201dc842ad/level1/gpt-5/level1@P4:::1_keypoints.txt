- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN using sparse top-K reads and sparse writes plus ANN data structures to scale to very large external memories.  
- Claims O(log N) time per read/write and O(1) per-step memory during BPTT via sparse deltas and rollback, argued near-optimal under content-based addressing.  
- Integrates ANN (randomized k-d trees, LSH) into end-to-end training without backpropagating through the index.  
- Demonstrates large empirical speedups (up to ~1000x runtime, ~3000x memory reduction) versus dense MANNs and scalability to millions of memory slots and >4k-step BPTT.  
- Evaluations include synthetic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling to long sequences, bAbI QA (with SDNC), and Omniglot one-shot classification with long episodes.  
- Technical strength: principled sparse read/write scheme and clear complexity analysis; simple effective sparse write allocation (previous-read + LRU).  
- Implementation strength: practical ANN integration, online index maintenance, and BPTT memory rollback are well engineered.  
- Empirical strength: maintains data efficiency, scales to long sequences, gives competitive bAbI and improved Omniglot results due to larger usable memory.  
- Generality: approach adapts to DNC (SDNC), indicating applicability across MANN architectures.  
- Theoretical weakness: optimality claims rest on assumptions (constant K, content-based addressing); limited formal treatment in main text and no analysis of ANN effect on gradient fidelity.  
- Experimental weakness: limited ablations and sensitivity analyses for K, activity threshold ε, rebuild frequency, and choice of ANN on accuracy/speed.  
- Writing policy concern: the previous-read + LRU write rule may limit expressivity for tasks needing broader/content-based updates; alternatives not empirically tested.  
- Evaluation gap: lacks experiments on large real-world sequential tasks (e.g., language modeling, translation) mentioned in motivation.  
- Reproducibility concern: specialized data structures and custom trackers may complicate reproduction; hyperparameter/code clarity and availability are not fully clear.  
- Quality assessment: method is technically sound with supported complexity claims and generally fair comparisons, though some experimental confounds could be discussed more.  
- Clarity assessment: paper is generally clear but important implementation details are in the supplement; pseudocode for memory/index rollback would aid reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel combination of end-to-end differentiable sparse access, ANN integration during training, and efficient BPTT rollback; SDNC extension is nontrivial.  
- Significance: addresses core MANN scaling bottleneck with 2–3 orders of magnitude efficiency gains and practical applicability to very large memories and long sequences.  
- Suggested improvements: summarize optimality/assumptions in main text, add ablations for K/ε/ANN choices, include at least one larger real-world sequence task, report variance across seeds, and clarify index rebuild policy.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, well-motivated scalability advance with convincing empirical evidence and broad applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete theoretical characterization of approximation effects, insufficient sensitivity/ablation studies, missing real-world task experiments, and potential reproducibility hurdles.  
- Overall judgment: substantial, impactful advance in scalable differentiable memory suitable for acceptance at the venue despite some theory and ablation gaps.
