Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that achieves scalable training by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes to external memory and by employing approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexing for content-based addressing. Reads attend to the top-K nearest memory slots (K constant) identified via kd-trees or LSH; writes update only a small subset—previously read locations and the least-recently-used slot—using a simple usage-based allocator. To avoid O(NT) memory during BPTT, the authors store sparse in-place diffs and roll back updates during the backward pass, yielding O(1) space per step and O(log N) time for memory operations.

This work builds directly on NTMs [Graves et al., 2014] and Memory Networks [Weston et al., 2014; Sukhbaatar et al., 2015], and is related to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) [Graves et al., 2016] in its use of usage-based allocation. It contrasts with reinforcement learning-based discrete addressing (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015), claiming lower-variance, end-to-end differentiability while still touching only a sparse subset. The use of FLANN kd-trees [Muja & Lowe] and LSH [Motwani et al.] inside a differentiable system is an interesting and pragmatic choice.

On benchmarks, SAM achieves large wall-clock and memory savings (up to ~1000× faster and ~3000× less physical memory than dense models) and matches or slightly exceeds dense baselines (NTM, a dense approximation DAM) on classic NTM tasks, scales with an exponential curriculum to millions of memory slots and thousands of untruncated BPTT steps, achieves strong results on bAbI without supervision (and an SDNC variant solves 19/20), and improves over prior MANNs on Omniglot one-shot classification at larger sequence lengths.

Strengths
- Compelling scalability: Asymptotic O(log N) time for memory access and O(1) space per step are backed by careful engineering (sparse diffs with rollback, ANN indices). Empirical curves corroborate the claims.
- Maintains learning performance: Despite sparsity and approximate retrieval, SAM learns algorithmic tasks as well as dense models, sometimes better, and generalizes to sequences far beyond training (e.g., 200k steps on associative recall).
- Generality: The approach extends to DNC (SDNC), yielding strong bAbI performance without supervised memory retrieval.
- Clear practical contributions: The memory allocator (LRU-like), write scheme, and BPTT rollback are simple and reproducible design choices.

Weaknesses
- Differentiability and selection bias: The top-K neighbor selection is non-differentiable (no gradients through the ANN or the index set). While effective empirically, the paper does not analyze how this impacts gradient bias or stability, nor compare to soft approximations of top-K.
- Limited ablations: Sensitivity to K, the threshold ε for usage, ANN type, and rebuild frequency are not thoroughly explored; the trade-off between speed and accuracy is not quantified.
- Benchmark scope: Most gains are shown on synthetic tasks and Omniglot. There are no large-scale, real-world sequence modeling results (e.g., language modeling, MT) where the advertised scaling would be most impactful.
- Clarity/placement of theory: Asymptotic optimality and key implementation details are relegated to the supplement; providing at least a concise sketch of assumptions for optimality and a pseudocode for rollback in the main text would improve accessibility.
- Fairness/transparency of speed comparisons: While the results are striking, more details on hardware, libraries, and implementation choices (e.g., BLAS use, ANN parameters) would bolster the claims and help reproducibility.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong. The algorithmic design is sound and the implementation is carefully thought out. Complexity claims are plausible and supported empirically; formal proofs and assumptions are in the supplement. Experimental coverage is good for synthetic tasks and small-scale reasoning, though more diverse real-world tasks would strengthen the case. Claims about “retaining representational power” would benefit from a more precise argument, given the non-differentiable selection.
- Clarity: Good. The core model (sparse read/write, usage-based allocation) is clearly presented with equations and intuitive motivation. However, crucial details (rollback mechanics, ANN maintenance, optimality assumptions) are deferred to the supplement; brief summaries in the main text would help. Figures convincingly show scaling behavior.
- Originality: High. While sparse addressing and ANN are known tools, combining (i) sparse, end-to-end training with (ii) efficient BPTT via in-place diffs/rollback and (iii) practical ANN integration for content-based addressing in MANNs is novel and impactful. The extension to DNC underscores generality.
- Significance: High. Efficient memory access is a key bottleneck for MANNs. Demonstrating feasible training with millions of slots and thousands of steps opens pathways for applying MANNs to real problems. Even if some choices are heuristic, the practical utility is substantial.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide ablations over K and ε, and quantify how increasing K affects accuracy vs runtime/memory.
- How often is the ANN rebuilt, and how sensitive are results to rebuild frequency and index parameters? Any instability when the neighbor set changes discretely across steps?
- Could a hybrid write scheme (e.g., sparse content-based writes when memory is sufficiently populated) improve flexibility?
- Can you report end-to-end training wall-clock times for the larger curricula, and specify hardware and library versions?
- Code release would notably enhance impact and reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a simple, effective, and general solution.
- Strong empirical speed/memory improvements without sacrificing learning performance.
- Generalizes to other MANNs (DNC) and yields competitive reasoning results without supervision.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited real-world evaluations beyond Omniglot; lack of results on large NLP tasks where impact would be most compelling.
- Non-differentiable neighbor selection is insufficiently analyzed; missing ablations and theoretical discussion in the main text.

Overall assessment
A well-executed, original, and practically significant contribution to scalable memory-augmented models. Despite some missing ablations and the reliance on supplemental material for theory/implementation details, the paper meets the NIPS criteria strongly. I recommend acceptance.
