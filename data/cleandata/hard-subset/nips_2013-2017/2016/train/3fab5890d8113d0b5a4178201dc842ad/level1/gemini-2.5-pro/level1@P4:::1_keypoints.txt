- Problem: Dense attention and full-memory caching in MANNs create O(N) time per step and O(N*T) space, limiting scalability.
- Contribution — Sparse Read: Reads only K most relevant memory words using ANN structures (e.g., k-d tree/LSH) to reduce search to O(log N).
- Contribution — Sparse Write: Restricts writes to a sparse set (recently read slots or LRU) to update relevant info and overwrite stale content.
- Contribution — Efficient BPTT: Stores only sparse O(1) modifications per step and reconstructs memory during backprop, reducing per-step space to O(1).
- Differentiability: SAM remains end-to-end differentiable, avoiding reinforcement-learning–based sparse addressing.
- Empirical results: Strong performance on algorithmic tasks, Babi, Omniglot, and DNC; large speedups (~1000x) and memory reductions (~3000x) while maintaining or improving learning.
- Significance: Enables scaling MANNs to millions of slots and very long sequences, opening applications on large corpora and knowledge bases.
- Originality: Novel integration of sparse differentiable operations, classical ANN data structures in the forward pass, and a rollback BPTT mechanism.
- Technical quality: Well-motivated design, supported by theoretical arguments and extensive benchmarks validating asymptotic and practical gains.
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written with clear motivation and presentation of design choices and results.
- Weakness — Implementation complexity: Integrating and periodically rebuilding ANN indices adds engineering overhead and nontrivial trade-offs.
- Weakness — Analysis of approximations: Lacks deeper analysis of failure modes and sensitivity to ANN search accuracy when relevant items are missed.
- Weakness — Hyperparameter sensitivity: No thorough study of sensitivity to new hyperparameters (K, epsilon, ANN parameters).
- Overall assessment: High-quality, significant, and likely influential work with mainly practical/engineering caveats rather than fundamental flaws.
