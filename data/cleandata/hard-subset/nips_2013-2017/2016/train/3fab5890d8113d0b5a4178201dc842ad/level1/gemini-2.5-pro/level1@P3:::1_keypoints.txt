- Identifies scalability bottleneck in MANNs: dense soft attention causes O(N) time and O(NT) backprop space, limiting large-memory/long-sequence use.  
- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to achieve near-optimal asymptotic complexity while remaining end-to-end differentiable.  
- Replaces dense read with sparse read over top-K memory slots using an ANN (k-d tree/LSH) to reduce read time from O(N) to O(log N).  
- Implements sparse write by updating read locations or an LRU slot and uses a rollback BPTT scheme to store only O(1) sparse changes per step.  
- Asymptotic analysis shows reduction of per-step space from O(N) to O(1) and large practical speed/memory gains (reported ~1,000x speedup, ~3,000x memory reduction).  
- Integrates classical ANN data structures into the forward pass—an original and technically sound idea.  
- Empirical validation is thorough: benchmarks confirm complexity claims and algorithmic tasks show sparse approximation does not harm learning.  
- Curriculum experiments scale to sequences >4,000 and generalize to 200,000 with millions of memory words, demonstrating practical utility.  
- Strong results on bAbI and Omniglot, and a Sparse DNC variant achieves state-of-the-art on unsupervised bAbI, indicating generality.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.  
- Weakness: lacks deeper analysis of failure modes where top-K sparse reads might miss many weakly relevant memories.  
- Weakness: needs more detail on ANN index maintenance (rebuild frequency/cost) and effects of shifting memory vector distributions during training.  
- Weakness: write mechanism (read-or-LRU heuristic) may overwrite critical infrequently accessed info; justification for not using content-based writes could be expanded.  
- Weakness: no ablation/sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameter K (choice of K and its effect on performance).  
- Overall recommendation: excellent, novel, and impactful work with minor issues; strong accept.
