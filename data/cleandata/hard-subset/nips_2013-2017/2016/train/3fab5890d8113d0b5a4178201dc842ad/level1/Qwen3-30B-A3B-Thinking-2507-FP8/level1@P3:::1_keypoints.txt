- Paper addresses MANN scalability by proposing Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that restricts reads/writes to a fixed small subset (K=4–8) using ANN and sparse memory management.  
- Key innovations: O(log N) sparse reads via ANN (k-d trees/LSH), sparse writes using read-location reuse + LRU eviction, and O(1) backpropagation space via sparse state tracking.  
- Theoretical complexity claims are rigorously derived and empirically validated, showing O(log N) scaling for SAM vs. O(N) for NTMs/DAM.  
- Sparse backpropagation (tracking only modified words) is elegant and resolves a fundamental MANN bottleneck.  
- ANN integration is practical and well-motivated, enabling large-scale memory (100k+ time steps, 2M slots).  
- Empirical results show dramatic speed/memory gains (up to 1,000× speedup, 3,000× memory reduction) and comparable learning efficiency on synthetic tasks, Omniglot, and Babi.  
- SAM is original in enabling continuous gradient training with sparse access, distinct from discrete RL or DNC pointer methods.  
- SDNC variant achieves strong results on Babi (19/20 tasks) without supervised memory access.  
- Omniglot comparison is confounded by unequal memory capacities (SAM uses 2M words vs. dense models’ 64), obscuring the effect of sparsity vs. capacity.  
- Claims about applicability to language modeling and MT are unsupported because no language/translation experiments are provided.  
- ANN limitations are underexplored: k-d tree imbalance during training is noted but alternatives (e.g., Mondrian forests, LSH forests) and hardware specifics (GPU vs. CPU) are not evaluated.  
- Paper could better contextualize SAM as a solution to the known O(N) cost of content-based addressing rather than presenting it in isolation.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to MANN scalability; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address experimental fairness and ANN discussion.
