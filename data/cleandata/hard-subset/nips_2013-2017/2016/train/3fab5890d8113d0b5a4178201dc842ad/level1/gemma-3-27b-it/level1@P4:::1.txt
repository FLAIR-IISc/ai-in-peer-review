This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a method for scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by restricting read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations. The core idea is to reduce the computational and memory overhead associated with dense memory access, while maintaining representational power. The authors demonstrate significant speedups and memory reductions compared to standard NTMs, and show comparable or improved performance on a range of tasks including synthetic algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning, and one-shot Omniglot classification. They further generalize the approach to create a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in MANNs, including Neural Turing Machines [7] and Memory Networks [21]. These models address the limitations of recurrent networks like LSTMs [11] in handling long-term dependencies by decoupling memory capacity from model parameters.  The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of dense memory access as a key obstacle to scaling these models. Recent works attempt to address this via reinforcement learning for discrete addressing [22] or architectural changes to the memory access mechanism. SAM provides a complementary approach through sparsity and efficient data structures.  The use of approximate nearest neighbor search for efficient read operations draws on established techniques in information retrieval [15, 3].

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Speedup & Memory Reduction:** The experimental results demonstrate compelling improvements in training speed (1000x) and memory usage (3000x) compared to standard NTM implementations, a major practical contribution.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper argues for the asymptotic optimality of SAM in space and time complexity, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the approach (detailed in the supplementary material).
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The results across a diverse set of tasks – synthetic, reasoning, and image classification – suggest the approach is robust and generalizes well. Scaling tasks via curriculum learning further highlights its potential.
* **Generalizability:** Adapting the approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer demonstrates the method’s broader applicability beyond the specifically designed SAM architecture.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **ANN Implementation Details:** While the use of ANN indexes is presented, details on parameter selection (e.g., number of trees in FLANN, hash function parameters in LSH) and their impact on performance are limited. A more detailed ablation study on the choice of ANN could strengthen the results.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity. While hyperparameters are reported in the supplementary material, a discussion of how performance varies with different settings would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to RL-based Approaches:** While the authors mention reinforcement learning approaches [22], a direct comparison – even with limited experiments – would provide a clearer understanding of the trade-offs between the proposed method and alternative scaling strategies.
* **Usage Definition Impact:** The paper utilizes two different usage definitions U(1) and U(2). The effect of switching between these definitions is not explicitly explored or discussed in detail, despite U(2) being the default for SAM.
* **Overstated Optimality Claim**: The claim of asymptotic optimality should be more carefully worded. While the paper demonstrates good scaling, true optimality depends on many practical considerations not fully addressed.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks, providing a practical solution for scaling these models to larger memory sizes.
* The results are compelling, demonstrating significant speedups and memory reductions without sacrificing performance.
* The method is generalizable and can be applied to different MANN architectures.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the proposed method and experimental results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed analysis of the ANN implementation and hyperparameter sensitivity limits the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
* A more thorough comparison with alternative scaling strategies, such as reinforcement learning, would strengthen the paper.
* The optimality claim is potentially overstated and needs to be qualified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed method (SAM) offers a compelling solution for scaling these models, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I believe this work represents a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Rating:** Accept.
