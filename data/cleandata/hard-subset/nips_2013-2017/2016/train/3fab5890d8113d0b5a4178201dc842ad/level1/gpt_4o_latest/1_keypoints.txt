- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN using sparse read/write and ANN indices to improve scalability.  
- Claims asymptotic time and space improvements, handling millions of memory slots with large speed/memory reductions (up to 1,600× speedup, 3,000× memory reduction).  
- Empirically matches or outperforms dense models on synthetic tasks, curriculum learning, Omniglot one-shot, and Babi reasoning, and scales to long sequences.  
- Strong technical contribution: sparsity yields significant computational and memory efficiency supported by theory and experiments.  
- Extensive empirical validation across diverse tasks, with notable generalization to much longer sequences than training.  
- Paper is well-written and organized, with clear architecture description and reproducible supplementary material.  
- Effectively compares SAM to prior work (NTMs, Memory Networks) and dense variants, strengthening claims.  
- Limited evaluation on complex real-world domains (e.g., large-scale NLP), reducing immediate practical impact.  
- Insufficient analysis of sparsity trade-offs (e.g., effects on gradient flow and long-term retention).  
- ANN implementation choices are only briefly discussed; more comparison of methods and training impact is needed.  
- Broader implications and applications beyond tested tasks are not fully explored.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, well-supported technical advance; scalability to large memory/long sequences; clear presentation.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited real-world validation and incomplete analysis of sparsity/ANN design choices.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — strengths outweigh limitations.
