Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales to very large external memories by enforcing sparsity in both read and write operations and by using efficient data structures for approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search. Unlike Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) and similar dense-access MANNs, SAM constrains reads to the top-K (small K) nearest memory slots via ANN search (randomized k-d trees or LSH), and constrains writes to a sparse combination of previously read locations and the least-recently-used slot. The design yields O(log N) time per step for read/write and O(1) memory per time step during backpropagation, with the memory rolled back using sparse deltas. The authors argue the approach is asymptotically optimal (under content-based addressing assumptions), and demonstrate large empirical speedups over NTM and a dense variant (DAM).

Experiments include: (1) synthetic NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), (2) scaling via curriculum to long sequences (up to >4k steps untruncated BPTT and memory in the millions), (3) bAbI question answering, where a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) also performs strongly, and (4) Omniglot one-shot classification with long episodes. Results show up to ~1000x runtime and ~3000x memory reductions versus dense models for large N, comparable or better learning efficiency on synthetic tasks, the ability to handle long sequences, competitive (largely unsupervised) bAbI performance, and improved Omniglot performance attributed to larger usable memory.

This work builds directly on NTMs (Graves et al., 2014) and Memory Networks/End-to-End Memory Networks (Weston et al., 2014; Sukhbaatar et al., 2015), and is closely related to the DNC (Graves et al., 2016). It differs by providing end-to-end differentiable sparse access with explicit complexity improvements and by integrating ANN data structures into the learning loop without backpropagating through the index. It contrasts with discrete addressing approaches trained by RL (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015), avoiding high-variance gradients.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: A principled sparse read/write scheme for MANNs with clear complexity analysis (O(log N) time per step; O(1) per-step memory during BPTT via rollback). The sparse write allocation (previous-read + LRU) is simple and effective.
- Implementation insights: Efficient ANN integration, maintenance of an online index, and memory rollback for BPTT are well thought out and practically valuable.
- Empirical validation: Strong speed and memory benchmarks; demonstration that sparse approximations retain data efficiency; scalability to long sequences and very large memories; competitive bAbI results (notably with SDNC) without supervised memory retrieval; meaningful gains on Omniglot due to increased capacity.
- Generality: The method adapts to DNC (SDNC), suggesting broader applicability to MANN architectures.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical guarantees: The optimality claims rely on assumptions (constant K, content-based addressing); formal proofs are deferred to Supplementary A with limited detail in the main text. The effect of ANN approximation on gradient fidelity is not theoretically characterized.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Limited exploration of sensitivity to K, threshold ε for activity, rebuild frequency of the ANN, and choice of ANN (k-d tree vs LSH) on both accuracy and speed. More systematic ablations would strengthen conclusions.
- Writing policy limitations: The write scheme (previously read + least recently used) may restrict expressivity for tasks requiring broader or content-based updates; the paper notes alternatives but does not empirically evaluate them.
- Evaluation breadth: While results are strong on synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot episodes, evaluations on large real-world sequential tasks targeted in the introduction (e.g., language modeling, translation) are absent.
- Reproducibility details: The heavy reliance on specialized data structures (custom usage tracker, index maintenance) may complicate reproduction; clarity on code availability and precise hyperparameters would help (some hyperparameters are in the supplement).

Quality:
- The method is technically sound and carefully engineered. Complexity claims are supported by algorithmic design and empirical scaling plots. Experimental comparisons are generally fair, though some setups (e.g., differing memory sizes matched on physical memory) could be discussed more explicitly for potential confounds. The inclusion of both exact and ANN variants is helpful.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear, with well-structured descriptions of sparse reads/writes, controller interactions, and BPTT rollback. Some critical implementation details are relegated to the supplement (usage tracking, index maintenance), which could be summarized more prominently. Adding pseudocode for the forward/backward memory update/rollback and index synchronization would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The combination of end-to-end differentiable sparse access, ANN integration within training, and efficient BPTT rollback is novel and nontrivial relative to prior MANNs. The SDNC extension further demonstrates novelty in adapting related architectures.

Significance:
- The contribution addresses a central bottleneck for MANNs: scaling memory and sequence length. The demonstrated 2–3 orders of magnitude efficiency gains and ability to train with million-slot memories and thousands of steps are significant and likely to influence subsequent work. The approach is practical and broadly applicable.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a concise main-text summary of the optimality argument and any assumptions.
- Add ablations varying K, ε, and ANN choices; report accuracy-speed trade-offs.
- Include experiments on at least one larger-scale real dataset relevant to the proposed applications (e.g., language modeling with long contexts), even if preliminary.
- Discuss stability and variance across seeds; report confidence intervals.
- Clarify index rebuild policy and its effect on performance; consider online-balanced alternatives (e.g., LSH forests, Mondrian-inspired partitioning).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, well-motivated contribution that materially advances the scalability of MANNs.
- Clear complexity advantages with convincing empirical validation.
- General method applicable to multiple memory architectures (including DNC).
- Competitive or superior performance on established benchmarks while using much larger memories.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical characterization of approximation effects on gradients and learning.
- Insufficient ablation studies and sensitivity analyses.
- Lack of experiments on the real-world tasks highlighted in the motivation.
- Dependence on specialized data structures may hinder reproducibility without released code.

Overall, the paper offers a substantial and well-supported advance in scalable differentiable memory, with high potential impact despite some ablation/theory gaps. The work is in scope for NIPS.
