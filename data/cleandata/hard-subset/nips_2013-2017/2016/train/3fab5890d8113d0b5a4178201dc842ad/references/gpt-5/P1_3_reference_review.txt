Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales to very large external memories by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes and by using efficient data structures for content-based addressing. Reads retrieve the top-K nearest memory slots by approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search (k-d trees or LSH), yielding O(log N) per-step time in memory size N. Writes are constrained to a small set: previously read locations (updates) and the least recently used (LRU) slot (allocation). Backpropagation is made memory-efficient by storing and reverting only sparse in-place modifications, avoiding O(NT) memory for BPTT. The authors claim asymptotic optimality in time and space for content-addressed MANNs. Empirically, SAM achieves orders-of-magnitude speed and memory savings over NTM/Dense variants, while maintaining similar or better data efficiency on synthetic NTM tasks, scaling to millions of memory slots under curriculum, strong performance on bAbI (especially with a sparse DNC variant), and improved one-shot learning on Omniglot.

Relation to prior work: SAM extends NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling memory size from compute via sparse access; compared to DNC, SAM adopts a simpler LRU-based allocation and adds an ANN index within the network. It contrasts with RL-based discrete addressing (e.g., RL-NTM), which trades compute for higher-variance gradients.

Strengths
- Clear, pragmatic idea with strong engineering: top-K sparse reads via ANN; sparse writes to read and LRU locations; constant per-step memory footprint via rollback of sparse diffs.
- Theoretical and systems contributions: complexity analysis (O(log N) time, O(1) space per step), and a practical BPTT scheme that avoids duplicating memory states.
- Significant empirical gains: up to 100–1600× speedups and huge reductions in physical memory; scalability to 2M slots and thousands of BPTT steps; algorithmic generalization to sequences >> training lengths.
- Generality: demonstration of a Sparse DNC (SDNC) with large speedups and state-of-the-art bAbI results without supervised memory access.
- Careful comparisons against NTM, dense variant (DAM), and LSTM on standard tasks; evidence that sparsity can aid learning on some tasks.

Weaknesses and questions
- Differentiability and gradient bias: The top-K selection and fixed ANN index introduce nondifferentiabilities and zero gradients to non-selected slots. While empirically effective, the paper should discuss the impact on gradient bias/variance, stability, and convergence (e.g., any straight-through or temperature-annealed alternatives?).
- ANN details and sensitivity: Performance of k-d trees vs LSH depends on word dimensionality and data distribution. Please quantify:
  - How choices of K, dimensionality M, and ANN type affect accuracy, training time, and stability.
  - Overheads and rebuild frequency; wall-clock breakdown of ANN maintenance vs forward/backward compute.
  - Whether the claimed O(log N) holds empirically at larger M and over long training when the index drifts.
- Allocation/erase policy: The LRU+update scheme is simple but may cause overwriting of rare but still relevant items. How sensitive is performance to the threshold for “non-negligible access” and to the erase policy (only LRU erased)? Ablations comparing LRU vs discounted usage (DAM), and content-based writes would strengthen the argument.
- Comparisons and reporting:
  - Main paper lacks full quantitative tables (numbers appear in Supplement). Including key results (e.g., bAbI per-task accuracies, Omniglot error rates) would aid assessment.
  - Baselines beyond NTM/DAM/LSTM on Omniglot (e.g., End-to-End Memory Networks, matching networks) would contextualize gains.
  - Reproducibility: provide more specifics on hardware (CPU/GPU use for ANN), seeds/variance across runs, and hyperparameters (K, epsilon) in main text.
- Asymptotic optimality claim: The argument is deferred to the supplement. A brief, formal statement in the main text clarifying assumptions (e.g., fixed K, content-based addressing) would help.
- Practical deployment: Discussion of GPU-friendliness is limited. ANN and sparse ops can be CPU-bound; clarify how training scales on GPUs and whether the speedups persist in end-to-end GPU pipelines.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a solid systems design and evidence. The main methodological risks (nondifferentiable top-K/ANN) are acknowledged implicitly by not backpropagating through the index; a more explicit treatment and ablations would improve rigor. The experimental suite is broad and well-motivated, though some key numbers are relegated to the supplement.

Clarity
Well-organized and readable. The core mechanisms (sparse read/write, LRU, rollback BPTT) are clearly described, but important implementation details (data structures for usage tracking and rollback, ANN maintenance schedule) are in the supplement. Adding pseudocode for forward/backward with rollback and a concise table summarizing complexities (including K and M) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Introducing sparse access with ANN inside a differentiable MANN, coupled with a constant-space BPTT scheme and a simple LRU allocation, is a novel and impactful combination. Related work (NTM, DNC, Memory Networks, RL-NTM) is appropriately cited.

Significance
High. The work addresses a core bottleneck in MANNs and demonstrates clear scalability and practical speedups, with evidence of applicability to reasoning and meta-learning tasks. The generalization to SDNC further increases impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, practical advance in scalable differentiable memory with compelling speed/memory savings.
- Broad and convincing empirical validation, including large-scale curricula and real tasks.
- Generalizable approach applicable to other MANNs (DNC).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of gradient implications of top-K/ANN; missing ablations on K, ANN type, and allocation policy.
- Key quantitative results and implementation details pushed to the supplement.
- Limited discussion of GPU implementation and wall-clock breakdowns.

Requested clarifications
- Sensitivity to K, epsilon, word dimension; ablations including content-based writes.
- ANN maintenance costs and rebuild policy; empirical scaling with M and N over long training.
- Formal statement of the optimality claim in the main text; effect of index errors on training dynamics.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a well-executed, impactful contribution that substantially advances the scalability of MANNs, with solid evidence and clear potential for adoption and follow-on work. Clarifications and small additions would further strengthen the paper.
