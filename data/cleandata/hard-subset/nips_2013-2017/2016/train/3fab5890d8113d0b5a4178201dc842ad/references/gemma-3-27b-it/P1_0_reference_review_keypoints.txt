- Problem: MANNs face high computational and memory costs from dense read/write operations as memory scales.  
- Contribution: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) that restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset of memory locations.  
- Implementation: Combines sparsity with approximate nearest neighbors (ANN) and custom usage tracking for efficiency.  
- Efficiency results: Reports up to three orders of magnitude speed and memory improvements over a standard NTM while maintaining performance.  
- Evaluation: Comprehensive experiments on algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot learning; curriculum learning shows scalable benefits.  
- Generality: Applies sparsity approach to create a sparse DNC (SDNC) and observes substantial gains, demonstrating broader applicability.  
- Technical soundness: Sparsity plus efficient data structures is well-motivated and clearly justified.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written with detailed explanations and useful supplementary material.  
- Related work: Properly situates contribution among NTMs, Memory Networks, DNCs, and ANN literature.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Lacks thorough analysis of sensitivity to key hyperparameters (e.g., K, usage discount).  
- ANN choice justification: Insufficient justification for choosing FLANN’s randomized k-d tree over LSH across tasks.  
- Comparison to other sparsity methods: Limited discussion contrasting their sparsity with other techniques like weight pruning.  
- Theoretical claims: The “asymptotically optimal” claim needs more rigorous theoretical justification.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution addressing a major scalability bottleneck; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Solves a practical problem, is technically sound, has strong empirical results, shows generality, and is well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Concerns about hyperparameter sensitivity, ANN choice rationale, and theoretical rigor.  
- Recommendation: Strong Accept.
