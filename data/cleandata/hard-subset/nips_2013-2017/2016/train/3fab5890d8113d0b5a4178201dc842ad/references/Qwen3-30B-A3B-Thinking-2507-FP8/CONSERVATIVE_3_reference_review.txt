

# Review of "Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes"

This paper proposes SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a new memory-augmented neural network architecture that constrains all memory reads and writes to a small sparse subset of memory words (K=4-8) rather than operating on the full memory. The authors claim this approach achieves asymptotic optimality in space and time complexity (O(log N) read time, O(1) space per time step), with up to three orders of magnitude improvement in memory and computational efficiency compared to vanilla NTMs, while maintaining comparable performance on benchmark tasks.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution by addressing a genuine limitation of memory-augmented networks: the linear memory and computational overhead of dense reads/writes. The authors demonstrate that sparse access, combined with efficient approximate nearest neighbor data structures, enables significant scaling of memory size without sacrificing performance on several benchmark tasks (NTM tasks, Babi, Omniglot). The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results on synthetic tasks are convincing.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims, methodology, and validation that prevent me from recommending acceptance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies a genuine problem in memory-augmented networks (computational and memory overhead for large memories).
- The technical implementation of sparse access is well-executed and provides clear empirical benefits in terms of scaling.
- The paper demonstrates SAM's effectiveness on standard benchmark tasks, including the Babi reasoning tasks where it achieves state-of-the-art results without supervision of memory access.

**Major Weaknesses:**
1. **Inadequate comparison with recent MANNs:** The paper compares primarily with NTMs and LSTMs, but fails to compare with more recent, sophisticated MANNs like the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) or Memory Networks with advanced attention mechanisms. The paper mentions the SDNC (Sparse DNC) in supplementary material but doesn't compare SAM directly with these more recent approaches in the main text. This is a critical omission given that the field has evolved significantly since the NTM.

2. **Overstated theoretical claims:** The paper claims SAM is "asymptotically optimal" without providing a formal proof or comparison to other theoretical bounds. The claim that SAM is optimal in space and time complexity is not properly qualified - optimal with respect to what? The paper doesn't establish a theoretical lower bound for this problem to compare against.

3. **Lack of explanation for "surprisingly" better performance:** The paper states that sparse models "learn more effectively for some tasks" (Figure 2), but provides no theoretical or empirical explanation for why sparsity would improve learning. This counterintuitive result needs thorough investigation and justification, which the paper lacks. If sparse access actually improves learning, why is this "surprising" to the authors?

4. **Insufficient ablation studies:** The paper doesn't explore how different choices (K=4 vs. K=8, different ANN implementations, different usage statistics for memory allocation) affect performance. Without these ablation studies, it's impossible to determine the robustness of the approach or the optimal parameter choices.

5. **Limited scope of validation:** The paper tests SAM only on synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) and a few standard benchmarks (Babi, Omniglot). It doesn't demonstrate the approach on more challenging, real-world problems where memory efficiency would be critical. The Omniglot results are marginal improvements over previous work.

6. **Overreliance on "surprise" factor:** The paper frequently uses terms like "surprisingly" and "unexpectedly" to describe SAM's performance, which undermines confidence in the results. If sparse access works better, the authors should provide a clear explanation, not just claim it's surprising.

7. **Lack of comparison with alternative sparse approaches:** The paper mentions that "the introduction of sparsity could be achieved via other write schemes" but doesn't actually compare these alternatives. This makes the claim that SAM's specific approach is optimal seem unsubstantiated.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a technically sound approach to scaling memory-augmented networks with sparse reads and writes, it falls short of the rigorous validation required for NIPS. The claims about "optimal" performance are overstated, the theoretical justification is insufficient, and the empirical validation is limited to a narrow set of tasks without adequate comparison to more recent MANNs. The "surprisingly" better performance on some tasks needs proper explanation rather than being presented as an unexpected finding.

The paper makes a modest contribution to a niche area but doesn't advance the field significantly. The approach appears to be a reasonable engineering improvement rather than a fundamental theoretical advance. Given the skepticism of new approaches without thorough validation, I cannot recommend acceptance. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent MANNs, deeper theoretical analysis, and more thorough ablation studies before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
