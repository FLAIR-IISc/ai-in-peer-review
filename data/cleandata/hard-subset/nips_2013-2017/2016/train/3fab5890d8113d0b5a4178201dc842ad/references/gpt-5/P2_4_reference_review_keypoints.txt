- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) scales content-addressable reads/writes by using top-K ANN reads, sparse writes (prev-read or LRU), and sparse rollback for memory-efficient BPTT, yielding O(log N) query time and O(1) per-step memory.  
- Relation to prior work: Direct response to NTM scalability limits; complementary to discrete-addressing RL methods and novel in integrating ANN search and LRU-style allocation into differentiable memory.  

Strengths:
- Clear scalability argument and useful engineering insights (CSR, in-place updates, index maintenance) enabling O(log N)/O(1) claims.  
- Large empirical gains: 100–1600× speedups and orders-of-magnitude memory savings; training with millions of slots and >4000 BPTT steps.  
- Broad validation across tasks (NTM tasks, curriculum scaling, bAbI/SDNC, Omniglot) with sparse matching or exceeding dense performance where capacity matters.  
- Generality: approach applies to multiple MANNs (demonstrated via SDNC).  

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Differentiability concerns: top-K reads and LRU writes are non-differentiable, so index/allocation get no gradient—risk of bias/optimization issues and potential failure modes.  
- Fairness of comparisons: SAM often given much larger memories; need NTM with truncated BPTT, DNC comparisons in main text, and ablations for K, ε, and interpolation/write gates.  
- ANN maintenance/amortized costs: index rebuilds every N insertions make amortized O(log N) dependent on update patterns and dimensionality—empirical rebuild profiling and sensitivity analysis needed.  
- "Optimality" claim depends on assumptions—these should be explicit and the claim tempered in the main text.  
- Reproducibility: many implementation/hyperparameter details are in the supplement and code availability is not stated; open-sourcing would aid impact.  

Evaluation (NeurIPS criteria):
- Quality: Technically sound with sensible complexity arguments and broad experiments, but needs deeper ablations, stability analysis for approximate/top-K reads, and clearer amortized cost accounting.  
- Clarity: Well-written overall; core assumptions and key ablation should be moved from supplement to main text to improve reproducibility.  
- Originality: High—novel combination of ANN in the training loop, sparse rollback, and simple LRU/prev-read allocation.  
- Significance: High—practically scales MANNs by orders of magnitude with likely influence on future work.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a central bottleneck in MANNs with a well-motivated design.  
- Convincing empirical evidence of massive efficiency gains with little loss in task performance.  
- General technique applicable beyond the specific architecture.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis; some design choices appear heuristic.  
- Non-differentiable components raise questions about training stability and when the approach fails.  
- Optimality claim and periodic index rebuild overhead require clearer analysis.  

Recommendation: Accept.  

Minor suggestions:
- Add ablations on K and ANN type and report index rebuild overhead.  
- Clarify assumptions behind the optimality claim and include training-time complexity with rebuild costs.  
- Discuss failure modes (e.g., high-dimensional queries, rapidly drifting memories) and provide guidance for choosing ANN settings.
