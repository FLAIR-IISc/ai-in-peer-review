- Introduces SAM, a memory-augmented neural network that restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset and uses ANN for content-based addressing to scale MANNs.
- Claims SAM achieves O(log N) time per operation and O(1) space per time step while matching dense MANN learning performance.
- Proposes Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) as a generalization and shows strong Babi task results.
- Theoretical analysis (Supplement A) argues asymptotic optimality in time and space.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive: synthetic (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort) and real-world (Babi, Omniglot) tasks with detailed ablations.
- Ablation study on sparsity convincingly demonstrates the effect of sparse access.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides precise architectural descriptions and training details.
- Novel integration of classical data structures (k-d trees, LSH) into neural memory addressing, avoiding RL-based high-variance gradients.
- Significant practical impact: enables scaling to millions of memory words and generalization to very long sequences (up to 200k tokens).
- Limited theoretical treatment of approximation error from sparsity; more formal analysis of read approximation needed.
- Sparse details on ANN implementation and dynamic index updates (e.g., rebuild frequency) hinder reproducibility.
- Lacks a thorough empirical comparison to other scalable approaches (e.g., RL-based discrete addressing) on training stability and convergence.
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a key scaling problem, large efficiency gains (up to 1600x), maintains performance, theoretically grounded, broadly applicable, strong empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient theoretical analysis of approximation errors, incomplete ANN implementation details, and limited comparative evaluation to alternatives.
- Overall assessment: strong, significant, and well-justified contribution that merits acceptance despite some minor theoretical and implementation-detail gaps.
