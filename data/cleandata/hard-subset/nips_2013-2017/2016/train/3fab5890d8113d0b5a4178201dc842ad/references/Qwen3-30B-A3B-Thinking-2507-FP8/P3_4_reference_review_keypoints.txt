- Summary: Proposes SAM, a memory-augmented NN with sparse reads/writes (K=4–8) and ANN structures to achieve O(log N) reads, showing large speedups without performance loss on several tasks.

- Strength — Technical innovation: Novel sparse access solves MANN scalability; theoretical O(log N) time and O(1) per-step space claims and practical ANN implementations are convincing.

- Strength — Experiments: Thorough validation across synthetic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot; curriculum learning shows handling sequences up to 200,000 after training on 10,000.

- Strength — Practical impact: Addresses a core scalability bottleneck with large speedups (up to ~1600x) and massive memory savings (e.g., 53MB vs 29GB for 64K words).

- Strength — Presentation: Well-organized paper with clear technical explanations, efficient BPTT details, and effective figures.

- Weakness — Missing comparison: Fails to adequately compare with RL-based sparse addressing (Zaremba & Sutskever 2016) and should clarify trade-offs in optimization stability and performance.

- Weakness — Lack of theory for learning benefit: Observed early-stage learning gains from sparsity are noted but not theoretically explained.

- Weakness — ANN approximation analysis missing: No investigation of how ANN approximation errors (k-d trees/LSH) affect learning or provide error bounds.

- Weakness — Overstated optimality: Claim of being "optimal in space and time" is overstated; asymptotic optimality holds under assumptions and should be tempered.

- Weakness — Limited failure-mode discussion: Paper does not explore scenarios where sparse access or approximation would harm tasks needing exact/fine-grained memory access.

- Overall assessment: Strong, technically sound contribution with significant practical advantages; minor but addressable weaknesses.

- Recommendation: Accept — important advancement for MANN scalability and relevant to the conference.
