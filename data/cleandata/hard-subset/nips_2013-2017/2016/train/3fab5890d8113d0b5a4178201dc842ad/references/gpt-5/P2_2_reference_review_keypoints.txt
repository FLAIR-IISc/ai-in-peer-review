- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): enforces K-sparse reads/writes using ANN for O(log N) reads and a usage-based allocator for limited writes.  
- Implements reversible sparse in-place updates for BPTT, reducing per-step space from O(NT) to O(1).  
- Claims asymptotic optimality for content-addressed MANNs under assumptions stated in the supplement.  
- Extends approach to a sparse DNC (SDNC).  
- Empirically matches or exceeds dense baselines on synthetic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling, bAbI, and Omniglot.  
- Demonstrates 100–1600× computational speedups and memory per-step independent of N.  
- SDNC achieves near–state-of-the-art on bAbI without supervised memory retrieval.  
- Technical soundness: sparse read/write scheme, allocator, and complexity analysis are clearly defined and plausible.  
- Using ANN indices without gradients is reasonable because query/key similarity remains differentiable.  
- Empirical support: substantial benchmarks showing time/space gains and generalization to very long sequences and large memory.  
- Caveat: insufficient ablation on ANN approximation effects, choice of K, and index hyperparameters.  
- Caveat: simple write policy (recent reads + LRU) may limit expressivity for novel-content writing.  
- Caveat: key proofs and engineering details are largely in the supplement; code availability not stated.  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear; read/write mechanisms and BPTT scheme are well-described.  
- Suggestion: restate assumptions behind asymptotic optimality and include K/ANN ablation in main text; clarify fairness of capacity comparisons.  
- Originality: novel integration of sparse attention, ANN-based addressing, reversible sparse BPTT, and usage-based allocation in a differentiable MANN.  
- Significance: directly addresses scalability bottleneck in MANNs with large empirical gains and likely high impact.  
- Strength: principled time/space complexity reduction with strong empirical validation and generality to other MANNs.  
- Weakness: limited ablations, potential unfair capacity comparisons, heavy supplement reliance, and limited large-scale real-world evaluations.  
- Questions: sensitivity to K and ANN parameters; ability to aggregate >K memories in one step; robustness of LRU allocator; plans to release code.  
- Arguments for acceptance: timely, technically solid, novel, well-motivated, and empirically convincing contribution.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing ablations, fairness issues, reliance on supplemental engineering details, and limited exploration of domains where dense attention may be needed.  
- Overall recommendation: leaning toward acceptance, pending additional ablations and clarity on assumptions.
