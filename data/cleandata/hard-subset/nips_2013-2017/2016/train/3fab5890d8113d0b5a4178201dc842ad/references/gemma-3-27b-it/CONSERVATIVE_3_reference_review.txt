This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), specifically focusing on the computational and memory overhead associated with read and write operations. The authors propose SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a MANN that restricts memory access to a sparse subset of memory locations, coupled with efficient data structures for approximate nearest neighbor search. The core idea – reducing the computational burden of MANNs through sparsity – is a reasonable and potentially impactful direction. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements in runtime and memory usage compared to a dense NTM implementation, and show competitive performance on several tasks, including scaled versions via curriculum learning.

However, while the technical contributions and results are promising, I find myself hesitant to strongly endorse this work for NIPS acceptance. My concerns center around the relative novelty of the core ideas, the framing of the contribution, and the limited depth of analysis in some areas. 

Firstly, the concept of sparsity as a means to reduce computational cost in MANNs isn't entirely novel. Previous work, such as [22], has explored sparse addressing schemes (albeit via reinforcement learning). While SAM differs in its approach—using deterministic, content-based sparsity and efficient data structures—the paper doesn’t sufficiently emphasize how it builds upon, or diverges significantly from, these earlier efforts. The connection to related work feels somewhat superficial.

Secondly, the framing of "optimality in space and time" (as claimed in the introduction) feels overstated. While the asymptotic complexity analysis presented in the supplementary material is appreciated, it relies on assumptions that may not hold in practice, and doesn't fully account for the overhead of the approximate nearest neighbor search. Claiming optimality requires more rigorous justification, particularly given the existing work on alternative sparse memory access methods. 

Thirdly, the experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, lacks sufficient comparison to strong baselines beyond the NTM. The inclusion of a standard LSTM is helpful, but a comparison to other recent, high-performing sequence models, even if not memory-augmented, would strengthen the results. Furthermore, the performance gains on the Babi tasks, while notable, are presented alongside the SDNC, which uses supervised memory access. It’s unclear how much of the performance improvement is attributable to SAM versus the supervised learning aspect of the SDNC. The assertion that SAM “achieves the best reported result” on Babi requires careful parsing – best *without* supervision would be a stronger claim.

Finally, the choice of the LSTM as the controller seems somewhat limiting. While simple, it doesn't fully explore the potential benefits of SAM with more sophisticated controllers that might be better equipped to exploit the sparse memory access pattern.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant improvements in runtime and memory usage compared to dense NTMs.
*   Demonstrated ability to scale to larger memory sizes and longer sequences.
*   Competitive performance on a variety of tasks, including Babi and Omniglot.
*   The combination of sparse access with efficient data structures is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea of sparsity isn’t entirely novel, and the paper doesn't fully contextualize its contribution within the existing literature.
*   Claims of “optimality” are overstated and lack sufficient justification.
*   Limited comparison to strong, non-memory-augmented baselines.
*   Potential limitations of the LSTM controller.
*   The presentation of the SDNC results muddies the waters regarding the true impact of SAM.



Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to scaling MANNs, but falls short of demonstrating a truly groundbreaking advance. The empirical results are encouraging, but the theoretical claims and the lack of a more thorough comparison to related work leave me unconvinced of its significance for a highly competitive NIPS conference. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the concerns raised in the review, and to conduct a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach. A stronger emphasis on the novelty of the method and a more rigorous justification of the claims would substantially improve the paper.
