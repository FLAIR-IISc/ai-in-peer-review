- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse top-K reads via ANN, sparse writes to read locations + LRU, and sparse rollback BPTT for O(1) per-step memory and sublinear time.
- Implements ANN indices (randomized k-d trees or LSH) for O(log N) query time and integrates them into a differentiable memory system.
- Claims asymptotically optimal time/space for content-addressable differentiable memories under stated assumptions (proof relegated to supplement).
- Empirically shows up to 100–1600× speedups and huge memory savings (e.g., 29 GiB vs 7.8 MiB) versus dense NTMs while retaining comparable learning performance.
- Demonstrates strong results across synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA (including SDNC variant without supervision), and Omniglot one-shot learning.
- Strength: clear, impactful problem focusing on scaling differentiable memory to large N and long T.
- Strength: solid algorithmic contributions — sparse read/write design, ANN integration, LRU allocator, and rollback BPTT.
- Strength: convincing complexity analysis and empirical validation of runtime and memory claims.
- Strength: shows sparsity does not harm learning and can improve performance on some tasks (e.g., priority sort).
- Strength: scalability demonstrated to >4k steps and generalization to 200k sequence length and million-word memories.
- Weakness: non-differentiable control (top-K selection and LRU) introduces potential optimization bias and selection not learned or analyzed.
- Weakness: dependence on ANN quality with limited ablations on K, index type, approximation error, and no quantitative ANN recall metrics during training.
- Weakness: some comparisons may be unfair — dense baselines limited in slots while SAM uses very large memory to equalize physical memory.
- Weakness: limited evaluation on large real-world long-context tasks (e.g., long-document QA, code completion, long-horizon RL).
- Weakness: “asymptotically optimal” claim is deferred to supplement; assumptions and comparison to known lower bounds should be in main text.
- Weakness: simple write policy (always to read + LRU) may cause destructive overwrites with noisy reads; failure modes and alternatives not analyzed.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns — many details in supplement, code availability not mentioned, and some hyperparameters/implementation details unclear.
- Quality: technically sound and carefully engineered; speed and memory claims convincingly measured; missing ablations on K, ANN accuracy, and write-policy sensitivity.
- Clarity: well written and organized but critical implementation and proof details are deferred to the supplement; main text should state optimality assumptions.
- Originality: novel and pragmatic combination of sparse read/write, ANN indices in a neural memory, and rollback BPTT; appropriate citation of related work.
- Significance: high — makes previously impractical MANN regimes feasible with substantial speed/memory gains and broad applicability.
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to K; ANN recall/precision during training; amortized costs of index maintenance; experiments with sparse or hybrid writes; robustness of LRU to noisy reads; multi-head effects; explicit optimality assumptions.
- Minor requests: clarify erase mechanism for LRU slots, include ablation comparing exact KNN vs ANN, fix a few incomplete references and supplement implementation details.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important scalability problem, strong empirical gains, general method and SDNC results, clear writing and engineering.
- Arguments against acceptance: inadequate ablations and real-world breadth, non-differentiable selection theory not analyzed in main text.
- Overall recommendation: Accept, with suggested additional analyses and ablations to strengthen the contribution.
