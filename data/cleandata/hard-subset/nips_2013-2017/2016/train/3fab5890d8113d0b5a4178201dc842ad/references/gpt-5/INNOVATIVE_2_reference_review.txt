Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales external memory to millions of slots by making both reads and writes sparse. Reads use top-K content-based addressing computed via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices (k-d trees or LSH), giving O(log N) time per step; writes target only a small set of locations (previously read positions and a least-recently-used slot). Backpropagation is made memory-efficient by logging and reverting sparse updates, yielding O(1) space per time step and avoiding the O(NT) cache of dense MANNs. The authors also present a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC). Experiments show 100–1600× speedups and orders-of-magnitude memory savings versus NTMs, with comparable or better learning on classic NTM tasks, extended curriculum scaling to sequences >4K (and generalization to 200K), competitive bAbI results without access supervision, and strong Omniglot one-shot performance.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling capacity from parameters but addresses the key bottleneck of dense soft attention and dense writes. It is related to discrete addressing learned via RL (Zaremba & Sutskever), but avoids high-variance gradients by keeping differentiable paths while sparsifying. The SDNC extension shows generality beyond NTM. Using ANN inside the computational graph for content lookup is a notable systems innovation.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: A clear, practical rethinking of differentiable memory access that makes MANNs viable at million-scale memory. The combination of sparse top-K addressing, ANN-backed content search, and reversible sparse-write BPTT is conceptually clean and broadly applicable.
- Technical contribution: 
  - Sparse read/write design with practical memory management (LRU-like scheme).
  - Efficient backward pass via in-place updates and rollback.
  - Complexity guarantees: O(log N) time per step and O(1) per-step space under content-based addressing; an optimality argument (in supp.).
- Empirical significance:
  - Massive speed/memory gains (up to three orders of magnitude) demonstrated credibly.
  - Comparable or better learning vs dense baselines on NTM tasks; improved early-stage learning in some tasks.
  - Strong curriculum scaling to long sequences and impressive length generalization.
  - Competitive bAbI without supervision; SDNC achieves near-SOTA.
  - Real-data validation on Omniglot with clear gains from larger memory capacity.
- Generality: The approach is shown to transfer to DNC, suggesting broad utility across MANNs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Differentiability and bias: Sparse top-K reads (with thresholding) and non-differentiable ANN indices imply a biased gradient relative to dense softmax attention. While empirically effective, the paper does not analyze the optimization implications or provide guarantees about convergence/stability. A brief discussion or ablation would strengthen the claim that “end-to-end gradient-based optimization” is retained in a principled sense.
- Asymptotic optimality: The main text asserts optimal time/space under “reasonable assumptions,” but the assumptions and formal statement are relegated to the supplement. A concise theorem-level summary in the main paper would improve credibility.
- Experimental scope: Beyond Omniglot and bAbI, no large-scale real-world task (e.g., long-document QA, program induction with real code, or large-scale sequential recommendation) is shown. This limits external validity, though I recognize training such systems is expensive.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Limited exploration of K (top-K size) trade-offs on accuracy, speed, and gradient sparsity.
  - Limited analysis of write policy variants (e.g., content-addressed sparse writes vs LRU, erase strategy).
  - ANN details (kd-tree vs LSH) are discussed, but a more systematic comparison of accuracy/speed and the impact of periodic index rebuilds on training dynamics would be helpful.
- Comparisons: While comparisons to NTM, DAM, and LSTM are fair and the Memory Networks literature is cited, more direct comparisons to end-to-end Memory Networks/Key-Value Memory Networks on bAbI with identical training regimes would be useful for positioning.
- Reproducibility: The engineering details (ANN maintenance, rebuild frequency, rollback data structure) are critical to the gains. Public code or more implementation pseudocode would help.

Quality
Overall technically sound and thoughtfully engineered. The systems choices are well-motivated, and the empirical evidence aligns with the claims. The lack of a deeper analysis of gradient bias is the main technical gap but is partially offset by strong empirical results.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Equations and control flow are understandable. I suggest:
- Summarize the optimality claim and assumptions in the main text.
- Provide a concise pseudocode for the rollback/backprop mechanism and the usage/LRU data structure.
- Clarify the effect of ANN rebuilds on amortized complexity and training.

Originality
High. Introducing sparse top-K differentiable memory access with ANN inside the training loop, coupled with a constant-space reversible BPTT for external memory, is a novel and impactful combination. The extension to SDNC underscores generality.

Significance
High. If adopted, this line of work removes a central barrier to scaling differentiable memory and could enable new applications. The impressive speed/space improvements, while maintaining learning performance, are compelling.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel idea that materially advances scalability of MANNs.
- Solid engineering with clear complexity advantages.
- Broad applicability (NTM, DNC) and promising results on both synthetic and real tasks.
- Opens a practical path to very long sequence modeling with external memory.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical treatment of bias introduced by sparse top-K/ANN.
- Limited ablations and large-scale real-world benchmarks.
- Some implementation details critical for replication are in the supplement.

Recommendation
Accept (Score: 8/10). Despite minor theoretical and experimental gaps, the conceptual advance and demonstrated practical impact are substantial and likely to catalyze further work.

Reviewer confidence
3.5/5 (moderately confident).
