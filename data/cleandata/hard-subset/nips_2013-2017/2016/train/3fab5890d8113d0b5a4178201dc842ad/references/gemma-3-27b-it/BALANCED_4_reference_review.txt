This paper introduces SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) designed to address the scalability issues of traditional MANNs like NTMs and Memory Networks. The core idea is to restrict read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, significantly reducing computational and memory overhead, particularly during backpropagation through time (BPTT). The authors demonstrate substantial improvements in runtime and memory usage compared to dense MANNs, while maintaining comparable or even improved performance on a variety of tasks, including algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification. They also extend the approach to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a crucial bottleneck in MANN research – scalability. The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the proposed solution is both intuitive and technically sound. The authors successfully identify and address the key limitations of existing MANNs, specifically the linear cost associated with dense read/write operations and the large memory footprint required for BPTT. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of SAM across diverse tasks and sequence lengths. The inclusion of a sparse version of the DNC further highlights the general applicability of the proposed technique. The detailed ablation studies and benchmarks comparing SAM with NTM and DAM are particularly valuable. The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear presentation of the architecture and experimental setup.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The core contribution – achieving substantial speedups and memory reductions – is a significant advance in the field of MANNs.  The reported 3 orders of magnitude improvement in runtime for large memories is impressive.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation across a range of tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of SAM. The results consistently show that SAM can achieve comparable or better performance than dense MANNs with significantly lower computational costs.
* **Sound Technical Approach:** The use of sparse reads and writes, combined with efficient data structures (ANNs and custom data structures for memory management), is a well-justified and effective strategy for addressing the scalability problem.
* **General Applicability:**  The extension to the SDNC demonstrates the generality of the approach and its potential to improve other memory-augmented architectures.
* **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **ANN Implementation Details:** While the authors mention using approximate nearest neighbor indexes, the specific choice and tuning of these indexes (FLANN vs. LSH) could be further elaborated.  A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between different ANN algorithms would be beneficial. The impact of ANN accuracy on overall performance is not fully explored.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameters are tuned, but a more detailed discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly related to the sparsity level (K) and the usage measure parameters, would be useful.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors state SAM is asymptotically optimal in space and time, a more rigorous theoretical analysis comparing SAM to other approaches (e.g., techniques using discrete addressing policies) could strengthen the contribution.
* **Curriculum Learning Dependency:** The benefits of SAM are particularly pronounced when combined with curriculum learning. It's unclear how well SAM would perform on complex tasks *without* a carefully designed curriculum.  A more direct comparison with other models without curriculum learning would be insightful.
* **Lack of Comparison to Recent Memory Compression Techniques:** There has been recent work on compressing the weights and activations of neural networks. A discussion of how SAM compares to these techniques in terms of memory savings and computational efficiency would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in MANN research – scalability.
* The proposed approach is technically sound, well-motivated, and effectively addresses the identified problem.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
* The work has the potential to enable the application of MANNs to more complex and real-world problems.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The reliance on curriculum learning may limit the generalizability of the results.
* The lack of a more detailed analysis of the ANN implementation and hyperparameter sensitivity.
* A more thorough theoretical analysis would strengthen the contribution.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM architecture offers a compelling solution to the scalability problem, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. 

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should provide more details on the ANN implementation, discuss hyperparameter sensitivity, and consider adding a discussion of recent memory compression techniques.  A brief exploration of SAM’s performance without curriculum learning would also be beneficial.
