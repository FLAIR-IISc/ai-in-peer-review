Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales external memory by enforcing sparse reads and writes and by integrating efficient data structures for content-based addressing. The key ideas are: (i) top-K content reads implemented via an approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) index so that only K memory slots are scored and receive gradients; (ii) sparse writes limited to previously read locations plus an LRU-style slot for new content, with a simple usage-based memory management; and (iii) a backward pass that rolls memory back in O(1) per step by storing only sparse deltas, avoiding O(NT) memory footprints of dense MANNs. The authors claim SAM achieves O(log N) time per step and O(1) space per step (w.r.t. memory size) while remaining trainable end-to-end.

The work builds on NTMs/DNCs (Graves et al.) by retaining differentiability but replacing dense attention and writes with sparse, index-backed operations. Compared to Memory Networks, SAM learns to write online and supports long-horizon BPTT. Relative to discrete addressing approaches (e.g., RL-NTM), SAM avoids high-variance gradient estimators. The paper also outlines a Sparse DNC (SDNC) variant. Similar in spirit to later “nearest-neighbor” memories, SAM is an early, principled attempt to marry ANN indices with differentiable memory.

Quality
Technically, the forward/backward engineering is sound and well-motivated. The sparse rollback scheme for BPTT and the careful choice of usage statistics for LRU are practical and important. Using ANN to restrict scoring to K candidates reduces the O(N) bottleneck of content addressing; the cost to maintain the ANN in sync and rebuild periodically is addressed (amortized O(log N)). Empirically, SAM delivers large speed/space gains and maintains or improves data efficiency on canonical NTM tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), scales to millions of slots with curriculum, performs competitively on bAbI without supervision, and improves one-shot Omniglot performance.

However, several claims would benefit from deeper analysis or ablation:
- Differentiability and bias: Top-K selection plus approximate KNN yields a biased, discontinuous attention operator. Gradients are computed only through the selected subset, ignoring the effect of selection changes. The paper should quantify the approximation error r̃t − rt and analyze conditions under which the gradient bias does not impair convergence. An ablation over K and over exact vs approximate KNN would clarify robustness.
- “Optimality” claims: The asymptotic optimality statements (time and space) hinge on assumptions relegated to the supplement. Please specify assumptions (e.g., content-based addressing requiring either scanning or an index with certain guarantees) and clarify amortized complexity including rebuild costs.
- ANN maintenance: Online k-d trees can become imbalanced; LSH has curse-of-dimensionality issues. Report empirical scaling vs memory word dimension M and compare k-d vs LSH across M and N. How sensitive are results to ANN recall/precision settings?
- Write policy: The mixture of “update recently read” and “overwrite least-recently-used” is intuitive but heuristic. Provide analysis or ablations on the write gate/interpolation parameters (αt, τt), the epsilon threshold for “non-negligible access,” and how often catastrophic overwrites harm performance. Alternatives (e.g., learned allocation as in DNC) could be compared.
- Fairness of comparisons: In the curriculum experiments, dense baselines use 64 slots while SAM uses 2e6, matched on physical memory footprint. This is acceptable to highlight scaling, but the text should more explicitly separate “algorithmic data efficiency” from “capacity-enabled performance,” and provide at least one control where all models have the same number of slots (where feasible) to isolate the effect of sparsity per se.
- Reproducibility: Core implementation details are in the supplement (data structures, rollback, ANN settings). A concise algorithmic description in the main text (or pseudocode) would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with concrete complexity claims and illustrative figures. Notation is mostly consistent, but Eq. (5) and the role of τt and αt could be explained more intuitively. Please ensure the backward rollback procedure and what is cached per timestep (indices, weights, deltas) is described in the main text. Summarize the bAbI and Omniglot setups more fully (number of seeds, variance, memory sizes).

Originality
Moving from dense soft attention to sparse top-K with an ANN inside a differentiable MANN, combined with a sparse rollback BPTT, is a novel and pragmatic contribution for 2016. While sparse attention ideas existed (e.g., sparsemax) they still require O(N) scoring; here the key novelty is computational scaling via indexing and write sparsity with trainability retained.

Significance
Scaling MANNs to millions of memory slots with orders-of-magnitude savings is important and likely to influence future work. The demonstrated ability to train with long BPTT horizons and large memories broadens applicability. The SDNC result on bAbI suggests that the approach generalizes to other architectures.

Specific suggestions
- Provide an ablation on K, exact vs ANN, and ANN recall; plot accuracy vs speed/memory.
- Quantify the gradient bias from top-K truncation (e.g., bound on error under assumptions on the tail of attention weights).
- Report amortized costs including periodic rebuilds; discuss background rebuild or batched maintenance.
- Analyze performance vs memory word dimension M and discuss when k-d vs LSH is preferable.
- Include variance across seeds for key tasks and more details for bAbI (10k setting, hops, supervision).
- Consider a comparison to a discrete addressing baseline (e.g., RL-NTM) to contextualize the trade-off.

Pros
- Substantial, well-engineered scalability gains with clean algorithmic ideas.
- Maintains end-to-end training; strong empirical performance on synthetic and semi-real tasks.
- Generalizable to other MANNs (SDNC) with strong bAbI results.
- Clear articulation of time/space complexity and practical implementation details.

Cons
- Limited theoretical treatment of approximation-induced bias and stability.
- Heuristic write allocation without thorough ablation.
- Some comparisons favor SAM via vastly larger capacity; need clearer fairness discussion.
- ANN maintenance and dimensionality effects underexplored.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper addresses an important bottleneck with a principled, effective approach, demonstrates clear benefits, and is likely to be impactful. Strengthening the empirical ablations and clarifying the approximation analysis would further solidify the contribution.
