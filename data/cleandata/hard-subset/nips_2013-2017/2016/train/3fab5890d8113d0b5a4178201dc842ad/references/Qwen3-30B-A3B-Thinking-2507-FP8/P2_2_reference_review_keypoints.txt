- Introduces SAM: a sparse memory architecture that limits reads/writes to K ≈ 4–8 words using approximate nearest neighbors.
- Achieves O(log N) time per read/write and O(1) space per time step, enabling scaling to very large memories.
- Maintains comparable performance to dense models on algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot classification.
- Presents SDNC (Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer) variant that attains state-of-the-art results on bAbI without supervised memory access.
- Provides strong theoretical foundation with asymptotic complexity analysis demonstrating optimality.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing to DAM, NTM, and LSTM with thorough ablation studies.
- Demonstrates scalability to extremely long sequences (e.g., 200,000 steps) and large memory sizes (e.g., 64K+ words).
- Implementation and supplementary materials are detailed enough to support reproducibility.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical formulations and effective figures.
- Novel integration of approximate nearest neighbor data structures into end-to-end gradient-based training for sparse access.
- Not merely a sparse approximation of prior models; designed to maintain differentiability while achieving asymptotic gains.
- Shows substantial practical impact: large speed/memory improvements (e.g., 1600× faster than NTM w/ k-d trees; 53MB vs 29GB for 64K words).
- Enables new applications requiring very large memory capacity (e.g., processing entire books or Wikipedia).
- Limitation: theoretical limits of sparsity not fully explored; unclear how required K relates to task complexity.
- Limitation: focus on content-based addressing leaves generalization to other addressing schemes (e.g., location-based) uncertain.
- Weakness: lacks deep analysis of why SAM outperforms dense variants and the learning dynamics of sparse access.
- Overall conclusion: technically sound, original, and significant work with a clear recommendation to accept.
