- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM): reads/writes only K memory slots per step using ANN (kd-tree/LSH) and an LRU-like allocator, decoupling memory size N from parameter count.
- Uses ANN indices to find top-K content matches in O(log N) time and proposes rollback BPTT storing sparse deltas to achieve O(1) space per step.
- Reports large empirical gains: 100–1600× speedups and ~1000× memory reduction versus NTMs, scaling to millions of slots and very long sequences.
- Demonstrates competitive learning: matches/exceeds NTMs/LSTMs on synthetic tasks, generalizes to >200k steps, and performs well on bAbI and Omniglot; sparse DNC achieves near-SOTA.
- Technical design is coherent and engineering contributions (sparse updates, rollback) are convincing, but asymptotic claims rely on ANN behavior assumptions not fully detailed in main text.
- Highlights risks of ANN methods: kd-tree degradation in high dimensions and LSH’s large constants/tuning needs; calls for empirical scaling vs word dimension M.
- Missing or insufficient ablations: sensitivity to K, effect of ANN approximation vs exact top-K, allocator variants, index rebuild frequency, and ANN hyperparameter impacts.
- Non-differentiable ANN neighbor selection can introduce discontinuities; the paper lacks a thorough analysis of training stability under changing neighbor sets.
- Reproducibility is hindered by important implementation details (rollback structures, ANN params, rebuild policies) being confined to the supplement.
- Clarity is generally good, but recommends moving complexity arguments and rollback/write policies into the main text and adding concise pseudocode for forward/backward and index updates.
- Originality: novel integration of sparse content-based read/write, ANN indices in the training loop, and O(1) rollback BPTT for MANNs.
- Significance: substantially advances scalability of MANNs with practical impact for long-context learning and algorithmic tasks; likely to be adopted and extended.
- Pros: principled solution to time/space bottleneck, strong engineering, large empirical gains, generalizes to other MANNs (sparse DNC).
- Cons: theoretical guarantees limited in high dimensions, non-differentiability concerns, missing ablations, and some unfair or unclear comparisons.
- Requests for authors: provide empirical scaling vs M, ablations on K and ANN error, compare allocator policies, quantify index rebuild amortized cost, clarify fairness in baselines, and report training stability measures.
- Overall recommendation: strong, novel, and influential work with convincing efficiency gains despite some theoretical caveats and missing experiments — recommend acceptance.
