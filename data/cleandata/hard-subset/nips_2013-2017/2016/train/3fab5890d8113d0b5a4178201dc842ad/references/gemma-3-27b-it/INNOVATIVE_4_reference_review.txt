This paper tackles a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) – the computational and memory overhead associated with scaling memory size. The authors propose SAM, a MANN with sparse read/write operations and an efficient memory management scheme, demonstrably reducing both time and space complexity. This is a compelling and highly relevant problem, and the proposed solution is both clever and well-motivated. The empirical results, showcasing significant speedups and comparable performance to dense MANNs, are impressive, especially the scaling to large memory sizes and the successful application to Babi tasks and Omniglot.

The core idea of sparsity – limiting modifications to a subset of memory – is intuitively sound and effectively implemented. The combination with approximate nearest neighbor search for efficient content-based addressing is a particularly strong point. The authors don't shy away from implementation details, providing a clear picture of how SAM operates and how the performance gains are achieved. The demonstration of a sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) further highlights the generality of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of applying aggressive sparsity to MANN operations is genuinely novel and directly addresses a major practical limitation.
*   **Significance:** Scaling MANNs is crucial for tackling complex tasks requiring long-term memory. This work makes a substantial step towards enabling that scalability.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on speed/memory benchmarks, NTM tasks, Babi, and Omniglot are compelling and validate the effectiveness of the approach. The scaling experiments are particularly encouraging.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the SAM architecture and its advantages. The supplementary material provides helpful details.
*   **Generality:** The extension to the SDNC demonstrates that the sparsity technique is broadly applicable to different MANN architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors claim asymptotic optimality, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the trade-offs between sparsity level and performance would be beneficial.
*   **ANN Sensitivity:** The performance gains are contingent on the effectiveness of the approximate nearest neighbor search. A more thorough exploration of different ANN algorithms and their impact on performance would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** While hyperparameters are reported, the sensitivity of the results to these choices isn't fully explored. A more systematic hyperparameter study would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Sparse Write Strategy:** The choice of writing to previously read locations or least recently accessed locations is somewhat arbitrary. A comparison of different write schemes would provide further insight.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to scaling MANNs, addressing a significant bottleneck in the field.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the development of more powerful and scalable memory-augmented neural networks.
*   The clarity of the presentation and the inclusion of supplementary material make the paper accessible and reproducible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is limited.
*   The reliance on approximate nearest neighbor search introduces a potential source of variability.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity isn't fully explored.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a significant new idea with compelling results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks and should be accepted to NIPS. The authors have successfully demonstrated a practical and effective approach to scaling MANNs, opening up new possibilities for tackling complex tasks that require long-term memory. I would consider this a borderline strong accept, leaning towards acceptance due to the originality and potential impact.
