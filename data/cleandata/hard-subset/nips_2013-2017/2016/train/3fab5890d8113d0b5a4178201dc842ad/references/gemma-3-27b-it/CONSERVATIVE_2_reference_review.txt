This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), specifically targeting the memory and computational bottlenecks arising from their smooth read/write operations. The authors propose SAM (Sparse Access Memory), which constrains memory modifications to a sparse subset and employs efficient data structures, claiming substantial improvements in runtime and memory overhead while maintaining performance. The work is clearly motivated and relevant, building upon established MANN architectures like NTMs and Memory Networks.

However, despite the promising premise and reported results, I find myself unconvinced of the substantial advance claimed here. My primary concern revolves around the incremental nature of the contribution and a lack of rigorous comparison against thoroughly optimized, established baselines. While the authors demonstrate speedups over a vanilla NTM implementation, the comparison feels somewhat unfair. Modern implementations of NTMs and related architectures often employ various optimizations (e.g., careful memory allocation, optimized BLAS routines) which aren’t explicitly acknowledged or accounted for in this evaluation. The claim of a "three orders of magnitude" improvement should be benchmarked against such optimized implementations, not a naive baseline. 

The core idea of sparsity is not novel in itself. Sparse representations are widely used in deep learning to address computational costs, and applying this to MANNs is a natural extension. What’s missing is a compelling demonstration that this sparsity *unlocks* capabilities that were previously impossible. The presented experiments – algorithmic tasks from NTM, Babi tasks, and Omniglot – are standard benchmarks, but the improvements achieved are often marginal. The authors state SAM learns "more effectively" on some tasks (e.g., priority sort), but the error bars and statistical significance of these differences are not sufficiently detailed to draw strong conclusions. 

The scaling experiments, while interesting, also lack sufficient detail. The choice of an exponentially increasing curriculum is sensible, but the specific parameters controlling the curriculum (e.g., rate of increase, thresholds for level advancement) are not clearly justified. The claim that SAM "advanced further" than other models is vague; a quantitative comparison of learning curves is necessary. 

Furthermore, the discussion of approximate nearest neighbor search feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors mention FLANN and LSH, the specific parameters used and the trade-offs between accuracy and speed are not thoroughly investigated. The potential for ANN errors to impact performance is acknowledged, but not rigorously addressed.

The supplementary material, detailing the Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), adds some value, but again, the reported speedup (400x) needs to be contextualized. Is this speedup achievable with optimized implementations of the original DNC? The claim of achieving the best reported result on Babi without supervising memory access is noteworthy, but the comparison to previous work needs to be more precise and comprehensive.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in MANN research: scalability.
*   Presents a plausible and well-motivated approach (sparsity).
*   Demonstrates improvements in runtime and memory usage.
*   Shows potential for scaling to larger memories and longer sequences.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution; sparsity is not a fundamentally new idea.
*   Lack of comparison to thoroughly optimized, state-of-the-art baselines.
*   Marginal performance improvements on standard benchmarks.
*   Insufficient detail in experimental setup and analysis.
*   Vague claims and a lack of statistical rigor.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and clearly presented, it lacks the originality and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. The claimed improvements, while potentially useful, are not substantial enough to justify the development of a new architecture, particularly without a more rigorous demonstration of its advantages over existing, optimized approaches. The paper would benefit from more thorough benchmarking, detailed experimental analysis, and a stronger emphasis on the novel aspects of the contribution.
