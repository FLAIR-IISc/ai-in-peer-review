## NIPS 2016 Review: Scaling Memory-Augmented Neural Networks with Sparse Reads and Writes

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in Memory-Augmented Neural Networks (MANNs): the computational and memory cost associated with dense read and write operations, particularly as memory size scales. The authors introduce Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a MANN architecture that restricts read and write access to a sparse subset of memory locations.  This is coupled with efficient data structures (approximate nearest neighbors, custom usage tracking) to maintain performance.  The authors demonstrate significant speedups and memory reductions (up to three orders of magnitude) compared to a standard Neural Turing Machine (NTM), while maintaining comparable performance on several synthetic and natural tasks, including scaling to larger sequence lengths via curriculum learning. They also showcase the applicability of their approach by presenting a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC).

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing field of MANNs, referencing key works like NTMs, Memory Networks, and the Differentiable Neural Computer.  It acknowledges the limitations of these models regarding scalability and highlights the importance of sparse operations. The discussion of approximate nearest neighbor search and its relevance to efficient memory access is also well-placed, referencing relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Practical Improvement:** The reported speedups and memory reductions are substantial, making MANNs with large memories far more feasible. This is a crucial contribution, as the scalability issue has been a major barrier to the real-world application of these models.
* **Sound Technical Approach:** The use of sparsity, combined with efficient data structures like approximate nearest neighbors and custom usage tracking, is a logical and well-motivated solution to the scalability problem.  The authors provide a clear rationale for their design choices.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate SAM on a diverse set of tasks – algorithmic tasks, babi reasoning tasks, and one-shot learning on Omniglot – providing a good indication of its generalizability.  The scaling experiments using curriculum learning are particularly compelling, showcasing the benefits of increased memory capacity.
* **Generality Demonstrated:**  The application of the sparsity approach to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and the substantial performance gains observed further demonstrate the broader applicability of the proposed technique.
* **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient detail to understand the proposed architecture and implementation. The supplementary material further enhances clarity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the results are strong, the paper doesn't extensively explore the sensitivity of SAM to various hyperparameters (e.g., the sparsity level *K*, the discount factor for usage tracking). A deeper analysis of these parameters could provide valuable insights for practical application.
* **ANN Choice Justification:** The choice between using FLANN’s randomized k-d tree versus LSH for approximate nearest neighbor search is briefly mentioned, but a more detailed justification for this choice, perhaps based on the characteristics of the different tasks, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Other Sparsity Techniques:** The paper focuses on sparsity induced by limiting read/write locations. Briefly discussing, and contrasting against, other sparsity inducing techniques within neural networks (e.g., weight pruning) could further contextualize the contribution.
* **Theoretical Optimality Claims:**  The statement that SAM is "asymptotically optimal" should be more thoroughly justified. While the supplementary material provides some analysis, a more rigorous theoretical grounding for this claim would be stronger.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed SAM architecture offers a compelling solution to the scalability problem, achieving substantial improvements in both speed and memory usage while maintaining competitive performance. The comprehensive evaluation and demonstration of generality (SDNC) further strengthen the contribution.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Solves a significant practical problem hindering the broader adoption of MANNs.
* Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Strong empirical results across diverse tasks.
* Demonstrates generality and applicability to other architectures.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly explored.
* Justification for ANN choice could be stronger.
* Theoretical optimality claims require more rigorous justification.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses.  It represents a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to be highly influential. I believe it’s a worthy addition to the NIPS program.
