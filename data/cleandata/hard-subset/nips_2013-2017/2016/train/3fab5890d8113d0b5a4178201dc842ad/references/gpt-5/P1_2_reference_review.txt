Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that makes both reads and writes to external memory sparse, enabling asymptotically efficient training and inference. Key ideas: (i) sparse read via top-K content-based addressing implemented with approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices (randomized k-d trees or LSH) yielding O(log N) time per query; (ii) sparse write limited to previously read locations and a least-recently-used (LRU) slot, with usage tracking; (iii) an efficient BPTT scheme that stores and reverts only sparse memory modifications (O(1) per step memory) rather than full memory snapshots (O(N) per step). They argue these yield optimal time/space complexity for content-addressable differentiable memories under reasonable assumptions. Empirically, SAM is up to three orders of magnitude faster and more memory-efficient than dense NTMs, while retaining similar learning performance on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning. They also show how to sparsify the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), achieving strong bAbI results without supervision of memory access.

This work builds on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling capacity from parameters and by making addressing scalable. It is related to RL-based discrete addressing (Reinforce-NTM) but maintains end-to-end differentiability with hard top-K selection and no gradient through the ANN. The use of ANN indices inside a neural network and the rollback BPTT mechanism are notable engineering contributions.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful problem: scaling differentiable memory to large N and long T.
- Strong algorithmic contribution: sparse read/write design, ANN integration, and a practical LRU allocator; efficient backprop via sparse deltas and rollback.
- Solid complexity story with empirical validation: sublinear time with ANN; O(1) per-step memory; compelling benchmarks (100–1600× speedups; 29 GiB vs 7.8 MiB).
- Demonstrated that sparsity does not harm learning; in some tasks it helps (priority sort, associative recall).
- Scalability: curriculum reaching >4k steps without truncation and generalization to 200k sequence length; million-word memories.
- Breadth: synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI (including SDNC), and Omniglot episodes. SDNC achieves near state-of-the-art on bAbI without supervision.
- Generally clear exposition; related work is discussed; supplementary materials appear to contain needed details.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Differentiability and bias: top-K selection and LRU introduce non-differentiable control flow. Gradients flow only to the selected memory rows; the selection mechanism itself is not learned. While effective empirically, some discussion/analysis of optimization bias and stability would strengthen the work.
- Dependence on ANN quality: training relies on approximate neighbors. There is limited ablation on the trade-off between approximation error, K, index type (k-d tree vs LSH), and task performance. No quantitative recall metrics of the ANN under training dynamics.
- Fairness of some comparisons: in curriculum experiments, dense baselines are constrained to 64 slots while SAM uses 2e6 slots (chosen to equalize physical memory). This demonstrates scalability rather than algorithmic superiority; a caveat should be emphasized more prominently.
- Limited real-world tasks: beyond Omniglot episodes and bAbI, there is no evaluation on large, naturally long-context problems (e.g., long-document QA, code completion, or long-horizon RL), where the approach could shine.
- Optimality claim: “asymptotically optimal” time/space for content-based addressing is relegated to the supplement; assumptions should be stated in the main text and contrasted with known lower bounds (e.g., LSH lower bounds, kd-tree behavior in high dimensions).
- Write policy simplicity: always write to previously read and LRU. This can cause destructive overwrites if read attention is noisy; analysis of failure modes, and comparisons to alternative sparse writing schemes (e.g., content-based sparse write) would be useful.
- Reproducibility: many details are in the supplement; code availability is not mentioned. Some hyperparameter and implementation details (ANN rebuild frequency, amortized costs) could be clarified.

Quality
Technically sound overall and carefully engineered. Claims about speed and memory are convincingly supported by measurements. Learning claims are supported across several tasks, including a strong SDNC bAbI result without supervision. Missing are ablations on K, ANN accuracy vs performance, and sensitivity analyses of the write policy. Theoretical “optimality” argument is not visible in the main text.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The core ideas are easy to follow; figures support the claims. However, several critical details are deferred to the supplement (data structures for usage tracking, proof sketch of optimality, full training settings). A concise statement of the assumptions behind complexity optimality should be included in the main paper.

Originality
The combination of sparse read/write with ANN indices inside a differentiable memory, plus the rollback BPTT mechanism, is novel and pragmatic. Related work is appropriately cited. The SDNC extension demonstrates generality.

Significance
High. Scaling MANNs is important, and the proposed approach makes previously impractical regimes feasible. The speedups and memory savings are substantial. The work should be useful to researchers and practitioners building long-context models.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to K? Is there a principled way to set K or adapt it during training?
- Can you report ANN recall/precision during training and correlate it with task performance? How often do approximate neighbors differ from exact ones?
- What are the amortized costs of index maintenance and periodic rebuilds? How does this impact wall-clock time at very large N and high-dimensional words?
- Did you try sparse content-based writes, or hybrid policies, and with what outcome?
- How robust is the LRU policy to noisy reads? Any mechanisms to prevent harmful overwrites?
- How does performance change with multiple read/write heads?
- Can you summarize the assumptions behind the optimality claim in the main text?

Minor comments
- Clarify whether erase uses a learned vector or full zeroing for LRU slots; Eq. (5) and text could be more explicit.
- Please include a small ablation comparing SAM with exact KNN vs ANN on at least one task.
- Typographical/formatting issues are minimal; a few references are incomplete.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and important scalability problem with a well-motivated, effective approach.
- Strong empirical speed/memory gains without sacrificing learning performance.
- General method applicable to multiple MANN architectures; SDNC results are excellent.
- Clear writing and solid engineering contributions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited ablations on key design choices (K, ANN accuracy, write policy).
- Real-world evaluation breadth is modest.
- Non-differentiable selection policies not analyzed theoretically; optimality claim is offloaded to supplement.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a substantial step toward scalable differentiable memory with convincing empirical evidence and broadly useful ideas. With a bit more analysis and ablation, it could be a landmark contribution.
