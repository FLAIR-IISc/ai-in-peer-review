Summary
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales content-addressable memory to millions of slots by enforcing sparse reads and writes and by using efficient data structures inside the model. The key ideas are: (1) sparse read: approximate top-K content-based addressing via an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to achieve O(log N) lookup; (2) sparse write: limit updates to previously read locations and the least-recently-used slot, yielding O(1) updates; (3) efficient BPTT: store only sparse deltas and roll memory backward during backprop, resulting in O(1) space per time step rather than O(N) per step; (4) a lightweight memory management scheme based on a usage statistic. Under reasonable assumptions (supplement), SAM is asymptotically optimal in time and space among content-addressed MANNs. The approach is shown to transfer to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), yielding large speedups.

Empirically, SAM achieves up to three orders of magnitude speed/memory improvements over NTMs, learns classic algorithmic NTM tasks with comparable or better data efficiency than dense baselines, scales curricula to thousands of time steps and millions of memory slots without truncated BPTT, achieves near-SOTA on bAbI (best among unsupervised access; SDNC nearly solves all tasks), and performs well on Omniglot one-shot episodes with longer sequences.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on NTMs, Memory Networks, and DNC by keeping content-based differentiability while introducing sparsity for scalability, in contrast to reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever) that suffers high variance. The use of ANN inside the computational graph (without backprop through the index) and sparse backward “rollback” is, to my knowledge, novel in MANNs.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: A clean, principled redesign of MANN access patterns to achieve asymptotically optimal space/time while retaining end-to-end differentiability. The sparse-backprop rollback is particularly elegant.
- Scalability: Demonstrated O(1) per-step memory and sublinear query time in practice; training with millions of memory slots and BPTT over >4000 steps is compelling.
- Generality: Method transfers to DNC with dramatic speedups, indicating the approach is not tied to a specific controller or memory layout.
- Empirical evidence: Large speed/memory gains; comparable or better learning efficiency on NTM tasks; strong bAbI results without supervision; meaningful Omniglot one-shot performance that benefits from larger memory.
- Practical engineering: Clear discussion of ANN choices, index maintenance, and memory management; the method is implementable and useful.

Weaknesses
- Differentiability/approximation analysis: The top-K sparsification and non-differentiable ANN selection introduce biased gradients whenever the support changes. The paper argues empirically that this is fine, but lacks theoretical or diagnostic analysis (e.g., stability under small perturbations, frequency of support flips, or bounds on the read approximation error versus K).
- Ablations/sensitivity: Limited exploration of K, threshold ε for usage, choice of ANN (k-d tree vs LSH), and write policy (read locations vs LRU). Understanding trade-offs would strengthen claims of generality.
- Comparisons: Some comparisons are constrained by memory usage (dense baselines at 64 slots vs SAM at 2e6). While this illustrates the point, additional baselines that use sparse/discrete addressing (e.g., RL-based NTMs) or alternative scalable content retrieval would be informative.
- Real-world tasks: bAbI and Omniglot are valuable but relatively small-scale or synthetic. Demonstrations on larger QA or long-document tasks (even as qualitative studies) would bolster significance.
- Clarity relegated to supplement: Complexity proofs, data structures, and many training details are in the supplementary; a brief in-paper distillation of assumptions behind “optimality” would help.

Quality
The technical contributions are sound and carefully engineered. Claims about speed/space are supported with measurements and align with the algorithmic design. Empirical results are thorough for the targeted tasks, though ablations are limited. The choice to avoid gradients through ANN is reasonable and well-motivated, but the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential failure modes.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. The core operations (sparse read/write, memory management, rollback BPTT) are described succinctly. Some notation in the write equation is slightly terse; adding explicit elementwise operators and a short worked example of rollback would aid readability. Consolidating key assumptions for the complexity claims into the main text would help non-experts.

Originality
High. Integrating ANN-backed sparse content reads inside a differentiable MANN, coupled with a constant-space rollback BPTT scheme and a simple yet effective sparse write policy, is a novel combination that addresses a central bottleneck in MANNs. The extension to DNC further underscores originality and breadth.

Significance
High. The work addresses a fundamental obstacle—scaling differentiable memory—and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude improvements that enable regimes previously inaccessible to dense MANNs. This is likely to influence future MANN designs and applications requiring long sequences and large memories.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to K? Is there a regime where increasing K stops helping or harms stability?
- Do you observe instability from support changes in top-K reads? Any smoothing (e.g., tie-breaking or temperature) used to mitigate this?
- How do different ANN choices impact training speed/accuracy beyond the small/large word-size heuristic?
- Can the write policy be extended to handle multi-read heads and avoid potential interference among tasks?
- Any plans to release code to aid reproducibility?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel approach that materially advances scalability of differentiable memory.
- Strong empirical speed/space gains with competitive accuracy.
- General method applicable to other MANNs (DNC).
- Clear potential impact on research and practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited ablation and theoretical analysis of approximation-induced biases.
- Comparisons and real-world experiments could be broader.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel direction for scaling MANNs with convincing empirical evidence and practical techniques. Minor limitations in analysis and breadth of tasks do not outweigh the significance and originality.

Confidence
High.
