- Introduces SAM, a memory-augmented neural network that uses sparse reads/writes to scale memory access efficiently.  
- Constraining access to a sparse subset of memory drastically reduces computation and memory footprint.  
- Empirically achieves comparable or better performance than dense MANNs on algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot, especially at large memory sizes and long sequences.  
- Applies sparsity to the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and reports substantial runtime speedups.  
- Builds on NTMs and Memory Networks while retaining end-to-end gradient-based training (unlike RL-based addressing methods).  
- Leverages approximate nearest neighbor search (e.g., FLANN/LSH) for sparse addressing, aligning with IR practices.  
- Major strength: large scalability improvements (up to three orders of magnitude in runtime) demonstrated in benchmarks.  
- Preserves or improves task performance despite sparse access, especially when scaling up.  
- Thorough evaluation across diverse tasks and use of curriculum learning to scale benchmarks.  
- General approach appears broadly applicable and implementation details/backpropagation are well-explained.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., sparsity K, usage discount).  
- Weakness: ANN index choice and its impact lack rigorous comparison across methods.  
- Weakness: limited theoretical discussion of trade-offs between sparsity, capacity, and representational power.  
- Weakness: brief comparison with other scalability approaches (e.g., RL-based methods).  
- Recommendation: strong accept—compelling, well-evaluated contribution despite minor analytical/theoretical gaps; rating 8/10.
