This paper introduces SAM, a sparse memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), aimed at addressing the computational and memory overheads associated with dense MANNs like NTMs. The core idea is to restrict read and write operations to a small, dynamically managed subset of memory locations, coupled with efficient data structures for approximate nearest neighbor search. The authors demonstrate significant speedups and memory savings compared to dense NTMs, while maintaining comparable or even improved performance on several tasks, including synthetic algorithmic problems, Babi reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification. They also present a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) with impressive results.

Despite the potentially significant contributions, numerous issues detract from the overall quality of the paper. While the core idea of sparsity is sound and the reported speedups are impressive, the presentation lacks rigor and suffers from numerous inconsistencies and omissions. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical bottleneck:** The paper tackles a well-recognized limitation of MANNs—scalability—and proposes a reasonable approach to mitigate it.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The performance improvements on several tasks are encouraging and suggest the viability of the sparse access approach. The scaling experiments, particularly on the Babi tasks, are noteworthy.
*   **Generality:** The extension to the SDNC demonstrates the broader applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity in ANN Implementation:** The description of the approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) implementation is vague. The authors mention using both randomized k-d trees and LSH, but fail to provide sufficient detail on how these are integrated into the training process and how the choice between them is made. The statement that the ANN is a “structured view” of the memory but doesn’t affect gradients is concerning. If it’s not affecting gradients, what is the point of using it within the neural network’s optimization? This requires more detailed explanation.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** The use of tildes (~) to denote sparse vectors is inconsistently applied, leading to confusion. The distinction between `w` and `w̃` isn’t always clear, and its purpose isn’t well motivated.
*   **Missing Experimental Details:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. For example, the hyperparameter search procedure isn’t described, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the results. The memory size of 64 for dense models versus 2 million for sparse models in the curriculum learning experiments feels arbitrarily chosen. Justification is needed.
*   **Weak Theoretical Justification:** The claim of asymptotic optimality (Supplementary A) is asserted without sufficient elaboration. While the authors mention space and time complexity, the specific assumptions underlying this claim aren't clearly stated, and a more rigorous analysis is needed.
*   **Questionable Controller Architecture:** Using a single-layer LSTM as the controller seems overly simplistic, especially given the complexity of the memory access mechanism. It’s unclear if the results are sensitive to the choice of controller.
*   **Omission of Related Work:** The discussion of related work, while present, is incomplete. Specifically, the paper doesn't adequately address existing techniques for sparsifying neural networks, such as weight pruning or quantization, which could be relevant to this problem.
*   **Implementation Details in Supplementary Material:** Critical implementation details are relegated to the supplementary material (e.g., the custom data structure for tracking memory usage), making it harder to fully understand and evaluate the approach. The supplementary material itself is not well-organized and lacks sufficient clarity.
*   **Ambiguous Usage Definition:** The definition of `U` for SAM is confusing, particularly the use of a small threshold `ε`. The motivation for this threshold isn’t clearly explained, and its impact on performance needs to be investigated.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The paper addresses a significant practical limitation of MANNs and presents a promising approach to overcome it. The empirical results are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of sparse memory access.

**Con:** The paper suffers from numerous issues related to clarity, rigor, and completeness. The lack of detailed experimental setup, theoretical justification, and thorough comparison with related work raises serious concerns about the reliability and generalizability of the results. The inconsistencies in notation and presentation further detract from the paper’s overall quality. 

**Overall Assessment:**

While the ideas presented in this paper are potentially valuable, the numerous flaws significantly diminish its overall quality. The lack of clarity, rigor, and completeness makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. I recommend **rejection**. The authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above, provide more detailed experimental setup, and offer a more thorough theoretical analysis before this paper is suitable for publication in NIPS. A major rewrite with a focus on clarity and precision is required.
