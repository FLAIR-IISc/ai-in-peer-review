Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that constrains both reads and writes to a small, constant number K of memory slots per time step and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices (randomized k-d trees or LSH) for content-based addressing. It introduces a memory management scheme based on least-recently-used (LRU) selection and an efficient backprop-through-time (BPTT) strategy that stores only sparse diffs and rolls back memory in reverse. The authors claim O(log N) time per step w.r.t. memory size N and O(1) space per time step, with empirical speed/memory gains of up to three orders of magnitude over NTMs. Experiments on synthetic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling to long sequences, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot classification show comparable or improved performance vs dense baselines and, in supplement, a sparse DNC (SDNC) variant with strong bAbI results.

Relation to prior work
SAM is positioned relative to NTMs, Memory Networks, DNC, and RL-NTM. The proposed approach resembles top-K/content-sparse attention with LRU writing, but applied to MANNs with explicit ANN indices. The idea of using sparse access for scalability is natural and has appeared in discrete addressing with RL and in efficient attention variants; the contribution here is a practical differentiable instantiation with approximate nearest neighbors and an efficient BPTT scheme.

Strengths
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck for MANNs with a simple, implementable design.
- Clear computational story: sparse top-K reads, LRU-based sparse writes, and rollback BPTT; complexity claims are plausible.
- Convincing systems-level benchmarks: runtime and memory scaling vs NTM and a dense variant (DAM).
- Demonstrates training with approximate indices inside the loop without supervision over memory access; performance generally holds.
- Curriculum results to very long sequences (4k–10k steps) and very large memories (up to millions of slots) are impressive.
- SDNC shows generality of the approach and strong bAbI performance (if substantiated).

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Complexity claims rely critically on ANN behavior; O(log N) is not guaranteed for high-dimensional queries with k-d trees (known to degrade toward linear) and LSH has query/update costs that depend on ρ and collision parameters. The paper should make explicit assumptions on dimensionality M, data distribution, and ANN recall; otherwise “asymptotically optimal” is overstated. Provide empirical scaling vs dimensionality M and report recall of ANN queries during training.
- The top-K truncation and non-differentiable selection (for both reads and LRU writes) introduce biased gradients. There is no analysis of the optimization consequences or ablations that vary K and compare exact top-K vs approximate ANN selection. Please report performance vs K, and compare exact KNN vs ANN (accuracy, speed, and convergence).
- The LRU usage U^(2) uses a hard threshold ε; results may be sensitive to ε and to the rebuild frequency of the ANN (“every N insertions”). Provide sensitivity analyses and ablate rebuild schedule.
- The backprop rollback argument ignores controller hidden-state/storage costs; the claim “O(1) space per time step” should be clarified as O(1) w.r.t. N, not total. Also specify exact storage per step (controller activations, indices, diffs).
- Experimental comparisons are not apples-to-apples in all cases: for curriculum scaling, sparse models use 2×10^6 memory slots while dense models use 64 slots “to match physical memory footprint.” This conflates architectural advantage with sheer capacity. Include comparisons at equal memory capacity (e.g., 64 vs 64) and at matched task difficulty (e.g., both with enough memory to solve).
- bAbI results are only summarized; the main text lacks per-task accuracies, number of hops, training details, and whether joint training is used. “Best reported” claims for SDNC require exact numbers and protocol parity (weak vs strong supervision). Provide full tables in the main paper or at least concise summaries.
- Omniglot protocol deviates from standard; error metric is unclear (“0.2 < errors with 100 characters” per episode or per sample?), and the curriculum stopping criteria (“threshold for a number of episodes”) are unspecified. Report exact metrics, thresholds, patience, seeds, and variance.

Clarity
- Numerous typographical/formatting errors hinder readability: e.g., Eq. (3) is missing an equals sign; strange symbols (⇥, ↵t, t) and inconsistent transposes; “th Conference…”; “400⇥”; “MiB/GiB” vs standard GB; missing spaces and punctuation (e.g., AT = wWt aTt). Define all symbols (αt, τt?) clearly and consistently.
- The controller parameters p_t = (q_t, a_t, α_t, τ_t?)—notation is unclear; specify all gates and their ranges. Define K early and state whether it is fixed or learned.
- Algorithmic descriptions would benefit from pseudocode for forward/backward updates (including rollback) and an explicit description of the ANN maintenance (insert/delete/rebuild).
- “Asymptotically optimal” is asserted but the key assumptions are only in the supplement; at least summarize the theorem and assumptions in the main text.

Originality
- The combination of sparse top-K memory access with ANN indices integrated into a differentiable MANN and the rollback BPTT implementation is a useful engineering contribution. However, the underlying ideas (sparse attention, KNN-based addressing, LRU allocation) are known; novelty is primarily in the systems design and practical demonstration at scale.

Significance
- If reproducible, the speed/memory improvements could materially impact research on differentiable memory and long-context learning. However, the paper leans heavily on the supplement for essential details and omits critical ablations and standardized comparisons, reducing confidence in the breadth of adoption.

Minor issues and formatting inconsistencies
- References lack years/venues for many entries; inconsistent styles; some are arXiv-only without identifiers; ensure complete and consistent bibliographic info.
- Citations to supplementary sections are frequent; move core proofs/algorithms/experimental tables into the main paper where possible.
- Define all hyperparameters (K, ε, rebuild frequency, ANN parameters, optimizer, batch sizes) and report number of seeds and variance.
- Clarify whether gradients flow through the choice of previously read locations for writes (since this depends on prior top-K selection).
- Discuss failure modes: what happens when the memory is empty or ANN returns low-recall neighbors? How are ties handled in I_U?

Questions for the authors
1. Provide ablations on K (e.g., K ∈ {1, 4, 8, 16}) and ANN vs exact KNN, including ANN recall and impact on accuracy and convergence.
2. Clarify the exact complexity with respect to dimensionality M and provide empirical scaling in M.
3. Report full bAbI task-wise results and training protocol parity; likewise, standardize Omniglot evaluation and report exact metrics and variance.
4. Detail the rollback BPTT memory accounting, including controller and index-related storage.
5. Can K be learned/adaptive? Any stability issues with dynamic K?

Pros
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a practical, implementable method.
- Strong empirical speed and memory savings; demonstrates very long sequence training.
- Generalizable to other MANNs (SDNC).

Cons
- Insufficient ablations and sensitivity analyses; key claims depend on ANN behavior and K.
- Non-standard/insufficiently detailed experimental protocols (bAbI, Omniglot); results hard to assess.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for core arguments; clarity/notation/formatting issues throughout.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The contribution is promising and likely impactful, but the accumulation of missing ablations, unclear protocols, and clarity/formatting issues undermines confidence. With stronger empirical rigor, clearer exposition, and standardized comparisons, I would lean to accept. Confidence: high.
