Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales content-addressable external memory to millions of slots by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes and by integrating efficient data structures for content-based addressing. Reads are limited to the K nearest memory slots via an ANN index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), and writes are constrained to previously read locations plus a least-recently-used slot; gradients are propagated only through these sparse accesses. A key systems contribution is an in-place, reversible sparse update scheme that enables backpropagation through time with O(1) space per step and O(log N) time per step (given ANN). Empirically, SAM matches or outperforms dense MANN baselines (NTM, dense approximation DAM) on algorithmic tasks, scales via curriculum to sequences of thousands of steps without truncation, achieves strong results on bAbI (with a sparse DNC variant solving all but 1 task without supervision), and improves Omniglot one-shot classification at much larger sequence lengths. Reported runtime/memory savings are up to three orders of magnitude vs. NTM.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling memory size from parameter count and enabling differentiable read/write. Differs in enforcing hard sparsity and ANN-based content lookup within the computational graph, plus an LRU-like allocator.
- Related to DNC in providing richer memory management; authors show a sparse DNC variant with large speedups.
- Contrasts with RL-based discrete addressing (RL-NTM) by retaining end-to-end differentiability (lower-variance training).
- The use of approximate nearest neighbors within a neural architecture is a novel systems/algorithmic contribution relative to earlier MANNs.

Strengths
- Clear, ambitious goal: make content-addressable external memory scale in both time and space without giving up differentiability.
- Elegant design: sparse reads/writes, LRU allocation, and reversible sparse BPTT form a coherent, practical system with strong asymptotics.
- Empirical evidence across synthetic algorithmic tasks, long-sequence curricula, bAbI, and Omniglot; demonstrates both speed/memory savings and competitive accuracy.
- Generality: extension to a Sparse DNC with very large speedups and strong bAbI results.
- Pragmatic engineering details (ANN choice, periodic rebuilds, CSR storage, O(1) per-step memory usage) increase the likelihood of adoption.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees: the “asymptotically optimal” claim is relegated to the supplement. It would help to state the formal assumptions (e.g., content addressing, constant K) and theorem in the main text, and clarify constant factors in practice.
- Differentiability and bias: sparsifying reads/writes and using ANN introduce biased gradients and potential non-smoothness. The paper shows empirically that learning works, but provides little analysis of when/why this bias is benign. An ablation quantifying performance vs. K and ANN approximation level would strengthen the case.
- Fairness of comparisons: in several experiments the sparse models use vastly larger memories (e.g., 2e6 slots) than dense ones (64), justified by equalizing physical memory usage over 100 steps. This is reasonable for the scaling story, but it muddies algorithmic comparisons. Add controls where memory budgets are matched in number of slots or where dense models use chunked/recurrent controllers to narrow the gap.
- ANN maintenance: rebalancing/rebuilding costs, staleness during rapid writes, and online degradation are acknowledged but not deeply evaluated. Wall-clock breakdowns (compute vs. ANN maintenance) and sensitivity to rebuild frequency would be useful.
- Write policy: the LRU-plus-read-location scheme is simple and effective, but alternatives (e.g., learned allocation policies, sparse content writes) are only briefly discussed. An ablation on allocation strategies and erase policies would clarify trade-offs.
- Real-world scale: beyond Omniglot and bAbI, there are no large natural tasks (language modeling, long-context QA, program induction) where memory scale is critical. Even a small pilot on longer documents would reinforce impact.
- Clarity: some crucial implementation details (rollback mechanics, CSR gradient bookkeeping, ANN synchronization) are deferred to the supplement; a compact schematic in the main paper would improve readability.

Quality
- Generally technically sound with careful engineering; the reversible sparse BPTT and CSR-based gradients are convincing. The empirical section is broad. The main gap is a lack of quantitative analysis of gradient bias due to sparsification/ANN and of ablations on key hyperparameters (K, allocation policy, ANN type).

Clarity
- Well written and organized. However, essential components are in the supplement (optimality assumptions, data structures, SDNC details). Bringing a concise theorem and algorithmic pseudocode into the main body would help expert reproducibility.

Originality
- High. Enforcing hard sparsity for both read and write with integrated ANN search and reversible sparse BPTT is a notable departure from prior dense MANNs. The systems-meets-learning angle is fresh and impactful.

Significance
- High. If widely adopted, this makes differentiable external memories practical at scales previously infeasible, enabling new research and applications. The SDNC extension and scaling curricula underscore generality and impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, bold idea addressing a central bottleneck in MANNs with strong empirical validation and massive practical speed/memory gains.
- General technique that ports to multiple architectures (NTM, DNC).
- Demonstrates very long-horizon BPTT and strong results on benchmark tasks.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical/empirical analysis of sparsification/ANN-induced bias and stability.
- Comparisons sometimes favor the sparse model via much larger memory capacity; fewer tests on large real-world tasks.
- Some critical implementation details are outside the main text.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces a significant, novel direction for scalable differentiable memory with well-executed engineering and convincing evidence. Minor gaps in analysis/ablations are outweighed by the conceptual advance and practical impact.

Confidence
High.
