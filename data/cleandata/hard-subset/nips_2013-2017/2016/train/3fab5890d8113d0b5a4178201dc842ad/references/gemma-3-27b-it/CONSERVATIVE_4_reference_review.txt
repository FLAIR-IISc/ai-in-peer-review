This paper introduces SAM, a sparse memory access scheme for memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs), aiming to address the computational and memory overhead associated with traditional approaches like NTMs. The core idea is to limit read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, coupled with efficient data structures for content-based access. The authors demonstrate significant speedups and reduced memory consumption compared to dense NTMs, and show comparable or improved performance on several synthetic and real-world tasks, including scaling to larger memory sizes and sequence lengths.

The motivation is well-articulated – the quadratic scaling of parameter count in traditional recurrent models and the computational burden of dense MANNs are significant limitations. The proposed approach is a logical step towards addressing these issues, and the potential for scaling MANNs to larger memory capacities is indeed appealing. The reported speedups (up to three orders of magnitude) and memory savings are substantial. 

However, my primary concern revolves around the novelty and depth of validation. While sparsity is a common technique in machine learning, its application within the specific context of MANNs, particularly the chosen mechanisms for sparse access (previously read/least recently accessed), feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper doesn’t offer substantial theoretical justification for *why* this particular sparsity pattern is effective, beyond empirical observation. The authors briefly touch upon asymptotic optimality in the supplementary material, but this feels underdeveloped. 

More critically, the comparison to existing methods, while present, isn't exhaustive enough to fully convince me of the method's superiority. The NTM comparison is essential, but the lack of more thorough comparison to other recent memory architectures beyond the SDNC (which is presented as a derivative work) is a weakness. For example, a direct comparison to more modern sparse activation techniques or other methods designed for efficient memory access within neural networks would be beneficial. The claim of “near-identical data efficiency” needs stronger quantification.

Furthermore, the reliance on curriculum learning to scale tasks is a point of concern. While curriculum learning can be helpful, it also introduces a potential bias in the results. It’s unclear whether the observed improvements are solely attributable to the sparse access scheme or also a consequence of the carefully designed curriculum. The generalization results, particularly on the long sequences, are promising but require careful scrutiny regarding potential overfitting to the curriculum.

The experimental setup, while reasonably comprehensive, could be strengthened. The choice of hyperparameters is mentioned, but a more detailed ablation study examining the sensitivity of performance to key parameters (e.g., the size of K, the discount factor in the usage definition) would be valuable. The ANN implementation choices (FLANN vs. LSH) and their impact on performance aren't thoroughly discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant reduction in computational cost and memory usage compared to dense NTMs.
*   Comparable or improved performance on several benchmark tasks.
*   Demonstrated ability to scale to larger memory sizes and sequence lengths.
*   Provides a practical approach towards making MANNs more feasible for real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty - sparsity is a well-established technique.
*   Limited theoretical justification for the chosen sparsity mechanisms.
*   Insufficient comparison to a wider range of recent memory architectures.
*   Potential bias introduced by curriculum learning.
*   Lack of a detailed ablation study.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable engineering improvement to MANNs, but it falls short of being a truly significant theoretical or methodological advance. While the empirical results are encouraging, they are not sufficiently compelling to overcome my concerns about novelty and the depth of validation. I recommend **weak reject**. The authors should consider addressing the shortcomings outlined above—particularly providing stronger theoretical justification, conducting a more thorough comparison to relevant literature, and carefully analyzing the impact of curriculum learning—before resubmission.
