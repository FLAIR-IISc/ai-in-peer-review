- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparsify reads/writes + ANN indices + rollback BPTT to achieve sublinear time/space for memory-augmented networks.  
- Claims O(log N) time per step and O(1) additional space per step after O(N) initialization, retaining end-to-end differentiability where continuous.  
- Empirically shows 2–3 orders of magnitude runtime/memory savings vs classic NTMs and comparable/better learning on algorithmic tasks.  
- Demonstrates curriculum scaling to millions of memory slots, strong bAbI results (via sparse DNC), and improved one-shot Omniglot performance.  
- Builds on NTMs, Memory Networks, and DNC ideas while offering a differentiable sparse-access alternative to discrete/RL approaches.  
- Core idea (restrict computation to content-selected sparse subset and maintain ANN indices) is technically sound and supported by implementation choices.  
- Complexity claims are plausible but contingent on assumptions (content-based addressing, constant K); these assumptions are mainly in the supplement.  
- Benchmarks robustly support wall-clock and memory savings across tasks, with comparisons to dense baselines and an SDNC variant.  
- Some components are non-differentiable (top-K, LRU allocation, ANN queries) and treated as fixed—this could introduce bias and needs explicit discussion.  
- Scaling experiments use much larger memory for SAM than for dense baselines (equalized physical memory), making some accuracy comparisons less directly comparable.  
- Ablation studies are limited: effects of K, write policies, ANN choice/rebuild frequency, and erase schemes need more systematic study.  
- Details about the “optimality” claim and BPTT rollback are mostly in the supplement; main text would benefit from brief pseudocode/theorem.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; high-level algorithm and motivations are easy to follow.  
- Need clearer main-text descriptions of gradient flow, which components receive gradients, and the non-differentiable parts.  
- The memory-rollback mechanism warrants a concise schematic/pseudocode in the main paper to aid reproducibility.  
- The SAM combination (top-K ANN reads, LRU sparse writes, constant-space BPTT rollback) is novel and impactful relative to prior MANNs.  
- Scaling MANNs with orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains is significant and likely influential for long-context and algorithmic tasks.  
- Pros: well-motivated, sound algorithmic/engineering design, extensive empirical validation, generality via SDNC, high potential impact.  
- Cons: non-differentiable components and limited failure-mode analysis, sparse ablations missing, key details deferred to supplement, fairness concerns in comparisons.  
- Suggested improvements: add concise optimality/rollback description in main text, perform ablations on K/ANN/write policies, clarify gradient pathways, provide wall-clock breakdown (ANN vs controller), and discuss scenarios where sparsity may hurt.  
- Overall: high-quality, original, and significant work with convincing experiments; recommendation for acceptance despite some reproducibility and ablation gaps.
