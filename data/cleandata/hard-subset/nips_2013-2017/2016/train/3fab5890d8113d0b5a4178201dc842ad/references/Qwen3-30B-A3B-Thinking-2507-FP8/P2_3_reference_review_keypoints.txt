- Proposes SAM, a Sparse Access Memory architecture that makes memory reads/writes sparse to improve efficiency.  
- Uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes to reduce time complexity from O(N) to O(log N).  
- Reports dramatic efficiency gains: up to 1600x faster than NTM and massive memory reduction (e.g., 53MB vs 29GB for 64K words).  
- Maintains performance comparable to dense models across synthetic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Provides theoretical complexity analysis showing SAM is asymptotically optimal in time and space.  
- Extensive experiments and ablation studies compare SAM to DAM, NTM, and LSTM, validating sparsity effects.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and supply supplementary material with additional implementation details.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; background and SAM architecture are explained with appropriate technical detail.  
- Minor clarity issues: the “usage measure” (U^(2)_T) definition is opaque and needs more explanation.  
- Minor clarity issue: distinction between “SAM linear” and “SAM ANN” could be clearer.  
- Original contribution: novel integration of ANN within a differentiable memory-access framework, distinct from prior sparse RL approaches.  
- High practical significance: enables scalable, real-world-capable MANNs by removing key computational bottlenecks.  
- Demonstrated practical impact on benchmarks (best bAbI result without supervised memory access; strong Omniglot performance).  
- Weakness: limited analysis of tasks requiring precise, dense memory access patterns and lack of concrete failure examples.  
- Weakness: insufficient exploration of broader real-world application domains where SAM would provide impact.  
- Method generalizes to other memory architectures (e.g., Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer).  
- Overall assessment: technically strong, well-executed, and impactful work — recommendation: Accept.
