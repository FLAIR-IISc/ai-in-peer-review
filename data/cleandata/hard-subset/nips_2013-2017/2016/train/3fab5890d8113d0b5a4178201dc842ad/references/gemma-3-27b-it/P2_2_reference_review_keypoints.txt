- Introduces SAM: a MANN using sparse reads/writes and efficient data structures to reduce time and space while maintaining performance.  
- Reports up to three orders of magnitude speedup and large memory savings compared to standard NTM implementations.  
- Empirical evaluation on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot learning shows comparable or improved performance with much larger memory.  
- Demonstrates scalability via curriculum learning experiments.  
- Shows how sparse access can be adapted to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC), indicating generality.  
- Provides theoretical discussion of time and space complexity arguing for near-optimality.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, with extensive and useful supplementary material.  
- Strength: directly addresses the major scalability bottleneck of MANNs with a practical solution.  
- Strength: substantial empirical gains and strong validation across diverse tasks.  
- Strength: effective integration of sparse access and data structures leading to significant benefits.  
- Weakness: individual components (sparse ops, approximate nearest neighbors) are not entirely novel; main novelty is their integration.  
- Weakness: ANN implementation details (choice/tuning of k-d trees vs LSH) are brief and could be more thorough.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly sparsity level K.  
- Weakness: lacks explicit comparison to recent memory compression techniques.  
- Quality/Clarity/Originality/Significance scores: 4.5/5, 4/5, 3.5/5, 4.5/5 respectively.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses critical bottleneck, shows large efficiency gains, strong empirical support, enables larger memory models.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty of individual components and insufficient ANN implementation detail.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept (recommended for NIPS).
