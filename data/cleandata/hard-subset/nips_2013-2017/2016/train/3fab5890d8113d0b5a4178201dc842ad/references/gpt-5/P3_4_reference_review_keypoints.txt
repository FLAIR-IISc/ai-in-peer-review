- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) uses top-K sparse reads via ANN, constant sparse writes (reads + LRU slot), and rollback BPTT storing sparse deltas for O(1) per-step memory and O(log N) read/write time with ANN.
- Relation to prior work: Extends NTMs/Memory Networks and DNC by decoupling capacity from parameters and keeping end-to-end differentiability versus discrete-addressing methods.
- Technical contribution: Coherent integration of sparse reads/writes, ANN index, LRU allocation, and rollback BPTT to enable scalable differentiable memory.
- Theoretical claims: Presents a complexity story (O(log N) with ANN; O(1) BPTT overhead) backed by practical data structures and a lower-bound argument in the supplement.
- Empirical evidence: Thorough runtime/memory benchmarks, parity or improvements over dense models on algorithmic tasks, curriculum training to millions of slots, strong bAbI (SDNC) and Omniglot results.
- Generality: Method transfers to DNC, indicating broad applicability to content-addressed MANNs.
- Differentiability/gradient-bias concern: Hard top-K and ANN cause discontinuities and gradient bias; no analysis or comparison to dense soft attention provided.
- Ablation gaps: Missing systematic studies on K sensitivity and exact vs approximate KNN at scale.
- Optimality clarity: “Asymptotically optimal” claim depends on supplement assumptions; those should be summarized in the main text.
- Memory management worry: LRU allocation with fixed threshold may erase rarely-used-but-relevant data; no analysis of overwrite/retention behavior.
- ANN maintenance impact: Rebuilding and maintaining the ANN index may be CPU-bound; benchmarks should report index maintenance time and CPU/GPU placement.
- Fairness of comparisons: Curriculum experiments conflate capacity with architecture (dense baselines small, SAM huge); need comparisons holding memory size fixed.
- Reproducibility: Many crucial algorithmic and implementation details live in the supplement; main paper needs more pseudocode and code release.
- Quality assessment: Technically sound and well-engineered; main missing element is systematic study of approximation effects and more real-data evaluation.
- Clarity assessment: Generally clear, but key definitions, ANN configuration, and rollback pseudocode are relegated to the supplement.
- Originality: Novel integrated combination of sparse top-K, ANN indexing, LRU allocation, and rollback BPTT enabling million-scale differentiable memory.
- Significance: High — addresses a major bottleneck for large content-addressable memories and long-context tasks, with practical speed/space gains.
- Suggested improvements: Add ablations (K, exact vs approx KNN, index rebuild freq, CPU/GPU placement), summarize assumptions/lower-bound, analyze retention and alternative allocators, report end-to-end throughput, include per-task bAbI and DNC comparisons, discuss softer alternatives to hard top-K.
- Arguments for acceptance: Important bottleneck solved, strong empirical speed/memory gains, demonstrated scalability, likely influential.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited analysis of approximation-induced gradient bias, heavy reliance on supplement, modest real-world evaluation.
- Recommendation: Accept.
