- Proposal: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) scales content-based reading/writing to very large memories using sparse top-K attention and efficient data structures.
- Key methods: sparse read via ANN (randomized k-d trees/LSH) for O(log N) reads, sparse write to small set including previous reads and LRU slot, and sparse-delta rollback for O(1) BPTT memory per step.
- Claim: SAM is asymptotically optimal under reasonable assumptions and can be applied to DNC to create a much faster SDNC.
- Empirics: SAM matches or outperforms dense MANNs on algorithmic tasks, scales to very long sequences (>4k steps), strong bAbI results, and improved few-shot Omniglot performance.
- Engineering: Practical integration of sparse data structures, ANN maintenance, and rollback BPTT yields orders-of-magnitude speed and memory savings up to N=10^6.
- Novelty: Combining top-K sparse access, ANN indices inside the computation graph (no gradients through ANN), and constant-memory BPTT is new and impactful.
- Strength: Clear scalability advance with theoretical and empirical complexity improvements and large empirical speedups.
- Strength: Strong empirical coverage across synthetic tasks, curricula, QA, and few-shot learning.
- Strength: Generality demonstrated by sparsifying DNC (SDNC) with retained performance.
- Strength: Insight that sparsity can aid early learning on some tasks.
- Weakness: Top-K selection without gradients introduces discontinuities and biased gradients that are acknowledged but not analyzed.
- Weakness: Dependence on ANN: k-d trees and LSH have limitations in high dimensions and rebuild/overhead costs are not fully quantified.
- Weakness: Simple LRU allocation may thrash when recency ≠ usefulness; more allocation ablations or learned policies needed.
- Weakness: Comparisons may conflate capacity with algorithmic gains since SAM uses much larger memories than dense baselines; matched-accuracy/compute comparisons lacking.
- Weakness: Important details and proofs are relegated to the supplement rather than the main text.
- Weakness: Evaluation on more realistic benchmarks (standard QA, standard few-shot splits) is limited.
- Quality: Mechanisms are well motivated and implemented; complexity claims plausible but a formal theorem with assumptions would strengthen rigor.
- Empirical gaps: Missing sensitivity analyses for K, memory size, ANN approximation, and write-policy ablations.
- Clarity: Generally well written; could be improved with a compact algorithm box, cleaner notation, and key supplemental tables moved to main text.
- Originality: The combination of techniques is novel and well positioned relative to discrete-addressing and prior acceleration attempts.
- Significance: High — provides a practical path to million-slot memories and is likely to be adopted and extended.
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to K/dimensionality/ANN parameters and exact vs ANN top-K effects on convergence; allocation ablations (U(1) vs U(2), learned gates); ANN runtime/rebuild overhead; observed instabilities from discontinuous top-K selection and mitigations.
- Pros for acceptance: Strong, general contribution with convincing empirical results and clear practical utility.
- Cons for acceptance: Limited theoretical analysis of gradient bias/ANN approximations and some reporting/comparison incompleteness.
- Recommendation: Accept (clear accept); minor clarifications and ablations suggested.
- Reviewer confidence: High.
