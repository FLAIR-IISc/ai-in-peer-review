Summary
The paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales external memory access via sparse reads/writes and efficient data structures. Key ideas: (i) read access is restricted to the top-K memories retrieved by an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index, and (ii) writes are sparse and targeted either to previously read locations or to the least-recently-used (LRU) location, enabling O(log N) time and O(1) per-step space. Backpropagation is made memory-efficient by storing and rolling back only sparse modifications to the memory across time steps. The approach is demonstrated on NTM-style algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot one-shot learning, with large-scale curricula. The authors also show how to sparsify the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), obtaining large speedups and strong bAbI performance.

Relation to prior work
SAM extends MANNs (NTM, DNC, Memory Networks) by explicitly enforcing sparsity to overcome the linear-time/space overhead of dense attention over memory. Unlike Memory Networks, SAM supports online writes; unlike NTM/DNC, writes are constrained to a small index set chosen via LRU and previous reads; unlike RL-based discrete addressing (e.g., RL-NTM), SAM retains gradient-based optimization by treating the address selection as a fixed, non-differentiable index and backpropagating through the soft weighting within the selected set. Using ANN search (randomized k-d trees or LSH) brings sublinear retrieval into the training loop.

Strengths
- Technical novelty and practicality: The sparse read/write plus ANN index and per-step O(1) BPTT memory via rollback are elegant and practically meaningful for scaling.
- Complexity claims and implementation details: The paper provides a convincing path to O(log N) time per step and O(1) per-step memory (with O(N) init), and discusses the necessary data structures (CSR, ANN indices, LRU tracking). This is rare in MANN papers and valuable to practitioners.
- Empirical evidence: Strong speed/memory benchmarks (up to 1600× faster than NTM, orders of magnitude less memory) and competitive or better learning on synthetic tasks, bAbI (SDNC nearly solves all tasks without supervision), and Omniglot one-shot, including impressive curriculum scaling (BPTT over >4000 steps; generalization up to 200k length).
- Generality: The construction applies beyond NTM; the SDNC variant substantiates this.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Differentiability and gradient bias: The top-K selection and ANN retrieval are non-differentiable; gradients are only propagated through the chosen K rows. This introduces a biased gradient estimator and breaks the equivalence to fully smooth attention. While results are good, the paper does not analyze the impact on optimization stability or provide ablations of K vs accuracy/speed, beyond limited comparisons.
- Write policy flexibility: Writes are restricted to previously read locations and an LRU slot, with full erase on the LRU write. This may limit expressivity for tasks requiring targeted updates without a prior read, or partial overwrites. A content-based sparse write is mentioned but not evaluated.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The NTM baseline appears to be a straightforward dense implementation; optimized dense baselines (e.g., kernelized attention with block-sparse ops or vector quantization) are not explored. For bAbI and Omniglot, comparisons to contemporary unsupervised MemN2N variants and key-value Memory Networks could be expanded.
- ANN details: The cost of periodic index rebuilding and its inclusion in timing are only briefly mentioned. The paper does not explore sensitivity to ANN hyperparameters or failure modes (e.g., imbalance, wrong neighbors), nor does it systematically compare FLANN vs LSH vs alternatives in the online regime.
- Reproducibility: Many crucial engineering pieces live in the supplement; it would help to state whether code/data structures will be released. Some notation inconsistencies (e.g., write-gate symbols) could be cleaned up.

Quality
The paper is technically sound overall and the engineering is thoughtful. The asymptotic optimality claims are plausible given content-based retrieval, though formal lower bounds are left to the supplement; more clarity on assumptions (e.g., constant K, amortization of index rebuild) would help. The experimental evidence is broad and generally supports the claims; however, a deeper ablation on K, erase policy, and ANN choices would strengthen the case and elucidate when sparse approximations help/hurt.

Clarity
The writing is clear and well-structured, with helpful background and a concise core description. Some key mechanisms (e.g., exact gradient flow with top-K truncation, effect of index rebuilds) could use a few more sentences in the main text. Notation for write gates and usage measures is occasionally inconsistent. The results are presented clearly; referring figures are informative.

Originality
The combination of sparse read/write with ANN inside the training loop, plus memory rollback for BPTT, is novel and impactful relative to prior MANNs. While sparsity and nearest-neighbor search are known, integrating them to make end-to-end trainable external memory scale is original and addresses a major bottleneck.

Significance
High. Scaling differentiable memory is central to many sequence and reasoning tasks. The reported improvements are large in both speed and memory, and the approach appears general. This work is likely to influence subsequent MANN designs and large-memory architectures.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Provide ablations on K and on the erase policy (full erase vs partial), and quantify the effect on learning and speed.
- Report wall-clock training time for end-to-end runs, including ANN maintenance costs; clarify whether timings include index rebuilds.
- Discuss stability: how often does ANN return incorrect top-K neighbors during training, and does this harm convergence?
- Consider a differentiable sparse attention alternative (e.g., top-k softmax with a straight-through estimator, sparsemax/entmax) to mitigate gradient bias from hard selection, and compare.
- Clarify whether code/data structures will be released.
- Expand comparisons to other scalable memory mechanisms available at the time (e.g., key-value memory networks).

Pros
- Elegant, general mechanism to scale MANNs with strong theoretical and empirical support.
- Large speed and memory savings; impressive long-horizon BPTT.
- Competitive/superior results on diverse tasks; SDNC result on bAbI is strong.
- Solid engineering contributions (rollback BPTT, LRU tracking, ANN integration).

Cons
- Non-differentiable selection introduces biased gradients; limited analysis/ablations.
- Write policy may be restrictive for some tasks.
- Baselines and ANN analysis could be more comprehensive.
- Heavy reliance on supplement for critical details; minor notation issues.

Recommendation: Accept (8).
