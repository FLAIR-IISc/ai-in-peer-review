This paper introduces SAM, a sparse access memory architecture designed to address the memory and computational bottlenecks of traditional memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) like NTMs. The core idea is to limit read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, coupled with efficient data structures for approximate nearest neighbor search and memory management. The authors demonstrate significant speedups and reduced memory consumption compared to dense NTMs, alongside comparable or even improved performance on several tasks, including synthetic algorithmic tasks, the Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot learning. They also present a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) with notable performance gains.

While the presented work tackles an important problem and demonstrates promising results, several shortcomings prevent a positive recommendation. My primary concern stems from a lack of rigorous comparison and a somewhat superficial exploration of the design choices. 

Firstly, the reported speedups, while impressive (up to three orders of magnitude), are largely presented without sufficient detail regarding the implementation and hardware. Figure 1 shows substantial speed differences, but lacks granularity about the specific hardware used (CPU, GPU, memory bandwidth) and the optimization levels applied to each implementation (NTM, DAM, SAM). A fair comparison requires controlling for these factors. Furthermore, the claim of "optimal in space and time" in the introduction is overly strong and not adequately substantiated by a formal analysis beyond the Supplementary A. The asymptotic complexity arguments are insufficient without a comparable analysis of the constant factors that dominate performance in practice.

Secondly, the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the constant *K* representing the sparsity level, feels arbitrarily chosen (K=4 or K=8). The paper lacks a systematic exploration of the impact of *K* on performance and efficiency. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the trade-off between sparsity and accuracy would significantly strengthen the findings.  The choice of the threshold parameter '’ in the usage statistic U(2)T is also presented without justification. 

Thirdly, the experimental setup, while covering a range of tasks, appears somewhat imbalanced. The scaling with curriculum learning is a strong point, but the description of the curriculum itself (exponential increase) feels somewhat ad-hoc. The Babi task results are reported, but a detailed comparison against other strong baselines specifically tailored for this benchmark is missing. The claim of achieving the best reported result on Babi without supervising memory access needs careful scrutiny and comparison to the relevant literature. 

Fourthly, the implementation details are occasionally vague. The description of the sparse matrix format (CSR) assumes familiarity but lacks sufficient explanation for reproducibility.  The exact implementation of the approximate nearest neighbor search, including the choice of parameters for FLANN or LSH, is not fully detailed.  The statement about rebuilding the ANN from scratch every N insertions needs a precise definition of N and the rationale behind this choice.

Finally, while the paper mentions a potential application to other MANN architectures, the description of the Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) is relegated to the supplementary material. The significance of this contribution deserves more attention in the main body of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant bottleneck in MANNs.
*   Demonstrates substantial speedups and memory reductions.
*   Shows comparable or improved performance on several benchmark tasks.
*   Presents a general approach applicable to other memory architectures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous comparison and detailed implementation specifics.
*   Arbitrary choice of hyperparameters without sensitivity analysis.
*   Imbalanced experimental setup and limited baseline comparisons.
*   Insufficient justification for strong claims (e.g., “optimal in space and time”).
*   Important contribution (SDNC) relegated to supplementary material.



**Overall:** Despite the novelty and potential of the presented work, the lack of thorough analysis, detailed implementation information, and rigorous comparison to established baselines prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a complete and polished research contribution. A major revision addressing the points raised above, with a strong emphasis on reproducibility and a more thorough evaluation, would be required for reconsideration. I rate this paper as **Reject**.
