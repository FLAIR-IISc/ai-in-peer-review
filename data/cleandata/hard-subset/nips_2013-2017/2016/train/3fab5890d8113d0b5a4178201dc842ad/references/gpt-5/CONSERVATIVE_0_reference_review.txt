Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that replaces dense content-based reads and writes with sparse, top-K reads (via approximate nearest neighbors) and sparse writes (to previously read locations and to the least-recently-used slot). The authors also describe an efficient backpropagation scheme that logs and reverts sparse modifications to avoid storing full memory snapshots, yielding O(1) space per step and O(log N) time per read/write under ANN indexing. Experiments show large speed/memory savings over NTM and a dense variant (DAM) on synthetic tasks, curricula that scale to long sequences and large memories, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot classification. A sparse DNC variant (SDNC) is also reported to be much faster with strong bAbI performance.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-known scaling bottleneck in MANNs with a concrete, implementable solution.
- Clear algorithmic contributions: top-K sparse attention via ANN, LRU-style memory allocation, and rollback-based backprop that avoids O(NT) memory.
- Theoretical complexity claims (O(log N) time, O(1) space per step) are plausible and practically important.
- Substantial measured speed and memory reductions (two to three orders of magnitude vs NTM) without major accuracy loss on several tasks.
- Demonstrates practical scaling to millions of memory slots and thousands of steps, with evidence of long-range generalization.
- Extension to DNC suggests generality of the approach.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Gradient approximation: Replacing softmax attention with top-K sparse selection and using an ANN index changes the gradient landscape. No analysis of bias or stability is provided; the claim that r̃ ≈ r lacks quantitative error bounds or sensitivity analysis. The ANN itself is non-differentiable; the implications for training dynamics are not explored.
- Fairness of comparisons: In curriculum experiments, sparse models have vastly larger memory than dense baselines (2e6 vs 64 slots) “for the same physical memory budget.” This confounds algorithmic benefit with capacity. Report accuracy vs wall-clock, and vs memory budget, and include capacity-matched dense baselines (or stronger dense architectures).
- Baselines: The main text lacks direct comparisons to DNC (beyond the supplement), end-to-end Memory Networks with tuned hops, pointer networks/attention-based seq2seq, or RL-addressing NTMs. bAbI and Omniglot are relatively small-scale; stronger real-world tasks would better substantiate claims.
- “Optimality” claims depend on assumptions relegated to the supplement. If the lower bounds for content-based addressing are central, a self-contained statement and proof sketch should be in the main paper.
- Practicality of ANN: K-d trees degrade in high dimensions and online updates can imbalance trees; LSH requires careful parameterization. The paper briefly mentions rebuilds every N insertions but does not quantify their overhead or show sensitivity to word dimension, K, number of hashes/tables, or rebuild frequency.
- Write policy: The mixture of updates to previously-read slots and LRU allocation is simple but ad hoc. There is no ablation isolating the contribution of the LRU vs content-based writes, nor analysis of collision/overwrite rates or retention over long horizons.
- Reproducibility: Many crucial details are in the supplement. Hyperparameters (K, epsilon threshold, ANN settings), controller size, initialization, and training stability (variance over seeds) are not fully reported in the main text. Formatting issues (garbled symbols) hinder clarity.

Quality
- The engineering is solid and the speed/memory measurements are compelling. However, the learning-theoretic implications of sparse/approximate attention are insufficiently characterized. Claims of “near-identical” data efficiency would benefit from multiple seeds, confidence intervals, and ablations on K/ANN. The fairness of capacity comparisons needs tightening. More rigorous baselines (incl. DNC in main) are needed.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but the main text depends heavily on the supplement. Fix typesetting issues (e.g., ↵, ⇡, 2⇥106). Provide pseudocode for forward/backward with rollback, and a clear description of the ANN maintenance overhead. Define all symbols in-line.

Originality
- The combination of sparse top-K attention, ANN inside the training loop, LRU-inspired allocation, and rollback-based BPTT for external memory is novel in this integrated form. The idea of sparse access is not entirely new (e.g., RL-trained discrete addressing), but the differentiable, efficient realization here is original and practically relevant.

Significance
- If robust, the approach materially advances the scalability of differentiable memories, potentially enabling new applications. However, the current empirical suite leans on synthetic/toy tasks; stronger evidence on large real datasets would increase impact.

Requested clarifications/experiments
- Ablations: K (2/4/8/16), epsilon threshold, write gates (alpha/tau), with/without LRU, with/without “write to previous reads,” exact top-K vs ANN top-K.
- ANN details: distance metric, word dimensionality, k-d tree vs LSH configuration (#trees/hashes/tables), rebuild schedule, amortized overhead, and accuracy-speed tradeoffs. Report failure modes in high dimensions.
- Fair comparisons: match models by parameter count, compute budget, and memory capacity; report accuracy vs wall-clock and vs peak memory. Include DNC and pointer-network baselines in the main text.
- Stability: results over multiple random seeds with variance. Sensitivity to curriculum schedule and truncation of BPTT.
- Quantify the gradient approximation: measure the angle between dense and sparse gradients, or the loss increase due to sparsification over training.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important bottleneck with a principled, efficient method.
- Large empirical speed/memory gains with competitive accuracy.
- General technique applicable to multiple MANNs (incl. DNC).
- Potentially broad impact if validated more thoroughly.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical and empirical analysis of the learning impact of sparse/approximate attention.
- Comparisons confounded by memory capacity differences; some baselines missing in main text.
- Reliance on supplements; limited real-world benchmarks; reproducibility details sparse.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The scalability contribution is compelling and likely useful to the community, but I strongly encourage the authors to strengthen fairness of comparisons, provide ablations and ANN details, and move key theoretical/algorithmic content from the supplement into the main paper.
