This paper tackles a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) – the computational and memory overhead associated with large external memories. The core idea, SAM (Sparse Access Memory), is compelling: restrict read/write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, significantly reducing computational cost and memory footprint without (apparently) sacrificing performance. The authors convincingly demonstrate substantial speedups and memory savings compared to standard NTM implementations, and importantly, show that this sparsity doesn’t cripple learning on a diverse set of tasks, including scaled-up versions requiring larger memory capacity. The extension to a sparse DNC, achieving state-of-the-art results on the Babi tasks *without* supervised memory access, is a particularly strong result.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of aggressively sparsifying memory access within MANNs is genuinely novel and addresses a long-standing practical limitation. While sparsity has been explored in other contexts, applying it so directly and effectively to the memory access patterns of MANNs is a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated speedups (up to three orders of magnitude) and memory savings are substantial. This unlocks the potential to apply MANNs to much larger and more complex problems than previously feasible, such as processing entire books or Wikipedia. The ability to scale to longer sequences via curriculum learning is also a key strength.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on a variety of tasks, including synthetic algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. The consistent performance, often matching or exceeding dense counterparts, is impressive.
* **Generality:** The demonstration of a sparse DNC highlights the broader applicability of the SAM approach to other MANN architectures.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and concise description of the SAM architecture, along with a detailed analysis of its computational complexity. The efficient backpropagation scheme and use of approximate nearest neighbor data structures are well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **ANN Implementation Details:** While the use of ANNs is justified, the paper lacks detailed discussion of the specific ANN parameters and their impact on performance. More information on how the ANN index is rebuilt and maintained would be beneficial.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the authors compare SAM to dense variants, a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different sparsity levels (K values) on performance would be helpful.
* **Curriculum Learning Specifics:** The curriculum learning scheme is mentioned but could benefit from more details. Understanding the exact parameters and schedule used to increase sequence length would provide valuable insight.
* **Theoretical Optimality:** The claim of asymptotic optimality in space and time (Supplementary A) feels a bit strong without a more rigorous comparison to alternative memory access schemes. A more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying this claim would be useful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution to the field of MANNs.
* The demonstrated speedups and memory savings are substantial and unlock new possibilities for applying MANNs to larger and more complex problems.
* The empirical results are strong and consistent across a variety of tasks.
* The approach is general and can be applied to other MANN architectures, as demonstrated by the sparse DNC.
* The paper is well-written and technically sound. 

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed analysis of the ANN implementation and sparsity levels represents a minor weakness.
* The claim of asymptotic optimality could be strengthened with a more rigorous analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper that addresses a critical limitation of MANNs. The core idea is novel, the results are compelling, and the potential impact is significant. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper represents a substantial advance in the field and strongly recommend acceptance, even though some details could benefit from further elaboration. The boldness of the approach and the significant practical benefits outweigh any minor shortcomings. The work is likely to spur further research in sparse memory access schemes for MANNs and beyond.
