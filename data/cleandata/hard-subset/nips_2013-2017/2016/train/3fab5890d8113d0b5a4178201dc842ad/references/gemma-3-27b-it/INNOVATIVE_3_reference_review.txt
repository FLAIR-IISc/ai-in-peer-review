This paper tackles a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs): the computational and memory overhead associated with large external memories. The authors propose SAM, a sparse access memory, which dramatically reduces this overhead through sparse read/write operations and efficient data structures. This is a compelling and highly relevant direction, directly addressing a major impediment to the broader adoption of MANNs. The core idea – limiting modifications to a sparse subset of memory – is elegant and, crucially, appears to maintain performance while providing substantial gains in efficiency.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The proposed solution is genuinely novel, moving beyond incremental improvements. The reported speedups (up to three orders of magnitude) and memory savings are impressive and, if validated, would be a game-changer for applying MANNs to complex, real-world problems. The extension to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) and its superior performance on the Babi tasks further demonstrates the generality and potential impact of the approach. The authors thoughtfully address the challenges of sparse operations, proposing practical solutions like approximate nearest neighbor search and efficient backpropagation strategies. The experimental validation on a range of tasks – NTM tasks, Babi tasks, and Omniglot – provides strong evidence of SAM’s effectiveness. The curriculum learning approach for scaling tasks is also well-motivated and adds to the paper's value.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the results are promising, the paper is somewhat light on detailed ablation studies. For example, the impact of different sparsity levels (K values) isn’t thoroughly explored. A more systematic analysis of how sparsity affects performance would strengthen the claims. Similarly, while different ANN approaches are mentioned, a more detailed comparison of their performance and computational trade-offs would be helpful. The implementation details, though alluded to (e.g., custom data structures), could benefit from more elaboration. While Supplementary A is mentioned, a greater level of detail within the main paper would increase reproducibility. Finally, while the SDNC results are impressive, it's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the sparsity itself versus other potential modifications made in adapting the DNC architecture.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The sparse access memory concept is a significant departure from traditional MANN designs and offers a compelling solution to a critical bottleneck.
*   **Significant impact:** The reported speedups and memory savings are substantial and could unlock the application of MANNs to previously intractable problems.
*   **Strong empirical results:** SAM performs competitively with, and in some cases outperforms, existing MANNs on a diverse set of tasks.
*   **Generalizability:** The extension to the SDNC demonstrates the broader applicability of the proposed approach.
*   **Bold approach:** The authors tackle a fundamental challenge in the field and propose a solution that, while complex, appears to be effective.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited ablation studies:** A more thorough analysis of the impact of sparsity and different ANN implementations would strengthen the claims.
*   **Implementation details:** More detail on the custom data structures and implementation choices would improve reproducibility.
*   **SDNC analysis:** Clarification on the contribution of sparsity versus architectural modifications in the SDNC would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a very strong paper that presents a genuinely novel and impactful contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors identify a critical problem, propose a clever and effective solution, and provide compelling empirical evidence to support their claims. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh the minor shortcomings. I recommend **acceptance**. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction for research in this area, and I anticipate it will stimulate further work on scalable memory architectures. The fact that the authors are willing to take a bold approach and prioritize innovation over absolute methodological perfection is commendable.
