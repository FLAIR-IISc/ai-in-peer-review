Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales external memory by enforcing sparsity on both reads and writes. Instead of dense content-based attention over all N memory slots (as in NTMs [7] and Memory Networks [21, 17]), SAM computes a top-K content-based read using an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), and writes only to (i) previously read locations and (ii) the least recently used (LRU) location according to a usage statistic. To support backpropagation through time (BPTT) efficiently, the authors store and revert only sparse memory modifications, achieving O(1) space per time step and O(log N) time per operation under ANN assumptions. They further show how to apply the same idea to the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) [8], producing a sparse variant (SDNC).

Empirically, the paper reports up to three orders of magnitude runtime and memory savings compared to dense NTMs, comparable or better learning on standard NTM algorithmic tasks, scalability via curriculum to million-word memories and sequences of thousands of steps (with generalization to much longer), strong bAbI [20] results without supervision (particularly with SDNC), and improved one-shot classification on Omniglot [12, 16] at long sequences.

The work connects to (and extends) content-addressable MANNs [7, 21], end-to-end memory networks [17], and RL-based sparse addressing for NTMs [22]. Unlike [22], SAM stays within a mostly differentiable training regime by approximating attention with top-K selection via ANN, and adds a practical sparse BPTT scheme.

Strengths
- Clear, practical contribution: a simple sparse read/write scheme coupled with efficient data structures (ANN index, LRU list) and a careful BPTT implementation that together enable truly large memories.
- Strong empirical evidence: large speed/memory gains; comparable or better learning to dense baselines on NTM tasks; significant scaling via curriculum; compelling bAbI and Omniglot results.
- Generality: the approach applies beyond the NTM, demonstrated by SDNC and strong bAbI performance.
- Thoughtful complexity analysis and careful systems engineering (rollback-based BPTT, ANN maintenance), with empirical validation of O(1) memory per step.

Weaknesses and questions
- Differentiability and gradient bias: The top-K truncation and ANN-based selection introduce non-smooth, data-dependent routing; gradients only pass through the selected K rows. This is a biased approximation to full soft attention. While effective empirically, the paper lacks analysis of when/why this bias does not harm optimization, and ablations comparing exact top-K, approximate top-K, and dense softmax on identical problems.
- ANN choice and stability: k-d trees are known to degrade in high dimensions and under online insertions; LSH performance depends on hashing choices. The paper briefly discusses switching between methods and periodic rebuilds, but provides limited quantitative analysis on recall/precision of the ANN during training, rebuild overhead, or sensitivity to M, K, and hyperparameters.
- Write policy expressivity: The “write to previously read or LRU” scheme is simple and efficient, but may be limiting compared to fully content-based writes (especially when a memory slot should be updated even if it has not just been read). An ablation or failure mode analysis would help understand trade-offs.
- Evaluation scope: Beyond bAbI and Omniglot, experiments focus on synthetic tasks. Demonstrations on larger real-world long-context tasks (e.g., document QA without supervision, very long sequence modeling) would further substantiate practical significance.
- Reproducibility details: Key hyperparameters (K, memory word dimension, ANN settings, controller size) are said to be in the supplement; more of this in the main text and code release would help adoption.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Overall technically sound, with careful engineering and convincing empirical validation on scaling and learning. Complexity claims are plausible and supported empirically. The main gap is a theoretical or systematic empirical analysis of the effects of sparse, approximate attention on optimization and final accuracy.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and well-organized. The core mechanisms (sparse read/write, LRU, rollback BPTT) are described clearly. A few notational points could be clarified (e.g., explicit K choices per task; dimensions and ANN configurations). Including pseudocode for rollback and memory management in the main text would aid clarity.
- Originality: The idea of integrating an ANN index inside a MANN to achieve top-K content-based sparse access, coupled with a sparse BPTT rollback scheme, is novel and nontrivial. The approach contrasts with prior RL-based discrete addressing [22] and dense attention [7, 17, 21].
- Significance: High. The work addresses a major bottleneck that has limited the practical use of MANNs, and shows substantial scaling without sacrificing end-to-end training. The SDNC extension and improved bAbI results underscore broader applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: effect of K on performance/speed; exact vs approximate KNN; content-based writes vs current write policy; erase strategy variants.
- Quantify ANN behavior during training: recall vs cost, rebuild frequency/overhead, dimensionality effects, and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Analyze gradient bias: discuss or empirically probe when top-K truncation harms/helps learning; consider alternatives like soft top-K or temperature annealing.
- Broaden evaluation: add at least one non-synthetic long-context task without supervision (e.g., longer document QA or sequence modeling) to demonstrate practical impact.
- Implementation details: include pseudocode for rollback and memory management in the main paper; consider releasing code.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether gradients flow through the softmax weights before top-K truncation or only through normalized weights after selection.
- Report controller size, memory word dimensionality, and K used in each experiment in the main text.
- Discuss numerical stability of usage counters and the threshold parameter for “non-negligible” access.

Arguments for acceptance
- Substantive, novel contribution that materially advances scalability of MANNs.
- Strong empirical results demonstrating both efficiency and learning capability at unprecedented memory sizes and sequence lengths.
- General approach applicable to other architectures (e.g., DNC), with state-of-the-art bAbI results without supervision.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical or thorough empirical analysis of the impact of approximate, sparse attention on optimization and accuracy.
- Dependence on ANN implementation details with limited quantitative characterization.
- Evaluation largely synthetic beyond bAbI/Omniglot; stronger real-world evidence would strengthen the case.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper makes a significant and timely contribution by enabling large-scale external memory with principled sparse access and efficient BPTT, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. The concerns are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution.
