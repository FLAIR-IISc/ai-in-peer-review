- Proposes SAM, a sparse-access MANN that restricts reads/writes to a subset of memory and uses ANN/data structures to scale efficiently.  
- Demonstrates up to three orders of magnitude speedup and large memory savings versus dense NTMs while maintaining comparable task performance.  
- Evaluates SAM on algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot learning, using curriculum learning for scaling experiments.  
- Extends the approach to create a sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) showing broad applicability.  
- Builds on NTMs and Memory Networks, differing from RL-based discrete addressing by using end-to-end gradient optimization.  
- Leverages approximate nearest neighbor search techniques from large-scale similarity search literature.  
- Addresses a key scalability bottleneck in memory-augmented networks.  
- Provides strong empirical results and comprehensive experimental validation across diverse benchmarks.  
- Offers theoretical analysis of time and space complexity, claiming asymptotic optimality.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized.  
- Lacks clear criteria for choosing between randomized k-d trees and LSH for ANN, and underexplores ANN limitations in training loops.  
- Limited comparison to other neural sparsity methods (e.g., weight pruning, dropout).  
- Insufficient analysis of sensitivity to key hyperparameters like K and memory-usage discounting.  
- Does not deeply analyze why sparse models sometimes outperform dense ones on certain tasks (e.g., priority sort).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound solution to a major problem with strong empirical backing and practical impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical analysis could be deeper, ANN implementation discussion is limited, and more analysis on sparsity-driven performance gains is needed.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper deserving acceptance with minor revisions to expand ANN details and analyze sparsity effects.  
- Reviewer rating: Strong Accept (8/10).
