Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that achieves scalable content-based reading and writing by enforcing sparsity: at each step, reads attend to only K memory slots via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search, and writes modify only a constant number of locations (previously read slots and a least-recently-used slot). The authors also introduce an efficient memory-management scheme and a rollback-based BPTT that stores only sparse deltas, yielding O(1) per-step memory overhead and O(log N) read/write time with an ANN index. They demonstrate large wall-clock and memory savings over NTM and dense variants, and show that sparse access does not degrade (and can sometimes help) learning on algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA (with an SDNC variant), and Omniglot one-shot classification. A curriculum allows training with millions of memory slots and sequences of several thousand steps.

The work builds directly on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling capacity from parameters and extending differentiable read/write to large memories. It contrasts with discrete-addressing approaches (e.g., RL-NTM) by retaining end-to-end differentiability, and is conceptually related to top-k attention and approximate KNN used in retrieval-augmented models. The idea of sparse addressing and explicit memory management is also close in spirit to DNC; the paper includes a sparse DNC variant with strong bAbI performance.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: A coherent set of algorithmic and systems ideas (sparse reads/writes, ANN index inside the network, LRU-based allocation, rollback BPTT) that together enable dramatic scaling while preserving differentiability.
- Theoretical claims: Clear complexity story (O(log N) time per step with ANN; O(1) per-step memory for BPTT) with practical data structures; rolling back sparse deltas is simple and effective.
- Empirical evidence: Thorough runtime/memory benchmarks; parity or improvements in learning efficiency vs dense models on NTM-style tasks; substantial curriculum scaling and generalization to very long sequences; competitive bAbI without supervision (SDNC solving all but one); solid Omniglot results that benefit from larger memory.
- Generality: The approach is shown transferable to DNC, suggesting broad applicability to content-addressed MANNs.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Differentiability and gradient bias: The hard top-K selection and ANN lookup introduce discontinuities. Gradients propagate only through the selected K slots; changes in neighbor set are non-differentiable. The paper shows that learning works in practice, but does not analyze the bias introduced or compare against a fully dense soft attention with matched compute. Ablations on K (e.g., K=1,4,8,16) and on exact vs approximate KNN at scale would strengthen the case.
- Optimality claims: “Asymptotically optimal” time/space under “reasonable assumptions” is deferred to the supplement. The exact assumptions (e.g., constant K, RAM model, constraints on content-based access) should be stated in the main text, and the lower-bound argument summarized to calibrate the claim.
- Memory management: The LRU-based allocation with a fixed significance threshold may erase rarely accessed but still relevant information. There is no analysis of catastrophic overwrites or retention under long delays. Simple diagnostics (distribution of reuse intervals, error vs threshold, replacing LRU with a learned allocator) would help.
- Impact of ANN maintenance: Rebuilding the index every N insertions and maintaining it online can be nontrivial in wall-clock time and may be CPU-bound. The benchmarks should make the hardware, CPU/GPU placement, and index maintenance time explicit, and report end-to-end training throughput with/without ANN.
- Fairness of comparisons: In curriculum experiments, dense baselines use 64 slots while SAM uses up to 2e6 slots, chosen to equalize physical memory use over 100 steps. This demonstrates scaling, but conflates architectural merit with capacity. A complementary comparison that fixes memory size (e.g., both at 1k or 4k slots) and varies compute would be informative.
- Reproducibility and reliance on supplement: Many crucial details (usage data structure, rollback, SDNC, full bAbI results, training setup) are in the supplement. The main text would benefit from more self-contained algorithmic descriptions or pseudocode, and an explicit release of code would be valuable.

Quality:
Overall technically sound and thoughtfully engineered. The complexity arguments make sense; the sparse-delta rollback for BPTT is a clean idea. The main missing piece is a more systematic study of the approximation effects (top-K and ANN) on gradients and accuracy. Experimental coverage is good but leans heavily on synthetic tasks; the real-data result (Omniglot) is positive but not large-scale.

Clarity:
Generally well-written and easy to follow. The controller, read, write, and memory-management interactions are clearly described. However, several important definitions are only in the supplement (e.g., the exact data structures, ANN configuration, and lower-bound assumptions). Equations for writing/erasing could use a brief intuitive explanation in-text, and a small block diagram/pseudocode for the backward rollback would improve readability.

Originality:
The combination of sparse top-K content-based reads with ANN, constant-size sparse writes with LRU allocation, and rollback BPTT within a differentiable MANN is novel and impactful in 2016. While each ingredient has antecedents, the integrated design and demonstration at million-scale memory is new.

Significance:
High. Training differentiable controllers with very large, content-addressable memories is important for long-context reasoning, algorithmic tasks, and retrieval-augmented learning. The approach is broadly applicable (shown via SDNC) and delivers large practical speed/space gains, which should catalyze further research and adoption.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablations: K sensitivity; exact vs approximate KNN at various N; effect of index rebuild frequency; CPU/GPU placement.
- Summarize the optimality assumptions and lower-bound intuition in the main paper.
- Analyze retention: how often is useful information overwritten by LRU? Try a learned or hybrid allocation policy.
- Report end-to-end throughput including ANN maintenance; clarify hardware and implementation details.
- Include per-task bAbI results in the main paper; compare to DNC in the same setting.
- Discuss alternatives to hard top-K (e.g., sparsemax/top-k softmax) and whether they help stability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important bottleneck with a clear, general solution.
- Strong empirical speed/memory benefits with maintained accuracy.
- Demonstrated scalability to very large memories and long sequences.
- Likely to influence subsequent MANN designs.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited analysis of approximation-induced gradient bias and stability.
- Heavy reliance on supplement; some comparisons could be more controlled.
- Real-world evaluation is modest in scope.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a coherent and practical advance in scalable differentiable memory with strong empirical and engineering contributions.
