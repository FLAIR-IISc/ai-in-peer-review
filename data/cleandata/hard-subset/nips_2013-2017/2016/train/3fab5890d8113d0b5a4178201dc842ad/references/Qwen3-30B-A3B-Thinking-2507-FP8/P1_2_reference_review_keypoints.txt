- Introduces SAM, a sparse-access MANN that constrains reads/writes to a few words, yielding O(log N) time and O(1) space per timestep and up to 1600x speedup with comparable performance.

- Validates SAM across synthetic tasks (Copy, Associative Recall, Priority Sort), Babi reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification.

- Significant practical contribution: solves a key scalability bottleneck in MANNs enabling large-scale memory use.

- Strong theoretical foundation: rigorous analysis showing asymptotic optimality of time/space complexity.

- Comprehensive experiments and curriculum learning strategy convincingly demonstrate empirical effectiveness.

- Generalizability: sparse-access approach applied to other architectures (SDNC) and achieves state-of-the-art on Babi without supervised memory access.

- Clear presentation with well-organized math, useful figures, and reproducibility details.

- Limited analysis of the sparse parameter K; lacks ablation on K’s impact across tasks and memory sizes.

- Superficial comparison of ANN implementations (k-d trees vs LSH) without clear rationale for choices or trade-offs.

- Usage statistic Ut is unclearly defined and the choice of U^(2)_T for SAM is not well justified.

- Insufficiently prominent comparison to recent related work (e.g., DNC) in the main text.

- Limited evaluation on truly extreme-scale real-world datasets, leaving practical limits somewhat unexplored.

- Overall assessment: high-quality, original, and practically significant work; minor weaknesses do not outweigh contributions.

- Recommendation: Accept.
