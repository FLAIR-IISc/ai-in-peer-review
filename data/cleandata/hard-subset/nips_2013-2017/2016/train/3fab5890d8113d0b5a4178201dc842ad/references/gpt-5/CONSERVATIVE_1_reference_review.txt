Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that performs sparse reads and writes to an external memory and uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indices for content-based addressing. The core claims are: (i) forward/backward time complexity O(T log N) and per-step space O(1), independent of the number of memory slots N (with O(N) init); (ii) an efficient “rollback” scheme for BPTT that stores only sparse deltas; (iii) a simple memory management policy combining updates to recently read locations with overwriting the least-recently-used (LRU) slot. The authors report large speed/memory gains over NTMs and comparable or better data efficiency on synthetic tasks, improved curriculum scaling to much longer sequences, near state-of-the-art performance on bAbI without supervision (via a sparse DNC variant in the supplement), and improved one-shot classification on Omniglot.

Relation to prior work
SAM builds on NTMs and Memory Networks by retaining differentiable content-based addressing but enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes. The sparse read relies on ANN (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to retrieve top-K neighbors; gradients are propagated only through K items. The write policy combines updating previously read locations with LRU-based allocation, akin to usage-based allocation in DNC. The reinforcement learning approach to discrete addressing (RL-NTM) is mentioned but not used, favoring continuous training stability. While sparse attention mechanisms and alternative differentiable approximations (e.g., sparsemax) exist, this work specifically targets scaling MANN controllers with external memory and contributes an engineering path to sublinear addressing.

Strengths
- Clear scalability target with plausible algorithmic ingredients (top-K reads via ANN; LRU allocation; sparse delta rollback for BPTT).
- Empirical evidence of dramatic runtime and memory reductions compared to dense NTMs, including million-word memories and O(1) per-step memory usage in practice.
- SAM maintains or improves learning efficiency on algorithmic tasks; demonstrates curriculum scaling to thousands of steps without truncation.
- The SDNC extension (supplement) indicates generality across MANN variants and strong bAbI performance without memory supervision.
- The presentation of complexity targets and practical data structures is valuable for practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Gradient fidelity: By zeroing all but K read weights and blocking gradients through the ANN, the method optimizes a biased objective. There is no theoretical analysis of the effect of this bias on convergence or solution quality, beyond empirical results. The claim of “retaining end-to-end gradient-based optimization” is correct mechanically, but gradient truncation to K entries is a substantial modeling change that requires more justification.
- Sensitivity and ablations: The paper lacks systematic ablations on key hyperparameters (K, threshold ε for usage, ANN rebuild frequency, k-d tree vs LSH) and on the write policy (e.g., degree of interpolation, dependence on LRU vs other usage metrics). This makes robustness claims difficult to assess.
- Fairness of baselines: Some comparisons fix physical memory budgets rather than controlling for algorithmic capacity. Dense baselines are restricted to 64 slots while SAM uses up to 2e6. While this highlights scaling, it complicates claims about data efficiency and algorithmic performance. Stronger baselines (e.g., DNC with proper allocation strategies, key–value memory networks, or more recent memory models) should be in the main text, not only in the supplement.
- Real-world validation: Beyond Omniglot episodes and bAbI, there are no large-scale, real-world sequential tasks (e.g., long-context language modeling, real QA on Wikipedia, code/dataflow tasks). The primary evidence remains synthetic or small-scale, which limits confidence that the method remains effective under realistic noise and distributional complexity.
- Practical overhead and implementation detail: The need to rebuild ANN indices periodically and keep them synchronized with frequent writes could introduce nontrivial constants and engineering complexity. The paper defers many details (data structure maintenance, memory rollback proofs, ANN update costs) to the supplement; bringing key guarantees and empirical overheads into the main text would improve credibility.
- “Optimality” claim: The assertion of asymptotic optimality (Supplement A) depends on assumptions about content-based access that are not fully spelled out in the main text. A clear theorem statement and comparison to known lower bounds would help.

Quality
Technically sound at the engineering level, with convincing speed/space measurements and reasonable training outcomes on standard NTM tasks and Omniglot. However, the lack of analysis on the gradient bias introduced by sparsification and limited ablations weaken the support for general claims.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, but over-reliant on the supplement for critical algorithmic and empirical details (rollback mechanics, SDNC results, ANN maintenance). Including a concise statement of the optimality assumptions and a schematic of the ANN update protocol would help.

Originality
Combines known components (top-K attention, ANN search, LRU-style allocation) in a MANN with an efficient BPTT rollback mechanism. While not conceptually radical, the integration into a practical, trainable system at scale is a useful contribution.

Significance
If robust, the approach substantially lowers the computational barrier to scaling MANNs and could enable research on longer-horizon reasoning. Current evidence is promising but concentrated on small/synthetic tasks.

Questions for the authors
- Provide ablations for K, ε, write gates, and ANN type. How does performance and training stability change?
- Quantify the cost of ANN maintenance (insertion/deletion/rebuild) as a fraction of training time, across N and word dimension.
- Clarify the “optimality” assumptions and include a formal statement in the main text.
- Analyze the gradient bias due to sparse reads/writes. Are there conditions under which the bias vanishes (e.g., when top-K captures most mass)?
- How often does LRU overwriting harm tasks requiring very long retention? Any catastrophic forgetting measurements?
- Can you report results on a more realistic long-context task (e.g., character-level LM with long BPTT, or QA on larger corpora)?

Pros
- Strong efficiency gains with clear engineering pathway.
- Comparable or improved learning on benchmark algorithmic tasks.
- Generality demonstrated via SDNC.
- Straightforward to implement with common ANN libraries.

Cons
- Limited theoretical treatment of approximation effects on learning.
- Sparse ablations; robustness unclear.
- Comparisons to stronger baselines and real-world tasks are limited.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for key evidence.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper addresses a relevant scalability bottleneck for MANNs with a practical and apparently effective approach. Despite reservations about gradient bias and limited ablations/real-world validation, the engineering contribution and empirical efficiency gains are significant and likely to be useful to the community.

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high.
