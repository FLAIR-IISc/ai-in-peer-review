This paper introduces SAM, a sparse access memory architecture aimed at scaling memory-augmented neural networks. The core idea – restricting read/write operations to a sparse subset of memory – is a reasonable approach to address the memory and computational bottlenecks of traditional MANNs like NTMs. The authors demonstrate promising results, showing significant speedups and memory savings compared to dense NTMs, alongside maintained or even improved performance on several tasks, including scaled versions using curriculum learning.

However, while the core concept and initial results are encouraging, the paper suffers from a multitude of inconsistencies, omissions, and a frustrating lack of thoroughness that, despite the potential significance of the work, lead me to recommend rejection. My concerns span methodology, presentation, and a general impression of incomplete validation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical problem:** The scaling issues of MANNs are well-recognized, and this paper tackles a central challenge.
*   **Promising speedups & memory savings:** The reported 3 orders of magnitude improvement in runtime and memory overhead is substantial.
*   **Competitive performance:** Maintaining comparable, or improving, performance on the tested tasks is a positive sign.
*   **Generality:** The extension to a sparse DNC (SDNC) suggests the approach has broader applicability.

**Weaknesses (Extensive):**

1.  **Lack of Rigorous Baselines:** While the authors compare against NTM and DAM, critical modern baselines are missing. For example, a comparison with recent work utilizing differentiable sparse structures (e.g., sparse transformers) would be highly relevant.  The choice of DAM as a dense baseline is questionable; it isn't a widely-adopted standard and seems specifically constructed for comparison, potentially downplaying the improvements.
2.  **Ambiguous Experimental Setup:**  Hyperparameter details are relegated to the supplementary material (C), which is unacceptable for crucial details like learning rates, batch sizes, controller size, and memory word size *across all experiments*. Reproducibility is severely hampered. The authors state "full hyperparameter details" are in the supplement, yet details such as the exact optimizer used are missing, making replication impossible.
3.  **Inconsistent ANN Implementation:** The choice between FLANN and LSH for approximate nearest neighbors seems arbitrary. The criteria for switching between them (word size) is not clearly justified, nor is a comparative analysis provided. Which ANN was used for *which* task/experiment is unclear, impacting interpretation of results.
4.  **Usage Statistic Definition:** The paper presents two definitions for the memory usage statistic *U<sub>t</sub>*. The rationale for choosing the second definition (time since last non-negligible access) *for SAM* versus the first (time-discounted sum) for DAM is weakly justified. It feels like a post-hoc choice to improve SAM's performance.
5.  **Vague "Custom Data Structure":** The repeated mention of "custom data structures" (for usage tracking and sparse matrix operations) without *any* detail is unacceptable.  Are these highly optimized implementations? Is there a performance trade-off? Without concrete information, it’s impossible to assess the contribution. Reference to “Supplementary A” is insufficient; a detailed description is needed in the main paper or a clearly identified appendix.
6.  **Inconsistent Terminology/Notation:** The use of tildes (̃) to denote sparse vectors is inconsistent. Sometimes they're used, sometimes they aren't, creating confusion.  Equation (5) uses *t* as both a time step and an interpolation gate parameter, a poor choice of notation.
7.  **Scaling Concerns:** While the authors demonstrate scaling with curriculum learning, the sequence lengths achieved are still relatively modest. The claim of BPTT for 4000 steps is notable, but the memory requirements for this are not explicitly addressed, particularly considering potential gradient explosion issues. 
8.  **Overstated Claims:**  Phrases like "optimal in space and time" (introduction) and "negligible fraction of non-zero error gradient" (Section 3.1) are overly strong and lack rigorous justification.
9.  **Formatting/Presentation:** Numerous minor errors are present, including inconsistent spacing, awkward phrasing, and a generally unpolished presentation.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Potentially significant improvements in scalability for MANNs.
*   Promising empirical results.
*   Generality demonstrated with the SDNC.

**Con:**

*   Numerous methodological inconsistencies and omissions.
*   Lack of thorough experimental validation with strong baselines.
*   Unclear implementation details hindering reproducibility.
*   Overstated claims and a lack of rigorous analysis.
*   Poor presentation and numerous minor errors.



**Overall:**

Despite the novelty of the core idea, the numerous shortcomings detailed above outweigh the strengths. The paper feels rushed and incomplete. A significant amount of additional experimentation, clarification, and polish are needed before this work could be considered suitable for NIPS. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit in the future.
