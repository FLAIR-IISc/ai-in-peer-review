Summary
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that enforces sparsity in both reads and writes, and uses approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) indices to implement content-based addressing in O(log N) time. It introduces an in-place sparse update/rollback scheme to avoid storing full memory snapshots for BPTT, claiming O(1) space per time step and O(T log N) time per sequence. The model writes either to previously read locations or to the least-recently-used slot. Empirically, SAM shows large speed and memory savings over dense NTMs on synthetic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), scales via curriculum to millions of slots and thousands of steps, and achieves competitive results on bAbI and episodic Omniglot. The authors also sketch a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC), reporting large speedups and strong bAbI results without supervised memory access.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and important limitation of MANNs: scaling memory size without prohibitive time/space.
- Clear, practical engineering contributions: sparse read/write, ANN-based content lookup, and an efficient rollback strategy for BPTT.
- Strong computational results: up to three orders of magnitude speed/memory improvements over NTMs at large N; empirical memory footprint consistent with claimed asymptotics.
- Demonstrates that sparse approximations can preserve or even improve learning on several tasks; trains on very long sequences and large memories that are out of reach for dense MANNs.
- Claims generality by applying the idea to DNC (SDNC) with encouraging bAbI performance.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “End-to-end” differentiability claims seem overstated. Both the top-K read selection (via ANN) and the hard least-recently-used allocation use discrete argmin/top-K operations, and gradients do not flow through the selection mechanism or the ANN. This is a substantive deviation from the fully differentiable addressing in NTM/DNC and should be clearly acknowledged and analyzed.
- Limited ablations. There is no systematic study of:
  - Effect of K (sparsity level) on performance/efficiency and stability.
  - Choice of ANN method (k-d trees vs LSH), rebuild frequency, and their impact on accuracy and training dynamics.
  - Write policy (previously-read vs LRU) versus content-based write with sparsity.
- Fairness of comparisons. In the curriculum scaling experiments, SAM uses up to 2×10^6 memory slots while dense baselines use 64, justified by equal physical memory usage. This conflates implementation efficiency with algorithmic capacity; more informative baselines would match memory capacity (to the extent feasible), or at least provide careful analysis to disentangle effects.
- Statistical rigor. Many plots appear to be single runs; selection of the “best run” on Omniglot raises concerns about variance. No error bars, seeds, or run-to-run variability are reported, making it hard to assess robustness.
- Evaluation scope. Heavy reliance on synthetic tasks and bAbI; Omniglot setup differs from prior art (e.g., one-hot labels, curriculum), impeding direct comparison. Stronger baselines (e.g., DNC with matched compute/memory, End-to-End Memory Networks without supervision, Santoro et al.’s MANN, and contemporary few-shot methods) should be more thoroughly compared under controlled settings.
- Theoretical claims. The “asymptotically optimal” statement (under assumptions) is deferred to the supplement; the main text does not clearly state assumptions or bounds. There is no analysis of approximation error introduced by sparse/ANN reads and hard allocation, or of stability when top-K neighborhoods change during training.
- Reproducibility. The approach depends on nontrivial systems components (ANN indices, custom usage tracking, rollback). There is no mention of code release; details needed for faithful reproduction (hyperparameters, ANN tuning, rebuild schedule, optimizer settings) are largely in the supplement.

Quality
Technically, the engineering is thoughtful and plausibly correct; the space/time complexity improvements are convincing empirically. However, important claims (end-to-end differentiability, optimality) are not fully supported in the main text, and the experimental validation lacks ablations and statistical rigor. The discrete components and ANN approximations deserve a careful error/stability analysis.

Clarity
The high-level description is clear and the motivation is strong. Nonetheless, key choices (hard LRU, ANN integration, rollback mechanics) need more explicit discussion of their implications for gradients and learning. Many crucial details are deferred to the supplement; the main paper would benefit from succinct summaries of assumptions and guarantees.

Originality
Using sparse reads/writes with ANN for content-based memory access and an efficient rollback mechanism is a useful combination; to my knowledge, integrating ANN indices into a trainable MANN is novel. The write policy (previously-read + LRU) is simple but not novel per se. Overall, the contribution is more systems/engineering than algorithmic.

Significance
If the approach generalizes, the potential impact is high: scaling MANNs to millions of slots with tractable training is valuable. However, current evidence is limited to synthetic tasks, bAbI, and a variant of Omniglot with nonstandard setup. More rigorous comparisons and ablations are needed to establish reliability and generality.

Questions for the authors
- Please precisely state the “optimality” theorem and its assumptions in the main paper. What lower bound model is used?
- How sensitive is performance to K? Is there a clear trade-off curve for accuracy vs efficiency?
- How often do ANN selection changes cause training instabilities? Any mitigation (e.g., soft neighborhoods)?
- What is the impact of the hard LRU choice on learning compared to differentiable allocation (as in DNC)?
- Can you report results with multiple seeds and error bars, especially for curriculum and Omniglot?
- For bAbI, please provide complete numbers (accuracy per task), training protocol details, and comparisons to strong unsupervised-retrieval baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with large empirical speed/space gains.
- Conceptually simple, practical, and broadly applicable to MANNs.
- Shows nontrivial capability on long-sequence tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Claims of end-to-end differentiability and optimality are not fully supported; key components are discrete/approximate.
- Limited ablations and statistical rigor; fairness of baselines questionable.
- Evaluation largely on synthetic/toy tasks; stronger real-world validation is needed.

Recommendation: Weak reject
I appreciate the practical significance and the promising results, but given the reliance on approximations/discrete choices without sufficient analysis, limited ablations, and concerns about evaluation rigor and comparability, I do not think the paper yet meets the bar for NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical study (ablations, variance, fair baselines), clarifying theoretical claims, and better analyzing the approximation effects would change my assessment.
