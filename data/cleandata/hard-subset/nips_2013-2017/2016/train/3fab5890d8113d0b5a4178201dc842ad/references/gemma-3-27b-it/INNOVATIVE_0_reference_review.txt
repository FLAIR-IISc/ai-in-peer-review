This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs): the computational and memory overhead associated with large external memories. The authors propose SAM, a sparse access memory architecture, and demonstrate significant improvements in both speed and memory usage compared to standard NTMs, while maintaining competitive performance on a range of tasks. This is a compelling contribution, particularly given the increasing interest in MANNs for tasks requiring long-term dependencies and reasoning.

The core idea – restricting read/write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations – is elegant and intuitively addresses the scalability issue. The use of approximate nearest neighbor search further enhances efficiency. The empirical results, showing up to three orders of magnitude improvement in runtime and substantial memory savings, are impressive. Scaling tasks via curriculum learning and achieving strong results on Babi and Omniglot demonstrates the practical potential of the approach. The extension to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) is a nice demonstration of the generality of the proposed technique.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The sparse access mechanism is a genuinely novel approach to tackling the scalability problem of MANNs. While sparsity has been explored in other contexts, applying it specifically to memory access in this manner, coupled with the efficient data structures, is a significant contribution.
*   **Practical Impact:** The demonstrated speedups and memory savings are substantial and directly address a key barrier to wider adoption of MANNs. This makes the work highly relevant to the community.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments, including established benchmarks (NTM tasks, Babi) and a more challenging real-world dataset (Omniglot). The scaling experiments are particularly convincing.
*   **Generality:** Showing how to apply the sparsity technique to the DNC highlights the broader applicability of the approach.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of SAM.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **ANN Sensitivity:** The performance gains with the approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index are significant, but the choice of ANN (k-d trees vs LSH) seems data-dependent. Further investigation into the robustness of the approach to different ANN implementations and parameter settings would be valuable.
*   **Usage Statistic Choice:** The comparison between the two usage statistic definitions (U(1) and U(2)) is brief. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different usage metrics on performance could be insightful.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors mention asymptotic optimality, a more formal theoretical analysis of the computational complexity and representational capacity of SAM would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While hyperparameters are reported, a more systematic exploration of their influence on performance would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and impactful solution to a critical problem in MANNs.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant practical advantages.
*   The generality of the approach is well-illustrated.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The ANN component introduces a potential source of instability or sensitivity.
*   The theoretical analysis is limited.
*   The impact of different usage statistics is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a fundamental challenge in the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors present a novel and effective solution with compelling empirical results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the overall contribution is significant and outweighs them. I believe this paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS program and recommend **acceptance**. The boldness of tackling the scalability issue directly and the promising results warrant prioritizing novelty over complete methodological perfection. The work opens up exciting avenues for future research in large-scale memory networks.
