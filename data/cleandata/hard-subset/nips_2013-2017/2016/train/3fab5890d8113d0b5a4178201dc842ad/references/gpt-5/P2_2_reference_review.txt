Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales external memory access by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes. Key ideas: (i) impose K-sparse attention over memory words via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search (k-d trees or LSH) to obtain O(log N) time reads; (ii) constrain writes to a small, fixed number of locations (previously read slots and a least-recently-used slot) with a simple usage-based allocator; (iii) implement backpropagation through time by rolling forward sparse in-place modifications and rolling them back during the backward pass, reducing space from O(NT) to O(1) per step. Under reasonable assumptions (in the supplement) SAM is argued to be asymptotically optimal in time and space among content-addressed MANNs. The approach is shown to generalize to a sparse DNC (SDNC).

SAM is evaluated against dense NTMs and a dense proxy (DAM) on synthetic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling to very long sequences and large memory, bAbI QA tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. Results show comparable or better learning than dense baselines, with 100–1600× computational speedups and memory usage per step independent of N. The SDNC attains near–state-of-the-art on bAbI without supervised memory retrieval.

The work builds directly on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling parameter count from memory size, but addresses a central bottleneck in those models: O(N) dense attention and O(NT) BPTT memory. It contrasts with discrete addressing approaches trained via RL (e.g., RL-NTM) by keeping end-to-end differentiability while achieving sparsity.

Evaluation by review criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The proposed sparse read/write scheme and usage-based allocator are clearly defined. The complexity analysis is plausible; the main asymptotic claims and data structures are in the supplement, but the forward/backward mechanics and O(1) per-step space via reversible updates are convincing and standard in sparse-delta checkpointing. Using ANN indices for content-based addressing with no gradients through the index is reasonable since the query/key similarity is still differentiable; the index provides a fixed, approximate candidate set.
- Empirical support: The paper provides substantial empirical evidence. Benchmarks show the intended time/space gains. On learning tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), SAM matches or exceeds dense baselines. The curriculum experiments demonstrate training with millions of memory slots and thousands of timesteps without truncated BPTT and generalization far beyond training lengths. bAbI results are competitive; SDNC achieves the best reported unsupervised-access result. Omniglot episodes show advantages when capacity matters.
- Caveats: (i) The effect of approximation (ANN vs exact KNN), the choice of K, and sensitivity to index hyperparameters are not thoroughly ablated. (ii) The simple write policy (previously read + LRU) may limit expressivity in tasks requiring writing to novel content not recently read; empirical evidence mitigates but does not rule this out. (iii) Some core claims (optimality) and engineering details (data structures) are relegated to the supplement, and code availability is not stated.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The read/write mechanisms, controller interface, and BPTT scheme are described precisely, with equations and intuitive rationale. The role of the ANN index is explained (no gradients, synchronized with memory).
- Suggestions: Briefly restate the assumptions behind “asymptotic optimality” in the main text, and include an ablation on K and ANN type in the main paper. Clarify fairness in capacity comparisons (dense models with 64 slots vs sparse with 2e6 slots) and report performance at equal task difficulty for smaller N where all models fit.

Originality
- The core novelty is combining sparse attention and writes with efficient ANN-based content addressing in an end-to-end differentiable MANN, plus an O(1)-space BPTT implementation via reversible sparse updates and a simple, effective memory allocator. While sparsity and ANN search are known techniques, their integration into a trainable external memory with rigorous attention to time/space complexity and demonstrated scaling is novel and well-executed. The extension to SDNC further shows generality.
- Prior related ideas (e.g., discrete addressing with RL) tackle scalability differently; SAM’s differentiable sparse access is a distinct design choice.

Significance
- The contribution is important: it directly targets the key obstacle to making MANNs practical on long sequences and large memories. The empirical improvements (orders-of-magnitude speed/space savings) and ability to train with millions of memory slots substantially advance the practicality of these models. The results on bAbI and Omniglot indicate that the sparse approximations do not degrade learning, and sometimes help.
- Likely impact: Researchers building neural memory systems will adopt these design patterns (sparse reads/writes, reversible sparse BPTT, usage-based allocation, ANN in-the-loop). The generality to other MANNs (DNC) increases impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled approach to reducing time and space complexity with strong theoretical motivation.
- Large empirical speed/space gains without sacrificing performance; scaling to very long sequences.
- Generalizable method (works for DNC) and broad evaluation across synthetic and real tasks.
- Simple, effective allocator and write scheme.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations on ANN approximation, K, and write policy; lack of analysis on failure modes where dense blending might be required.
- Some comparisons confound capacity (very small dense memory vs very large sparse memory), making it hard to disentangle algorithmic advantages from capacity.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for proofs/details; reproducibility might hinge on nontrivial engineering (custom data structures).
- Limited evaluation on large real-world domains (e.g., long documents, large-scale QA).

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to K and to the choice/parameters of the ANN index? Is there a quality/speed tradeoff curve?
- Can SAM handle tasks that require aggregating many memories (greater than K) in a single step?
- How robust is the LRU-based allocator under distribution shift and catastrophic overwriting?
- Will you release code for the data structures and ANN integration?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and timely contribution that materially advances scalability of MANNs.
- Sound method, well-motivated complexity claims, and convincing empirical results.
- Broad applicability and clear path for others to build upon.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing ablations and some fairness issues in comparisons.
- Practical engineering details moved to the supplement; reproducibility may be challenging.
- Limited exploration of domains where dense attention may be necessary.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria well: it is technically solid, clearly written, proposes a novel and important approach to a significant problem, and supports claims with strong experiments. Addressing the suggested ablations and adding clarity on assumptions would further strengthen it.
