- Introduces SAM: a MANN that constrains reads/writes to a small sparse subset (K=4–8) while preserving performance.  
- Uses approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) data structures for content-based addressing, reducing per-step time from O(N) to O(log N).  
- Adds a memory management scheme that tracks usage to efficiently allocate new memory slots.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification with up to 1600× speedup over NTMs.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical complexity analysis showing asymptotic optimality in time and space.  
- Experiments are comprehensive and include thorough comparisons to dense baselines (DAM, NTM) and LSTM, with convincing memory/runtime benchmarks.  
- Authors acknowledge practical limitations like ANN parameter tuning and potential ANN imbalance.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and mathematically consistent; architecture and experiments are transparent and reproducible.  
- Original contribution: novel application of sparse memory access plus efficient data structures to MANNs, distinct from prior sparse-attention or MANN work.  
- Shows significant practical impact by enabling training with memory sizes (1M+ slots) and sequence lengths previously infeasible.  
- Strengths: solid theory, broad experimental validation, demonstrated scalability, practical ANN-based implementation, strong performance across tasks.  
- Weaknesses: limited exploration of extremely long sequences (>4000 steps), insufficient comparison of ANN implementations (k-d trees vs LSH), no analysis of memory utilization patterns, and incomplete study of K vs performance trade-offs.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — technically strong, well-executed, and likely to influence future MANN research.
