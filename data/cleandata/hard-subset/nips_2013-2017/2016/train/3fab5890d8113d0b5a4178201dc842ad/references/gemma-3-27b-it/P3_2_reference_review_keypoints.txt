- Overall assessment: Strong, well-executed contribution recommending acceptance.  
- Problem addressed: MANNs suffer from quadratic compute/memory cost due to dense memory access.  
- Core idea: Sparse Access Memory (SAM) restricts reads/writes to a sparse subset of locations to reduce overhead.  
- ANN usage: Incorporates approximate nearest neighbor search to optimize sparse access.  
- Empirical results: Large speedups (up to three orders of magnitude) and memory savings while maintaining performance.  
- Tasks evaluated: Validated on NTM algorithmic tasks, bAbI reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification; scales to long sequences.  
- Theoretical grounding: Claims asymptotic optimality in time and space (detailed in supplementary).  
- Implementation strengths: Efficient backprop without caching full memory, sparse matrix formats, and usage-based write scheme.  
- Generalizability: Technique applied to Sparse DNC, demonstrating broader applicability.  
- Clarity: Paper is clear, well-written, with helpful supplementary material.  
- Weakness — hyperparameters: Needs more analysis of sensitivity to K and usage-threshold parameters.  
- Weakness — ANN details: Choice between FLANN and LSH seems arbitrary; cost of rebuilding ANN not fully accounted for.  
- Weakness — comparisons: Limited discussion vs. other scaling techniques (e.g., RL-based discrete addressing).  
- Weakness — curriculum: Curriculum learning rules lack clear specification.  
- Originality: Sparsity in memory access not wholly novel, but the specific combination of sparse reads/writes, efficient backprop, and ANN is novel and effective.  
- Significance: Enables applying MANNs to larger, more realistic problems by overcoming major scaling bottlenecks.  
- For acceptance: Addresses major bottleneck, shows major performance gains, validated across tasks, novel/effective technique, broad applicability.  
- Against acceptance: Needs deeper hyperparameter analysis, more ANN implementation/runtime accounting, and broader comparisons.
