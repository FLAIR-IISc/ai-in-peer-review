- Proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM): sparse reads/writes + ANN content addressing for scalable MANNs.  
- Sparse reads via top-K content attention using ANN (randomized k-d trees or LSH) with O(log N) access time.  
- Sparse writes either update previously read locations or overwrite LRU slot, tracked in O(1) time.  
- Sparse BPTT stores and reverts only sparse memory modifications, giving O(1) per-step memory overhead independent of N.  
- Empirical results show large runtime/memory gains (up to 100–1600× faster) with comparable task performance to dense NTMs.  
- Demonstrates training stability on very long sequences (BPTT >4k steps) and scalability to millions of slots.  
- Broad evaluation: algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA (SDNC variant), and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- SDNC supplement indicates the sparsification approach generalizes to other MANNs like DNC.  
- Technical novelty: coherent systems-level contributions (sparse attention, LRU writes, ANN integration, sparse rollback).  
- Theoretical clarity: clear asymptotic claims and practical implementation details (CSR formats, ANN rebuild policy).  
- Strength of sparse rollback technique likely of broad interest.  
- Weakness—gradient bias: truncating attention and ANN approximation may induce biased/noisy gradients; lacks analysis.  
- Weakness—fairness: some comparisons use vastly different memory sizes (e.g., 64 vs 2e6 slots), conflating algorithmic ability with hardware efficiency.  
- Weakness—empirical breadth: limited real-world benchmarks beyond Omniglot; suggests long-document QA or language modeling tasks.  
- Weakness—ANN overhead: rebuild frequency and amortized cost/sensitivity to imbalance not quantified.  
- Clarity issues: important details (proof sketch, O(1) usage data structure, optimizer schedules, SDNC) are in supplement; notation could better distinguish dense vs sparse weights.  
- Originality: combination of methods is novel and well-motivated; write policy relates to DNC allocation but is acknowledged.  
- Significance: addresses central MANN scalability bottleneck with 2–3 orders-of-magnitude empirical gains; likely high impact.  
- Additional empirical questions: sensitivity to K and usage threshold epsilon; catastrophic forgetting with LRU; exact KNN vs ANN discrepancy; gradient flow early in training and any warm-up heuristics; optimizer/gradient clipping used.  
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, strong empirical gains, generalizable method, clear exposition and ablations.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited analysis of ANN/truncation impact, fairness of baselines, and real-world evaluations; some details deferred to supplement.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested revisions to clarify assumptions, broaden baselines, and add ANN/truncation ablations.
