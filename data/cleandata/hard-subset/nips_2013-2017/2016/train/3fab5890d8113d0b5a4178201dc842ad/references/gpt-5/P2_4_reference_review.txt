Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales content-addressable read and write operations to very large external memories by enforcing sparsity and using efficient data structures. Concretely: (1) Reads are restricted to the top-K nearest memory slots retrieved via an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), yielding O(log N) query time. (2) Writes are sparse and targeted either to locations read in the previous step (to update relevant content) or to the least-recently-used (LRU) slot (to allocate), with constant-time cost per step. (3) Backpropagation through time is made memory-efficient by storing only sparse modifications and “rolling back” the memory during the backward pass, giving O(1) space per time step (O(T) total) instead of O(NT). The authors argue asymptotic optimality (under assumptions) in time/space for content-addressed memory. They show large empirical speed and memory savings relative to dense NTMs, while maintaining performance on algorithmic tasks, scaling via curriculum to millions of slots and thousands of time steps, achieving strong results on bAbI, and improved one-shot classification on Omniglot. They also sketch a sparse variant of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) with strong bAbI results.

This work is a direct response to the known scalability limitations of NTMs and related memory networks [Graves et al. 2014; Weston et al. 2015], and is complementary to discrete-addressing approaches trained by RL [Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015]. It advances engineering and algorithmic design by integrating ANN search and LRU-style allocation into a differentiable memory system.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled scalability story: O(log N) time per step using ANN and O(1) per-step memory via sparse rollback. The engineering insight (CSR, in-place updates, index maintenance) is valuable.
- Significant empirical gains: 100–1600× speedups over NTM and orders-of-magnitude memory savings; training with millions of memory slots and BPTT over >4000 steps; impressive generalization on associative recall (to 200k length).
- Broad validation: classic NTM tasks, curriculum scaling, bAbI (including SDNC), and Omniglot one-shot. Sparse and dense variants perform comparably where memory is not the bottleneck; sparse excels where capacity matters.
- Generality: Ideas apply to multiple MANNs; authors demonstrate SDNC as a case study.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Differentiability and learning signal: Selecting top-K reads (via ANN) and LRU writes introduces non-differentiable, piecewise-constant choices. Gradients flow only to K selected slots; the index and allocation policy receive no gradient. While experiments are positive, a discussion of potential bias/optimization issues and when it fails would strengthen the work.
- Fairness and breadth of comparisons: Some comparisons give SAM vastly larger memories (e.g., 2e6 vs 64) to match physical RAM, which advantages SAM on tasks where capacity dominates. This is reasonable for a scaling paper, but it would help to include (a) NTMs with truncated BPTT and larger memories until OOM, (b) DNC comparisons in the main text (beyond the supplement), and (c) ablations showing sensitivity to K, the threshold ε for usage, and the interpolation/write gates.
- ANN maintenance and amortized costs: The index is rebuilt every N insertions. The amortized O(log N) claim is plausible but depends on update patterns and dimensionality; empirical profiling of rebuild cost and sensitivity to tree imbalance vs LSH parameters would be helpful, especially in fully online regimes.
- “Optimality” claim: The asymptotic optimality in Supplementary A depends on assumptions (e.g., content-based addressing requiring nearest-neighbor-type queries). Please make those assumptions explicit in the main paper to temper the claim.
- Reproducibility: Many crucial details (data structures, hyperparameters, index choices, rebuild frequency) are in the supplement; code availability is not mentioned. Given the engineering complexity, open-sourcing would be important for impact.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound and thoughtfully engineered. The complexity arguments are sensible, and the speed/space measurements substantiate them. Experimental coverage is broad. However, the paper would benefit from deeper ablations (K, ANN type, ε, allocation strategy), stability analysis for approximate/top-K reads, and clearer accounting of amortized costs including periodic rebuilds. Claims are generally well-supported; the “optimality” point should be qualified by explicit assumptions.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, with intuitive explanations of the sparse read/write/rollback mechanisms. Some important implementation and theoretical details are deferred to the supplement; relocating the core assumptions and a short ablation to the main text would improve readability and reproducibility.
- Originality: High. While external memory and content-based addressing are established, integrating ANN search into the training loop, sparse backprop via rollback, and a simple yet effective LRU/prev-read allocation policy is a novel and impactful combination at the time.
- Significance: High. Scaling MANNs by orders of magnitude is important and likely to influence subsequent work and applications. The empirical results suggest the approach is practical, not just asymptotic. The generalization to SDNC and success on bAbI and Omniglot broaden relevance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, well-motivated contribution addressing a central bottleneck in MANNs.
- Convincing empirical evidence of massive efficiency gains with little loss in task performance.
- General technique applicable beyond the specific architecture.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis; some key design choices feel heuristic.
- Non-differentiable components (ANN, LRU) raise questions about when/why training remains stable.
- Optimality claim needs clearer caveats in the main text; periodic index rebuild overhead not deeply analyzed.

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a substantial and timely contribution to scaling differentiable memory systems, with solid empirical support and ideas likely to be adopted and extended by the community.

Minor suggestions:
- Add a brief ablation on K and ANN type, and report rebuild overhead.
- Clarify assumptions behind the optimality claim and the training-time complexity including rebuilds.
- Discuss failure modes (e.g., high-dimensional queries, rapidly drifting memories) and guidance on choosing ANN settings.
