This paper introduces SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) designed to address the computational and memory overhead issues of traditional MANNs like NTMs and Memory Networks. The core idea is to constrain read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, significantly reducing the computational cost and memory footprint, particularly when dealing with large external memories. The authors demonstrate improvements in runtime and memory overhead (up to three orders of magnitude) compared to vanilla NTMs, while maintaining comparable performance on several tasks, including algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning, and Omniglot one-shot classification. They also present a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) derived from the Differentiable Neural Computer, achieving state-of-the-art results on the Babi tasks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a well-motivated solution to a significant problem in MANN research – scalability. The rationale behind sparsity is clear, and the implementation details (sparse read/write operations, efficient data structures, approximate nearest neighbor search) are well-explained. The analysis of time and space complexity appears correct, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The use of approximate nearest neighbor search is a pragmatic and effective optimization. The extension to the SDNC further highlights the generality of the approach. The backpropagation through time optimization is well explained and crucial for scaling.  The authors acknowledge limitations and potential improvements (e.g., exploring different ANN approaches).  The paper is more than a position paper; it presents a complete and functional system with thorough evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and motivates the proposed solution. The background section provides necessary context for understanding the core concepts. The architecture section clearly explains the key components of SAM, and the results section is well-structured and presents the findings in a clear and concise manner.  The inclusion of supplementary material for details on data structures and experimental setup is helpful.  However, some sections (e.g., the write operation explanation) could benefit from further simplification. More details on the ANN implementation choices would also enhance clarity.

**Originality:** The core idea of applying sparsity to MANNs is not entirely novel, as other works have explored sparse updates for NTMs. However, the specific combination of sparse read/write operations, efficient data structures (CSR, custom usage tracking), and the integration of approximate nearest neighbor search within a MANN framework represents a significant and original contribution. The extension to the SDNC is also a novel application of the proposed techniques. The authors adequately reference related work, including NTMs, Memory Networks, and Differentiable Neural Computers. They clearly distinguish their work from previous contributions by focusing on a comprehensive approach to scaling MANNs through sparsity and optimized data structures.

**Significance:** The results are important because they address a major bottleneck in MANN research – the scalability to large memory sizes and long sequences. By reducing the computational and memory overhead, SAM opens up the possibility of applying MANNs to more complex real-world problems, such as processing large text corpora or long time-series data. The demonstrated improvements in runtime and memory usage are substantial. The ability to train on longer sequences and achieve state-of-the-art results on the Babi tasks are significant achievements. The successful application to the Omniglot dataset further demonstrates the versatility of the approach. The work is likely to be influential in the field of memory-augmented neural networks and could inspire further research in scalable memory architectures.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical limitation of MANNs (scalability).
*   Presents a technically sound and well-motivated solution.
*   Demonstrates significant improvements in runtime and memory usage.
*   Achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on multiple tasks.
*   The SDNC extension shows the generalizability of the approach.
*   Thorough empirical evaluation and clear presentation of results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea of sparsity in MANNs isn't entirely new, though the implementation is.
*   Some sections could benefit from further simplification and clarity.
*   A more detailed discussion of the ANN implementation choices would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept. This paper presents a valuable and original contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The proposed approach effectively addresses the scalability challenges of traditional MANNs and opens up new possibilities for applying these models to more complex problems. The technical soundness, clarity, and significance of the work justify a strong recommendation for acceptance.
