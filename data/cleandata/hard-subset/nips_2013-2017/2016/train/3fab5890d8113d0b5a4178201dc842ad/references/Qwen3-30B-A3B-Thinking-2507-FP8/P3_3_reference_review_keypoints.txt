- Introduces SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a MANN that constrains reads/writes to a small K (4–8) subset to improve scalability.  
- Achieves O(log N) read time and O(1) space per time step using sparse content-based addressing and ANN search.  
- Novel write scheme updates previously read locations or overwrites least-recently-used slots; backprop tracks only sparse memory modifications.  
- Empirical validation on NTM benchmarks, bAbI reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification.  
- Reports up to 1,600× speedup and 29,000× memory reduction compared to vanilla NTMs.  
- Quality/technical soundness rated 9/10: well-motivated, sound complexity analysis, and elegant sparse-BPTT implementation.  
- Originality rated 8/10: combination of sparse addressing, ANN search, and memory management is novel versus prior sparse RL approaches.  
- Significance rated 9/10: directly addresses major scalability bottleneck and enables large-memory practical MANN applications.  
- Clarity rated 8/10: well-organized and illustrated, though memory-usage tracking and ANN choices could use more detail.  
- Theoretical depth concern (7/10): asymptotic optimality claim lacks rigorous proof and precise assumptions; limited approximation-error analysis.  
- Experimental scope concern (7/10): mainly repeats tasks dense MANNs solved; lacks exploration of problems previously intractable for dense MANNs and K vs. performance trade-offs.  
- Comparison weakness (6/10): insufficient detailed comparison to alternative scaling methods (e.g., RL-based addressing) regarding gradient variance and sample efficiency.  
- Extension to SDNC in supplementary material shows generalizability of the approach.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution with robust empirical results; recommendation: Accept.
