Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that scales content-addressable memory via sparse reads and writes. Reads are limited to the top-K memory slots selected by an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), yielding O(log N) per-step computation. Writes are constrained to a small set composed of previously read locations and the least recently used (LRU) location, with a simple usage-based memory management scheme. The authors also describe an efficient BPTT implementation that stores only sparse deltas and “rolls back” the memory during the backward pass, reducing per-step space to O(1) (after O(N) initialization). They claim asymptotic optimality (under assumptions) for content-based MANNs. Experiments demonstrate large speed/memory gains versus NTMs and dense variants on synthetic algorithmic tasks, curriculum scaling to millions of slots and thousands of time steps, bAbI QA, and Omniglot one-shot classification. A sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) is presented in the supplement, with large speedups and strong bAbI performance.

Relation to prior work
SAM is closely related to NTMs and Memory Networks in using content-based addressing. Unlike dense NTMs, SAM enforces sparsity in both reads and writes and integrates ANN structures to avoid linear scans. The write policy (read locations + LRU) echoes usage-based allocation ideas (e.g., DNC’s allocation/use vectors) but with a simpler heuristic. Compared to reinforcement learning approaches for hard/discrete addressing (e.g., Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015), SAM keeps end-to-end differentiability over the retrieved set, though the neighbor selection is approximate and discrete. The work is also in dialogue with efficient nearest neighbor search (FLANN, LSH) and online tree structures.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound engineering and algorithmic design that directly targets the dominant bottlenecks in MANNs: linear-time content addressing and linear-space BPTT. The rollback BPTT with sparse deltas is elegant and practically impactful.
- Empirical evidence supports the scalability claims: three orders of magnitude speed/memory improvements in some regimes; successful training with millions of memory slots and sequence lengths in the thousands without truncated BPTT; strong performance on bAbI and Omniglot.
- The approach generalizes to other architectures (SDNC), demonstrating breadth.
- Clear motivation and well-argued complexity narrative; the claimed O(log N) time and O(1) per-step space are compelling under the stated model of computation.

Concerns/weaknesses:
- Differentiability and approximation: The sparse top-K truncation and the ANN-based neighbor selection introduce discrete changes in the retrieved set. The paper states “no gradients with respect to the ANN,” which implies no gradient through the set selection. This creates a biased gradient relative to the dense soft attention. While the experiments are positive, the paper would benefit from:
  - A clear statement of how w̃ is normalized after truncation (do you renormalize over K neighbors?).
  - An analysis or ablation quantifying the effect of K, exact vs approximate neighbors, and the bias introduced by truncation on learning stability and final accuracy.
- Write policy and memory management: The heuristic combination of “write to previously read” and “overwrite LRU” is simple and effective, but the paper lacks analysis of failure modes (e.g., rapid context switching, aliasing, or high reuse scenarios that might thrash LRU). Sensitivity to the threshold for “non-negligible access” (epsilon=0.005) and the choice of K for write/read is not explored.
- Optimality claim: The asymptotic optimality argument is deferred to the supplement and appears to rest on specific assumptions (constant K, content-based addressing, data structure costs). It would help to more crisply state the theorem and assumptions in the main text (e.g., amortized costs, rebuild frequency, dimensionality impacts on kd-trees/LSH).
- Fairness/coverage of baselines: While dense NTM/DAM are natural baselines, stronger comparisons would include the dense DNC (for which the SDNC variant is provided; it would be useful to include dense DNC numbers in the main text), key-value Memory Networks, and RL-based hard attention. Some results (e.g., Omniglot) compare models with vastly different memory capacities due to resource constraints; this is practically relevant but should be explicitly discussed as a trade-off rather than a pure algorithmic advantage.
- Reproducibility details: Performance of ANN indices depends strongly on hyperparameters (e.g., number of trees, checks; number of hashes; rebuild schedule). While the authors mention rebuilds every N insertions, more details (or releasing code) would make the work easier to reproduce.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. However:
- Precisely define w̃ (normalization, relation to the full softmax) and gradients with respect to q when the neighbor set changes.
- Provide more specifics on ANN settings and on the sparse data structures used for BPTT and usage tracking (even if summarized, with full details in the supplement).
- Clarify the erase behavior when writing to LRU (Rt = IUt 1^T seems to imply full erase; how does this interact with αt and the write gates?).

Originality
The combination of sparse reads/writes, ANN-based content addressing, and an efficient backward implementation for MANNs is novel and practically significant. While the components (k-NN/ANN, LRU-like allocation) are known, their integration into a differentiable memory with careful complexity accounting is original at the time and advances the state of the art in scalable MANNs.

Significance
High. Scaling differentiable memory is central for long-context reasoning and one-shot learning. The demonstrated speedups and the ability to train with very large memories and long sequences, plus strong results on bAbI and Omniglot, make this a contribution likely to influence subsequent work and practice.

Pros
- Strong, practical solution to a key bottleneck in MANNs; compelling complexity improvements.
- Broad applicability (SAM, SDNC), with substantial empirical validation.
- Demonstrated scaling to millions of slots and thousands of time steps with end-to-end training.
- Competitive or superior results on multiple benchmarks.

Cons
- Limited analysis of the impact of approximation/truncation on learning and stability; missing ablations.
- Heuristic write/usage policy may have edge-case failures; sensitivity not explored.
- Optimality claim could be sharper in the main text.
- Baseline coverage and fairness caveats on capacity.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a substantial and timely contribution that advances scalable differentiable memory both algorithmically and empirically. Addressing the noted clarity and ablation points would further strengthen the paper.
