- Introduces SAM (Sparse Access Memory) that constrains reads/writes to a small sparse subset (K=4-8) to scale memory-augmented neural networks.  
- Achieves near-identical performance to dense models while reducing time complexity from O(N) to O(log N) and space to O(1) per step using ANN data structures.  
- Empirically validated across synthetic and real tasks (NTM tasks, Babi, Omniglot) with speedups up to 1600x and memory reductions up to three orders of magnitude.  
- Extends approach to Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) that attains state-of-the-art results on the Babi tasks without supervised memory access.  
- Maintains end-to-end differentiability, distinguishing it from prior discrete-addressing or RL-based scaling methods.  
- Novel contribution: use of approximate nearest neighbor indices within MANNs for sublinear content-based addressing.  
- Significant practical impact by enabling memory sizes up to ~1 million words, opening applications requiring large external memory.  
- Paper is technically rigorous with solid theoretical complexity claims supported by empirical measurements.  
- Experiments are comprehensive and the paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis explaining why sparse models sometimes learn better than dense models.  
- Weakness: limited exploration and comparison of alternative sparse write/addressing schemes.  
- Weakness: some implementation details of ANN index maintenance and rebalancing during training are under-explained.  
- Weakness: scalability limits not fully explored—unclear at what point ANN overheads become significant.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — strong, novel, and significant contribution despite some theoretical and implementation-detail gaps.
