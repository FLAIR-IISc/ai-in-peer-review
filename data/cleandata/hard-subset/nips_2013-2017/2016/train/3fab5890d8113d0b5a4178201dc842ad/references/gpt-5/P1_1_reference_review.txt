Summary
This paper proposes Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) that achieves scalable training and inference by enforcing sparsity in both reads and writes to an external memory and by using efficient data structures for content-based addressing. The key ideas are: (1) sparse reads via top-K content-based attention computed with an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index (randomized k-d trees or LSH), yielding O(log N) time per memory access for N memory slots; (2) sparse writes that either update previously read locations or overwrite the least recently used slot, tracked in O(1) time with a custom usage structure; and (3) an efficient BPTT scheme that stores and reverts only the sparse modifications to the external memory, reducing space overhead per step to O(1) independent of N. The authors show substantial runtime and memory savings (up to 100–1600× faster and O(1) memory per step) compared to dense NTMs, with comparable learning performance on classic NTM algorithmic tasks, strong performance on bAbI (with a sparse DNC variant in supplement), and improved scalability on curriculum versions of associative recall, copy, and priority sort. On Omniglot one-shot classification, SAM achieves lower error than dense MANN baselines when allowed a much larger memory under equal physical RAM constraints.

Relation to prior work: SAM builds directly on NTMs and Memory Networks by decoupling parameter count from memory size but departs by making both read and write sparse, enabling sublinear access via ANN and constant-time updates and BPTT bookkeeping. It complements reinforcement-learning-based discrete addressing (RL-NTM) by keeping end-to-end differentiability and lower-variance gradients. The supplement’s SDNC shows generality to other memory architectures like DNC.

Strengths
- Technical novelty and practicality: A coherent set of systems-level innovations (sparse attention, sparse writes with LRU, ANN integration, and sparse rollback for BPTT) address a real scalability bottleneck in MANNs. The end-to-end differentiable design with minimal changes to the controller is elegant and practical.
- Theoretical and algorithmic clarity: Clear asymptotic claims (O(log N) time per access; O(1) per-step memory), with reasonable implementation details (CSR formats, ANN rebuild policy). The sparse rollback method is particularly useful and likely of broad interest.
- Strong empirical evidence of scaling: Convincing speed and memory benchmarks show orders-of-magnitude gains. The curriculum experiments demonstrate training stability with very long sequences (BPTT over >4k steps) and millions of slots.
- Breadth of tasks: Algorithmic tasks, bAbI QA (with SDNC performing best among unsupervised-access approaches), and Omniglot one-shot provide a diverse evaluation that speaks to generality.
- Generality: The SDNC in the supplement suggests the sparsification approach is portable across MANN architectures.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Gradient bias and stability: Sparse top-K attention implies zero gradients to non-selected slots and a discontinuous selection map via ANN. While it works empirically, the paper does not analyze the gradient bias introduced by truncating attention or by approximate neighbors. Suggested: an ablation comparing exact top-K vs ANN (k-d tree vs LSH), varying K, and reporting accuracy-speed tradeoffs, plus robustness of training to ANN approximation quality.
- Fairness of comparisons: Some comparisons allocate drastically different memory sizes (e.g., 64 slots for dense models vs 2e6 for sparse) under equal RAM. This is a valid systems-level comparison but conflates algorithmic capability with hardware efficiency. Suggested: include a dense baseline at the largest feasible size (e.g., 1–2k slots) and an SDNC/DNC dense baseline in main text, not just supplement. Also equalize training steps/updates, not just wall-clock time.
- Empirical breadth: Real-world evaluations remain limited. Omniglot is helpful but still episodic toy-like. Suggested: include a long-document QA or language modeling benchmark (e.g., WikiText) or multi-hop retrieval from large knowledge stores to demonstrate utility at scale.
- ANN maintenance overhead: The index is rebuilt every N insertions; the amortized cost and sensitivity to imbalance are not quantified. Suggested: report the fraction of time in ANN rebuilds and compare alternative online indices (LSH forests, Mondrian forests) more thoroughly.

Clarity
- Overall writing is clear and well-organized. However, some crucial details are relegated to the supplement (proof sketch of optimality, data structure for O(1) usage tracking, exact optimizer/learning-rate schedules, and SDNC architecture). Suggested: bring a brief statement of assumptions behind “asymptotically optimal” into the main text; include a concise algorithm box for the sparse rollback procedure; provide a short table of ANN hyperparameters and rebuild policy.
- Notation: Distinguish clearly between dense and sparse weights when both appear in the same equation to avoid confusion.

Originality
- The specific combination of sparse reads/writes, ANN-based content addressing, and sparse rollback appears novel and well-motivated. The write policy (prev-read + LRU) is simple and effective, though related ideas exist in DNC’s memory allocation; this relationship is acknowledged via SDNC.

Significance
- The work addresses a central bottleneck for MANNs and demonstrates 2–3 orders-of-magnitude empirical gains without sacrificing performance. This is likely to influence subsequent research and practice. The generality to DNC strengthens impact.

Additional questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to K and to the threshold epsilon for usage? Is there an optimal regime across tasks?
- Do you observe catastrophic forgetting or pathological overwrite patterns with LRU-based allocation on long sequences?
- What is the empirical discrepancy (if any) between exact KNN and ANN during training (neighbor recall, final loss)?
- Can the sparse scheme degrade gradient flow early in training (before memory is populated)? Any warm-up heuristics used?
- What optimizer and gradient clipping were used to sustain BPTT over >4k steps?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a sound, implementable approach.
- Large empirical speed/memory gains with competitive or superior accuracy.
- General method applicable to multiple MANNs (NTM, DNC).
- Clear exposition and meaningful ablations across tasks/curricula.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of the impact of ANN/truncation on gradient bias and convergence.
- Fairness of some comparisons (memory sizes, baselines) could be improved.
- Real-world evaluations beyond Omniglot are limited.
- Some key technical details are deferred to the supplement.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a strong systems-and-algorithms contribution that substantially advances the practicality of differentiable memory models, with rigorous engineering and solid empirical validation. With minor revisions to clarify assumptions, broaden baselines, and add ablations on ANN/truncation parameters, the paper will be an impactful NIPS contribution.
