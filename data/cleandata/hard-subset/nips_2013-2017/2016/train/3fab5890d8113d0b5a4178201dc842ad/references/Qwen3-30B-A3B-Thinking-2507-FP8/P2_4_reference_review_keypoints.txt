- Introduces SAM (Sparse Access Memory), restricting reads/writes to K words to achieve O(log N) reads and O(1) per-step space while preserving task performance.  
- Demonstrates large efficiency gains (up to 1600x speedup, huge memory reduction) and scales to very large memories and long sequences.  
- Extends approach to a Sparse Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) achieving state-of-the-art on Babi without supervised memory access.  
- Theoretical analysis shows asymptotic optimality in time and space (Supplementary A), providing strong theoretical grounding.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive with ablations and comparisons (DAM, NTM, LSTM) across synthetic and real tasks, convincingly supporting claims.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations (tuning K, approximate nearest neighbor) and provide reproducibility details in supplementary materials.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear architecture descriptions and compelling visualizations.  
- Minor clarity suggestion: briefly explain ANN index maintenance/rebuild frequency during training.  
- Core idea of sparse memory access for MANNs is novel and clearly differentiated from prior work, including end-to-end differentiability.  
- Builds on approximate nearest neighbor concepts and contrasts with RL-based sparse access approaches.  
- Addresses a critical scalability bottleneck in MANNs, enabling practical large-memory applications (e.g., book/Wikipedia-scale processing).  
- Empirical gains include not just efficiency but improved performance on some tasks (priority sort, associative recall).  
- Strengths: practical/theoretically grounded solution, major empirical improvements, broad evaluation, clear theory, generalizable to other architectures.  
- Weaknesses: limited exploration of different K values, incomplete ANN implementation details, limited comparisons to recent MANNs beyond SDNC, key theoretical claims could be summarized in main text.  
- Recommendation: accept — strong contribution with high technical quality, clear significance, and well-executed experiments.
