This paper addresses the critical issue of scaling memory-augmented neural networks (MANNs) by introducing SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a novel architecture designed to reduce the computational and memory overhead associated with traditional MANNs like NTMs. The core idea is to constrain read and write operations to a sparse subset of the memory, coupled with efficient data structures for content-based read operations. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in runtime and memory usage, while maintaining comparable or even improved performance on a variety of tasks, including algorithmic tasks, Babi reasoning tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. They also present a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) with impressive speedups and performance on Babi tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a very important problem in the field of MANNs. The motivation is strong: the memory and computational cost of standard MANNs severely limits their applicability to real-world problems. The proposed solution – sparsity – is intuitive and effectively implemented. The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling, demonstrating substantial gains in efficiency without sacrificing accuracy. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets strengthens the validity of the findings. The comparison with existing methods (NTM, DAM, LSTM) is fair and highlights the advantages of SAM.  The supplementary material is thorough, providing additional details on the architecture, implementation, and experiments. The application of the sparsity concept to the DNC (SDNC) is a nice demonstration of the generality of the approach.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **ANN Implementation Details:** While the use of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search is mentioned, the details of the ANN implementation (specifically the choice between FLANN and LSH, and the rebuilding frequency) feel somewhat glossed over. More details on how these choices impact performance would be beneficial. The authors mention rebuilding, but not the criteria for doing so.  A discussion on the potential trade-offs between ANN accuracy and computational speed would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper reports good results, but doesn't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of performance to key hyperparameters, such as the sparsity level (K), the discount factor in the usage definition (λ), or the threshold parameter for least recently accessed memory (ε). Exploring these sensitivities would provide more insight into the robustness of the SAM architecture.
* **Theoretical Optimality:** The authors claim that SAM is asymptotically optimal in space and time. While the supplementary material provides some arguments, a more rigorous and formal theoretical analysis would strengthen this claim.  Specifically, the discussion in Supplementary A could be expanded.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While comparisons to DAM are present, a more granular ablation study examining the contributions of the sparse read/write mechanisms *individually* would be helpful. For example, what is the impact of using only sparse writes, or only sparse reads?
* **Scaling Beyond Curriculum Learning:**  While the curriculum learning approach successfully demonstrates scaling, it doesn't fully address the challenges of training on *truly* long sequences without explicit curriculum design. Discussing potential challenges and future directions for addressing these challenges would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Efficiency Gains:** The reported speedups and memory savings are substantial and make MANNs more practical for real-world applications.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance across diverse tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Generality:** The application of the sparsity concept to the DNC highlights the broad applicability of the technique.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is easy to understand and well-organized.
* **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and include comparisons to relevant baselines.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical bottleneck in MANN research (scalability).
* Presents a novel and effective solution (sparse access memory).
* Demonstrates significant improvements in both efficiency and performance.
* Results are compelling and well-supported by experimental evidence.
* The work has the potential to unlock new applications for MANNs in areas like long-sequence modeling and knowledge representation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Some aspects of the implementation (ANN details) lack sufficient detail.
* The theoretical optimality claim could benefit from a more rigorous analysis.
* Limited ablation studies prevent a deeper understanding of the contribution of individual components.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of memory-augmented neural networks. The authors have successfully addressed a critical scalability issue and demonstrated a practical and effective solution. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the suggestions for improvement, particularly concerning the ANN implementation details and the theoretical analysis of optimality, in a post-review revision. Specifically, adding a table in the appendix detailing ANN parameters and their impact would be beneficial.
