- Overall assessment: SAM is a significant, well-executed contribution that enables scalable MANNs and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
- Core idea: Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) which uses sparse read/write to reduce computational and memory bottlenecks in MANNs.
- Empirical results: Demonstrates large speed and memory improvements while maintaining or improving performance across tasks and larger memory/sequence scales.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on NTMs and Memory Networks, resolving linear read/write overhead and backprop memory duplication.
- Technical approach: Combines sparse accesses with efficient data structures (CSR, ANN) and sparse backpropagation, convincingly implemented.
- Experimental evaluation: Thorough tests on algorithmic tasks, BABI, Omniglot, and scaling/curriculum experiments showing strong performance.
- Generality: Method applied to DNC as well, indicating broad applicability to other MANN architectures.
- Writing and presentation: Clear, well-written paper with useful supplementary material.
- Practical impact: Up to three orders of magnitude runtime reduction for large memories, enabling previously intractable applications.
- Weakness — sparsity scheme: Limited exploration of alternative write/read sparsity strategies beyond the chosen heuristic.
- Weakness — ANN details: Insufficient detail on FLANN/LSH configurations and trade-offs affecting reproducibility and performance understanding.
- Weakness — theory: Lacks deeper theoretical analysis of asymptotic optimality and complexity comparisons to other MANNs.
- Weakness — hyperparameters: Unclear sensitivity to hyperparameters; a sensitivity analysis is missing.
- Pro-accept arguments: Addresses a critical bottleneck, is technically sound, empirically compelling, scalable, and generalizable.
- Con-accept arguments: Needs more exploration of sparsity strategies, ANN implementation details, theoretical rigor, and hyperparameter robustness.
- Qualitative evaluation: Technically sound, original, significant, generally clear, and likely to inspire further research.
- Recommendation: Strong accept for NIPS.
