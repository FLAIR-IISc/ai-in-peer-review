This paper introduces SAM (Sparse Access Memory), a memory-augmented neural network (MANN) designed to address the scalability issues of existing MANNs like NTMs and Memory Networks. The core idea is to restrict read and write operations to a sparse subset of memory locations, significantly reducing computational and memory overhead. The authors demonstrate improvements in runtime and memory usage (up to three orders of magnitude) while maintaining comparable performance on algorithmic tasks, Babi tasks, and Omniglot one-shot classification. They also present a sparse version of the Differentiable Neural Computer (SDNC) with improved performance.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a well-defined solution to a significant problem in MANNs. The core concept of sparsity is clearly explained, and the benefits in terms of computational complexity are convincingly demonstrated. The introduction of efficient data structures (ANNs, sparse matrices) is crucial and appropriately justified. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets. The ablation studies (comparing SAM with DAM and other MANNs) are valuable for understanding the impact of sparsity. However, the discussion of the ANN choices (FLANN vs. LSH) is somewhat brief, and the selection criteria aren’t fully explained. While the claim of asymptotic optimality is made, a more detailed theoretical analysis (beyond the supplementary material) would strengthen this aspect. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging limitations and potential avenues for future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background section provides a concise overview of relevant concepts. The architecture description is clear, and the mathematical notation is used consistently. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the control flow (in the supplementary material) is helpful. However, some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations. For example, the write operation details and the usage definition `U^(2)_T(i)` could be elaborated for better understanding. The connections between the sparse write schemes considered (previously read / least recently accessed) and potential alternatives are not fully explored.

**Originality:** The idea of applying sparsity to MANN read/write operations is novel and addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling these models. While sparse representations are common in other areas of machine learning, their application to the specific context of differentiable memory access is a significant contribution. The adaptation of the DNC to a sparse variant (SDNC) further highlights the generality of the approach. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by focusing on the computational and memory efficiency gains enabled by sparsity. The references are comprehensive and demonstrate awareness of related work.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant, as they pave the way for training MANNs with much larger memory capacities, enabling them to tackle more complex tasks requiring long-term dependencies. The demonstrated performance on tasks like Babi and Omniglot indicates the practicality of the approach. The substantial speedups and memory savings make SAM a promising candidate for real-world applications where resource constraints are a concern. The scalability of the approach allows for exploration of larger and more complex datasets, potentially leading to breakthroughs in areas like natural language processing and reinforcement learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial bottleneck in MANN scalability.
*   Technically sound with strong experimental validation.
*   Demonstrates significant improvements in runtime and memory usage.
*   Novel approach with clear benefits over existing methods.
*   Potential for significant impact in various applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   ANN selection rationale could be more detailed.
*   Theoretical analysis could be expanded to fully support the optimality claims.
*   Some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations to improve readability.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with a novel and impactful contribution. The authors have successfully addressed a key challenge in scaling memory-augmented neural networks, and the experimental results are compelling. While minor improvements could be made in terms of clarity and theoretical depth, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance.
