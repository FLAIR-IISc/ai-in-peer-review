Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM), a memory-augmented neural network that scales content-based reads and writes to very large external memories by enforcing sparsity and using efficient data structures. The key ideas are: (1) top-K sparse reads via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexing (randomized k-d trees or LSH) to achieve O(log N) query time; (2) sparse writes limited to previously read locations and the least-recently-used (LRU) location, with O(1) per-step cost; (3) an in-place “rollback” scheme for backpropagation that tracks sparse modifications and restores memory states in O(1) per step, yielding O(1) space per step and avoiding the O(NT) memory blow-up typical of BPTT with dense memories. Under assumptions outlined in the supplement, the authors argue SAM is asymptotically optimal in time and space among content-addressable MANNs.

Empirically, SAM matches or exceeds dense NTMs and a dense variant (DAM) on algorithmic tasks (copy, associative recall, priority sort), scales dramatically via curriculum (up to millions of memory slots and >4000 untruncated BPTT steps), shows strong generalization to much longer sequences than trained on, achieves near SOTA on bAbI without supervised memory access (and with a sparse DNC variant, SDNC, solves all but one task), and outperforms baselines on Omniglot one-shot episodes at larger sequence lengths. Speed/memory benchmarks show 100–1600× speedups and orders-of-magnitude lower memory footprint versus NTMs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The design is careful and addresses core bottlenecks of MANNs. The sparse read/write mechanisms, ANN integration, and rollback BPTT are well-motivated and plausible. Complexity claims are supported by benchmarks and reasonable algorithmic arguments.
- Empirical support: Experiments span synthetic algorithmic tasks, bAbI, and Omniglot. The scaling curriculum and million-scale memory benchmarks are compelling. Results indicate that sparse approximations do not harm learning and may aid it early on.
- Caveats: The “optimality” claim depends on assumptions relegated to the supplement; it would help to state them more explicitly in the main text and clarify lower-bound arguments for content-addressable models. Ablations are light: sensitivity to K, choice of ANN, write policy (LRU + read locations) vs content-based writes, and the threshold parameter for usage would strengthen the evidence. Comparisons on more realistic large-scale language tasks would further validate significance, though I appreciate the practical challenges.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The core algorithms are described concretely, and the rationale for each design choice is explained. Suggestions:
- Provide pseudo-code for the rollback BPTT mechanism and for maintaining the LRU/usage structure in the main text (not only in the supplement).
- Be explicit about how gradients are handled through the top-K selection (no gradients through the index; gradients only to selected rows). A brief discussion of the bias this introduces and why it works empirically would help.
- Summarize the assumptions behind the optimality discussion in the main paper.

Originality
High. Introducing ANN-based content retrieval inside a differentiable memory, enforcing strict sparsity in read and write, and designing an O(1)-space BPTT rollback are a novel synthesis addressing a long-standing scalability limitation of MANNs. The extension to a sparse DNC underscores generality.

Significance
High. This work unlocks the practical training of MANNs with very large memories, which is a key obstacle for algorithmic reasoning and rapid learning tasks. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude computational gains, coupled with maintained or improved task performance and very long-range generalization, make this a substantial advance likely to influence subsequent research on memory architectures and retrieval-augmented models.

Pros
- Conceptually bold and practically impactful approach to scaling content-based memory.
- Strong theoretical motivation and careful systems design (ANN integration, LRU allocation, rollback BPTT).
- Dramatic empirical speed/space improvements with competitive or better task performance.
- Demonstrates generality by sparsifying DNC and achieving best-reported bAbI results (without supervision).
- Excellent long-sequence generalization; handles millions of memory slots.

Cons / questions for the authors
- Clarify and foreground the assumptions behind the “asymptotically optimal” claim; include a short theorem statement or summary in the main text.
- Provide ablations: K (e.g., 2/4/8/16), effect of ANN type/parameters, usage threshold, and write policy variants (e.g., allowing content-based writes with sparsity).
- Discuss stability: does the non-differentiable top-K introduce training instability? Any use of straight-through or alternatives tried?
- How robust is the ANN maintenance in fully online training (imbalance, rebuild frequency)? Any observed degradation over very long runs?
- Implementation practicality: any GPU/CPU trade-offs, and are CSR operations and index maintenance a bottleneck at large scale?
- Could SAM handle multiple read heads and parallel queries effectively? Any contention issues for writes?
- Real-world tasks: any preliminary results on larger, less synthetic corpora (e.g., QA beyond bAbI), or retrieval-augmented language modeling?

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. This paper introduces a significant and novel direction for scalable memory-augmented neural networks, with elegant algorithmic ideas and convincing empirical results. Minor limitations in ablations and the reliance on synthetic tasks are outweighed by the conceptual contribution and demonstrated scalability. I expect this work to spur follow-on research and practical adoption.
