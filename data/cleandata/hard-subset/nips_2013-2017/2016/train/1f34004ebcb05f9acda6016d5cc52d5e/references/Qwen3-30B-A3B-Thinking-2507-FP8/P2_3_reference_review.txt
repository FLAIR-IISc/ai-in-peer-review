

# Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking algorithm that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free approach, both with theoretical convergence guarantees for the non-convex loss-minimization problem. Unlike prior work [21], which lacked convergence guarantees, the authors provide rigorous analysis of both methods, including complexity bounds. The paper builds on the Supervised PageRank framework [21] but extends it with theoretically sound optimization techniques that address the key limitation of previous work.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide rigorous convergence proofs for both optimization methods, establishing their theoretical soundness. The paper correctly identifies the key challenge in Supervised PageRank learning: the non-convex loss-minimization problem with an inexact oracle (since the stationary distribution must be approximated). The two-level framework they propose—where the lower level provides approximate stationary distributions with controlled accuracy and the upper level uses these approximations for optimization—is well-motivated and technically sound.

The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality. The authors carefully analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is crucial for practical implementation. The paper also properly acknowledges limitations, such as the need for a local minimum in a convex region for the gradient-based method.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem formulation (Section 2) is precise, with well-defined mathematical notation. The two-level optimization framework is clearly explained, and the algorithms (Algorithms 2 and 4) are described with sufficient detail. The experimental setup (Section 6.1) is adequately described, including the dataset characteristics and evaluation metrics.

The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the theorems are clearly stated with relevant proofs referenced to supplementary materials. The paper effectively uses lemmas (Lemmas 1 and 2) to establish the accuracy of the lower-level approximations, which are crucial for the upper-level optimization.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature by adapting gradient-based and gradient-free methods to constrained non-convex problems with inexact oracles. While Supervised PageRank [21] has been proposed before, the authors correctly identify the lack of theoretical guarantees in the original approach. Their two-level framework with controlled approximation accuracy represents a novel contribution.

The adaptation of random gradient-free methods [16] to constrained optimization with inexact oracles (Section 4.1) is particularly novel. The paper also provides the first complexity analysis for the specific problem of learning Supervised PageRank, which is a significant advance over prior work.

The paper could strengthen its originality claim by more explicitly comparing with related work in optimization theory (e.g., [7, 16, 25]), but the contribution is still clear and significant within the context of the specific learning problem.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Web ranking is a critical application area for machine learning, and the paper provides methods that not only outperform state-of-the-art approaches in ranking quality but also come with theoretical guarantees. This combination of practical performance improvements and theoretical soundness is rare and valuable.

The two-level optimization framework with inexact oracles could be applied to other graph-based learning problems beyond web ranking, such as recommendation systems or social network analysis. The paper's focus on the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational complexity is particularly relevant for large-scale applications.

The experimental results on the Yandex dataset demonstrate practical relevance, and the authors properly evaluate both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, highlighting their respective advantages (e.g., the gradient-free method avoids derivative calculations for large matrices).

## Weaknesses
1. The experimental comparison is limited to one dataset (Yandex) and one ranking task. More comprehensive comparisons with other ranking algorithms and datasets would strengthen the significance claim.

2. The paper could better clarify how the proposed methods compare to other optimization approaches for non-convex problems with inexact oracles (e.g., [7, 25]) in terms of practical performance.

3. While the paper mentions the importance of the loss function, it doesn't explore how the methods might generalize to other ranking loss functions (e.g., pairwise or listwise losses commonly used in ranking problems).

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization of graph-based ranking algorithms. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results support the claims, and the problem addressed is both practically relevant and theoretically interesting. The paper's main weakness is the limited experimental evaluation, but this doesn't undermine the strong theoretical contribution.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria well:
- Quality: High (rigorous proofs, proper experimental validation)
- Clarity: High (well-organized, well-written)
- Originality: High (novel optimization framework for this specific problem)
- Significance: High (practical applications with theoretical guarantees)

This paper represents a meaningful advance over prior work [21] and would be valuable for both theoretical optimization researchers and practitioners working on graph-based ranking problems. The combination of theoretical guarantees with practical performance improvements makes it particularly compelling for NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
