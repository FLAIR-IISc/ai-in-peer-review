

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two new optimization algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank, a method for ranking nodes in graphs that incorporates node and edge features. The authors claim their methods provide theoretical convergence guarantees, unlike the state-of-the-art method [21]. They present a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method, both operating in a two-level framework where the lower level computes approximations to the stationary distribution using a linearly convergent method, and the upper level uses these approximations for parameter optimization.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods under inexact oracle assumptions. The authors properly acknowledge related work in optimization theory (Nesterov, Ghadimi, etc.) and provide detailed proofs in the supplementary materials. The experiments on Yandex web browsing data show modest improvements over the baseline method [21] for the specific ranking task.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides a well-structured theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees for both optimization approaches.
2. The experimental setup is reasonable, with a real-world dataset and appropriate evaluation metrics.
3. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution within existing optimization literature.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical novelty is overstated. The core techniques (gradient-free and gradient-based methods with inexact oracles) are well-established in optimization literature (e.g., Nesterov's work on inexact oracles [25] and Nesterov & Spokoiny's work on random gradient-free methods [16]). The paper does not sufficiently clarify how these standard techniques are novel when applied to this specific problem, particularly since the underlying optimization problem (non-convex with inexact oracle) has been studied before.

2. The practical significance of the improvements is unclear. The paper claims "GFN significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art algorithms on all test sets" but doesn't provide sufficient context about the magnitude of improvements (e.g., percentage improvements, statistical significance beyond p<0.005). The improvements appear marginal based on the data presented, and it's unclear whether these would translate to meaningful gains in real-world search engines.

3. The comparison to baselines is inadequate. The paper only compares against the 2011 method [21], which is significantly outdated. Modern graph neural network approaches for ranking tasks (e.g., GraphSAGE, GATs) would be more relevant baselines, especially given the "web page ranking" context. The paper makes no mention of these approaches, despite their prevalence in recent literature.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the practical challenges of implementing these methods. The complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) depend on constants like L (Lipschitz constant) that are not clearly defined in practice. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to choose these constants effectively in real applications.

5. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the results. It mentions "different values of the accuracies" but doesn't specify how these were chosen or their impact on performance. The paper also doesn't report computational time or memory requirements for the algorithms, which are critical for large-scale applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents technically sound theoretical results, it overstates the novelty of its contributions and fails to adequately contextualize them within the broader landscape of graph ranking methods. The marginal improvements over an outdated baseline do not justify the claim of significant advancement in the state of the art. The paper would benefit from:
- More thorough comparison with recent graph-based ranking methods
- Clearer explanation of the practical significance of the improvements
- Better discussion of the constants in the complexity bounds and how they might be estimated in practice

The paper is well-written and technically rigorous, but its contributions do not meet the high bar for acceptance at NeurIPS. The work represents a careful application of established optimization techniques to a specific problem, but it does not provide the kind of significant theoretical or practical breakthrough that NeurIPS typically seeks.

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Pro:** Theoretical analysis is sound and properly contextualized within optimization literature. The problem of learning Supervised PageRank is well-motivated.

**Con:** Overstated novelty claims. Marginal empirical improvements over an outdated baseline. Inadequate comparison with more recent relevant methods. Insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges and constants in complexity bounds.
