**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank where restart and transition probabilities in a random walk are defined as linear combinations of node and edge features. The learning task is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization problem. The central challenge is that the loss function and its gradient depend on the stationary distribution of a large, parameter-dependent Markov chain, which cannot be computed exactly. This places the problem in an "inexact oracle" setting.

The authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem, both of which come with theoretical convergence guarantees. The key contributions are:
1.  On the lower level, they employ a linearly convergent method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, which allows for explicit control over the approximation error.
2.  On the upper level, they develop two new methods:
    a) A gradient-free method that adapts random search techniques to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is computed with a known, bounded error.
    b) A gradient-based method that extends an adaptive gradient framework to general non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle.
3.  For both methods, the authors provide a formal convergence rate analysis and derive the overall computational complexity, explicitly managing the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the efficiency of the upper-level optimization. This is a significant improvement over the prior state-of-the-art method [21], which lacked such guarantees.
4.  Experimental results on a real-world web page ranking task demonstrate that the proposed algorithms, particularly the gradient-free variant, outperform the previous method.

**Strengths**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. It provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank. The authors successfully bridge the gap between the practical need for iterative approximation of the stationary distribution (the inexact oracle) and the theoretical requirements for optimization algorithm convergence. The complexity analysis, which connects the upper-level iterations to the lower-level computational cost, is thorough and valuable.

*   **Novelty and Originality:** The paper makes novel contributions to optimization with inexact oracles. It extends existing frameworks for gradient-free and gradient-based methods to new settings: the gradient-free method is adapted for constrained problems with a biased inexact oracle, and the gradient-based method is generalized for non-convex constrained problems. Applying these advanced optimization techniques to the Supervised PageRank problem is a novel and impactful combination.

*   **Problem Significance:** Learning graph-based ranking functions is an important problem with direct applications in search engines, recommendation systems, and social network analysis. By providing robust and theoretically-grounded learning algorithms, this work significantly advances the state of the art for a practical and relevant model. The paper directly addresses and resolves a key weakness (lack of convergence guarantees) in a previously published method [21].

*   **Experimental Validation:** The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on a large-scale, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine. The results show that the proposed methods achieve a lower loss value than the previous state-of-the-art, confirming the practical benefits of the new algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity on Convexity Assumption:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and its application in Theorem 2) assumes that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. However, the paper repeatedly states that the problem is generally non-convex. The analysis then relies on the assumption that `Φ` is chosen to be a small vicinity of a local minimum `ϕ*` where `f` *is* convex. This is a strong assumption that needs more justification. Is there any theoretical or empirical evidence to suggest the loss function for Supervised PageRank exhibits local convexity around its minima? A more detailed discussion on the implications and validity of this assumption would strengthen the paper. For the gradient-based method, which is designed for non-convex problems, this is less of a concern.

*   **Experimental Details and Discussion:** The experimental section could be strengthened with more details and analysis.
    *   Table 1 is the core of the results, but it would be more compelling if it included performance metrics beyond the final loss function value, such as a standard ranking metric (e.g., NDCG). While minimizing the loss is the direct goal of the optimization, showing improvement in a user-centric metric would better demonstrate the real-world impact.
    *   A discussion of wall-clock time would be very helpful. The paper provides theoretical complexity bounds, but comparing the actual training time of GFN, GBN, and GBP would provide a practical complement to this analysis. GFN, for example, avoids computing derivatives but requires two function evaluations per step, making the trade-off unclear without timing information.
    *   The paper notes that GBN outperforms GBP on one dataset but requires fewer iterations on others. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this. Why might the theoretically superior method not always achieve a lower loss in practice within a reasonable number of iterations? Could this be related to the adaptive choice of `Mk` or other hyperparameters?

*   **Reproducibility:** The derivation of the inexact first-order oracle property (Eq. 5.1) from the bounds in Lemmas 1 and 2 is crucial for the gradient-based method. The paper asserts this connection by citing [25] but does not provide a brief derivation. Including a short sketch of how `δ1` and `δ2` combine to satisfy the condition in Eq. (5.1) would improve the paper's self-containedness and clarity for the reader.

**Decision**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and well-supported contribution. It tackles an important practical problem—learning a supervised ranking model on graphs—with rigorous theoretical tools. The development of provably convergent optimization methods for the inexact oracle setting of Supervised PageRank is a substantial advance over prior work. The strengths in theoretical soundness and originality clearly outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the need for clarifying assumptions and adding more detail to the experimental discussion. These points can be addressed in a final version. The paper is technically sound, novel, and the results are significant for both the optimization and web-ranking communities. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
