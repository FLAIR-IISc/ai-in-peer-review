Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, where node and edge features shape the restart and transition probabilities of a query-dependent random walk. The key difficulty is that the loss f(ϕ), a pairwise squared hinge over stationary distributions πq(ϕ), is only accessible via iterative approximations, and gradients are similarly approximated. The authors propose two two-level methods: (i) a gradient-based approach with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a gradient-free approach with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, both rely on the Nesterov–Nemirovski method to approximate stationary distributions, and a generalization of it to approximate derivatives with respect to ϕ. On the upper level, they develop convergence guarantees for a projected adaptive gradient method with inexact oracle for nonconvex composite problems, and for a randomized gradient-free method with inexact, bounded-error function values for constrained convex problems.

Compared to prior work: PageRank/HITS etc. are classical. Supervised variants (e.g., Richardson & Domingos; Backstrom & Leskovec; Gao et al.) and Supervised PageRank [21] learn weights for features but typically use power methods and lack guarantees under inexactness. Theoretical foundations for inexact oracles exist for convex optimization (e.g., Devolder et al.) and for nonconvex stochastic/zeroth-order optimization (Ghadimi & Lan; Nesterov & Spokoiny). This paper extends these frameworks to: (a) constrained optimization with bounded error zero-order oracle using sphere randomization, and (b) adaptive projected gradient for potentially nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles and unknown L. The empirical study applies the methods to browsing-graph ranking on Yandex data and compares against the state-of-the-art two-level gradient approach [21] (GBP).

Strengths
- Technical development: Clear two-level integration of a linearly convergent lower-level Markov chain solver (Nesterov–Nemirovski) with upper-level methods designed to handle inexact oracles. The derivation of lower-level accuracy-to-cost tradeoffs and their propagation to overall complexity is valuable.
- Theoretical guarantees: Convergence and complexity bounds are provided for both the gradient-free and gradient-based upper-level algorithms, explicitly accounting for oracle inexactness, constraints, and (for the gradient method) nonconvexity via a stationarity measure.
- Practical insight: The derivative approximation scheme (Equations 3.2–3.3) is a useful extension of the stationary distribution averaging method to gradients. The study of trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and total runtime complexity is relevant to large-scale graph ranking.
- Empirical indications: On the given dataset, the proposed methods reportedly outperform the prior two-level gradient method in final loss, and the lower-level method converges faster than the power method.

Weaknesses
Quality (soundness and evaluation)
- Assumptions vs applicability: The gradient-free convergence (Theorem 1) requires convexity of f on Φ and interior optimum. However, f is generally nonconvex in ϕ; Section 4.2 assumes local convexity around ϕ∗ without justification or diagnostics to verify this in practice. The gradient-based theory handles nonconvexity but requires the inexact oracle property (5.1) with a finite L; the paper does not establish or upper bound L for f(ϕ) or verify Lipschitz gradient assumptions for this Markovian objective.
- Missing experimental rigor: Results are reported primarily in terms of the training objective f. Standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, pairwise accuracy) on held-out labels are not reported, making it hard to assess practical significance. Runtime/wall-clock and memory comparisons (including lower-level iterations and their costs) are absent; “significantly outperforms” is not substantiated with time-accuracy tradeoffs.
- Baselines: Only the prior Supervised PageRank method [21] is compared. No comparisons to other supervised random walk approaches or modern learning-to-rank baselines (even simple linear/ridge on features producing priors or personalized PageRank weights) are provided.
- Reproducibility: Key constants (e.g., β1), implementation details, and parameter tuning strategies are relegated to the supplement. Data is proprietary; no public reproduction or synthetic benchmark is provided.

Clarity
- The paper has numerous typos and stylistic issues, making parts hard to parse. Some notation is overloaded or insufficiently defined in the main text. Important assumptions (positivity constraints to ensure well-defined probabilities) and their enforcement via Φ are briefly stated but not thoroughly discussed. The handling of zero out-degree nodes and scale invariance claims deserve clearer treatment.

Originality
- The lower-level use of Nesterov–Nemirovski and the derivative extension are well-motivated. The upper-level theoretical contributions appear to be incremental adaptations of known inexact-oracle frameworks to constrained settings and to nonconvex composite problems with adaptivity. While useful, the novelty is moderate; the main contribution lies in integrating these pieces for Supervised PageRank with explicit complexity accounting.

Significance
- The methodological framework could be broadly useful for learning on graphs with inexact Markov chain computations. However, the experimental scope is limited (small labeled set, one proprietary dataset, few baselines), and the lack of standard IR metrics and runtime analysis limits the demonstrated impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide or bound the Lipschitz constant L for ∇f(ϕ) (or conditions under which (5.1) holds), given the dependence of π(ϕ) on ϕ through normalized features?
- How sensitive are results to α, the choice of Φ (center and radius), and regularization to ensure positivity and avoid near-zero denominators?
- Can you report NDCG@k and pairwise accuracy on held-out labeled pairs, as well as wall-clock time per method (including lower-level iterations)?
- How do the methods compare when using the power method vs Nesterov–Nemirovski at the lower level with equivalent accuracy targets?
- What are typical N1, N2 chosen by the gradient-based method, and how do they scale with graph size and desired ε?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen empirical evaluation: add standard ranking metrics on test data, runtime and memory profiles, and ablations over N, N1, N2, τ, and δ. Include additional baselines (e.g., supervised random walks, simple learning-to-rank on features).
- Provide theoretical conditions that ensure f has Lipschitz gradient (or a verifiable surrogate) and clarify the local convexity assumption in Section 4.2, or replace it with a nonconvex zeroth-order guarantee.
- Improve clarity: tighten notation, move key constants (e.g., β1) and proof sketches to the main text where critical to practice, and correct typos.
- Discuss robustness to feature scaling and positivity constraints; consider explicit regularization to ensure strictly positive denominators.
- Release code and, if possible, a public dataset or synthetic benchmark to facilitate reproducibility.

Pros
- Solid integration of inexact-oracle optimization with Markov chain computations.
- Convergence and complexity guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based regimes.
- Practical lower-level algorithm and derivative approximation with tunable accuracy.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on assumptions (convexity, smoothness) not justified for the actual objective.
- Limited empirical validation (metrics, baselines, runtime).
- Clarity and reproducibility issues.

Recommendation: Reject (leaning borderline). The theoretical developments are interesting and the application is relevant, but the current empirical evidence and justification of assumptions fall short of NeurIPS standards. With stronger experiments, clearer assumptions, and improved presentation, this work could be competitive.
