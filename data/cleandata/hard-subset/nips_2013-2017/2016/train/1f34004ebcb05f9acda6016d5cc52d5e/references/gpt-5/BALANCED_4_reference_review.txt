Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses learning in Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities depend on features with learnable parameters. The objective is a nonconvex loss aggregating pairwise violations across query-specific graphs. The key engineering challenge is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires stationary distributions and their derivatives for many queries, which must be approximated.

The paper proposes two two-level algorithms:
- A gradient-free method that extends random zeroth-order optimization to constrained problems with an inexact, biased zero-order oracle (function values approximated to a chosen tolerance), using randomization on the Euclidean sphere. It provides convergence guarantees for convex problems, with complexity bounds that explicitly couple upper-level convergence with lower-level approximation error (via a Nesterov–Nemirovski-type method to approximate PageRank).
- A gradient-based method for constrained, possibly nonconvex optimization with an adaptive stepsize, using an inexact first-order oracle that supplies both approximate function values and gradients. The oracle is built by extending a linearly convergent PageRank estimation method to compute stationary distributions and their parameter derivatives with a priori accuracy control.

The authors analyze the trade-off between inner-accuracy and overall complexity, and give total arithmetic operation bounds for both methods. Empirically, on Yandex browsing graphs (600 queries, ≈1.7K labeled pairs), both methods reportedly outperform the prior Supervised PageRank training approach [21], with the gradient-free method performing best on the provided loss and the gradient-based method using fewer iterations than the state-of-the-art for some settings.

This work situates itself among random walks with features (e.g., BrowseRank, supervised random walks), inexact-oracle optimization (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov, Ghadimi–Lan), and efficient PageRank computation (power methods vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski schemes).

Strengths
- Clear identification of an inexact-oracle bottleneck in learning graph-based ranking and a principled treatment of the bias/error trade-off between inner PageRank computation and outer optimization.
- Extension of random zeroth-order methods to constrained optimization with biased function evaluations, with explicit convergence bounds and parameter selection guidance.
- Adaptive projected gradient framework for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracles, including a backtracking strategy that does not assume known Lipschitz constants.
- Concrete algorithms tailored to Supervised PageRank with provable complexity, using a linearly convergent PageRank estimator and a novel derivative-accumulation scheme.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over the previous Supervised PageRank training baseline on the provided task; statistical tests are reported.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope: a small labeled set (≈1.7K pairs) and a single domain. Results are reported largely in terms of the surrogate loss, not standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP). Comparisons to broader learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., supervised random walks [Backstrom & Leskovec], LambdaMART) are missing.
- The gradient-free convergence guarantee assumes convexity on Φ; Section 4.2 essentially assumes local convexity around a local minimum. It remains unclear how often this holds for the proposed loss and whether the chosen Φ satisfies positivity constraints rigorously.
- The nonconvex gradient method’s stationarity guarantee is expressed in terms of ‖MK(xK−xK+1)‖, which is less interpretable than standard measures (e.g., projected gradient norm). A clearer mapping to common stationarity criteria would help.
- Practical aspects are underreported: no runtime, memory, or wall-clock comparisons; no ablation on inner tolerances N, N1, N2; no sensitivity to α. The claim of “lowest proved complexity” for the inner method versus [5] is stated but not empirically corroborated.
- Several presentation issues: incomplete/missing references and years; some typos; undefined constants (β1 relegated to supplement) that matter for implementability.

Quality
- Technical development is largely sound and well-motivated. The extensions to inexact oracles (both zero- and first-order) are reasonable and tie to existing theory. However, the reliance on local convexity for the zeroth-order method and the nonstandard stationarity measure limit interpretability of guarantees.
- The derivative computation scheme is interesting, but its numerical stability and cost relative to alternatives (e.g., implicit differentiation via linear solves with (I−(1−α)PT)) are not empirically explored.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but mostly clear. Some constants and assumptions are deferred to the supplement; the main text could be more self-contained. The dataset and feature descriptions are brief; evaluation metrics focus on the surrogate loss rather than rank-quality metrics.

Originality
- The combination of inexact-oracle optimization with supervised PageRank and explicit two-level complexity coupling is a solid, albeit incremental contribution. The gradient-free extension to constrained, biased oracles and the adaptive inexact composite gradient method for nonconvex settings are reasonable adaptations of known frameworks rather than fundamentally new algorithms.

Significance
- Methodologically, the paper may interest researchers working on optimization with inexact inner solves and on learning over large graphs. Practically, broader adoption would require stronger empirical validation and comparisons to standard ranking baselines, plus clearer runtime evidence.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- Can you report NDCG@k/MAP on Q2 and compare with supervised random walks and strong LTR baselines (e.g., LambdaMART using the same features)?
- How sensitive are results to α and to the choice of Φ? How do you ensure positivity constraints throughout optimization?
- Can you relate ‖MK(xK−xK+1)‖ to a projected gradient norm bound or stationarity gap?
- How do the inner gradient computations (Π̃) compare in runtime to solving the linear system for ∂π/∂ϕ via implicit differentiation?
- Please provide wall-clock times and memory usage for GBN, GFN, and GBP.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled treatment of inexact oracles with concrete, provably convergent two-level methods.
- Clear theoretical coupling between inner accuracy and outer complexity.
- Positive empirical improvements over a prior Supervised PageRank training baseline.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scope; lack of standard ranking metrics and strong baselines.
- Guarantees for gradient-free method hinge on local convexity; nonconvex stationarity measure is nonstandard.
- Practical performance (time/memory) not demonstrated; some presentation/citation issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The theoretical contributions are solid adaptations, and the application to Supervised PageRank is interesting. However, for NeurIPS standards, the empirical evaluation is too narrow, comparisons are limited, and practical performance is underreported. Strengthening experiments (standard ranking metrics, stronger baselines, runtime/memory, larger datasets), clarifying assumptions/guarantees, and improving presentation would make the work much more compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate on standard ranking metrics and include strong LTR baselines and supervised random walks.
- Provide comprehensive runtime and scalability analyses; ablate inner tolerances (N, N1, N2) vs. accuracy/speed.
- Clarify and justify the convexity assumption region; tighten positivity constraint handling.
- Relate the nonconvex stationarity measure to standard criteria; discuss gradient norm bounds if possible.
- Explore implicit differentiation for ∇π(ϕ) and compare numerically.
- Improve completeness and formatting of references; bring key constants (e.g., β1) to the main text.
- Consider broader datasets to demonstrate generality.
