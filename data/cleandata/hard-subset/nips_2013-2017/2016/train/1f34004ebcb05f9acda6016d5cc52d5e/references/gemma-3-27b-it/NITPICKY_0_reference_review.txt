This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, aiming to address the convergence issues of existing gradient-based methods and the limitations of applying standard optimization techniques to the problem. The authors present theoretical convergence guarantees for both a gradient-based and a gradient-free approach, along with an analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity. Experimental results on a web page ranking task demonstrate improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art.

While the paper tackles a relevant problem and presents a comprehensive theoretical analysis, a substantial number of minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from its overall quality. The writing style is often dense and verbose, making it difficult to follow the technical details. Numerous instances of imprecise language and lack of detail hinder reproducibility and comprehension.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contributions:** The convergence analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant contribution, addressing a known gap in the literature concerning Supervised PageRank optimization.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The investigation of the accuracy-complexity trade-off is valuable and provides practical insights.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, albeit on a single dataset.
*   **Problem Relevance:** Learning Supervised PageRank is an important problem with practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity. Equations are often introduced without sufficient explanation. For instance, Equation 2.1 lacks a clear explanation of what “〈ϕ1,Vi〉” represents in terms of feature interaction. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both `π0` and `π0(ϕ)` interchangeably). The introduction is overly long and doesn’t concisely establish the paper’s core contributions.
*   **Technical Details & Rigor:** Several technical aspects require more careful consideration. Lemma 1 claims an accuracy guarantee based on `N` iterations of Method 3.1, but the justification for choosing `N = ⌈ 1 α ln 8r δ1 ⌉ − 1` is not adequately explained. What is the source of this bound? Similarly, the constant `β1` in Lemma 2 is mentioned as "explicitly computable" but its definition or computation isn't provided in the paper or supplementary material. The justification for choosing Φ = {ϕ ∈ Rm : ‖ϕ− em‖2 ≤ 0.99} is weak; why is this set particularly well-suited, and how does it relate to the problem's constraints?
*   **Experimental Setup:** The experimental evaluation is limited. Only one dataset is used, and the parameter tuning process for the baseline (GBP) is described insufficiently. The statement that the differences in loss values were less than 10−7 for different L0 values is not informative. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to these parameters. The figures are not properly labeled.
*   **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:**
    *   The phrase "summing up" in the introduction feels informal for a scientific paper.
    *   The statement that the algorithms "outperform" the state-of-the-art isn't consistently supported by the data presented. Some differences appear marginal.
    *   Throughout the paper, mathematical symbols are sometimes used without proper definition (e.g. ‘s’ in Section 2).
    *   Typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies are present.
* **Redundancy**: The paper repeats ideas and derivations multiple times across sections (e.g. the description of the two-level optimization framework).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, accumulate to significantly impact the paper's readability and trustworthiness.
*   The lack of detailed justification for key technical choices and parameters undermines the rigor of the analysis.
*   The limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to generalize the findings.
*   The writing quality needs substantial improvement to meet the standards expected for NIPS publication.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Weak):**

*   The theoretical contributions are valuable and address an important open problem.
*   The experimental results provide some evidence of the proposed methods' effectiveness.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the theoretical interest of the work, I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents novel ideas and significant analytical efforts, the numerous technical details needing clarification, inconsistencies, and poor presentation outweigh the contributions. Addressing these issues would significantly improve the paper's quality and make it suitable for publication. The authors should focus on improving clarity, providing more detailed justifications for their choices, and expanding the experimental evaluation. The level of polish required to bring this paper up to NIPS standards is substantial.
