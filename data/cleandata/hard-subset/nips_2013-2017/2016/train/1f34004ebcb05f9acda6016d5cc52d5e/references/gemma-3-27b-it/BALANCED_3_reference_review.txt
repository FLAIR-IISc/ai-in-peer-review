This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, addressing limitations of existing approaches regarding convergence guarantees and practical performance. The authors present both a gradient-based and a gradient-free method, providing convergence analysis and experimental validation on a web ranking task. The core contribution lies in establishing theoretical convergence rates and demonstrating improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework [21], which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features into the transition probabilities. A key challenge in learning these parameters is the non-convexity of the loss function and the difficulty in accurately estimating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. Existing methods [6, 21] rely on power methods, which lack guaranteed convergence. This work addresses this gap by utilizing a more precise linearly convergent method [17] for estimating the stationary distribution and developing new gradient-based and gradient-free optimization algorithms with convergence guarantees.  The paper acknowledges and builds upon existing optimization techniques like those in [16, 7] but adapts them to the constrained, inexact oracle setting of Supervised PageRank.  It correctly identifies the limitations of applying standard global optimization methods due to constraints and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. The comparison to related methods for estimating stationary distributions, like those discussed in [5], is a strong point.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  A significant strength is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both proposed methods (gradient-based and gradient-free). This is a crucial contribution, as previous work lacked such guarantees. The authors meticulously detail the conditions under which convergence is achieved.
* **Novelty:** The adaptation of existing optimization techniques (gradient descent and gradient-free methods) to the specific challenges of Supervised PageRank – the constraints, the inexact oracle, and the non-convexity – is novel and valuable.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of the trade-off between accuracy of the lower-level algorithm (stationary distribution estimation) and the overall computational complexity. The lemmas and theorems provide concrete bounds on performance.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality.  The use of a real-world dataset (Yandex search logs) strengthens the practical relevance of the work.
* **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The paper is generally well-structured, with a logical flow of ideas. The mathematical notation is clear, and the connection between different parts of the paper is well-maintained.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough overview of relevant literature, including references to key papers in PageRank, optimization, and ranking.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The level of mathematical detail, while rigorous, can be daunting for readers not deeply familiar with optimization theory.  Some derivations and explanations could benefit from further simplification or intuitive explanations. While detailed, the lengthy proofs in the supplementary material could be consolidated or streamlined.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter settings, a more extensive sensitivity analysis of these parameters (e.g., the learning rate in the gradient-based method, the smoothing parameter τ in the gradient-free method) would be beneficial.  How robust are the results to changes in these settings?
* **Computational Cost:** The theoretical complexity analysis, while present, is quite involved. A more practical assessment of the runtime performance of the methods, perhaps using different graph sizes, would be helpful.  The discussion of computational cost could be made more concrete.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other baseline ranking algorithms (beyond Supervised PageRank [21]) would strengthen the evaluation.  How does the proposed approach compare to traditional PageRank or other state-of-the-art ranking methods?
* **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are based on a single dataset from Yandex. While realistic, results on additional datasets could improve the generalizability of the findings.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:**  There are a few instances where the notation is slightly ambiguous or could be improved for clarity (e.g., the use of 'ϕ' for both a parameter vector and a function).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding the theoretical guarantees of learning Supervised PageRank.
* The proposed methods are novel and demonstrably outperform the state-of-the-art in empirical evaluations.
* The rigorous analysis and detailed proofs are a testament to the technical soundness of the work.
* The practical application to a real-world web ranking task adds to the relevance and impact of the research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high level of mathematical complexity may limit accessibility for some readers.
* The limited comparison to other ranking algorithms weakens the overall evaluation.
* The potential sensitivity to hyperparameters and the lack of a detailed runtime analysis are concerns.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. While the complexity of the presentation and the limited comparisons are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Simplify the mathematical explanations:** Add more intuitive explanations to accompany the detailed derivations.
* **Expand the experimental evaluation:**  Include comparisons to other baseline ranking algorithms and consider evaluating the methods on additional datasets.
* **Perform a sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the performance of the algorithms.
* **Provide a more practical assessment of runtime performance:**  Report runtime measurements for different graph sizes.
* **Clarify any ambiguous notation.**
* **Consolidate proofs in supplementary material where possible.**
