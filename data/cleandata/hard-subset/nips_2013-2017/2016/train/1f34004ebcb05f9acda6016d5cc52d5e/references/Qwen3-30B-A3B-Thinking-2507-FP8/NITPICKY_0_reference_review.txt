

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank parameters: a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method. The authors claim their methods provide convergence guarantees and outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in both theoretical analysis and experimental results. While the problem is relevant and the theoretical framework is generally sound, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting issues, and insufficient experimental details that undermine its quality.

## Major Issues

**1. Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies**
- The paper states "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" - a clear typographical error.
- Inconsistent notation throughout: "π0" vs "π0(ϕ)", "[P]i,j" vs "P(ϕ)", and "a.o." without definition.
- Inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "1− α" should be "1 - α").
- The paper references "Supplementary Materials" for proofs but provides none, making verification impossible.

**2. Technical Ambiguities**
- Lemma 1 states "N = ⌈ 1 α ln 8r δ1 ⌉ − 1" without justification for this specific choice.
- The paper claims "the calculation of f̃(ϕ, δ1) requires not more than |Q|(3mps+ 3psN + 6r) a.o." but never defines "a.o.".
- In Theorem 1, the notation "EΞM−1f(yM ) − f∗" is inconsistent with the paper's definition of f as the objective function.
- The paper states "the most computationally hard on each iteration... are calculations of f̃(ϕk + τξk, δ), f̃(ϕk, δ)" but provides no justification for this claim.

**3. Experimental Deficiencies**
- The paper claims "GFN significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art algorithms on all test sets" but provides no statistical significance testing for these results.
- The hyperparameter selection (L0 ∈ {10−4, 10−3, 10−2, 10−1, 1}) is arbitrary and lacks explanation.
- The paper states "the differences are less than 10−7" but doesn't explain why this small difference is meaningful.
- The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about implementation details and computational resources.

**4. Theoretical Presentation Issues**
- The paper claims "our new algorithms not only outperform existing algorithms, but also are guaranteed to solve this learning problem" without defining what "guaranteed to solve" means in the context of non-convex optimization.
- The paper doesn't clearly explain why the problem is non-convex, which is crucial for understanding the need for non-convex optimization methods.
- The paper refers to "the state-of-the-art gradient-based method [21]" but doesn't provide a clear comparison with it in terms of convergence guarantees.

## Strengths

- The problem of learning Supervised PageRank is relevant to practical web search applications.
- The theoretical framework for inexact oracle methods is generally sound.
- The paper provides a clear description of the optimization problem and its challenges.

## Weaknesses

- The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation with statistical significance testing.
- The theoretical analysis relies on supplementary materials not provided.
- The presentation is marred by inconsistent notation and undefined terms.
- The paper doesn't clearly distinguish its contributions from previous work.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a relevant problem in web search ranking with a theoretically sound approach, the numerous technical inconsistencies, undefined terms, and insufficient experimental details significantly undermine its quality. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading, consistent notation, and more complete experimental reporting.

The paper's main theoretical contribution (a two-level optimization framework for Supervised PageRank) is not sufficiently novel given existing work on inexact oracle methods. The experimental claims are not supported by sufficient evidence, and the lack of statistical significance testing for the performance differences weakens the paper's main claim.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find the paper's numerous technical and presentation flaws to be substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's relevance to the field. The paper would require significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies and presentation flaws that prevent a thorough evaluation of its contributions. The experimental results lack statistical significance testing, and the theoretical analysis relies on supplementary materials not provided. While the problem is relevant, the paper does not adequately demonstrate its novelty or significance in the context of existing work on inexact oracle methods.
