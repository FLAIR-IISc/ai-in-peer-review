This paper presents two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors address a non-convex loss minimization problem arising in the context of ranking and provide convergence rate analysis for both proposed methods. While the problem setting is relevant and the desire to provide theoretical guarantees is commendable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and justification of design choices.

The introduction lacks sufficient motivation beyond stating that existing methods lack convergence proofs. It fails to clearly articulate *why* the convergence of existing methods is a critical issue in practice. The statement that standard global optimization methods cannot be applied is vague; a more precise explanation of their limitations in this specific context would be beneficial. Furthermore, the authors claim their methods outperform the state-of-the-art in experiments, but this claim feels weak given the lack of detailed experimental setup description (see below).

Section 2, while defining the Supervised PageRank model, contains several inconsistencies and lacks clarity. Equation (2.1) defines the restart probability, yet it’s not immediately obvious why the summation is necessary or what the role of ϕ1 is beyond setting these probabilities. The assumption that ∑ l∈U 〈ϕ1,Vl〉 should be non-zero is stated without explanation of what happens if it *is* zero, and how this could be handled. The description of handling zero outdegree nodes ([P (ϕ)]i,j = [π0(ϕ)]j) feels ad-hoc and requires further justification. The transition to query-dependent graphs in the latter half of Section 2 feels abrupt and not well-motivated. The notation quickly becomes overloaded (e.g., multiple uses of 'q').

Section 3, detailing the numerical calculation of f(ϕ) and its gradient, is overly dense and lacks sufficient explanation. Lemma 1 states an accuracy bound but doesn't provide enough context to understand its practical implications. The choice of the method [17] is justified by its complexity bound but lacks a comparison to other potential methods. The complexities quoted in Lemma 1 and 2 are presented without explaining the meaning of 'a.o.' (apparently arithmetic operations), which hinders understanding. The proofs referenced as "in Supplementary Materials" are crucial for assessing the validity of these lemmas and should be carefully scrutinized. The statement about choosing Φ within the simplex is unclear and its connection to the problem isn't fully explained.

Section 4, focused on gradient-free optimization, suffers from similar issues. The generalization of the framework from [16] is claimed, but the details of the adaptation are not sufficiently clear. Theorem 1's assumptions (f ∈ C1,1L) feel strong and may not hold in realistic scenarios. The lack of practical guidance on choosing parameters like τ is a weakness. The complexity result in Theorem 2 feels overly pessimistic and requires further analysis.

Section 5, the gradient-based method section, also has issues. The assumptions and conditions for Theorem 3 are not clearly stated and their relevance to the Supervised PageRank problem is questionable. The choice of the prox-function seems arbitrary. Theorem 4's complexity result again lacks practical insight.

Section 6, the experimental results, is the weakest part of the paper. The description of the dataset is insufficient. While the authors mention a commercial search engine (Yandex), details about the queries and relevance judgments are sparse. The hyperparameter settings are provided, but the rationale behind their choices is missing. Most critically, the comparison to the state-of-the-art method lacks sufficient detail. The reported p-values are encouraging but don't compensate for the lack of a thorough experimental setup description. The figures are not adequately explained.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* **Lack of Clarity and Detail:** The paper is overly dense and lacks sufficient explanation of key technical details.
* **Numerous Minor Flaws:** A multitude of inconsistencies, unclear notations, and unsubstantiated claims weaken the overall presentation.
* **Weak Experimental Validation:** The experimental setup is poorly described, and the claims of improved performance are not convincingly demonstrated.
* **Overly Optimistic Complexity Analysis:** The complexity results, while theoretically interesting, lack practical insight and may be overly pessimistic.
* **Insufficient Justification:** Many design choices and assumptions are not adequately justified.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The attempt to provide convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank is a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors demonstrate a commitment to theoretical analysis.
* **Relevance:** The problem of learning Supervised PageRank is relevant to information retrieval and ranking.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the theoretical contributions, the numerous flaws in clarity, detail, and experimental validation outweigh the strengths of this paper. The lack of practical guidance and the overly complex presentation make it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed methods. I recommend **rejection**. A significant revision addressing the aforementioned issues is required before this paper could be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the authors must provide clearer explanations of the technical details, a more thorough experimental evaluation, and a more practical discussion of the complexity results. The supplemental material needs to be accessible and complete.
