This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, one gradient-based and one gradient-free. The core contribution lies in providing convergence guarantees for these methods, addressing a gap in the existing literature where the state-of-the-art gradient-based method lacked such guarantees. The authors also present experimental results demonstrating that their methods outperform the existing approach in ranking quality.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing PageRank methods and the Supervised PageRank framework. The problem setting is well-defined, and the mathematical formulation is generally clear. The connection to previous work is adequately established, referencing key papers in the field of PageRank, optimization, and ranking.  The authors correctly identify the challenges associated with solving the non-convex loss minimization problem and highlight the need for specialized optimization techniques. The derivation of convergence rates for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant contribution. The experimental results provide empirical evidence supporting the theoretical findings, showcasing the practical benefits of the proposed approaches. The organization of the paper is logical, with each section building upon the previous ones.  The inclusion of detailed complexity analysis is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The presentation is often dense and difficult to follow.  While the mathematical derivations are present, the explanations surrounding them are sometimes lacking, making it hard to understand the rationale behind certain choices or assumptions. The notation is heavy, and could benefit from simplification.  For instance, repeatedly defining variables like `nq`, `m`, `r`, and `p` within each section feels redundant.  The paper extensively refers to "Supplementary Materials," which is problematic for a NIPS submission. Crucial details like proofs of lemmas and theorems should be included in the main text, or at least summarized with key insights. The experimental section is somewhat lacking in detail. The description of the dataset is brief, and the experimental setup could benefit from a more thorough explanation of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis. A more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art ranking algorithms beyond the baseline Supervised PageRank method would strengthen the results. The explanation of why the Euclidean ball constraint is chosen (Φ) is a bit weak; a more detailed justification would be helpful.  The discussion of practical implications, while present, feels superficial. Expanding on potential applications and addressing limitations in real-world scenarios would add value.  The choice of the norm ||.||2 seems arbitrary. A justification as to why this is more suitable than, say, the 1-norm would be beneficial. The statement that “this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle” is a strong claim that requires more rigorous substantiation with citations.  Finally, there are minor grammatical errors and stylistic issues throughout the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical contributions:** The paper provides convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, a significant advance over existing methods.
*   **Practical improvement:** Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach in ranking quality.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques with inexact oracle information is novel.
*   **Thorough analysis:** The paper includes a detailed complexity analysis of the proposed algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity and readability:** The paper is often dense and difficult to follow due to heavy notation and lacking explanations.
*   **Lack of detail in experiments:** The experimental section could benefit from a more thorough explanation of the setup and results.
*   **Reliance on supplementary materials:**  Critical information is relegated to supplementary materials.
*   **Limited comparison:** The comparison with other ranking algorithms is limited.
*   **Lack of justification:**  Certain choices, like the Euclidean ball constraint and the chosen norm, are not well-justified.




**Overall assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the theoretical contributions are valuable and the experimental results are promising, the issues with clarity, completeness, and experimental detail significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by including key proofs in the main text and providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, would substantially improve the paper's potential for acceptance. The core ideas are solid, but the presentation needs considerable refinement to meet the standards of NIPS.



**Recommendations for improvement:**

*   **Simplify notation and explanations:**  Reduce the number of variables and provide more intuitive explanations of the mathematical derivations.
*   **Include proofs in the main text (or summarize key insights):**  Avoid relying heavily on supplementary materials.
*   **Expand the experimental section:** Provide more detail on the dataset, experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and sensitivity analysis.
*   **Compare with a wider range of ranking algorithms:**  Benchmark against other state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Justify key design choices:**  Provide a rationale for the selection of the Euclidean ball constraint and the chosen norm.
*   **Revise for clarity and grammatical correctness:**  Improve the overall readability of the paper.
*   **Substantiate strong claims:** Provide citations to support the claim of novelty in combining gradient-free and gradient optimization with inexact oracles.
