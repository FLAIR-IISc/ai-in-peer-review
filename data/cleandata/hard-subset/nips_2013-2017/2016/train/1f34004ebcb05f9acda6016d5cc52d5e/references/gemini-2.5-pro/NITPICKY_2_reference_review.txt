### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the optimization problem associated with Supervised PageRank, a framework for learning node and edge weights in a graph for ranking tasks. The authors identify that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to its use of inexact gradient information derived from the power method. The core contribution of this work is the proposal of two new two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—with formal proofs of convergence. For the lower level (approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives), the authors employ the method from [17], for which they can control the approximation accuracy. For the upper level, they develop a gradient-free method for constrained optimization with an inexact zero-order oracle (adapting [16]) and an adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle (extending [7]). The paper provides complexity analyses for both algorithms and presents experimental results on a web page ranking task, showing their methods achieve lower loss values than the previous state-of-the-art.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The primary contribution of the paper is the rigorous theoretical analysis of optimization algorithms for Supervised PageRank. Providing convergence guarantees and complexity bounds in an inexact oracle setting is a significant and valuable step beyond the prior work [21], which relied on heuristics.
2.  **Novel Methodological Adaptations:** The paper successfully adapts existing optimization frameworks to a challenging setting. Specifically, the extension of a gradient-free method to handle constraints and a known error bound, and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle, are valuable contributions to the optimization literature.
3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall complexity of the two-level algorithm. This is a practical and important consideration for implementing such methods.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while containing some flaws (see weaknesses), demonstrate that the proposed methods can achieve a lower value of the objective function compared to the baseline, suggesting their practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper suffers from a notable lack of polish, which significantly hinders readability and credibility.
    *   **Formatting and Typos:** There are numerous typographical errors (e.g., "concider" in Section 2, "probablities" in Section 3) and formatting inconsistencies. A leftover template line ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)...") appears under the introduction. The notation for vector elements is inconsistent (e.g., `[π0]i` vs. `[π(ϕ)]i`). The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted, with missing information and inconsistent styles, making it difficult to use.
    *   **Clarity of Exposition:** The writing is often dense and grammatically awkward (e.g., "The main advantages of the first gradient-based algorithm: the guarantees of a convergence do not require the convexity..."). This makes the technical details harder to follow than necessary.

2.  **Questionable Theoretical Assumptions:** The analysis of the gradient-free method in Section 4.2 relies on a critical assumption: "f(ϕ) is convex on Φ". This is a very strong assumption that seems to contradict the paper's motivation of solving a generally non-convex problem. While the gradient-based method is analyzed for the non-convex case, applying a convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's convergence proof feels like a significant shortcut that limits the generality of that result.

3.  **Flawed Experimental Methodology:**
    *   **Baseline Comparison:** The stopping criterion for the baseline method (GBP) is described as when "the differences between the values of the loss function on the next step and the current step are less than −10−5". This condition is nonsensical; a decreasing loss function would have a negative difference, so this criterion would almost never be met unless the loss *increases*. This calls into serious question the validity of the experimental comparison with the state-of-the-art.
    *   **Missing Evaluation Metrics:** The paper only reports the value of the loss function (2.4). While this is the function being optimized, its practical meaning for a ranking task is indirect. Standard information retrieval metrics like NDCG or ERR are necessary to evaluate whether a lower loss value actually translates to improved ranking quality. Without these, the "significance" of the empirical results is unverified.
    *   **Hyperparameter Justification:** The choice of the Lipschitz constant `L = 10^-4` for the gradient-free method (GFN) is not justified. The text is also confusing, stating "we choose L in GFN to be equal to L0," conflating a parameter of GFN with the initial guess for the adaptive GBN.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Quality:** The theoretical ideas are the strongest part of the paper. The formalization of the inexact oracle and the subsequent convergence proofs are well-executed. However, the overall quality is severely diminished by the numerous presentation flaws, the strong and potentially unwarranted convexity assumption for the gradient-free method, and the flawed experimental comparison. This feels like an incomplete or hastily prepared piece of work.
*   **Clarity:** As noted above, the paper is not clearly written. Significant editing for grammar, style, and consistency is required. The reference section, in particular, needs a complete overhaul.
*   **Originality:** The work is original in its specific application and synthesis. While it builds on established lines of research in optimization ([7], [16]) and ranking ([21]), the combination of these elements to provide the first provably convergent algorithms for this specific learning problem is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** The problem of learning to rank with theoretical guarantees is important. This work could be significant if the methodological and presentational issues were addressed. Researchers in optimization and learning-to-rank could build on these ideas. However, in its current state, the questionable experimental results and theoretical assumptions limit its immediate impact.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting direction for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, its current execution is not up to the standards of NIPS. The combination of a poorly prepared manuscript, a critical and questionable assumption in the theoretical analysis of one of its core methods, and a flawed experimental comparison with the baseline makes it impossible to confidently accept the paper's conclusions. The sheer number of minor flaws, from typos to a broken reference list, suggests a lack of care that undermines the entire work. The authors have a strong theoretical core, but a major revision is necessary to address the numerous weaknesses before it can be considered for publication.
