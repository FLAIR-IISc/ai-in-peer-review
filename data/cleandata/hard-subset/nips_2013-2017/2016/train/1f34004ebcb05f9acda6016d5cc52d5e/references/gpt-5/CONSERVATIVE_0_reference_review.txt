Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The training objective is a pairwise squared loss over query-dependent graphs. The key difficulty is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires stationary distributions and their derivatives of parameterized Markov chains, which are only computable approximately.

The authors propose two two-level optimization schemes:
- Gradient-free upper level: a random gradient-free method over a constrained set with an inexact zero-order oracle. The lower level approximates the stationary distributions using a linearly convergent PageRank solver (Nesterov–Nemirovski 1988).
- Gradient-based upper level: an adaptive projected gradient method for (possibly nonconvex) composite objectives using an inexact first-order oracle (approximations to f and ∇f). The lower level additionally approximates derivatives of the stationary distribution via a generalization of [17], with complexity/accuracy trade-offs.

For both, the paper derives convergence guarantees and operation counts that account for the lower-level accuracy. Experiments on a proprietary Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries, ≈1.7K labeled pairs total) compare against the “state-of-the-art” method [21] and report lower training loss and faster convergence.

This line of work connects to: PageRank/HITS and weighted/random-walk ranking variants; supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec), and generalized supervised link analysis; zeroth- and first-order optimization with inexact oracles (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny; Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Ghadimi–Lan); and iterative PageRank solvers.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical issue: learning parameters of random-walk-based ranking with only approximate lower-level computations.
- Provides convergence-rate statements for both the gradient-free and gradient-based upper levels in the presence of inexact oracles, with explicit dependence on lower-level accuracy (δ) and iteration counts (N, N1, N2). This is a useful synthesis for two-level problems.
- Uses a linearly convergent PageRank estimator at the lower level and extends it to derivative estimation; the stated lemmas bound oracle errors and arithmetic operations, enabling end-to-end complexity bounds.
- Attempts to compare gradient-free vs gradient-based vs a known baseline on a real dataset.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Experimental validation is too limited to substantiate the claims. Only one proprietary dataset with ≈600 queries and ≈1.7K labeled pairs is used. No standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, P@k) are reported; the paper primarily shows optimization loss (Eq. 2.4). Without standard ranking metrics, it is unclear if the observed loss reductions translate into meaningful ranking improvements.
- Baselines are insufficient. The comparison is only to [21] (and an “untuned” model). Missing are supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec 2011), more recent supervised PageRank variants, and non-walk learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART) on the same features, which are standard for query-document ranking. This limits the significance of the empirical results.
- Practicality and scalability are not demonstrated. The graphs are small (≈11.7K vertices total across queries). There is no wall-clock, memory, or parallelization discussion. Given the per-iteration costs (dependence on m, p, s, |Q|, r, and logarithmic factors in α, δ), it is unclear whether the methods scale to industrial-sized graphs.
- Several key implementation choices are under-specified. The gradient-free method requires τ and δ selection tied to L; experiments set L=1e−4 without justification. For the gradient-based method, the schedule linking ε to δ, and δ to N/N1/N2 via Lemmas 1–2, is not instantiated in practice (no reported N, N1, N2). The derivative approximation of the stationary distribution is nontrivial; its numerical stability and variance are not evaluated.
- The theoretical guarantees rely on assumptions that are not verified in the application: the gradient-free algorithm assumes convexity of f over the feasible set Φ; the authors posit local convexity near a local minimum but provide no evidence. The inexact first-order oracle inequality (5.1) is assumed to hold with their approximations; while plausible, its constants and robustness in practice are not empirically probed.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and notation-heavy. Several constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement without intuition. Many proofs are omitted. Some typos and editorial issues remain (e.g., incomplete references). Detailed algorithmic parameters (τ, δ, N, N1, N2, stopping criteria for lower-level) should be made explicit in the main text to aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The optimization contributions appear incremental: constrained random gradient-free methods with inexact oracles and adaptive first-order methods with inexact oracles build directly on Nesterov–Spokoiny and Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov, respectively. The main novelty is adapting to constrained sets and tying δ to lower-level PageRank accuracies. The derivative estimator for the stationary distribution is an adaptation of known power-method techniques. Claims of being “the first time” to combine these ideas seem overstated.

Significance
- Without comparisons to standard learning-to-rank baselines and without public datasets/metrics, the impact on ranking practice is unclear. The theoretical framework is sound but, in my view, not sufficiently novel for NeurIPS unless accompanied by stronger empirical evidence or broader applicability.

Questions for the authors
- Please report NDCG@k (k=1,3,5,10) and MAP on a standard public dataset (e.g., LETOR, Yahoo L2R) and compare against SRW and LambdaMART using the same features.
- Provide wall-clock runtime and memory usage versus [21], including lower-level iteration counts (N, N1, N2) chosen via Lemmas 1–2.
- How sensitive are results to α, R, and the choice of L and τ in the gradient-free method? Provide ablations.
- Can you justify or empirically verify the local convexity assumption used to apply the gradient-free analysis?
- Please clarify the numerical stability and variance of the gradient approximation Π̃q(ϕ,N2), and whether regularization or damping was necessary.

Pros
- Clear articulation of a two-level inexact-oracle framework with end-to-end complexity accounting.
- Convergence guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based upper levels under approximate lower-level computations.
- Potentially useful methodology for other problems with embedded Markov chains.

Cons
- Insufficient empirical validation (datasets, metrics, baselines, scalability).
- Incremental theoretical novelty relative to established inexact-oracle frameworks.
- Some assumptions (local convexity) are unverified; several implementation details are underspecified.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper has solid analysis and a reasonable synthesis, but the empirical validation and comparative context are too weak for NeurIPS, and the theoretical contributions feel incremental. Strengthening experiments (public datasets/metrics, broader baselines, scalability), tightening claims, and clarifying implementation/theory links would significantly improve the submission.

Confidence: High.
