## NIPS 2024 Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the optimization problem within the Supervised PageRank (SPR) framework, aiming to learn node and edge weights based on features to improve ranking quality. The core issue addressed is the lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based methods due to inexact oracle information (approximated stationary distributions). The authors propose two novel two-level methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both incorporating a linearly convergent method for stationary distribution approximation. They provide convergence rate analysis for both methods and analyze the trade-off between accuracy, generalization, and computational complexity. Experimental results on a real-world web page ranking task demonstrate improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art SPR approach.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the broader context of graph ranking algorithms (PageRank, HITS), supervised ranking techniques, and optimization methods for non-convex problems. The references to related work in power methods, stochastic optimization, and constrained optimization appear comprehensive and relevant. The acknowledgement of limitations of existing optimization methods for the SPR problem is well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  A significant strength is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a key contribution, addressing a major shortcoming of previous SPR implementations. The theoretical work on inexact oracle optimization is valuable in itself.
* **Novelty:** The adaptation of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods to the constrained, inexact oracle setting of SPR is a novel approach. The combination of a linearly convergent method for distribution estimation with these optimization techniques is well-motivated.
* **Practical Improvement:**  The experimental results demonstrate improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art SPR method. This provides empirical validation of the theoretical advancements.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of the computational complexity and the trade-offs between accuracy, generalization, and complexity. This level of detail is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is reasonably clear, and the algorithmic steps are well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:**  While theoretically sound, the proposed methods appear computationally intensive. The nested structure of the two-level approach, combined with the requirements for accurate stationary distribution estimation, may pose practical challenges for very large graphs. The discussion of complexity is thorough, but a more detailed breakdown of the computational costs associated with each step would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of the algorithms likely depends on careful tuning of several hyperparameters (e.g., step size, smoothing parameter, accuracy parameters). The paper mentions parameter selection but lacks a comprehensive sensitivity analysis. A more thorough exploration of hyperparameter tuning strategies would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental results are positive, they are limited to a single dataset (Yandex web browsing data).  Evaluating the methods on a broader range of datasets, including different graph structures and feature types, would provide more robust evidence of their generalizability.
* **Assumptions about Convexity:** The authors state that the problem is assumed convex in a small vicinity of the optimal solution. While this is a common approach, a more detailed justification for this assumption would be helpful.  What guarantees do they have that the solution they find lies within this convex region?
* **Reproducibility:** The paper could benefit from providing more details about the implementation to aid reproducibility.  Specifically, providing code or detailed pseudocode for the lower-level stationary distribution estimation would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Supervised PageRank methods: the lack of convergence guarantees.
* The theoretical contributions are substantial and the convergence analysis is rigorous.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement in ranking quality.
* The detailed analysis of complexity and trade-offs provides valuable insights.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity may be a barrier to practical application on very large graphs.
* The limited scope of the experimental evaluation raises questions about generalizability.
* The paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of graph ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. While the computational complexity and limited experimental scope are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance contingent upon the following minor revisions:

1. **Complexity Breakdown:** Provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of each step in the algorithms.
2. **Hyperparameter Analysis:** Include a brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the strategies used for their selection.
3. **Reproducibility Details:** Consider providing more detailed pseudocode or even sample code for the lower-level stationary distribution estimation.
4. **Convexity Justification:** Provide a more thorough justification for the assumption of local convexity.
