## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the optimization challenges inherent in Supervised PageRank (SPR), a powerful framework for incorporating node and edge features into the classic PageRank algorithm. The authors identify the lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based SPR learning methods and the limitations of applying standard optimization techniques due to constraints and the inability to calculate exact loss function values.  They propose two novel two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free approach, both leveraging an improved iterative method for approximating the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk.  Crucially, they provide convergence analysis for both methods and experimentally demonstrate their superior performance compared to the state-of-the-art on a real-world web page ranking task.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the foundation laid by Lempel & Moran (PageRank) and the subsequent development of SPR by Zhukovskii et al.  It correctly identifies the core difficulty – the non-convexity and constrained nature of the SPR optimization problem – and situates itself within the broader landscape of optimization techniques applicable to this setting. The authors acknowledge and attempt to overcome limitations of prior work (e.g., power method inaccuracies), and appropriately position their work with respect to related optimization methods like those described by Nesterov and Polyak for smooth convex optimization. The consideration of inexact oracle methods places the work within the growing area of optimization under noisy or limited information. The comparison to existing SPR implementations and the acknowledgement of relevant work in randomized optimization (Agarwal et al.) demonstrate a good understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contributions:** The provision of convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant contribution.  Many SPR implementations lack such guarantees, making this work valuable from a theoretical perspective.  The convergence rate analysis, while complex, provides insight into the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
* **Novel Methodology:**  The utilization of a linearly convergent method (from Dhamankar et al.) for approximating the stationary distribution and the adaptation of existing gradient-free methods to the constrained optimization problem are novel approaches.
* **Practical Impact:** The experimental results on a real-world web ranking dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the state-of-the-art.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper includes a thorough complexity analysis of the two-level algorithms, considering the impact of lower-level accuracy on overall computational cost.  The inclusion of lemmas and proofs in the supplementary material enhances transparency and allows for rigorous review.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are reasonably clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Readability:** The paper is dense with mathematical notation and the derivations are quite involved. While the authors strive for rigor, this sometimes comes at the cost of readability, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the specific optimization techniques employed.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset (Yandex web browsing data) and a single task (web page ranking). While the results are compelling, it would be beneficial to see the methods evaluated on a broader range of graph structures and ranking tasks to assess their generalizability.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the algorithms to hyperparameter settings (e.g., Lipschitz constant L) is mentioned but not thoroughly explored.  A more in-depth analysis of how these parameters affect performance would be valuable.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the implementation of the algorithms. Specific choices of optimization parameters and software used would increase reproducibility.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the paper compares to the state-of-the-art SPR method, it doesn’t explicitly compare to other contemporary ranking algorithms or alternative optimization methods that might be applicable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of SPR with a theoretically grounded and practically effective solution.
* The convergence guarantees provided are a major strength, differentiating this work from many existing SPR implementations.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear performance improvement over the state-of-the-art.
* The detailed analysis of computational complexity provides valuable insights into the trade-offs involved in using these methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high level of mathematical complexity and density may make the paper inaccessible to some readers.
* The limited scope of the experimental evaluation raises questions about the generalizability of the results.
* The lack of implementation details hinders reproducibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of Supervised PageRank.  The convergence guarantees and experimental validation are compelling.  While the paper could benefit from improved readability, a more extensive experimental evaluation, and greater detail regarding implementation, its strengths outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** (with minor revisions).  I recommend accepting this paper pending minor revisions focused on clarifying the presentation, expanding the experimental evaluation (perhaps including additional datasets or a comparison to alternative ranking algorithms), and providing more implementation details.  Addressing these points would further enhance the impact and reproducibility of this valuable work.
