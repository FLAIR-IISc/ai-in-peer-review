### Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a generalization of the classic PageRank algorithm where restart and transition probabilities are learned from node and edge features. The core challenge is that the associated loss-minimization problem is non-convex, and the loss function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly, as they depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain. This is known as an "inexact oracle" setting. The state-of-the-art method for this problem [21] lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to this inexactness.

The authors make several key contributions. First, they propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms with provable convergence guarantees. On the lower level, both methods use an efficient iterative scheme [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, allowing them to precisely control the oracle's error. On the upper level, they introduce:
1.  A gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle. It features an adaptive step-size rule and guarantees convergence to a stationary point.
2.  A random gradient-free method, extending existing work [16] to handle constrained problems and a zero-order oracle with a known error bound. This method is proven to converge under a local convexity assumption.

A significant contribution is the detailed complexity analysis for both algorithms, which explicitly links the desired overall accuracy of the optimization to the required precision (and thus, computational cost) of the lower-level oracle. Finally, the authors conduct experiments on a real-world web ranking task, demonstrating that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of final ranking quality.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated. The development of general optimization frameworks for non-convex/constrained problems with inexact oracles is a solid achievement. The convergence analysis (Theorems 1 and 3) and the subsequent complexity analysis for the full two-level algorithms (Theorems 2 and 4) are rigorous and provide valuable theoretical insight. The paper is a complete piece of work, connecting a general theoretical development to a specific, important application and validating it empirically. The experimental comparison against the most relevant baseline [21] is appropriate and convincingly demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed methods.
*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set Φ. The authors acknowledge that the function is generally non-convex and state this assumption is for a small vicinity of a local minimum. While this is a common approach for local analysis, it would be beneficial to discuss the practical implications of this assumption. For instance, how sensitive is the algorithm if this local convexity is not met? The gradient-based method, which does not require this assumption, is a strong point of contrast.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but could be improved in a few areas.
*   **Strengths:** The structure is logical, moving from the model description to the lower-level oracle computation, then to the two general optimization frameworks, and finally to experiments. The introduction clearly sets up the problem, highlights the shortcomings of prior work, and summarizes the paper's contributions.
*   **Suggestions for Improvement:** The paper is notation-heavy, with many parameters (`N`, `N1`, `N2`, `δ`, `δ1`, `δ2`, `ε`, `L`, `R`, etc.) governing different levels of accuracy and the algorithm's behavior. While the symbols are defined, their interplay can be hard to track. A small table in the appendix summarizing the key parameters, their roles (e.g., "target accuracy for objective function," "smoothing parameter for GFN"), and their relationships would significantly enhance readability. The distinction between the general frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1, using `x`) and their application to the specific problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2, using `ϕ`) is clear, but a more explicit mapping of the general components (e.g., `h(x)`, `X`) to the specifics of the Supervised PageRank problem would be helpful for readers.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes a novel contribution to the field.
*   **Strengths:** While building on existing work in optimization with inexact oracles ([7], [25]) and random gradient-free methods ([16]), the paper provides significant new extensions. The development of an adaptive gradient method for *constrained non-convex* problems with an inexact first-order oracle is a valuable contribution. Similarly, adapting random gradient-free methods to the *constrained* setting with a known error bound is a novel and useful extension. The primary originality lies in successfully bridging the gap between these advanced optimization theories and the practical, challenging problem of learning Supervised PageRank, providing the first solution with formal guarantees.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Strengths:** Learning to rank on graphs is a fundamental problem in information retrieval and machine learning. This paper provides robust, theoretically-grounded algorithms to solve a state-of-the-art model in this area. The provable convergence guarantees are crucial for deploying such models in production systems like search engines, where reliability and predictable performance are paramount. Furthermore, the general optimization frameworks for inexact oracles (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) are of broader interest and could be applied to other large-scale machine learning problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are intractable, such as in variational inference or agent-based modeling. The paper advances the state of the art by replacing an ad-hoc optimization procedure with principled algorithms that perform better in practice.

**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem, a notable theoretical advance.
2.  **Generalizable Frameworks:** The two proposed optimization methods for inexact oracles are general enough to be of independent interest and applicable to other problems.
3.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The problem is important, and the proposed methods are shown to outperform the existing state-of-the-art on a real-world task, improving ranking quality.
4.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper includes a detailed complexity analysis that connects the accuracy of the oracle to the overall convergence rate, providing practical guidance for implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Local Convexity Assumption:** The theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption, which may be a limitation in practice for this non-convex problem. However, the authors also present a gradient-based method that does not require this assumption.
2.  **Clarity of Notation:** The density of notation can make the paper challenging to parse on a first read, though the core ideas are clear. This is a minor point that could be addressed with revisions.

**Conclusion:**
This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to both optimization theory and its application to graph-based ranking models. The authors tackle a difficult and important problem, providing two novel, theoretically-sound algorithms that outperform the previous state-of-the-art. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths. I strongly recommend acceptance.
