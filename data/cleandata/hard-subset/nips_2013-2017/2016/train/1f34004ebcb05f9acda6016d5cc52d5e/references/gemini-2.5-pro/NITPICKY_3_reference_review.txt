**Summary:**
This paper addresses the optimization problem associated with Supervised PageRank, where node and edge weights are parameterized linear functions of features. The authors identify that existing methods lack convergence guarantees because they rely on inexact calculations of the PageRank vector and its derivatives (an "inexact oracle"). The main contribution is the proposal of two two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-free and one gradient-based—that explicitly account for this inexactness. For both methods, the authors provide theoretical convergence rate analyses and complexity bounds. The gradient-based method is adaptive and suitable for general non-convex problems, while the gradient-free method's analysis is presented for convex problems. The authors claim that their methods outperform the prior state-of-the-art method in experiments on a real-world web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The primary strength of this work is in providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for optimizing the Supervised PageRank model. The lack of convergence guarantees in prior work is a significant gap, and this paper addresses it directly by formalizing the problem in a framework of optimization with an inexact oracle.
*   **Novel Methodological Contributions:** The paper presents a novel adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (Section 5.1). It also adapts existing random gradient-free methods to the constrained setting with a zero-order inexact oracle. These contributions are potentially of broader interest beyond the specific PageRank application.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem of learning to rank on graphs is important, and improving the optimization methods is a valuable contribution. The proposed methods appear to yield better results than the existing baseline, demonstrating their practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising contributions, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, technical clarity, and experimental methodology. These issues, in aggregate, make it difficult to fully endorse the paper for publication in its current form.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**
*   **Formatting and Typos:** The manuscript is replete with typographical and formatting errors. For instance, the first page contains an incomplete sentence fragment: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...". The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted, with missing journal/conference names, inconsistent author formatting, and incomplete entries (e.g., [1], [4], [9]). This lack of care suggests a hastily prepared manuscript and detracts significantly from its professional quality.
*   **Organization:** The paper's structure could be improved. For example, the choice of the feasible set Φ for the experiments (`‖ϕ− em‖2 ≤ 0.99`) is introduced and justified in Section 3, rather than in the model description (Section 2) or the experimental setup (Section 6), where it would be more appropriate.
*   **Confusing Notation:** The notation for query-dependent graphs in Section 2 is needlessly confusing. The authors state `Uq := U`, `π0q := π`, etc., which reads as a redefinition of symbols rather than the intended introduction of query-specific versions of those objects. This could be written much more clearly.

**2. Methodological and Theoretical Concerns:**
*   **Unjustified Convexity Assumption:** A major weakness lies in Section 4.2, where the analysis for the gradient-free method is applied. The authors state: "We assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) (2.4) is convex." This is a critical assumption that is not justified. The loss function f(ϕ) is generally non-convex, and assuming local convexity effectively sidesteps the core difficulty of the problem for this part of the analysis. This significantly limits the claimed generality of the gradient-free approach and seems to contradict the paper's broader positioning.
*   **Vagueness of Constants:** The complexity analyses rely on constants like `β1` in Lemma 2, which is described merely as an "explicitly computable constant (see Supplementary Materials)". While deferring details is common, this leaves a notable gap in the main paper, as the magnitude of such constants can be crucial to the practical relevance of the complexity bounds.
*   **Choice of Oracle:** The authors state that the method from [17] is the "most suitable" for the lower-level problem, primarily due to its complexity bound. However, a more detailed comparison to other methods (e.g., Monte Carlo methods) in the context of an inexact oracle would strengthen this claim.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**
*   **Reproducibility:** The experimental description lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. The baseline method (GBP) stopping criterion is given as "less than −10−5", which is presumably a typo for a small positive epsilon. The selection of the Lipschitz constant `L = 10^-4` for the gradient-free method (GFN) is critical, as the step size depends directly on it, yet its choice is not justified in the main text.
*   **Dataset Description:** The characterization of the dataset is minimal. Providing statistics like the mean, median, and range of graph sizes per query would give a better context for the results.
*   **Clarity of Results:** While p-values are reported, the main results are presented in the text with references to Table 1 and Figure 1, which are not included in the provided paper text. This makes a full assessment of the empirical claims impossible.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper provides the first (to my knowledge) convergence guarantees for optimizing Supervised PageRank.
*   The general frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles (especially the adaptive non-convex gradient method) are valuable contributions.
*   The empirical results, as described, suggest a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of polish, including numerous typos and a severely malformed reference list, which is below the standard for NIPS.
*   The crucial assumption of local convexity for the gradient-free method's analysis is a significant theoretical weakness that is not adequately addressed or justified.
*   The experimental section lacks the detail needed for reproducibility and a full assessment of the claims.
*   Multiple instances of confusing notation and poor organization hinder readability and understanding.

**Recommendation:**
While the core ideas of this paper are strong and address an important problem, the execution is deeply flawed. The combination of poor presentation, questionable theoretical assumptions for one of the core methods, and an irreproducible experimental section leads me to recommend **rejection**. The paper has the potential to be a solid contribution, but it requires a very substantial revision to correct the numerous deficiencies.
