Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank via two two-level optimization schemes. The lower level computes an approximate stationary distribution of a query-dependent Markov chain and, for the gradient-based variant, its derivatives w.r.t. parameters. The upper level uses either (i) a randomized gradient-free algorithm with an inexact zero-order oracle, or (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle. Theoretical contributions include: (a) using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linear-rate scheme to approximate PageRank and its derivatives with prescribed accuracy, (b) extending random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with uniformly bounded zero-order oracle error (Theorem 1) and deriving complexity for the full two-level method (Theorem 2), and (c) an adaptive projected gradient framework for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant (Theorem 3), with complexity for the full two-level approach (Theorem 4). Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph ranking task report that both proposed methods outperform a state-of-the-art gradient-based baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank and supervised random walks, and on optimization with inexact oracles (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny; Ghadimi–Lan; Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov). It also leverages the Nesterov–Nemirovski method for computing stationary distributions. The combination of inexact-oracle optimization with a structured PageRank lower-level solver is a natural application.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem (learning query-dependent graph ranking with features) and provides convergence guarantees under inexact lower-level computations.
- Lower-level: uses a linearly convergent scheme to approximate π and extends it to compute derivatives, with explicit accuracy/complexity trade-offs (Lemmas 1–2).
- Upper-level: develops constrained, inexact-oracle variants for both gradient-free and gradient-based algorithms with adaptive step control and provides complexity bounds (Theorems 1–4).
- Empirical section suggests practical gains over a baseline, and discusses the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and assumptions:
- Theorem 1 (gradient-free) is stated for convex f with x* ∈ int X; the application in §4.2 appeals to local convexity around a local minimizer and choosing Φ “small enough,” but this is unverifiable and undermines the guarantee on the actual nonconvex problem. Similarly, in §5 the paper claims a method for nonconvex problems, yet relies on an inexact first-order oracle satisfying (5.1) with an unknown L; the verification that f meets C1,1 and (5.1) on Φ is not provided in the main text.
- The approximation formula (3.1) divides by 1 − (1−α)N+1, yielding a normalized truncated geometric sum. This does not equal the standard truncated Neumann series for the fixed point; the rationale for this normalization and its effect on bias vs. convergence rate needs to be explained in the main text (currently deferred to the supplement).
- The complexity claim “lowest proved complexity bound among methods proposed in [5]” is asserted without quantitative comparison in the main text (no table/statement of bounds for the competitors).
- The derivative recursion (3.2)–(3.3) depends on dpi(ϕ)/dϕT, i.e., derivatives of normalized probabilities. The paper relies on a constant β1 “explicitly computable (see Supplementary),” but without any insight into its magnitude or dependence on graph parameters, making the practical meaning of Lemma 2 opaque.
- Mixing of norms and constants: §5.2 jumps between ∥·∥1, ∥·∥2, ∥·∥∞ bounds and uses δ1=δ2 and δ2=δ/(4R√m). The chain of inequalities that yields (5.1) from Lemmas 1–2 is only sketched; more detail is needed to ensure consistency of norms and constants.
- The stopping criterion for GBP in §6.2 “differences […] less than −10−5” appears sign-inconsistent (this would trigger on certain increases, not convergence).

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper contains numerous typos and formatting errors, e.g., “concider,” “probablities,” “Rm++1,” “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016),” stray tildes and indices ([Pq(ϕ)]̃i,i), inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts (π0q vs π 0 q), and inconsistent notation for vectors/matrices. These impede readability and reproducibility.
- Critical constants (β1), proof sketches, and algorithm details are deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks enough detail for an expert reader to implement or verify the methods.
- Reference list is severely incomplete/corrupted: many entries lack years, venues, titles; author names are garbled; ordering and punctuation are inconsistent.

Experimental evaluation:
- Metrics: Only the surrogate loss (2.4) is reported. For ranking, standard metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) should be reported on held-out data. The text claims gains in “ranking quality,” but no such metrics are shown in the main paper.
- Scale and realism: The dataset has ~600 queries with ~1.7K labeled pairs in total and ~11.7K vertices but only ~7.5K edges across all graphs (average outdegree <1). This is atypical for browsing graphs and raises questions about generality to larger, denser graphs.
- Reproducibility: Important experimental hyperparameters are underspecified (e.g., α value; τ in gradient-free; the exact choice of δ, N, N1, N2 from theory vs. tuning; projection radius rationale). No run-time or wall-clock comparisons are provided, only iteration counts; lower-level compute dominates, so time matters.
- Baselines: The “state-of-the-art” baseline (GBP) is vaguely described (raising a matrix and its derivative to power 100; step sizes 50–500), which does not reflect standard practice for PageRank learning and may be a weak baseline. Comparisons to other optimization approaches for nonconvex problems with inexact gradients (e.g., Ghadimi–Lan) are missing.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem with a coherent inexact-oracle framework.
- Provides convergence/complexity analyses for both zero- and first-order methods under oracle errors.
- The lower-level derivative computation for PageRank is of independent interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Numerous presentation issues (typos, broken references, notation) and missing details hinder verification and reproducibility.
- Key theoretical assumptions (convexity/local convexity, oracle properties) are not convincingly justified for the actual learning objective.
- Experimental evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics, strong baselines, and runtime analysis; dataset is small and atypical.
- Certain algorithmic details (e.g., normalization in (3.1), constants in Lemma 2) are unclear.

Recommendation: Reject
While the direction is promising and the theoretical framing is interesting, the paper in its current form has substantial clarity, methodological, and experimental shortcomings. With a thorough rewrite, stronger experimental evaluation (including ranking metrics and runtimes on larger benchmarks), complete and correct references, and clearer justification of assumptions and constants in the main text, this work could become publishable.
