

# Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, an extension of traditional PageRank that incorporates node and edge features to optimize ranking quality. The authors identify a key gap in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]—lack of convergence guarantees for the parameter learning process. They propose two novel two-level optimization methods (one gradient-based, one gradient-free) that provide theoretical convergence guarantees while outperforming [21] in empirical ranking quality. The paper makes significant contributions by developing a framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle information and applying it to the Supervised PageRank problem.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for both their gradient-based and gradient-free methods, addressing a clear limitation in the existing work. The key theoretical contributions include Lemmas 1-2 (proving accurate approximations for the loss function and its gradient) and Theorems 1-4 (establishing convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods). The proofs are well-structured, with detailed arguments provided in supplementary materials.

The experimental evaluation is appropriate for the problem domain, using a real-world web ranking dataset from Yandex. The comparison with the state-of-the-art method [21] is thorough, showing consistent improvements in ranking quality across multiple query sets. The authors also provide evidence of the efficiency of their lower-level approximation method compared to the power method used in prior work.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is precise, with well-defined notation and careful explanation of the Supervised PageRank framework. The two-level optimization approach is explained logically, with clear separation between the lower-level approximation methods and upper-level optimization algorithms.

The presentation of algorithms (Algorithms 1-4) is sufficiently detailed to understand the methodology, though some readers without optimization background might find the technical details challenging. The experimental setup is well-described, including dataset characteristics and parameter choices. The paper would benefit from a more concise summary of the key contributions in the introduction, which currently contains some redundant explanations.

## Originality

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field by addressing a specific gap in the Supervised PageRank literature. While the general framework of using inexact oracles in optimization has been explored in previous work (e.g., [16, 7]), the authors successfully adapt these techniques to the specific problem of learning Supervised PageRank, which has unique constraints and challenges.

The key originality lies in the combination of two-level optimization with the specific structure of the PageRank problem, particularly the ability to control the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and analyze the trade-off between this accuracy and overall algorithm complexity. This is distinct from previous approaches that either lacked convergence guarantees or used simpler approximation methods.

The paper properly situates its work within the literature, citing relevant prior work on PageRank (e.g., [18, 11]), Supervised PageRank [21], and optimization methods [16, 7].

## Significance

The problem of learning ranking algorithms is highly significant for search engines and recommendation systems. The authors demonstrate that their methods not only outperform the state-of-the-art in ranking quality but also provide theoretical guarantees that were previously missing. This combination of practical performance improvements with theoretical guarantees makes the work particularly valuable.

The significance is further enhanced by the paper's potential applicability to other graph-based learning problems beyond web ranking. The two-level optimization framework they develop could be adapted to other scenarios where the objective function requires expensive approximations.

## Strengths

1. Theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified with rigorous proofs.
2. The two-level optimization framework provides a principled way to handle the inexact oracle problem in Supervised PageRank learning.
3. The experimental results clearly demonstrate improvements over the state-of-the-art.
4. The complexity analysis provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
5. The paper successfully bridges theoretical optimization with practical application.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper focuses on a single application domain (web ranking), which limits the perceived broader significance. While the framework is generalizable, the authors could better emphasize potential applications to other graph-based learning problems.

2. The experimental section lacks comparison with more recent methods beyond [21], particularly those that might have addressed similar limitations. A comparison with [22] (Fresh BrowseRank) would strengthen the empirical claims.

3. The paper mentions that the gradient-free method avoids calculating derivatives for large matrices but doesn't provide concrete runtime comparisons to demonstrate this advantage in practice.

4. The parameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., for the Lipschitz constant L) is limited to the supplementary materials, which makes it harder to assess the robustness of the methods.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong technical contribution that addresses a clear gap in the Supervised PageRank literature. The paper provides theoretically sound methods with convergence guarantees that were previously missing, while demonstrating practical improvements in ranking quality. The quality of the theoretical work is high, and the experimental results are convincing for the specific application domain.

The main limitation is the narrow focus on a single application, though the framework itself has broader potential. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with recent methods and more explicit discussion of the generalizability of their approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contributions are solid, the empirical results are convincing, and the work addresses an important gap in the literature. While the application domain is somewhat narrow, the methodological contributions have broader implications for optimization with inexact oracles in graph-based learning problems.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, demonstrating how to combine theoretical optimization with practical application in a way that provides both guarantees and performance improvements. The work advances the state of the art in learning ranking algorithms and provides a framework that could be applied to other similar problems.
