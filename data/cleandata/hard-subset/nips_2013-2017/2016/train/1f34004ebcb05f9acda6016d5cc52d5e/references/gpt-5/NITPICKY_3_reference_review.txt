Summary
The paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features. It proposes two “two-level” algorithms: (i) a gradient-free method using a randomized sphere-based finite-difference estimator with an inexact zero-order oracle; and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle. On the lower level, both methods approximate the stationary distribution and, for the gradient-based method, its derivatives via a truncated geometric series based on Nesterov–Nemirovski (1999), claiming improved complexity over standard power iteration. The authors present convergence guarantees and complexity bounds under oracle inexactness and apply the methods to a Yandex user-browsing graph ranking task, reporting improved loss vs. a prior gradient-based baseline and the untuned model.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Supervised PageRank (e.g., Nesterov et al., Serdyukov et al.), supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec), and inexact-oracle optimization (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov). For gradient-free optimization it references Nesterov–Spokoiny and bandit optimization (Agarwal–Dekel–Xiao). The lower-level PageRank computation relates to Haveliwala’s power method and the linear-convergence summation method (Nesterov–Nemirovski). The adaptation to constrained, inexact oracles is a useful angle; however, several key differences/novelties relative to [16, 22, 25] are not made sufficiently precise.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical problem: learning personalized/random-walk-based rankings with feature-parameterized restart/transition probabilities.
- Provides convergence analyses for both a gradient-free and a gradient-based method under inexact oracles and constraints, which are relevant in this setting.
- Clear two-level architecture with explicit trade-off between lower-level accuracy (N, N1, N2) and upper-level convergence, with complexity estimates.
- Empirical evaluation on a real (Yandex) dataset and comparison to a published baseline.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality/technical soundness
- Assumptions and feasibility:
  - The feasibility set Φ must ensure denominators in (2.1) and (2.2) are nonzero; positivity of ϕ alone does not guarantee ∑l∈U⟨ϕ1, Vl⟩ and ∑l:i→l⟨ϕ2, Eil⟩ are strictly positive. Please state conditions on features to ensure this and justify that they hold in the dataset.
  - “Rm++1” appears multiple times; likely meant Rm++; please fix and ensure positivity is well-defined.
  - The gradient-free method requires evaluating f̃(x+τξ, δ) with x constrained to Φ. You assume x∗ ∈ int X and choose τ, but you do not ensure xk+τξk ∈ X for all k. Without either shrinking τ per-iteration or evaluating projections/smoothing restricted to X, your oracle may be undefined. This is a critical gap.
  - In Sec. 4.2 you assume local convexity of f over Φ to apply the convex analysis. This is undeclared/untested; provide a justification or diagnostics that f is convex on the chosen ball in practice, or use nonconvex analyses.
- Lower-level accuracy:
  - Lemma 1: the dependence N = ceil((1/α) ln(8r/δ1))−1 suggests a uniform bound translating π-approximation to loss-approximation via Aq. Please state the norm in which π̃q approximates πq, the bound on ∥Aq∥, and the exact Lipschitz constant of x → ∥(Ax)+∥2^2. Currently, the step from stationary distribution error to function error is opaque.
  - Lemma 2: Πk recursion (3.2)–(3.3) is not derived; dpi(ϕ)/dϕT is undefined (dimensions, sparsity assumptions). The norm ∥·∥1 and constant β1 are referenced but not given; since they enter logarithmic terms in N1, N2 and complexity bounds, please provide explicit expressions and their dependence on m, p, s, α.
  - The claim that method [17] “has the lowest proved complexity bound among methods proposed in [5]” is unsubstantiated without a quantitative comparison; please add a table or lemma comparing bounds.
- Gradient-free estimator:
  - The estimator gτ(x,δ) = (m/τ)(f̃(x+τξ,δ) − f̃(x,δ)) ξ is a two-point, biased estimator due to δ. The constants in Theorem 1 (stepsize h = 1/(18 m L), error terms τ2 L (m+8)/8, δ m D/(4τ), δ2 m/(L τ2)) need either a proof sketch or a citation with matching constants; otherwise implementers cannot reproduce the rate.
- Gradient-based method:
  - Inequality (5.1) resembles an inexact oracle; however, in Sec. 5.2 you set δ1 = δ2 and also δ2 = δ/(4R√m), which is inconsistent. Please reconcile.
  - The stationarity measure in Theorem 3 (‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖^2) should be related to a standard projected gradient norm; please clarify and interpret the stopping criterion.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typos and grammar: “concider,” “probablities,” “th Conference,” inconsistent indices (ĩ vs. ĩ→i), missing spaces, misuse of subscripts/superscripts. “a.o.” for arithmetic operations is undefined.
- Notation inconsistencies: π0q := π, Pq := P, πq := π used ambiguously; please index consistently by q. Define all norms when first used and stick to one where possible.
- Many references are incomplete or malformed (missing years/venues/authors, e.g., [1], [5], [7], [17], [22], [23], [24]). This is below NeurIPS standards.

Originality
- The combination of inexact oracles with constrained gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods is interesting, but much of the machinery builds on established work (Nesterov–Spokoiny; Devolder et al.; Nesterov’s composite methods). The specific derivative-approximation recursion and the complexity linkage to the lower-level PageRank approximation are useful but require clearer differentiation from prior analyses.

Significance/empirical evaluation
- Evaluation reports only the training loss surrogate; no standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP, P@k) are reported, despite claims about “ranking quality.” This is a major omission.
- Experimental details are insufficient for reproducibility: no wall-clock times or memory use; no ablation on N, N1, N2 vs. accuracy/runtime; no sensitivity to α; and no comparison to other learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) or to more recent supervised random-walk methods.
- Stopping rule for GBP: “differences … are less than −10−5” appears to stop when the objective decreases by at least 1e−5, which is illogical. Likely a sign error; please correct. The chosen baseline (“raise to power 100”) may be an unfair strawman; ensure best tuning.

Pros
- Addresses a practical and well-studied problem with nontrivial theoretical components.
- Provides convergence guarantees under inexact oracles for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods in a constrained setting.
- Potentially useful lower-level derivative-approximation scheme.

Cons
- Multiple technical gaps (feasibility of gradient-free evaluations; unclear constants; inconsistent δ choices; incomplete lemmas).
- Poor presentation quality and incomplete references.
- Empirical evidence insufficient and partially flawed; lacks standard ranking metrics and strong baselines.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has promising ideas, but the accumulation of technical ambiguities, presentation issues, and insufficient empirical validation prevents acceptance. I encourage the authors to: (1) fix the oracle domain issue and fully specify constants; (2) provide complete proofs or detailed sketches; (3) clean up notation/grammar and references; and (4) add comprehensive experiments with standard ranking metrics, strong baselines, ablations, and runtime analysis.
