### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a framework where node and edge importance are parameterized by feature vectors. The core challenge is that the associated loss function is non-convex, and its value and gradient can only be approximated inexactly. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms with theoretical convergence guarantees, improving upon the existing method from [21] which lacks such guarantees.

The first proposed method is gradient-based. It leverages a linearly convergent method [17] for the lower-level task of approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives with controllable accuracy. For the upper-level optimization, the authors develop a general adaptive projected gradient method for constrained non-convex problems that is robust to inexact first-order oracle information.

The second method is gradient-free. It uses the same lower-level method to approximate the loss function value (an inexact zero-order oracle). The upper level employs a random search method, adapted from [16], to handle constrained optimization and inexact function evaluations, under a local convexity assumption.

For both methods, the paper provides a rigorous complexity analysis that connects the accuracy of the lower-level approximation to the overall convergence rate. Experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate that the proposed algorithms outperform the previous state-of-the-art method in terms of the final loss value.

### Strengths (Reasons for acceptance)

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is providing the first formal convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The problem of optimizing a function with an inexact, computationally expensive oracle is fundamental and challenging. The authors provide a rigorous treatment for both gradient-based (non-convex) and gradient-free (locally convex) settings, which is a significant advance over the prior heuristic approach [21].

2.  **General and Novel Optimization Frameworks:** The paper develops two optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) that are of independent interest beyond the PageRank application. The adaptive gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle is a valuable extension of recent work [7, 25]. Similarly, the adaptation of random gradient-free methods to the constrained, inexact-oracle setting is a useful contribution. These frameworks are likely to be applicable to other large-scale learning problems with similar two-level structures.

3.  **Thorough Complexity Analysis:** The analysis in Theorems 2 and 4, which establishes the total computational complexity by considering both the upper-level iterations and the cost of the lower-level oracle calls, is a key contribution. It provides concrete insights into the trade-off between oracle accuracy and overall optimization efficiency. This level of detail is commendable and adds significant value.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by experiments on a large-scale, real-world dataset. The proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free approach (GFN), are shown to achieve a lower loss than the existing baseline (GBP). This demonstrates that the theoretical improvements translate into practical benefits. The comparison of the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] against the power method for the lower-level task further strengthens the paper's methodological choices.

### Weaknesses (Areas for improvement)

1.  **Local Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set Φ. The paper acknowledges that `f(ϕ)` is generally non-convex and states, "We assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) is convex." This is a strong and potentially restrictive assumption. The practical utility of the guarantee depends on being able to identify such a convex region, which is non-trivial. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the implications if this assumption is violated or practical guidance on how to choose Φ.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Comparison:** While the comparison to the previous state-of-the-art [21] is essential for demonstrating improvement in the optimization procedure, the evaluation of ranking quality is limited. The paper reports final loss function values, but not standard information retrieval metrics like NDCG or MAP. It is crucial to show that a lower loss on this specific pairwise objective function translates to improved ranking for users. Comparing against other standard learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART) using the same features would also provide a more comprehensive picture of the model's absolute performance.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper is notationally dense, which can make it challenging to follow. The distinction and relationship between various accuracy parameters (ε, δ, δ1, δ2) and iteration counts (N, M, K) could be made clearer, perhaps with a summary table.
    *   There appears to be a typo in the main text: "Algorithm 2" is used to refer to the gradient-free method in Section 4 and the adaptive gradient method in Section 5. While the algorithm box itself is labeled correctly as Algorithm 1, the text refers to "Algorithm 2 in Supplementary Materials". Then, the header for the algorithm in Section 5 is "Algorithm 2 Adaptive projected gradient algorithm". This is confusing and should be corrected to avoid ambiguity.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant theoretical and practical contributions to the problem of learning graph-based ranking models. The development of optimization methods with formal convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank problem is a solid and important result. The general optimization frameworks presented are valuable in their own right. The work is technically sound, original, and the results are significant for researchers in both optimization and web search. Despite some weaknesses, primarily the strong local convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's analysis and the limited scope of the ranking evaluation, the strengths of the paper are compelling. I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version to further improve the paper's impact.
