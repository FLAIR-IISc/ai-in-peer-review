

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing the non-convex loss-minimization problem with inexact oracle information. The authors develop gradient-based and gradient-free methods that provide theoretical convergence guarantees, which the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks. They analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity and demonstrate empirical improvements over [21] on a web ranking task.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in graph-based ranking, and the proposed methods do provide theoretical convergence guarantees that the state-of-the-art method lacks. The complexity analysis is comprehensive, and the experimental results show consistent improvements over the baseline. The authors correctly identify the limitations of the existing approach (no convergence proof) and address them.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Formatting Issues
1. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors in mathematical expressions, such as missing parentheses and inconsistent spacing (e.g., "〈ϕ1,Vi〉∑ l∈U 〈ϕ1,Vl〉" should be "〈ϕ₁, Vᵢ〉 / ∑ₗ∈U 〈ϕ₁, Vₗ〉"). These make the paper difficult to follow.

2. **Missing reference details**: The paper references "the review [5]" but [5] is not properly cited in the reference list, and the claim that their method has "the lowest proved complexity bound among methods proposed in [5]" is unsubstantiated.

3. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for matrices and norms (e.g., the description of ‖A‖₁ in Section 3 is actually describing the infinity norm, not the 1-norm).

4. **Incomplete references**: Several references are incomplete (e.g., "Efficient randomized algorithms for PageRank problem, A. Gasnikov, D. Dmitriev, Comp. Math. & Math. Phys, ,") which undermines the paper's credibility.

### Theoretical and Conceptual Issues
5. **Misleading novelty claim**: The paper claims "this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle" despite Nesterov and Spokoiny (2011) having already developed random gradient-free methods. This is a significant oversight.

6. **Incomplete explanation of inexact oracle**: The paper fails to clearly explain what makes the oracle "inexact" in this context or why it's fundamentally different from standard optimization settings. This is critical for the paper's main contribution.

7. **Lack of clarity in complexity analysis**: The paper states "arithmetic operations" but never defines what constitutes one. This makes the complexity bounds difficult to interpret and compare with previous work.

8. **Unclear experimental details**: The paper states they use "data of a popular commercial search engine Yandex" but provides no details about the dataset size, collection methodology, or how it was split into training and test sets. The claim that "our new algorithms not only outperform existing algorithms, but also are guaranteed to solve this learning problem" is unsubstantiated without clear experimental details.

### Minor but Significant Issues
9. **Typos and grammatical errors**: "concider" (Section 2), "probablities" (Section 3), "optimizations methods" (Abstract), and "the obtained gradient" (Abstract) should be corrected.

10. **Misleading claims**: The abstract claims "the main advantages of the first gradient-based algorithm: the guarantees of a convergence do not require the convexity" which should read "the convergence guarantees do not require convexity."

11. **Inconsistent notation in results**: The paper refers to "GFN" and "GBN" in Section 6.2 but never defines these acronyms in the main text.

12. **Missing comparison with other methods**: The paper claims to be the first to combine gradient-free methods with inexact oracle, but doesn't compare with other relevant gradient-free methods that could be applicable to this problem.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an interesting problem and presents theoretically sound methods, the numerous technical and formatting issues, misleading novelty claims, and incomplete experimental details significantly undermine its contribution. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to clarify its theoretical contributions, properly cite related work, and provide complete experimental details.

The most concerning issues are the misleading novelty claim (which is a significant problem in a theoretical paper) and the incomplete references, which suggest the authors may not have fully engaged with the relevant literature. The paper's technical content is strong, but the presentation and contextualization are inadequate for a NIPS submission.

I recommend rejection due to these significant issues, despite the paper's theoretical merits. The authors should address these concerns before considering submission to a top-tier venue like NIPS.
