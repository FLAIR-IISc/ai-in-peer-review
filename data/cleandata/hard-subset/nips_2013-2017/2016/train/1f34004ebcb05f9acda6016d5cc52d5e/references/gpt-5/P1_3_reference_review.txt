Summary
The paper tackles learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The learning objective is a pairwise square loss over query-dependent graphs, optimized over a constrained parameter set. The authors propose two two-level algorithms:

- A gradient-free (zeroth-order) method: lower level approximates stationary distributions via a linearly convergent iterative method (Nesterov–Nemirovski), producing an inexact zero-order oracle; upper level is a randomized gradient-free projected method adapted to constrained domains and to uniformly bounded function-value noise. They provide a convergence-rate bound and overall complexity accounting for the cost of the lower-level solver.

- A gradient-based (first-order) method: lower level also computes stationary distributions and their parameter derivatives via a generalization of the iterative method, yielding an inexact first-order oracle; upper level is an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite optimization with inexact oracle (a backtracking variant that does not require knowing L a priori), with convergence guarantees in terms of a gradient-mapping–like stationarity measure and complexity that again includes lower-level costs.

They analyze the trade-off between oracle accuracy (set via the number of inner iterations) and total complexity. Experiments on a proprietary Yandex query-dependent browsing graph (≈600 queries; ≈1.7k judged pairs) compare their methods to the prior Supervised PageRank learning approach [21] that used power iterations for both value and derivative estimation. They report better loss values and fewer outer iterations, and show the lower-level method is faster than power iteration.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank and related supervised random walks, and on inexact oracle optimization frameworks (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov for convex inexact oracles; Ghadimi–Lan for nonconvex stochastic first/zeroth order). The lower-level stationary distribution approximation leverages Nesterov–Nemirovski’s linear-convergence scheme; the paper extends it to derivative computation with error control. On the upper level, they adapt random gradient-free methods (Nesterov–Spokoiny) to constrained domains and to bounded function-value noise, and propose an adaptive projected gradient scheme for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracles.

Quality
- Theory: The lower-level error bounds (Lemmas 1–2) are carefully stated with explicit iteration counts to reach prescribed accuracy and with operation counts. The upper-level convergence statements for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1) under convexity and bounded noise, and for the adaptive gradient method (Theorem 3) for nonconvex composite with inexact oracles, are plausible extensions of established frameworks. The end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) correctly account for inner-loop work.

  Concerns:
  - The gradient-free upper-level guarantee requires convexity on Φ and x* in the interior; the learning problem is nonconvex. The paper assumes a locally convex region around a local minimizer. This is a strong and unverifiable assumption in practice; the algorithm’s behavior outside such a region is not analyzed.
  - The smoothness constant L of f(ϕ) is used in theory/algorithms but is not derived or bounded for the Supervised PageRank objective. In experiments, L (and L0) are set ad hoc (e.g., 1e-4), which weakens the claimed guarantees.
  - The inexact first-order oracle condition (5.1) is asserted to follow from Lemmas 1–2 and smoothness, but the paper does not establish smoothness (Lipschitz gradient) of f(ϕ) with an explicit L given the Markov chain dependence; this is nontrivial.
  - The constant β1 and several proof details crucial to tuning N1, N2 are deferred to the supplement; without them, reproducibility is difficult.

- Experiments: The dataset is small, proprietary, and only square loss is reported. There are no standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP, pairwise accuracy) on held-out data, no ablations on α/feature sets, and no wall-clock/runtime comparisons, which are important given the motivation. Only a single baseline (the prior Supervised PageRank learner) is compared; modern learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM/LambdaMART; Supervised Random Walks) are not included. Statistical testing is reported for loss only.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable but would benefit from substantial polishing:
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues impede readability.
- Several definitions/constants (β1, norms, assumptions on smoothness, stationarity measure used for stopping) are scattered or only in the supplement.
- The references are incomplete/garbled in places.
- Algorithmic descriptions are split between main text and supplement; key pseudo-code and parameter choices should be in the main paper.
- Complexity expressions are heavy with constants; some intuition or dominant-term simplifications would help.

Originality
- The combination of inexact-oracle theory with the two-level PageRank learning setup and the explicit derivative-approximation scheme is a useful synthesis.
- The gradient-free constrained method with bounded function-value noise on the sphere and the adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex inexact oracles are incremental extensions of known frameworks, but the end-to-end application and complexity accounting are novel for this problem class.

Significance
Improving learning for supervised PageRank and providing methods that avoid explicit matrix derivatives can be practically valuable for large graphs. However, due to the strong convexity assumption for the zeroth-order method, the lack of a proven smoothness constant, and limited empirical evaluation, the impact is currently moderate. Stronger experiments, additional baselines, and clearer practical guidance on setting δ/N/N1/N2 would increase significance.

Pros
- Clear identification of the two-level structure and rigorous control of inner-loop approximation errors.
- Convergence and complexity bounds for both gradient-free and gradient-based settings with inexact oracles.
- Practical algorithmic advantage of avoiding full derivative computation (gradient-free method).
- Empirical improvements over the prior Supervised PageRank learner.

Cons
- Reliance on unproven smoothness constant L and local convexity assumption for guarantees.
- Limited and proprietary experimental evaluation; lack of standard ranking metrics and baselines.
- Important constants/proofs deferred to supplement; some notational and presentation issues.
- No wall-clock/runtime comparisons despite algorithmic motivation.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a bound or derivation of L for f(ϕ), or at least a practical estimation procedure.
- Clarify the exact stationarity measure and stopping criteria for the nonconvex gradient method; report gradients or gradient-map norms in experiments.
- Add standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k) and compare to additional baselines (e.g., Supervised Random Walks, RankSVM/LambdaMART).
- Report wall-clock time and memory, and sensitivity to α and feature dimensionality; include ablations on inner-loop accuracies versus outer-loop progress.
- Move key constants (β1), algorithm pseudo-code, and main proof sketches to the main paper; fix typos and references.

Recommendation: Borderline. The theoretical framework is interesting and relevant, but the experimental evaluation is too limited and several assumptions underlying the guarantees are not validated. Strengthening experiments and clarifying/justifying assumptions would substantially improve the paper.
