### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a framework for ranking nodes in a graph by incorporating node and edge features. The authors identify a key deficiency in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: the optimization procedure, which relies on minimizing a non-convex loss function, lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. This is due to the objective function and its gradient being computed inexactly through iterative methods (e.g., power method).

The main contribution of this work is to propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms with provable convergence guarantees.
1.  A **gradient-free method** that adapts the random search framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is computed with a known, controllable error.
2.  A **gradient-based method** that extends the adaptive gradient descent framework for non-convex optimization [7] to the setting of an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors leverage an efficient algorithm [17] on the lower level to approximate the stationary PageRank distribution and its derivatives, allowing them to precisely control the oracle's inexactness. They provide a full theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates and overall complexity bounds. Experiments on a real-world web page ranking task show that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free one, outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach in terms of the final loss value.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution. It is the first work to provide optimization algorithms with provable convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. The adaptation of modern optimization theory (for inexact oracles and non-convex problems) to this specific, practical application is a significant and novel contribution. The analysis connecting the required oracle accuracy (`δ`) to the number of lower-level iterations (`N`) and the overall complexity is thorough and valuable.

2.  **Generalizable Frameworks:** While applied to Supervised PageRank, the optimization frameworks presented in Sections 4.1 (gradient-free for constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracle) and 5.1 (adaptive gradient for non-convex problems with inexact first-order oracle) are of independent interest. These frameworks could potentially be applied to other large-scale machine learning problems where objective functions or gradients are estimated, such as certain types of variational inference or policy optimization.

3.  **Clarity of Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly identifying the limitations of the prior art (lack of convergence guarantees). The problem is well-defined, and the challenges associated with the inexact oracle are laid out clearly, setting the stage for the proposed solutions.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments, while limited to one dataset, demonstrate the practical value of the proposed methods. The new gradient-free algorithm (GFN) consistently and significantly outperforms the previous baseline (GBP) across all test sets. This provides compelling evidence that the theoretically-grounded approach leads to better practical outcomes.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Strong Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. The paper acknowledges that the function is non-convex in general and justifies this by assuming `Φ` is a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that limits the generality of the theoretical guarantee. The work would be stronger if it discussed the implications of this assumption or provided analysis for the non-convex case, similar to what was done for the gradient-based method.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Details and Results:** The experimental section could be improved.
    *   The stopping criteria for the three compared algorithms (GFN, GBN, GBP) appear to be different, which makes comparing the number of "upper-level iterations" in Figure 1 potentially misleading. A comparison based on wall-clock time or the total number of arithmetic operations would provide a more complete picture of the practical efficiency trade-offs.
    *   The performance of the new gradient-based method (GBN) is mixed. While theoretically superior to GBP, it does not consistently outperform it in Table 1 and in some cases performs worse. A discussion of why this might be (e.g., loose constants in the theory, conservative adaptive line search) would add valuable insight.

3.  **Presentation:** The paper is quite dense with notation and technical details. While this is necessary for a theoretical paper, the main text could benefit from more high-level intuition. For example, a brief, intuitive explanation of *why* the method from [17] is more suitable than the standard power method for establishing convergence guarantees would be helpful for the reader. Similarly, short proof sketches for the main theorems in the main body could improve readability and reliance on the supplementary material.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Addresses a clear and important theoretical gap in a widely used model (Supervised PageRank).
*   Provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for this problem.
*   The developed optimization frameworks are potentially applicable to other ML problems.
*   The empirical results, especially for the gradient-free method, are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The theoretical guarantee for the best-performing empirical method (gradient-free) relies on a strong local convexity assumption.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more thorough (e.g., wall-clock time, more datasets, discussion of mixed results for the gradient-based method).

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Regarding the convexity assumption:** Please discuss this limitation more explicitly. Is it possible to extend the analysis of the gradient-free method to the non-convex case, perhaps to find a stationary point, similar to the analysis in [7]? Even if not, discussing when this local convexity might hold in practice would be beneficial.
*   **In the experiments:**
    *   Please clarify the stopping criteria for all algorithms and justify their fairness.
    *   Consider adding wall-clock time results to Table 1 to provide a direct comparison of computational efficiency.
    *   Please add a brief discussion on the mixed results of GBN versus GBP. Does this suggest that the theoretical advantages of GBN do not always translate to practice, or is there another explanation?
*   **For clarity:** Consider adding a small paragraph in Section 3 that provides more intuition about the benefits of method [17] over the power method in the context of building a provably convergent two-level algorithm. This would strengthen the motivation for its use.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a solid theoretical contribution that solves a well-motivated problem. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed. The work advances our understanding of how to reliably optimize models that rely on inexact, iterative computations.
