

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank parameters, a significant extension of the classic PageRank algorithm that incorporates node and edge features to improve ranking quality. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) to solve the non-convex loss minimization problem for learning these parameters, which the previous state-of-the-art method [21] lacked theoretical convergence guarantees for. The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21] and extends the optimization techniques from recent advances in stochastic optimization [7, 16, 25], specifically adapting them to handle the inexact oracle setting that arises from approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk.

The authors make a clear contribution by providing the first theoretical guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a significant limitation in prior work. Their methods are grounded in the observation that the standard power method for approximating stationary distributions can be replaced with a more efficient linearly convergent method [17], which allows them to establish tighter complexity bounds.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contributions**: The paper's strongest aspect is its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors successfully develop a framework for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization with inexact oracles, establishing convergence rates and complexity bounds that are novel for this specific problem. This addresses a critical gap in the literature, as the previous state-of-the-art [21] lacked convergence guarantees.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly defines the Supervised PageRank problem, the loss function, and the challenges in optimization due to the inexact oracle (requiring approximation of the stationary distribution). The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality across multiple test sets. The results are statistically significant (p-values < 0.005), and the authors carefully analyze the trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The work has direct application to web search ranking, a problem of significant practical importance. The authors demonstrate their methods on real-world data from Yandex, providing concrete evidence of their value.

5. **Clear Comparison with Related Work**: The paper properly situates itself against previous work, particularly [21], and clearly articulates how their contributions differ and improve upon prior approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Supplementary Materials Dependency**: While the main paper presents the high-level ideas and results, many key proofs and detailed complexity analyses are relegated to the supplementary materials. This makes it more difficult to fully assess the theoretical contributions without reading extensive supplementary content.

2. **Limited Comparison with Other Optimization Methods**: The experimental comparison is primarily against the state-of-the-art [21], but the paper doesn't compare against other optimization approaches that might be relevant for similar problems (e.g., stochastic gradient methods for non-convex problems).

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis**: While the paper mentions the choice of hyperparameters (L, ε, R), it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how sensitive the methods are to these choices. The supplementary materials reference a figure showing this sensitivity, but the main paper doesn't discuss it.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss potential limitations of their approach, such as how the methods scale to extremely large graphs (beyond the 11.7K vertices tested) or how they might perform on graphs with different structural properties.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank, addressing a critical gap in the literature by providing the first convergence guarantees for this problem. The methods are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated on a real-world ranking task. The results demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to both optimization theory and its application to ranking problems. While there are some minor weaknesses in the experimental design and presentation, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. Consider moving some of the more critical proofs from the supplementary materials to the main paper to improve accessibility.

2. Add a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the methods to key hyperparameters (L, ε, R) in the main paper.

3. Include a more detailed comparison with other optimization methods for non-convex problems (beyond [21]) in the experimental section.

4. Expand the discussion of potential limitations to better inform practitioners about when these methods might not be suitable.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 9/10 - The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and strong experimental validation.

**Clarity**: 8/10 - The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some technical details would benefit from being in the main text.

**Originality**: 9/10 - The combination of inexact oracle optimization with Supervised PageRank is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

**Significance**: 9/10 - The results have both theoretical and practical importance for ranking problems, with clear demonstrations of improvements over the state-of-the-art.

**Overall Recommendation**: Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by providing the first theoretical guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, with strong experimental validation. The work is well-executed, addresses an important problem, and will be of interest to both the optimization and information retrieval communities. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable in the camera-ready version.
