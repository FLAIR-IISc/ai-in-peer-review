Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features. The problem is formulated as minimizing a pairwise squared hinge loss over parameters ϕ, with the stationary distribution πq(ϕ) defined by a Markov chain for each query. The main challenge is that f(ϕ) and ∇f(ϕ) are not available in closed form; they require approximating the stationary distribution and its sensitivities, and the overall optimization is nonconvex with constraints.

On the lower level, the paper adopts a linearly convergent method of Nesterov–Nemirovski [17] to approximate πq(ϕ) (Eq. 3.1) rather than the power method commonly used in PageRank and its supervised variants [18, 21]. It further proposes a scheme (Eqs. 3.2–3.3) to approximate derivatives dπq/dϕ by propagating linear recurrences coupled with an initial derivative term, providing infinity-norm error bounds and arithmetic complexity (Lemma 2). These lower-level routines yield inexact zero- and first-order oracles for f(ϕ).

On the upper level, two methods are proposed:
- A random gradient-free method with inexact zero-order oracle for constrained optimization (Section 4), extending ideas from random directional search and smoothing [1, 16] to constrained settings and to bounded function-evaluation error. The method uses projections and a spherical randomization, and provides an expected-suboptimality bound for convex objectives (Theorem 1), then specialized to the learning problem (Theorem 2).
- An adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant (Section 5), building on composite gradient methods [7, 25] but extending to constrained and inexact-oracle settings with line-search-like adaptivity. Theorem 3 bounds a stationarity measure, and Theorem 4 gives the overall arithmetic complexity for the learning problem when combined with the lower-level oracles.

Empirically, on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (600 queries, with query-dependent graphs and pairwise assessors’ labels), the proposed methods are compared against the state-of-the-art two-level gradient-based approach of [21] that uses the power method for both π and derivative computations. The gradient-free method (GFN) consistently achieves lower loss than the baseline across test sets; the gradient-based method (GBN) also improves on the baseline on one split and uses fewer upper-level iterations in some settings. The Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level solver converges faster than the power method.

Quality:
- Theory: The lower-level approximation bounds for f and ∇f under the chosen iterative schemes (Lemmas 1–2) are clearly stated with explicit iteration budgets N, N1, N2 as functions of desired error. The upper-level gradient-free method provides an expected-suboptimality bound for convex objectives with bounded-error oracles (Theorem 1), and the adaptive gradient method provides a stationarity guarantee in terms of the norm of scaled steps (Theorem 3). The mappings from desired ε to oracle accuracies and lower-level iterations are made explicit, and overall arithmetic complexity is derived (Theorems 2 and 4).
- Assumptions: The gradient-free upper-level guarantee requires convexity of f over Φ, whereas the learning problem is generally nonconvex; the paper assumes local convexity by choosing Φ as a small ball, but this is not verified or conditioned on specific feature/graph properties. For the gradient-based method, the stationarity measure bound is appropriate for nonconvex settings, but its relation to more standard projected gradient norms could be clarified.
- Experiments: The empirical section demonstrates improvements in the training objective and provides significance tests. However, evaluation is limited to the loss (Eq. 2.4); standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) on held-out data would better substantiate ranking quality improvements. Runtime comparisons and sensitivity to lower-level accuracies would clarify practical efficiency.

Clarity:
The overall structure and algorithmic ideas are understandable. There are numerous typographical errors and some incomplete citations; important constants (e.g., β1) are relegated to the supplement. Notation is dense but consistent. The connection between Theorem 3’s stationarity metric and practical stopping in Algorithm 4 could be explained more explicitly.

Originality:
The combination of:
- a lower-level linearly convergent PageRank solver with error-controlled gradient approximations,
- a constrained random gradient-free method with bounded inexact oracle values, and
- an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracles,
constitutes a nontrivial integration. Each component builds on known techniques ([17], [16]/[1], [7]/[25]) but extends them in directions directly applicable to the Supervised PageRank setting (constrained domain, inexactness, derivative computation). The novelty is moderate and primarily methodological in adapting/combining known tools with analysis tailored to this application.

Significance:
The problem of learning graph-based ranking with feature-dependent transition/restart probabilities is important. Providing convergence guarantees for two-level learning with inexact oracles addresses a gap in [21]. That said, the empirical evaluation is limited in scope (loss values, one baseline) and on non-public data, which constrains practical impact assessment. The theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle methods may be of broader interest in ML optimization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Provides convergence-rate guarantees for a two-level learning framework where previous methods lacked them.
- Error-controlled approximations for both π and its derivatives with explicit complexity; demonstrates faster lower-level convergence than the power method.
- Extends random gradient-free methods to constrained and bounded-error settings, and develops an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex problems with inexact oracles.
- Empirical improvements over a relevant baseline on a real dataset with statistical significance.

Cons for acceptance:
- Gradient-free method’s theoretical guarantee relies on convexity over Φ, not established for the target problem; the “guaranteed to solve” claim is stronger than supported for nonconvex f.
- Experimental evaluation focuses on the training loss; lacks standard ranking metrics, ablations, and runtime/wall-clock comparisons to validate practical gains.
- Presentation issues: typos, incomplete references, and some key constants deferred to the supplement hinder reproducibility and clarity.
- Limited baseline coverage (no comparisons to other learning-to-rank methods or alternative supervised random walk approaches).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Evaluate on standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k) and report both training and test metrics, along with wall-clock/runtime for lower- and upper-level routines.
- Provide an ablation on the lower-level solver (power vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski) and on oracle accuracies N, N1, N2 to illuminate accuracy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Clarify the conditions under which f is convex over Φ, or empirically probe the sensitivity to Φ’s radius; alternatively, discuss nonconvex guarantees (e.g., expected stationarity) for the gradient-free method.
- Strengthen and complete references; correct typographical errors; bring key constants (e.g., β1) into the main text or summarize their magnitude.
- Compare against additional baselines (e.g., RankSVM/LambdaMART or other supervised PageRank variants) to contextualize significance.

Overall, the work presents solid theoretical developments for inexact-oracle optimization in a practically relevant two-level learning setup, with promising but preliminarily presented empirical results.
