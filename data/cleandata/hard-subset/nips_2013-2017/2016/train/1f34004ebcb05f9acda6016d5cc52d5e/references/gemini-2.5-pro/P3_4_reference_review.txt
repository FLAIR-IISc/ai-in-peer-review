### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a framework where node and edge weights in a graph are parameterized by linear functions of their features. The core challenge is that the associated loss-minimization problem is non-convex, and the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly. Instead, they must be approximated via iterative methods (an "inexact oracle"). The state-of-the-art method for this problem uses a gradient-based approach with the power method for approximations, but lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence.

The authors make several key contributions. First, on the lower level (oracle approximation), they propose using a linearly convergent method by Nesterov & Nemirovski to approximate the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) with provable accuracy bounds. Second, on the upper level (parameter optimization), they propose two new two-level algorithms with convergence guarantees:
1.  A **gradient-free method** that adapts random search techniques to handle constrained optimization problems where the objective function is evaluated with a known error bound.
2.  A **gradient-based method** for general constrained non-convex problems that uses an adaptive line-search procedure to cope with an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence rate and derive the overall computational complexity, explicitly linking the required oracle accuracy to the desired optimization accuracy. Experiments on a large-scale web ranking task from a commercial search engine show that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Significant Theoretical Gap:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical treatment of a practical machine learning problem. The lack of convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank with inexact oracles was a clear open issue. This paper provides the first provably convergent algorithms for this problem, which is a substantial contribution.
2.  **Novel and General Optimization Frameworks:** The contributions in Sections 4.1 and 5.1 are valuable in their own right, beyond the specific application to PageRank. The extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained settings with an inexact zero-order oracle, and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle, are of general interest to the optimization community at NIPS.
3.  **Thorough Complexity Analysis:** A key contribution is the end-to-end complexity analysis. The authors successfully connect the number of iterations of the lower-level approximation method (e.g., `N` in Lemma 1) to the desired accuracy `epsilon` of the upper-level optimization algorithm. This provides a principled way to set the precision for the oracle, which is often done heuristically in practice.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The use of a large, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine (Yandex) demonstrates the practical applicability and relevance of the proposed methods. The consistent outperformance of both the gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods over the previous state-of-the-art (GBP) in terms of the final loss value is a strong piece of evidence for their effectiveness.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors
1.  **Clarity and Density:** The paper is technically very dense and can be difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and many crucial details and all proofs are deferred to the supplementary materials. While this is common, a bit more intuition in the main text, especially around the derivation of the oracle accuracy requirements (e.g., how `delta` in Theorem 1 relates to `delta1` in Lemma 1), would improve readability.
2.  **Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(phi)` is convex on the feasible set `Phi`. The authors acknowledge that the function is generally non-convex and state that `Phi` is assumed to be a small vicinity of a local minimum where `f` is convex. This is a very strong assumption that significantly limits the generality of this theoretical result. Could the authors comment on how restrictive this is in practice? Have they considered extending the analysis to non-convex settings, similar to the work of Ghadimi & Lan [7], which is cited for the gradient-based case?
3.  **Experimental Evaluation Metrics:** The experiments exclusively report the value of the loss function. While this is the direct objective of the optimization, it would be highly beneficial to also report standard information retrieval metrics like Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) on the test set. This would demonstrate that a lower loss value indeed translates to better ranking quality, which is the ultimate goal of the task.
4.  **Practicality of the Gradient-Free Method:** The complexity of the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) has a strong dependence on the dimension `m`. Given that the feature space can be large (`m = m1+m2 = 78` in the experiments), this method may not scale as well as the gradient-based one to very high-dimensional feature spaces. The experiments show it works well here, but some discussion on scalability would be welcome. The gradient-based method appears more practical for larger `m`.
5.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the Lipschitz constant `L` in the gradient-free method (GFN) is crucial as it determines the step size. The paper sets it to `10^-4` but does not provide a clear justification for this choice. While the gradient-based method (GBN) is adaptive, GFN is not. A sensitivity analysis for `L` would strengthen the experimental section.

### Detailed Comments
-   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical developments appear solid, building upon established results in optimization. The connection between the inexact oracle and the optimization algorithm is carefully handled. The experimental setup is well-designed.
-   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, but the density of the mathematical content makes it challenging. The description of the algorithms and the statements of the main theorems are clear, but the intuition behind them could be better explained.
-   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on existing optimization techniques ([7], [16], [17]), the synthesis is novel. Applying these advanced optimization concepts to provide the first provably convergent methods for learning Supervised PageRank is a significant and new contribution. The development of the general optimization frameworks for inexact oracles is also an original contribution.
-   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners. For researchers, it provides a rigorous foundation for optimizing models with computationally expensive or intractable components. For practitioners in areas like web search or recommendation systems, it offers reliable and more effective algorithms for tuning a powerful class of ranking models. The general optimization methods presented are likely to be built upon by others.

In conclusion, this is a very strong paper that combines deep theoretical insights from optimization with a practical application in machine learning. It solves an important open problem with rigor and validates its findings with solid experiments. Despite some minor weaknesses in presentation and a strong assumption in one of its theoretical results, the overall contribution is excellent and of high quality.
