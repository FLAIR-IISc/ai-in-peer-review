This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a generalization of the classic PageRank algorithm where restart and transition probabilities are parameterized linear functions of node and edge features. The core challenge is that the loss function, which depends on the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk, cannot be computed exactly. This leads to an optimization problem where one only has access to an "inexact oracle" for the function value and its gradient.

The authors' main contribution is to propose and analyze two two-level optimization algorithms for this problem, both of which come with theoretical convergence guarantees, a feature lacking in the previous state-of-the-art method for this problem [21]. The first is a random gradient-free method adapted from the work of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization and inexact zero-order oracles. The second is an adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex problems, extending the framework of [7] to a setting with an inexact first-order oracle. For both methods, the authors provide a rigorous analysis of the convergence rate and the total computational complexity, explicitly connecting the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (which computes the stationary distribution) to the number of iterations required by the upper-level optimization algorithm. Experiments on a real-world web page ranking task show that both proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality & Originality:** The paper is technically very strong. The primary contribution is not just the application to PageRank, but the development of general optimization frameworks for problems with inexact oracles, which is a common and challenging setting in machine learning.
    *   The extension of random gradient-free methods to the constrained case with a provably-bounded inexact oracle (Section 4) is a novel and valuable contribution.
    *   The development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle (Section 5) is also a significant theoretical result. Providing convergence guarantees to a stationary point in such a setting is non-trivial.
    *   The careful complexity analysis, which investigates the trade-off between the precision of the lower-level approximation and the overall computational cost, is a complete and impressive piece of theoretical work.

2.  **Significance:** The work is highly significant for several reasons.
    *   It provides the first theoretically-grounded learning algorithms for the Supervised PageRank model, moving it from a heuristic approach to one with provable convergence.
    *   The optimization frameworks developed are general and can be applied to a wide range of other machine learning problems where a sub-problem must be solved iteratively, such as bi-level optimization or training models that involve solving differential equations.
    *   The empirical results, showing significant improvements over the baseline on a real-world industrial dataset, demonstrate the practical value of the proposed methods.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured. It clearly separates the general optimization frameworks (4.1, 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (4.2, 5.2). The problem statement and model description are clear.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** While well-structured, the paper is extremely dense and theoretically heavy, which can make it difficult to parse. The connection between the various accuracy parameters (ε, δ, δ1, δ2) and the number of iterations (N, N1, N2, M, K) is complex. A more intuitive, high-level discussion of how these parameters are set and how they influence each other, perhaps with a diagram, would greatly improve readability.

2.  **Assumptions vs. Practice:** There is a slight disconnect between the theory for the gradient-free method and its application. Theorem 1 assumes the objective function is convex, but the authors acknowledge that the Supervised PageRank loss function `f(ϕ)` is generally non-convex. They state they assume `Φ` is a small vicinity of a local minimum `ϕ*` where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption, and the paper would be stronger if it discussed the implications of violating it or presented a theoretical result for the non-convex case, even if it were weaker (e.g., convergence to a stationary point).

3.  **Experimental Details:** The experimental section is solid but could benefit from more detail.
    *   The choice of the Lipschitz constant `L = 10^-4` for GFN is not well-justified. The theory requires `L` to be an upper bound on the Hessian's norm, which is hard to know. How sensitive is the algorithm's performance to this choice?
    *   The GBP baseline is stopped when the loss change is less than `-10^-5`. This seems unusual, as one would typically stop when the change is small in magnitude (e.g., `|Δf| < 10^-5`). This choice might have prematurely stopped the baseline, although the performance curves in Figure 1 suggest this is not the case.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   The use of the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] on the lower level is well-motivated, as it provides an explicit error bound necessary for the upper-level convergence theory. This is a key component that was missing from prior work.
*   The adaptive nature of the gradient-based method (Algorithm 2 in the supplement, described in Section 5) is a major practical advantage, as it avoids the need to know the Lipschitz constant `L` of the gradient, which is often intractable for complex models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental and practical problem: optimization with inexact oracles.
*   Provides the first convergence guarantees for learning an important ranking model (Supervised PageRank).
*   Develops two novel optimization algorithms with rigorous theoretical analysis (convergence rates and complexity bounds).
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming the state-of-the-art on a large-scale, real-world task.
*   The theoretical contributions are general and of potential interest to the broader optimization and machine learning community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is very dense and may be inaccessible to a broad audience without significant effort.
*   The convexity assumption for the theory of the gradient-free method is not fully justified in the context of the non-convex application.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to both the theory of optimization with inexact oracles and the practical application of learning graph-based ranking models. The theoretical work is rigorous, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are minor and mostly related to presentation and clarity, which can be addressed. The strengths, particularly the novel theoretical guarantees for a difficult and important problem, far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
