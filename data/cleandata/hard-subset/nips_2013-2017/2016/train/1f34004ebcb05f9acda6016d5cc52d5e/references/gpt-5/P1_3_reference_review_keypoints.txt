- Proposes two two-level algorithms for Supervised PageRank: a gradient-free zeroth-order method and a gradient-based first-order method, both using inexact lower-level stationary-distribution solvers.
- Lower-level solver: adapts Nesterov–Nemirovski’s linearly convergent iterative method to approximate stationary distributions and (for first-order) their parameter derivatives with error control.
- Upper-level zeroth-order: randomized gradient-free projected method adapted to constrained domains and bounded function-value noise, with convergence-rate bound and complexity including inner-loop cost.
- Upper-level first-order: adaptive projected gradient method (backtracking) for nonconvex composite optimization with inexact oracle, with convergence guarantees in terms of a gradient-mapping–like stationarity measure.
- Provides end-to-end complexity bounds that account for the trade-off between inner-loop accuracy (number of iterations) and total computational cost.
- Builds on prior work in Supervised PageRank, supervised random walks, and inexact-oracle optimization frameworks, combining these elements for the two-level setup.
- Theoretical contributions include explicit lower-level iteration counts and plausible extensions of inexact-oracle convergence results to the proposed methods.
- Major theoretical concern: zeroth-order guarantee requires convexity and interior x*, but the actual learning problem is nonconvex and this local-convexity assumption is strong and unverifiable.
- Key smoothness issue: the Lipschitz constant L of f(ϕ) is used but not derived or bounded for the PageRank-based objective; experiments set L ad hoc, weakening guarantees.
- The inexact first-order oracle condition is asserted to follow from lemmas and smoothness, but smoothness (Lipschitz gradient) is not established given Markov-chain dependence.
- Important constants (e.g., β1) and proof details crucial for tuning inner-loop iterations are deferred to the supplement, harming reproducibility.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: small proprietary dataset (~600 queries, ~1.7k pairs), only square loss reported, and no standard ranking metrics on held-out data.
- Baselines are inadequate: only compared to prior Supervised PageRank learner (power iterations); missing comparisons to modern learning-to-rank methods (RankSVM, LambdaMART, Supervised Random Walks).
- Empirical reporting lacks wall-clock/runtime comparisons, ablations on α or feature sets, sensitivity analyses for inner-loop accuracies, and broader statistical evaluation beyond loss.
- Presentation and clarity issues: numerous typos, scattered definitions/constants, incomplete/garbled references, and algorithmic details split between main text and supplement.
- Originality: useful synthesis applying inexact-oracle theory to two-level PageRank learning and derivative approximation; algorithmic extensions are incremental but application-specific novelty is present.
- Significance: potentially practical for large graphs by avoiding full derivative computation, but impact is moderate due to strong assumptions and limited experiments.
- Pros: clear two-level formulation, rigorous inner-loop error control, convergence/complexity bounds for inexact-oracle settings, and empirical improvement over the prior Supervised PageRank learner.
- Cons: reliance on unproven smoothness and local convexity assumptions, limited/proprietary experiments, deferred constants/proofs, notational and presentation shortcomings, and no runtime comparisons.
- Suggested fixes: derive or estimate L practically, clarify stationarity measure and stopping criteria, report gradient-map norms, add standard ranking metrics and stronger baselines, provide wall-clock/memory and sensitivity analyses, and move key constants/pseudocode to the main paper.
- Recommendation: Borderline — theory is interesting but experiments and validation of assumptions are insufficient; strengthening empirical evaluation and justifying assumptions would improve the paper.
