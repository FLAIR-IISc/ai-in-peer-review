### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank that incorporates node and edge features. The core challenge is that the associated loss-minimization problem is non-convex, and the objective function and its gradient (which depend on the stationary distribution of a Markov process) cannot be computed exactly. This sets up an optimization problem with an "inexact oracle."

The authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem, both of which come with theoretical convergence guarantees, a key limitation of prior work [21]. At the lower level, both methods leverage an efficient, linearly convergent algorithm [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives to a controllable accuracy. At the upper level, they develop:
1.  A **random gradient-free method** that extends existing work [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is computed with a known error bound.
2.  An **adaptive gradient-based method** that generalizes the framework of [7] to non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle (i.e., inexact function and gradient values).

For both complete two-level algorithms, the authors provide a full computational complexity analysis to reach a desired accuracy. Finally, they demonstrate experimentally on a real-world web search dataset that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art algorithm [21] in terms of final loss value.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. Its primary strength is the rigorous theoretical treatment of a practical and challenging optimization problem.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper successfully bridges the gap between the theoretical requirements of optimization algorithms and the practical limitations of computing the Supervised PageRank loss function. Providing convergence guarantees and complexity bounds where none previously existed is a significant contribution.
    *   The derivation of the inexact first-order oracle (Lemmas 1 and 2), which quantifies the number of lower-level iterations needed to achieve a desired upper-level accuracy, is elegant and essential to the overall theoretical framework.
    *   The generalization of the adaptive gradient method to non-convex problems with an inexact oracle (Section 5.1, Theorem 3) is a strong theoretical contribution in its own right, potentially applicable beyond the scope of this paper.
    *   The experimental results, while focused on one dataset, are well-executed. They clearly support the theoretical claims by showing that the proposed methods (GFN and GBN) achieve lower loss values than the previous state-of-the-art (GBP).

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) assumes that the objective function `f(phi)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. The authors acknowledge that the problem is generally non-convex but justify this by assuming `Φ` is a small vicinity of a local minimum. While this is a common approach, it is a limitation. It is worth noting that the gradient-based method does not require this convexity assumption, which makes it the more general of the two proposed solutions.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The separation of the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2) makes the paper's contributions easy to parse for readers from both the optimization and information retrieval communities.
    *   The introduction clearly motivates the problem, reviews the state-of-the-art, and explicitly states the paper's contributions.
    *   The model and the optimization problem are defined clearly in Section 2.

*   **Suggestions for Improvement:**
    *   The notation can become quite dense, with multiple accuracy parameters (`ε`, `δ`, `δ1`, `δ2`) and iteration counts (`N`, `N1`, `N2`, `M`, `K`). A small table summarizing these key parameters and their roles in the two-level algorithms could further enhance readability.
    *   While proofs are appropriately placed in the supplementary material, a brief, intuitive explanation in the main text for why the method of [17] is superior to the power method for the lower-level problem would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The work demonstrates strong originality in its synthesis and extension of existing techniques to create a novel, theoretically-grounded solution.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   While the paper builds on existing work for the Supervised PageRank model [21], the optimization approach is entirely new and addresses a fundamental flaw (lack of convergence guarantees) in the previous method.
    *   To my knowledge, this is the first paper to provide a complete, end-to-end complexity analysis for learning Supervised PageRank under the inexact oracle model.
    *   The specific theoretical extensions—adapting random search to constrained problems with a zero-order inexact oracle, and adapting projected gradient methods to non-convex problems with a first-order inexact oracle—are novel contributions to the optimization literature.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to have an impact on both researchers and practitioners.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **For the optimization community:** The paper provides two general and well-analyzed frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles, a setting that arises in many large-scale machine learning problems (e.g., training models where gradients are estimated via simulation or iterative sub-procedures). The non-convex gradient-based method is particularly powerful and general.
    *   **For the information retrieval community:** This work provides a principled and demonstrably better way to train an important class of feature-based graph ranking models. Having algorithms that are guaranteed to converge increases the reliability and predictability of model training, which is crucial for production systems like search engines.
    *   The paper successfully demonstrates how to combine recent advances in optimization theory with a concrete, large-scale application, serving as an excellent example for future work at this intersection.

### **Conclusion and Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to both optimization theory and its application in information retrieval. It rigorously addresses a known weakness in the state-of-the-art for learning Supervised PageRank by developing two new algorithms with provable convergence guarantees and full complexity analyses. The theoretical contributions are sound, and the experimental results validate their practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem.
*   Develops novel extensions to gradient-free and gradient-based optimization methods for the challenging setting of non-convex, constrained problems with inexact oracles.
*   The proposed methods are shown to outperform the existing state-of-the-art empirically.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and its contributions are significant for multiple research communities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The convexity assumption for the analysis of the gradient-free method is a minor theoretical limitation, although the paper's second (gradient-based) method does not share it.

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a complete and high-quality piece of research that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. I recommend acceptance.
