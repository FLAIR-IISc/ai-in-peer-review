## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank, a crucial technique for ranking in networked data. The authors address a significant challenge – the lack of convergence guarantees for existing methods – by proposing two novel two-level optimization schemes (gradient-based and gradient-free) and providing theoretical convergence analysis. While the paper is technically dense and could benefit from clearer exposition, the rigor of the analysis and the experimental validation demonstrate a solid contribution worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in Supervised PageRank, a framework that extends the classical PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features into the transition and restart probabilities.  The core issue is the non-convexity of the loss function and the difficulty of obtaining exact gradients. Previous work [21] used a two-level gradient-based method but lacked convergence guarantees. This paper proposes two two-level methods – one gradient-based and one gradient-free – coupled with a refined lower-level approximation of the stationary distribution using a method from [17], which demonstrably improves efficiency. The authors situate their work within the broader context of random walk-based ranking algorithms (PageRank, HITS) and supervised ranking techniques. They rightly identify a gap in the literature concerning convergence guarantees for these methods, particularly with inexact oracle information. They appropriately contrast their work with other optimization techniques that are unsuitable due to constraints or the inability to calculate exact loss values.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The most significant strength of this paper is the rigorous mathematical analysis.  The authors provide convergence rate estimates for both their gradient-based and gradient-free methods, a crucial element missing from prior work.  The analysis of the lower-level approximation method [17] and its impact on the overall algorithm’s complexity is particularly commendable.
* **Novelty of Approach:** Adapting and combining existing techniques – the two-level framework from [21], the approximation method from [17], and the gradient-free optimization approach from [16] – to address the specific challenges of Supervised PageRank is a novel and valuable contribution.
* **Practical Validation:**  The experimental results on a real-world web page ranking task demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed methods compared to the state-of-the-art [21].  This provides empirical evidence to support the theoretical findings.
* **Inexact Oracle Handling:** The paper explicitly addresses the challenges posed by inexact oracle information, a realistic scenario in large-scale optimization problems.  The theoretical framework developed is applicable beyond Supervised PageRank.
* **Complexity Analysis:** Detailed complexity analysis of both algorithms provides insight into the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is densely written and requires considerable effort to follow the mathematical derivations.  The notation is sometimes heavy and could be simplified.  More intuitive explanations of the core ideas would significantly improve accessibility. For example, the connection between the choice of the norm and the algorithm's performance is not thoroughly explained.
* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper makes several assumptions, such as the convexity of the loss function within a specific neighborhood, which are not fully justified or discussed.  Exploring the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions would strengthen the work.
* **Experimental Setup Detail:** While the experimental results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail about the experimental setup.  Specifically, the choice of hyperparameters (beyond those explicitly mentioned) and the baseline configurations used for comparison are not clearly explained.  It would be beneficial to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices.
* **Comparative Analysis:** While the paper outperforms [21], a comparison to more recent and widely used ranking algorithms (beyond Supervised PageRank) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the methods’ effectiveness.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous analysis and well-supported claims. The completeness of the work is high, providing both theoretical and experimental validation. The authors are careful about pointing out limitations and potential areas for future research.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its exposition. The mathematical derivations are dense, and the notation can be cumbersome. 
* **Originality (8/10):** The combination of existing techniques in a novel way and the development of convergence guarantees for a previously unaddressed problem demonstrate significant originality.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important and address a challenging problem in ranking algorithms.  The theoretical contributions and the practical validation suggest that these methods are likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners in the field.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
* Novel combination of optimization techniques.
* Strong experimental validation.
* Addresses a significant problem in ranking algorithms.
* Handles inexact oracle information effectively.

**Con:**

* Dense and difficult to follow exposition.
* Limited discussion of assumptions.
* Lack of detailed experimental setup information.
* Relatively limited comparative analysis.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016.  The technical contributions are substantial and the results are promising.  However, I strongly recommend that the authors revise the paper to improve its clarity and accessibility.  Specifically, they should simplify the notation, provide more intuitive explanations of the core ideas, and provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup. Addressing the discussion of assumptions will further strengthen the paper. The contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance despite these shortcomings.
