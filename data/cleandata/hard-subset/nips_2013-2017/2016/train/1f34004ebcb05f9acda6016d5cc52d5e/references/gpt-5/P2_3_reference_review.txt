Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The key difficulty is that the loss depends on stationary distributions of query-specific Markov chains, so only inexact function/gradient information is available. The authors propose two two-level approaches: (i) a gradient-based method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle; and (ii) a random gradient-free method for constrained convex problems with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, they use the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate stationary distributions and generalize it to approximate derivatives, providing explicit error–iteration tradeoffs. They establish convergence/complexity bounds for both upper-level algorithms with inexact oracles and analyze the tradeoff between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity. Experiments on a Yandex user browsing graph dataset compare their methods to a prior two-level gradient method and the untuned Supervised PageRank.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on Supervised PageRank (e.g., Serdyukov et al.) and supervised/random-walk ranking methods (Backstrom & Leskovec; BrowseRank). The lower-level computation uses the averaging/acceleration method of Nesterov–Nemirovski instead of the classical power method.
- Extends random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov & Spokoiny) and inexact oracle frameworks (Devolder et al.; Ghadimi & Lan) to constrained settings with bounded oracle errors and to an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives.
- Positions against the state-of-the-art two-level gradient method for Supervised PageRank that lacked convergence guarantees due to inexact power-method oracles.

Quality
Strengths:
- The lower-level analysis is careful: Lemma 1 provides an explicit N bound to control the loss approximation; Lemma 2 generalizes derivative computation and gives an infinity-norm gradient error bound. This is a sensible way to make inexact oracles “anytime” with target accuracy.
- The upper-level methods come with convergence guarantees under inexact oracle assumptions: Theorem 1 for gradient-free (convex), Theorem 3 for an adaptive projected gradient algorithm yielding a stationarity measure for nonconvex composite problems, and end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4).
- The paper explicitly reasons about the lower/upper-level accuracy tradeoff, which is central in practice.

Weaknesses:
- The gradient-free method requires convexity of f over Φ, and for the nonconvex problem the authors assume Φ is a “small vicinity” where f is convex. This is a strong, unverified assumption; no empirical or theoretical justification of local convexity is provided.
- The inexact oracle condition (5.1) for the gradient-based method is derived by combining Lemmas 1 and 2 with norm conversions; however, important constants (e.g., β1) are relegated to the supplement. Without clear expressions and magnitudes, it is hard to assess the practical tightness of the bounds.
- Experimental validation is limited: only one real dataset (600 queries, ~1.7k labeled pairs total), one baseline (the prior gradient-based method), and evaluation mainly in terms of the surrogate loss. There are no standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP), no wall-clock/iteration comparisons at scale, and limited hyperparameter sensitivity, which weakens claims of practical impact.
- The stopping criterion for GBP is unclear (“differences … less than −10−5”) and the power-method parameters may not be fairly tuned vis-à-vis the proposed lower-level method, leaving room for under-tuned baselines.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-structured with clear separation of lower/upper-level methods and theorems. However, there are grammatical issues, typos, and some confusing notation (e.g., switching norms; missing details on β1; incomplete references).
- Important experimental details are missing from the main text (exact numbers in Table 1; ranking metrics). Provide explicit expressions for constants, and clarify the step-size/accuracy schedules and stopping rules.

Originality
- The combination is incremental but nontrivial: adapting random gradient-free methods to constrained, inexact-oracle settings and proposing an adaptive gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracles, tied to a particular two-level learning-to-rank-with-random-walks scenario. The derivative computation scheme (Πk recursion) is a useful generalization.
- Related work is appropriately cited, but the differences from existing inexact-oracle methods could be made more explicit (e.g., what is new beyond Devolder et al. and Ghadimi & Lan in the nonconvex constrained case).

Significance
- Theoretically, the work provides end-to-end guarantees for a class of practical two-level problems that occur in graph-based learning-to-rank. This is valuable.
- Practically, the experimental evidence is not yet compelling: small dataset, limited baselines, and lack of standard ranking metrics and scalability demonstrations. This limits the immediate impact for practitioners.

Pros
- Sound formulation of inexact oracles for a hard two-level problem; clear lower/upper-level accuracy coupling.
- Convergence and complexity guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based variants.
- Empirical improvements over an earlier baseline on a real dataset.

Cons
- Strong convexity-in-vicinity assumption for the gradient-free method; unclear when it holds.
- Limited experiments (size, baselines, metrics), making the empirical claims less convincing.
- Some constants and assumptions are opaque; clarity and completeness need improvement.

Questions for authors
1) Can you report NDCG@k/MAP on held-out sets, in addition to the surrogate loss?  
2) How sensitive are results to α, R, ε, and to the lower-level accuracy parameters (N, N1, N2)?  
3) Please disclose β1 and other constants explicitly, and provide a practical recipe for setting δ, τ, and L0.  
4) Can you demonstrate scalability on larger graphs and provide wall-clock comparisons vs. power method baselines with tuned iteration budgets?  
5) Clarify the GBP stopping rule and ensure fair tuning across methods.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10)
The theoretical contributions are solid and relevant, bringing end-to-end guarantees to a practical learning-to-rank problem with inexact oracles. However, the empirical section needs strengthening (metrics, baselines, scale) and some assumptions/constant choices should be clarified. With revisions, this could be a valuable NeurIPS contribution.

Confidence: 0.7 (familiar with optimization with inexact oracles and learning-to-rank; did not verify supplementary proofs).
