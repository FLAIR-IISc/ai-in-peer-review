Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The authors propose two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle and (ii) a random gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, both rely on a linearly convergent method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate the stationary distribution π(ϕ) and, for the gradient-based variant, a new recursion to approximate its derivatives with respect to ϕ. On the upper level, they provide: (a) an adaptive projected first-order method for constrained, possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact oracle (deriving rates in terms of a gradient-mapping norm), and (b) an extension of random gradient-free optimization with biased function evaluations of bounded magnitude error over a constrained domain, with explicit parameter–accuracy trade-offs. They analyze the end-to-end complexity including the cost of the lower-level approximations. On a Yandex browsing graph dataset (600 queries), they report improved loss versus a prior gradient-based Supervised PageRank learner that uses power-method-based approximations.

Relation to prior work
The formulation builds on Supervised PageRank and BrowseRank. The lower-level use of the Nesterov–Nemirovski linear convergence scheme for PageRank is known, but its use in the learning procedure (and extension to derivative computation) is a useful variant compared to earlier power-method-based approaches and Andrew’s derivative power methods. The inexact oracle framework is closely related to Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov’s inexact oracle analysis for convex optimization and to Ghadimi–Lan’s nonconvex first- and zeroth-order methods; the paper adapts these to constrained domains and to bounded deterministic errors in function values, with specific bias–variance trade-offs for sphere-smoothing gradient-free estimators. The overall combination with a specific supervised PageRank loss is, to my knowledge, new, though several components are incremental extensions of known techniques.

Quality
- Theory: The lower-level error bounds (Lemmas 1–2) that propagate the approximation error in π and ∂π/∂ϕ to bounded errors in f and ∇f are plausible and technically valuable. The derivative recursion (Πk) mirrors the Neumann-series structure of the sensitivity of the stationary distribution and is a natural generalization of [17]. The upper-level gradient-free Theorem 1 quantifies the dependence on smoothing τ and oracle error δ; the convergence bound reflects the bias introduced by δ and smoothing. The adaptive gradient method (Algorithm 2) with inexact oracle and backtracking produces a standard gradient-mapping-type guarantee (Theorem 3). End-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) are informative in terms of problem sizes |Q|, m, p, s, r, α.
- Assumptions: The gradient-free method’s guarantee requires convexity of f on Φ, while the target problem is nonconvex in ϕ; the authors assume local convexity near a local minimizer, which weakens the global significance. The gradient-based method addresses nonconvexity via a stationarity criterion, which is appropriate. Parameter set Φ is a Euclidean ball within the positive orthant to ensure valid probabilities; the scale-invariance observation is helpful, though the effect of this constraint on expressivity is not studied.
- Experiments: The empirical study is limited. Only one real dataset (proprietary) is used. The primary metric reported is the training loss (pairwise squared loss with hinge); no standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) are presented. The baselines are restricted to the prior Supervised PageRank learner using the power method; there is no comparison to mainstream learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) that would contextualize the practical impact. Hyperparameter tuning and stopping criteria for the baseline seem ad hoc (e.g., raising the stochastic matrix to a fixed power, unusual stopping thresholds). Statistical testing is mentioned but detailed results (tables/figures) are missing in the main text.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but has numerous typos/grammar issues and occasional imprecision (e.g., “probablities”, “concider”, inconsistent notation). Several key constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement, which hinders self-containment. The algorithms are clearly stated, and the decomposition into lower/upper levels is well explained. However, the exposition would benefit from: clearer intuition for the derivative recursion; explicit statements of all assumptions near the main theorems; and a crisper discussion of when f is convex on Φ.

Originality
- Positive: Combining linearly convergent PageRank solvers with a principled inexact oracle framework for learning, and providing explicit complexity bounds that account for lower-level approximations, is a worthwhile contribution. The derivative computation scheme tailored to the NN method is a sensible extension.
- Limited: The upper-level methods and analyses are largely adaptations of established frameworks (Devolder et al.; Ghadimi–Lan; Nesterov–Spokoiny), extended to constraints and bounded deterministic errors. The novelty is incremental on the optimization side.

Significance
Methodologically, the work provides a more principled and efficient way to learn supervised random walk parameters with convergence guarantees, which is relevant to graph-based ranking/recommendation. However, the practical significance is undermined by the limited empirical validation and the absence of comparisons to strong ranking baselines. The theoretical contributions, while solid, are incremental and may be of narrower interest to the optimization community given existing inexact-oracle literature.

Pros
- Clear two-level design with end-to-end complexity accounting.
- Sound lower-level approximation of π and ∇π with tunable accuracy and provable bounds.
- Adaptive gradient method for inexact first-order oracle in constrained, nonconvex setting.
- Empirical indication that NN-based lower level is faster/more accurate than power method in this application.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: small labeled set, no standard IR metrics, limited baselines, proprietary data without reproducibility resources.
- Gradient-free method relies on local convexity; applicability to the nonconvex problem is not compelling.
- Several theoretical components are modest extensions of known results; the degree of novelty is limited.
- Clarity and presentation issues; important constants/assumptions deferred to supplement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) and include ablations on ε, N1, N2, and α.
- Compare against strong learning-to-rank baselines and ablate the impact of the NN lower-level vs. power method within the same upper-level scheme.
- Clarify and consolidate assumptions; bring key constants and proofs sketches into the main text.
- Improve writing and fix typos; standardize references and ensure completeness.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper addresses a relevant problem and contains technically correct analyses with some useful engineering of inexact-oracle methods to supervised PageRank. However, the optimization contributions are largely incremental, and the empirical evaluation falls short of NeurIPS standards for significance and reproducibility. Strengthening the experimental section and sharpening the novelty claims would substantially improve the paper.
