## NIPS 2024 Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, a framework that extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features into the random walk process. The authors identify limitations of existing optimization methods for this problem – primarily a lack of convergence guarantees and difficulties handling constraints – and propose two novel two-level optimization approaches: one gradient-based and one gradient-free.  Both methods leverage a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, and the authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees for both algorithms.  They demonstrate empirically that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach on a web page ranking task.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21]. It acknowledges the challenges faced in optimizing the parameters within this framework, referencing existing work on PageRank, HITS, and related ranking algorithms [18, 11, 8, 9, 19]. The authors position their work within the broader context of optimization methods for non-convex problems, citing relevant techniques like stochastic gradient descent [7, 16], and methods for estimating stationary distributions [17, 23, 24].  The comparison to Nesterov’s work on optimization with inexact oracles [32] is also relevant. The focus on combining random gradient methods with inexact oracle estimation is a less common, and potentially impactful, direction.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** A significant strength of this work is the provision of theoretical convergence guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms. This is a crucial contribution, as the original Supervised PageRank method lacked such assurances.
* **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of a linearly convergent method for stationary distribution approximation with both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques is a novel approach to this problem.  Offering both options provides flexibility depending on the characteristics of the data and computational resources.
* **Inexact Oracle Handling:** The paper explicitly addresses the challenges of working with inexact oracle information and develops a framework for analyzing convergence in this setting. This is particularly important for large-scale graph problems where exact computation is often intractable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the existing state-of-the-art.
* **Detailed Analysis & Complexity Bounds:** The paper provides a detailed complexity analysis of the algorithms, offering insights into the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. The supplementary material seems extensive, which is positive.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making it easier to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Problem Setting Specificity:** The focus is heavily tailored to the Supervised PageRank problem. While the authors aim to develop more general optimization techniques, the applicability of these methods to other domains is not fully explored. It’s unclear how easily the core ideas could be translated without the specific structure of the PageRank problem.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the paper demonstrates superiority over the baseline [21], the comparison is limited to one specific method. Comparing against other relevant optimization techniques, even briefly, would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithms likely depends on careful tuning of hyperparameters (e.g., step size, accuracy parameters). The paper doesn't delve deeply into the sensitivity of the results to these choices.  While they demonstrate some sensitivity analysis for the GBP algorithm, similar analysis for their algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis and algorithmic development.  Discussion of practical considerations, such as scalability to extremely large graphs or efficient implementation strategies, is limited.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of convexity within the feasible region (Φ) for the gradient-based method feels a bit strong and should be more thoroughly justified or discussed.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a solid theoretical foundation for solving a challenging optimization problem in the context of PageRank.
* Offers novel algorithms with proven convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements over the existing state-of-the-art.
* Contributes to the broader field of optimization by addressing the challenges of inexact oracle information.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scope of the work is relatively narrow, focusing primarily on the Supervised PageRank problem.
* The evaluation is limited, and more comprehensive comparisons are needed.
* The practical implications and scalability of the algorithms are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, and the authors have addressed a significant challenge in the optimization of Supervised PageRank models. While the work is somewhat narrowly focused, the novel techniques and rigorous analysis warrant acceptance.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10.  The paper is technically sound and offers a clear advancement, but the limited scope and evaluation prevent a higher confidence level. I suggest the authors consider adding a brief discussion of potential applications beyond Supervised PageRank and providing additional insights into hyperparameter sensitivity.



**Grading based on the NIPS criteria:**

*   **Quality:** 8/10 - Technically sound, well-supported claims, complete work within its defined scope.
*   **Clarity:** 8/10 - Clearly written and well-organized, but the mathematical details could be more accessible to a broader audience.
*   **Originality:** 7/10 - Novel combination of techniques, but the core problem (Supervised PageRank) is not entirely new.
*   **Significance:** 7/10 - The results are important within the context of PageRank and related ranking algorithms, but their broader impact is less clear.
