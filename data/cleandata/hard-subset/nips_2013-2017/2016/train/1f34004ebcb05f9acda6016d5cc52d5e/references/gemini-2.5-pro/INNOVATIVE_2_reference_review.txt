**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a framework that enriches the classic PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features. The authors identify a critical gap in the existing work [21]: the state-of-the-art optimization method, a two-level gradient descent algorithm, lacks theoretical convergence guarantees because it relies on inexact estimations of the stationary distribution and its derivatives via the power method.

The core contribution of this work is to place the learning problem on a firm theoretical footing. The authors propose two new two-level optimization algorithms, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both with provable convergence rates. To achieve this, they first replace the power method at the lower level with a linearly convergent method [17] that allows for controllable approximation accuracy. At the upper level, they make novel theoretical contributions to optimization with inexact oracles: (1) they develop a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex problems, generalizing prior work, and (2) they adapt random gradient-free methods [16] to handle constrained problems where function evaluations have a known error bound. The paper provides a full complexity analysis and demonstrates experimentally that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art on a real-world web ranking task.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is an excellent paper that marries strong optimization theory with a practical and important application in graph-based machine learning.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The main thrust is theoretical, providing convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for optimization algorithms under inexact oracles. This is a challenging but crucial area as many large-scale ML problems rely on approximations. The theorems appear sound and represent a solid contribution. The experimental validation, while focused on a single baseline, is well-executed on real-world data and clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed methods over the existing approach they aim to fix.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors do a commendable job of motivating the problem by pinpointing the theoretical deficiencies of prior work. They clearly separate the lower-level oracle approximation from the upper-level optimization, making the two-level structure easy to follow. The contributions are laid out precisely in the introduction.

**Originality:** The originality is the paper's most significant strength. While the Supervised PageRank model itself is not new, the approach to solving the learning problem is highly novel and constitutes a bold step forward. Instead of offering an incremental improvement, the authors fundamentally reframe the optimization as a problem of learning with an inexact oracle. The extensions of gradient-based and gradient-free optimization frameworks to handle constraints and non-convexity in this setting are significant theoretical contributions in their own right. This work sits at a valuable intersection of numerical methods, optimization theory, and machine learning, and it is rare to see these fields combined so effectively.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is twofold. First, for the learning-to-rank and graph ML communities, it provides the first provably convergent algorithms for a powerful class of feature-aware PageRank models. This moves the methodology from a heuristic to a principled approach, increasing reliability. Second, and perhaps more broadly, the optimization frameworks developed are applicable beyond this specific problem. The analysis of optimization with inexact oracles is of growing importance across NIPS, and the methods presented here could inspire similar rigorous treatments for other large-scale problems where exact function and gradient evaluations are intractable. The analysis of the trade-off between oracle accuracy and overall complexity is a particularly insightful and useful contribution.

A minor weakness is that the convexity assumption made for the analysis of the gradient-free method is somewhat restrictive, although the authors correctly point out that their gradient-based method is more general and handles non-convexity directly. Furthermore, the experimental comparison could have been broadened to other learning-to-rank baselines. However, given that the paper's primary goal is to provide a correct optimization procedure for the Supervised PageRank model, the direct comparison with the previous method [21] is the most relevant and is sufficient to demonstrate the value of their contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's main contribution is a novel theoretical framework for an important problem, not just an incremental improvement on an existing method. It introduces sophisticated ideas from optimization theory to the domain of learning on graphs.
2.  **Addresses a Fundamental Flaw:** It provides the first provable convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, correcting a significant theoretical gap in a previously published method.
3.  **Broadly Interesting Theory:** The contributions to optimization with inexact oracles are of independent interest and could have a significant impact beyond the specific application demonstrated.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The theoretical improvements are backed by experiments showing superior performance on a real-world task.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Application:** The paper focuses on improving the optimization of one specific model (Supervised PageRank) rather than proposing a new model.
2.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The local convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's analysis is a theoretical limitation.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance decisively outweigh the arguments against. The paper's novelty, theoretical depth, and significance are precisely what this conference should champion. It is an impressive piece of work that elevates the rigor in its application area while simultaneously contributing to core optimization theory.
