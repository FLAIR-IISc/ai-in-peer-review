

# Peer Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, an extension of standard PageRank that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem inherent in this framework. Their key contributions include theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, complexity analysis, and empirical validation showing improved ranking quality over the state-of-the-art [21]. The work builds on the Supervised PageRank framework [21] but provides the first convergence guarantees for the optimization problem, addressing a critical gap in the literature.

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide proofs for their convergence theorems (Theorems 1 and 3) and complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), which are well-structured and logically developed. The numerical experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] on a real-world web ranking task.

However, there are important quality concerns. The gradient-based method (Algorithm 4) requires a local convexity assumption in Section 5.2 ("we assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) is convex"), which contradicts the paper's claim of handling "general non-convex problems." This limitation should be more prominently discussed. The experimental validation is also limited to a single dataset (Yandex web browsing data) with only one ranking task, making it difficult to assess the broader applicability of the results. The paper claims that "the main advantage of the second gradient-free algorithm is that it avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix," but fails to provide empirical evidence of computational efficiency gains beyond the loss function value.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical notation and logical flow. The two-level optimization framework is well-explained, and the connection between the lower-level numerical methods (Section 3) and upper-level optimization algorithms (Sections 4-5) is clearly articulated. The supplementary materials provide necessary technical details for the proofs.

However, the paper is quite dense with mathematical notation, which may hinder readability for readers not deeply familiar with optimization theory. The experimental section (Section 6) would benefit from more detailed presentation of ranking quality metrics beyond the loss function (e.g., NDCG, MAP) to better assess practical significance. The hyperparameter choices (L, ε, R) are not well-justified, with the paper stating "the differences are less than 10^-7" without explaining why these values were chosen.

## Originality

The paper's main contribution is original in the context of the Supervised PageRank problem. While Supervised PageRank was introduced in [21], this work provides the first theoretical guarantees for its optimization. The adaptation of gradient-free methods [16] to constrained optimization with inexact oracle (Section 4.1) and the generalization of [7] to constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle (Section 5.1) represent meaningful technical contributions. The two-level framework for handling the inexact oracle in the context of graph-based ranking is novel.

However, the problem itself is not entirely new (it builds directly on [21]), and the optimization methods themselves are adaptations of existing techniques rather than completely novel algorithms. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how these methods differ from prior work in the optimization literature, particularly in relation to [5, 7, 16].

## Significance

The problem of learning ranking algorithms is highly significant for search engines and recommendation systems, making this work potentially impactful. The authors claim their methods "can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine," which is a strong claim that would be more convincing with additional experimental evidence on computational efficiency.

The theoretical guarantees are a significant contribution to the field, as the state-of-the-art method [21] had no convergence guarantees. The empirical results showing improved ranking quality over [21] are promising, but the limited scope of the experiments (one dataset, one ranking task) limits the significance assessment. The paper's claim that this is "the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle" is overstated, as similar ideas appear in [7, 16, 25].

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank, providing the first convergence guarantees for this problem. The gradient-based and gradient-free methods are well-motivated and properly analyzed. However, the experimental validation is limited, the local convexity assumption for the gradient-based method weakens the non-convex claim, and the significance of the computational efficiency claims is not fully demonstrated.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept

**Pro:** Strong theoretical contribution with convergence guarantees for a problem that previously lacked them; well-structured mathematical analysis; promising empirical results showing improved ranking quality.

**Con:** Limited experimental validation; local convexity assumption contradicts non-convex claim; computational efficiency claims not fully supported; some overstatements about novelty.

The paper would be significantly strengthened with broader experimental validation, clearer discussion of the local convexity assumption, and more detailed analysis of computational efficiency. With these improvements, it would be an excellent contribution to the optimization and ranking literature.
