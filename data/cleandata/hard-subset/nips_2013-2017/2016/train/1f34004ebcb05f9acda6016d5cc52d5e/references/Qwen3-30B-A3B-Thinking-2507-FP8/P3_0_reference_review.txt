

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods for learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a framework that extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors address a key limitation of prior work [21], which lacked theoretical convergence guarantees for their gradient-based approach, by developing both gradient-based and gradient-free methods with rigorous convergence analysis. The main contributions are: (1) a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, (2) a gradient-free method adapted to constrained optimization with inexact oracle, and (3) complexity analysis of both methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning. The authors carefully analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy (number of iterations in the power method) and overall computational complexity, which is crucial for practical implementation. Theorems 1-4 are well-motivated and properly contextualized within the constrained optimization framework.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper effectively frames the Supervised PageRank learning problem as a constrained non-convex optimization problem with an inexact oracle, which is well-justified by the inherent challenges in calculating exact stationary distributions for large graphs.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on real-world Yandex data demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality, with statistical significance confirmed through paired t-tests. The comparison across different graph sizes (Q12, Q22, Q32) shows robustness.

4. **Practical Relevance:** The paper properly acknowledges the computational challenges of large-scale graph ranking, and the proposed methods offer both theoretical guarantees and practical improvements over existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are conducted on only one dataset (Yandex) with a single query graph structure. The authors claim the algorithms "can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine," but without testing on diverse graph structures (e.g., social networks, citation graphs), it's unclear how broadly applicable their findings are. A comparison with other graph ranking methods (e.g., [12, 19]) would strengthen the empirical contribution.

2. **Overstated Novelty Claims:** The paper states "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization." However, recent work like [7, 16] already explores similar concepts for non-convex optimization. The authors should more precisely articulate how their specific combination for Supervised PageRank differs from these prior approaches.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the theoretical complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) are thorough, they don't provide clear guidance on practical performance. For instance, the complexity terms include large constants (e.g., 1024β1RL/αε) that aren't numerically evaluated. The paper doesn't address how the proposed methods scale to graphs with millions of nodes/edges, which is critical for real-world search engines.

4. **Theoretical Limitations:** The gradient-based method (Algorithm 4) assumes convexity in a local neighborhood of the optimum (Section 5.2), but the problem is fundamentally non-convex as acknowledged in the introduction. The convergence guarantees thus only apply to local minima, which might not be the global optimum.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the theory of optimization for graph-based ranking problems. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art are significant and well-documented. The main weaknesses are the limited experimental scope and the overstatement of novelty claims regarding the optimization framework.

The paper addresses an important problem with clear practical implications for search engines and recommendation systems. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical impact could be further strengthened by more comprehensive experiments on diverse graph structures and a clearer demonstration of scalability to very large graphs.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution with strong empirical validation, addressing a real limitation in the existing literature on Supervised PageRank. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the core contribution. With minor clarifications regarding the novelty claims and experimental scope, this would be an excellent NIPS paper.

**Key Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Rigorous complexity analysis of the two-level optimization approach
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over state-of-the-art
- Well-motivated problem formulation with clear practical relevance

**Key Arguments for Rejection (if any):**
- Limited experimental validation on diverse graph structures
- Overstated novelty claims regarding optimization framework
- Insufficient discussion of practical scalability for extremely large graphs

The paper is a strong candidate for NIPS, contributing both theoretical advances and practical improvements to an important problem in graph-based ranking. The authors have made a clear contribution to the field that advances the state-of-the-art in both optimization theory and graph learning.
