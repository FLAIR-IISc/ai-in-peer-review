

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank, a ranking algorithm that extends PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors claim to provide theoretical convergence guarantees for this problem, which they state was lacking in previous work [21]. The first method is a gradient-based approach using an adaptive projected gradient algorithm with inexact oracle, while the second is a gradient-free method adapted from [16] for constrained optimization with inexact oracle. The paper provides theoretical complexity bounds and demonstrates empirical improvements on a web ranking task compared to the state-of-the-art [21].

## Strengths

The paper makes a valid theoretical contribution by addressing the convergence guarantees for a problem that previous work [21] lacked. The authors correctly identify that the state-of-the-art method for Supervised PageRank has no convergence proof due to the inexact oracle nature of the problem. Their theoretical development for constrained optimization with inexact oracle (Lemma 1, Lemma 2, Theorem 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4) is technically sound and extends existing optimization theory to a new application domain. The complexity analysis is thorough, providing explicit bounds on computational requirements.

## Weaknesses

Despite the theoretical soundness, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong NIPS submission:

1. **Lack of meaningful empirical validation**: The paper claims "outperform it in the ranking quality in experiments" but provides no standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) to measure actual ranking quality. The only metric reported is the loss function value (Equation 2.4), which is a proxy for ranking quality but not a direct measure. Without standard ranking metrics, it's impossible to determine if the lower loss translates to better user experience.

2. **Insufficient experimental comparison**: The experiments only use a single dataset (Yandex with 600 queries) and compare against one baseline [21]. The paper lacks comparisons against other ranking algorithms (e.g., learning-to-rank methods like LambdaRank, RankNet) that are standard in the field. The paper also doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the reported improvements (e.g., p-values for paired t-tests).

3. **Theoretical guarantees may not translate to practice**: The theoretical complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) are quite pessimistic. For instance, Theorem 4 shows a complexity bound of roughly O(L(f(ϕ₀)-f*)/ε × (r|Q| + 6mps|Q|/α ln(1/ε))), which suggests that the computational cost could be prohibitively high in practice, especially for large graphs. The paper doesn't provide any runtime measurements to verify if these theoretical bounds translate to practical improvements.

4. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle." However, the core optimization techniques (inexact oracle methods) are well-established in the literature (e.g., [7, 16, 25]), and their application to this specific problem is a natural extension rather than a significant innovation.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to determine which components of the proposed methods contribute most to the performance gains. For example, it's unclear whether the theoretical improvements in convergence rates actually lead to faster convergence in practice.

6. **Missing practical considerations**: The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed methods scale to very large graphs (beyond the Yandex dataset) or how sensitive they are to hyperparameters (beyond reporting results for ε=10⁻⁶). The claim that "the main advantage of the second gradient-free algorithm is that it avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix" is undermined by the paper's own complexity analysis showing the gradient-free method has higher computational complexity.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the theoretical contribution is valid and technically sound, the empirical validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims of "outperforming the state-of-the-art in ranking quality." The lack of standard ranking metrics, limited experimental comparison, and absence of practical runtime analysis significantly weaken the paper's contribution. The theoretical results, while correct, are incremental extensions of existing optimization theory rather than a significant advance for the field.

The paper would be much stronger with:
1. Standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP) to measure actual ranking quality
2. Comparisons against multiple baselines beyond [21]
3. Runtime measurements showing practical computational benefits
4. Ablation studies to identify key components of the proposed methods
5. More detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical bounds

As it stands, the paper presents an incremental theoretical extension with insufficient empirical validation to justify its claims of practical superiority. The NIPS community would benefit more from a paper that demonstrates both theoretical novelty and practical impact with comprehensive empirical validation.
