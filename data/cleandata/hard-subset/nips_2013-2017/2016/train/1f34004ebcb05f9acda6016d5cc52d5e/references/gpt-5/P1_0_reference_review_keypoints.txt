- Proposes a two-level learning framework for Supervised PageRank with restart/transition probabilities linear in features.

- Lower level: replaces power iteration with Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration to approximate stationary distribution π and extends it to approximate the Jacobian; provides explicit iteration counts (N, N1, N2) and operation-complexity bounds.

- Upper level: introduces (a) a random gradient-free constrained method with an inexact zero-order oracle and spherical smoothing (Theorem 1), and (b) an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle and backtracking (Theorem 3).

- Instantiates both upper-level methods for the Supervised PageRank loss and gives end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4).

- Theoretical contribution: solid inexact-oracle analysis combining lower-level approximation lemmas with upper-level optimization frameworks and appropriate stopping criteria for nonconvexity.

- Gradient-free analysis extends prior bandit/smoothing methods to constrained domains with bounded oracle noise and carefully tracked error terms.

- Assumptions critique: gradient-free method assumes convexity over the feasible ball, weakening the nonconvex claim; Lipschitz constant L’s dependence on graph/feature parameters is not explicitly bounded.

- Requests a brief argument that the objective f is C1,1 and explicit bounds or conditions guaranteeing Lipschitz continuity of the gradient given π(ϕ) and its Jacobian.

- Experiments use a proprietary Yandex browsing-graph dataset and report lower training loss for GBN/GFN vs prior GBP, with statistical tests and faster convergence of the Nesterov–Nemirovski solver.

- Empirical limitations: evaluation only on training objective, missing standard held-out ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k), and lacking wall-clock time, per-iteration cost, and memory usage.

- Recommends ablations isolating (i) lower-level solver (power vs Nesterov–Nemirovski) and (ii) upper-level optimizer (GBP vs GBN) to pinpoint sources of improvement.

- Hyperparameter concerns: choices (e.g., L=1e−4, ε=1e−6) appear ad hoc; sensitivity analyses are insufficient.

- Clarity issues: numerous typos, notational inconsistencies, deferred definitions (e.g., β1), missing references details; suggests clearer presentation of the lower-level algorithm, pseudocode for two-level procedures, and explicit δ→N mappings.

- Suggests summarizing complexity in big-O form with key parameters (|Q|, p, s, r, m, α) and highlighting dominant terms.

- Originality: incremental but meaningful extension of gradient-free methods to constrained inexact settings and a notable synthesis for adaptive inexact first-order methods in nonconvex settings.

- Encourages empirical comparisons to supervised random walks and mainstream learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART/GBDT) which are currently missing.

- Significance: strong theoretical contributions for inexact-oracle optimization on Markov-chain-based ranking, but practical impact is unclear without stronger empirical evidence.

- Pros: coherent two-level framework, adaptive gradient method for nonconvex inexact oracles, extension of gradient-free methods, and a practical lower-level improvement over power iteration.

- Cons: empirical evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics and runtime/memory comparisons, convexity assumption limits generality of GF method, hyperparameter justification weak, and presentation/referencing issues persist.

- Key questions for authors: report held-out NDCG/MAP vs baselines; provide wall-clock times and memory for π/Jacobian computations; sensitivity of GBN/GFN to ε, α, L and practical effects on N, N1, N2; bounds/estimates for L in terms of graph/features; scalability limits for |Vq|, |Eq| and sparsity or AD tricks.

- Recommendation: borderline accept (leaning weak accept) contingent on strengthening empirical evaluation, runtime comparisons, broader baselines, and improved clarity/references.
