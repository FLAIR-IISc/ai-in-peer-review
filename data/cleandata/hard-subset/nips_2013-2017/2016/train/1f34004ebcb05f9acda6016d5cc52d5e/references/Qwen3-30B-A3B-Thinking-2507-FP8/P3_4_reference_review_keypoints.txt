- Proposes two-level gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for Supervised PageRank with an inexact oracle framework and provides convergence guarantees.
- Addresses lack of theoretical guarantees in prior work (e.g., [21]) by rigorously analyzing approximation errors of stationary distribution and derivatives.
- Presents detailed proofs (Theorems 1–4) and thorough complexity analysis linking lower-level approximation accuracy to overall cost.
- Empirically outperforms the state-of-the-art method [21] on a Yandex web-ranking dataset.
- Major concern: mismatch between convexity assumptions in some theorems (e.g., Theorem 1) and the stated non-convex problem setting, requiring clarification.
- The gradient-based convergence guarantees are limited to non-convex problems with a specific inexact-first-order-oracle structure, creating a theoretical discrepancy to resolve.
- Experimental design is appropriate but lacks comparisons to other modern ranking algorithms beyond [21], limiting assessment of relative significance.
- Scalability is unclear: experiments use a modest dataset (~11.7K nodes), so applicability to web-scale graphs (billions of nodes) is not addressed.
- Clarity is strong: well-structured writing, consistent notation, and a clear explanation of the two-level framework and inexact oracle concept.
- Practical guidance is lacking: relationship between approximation parameters (δ, N) and final accuracy ε is abstract and could be made more concrete for implementers.
- Originality: novel application of inexact oracle methods to Supervised PageRank, but insufficiently distinguished from prior inexact-oracle and stationary-distribution approximation works (e.g., [16], [25], [17]).
- Strengths: rigorous theoretical analysis, comprehensive complexity bounds, clear empirical gains over [21], and well-presented exposition.
- Weaknesses: convexity-theory mismatch, limited algorithmic baselines in experiments, insufficient scalability discussion, and use of square loss not aligned with standard ranking metrics.
- Recommendation: accept with revisions — clarify convexity assumptions, expand experimental baselines, discuss scalability to large graphs, and give practical parameter-selection guidance.
