Summary
The paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where node and edge transition/restart probabilities are linear functions of features. The training objective is a (pairwise) squared hinge-style loss over query-specific graphs (Eq. 2.4). The key technical challenge is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires computing stationary distributions and their derivatives for many query graphs, which can only be approximated.

The authors propose two two-level optimization schemes:
- Gradient-based: On the lower level, they approximate stationary distributions using the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method (instead of the power method) and derive an iterative scheme to approximate derivatives of stationary distributions w.r.t. parameters. On the upper level, they develop an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex, composite optimization with an inexact first-order oracle, and provide convergence guarantees to a stationary point and complexity bounds (Theorem 3, Theorem 4).
- Gradient-free: They adapt random gradient-free (bandit) optimization to constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracles, using uniform sphere sampling and projection (Algorithm 1). They provide convergence rates and total complexity bounds (Theorem 1, Theorem 2), and apply it to the same learning problem.

They also analyze the trade-off between oracle accuracy (number of lower-level iterations) and total complexity. Experiments on a Yandex web browsing graph dataset (≈600 queries, ≈1.7K labeled pairs) show both proposed methods outperform a prior gradient-based approach that used the power method and had no convergence guarantee.

Strengths
- Sound problem formulation and careful handling of inexact oracles at both zero- and first-order levels. The two-level perspective is appropriate and well-motivated.
- Solid theoretical contributions: 
  - A constrained, inexact zero-order method with explicit bounds (Theorem 1) and complexity specialization to the PageRank learning problem (Theorem 2).
  - An adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant (Theorem 3), specialized complexity for Supervised PageRank (Theorem 4).
  - Lower-level analysis ensuring prescribed-accuracy approximations for stationary distributions and their derivatives (Lemmas 1–2).
- Practical relevance: replacing the power method with a linearly convergent scheme, and explicitly quantifying oracle accuracy vs. work, is useful for large graph learning tasks.
- Empirical results suggest improved loss and convergence speed over prior work on a realistic dataset.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is limited and underspecified:
  - Primary reported metric is the training/test objective (Eq. 2.4). Standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) are not reported, making it hard to assess real ranking quality gains.
  - Tables/figures referenced are not visible; details of statistical testing are sparse (p-values on loss only).
  - Comparisons are only to one “state-of-the-art” baseline; no comparisons to other supervised random walk / personalized PageRank learning baselines (e.g., Backstrom–Leskovec Supervised Random Walks) or strong learning-to-rank methods.
  - No wall-clock time or memory usage; complexity advantages are argued theoretically but not demonstrated empirically.
- Assumptions and scope of guarantees:
  - The gradient-free guarantees require convexity of f on Φ; the problem is generally nonconvex and convexity is assumed locally without empirical verification. Thus guarantees may not apply in typical settings.
  - The feasible set Φ is a small Euclidean ball around the all-ones vector to ensure positivity. This could unduly restrict the parameter search space and may bias results; justification is thin, and sensitivity to R is not studied.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Several typos and grammatical issues; some notation and constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement and not sufficiently explained in the main text.
  - The description of the competing GBP method (e.g., “matrix raised to the power 100,” “step size 50, 100, 200, 500”) is unclear, as is the stopping criterion (“differences less than −1e−5”).
  - Important implementation details (choice/learning of α, computation of dp/dϕ, sparsity exploitation) are not fully elaborated.
- Reproducibility: Dataset is proprietary, and there is no mention of code or a synthetic benchmark. This limits verifiability.

Quality
- The theoretical development appears technically sound and extends known frameworks to a constrained, inexact-oracle setting. The two-level accuracy/complexity coupling is carefully reasoned. However, proofs and constants critical for practical tuning are deferred to the supplementary material; the main text could benefit from more intuition and concrete parameter choices.
- The derivative computation scheme for stationary distributions is reasonable, but a discussion comparing it to alternative linear-system-based approaches would strengthen the work.

Clarity
- Overall structure is clear (model, oracles, algorithms, theory, experiments), but language issues and missing details impede readability. Clarify the baseline, hyperparameters, and stopping criteria; define all constants in the main text or summarize them; add a concise algorithm box for the lower-level derivative iteration.

Originality
- The paper combines and extends known ideas (inexact oracles, gradient-free smoothing, adaptive gradient with backtracking) to a constrained, inexact setting and applies them to learning Supervised PageRank. The contribution is incremental but meaningful: the inexact-oracle framework and complexity results adapted to this problem class are, to my knowledge, not previously given.

Significance
- The techniques are likely useful beyond PageRank learning for problems where only approximate function/gradient evaluations are available. Within graph ranking, offering provable convergence with practical approximations is valuable. The empirical section needs more comprehensive evaluation to convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) with statistical tests; include validation vs. strong baselines beyond [21].
- Provide wall-clock time and memory comparisons; ablate N, N1, N2 and oracle accuracies to validate the theory–practice trade-off.
- Clarify whether α is learned or fixed; justify Φ and study sensitivity to R.
- Improve clarity of the GBP baseline description and stopping rules.
- Bring key constants (e.g., β1) and practical parameter choices into the main text; add pseudocode for the derivative iteration.
- Release code and, if possible, a synthetic or public dataset experiment.

Pros
- Addresses an important and practical learning problem with principled treatment of inexact oracles.
- Provides convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods under constraints.
- Demonstrates empirical improvements over a prior method on a realistic dataset.

Cons
- Limited and unclear experimental evaluation; no standard ranking metrics, scarce baselines, no runtime analysis.
- Guarantees for the gradient-free method rely on local convexity; practical applicability not validated.
- Presentation issues and missing details hinder reproducibility and understanding.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10). With strengthened experiments and clearer presentation, this could be a solid contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
