**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a framework that incorporates node and edge features into a PageRank-style random walk. The authors identify a critical weakness in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: while it uses a two-level optimization process, it relies on imprecise calculations of the PageRank stationary distribution and its gradient (an "inexact oracle") without any theoretical guarantees of convergence.

The main contribution of this work is to provide two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem that come with formal convergence guarantees. To achieve this, the authors first leverage an existing method [17] for computing the stationary distribution, showing how to control its approximation error. They then develop two general optimization frameworks for the upper level:
1.  A random gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization with an inexact zero-order oracle (function values are noisy).
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (both function and gradient values are noisy).

For both frameworks, they provide convergence rates and overall computational complexity analyses. Finally, they demonstrate experimentally on a real-world web ranking task that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of final loss value.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Rigor:** The paper is technically very strong. The primary contribution is bridging the theoretical gap in the learning procedure for Supervised PageRank. By formalizing the lower-level computation as an inexact oracle with controllable error (`δ`) and then developing upper-level optimization algorithms that explicitly account for this error, the authors provide the first provably convergent methods for this problem. The theoretical analysis, leading to convergence rates and complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), is thorough and constitutes a complete piece of work.

2.  **General and Significant Contributions to Optimization:** Beyond the specific application to PageRank, the paper makes valuable contributions to the broader optimization literature. The proposed frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) are of independent interest. Many machine learning problems involve objective functions that are expensive or impossible to compute exactly (e.g., those involving expectations over large datasets or solutions to iterative procedures). The methods developed here, particularly the adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems, are significant and likely to be useful for other researchers.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the work. It clearly explains the Supervised PageRank model, identifies the specific shortcoming of the existing learning algorithm (lack of convergence guarantees due to inexact computations), and lays out a clear roadmap of the paper's contributions to solve this issue.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments, conducted on a large-scale, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, convincingly support the paper's claims. Both proposed methods (GFN and GBN) achieve a lower loss than the previous method (GBP). The fact that the gradient-free method performs best is an interesting and practical result, as it avoids the complex and potentially expensive computation of the stationary distribution's derivatives. The statistical significance tests further strengthen these conclusions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Convexity Assumption for the Gradient-Free Method:** The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) assumes that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex. However, the pairwise squared hinge-like loss function is generally non-convex. The authors acknowledge this by stating they assume convexity in a small vicinity of a local minimum. While this is a common assumption, it weakens the guarantee. In contrast, the gradient-based method is rightly developed for the non-convex case, which is a major strength. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the implications of this non-convexity on the gradient-free algorithm's practical performance and guarantees.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While the paper is well-structured, the density of mathematical notation can be challenging. A table summarizing the key parameters (`ε`, `δ`, `L`, `R`, `N`, `M`, etc.) and their roles in the algorithms and theorems would greatly improve readability for a broader audience. Furthermore, providing a bit more intuition behind the convergence results—for instance, how the oracle error `δ` propagates into the final error bounds in Theorem 1—could make the theoretical sections more accessible.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are well-executed but are limited to comparing against the direct predecessor [21]. While this is the most direct and important baseline, positioning the performance of Supervised PageRank against other standard learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) on the same dataset would provide valuable context about the absolute effectiveness of this entire class of models.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis that directly addresses a clear gap in prior work. Claims are well-supported by both theorems and strong empirical results. This is a complete and polished research paper.

*   **Clarity:** Good, but could be improved. The paper's structure is logical, but the notation is dense. Minor additions like a parameter table could enhance readability. The core ideas are communicated effectively.

*   **Originality:** High. The primary novelty lies in creating provably convergent optimization algorithms for problems with computationally expensive, inexact oracles. Adapting and extending existing optimization frameworks ([7], [16]) to the constrained, inexact setting is a non-trivial and original contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide end-to-end complexity guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank.

*   **Significance:** High. The work is significant for two communities. For researchers in graph-based learning and information retrieval, it provides a principled and better-performing method for training a relevant class of ranking models. For the optimization community, it provides new, general-purpose algorithms for a common and challenging problem setting (optimization with inexact information), which could see broad application. The practical improvements shown are a clear advance for the state of the art in this specific problem.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions in both optimization theory and its application to graph-based machine learning. It rigorously solves an open theoretical problem from prior work and demonstrates the practical benefits of its solution. The development of general-purpose optimization algorithms for inexact oracles is a particularly strong and impactful contribution. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's high quality. I strongly recommend acceptance.
