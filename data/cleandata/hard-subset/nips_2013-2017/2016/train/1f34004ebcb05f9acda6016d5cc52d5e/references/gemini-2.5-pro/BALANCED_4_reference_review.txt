### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a powerful extension of the classic PageRank algorithm that incorporates node and edge features into the random walk process. The learning problem is framed as a non-convex loss minimization task. A key challenge is that the loss function and its gradient depend on the stationary distribution of a large, parameter-dependent Markov chain, which cannot be computed exactly. This creates an "inexact oracle" setting where function and gradient evaluations are always approximate.

The authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem, both of which come with theoretical convergence guarantees. The first is a gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems with an inexact first-order oracle. The second is a random gradient-free method adapted for constrained convex problems with an inexact zero-order oracle. For the lower-level task of approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, the authors employ a linearly convergent method from [17], which allows them to precisely control the oracle's error. The paper's main contributions are the development and analysis of these optimization frameworks, including complexity bounds that account for the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational cost. Experiments on a real-world web ranking task show that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach from [21], which lacked convergence guarantees.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide novel convergence guarantees for both gradient-based (for non-convex) and gradient-free (for convex) optimization in a constrained setting with an inexact oracle. This is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature, extending prior work like [7] and [16]. Proving convergence for two-level optimization schemes where the inner loop is an iterative approximation is non-trivial and highly relevant for many large-scale machine learning problems.

2.  **Addressing a Gap in Prior Work:** The paper directly addresses a major limitation of the original Supervised PageRank paper [21], which used a heuristic gradient descent method without any proof of convergence. By providing two algorithms with explicit convergence rates and complexity bounds, this work places the learning of Supervised PageRank on a solid theoretical foundation.

3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (controlled by the number of iterations `N`) and the overall complexity of the algorithm is a key feature. This provides a principled way to set hyperparameters, which is often done heuristically in practice.

4.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free one (GFN), are shown to significantly outperform the previous state-of-the-art (GBP) in terms of the final loss value on a real-world dataset. This demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretically-grounded approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Mismatch between Problem and Theory for the Gradient-Free Method:** The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free algorithm (Theorem 1 and 2) assumes that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. However, the loss function defined in (2.4) is generally non-convex due to the `max(·, 0)` operator and the non-linear dependency of `πq` on `ϕ`. The authors acknowledge this by stating they assume `Φ` is a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a very strong assumption that may not hold in practice and weakens the provided guarantee for this method. The gradient-based method, which is analyzed for the non-convex case, seems more appropriate for the problem as stated.

2.  **Limited and Non-Reproducible Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on a single, proprietary dataset from the Yandex search engine. While this is a valuable real-world dataset, its inaccessibility makes the results impossible to reproduce. The paper's empirical claims would be significantly stronger if the methods were also evaluated on publicly available graph datasets or standard learning-to-rank benchmarks (e.g., LETOR, adapted for a graph setting).

3.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is technically dense and the notation is heavy, which can make it challenging to follow. The separation between the general optimization framework (Sections 4.1, 5.1) and its specific application (Sections 4.2, 5.2) is logical but also makes the paper feel somewhat disjointed. A more streamlined presentation could improve readability. For example, the motivation for using the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] over the simpler power method is crucial, but the empirical comparison is relegated to the supplementary materials.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Address the Convexity Assumption:** The authors should be more explicit about the non-convexity of the problem and the limitations of the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's theory. Could the analysis be extended to convergence to a stationary point for the non-convex case, similar to recent work on zeroth-order non-convex optimization? Alternatively, a discussion on why this local convexity assumption might be reasonable in this specific application would be helpful.
*   **Broaden Experimental Evaluation:** To strengthen the paper's impact and facilitate future research, I strongly encourage the authors to test their algorithms on at least one publicly available dataset. This would demonstrate the general applicability of their methods beyond a single industrial use case.
*   **Refine Experimental Discussion:** The comparison of convergence speed in Section 6.2 and Figure 1 could be clarified. The text states GBN requires fewer iterations to stop than some variants of GBP, but the plot shows GBP's loss decreasing very quickly initially. A clearer discussion of "convergence speed" (e.g., wall-clock time vs. iterations to reach a certain loss value vs. iterations until the stopping criterion is met) would be beneficial.
*   **Improve Presentation:** Bringing the key figure comparing the lower-level solvers ([17] vs. power method) from the supplement into the main text would better motivate the methodological choices. Simplifying notation where possible or providing a more intuitive walkthrough of the algorithms before diving into the theorems could also enhance the paper's accessibility.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of optimization for machine learning. The development of theoretically-grounded algorithms for learning with an inexact oracle is an important problem, and the application to Supervised PageRank is compelling. The paper's main strengths are its rigorous convergence analysis and the demonstration of empirical improvements over prior work. The primary weakness is the disconnect between the non-convex nature of the problem and the convexity assumption required for the gradient-free method's analysis. Despite this and the limited scope of the experiments, the theoretical contributions, particularly for the non-convex gradient-based method, are strong enough to warrant acceptance.

**Decision:** Accept.
