Paper proposes two two-level methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank parameters using a linearly convergent stationary distribution approximation.  
Provides convergence guarantees for both methods, addressing a gap in prior work [21].  
Analyzes trade-off between lower-level (stationary distribution) accuracy and overall computational complexity.  
Builds on and improves over prior stationary-distribution methods (e.g., power method) by using the method from [17].  
Extends gradient-free and gradient-based techniques to handle constrained optimization with inexact oracle information.  
Positions gradient-free approach against prior techniques [16,7] and attempts to adapt them for constraints/inexactness.  
Connection to online convex optimization and bandit feedback literature is mentioned but not fully developed.  
Rigorous theoretical guarantees and convergence rate analysis are the paper’s main strengths.  
Detailed analysis of the linearly convergent method for estimating stationary distributions is a strong contribution.  
Empirical results show improved ranking quality over the state-of-the-art on a real-world web ranking task.  
Comprehensive computational complexity analysis is provided.  
Paper is generally well-structured, though notation and presentation are dense.  
Major weakness: notation is very dense and hinders readability and intuition.  
Limited practical guidance on parameter tuning (smoothing, step sizes) and implementation details.  
Experimental evaluation is somewhat narrow; more baselines and ablation studies are needed and radius R choice is unexplained.  
Could better connect the inexact-oracle framework to broader optimization trends (e.g., variance reduction).  
Rationale for specific algorithmic and parameter choices lacks sufficient intuition and motivation.  
Overall assessment: strong, valuable contribution with rigorous analysis; weaknesses are mostly clarity and experimental depth.  
Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to improve readability and expand empirical guidance.  
Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
