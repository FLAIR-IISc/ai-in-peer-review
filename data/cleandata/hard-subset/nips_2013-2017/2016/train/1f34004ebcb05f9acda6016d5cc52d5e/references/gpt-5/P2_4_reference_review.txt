Summary
The paper studies learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The learning objective is a (piecewise) smooth pairwise squared-hinge loss over stationary distributions of query-specific Markov chains. The key challenge is that neither the stationary distributions nor their derivatives can be computed exactly at scale. The paper proposes two two-level approaches:
- A gradient-free method: extend one-point randomized zeroth-order optimization to constrained problems with a uniformly bounded inexact oracle, and combine it with a linearly convergent method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate PageRank values to prescribed accuracy.
- A gradient-based method: extend projected (composite) gradient methods to constrained nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle of unknown Lipschitz constant, again using the same lower-level PageRank approximation (and a new iterative scheme for derivatives) to provide oracle accuracy guarantees.
Theoretical results give oracle-accuracy controls (lemmas) and overall complexity bounds (theorems) for both methods. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (600 queries) compare against the Supervised PageRank learner of [21] and report improved loss values; the authors also claim fewer upper-level iterations in some settings.

Relation to prior work
- Supervised random walks/PageRank variants (e.g., [21], BrowseRank): the paper aims to replace power-method-based approximations and unproven convergence with linearly convergent lower-level solvers and provable upper-level convergence under inexact oracles.
- Zeroth-order methods: builds on Nesterov–Spokoiny and bandit optimization (e.g., Agarwal et al.), adapting to constrained sets and deterministic bounded inexactness.
- First-order with inexact oracles: builds on Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov and Ghadimi–Lan; the contribution is an adaptive projected scheme and analysis for nonconvex composite problems with oracle (δ, L)-type bounds, integrated with the PageRank inner solver.
- Markov chain stationary computation: adopts Nesterov–Nemirovski’s linearly convergent averaging scheme and proposes a related iterative scheme for derivatives.

Quality
- Theory: The upper-level analyses are largely sound and align with known techniques. The gradient-free result gives a clear bias/variance/accuracy trade-off in terms of τ, δ, and dimension m; the gradient-based result bounds the norm of the gradient mapping, which is appropriate for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracles. The inner-level lemmas provide explicit oracle-accuracy controls based on iteration counts N, N1, N2. However:
  - The gradient-free result applies to convex f; the application to the learning problem relies on the strong assumption that f is convex on a small ball Φ around some local minimum, which is nontrivial and unverified empirically.
  - The derivative-approximation scheme (Equations 3.2–3.3) and the constant β1 are deferred to the supplement; proof sketches in the main text are minimal, which makes it hard to assess tightness and implementability from the paper alone.
  - The global complexity statements include large constants and logs; while acceptable theoretically, practical guidance (e.g., how to pick τ, δ) is sparse beyond formulas.
- Experiments: The empirical validation is limited. Reporting focuses on the training loss (objective), not on standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP) that matter for the application. Only one baseline (the prior GBP) is compared; no comparisons to alternative learning-to-rank or supervised random walk baselines. Details of GBP configuration are somewhat unclear (e.g., “stochastic matrix raised to power 100,” stopping criterion “less than −1e−5” on test sets). Statistical testing is mentioned, but only for loss. Runtime and wall-clock comparisons are missing; iteration counts alone do not capture inner-level costs, especially since the proposed methods tune N,N1,N2 to reach specified δ.

Clarity
- The paper is generally organized but contains numerous typos and grammatical issues, and several references are incomplete or missing years/pages. Key algorithmic pieces (e.g., how β1 is computed; derivation of dP/dϕ; handling zero out-degree nodes in differentiation) are postponed to the supplement; at a minimum, succinct derivations or pseudo-code would aid reproducibility. The definition and role of several hyperparameters (e.g., L0, ε) are given, but practical selection guidance is thin.

Originality
- The combination of: (i) linearly convergent PageRank computation with explicit accuracy control; (ii) an inexact-oracle gradient-free method for constrained problems; and (iii) an adaptive projected first-order method for nonconvex composite optimization with inexact oracle; and (iv) end-to-end complexity bounds for this two-level structure, appears novel in aggregate. Individually, each component is close to known techniques, but the integrated analysis tailored to Supervised PageRank is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is moderate: it provides a principled, convergent alternative to heuristic inner approximations in supervised PageRank and clarifies the accuracy–complexity trade-off. Practical significance is currently limited by the experimental scope: small labeled set, lack of ranking metrics, and narrow baselines. With stronger empirical validation and comparisons, impact on graph ranking could be substantial.

Pros
- Clear articulation of inexact-oracle framework for both zeroth- and first-order methods and its connection to PageRank approximation accuracy.
- End-to-end complexity bounds connecting inner and outer loops.
- Potentially valuable in practice where exact gradients are costly.
- Uses a linearly convergent inner method rather than a power method; this is a real improvement.

Cons
- Application relies on unverified local convexity within Φ; otherwise gradient-free guarantees do not apply.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: objective-only reporting, few baselines, unclear stopping/tuning, no runtime, and no ranking metrics.
- Several technical details are deferred; some notational and editorial issues.
- The feasible set choice (ball around all-ones) to ensure positivity is ad hoc; implications for modeling and generalization are not discussed.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please report NDCG/MAP on test queries, and compare against additional baselines (e.g., supervised random walks, learning-to-rank methods using the same features).
- Provide wall-clock time and inner-iteration counts (N, N1, N2) per outer iteration; include ablations showing accuracy–cost trade-offs.
- Clarify computation of β1 and provide pseudo-code for derivative approximation; specify how dP/dϕ is handled when denominators are small or nodes have zero out-degree.
- Justify or relax the local convexity assumption; at least empirically test convexity in Φ or discuss failure modes.
- Improve references and proofreading; ensure all citations are complete.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical development is solid and relevant, but the paper falls short on empirical validation and clarity. Strengthening experiments with standard ranking metrics, more baselines, runtime analysis, and clearer algorithmic details could move this to a strong accept.
