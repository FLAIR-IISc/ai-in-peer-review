Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. It proposes two two-level optimization schemes for the nonconvex, constrained objective that uses a pairwise hinge-squared loss over query-dependent graphs:

- Lower level: approximate the stationary distribution π(ϕ) of a Markov chain for each query using the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method [17], rather than the classical power method [18], and similarly approximate derivatives dπ/dϕ using a generalized iterative scheme.
- Upper level: optimize ϕ using either (i) a random gradient-free method under an inexact zero-order oracle (adapting ideas from [16] to constrained problems with bounded evaluation error), or (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle (extending [7, 25] to constrained settings and unknown smoothness).

The authors provide complexity bounds that explicitly capture the trade-off between the lower-level approximation accuracy and the overall cost to reach a prescribed accuracy at the upper level (Theorems 2 and 4). Empirically, on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries), both methods outperform a prior two-level gradient-based scheme [21] using the power method on the lower level. This line of work builds on supervised/random-walk ranking approaches [10, 12, 21] and optimization with inexact oracles [7, 16, 25].

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important issue in supervised random-walk ranking: end-to-end convergence guarantees for learning parameters when only inexact stationary distributions and gradients are available.
- Lower-level choice of [17] is well-motivated and likely more efficient than the power method; the paper quantifies accuracy/cost tradeoffs and extends [17] to derivative computation.
- Provides general-purpose optimization frameworks (gradient-free with inexact zero-order oracle; adaptive projected gradient with inexact first-order oracle) with convergence analyses, applicable beyond the ranking problem.
- Complexity bounds for the overall two-level procedures are explicit in problem dimensions (m, p, s, |Q|) and accuracy.
- Experiments on real data show consistent improvements over the baseline algorithm [21] and suggest faster convergence at the lower level.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions vs. target problem: The gradient-free method requires convexity (Theorem 1), and the paper applies it by assuming local convexity of f in a neighborhood of a local minimum. This is strong, unverifiable from the data, and not justified for the nonconvex objective at hand.
- Smoothness of f: The analysis assumes f ∈ C1,1 (Lipschitz gradient), yet f uses (·)+, making it nonsmooth at zero. The paper treats gradients of (Aπ)+ implicitly; the Lipschitz-gradient assumption needs justification (e.g., smoothing or handling via the composite framework with h capturing nonsmoothness). As written, the theoretical bridge from Lemmas 1–2 to condition (5.1) relies on C1,1 without proof for this loss.
- Inexact-oracle constants: Several results rely on constants (e.g., β1, L) that are not clearly specified or are set ad hoc in experiments (L = 1e-4), making practical reproducibility of the convergence guarantees unclear.
- Constraint set Φ: Choosing Φ as a small Euclidean ball around the all-ones vector to ensure positivity is restrictive and may limit modeling flexibility (e.g., when features vary in scale or negative weights would be useful). Discussion of how sensitive performance is to Φ and whether nonnegativity constraints suffice would help.
- Experimental evaluation: 
  - Only one baseline (the previous two-level gradient method [21]) is reported. Comparisons to other learning-to-rank and supervised random walk methods (e.g., BrowseRank [10], supervised random walks [2], learning-to-rank baselines like RankSVM/LambdaMART on the same features) are absent.
  - Evaluation focuses on the training objective, with limited reporting on standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP). The text mentions “ranking quality,” but the main table reports only the surrogate loss.
  - Limited scale: ~600 queries with ~1.7k labeled pairs is modest; reporting wall-clock time, memory, and sensitivity to graph size more thoroughly would strengthen significance claims.
- Clarity and presentation: Numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., “concider,” stray “th Conference…” line, incomplete references with missing years/venues) impede readability. Some critical constants and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement.

Quality
- Technical contributions are largely sound in the context of inexact oracles, with careful accounting of lower-level approximation errors propagated to upper-level convergence. However, applicability to the stated nonconvex, nonsmooth objective is undermined by the convexity and smoothness assumptions (gradient-free) and by the unproven smoothness claim (gradient-based). Experimental evidence supports practical usefulness but lacks breadth in baselines and metrics.

Clarity
- The paper’s main ideas are understandable, and the two-level structure is clear. Proof sketches and complexity expressions are informative. Nonetheless, clarity suffers from typos, incomplete references, and reliance on undefined constants. A more explicit statement of assumptions (convexity region, smoothness, positivity) and a concise algorithm summary with all hyperparameters would help.

Originality
- The combination of [17] for stationary distribution and derivative approximation with inexact-oracle optimization is a reasonable synthesis. Extending random gradient-free methods to constrained problems with bounded oracle error, and adapting adaptive projected gradient methods to inexact oracles and unknown L, are incremental but useful contributions. Novelty is moderate given prior work on inexact oracles [7, 25] and nonconvex stochastic methods [7].

Significance
- For supervised random-walk ranking, providing convergent, tunable two-level methods is valuable. The optimization templates may be of broader interest. However, limited empirical comparisons and unverified assumptions reduce the impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Justify smoothness (or reformulate as composite with the nonsmooth part in h) and clarify when (5.1) holds for the chosen loss. If necessary, adopt a smoothed hinge and update theory accordingly.
- Address the convexity assumption behind the gradient-free method, or provide a nonconvex analysis (e.g., expected stationarity gap) akin to [7].
- Specify all constants (β1, L) or provide practical estimation procedures; report sensitivity to these choices.
- Broaden experiments: include standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP), stronger baselines, wall-clock time, and scalability analysis. Clarify whether α is learned.
- Improve exposition and complete references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear need: learning PageRank-like models with convergence guarantees under inexact computations.
- Theoretical development of inexact-oracle methods tailored to this setting with explicit complexity.
- Empirical gains over a reasonable baseline and faster lower-level convergence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical assumptions (convexity, smoothness) do not align with the stated nonconvex, nonsmooth objective; guarantees may not apply as claimed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in baselines and metrics.
- Presentation issues and incomplete references hinder clarity and reproducibility.
