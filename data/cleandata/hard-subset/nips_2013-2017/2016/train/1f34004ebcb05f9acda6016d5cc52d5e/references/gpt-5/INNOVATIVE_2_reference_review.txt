Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank where both the objective and its gradient can only be accessed via approximate computations of stationary distributions and their derivatives. The authors propose two two-level procedures: (i) a gradient-based method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, coupled with a lower-level, linearly convergent method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate stationary distributions and their sensitivities; and (ii) a random gradient-free method adapted to constrained problems with biased, inexact zero-order oracles. For both, they provide convergence guarantees and complexity bounds that account for the error/iteration trade-off at the lower level. Empirically, they evaluate on a Yandex user-browsing graph ranking task, reporting improved loss and ranking quality over the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach previously used for Supervised PageRank.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on Supervised PageRank and supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec, Wang/Liu et al., Gusev et al.), where features enter restart/transition probabilities.
- Replaces power iteration with the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski approach for computing stationary distributions and generalizes it to derivative computation.
- Extends random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny) to constrained settings with uniformly bounded inexactness (biased estimator), and adapts inexact oracle frameworks (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov) to a nonconvex, composite, constrained setting with backtracking on unknown L.
- Provides explicit complexity bounds that couple lower- and upper-level accuracies, which is rarely done in this application domain.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Treating learning over Markov-chain-based ranking with explicit inexact-oracle theory at both zero- and first-order levels is a clean, useful abstraction. The constrained/bounded-noise gradient-free framework with a sphere randomization and the adaptive projected gradient method for a nonconvex problem with inexact first-order oracle and unknown L are interesting extensions.
- Theory: Convergence results for both methods, with bounds that expose the dependence on the smoothing radius, oracle accuracy, and lower-level iteration counts. The inexact first-order oracle condition and backtracking scheme are clearly stated, with a stopping criterion tied to a stationarity measure.
- Practical relevance: Replacing power iteration by a linearly convergent method at the lower level is sensible and likely impactful across similar problems (e.g., personalized PageRank with features).
- Empirical results: On a real query-dependent browsing-graph dataset, both proposed methods reduce loss and improve ranking quality relative to the prior gradient-based approach; the gradient-free method performs particularly well.

Weaknesses
- Experimental depth/metrics: The evaluation is limited (≈600 queries, ≈1.7K labeled pairs), with proprietary data and no code. Results rely primarily on the training loss; standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, ERR) on held-out labels are not reported in the main text. Wall-clock time and memory comparisons are missing despite the paper’s focus on algorithmic efficiency; complexity bounds are informative but empirical runtime/throughput would strengthen the case.
- Baselines: Only the previous Supervised PageRank learner is compared. Modern learning-to-rank baselines (pairwise/logistic losses, GBDT/GBRT, LambdaMART, or even PageRank variants using features) are absent; even if not directly comparable, including them would contextualize the practical gains.
- Assumptions and scope: The gradient-free convergence guarantee assumes convexity over Φ, while the application is nonconvex and only argued to be locally convex within a ball. The practical procedure for choosing Φ and ensuring that a good local minimizer lies in int Φ is somewhat ad hoc. Similarly, Theorem 1 is stated for convex f, but the final application falls back to a local convexity assumption without diagnostic checks in experiments.
- Clarity and polish: Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted. Some symbols and constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement; a brief intuition or bound magnitude in the main text would aid readability. The exposition occasionally mixes norms and assumptions (∞-norm in Lemma 2, 1-norm diameter, but Euclidean prox) in a way that could be streamlined.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The oracle constructions, bias/variance control via τ, and coupling of lower- and upper-level accuracies are carefully handled. Some proofs are deferred, but the statements appear consistent with prior literature. Empirics demonstrate improvements but are not exhaustive.

Clarity
Mostly clear, with good structure. However, the paper would benefit from:
- A concise “recipe” for setting δ, N, N1, N2 from ε in one place.
- Reporting standard IR metrics and runtime.
- Cleaning up references and notation consistency.

Originality
Moderately strong. The combination of:
- A constrained, biased, inexact zero-order framework with guarantees;
- An adaptive inexact first-order method for nonconvex composite problems with unknown L;
- A principled two-level complexity trade-off for Markov-chain-based learning;
constitutes a nontrivial contribution beyond standard applications of known methods.

Significance
Potentially impactful for practitioners facing inexact oracles in large-scale graph learning (e.g., personalized ranking, graph inference). The lower-level substitution and the inexact-oracle perspective could generalize to other stochastic fixed-point learning problems. Stronger empirical validation would enhance perceived significance.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report NDCG/MAP/ERR on held-out labels, and wall-clock runtimes for GFN, GBN, and GBP? How do the methods scale with |V| and |E| in practice?
- How sensitive are results to α and to the choice/size of Φ? Any diagnostics to verify local convexity on Φ for the tested problems?
- How stable are the methods across different τ and δ schedules? Any adaptive schemes for τ that you found effective?
- Could you comment on the bias introduced by the zero-order estimator and how it affects practical convergence vs. unbiased two-point estimators?

Pros
- Clear, principled handling of inexact oracles at both zero- and first-order levels.
- Convergence and complexity results tailored to the two-level structure.
- Practical lower-level improvement (linearly convergent method) with empirical gains.
- Opens a useful direction for learning with Markov-chain-based objectives.

Cons
- Limited empirical scope, lack of standard ranking metrics and runtime comparisons.
- Few baselines; reproducibility concerns (proprietary data, no code).
- Reliance on local convexity without empirical verification.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper advances a meaningful, generalizable perspective on learning with inexact oracles for graph-based ranking, with nontrivial theoretical contributions and promising empirical signals. With stronger experiments and polishing, it could be a solid NeurIPS contribution.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
