

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank, a problem where node and edge features are linearly combined with model parameters to improve ranking quality. The authors develop a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method, both with theoretical convergence guarantees that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks. The methods involve a lower-level algorithm for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk and an upper-level optimization algorithm that uses these approximations. The paper includes theoretical convergence rate analysis and experimental results on a web page ranking task.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a practical and important problem in web search ranking with clear potential applications.
2. The two-level optimization framework is well-motivated and novel for this specific application.
3. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, covering both gradient-based and gradient-free methods with inexact oracles.
4. The experimental results show clear improvements over the state-of-the-art method on real-world data.

## Weaknesses
The paper contains numerous minor technical inconsistencies that undermine its technical rigor and clarity. These issues, while individually small, collectively suggest a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. Here are the most significant issues:

1. **Inconsistent mathematical notation**: The paper uses ϕ and ϕ̂ interchangeably without clear distinction. For example, Φ = {ϕ ∈ R^m : ||ϕ - ϕ̂||_2 ≤ R} is defined with ϕ̂, but in later sections, the authors refer to ϕ without the hat. This inconsistency appears throughout the paper.

2. **Misleading equations**: Equation (2.1) states [π0]i = 〈ϕ1,Vi〉/∑ l∈U 〈ϕ1,Vl〉 but doesn't specify that this is only for i ∈ U, creating confusion. Similarly, Equation (2.2) doesn't clarify that it only applies to nodes with non-zero outdegree, though the text later mentions this constraint.

3. **Inaccurate technical claims**: The paper claims "probabilities [π0q(ϕ)]i, i ∈ Vq, [Pq(ϕ)]̃i,i, ĩ→ i ∈ Eq, are scale-invariant" but this is incorrect. The probabilities are scale-invariant with respect to the parameters ϕ (i.e., π0q(λϕ) = π0q(ϕ)), but they are not scale-invariant with respect to the feature vectors Vi or Eij.

4. **Undefined terms**: The paper repeatedly uses "a.o." without defining it (presumably "arithmetic operations"), which is unprofessional for a technical paper.

5. **Confusing notation in Theorem 1**: The expression "EΞM−1f(yM ) − f∗" should be "E[f(yM)] - f*" to avoid confusion with the random variable Ξ.

6. **Inconsistent use of δ1 and δ2**: In Section 5.2, the paper claims "Lemma 1 with δ1 = δ2 allows us to obtain f̃(ϕ, δ1) such that inequality |f̃(ϕ, δ1)− f(ϕ)| ≤ δ1 holds" but should specify δ1 = δ (not δ2).

7. **Missing reference**: The paper cites [5] as a review of state-of-the-art methods but fails to provide the full reference in the bibliography.

8. **Typos and grammatical errors**: "concider" (should be "consider"), "probablities" (should be "probabilities"), and inconsistent capitalization of "Supervised PageRank" throughout the paper.

9. **Confusing statement about convexity**: In Section 5.2, the paper states "we assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) (2.4) is convex (generally speaking, it is nonconvex)." This is contradictory and confusing.

10. **Unjustified hyperparameter choices**: The paper chooses L0 ∈ {10−4, 10−3, 10−2, 10−1, 1} without explanation, making it difficult to assess the robustness of their results.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a theoretically sound and practically relevant approach to learning Supervised PageRank, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and formatting issues are unacceptable for a NIPS submission. These issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility and suggest a lack of attention to detail that is critical for a high-quality scientific contribution.

The paper's main strength is its novel two-level optimization framework with convergence guarantees, but the theoretical analysis is weakened by the lack of clarity in the main text (with proofs relegated to supplementary materials). The experimental results are promising but lack sufficient detail about the feature engineering process and data splitting.

Given my perfectionist standards and the importance of technical precision in optimization research, I recommend rejection due to the significant number of minor but pervasive technical flaws that detract from the paper's overall quality. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before considering resubmission to a venue like NIPS.
