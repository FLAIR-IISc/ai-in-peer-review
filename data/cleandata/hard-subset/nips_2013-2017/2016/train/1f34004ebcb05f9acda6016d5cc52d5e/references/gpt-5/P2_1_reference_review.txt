Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank where transition and restart probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The key difficulty is that the loss (a squared pairwise hinge over query-dependent stationary distributions) and its gradient cannot be computed exactly; only approximations via iterative PageRank computations are feasible. The authors propose two two-level methods:

- Gradient-based: use the linearly convergent iteration of Nesterov–Nemirovski [17] to approximate stationary distributions and a generalized iteration to approximate their derivatives; plug these into an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle, with provable convergence to a stationary point under nonconvexity.

- Gradient-free: adapt random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with a bounded-error zero-order oracle, using the same lower-level approximation to get a function value within a prescribed accuracy; provide a convergence bound under convexity in the feasible ball.

They analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity, yielding end-to-end arithmetic operation bounds. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (600 queries) compare their methods to the prior two-level gradient method that used the power method [21], reporting improved final loss and faster convergence of the lower-level iteration.

Quality
- Theory: The paper gives clear error bounds for the lower-level approximation of f and ∇f (Lemmas 1–2) with explicit iteration counts, and complexity estimates. The gradient-free method’s analysis (Theorem 1) adapts Nesterov–Spokoiny-style smoothing to constrained domains and bounded inexact oracles; the gradient method (Theorem 3) generalizes projected gradient with backtracking to inexact first-order oracles and nonconvex objectives, measuring stationarity via the prox-gradient norm. The coupling to PageRank estimation is carefully done and the end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) are useful.

- Some technical concerns require clarification:
  - Smoothness of the loss: f uses squared positive part, ‖(Aπ)+‖^2, which is nondifferentiable where components cross zero. The analysis assumes f ∈ C1,1 or uses (δ,L)-oracle-type bounds; please clarify whether you work with subgradients, a smoothed hinge, or argue that nondifferentiability is negligible for the theory used.
  - Gradient-free method assumes convexity of f on Φ, while the problem is generally nonconvex; the paper assumes local convexity near a minimizer. This limits the generality of the guarantee and should be discussed more prominently.
  - The constrained zeroth-order oracle evaluates f̃ at x and x+τξ. For a ball constraint, x+τξ may leave Φ unless τ is adaptively clipped near the boundary; the paper should state how feasibility for the two queries is enforced.
  - The constant β1 and some derivations are deferred to the supplement; enough detail should be in the main paper to assess magnitudes and practical parameter choices.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-structured, with a logical flow from model to lower-level approximations to upper-level methods, theory, and experiments. However, the presentation suffers from numerous typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., π0q/π0, pq/p; occasional missing indices), and some references are incomplete or lack years. Important constants (β1) and proofs are entirely in the supplement, making it hard to fully verify bounds. A brief proof sketch and explicit statement of all assumptions would improve readability.

Originality
- Lower-level: Using the Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration for PageRank is known, but the extension to compute derivatives with accuracy control is a useful contribution in this application.
- Upper-level: The inexact first-order oracle framework for constrained nonconvex composite problems with backtracking is an incremental but meaningful extension of known results (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Nesterov composite gradient; Ghadimi–Lan nonconvex). The gradient-free method adapts random smoothing to constrained domains and bounded-error oracles; again, incremental but relevant.
- The novelty lies in integrating these pieces to produce end-to-end complexity guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank and in proposing a viable gradient-free alternative when derivative computation is too costly.

Significance
- The problem is practically relevant in ranking on graphs with features. Providing methods with convergence guarantees in an inexact-oracle setting is valuable.
- Empirical significance is limited by:
  - Dataset scale and evaluation: 600 queries, ~1.7K labeled pairs; results are reported in terms of the training objective only. Standard IR metrics (NDCG, MAP) on held-out labels would substantiate practical impact. Statistical tests on the objective alone are less informative.
  - Baselines: Comparison is only to the previous Supervised PageRank training procedure. It would strengthen the case to compare to strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART) or to demonstrate benefits over simpler feature-based rankers on the same graphs.
  - Runtime: While iteration counts and theoretical complexity are discussed, wall-clock timings for the two-level methods (including lower-level iteration counts) would help practitioners.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with principled methods.
- Provides explicit convergence and complexity bounds under realistic inexact-oracle assumptions.
- Extends PageRank derivative computation with accuracy control.
- Empirically improves the objective and shows faster lower-level convergence vs. power method.

Cons
- Reliance on local convexity for the gradient-free guarantee; the nonconvex case is not covered.
- Nondifferentiability of the loss is not reconciled with smoothness assumptions.
- Experimental evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics, broader baselines, and runtime comparisons.
- Some algorithmic details (boundary handling in zeroth-order steps, choice/calibration of τ, δ, N, N1, N2) are underspecified in the main text.

Questions for authors
- How do you handle nondifferentiability of (·)+ in the theory and in practice? Do you smooth it or rely on subgradients?
- How is τ chosen near the boundary to ensure x+τξ ∈ Φ? Do you resample ξ or shrink τ adaptively?
- Can you provide wall-clock runtimes and lower-level iteration counts, and sensitivity to α?
- How large is β1 in typical graphs, and how does it affect N1, N2 in practice?

Suggestions
- Report NDCG/MAP on held-out labels and compare to standard learning-to-rank baselines.
- Add runtime plots and ablations over τ, δ, Nesterov–Nemirovski vs. power, and gradient vs. gradient-free.
- Clarify all assumptions and provide brief proof sketches and constants in the main paper.
- Clean up typos and complete references.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a technically solid, well-motivated contribution with end-to-end guarantees and promising empirical results on a relevant task. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying theoretical assumptions would further increase its impact. Confidence: medium.
