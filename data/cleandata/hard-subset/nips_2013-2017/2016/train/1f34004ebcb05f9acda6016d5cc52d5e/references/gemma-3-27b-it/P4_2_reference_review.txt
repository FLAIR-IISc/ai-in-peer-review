This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, addressing limitations of existing approaches regarding convergence guarantees and performance. The methods consist of a lower level approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk and an upper level tuning parameters using either gradient-based or gradient-free optimization. The authors provide convergence analysis for both methods and demonstrate empirical improvements on a web page ranking task.

The paper builds upon existing work in PageRank variants [18, 11, 8, 9, 19] and Supervised PageRank [21]. It acknowledges the limitations of existing optimization techniques [16, 7] when applied to this specific problem due to constraints and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. The work is situated within the broader context of iterative methods for solving eigensystems [23, 24] and stochastic optimization [1, 16, 7, 25].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** A key strength is the provision of convergence guarantees for both proposed methods, which are lacking in the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach [21]. This is a significant contribution, as it provides a solid theoretical foundation for the algorithms.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity, offering valuable insights into practical implementation.
*   **Novel Extension of Existing Methods:** The paper extends existing random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods [1, 16, 7, 25] to handle inexact oracles and constrained optimization problems, making it broadly applicable beyond Supervised PageRank.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the existing state-of-the-art approach on a real-world ranking task.
*   **Detailed Lemmas & Theorems:** The inclusion of detailed lemmas and theorems with proofs (some relegated to supplementary materials) reinforces the technical rigor of the work.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the Supervised PageRank problem and the associated optimization challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notationally Dense:** The paper is quite dense with notation, particularly in sections 3, 4, and 5. This can make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific mathematical background to follow the derivations and arguments.
*   **Repetitive Explanations:** The explanation of the inexact oracle concept is somewhat repetitive across sections 4 and 5. A more consolidated presentation could improve readability.
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experimental results are promising, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset and task. Exploring performance on other datasets or ranking problems would strengthen the conclusions. The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., L) also isn't fully justified beyond stating a value.
*   **Dependence on Supplementary Materials:** A substantial amount of technical details, including proofs, are relegated to the supplementary materials. While this is common, it can hinder the ability of reviewers to fully assess the contributions without a careful examination of the supplement.
* **Connection to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, a more detailed discussion of the limitations of these existing methods *specifically* in the context of the Supervised PageRank problem (beyond mentioning they "cannot be applied") would be beneficial. For example, why exactly do existing global optimization methods fail due to the constraints?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for a problem where they were previously lacking.
*   Offers a significant improvement in ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Develops novel extensions to existing optimization frameworks for handling inexact oracles and constraints.
*   The work is technically sound and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The high notational density and complexity may limit accessibility.
*   The experimental evaluation is relatively narrow in scope.
*   Reliance on supplementary materials for key technical details.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of ranking algorithms and optimization. The theoretical guarantees, combined with the empirical improvements, make this work worthy of acceptance. The weaknesses, while present, are not critical and could be addressed in a revised version. The paper is well-written (albeit dense) and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve readability and potentially expand the experimental evaluation.
