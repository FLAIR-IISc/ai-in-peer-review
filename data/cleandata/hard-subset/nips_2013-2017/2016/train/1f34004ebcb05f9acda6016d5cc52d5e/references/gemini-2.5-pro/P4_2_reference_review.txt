### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are learned from node and edge features. The learning task is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization problem. The authors identify a key limitation in prior work [21], where the optimization method—based on gradient descent with the power method for estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives—lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to the inexactness of these estimates.

The main contribution of this work is the development of two novel two-level optimization algorithms for this problem, both with theoretical guarantees. At the lower level, both algorithms replace the standard power method with a more efficient, linearly convergent method [17] to approximate the PageRank vector and its derivatives, providing explicit bounds on the approximation error. At the upper level, two distinct optimization strategies are proposed:

1.  A random gradient-free method, which adapts the framework of [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the objective function can only be evaluated with a known error (an inexact zero-order oracle). A convergence rate is proven for the convex case.
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method for constrained, non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle. This method generalizes existing approaches [7, 25] by incorporating an adaptive step-size search, removing the need to know the gradient's Lipschitz constant beforehand. A convergence rate to a stationary point is established.

For both complete two-level algorithms, the authors provide a full computational complexity analysis. Finally, they present experimental results on a real-world web page ranking task, demonstrating that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of the final loss value.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous and technically sound approach to a challenging optimization problem. The formulation of the Supervised PageRank learning problem within the framework of optimization with inexact oracles is well-motivated and precise. The theoretical contributions, including the convergence and complexity analyses for both the gradient-free (Theorem 1 & 2) and gradient-based (Theorem 3 & 4) methods, are substantial. The analysis systematically accounts for the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and the overall complexity of the upper-level algorithm, which is a key aspect of this class of problems.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes several novel contributions. It is the first to provide provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. More broadly, the general optimization frameworks developed in Sections 4.1 and 5.1 are significant contributions to the optimization literature. The extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained settings with an inexact oracle, and the development of an adaptive gradient method for constrained non-convex problems with an inexact oracle, are valuable and could be applied to other machine learning problems with similar nested structures (e.g., bi-level optimization, certain M-estimation problems). The work successfully bridges the gap between advanced optimization theory and a practical application in information retrieval.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It begins by motivating the problem and outlining the limitations of prior work. The two proposed methods are presented in separate, parallel sections, each starting with the general theoretical framework before applying it to the specific Supervised PageRank problem. This organization makes the core ideas easy to follow. The problem statement, assumptions, and main theoretical results are stated formally and clearly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Assumptions in Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) assumes that the objective function is convex. However, the Supervised PageRank loss function (2.4) is stated to be non-convex in general. The authors address this by assuming that the optimization is performed within a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice, and the paper does not provide guidance on how to ensure this condition is met. The analysis for the gradient-based method is more general as it applies to non-convex functions, which better matches the problem description. A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method would be beneficial.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments show favorable results for the proposed methods, the evaluation could be strengthened in a few areas.
    *   The baseline method (GBP) is stopped using a criterion based on a negative change in the loss function (`< -10^-5`), which is unconventional. A comparison based on a fixed computational budget (e.g., wall-clock time or total arithmetic operations) or more standard convergence criteria would provide a fairer and more comprehensive picture of the algorithms' efficiency.
    *   The evaluation is based solely on the value of the surrogate loss function. While this directly tests the optimization performance, the ultimate goal of a ranking algorithm is to improve ranking quality. Including standard ranking metrics like NDCG or MAP on the test set would provide crucial evidence that minimizing this specific loss function more effectively also leads to better practical performance.
    *   The choice of the Lipschitz constant `L` for the gradient-free method is fixed. Given its importance in the theoretical bounds, a sensitivity analysis showing how its choice affects performance would add value.

3.  **Presentation Details:** The complexity bounds in Theorems 2 and 4 are dense and difficult to parse. While mathematically precise, a more intuitive discussion of how key problem parameters (e.g., number of features `m`, graph size `p`, desired accuracy `ε`) impact the overall complexity would improve the paper's accessibility.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to both the specific problem of learning Supervised PageRank and the general field of optimization with inexact oracles. The proposed algorithms are novel, well-supported by rigorous analysis, and demonstrate empirical improvements over existing methods. The primary weaknesses relate to the strong convexity assumption for one of the methods and the scope of the experimental evaluation. These points, however, do not detract from the paper's core contributions.
