- Summary: Proposes two two-level methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) to learn Supervised PageRank parameters, providing convergence guarantees and outperforming prior work on a Yandex ranking task.

- Strength 1: First theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank with a rigorous inexact-oracle framework.

- Strength 2: Novel two-level framework (lower-level stationary distribution approximation, upper-level optimization) that analyzes trade-offs between approximation accuracy and complexity.

- Strength 3: Detailed complexity bounds and convergence-rate analysis (Theorems 2 and 4).

- Strength 4: Empirical validation on a real-world Yandex dataset showing statistically significant ranking improvements and analysis of accuracy vs. computational cost.

- Strength 5: Clear, well-structured presentation separating theoretical development from empirical results.

- Weakness 1: Experimental scope limited to a single dataset (Yandex); lacking results on other benchmarks (e.g., LETOR).

- Weakness 2: No reported wall-clock runtimes to complement the theoretical complexity bounds.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., Lipschitz constant, accuracy parameters), beyond supplementary figures.

- Weakness 4: Lacks practical implementation details for handling large-scale graphs.

- Weakness 5: Limited discussion of broader applicability and impact beyond web search.

- Overall assessment: Strong and novel theoretical contribution with convincing empirical support; main limitations are experimental breadth and runtime analysis but do not undermine the core contributions.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Pros (compact): First convergence guarantees, rigorous complexity analysis, well-motivated two-level approach, clear empirical improvements, practical relevance to search.

- Cons (compact): Single-dataset evaluation, missing runtime measurements, limited hyperparameter and implementation details, narrow discussion of broader impact.
