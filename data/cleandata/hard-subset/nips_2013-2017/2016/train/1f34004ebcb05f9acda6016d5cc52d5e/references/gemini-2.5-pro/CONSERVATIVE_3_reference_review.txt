### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a graph-based ranking algorithm where node and edge weights are parameterized functions of features. The authors correctly identify a key deficiency in the original training method proposed for this model: due to the iterative nature of calculating the stationary distribution (the PageRank vector) and its derivatives, the optimization is performed with an inexact oracle, and the original work provided no convergence guarantees.

The main contributions of this paper are two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem, both with theoretical convergence guarantees. The first is a gradient-based method that extends existing work on non-convex optimization with inexact oracles to a constrained setting. The second is a gradient-free method that adapts random search techniques for constrained problems where only noisy function evaluations are available. For both methods, the authors provide a formal analysis of the convergence rate and the overall computational complexity, linking the accuracy of the lower-level PageRank computation to the upper-level optimization progress. The proposed methods are evaluated on a web page ranking task using a proprietary dataset, where they are shown to achieve a lower final loss value than the previous state-of-the-art method.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its focus on methodological soundness. It takes an existing, heuristically-optimized model and places its training on a solid theoretical foundation. Providing convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for a non-convex problem with an inexact oracle is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. This addresses a significant gap in the original Supervised PageRank work [21].

2.  **Sound Theoretical Framework:** The proposed optimization methods are not ad-hoc; they are careful adaptations of well-established and powerful optimization frameworks. The gradient-based method builds upon the line of work on inexact first-order methods [7, 25], while the gradient-free method extends the random search framework of Nesterov [16]. This grounding in established theory inspires confidence in the results.

3.  **Analysis of the Inexact Oracle:** The paper does a good job of formalizing the trade-off between computational effort and accuracy. The analysis in Lemmas 1 and 2, which connects the number of lower-level iterations (`N`) to the approximation error of the function and its gradient (`δ`), is crucial for the overall convergence proofs and provides a principled way to set parameters.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The most significant weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is not comprehensive enough to support the claims of practical superiority.
    *   **Lack of Standard Ranking Metrics:** The experiments only report the value of the loss function (2.4). While this is the objective being minimized, it is only a proxy for ranking quality. Standard information retrieval metrics such as Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) are essential for demonstrating that a lower loss translates into a tangible improvement in ranking performance. Without these, the practical significance of the results is unclear.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The proposed methods (GFN, GBN) are only compared against the optimization procedure from the original Supervised PageRank paper (GBP). While this is a necessary comparison, it is insufficient. To demonstrate the significance of this work for the NIPS community, the authors should compare their fully-trained Supervised PageRank model against other established and state-of-the-art learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., coordinate ascent, LambdaMART, or even more recent GNN-based ranking models). As it stands, the paper shows how to better optimize one specific model, but fails to show that this model, even when properly optimized, is competitive in the broader field of learning-to-rank.

2.  **Incremental Originality:** The core Supervised PageRank model is not new. The contribution is purely algorithmic, focusing on the optimization machinery. While this is a valid and useful contribution, it is an incremental improvement on an existing method. The paper would be stronger if the novel optimization techniques enabled new modeling capabilities or unlocked performance on a scale that was previously intractable.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within the optimization domain. For a generally non-convex problem, this is a restrictive assumption that limits the applicability of the formal guarantee. While local convexity around a minimum is a common assumption, its validity here is not discussed or justified.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous theoretical treatment of the optimization of Supervised PageRank.
*   The development of optimization algorithms for non-convex, constrained problems with inexact oracles is a relevant topic for the NIPS community.
*   It successfully resolves an open theoretical question from a previously published work, namely the lack of convergence guarantees.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is critically flawed by the omission of standard ranking metrics, making it impossible to assess the practical impact of the work.
*   The baseline comparison is too narrow, failing to position the work within the broader context of state-of-the-art learning-to-rank methods.
*   The contribution is an incremental improvement to the optimization of a single, somewhat dated model.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a solid piece of theoretical work in optimization, providing the first convergence guarantees for training the Supervised PageRank model. The methodological approach is sound, and the analysis is rigorous. However, as a paper submitted to NIPS, it must also demonstrate significant impact, which requires thorough empirical validation. The current experiments are insufficient in this regard. The lack of standard ranking metrics and the narrow set of baselines prevent a clear assessment of whether the proposed methods lead to a state-of-the-art ranking algorithm. While the technical contribution is commendable, the paper in its current form feels more like a submission to an optimization-focused venue. I am skeptical of its readiness for NIPS without a substantial revision of the experimental section to include proper ranking metrics and a more comprehensive set of baselines.
