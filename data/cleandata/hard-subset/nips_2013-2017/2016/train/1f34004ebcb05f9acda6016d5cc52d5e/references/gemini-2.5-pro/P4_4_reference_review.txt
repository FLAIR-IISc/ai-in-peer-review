### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank that incorporates node and edge features into the random walk process. The learning problem is formulated as the minimization of a non-convex loss function that measures the discrepancy between ranking scores and ground-truth relevance labels. The authors identify a key limitation in the existing state-of-the-art learning algorithm [21]: it relies on a two-level gradient-based method where the oracle for the stationary distribution and its derivatives is inexact (approximated via the power method), and it lacks theoretical convergence guarantees.

The main contribution of this work is to propose and analyze two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem, both of which come with formal convergence guarantees. The first is a gradient-based method, and the second is a gradient-free method.
For the lower level of both algorithms, the authors employ a linearly convergent method from [17] to approximate the stationary distribution, which allows them to control the approximation error. They generalize this method to also estimate the derivatives of the stationary distribution with controlled accuracy.
For the upper level, the paper develops two general optimization frameworks:
1.  An adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, non-convex optimization problems with an inexact first-order oracle. This framework extends prior work [7] and does not require knowledge of the objective function's Lipschitz constant.
2.  A random gradient-free method adapted from [16] for constrained optimization problems where the function value is computed with a known error bound.

The paper provides a full complexity analysis for both proposed algorithms, establishing the number of arithmetic operations required to reach a solution of a given accuracy. Finally, the authors conduct experiments on a real-world web page ranking dataset, demonstrating that their proposed methods outperform the existing algorithm from [21] in terms of ranking quality.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The main claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide convergence rate proofs for both the gradient-based method (to a stationary point for a non-convex problem) and the gradient-free method (to an optimal solution under a local convexity assumption). This theoretical grounding is a significant improvement over the prior work [21], which was heuristic in nature and lacked convergence guarantees. The work is complete, presenting the theoretical development, complexity analysis, and empirical validation on a real-world task. The analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall complexity of the upper-level optimization is a valuable contribution.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-structured and generally easy to follow. The problem setup, the proposed methods, and the theoretical results are presented in a logical order. The distinction between the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) and their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2) is clear. The notation is consistent.

However, a point of potential confusion arises in the analysis of the gradient-free method. The paper initially frames the problem as non-convex, but the convergence analysis for the gradient-free method in Theorem 1 and its subsequent application in Theorem 2 assume that the objective function is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. The paper briefly justifies this by stating that `Φ` can be considered a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a critical assumption that limits the scope of the theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free method to local, rather than global, convergence. This point should be highlighted more explicitly as a limitation of that specific analysis.

**Originality:**
The core optimization techniques (adaptive gradient methods, random gradient-free search) are built upon existing work (e.g., [7], [16], [25]). The main originality of this paper lies in the novel synthesis and extension of these methods to tackle the specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. Key original contributions include:
1.  Developing a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle and an unknown Lipschitz constant.
2.  Adapting random gradient-free methods to the setting of constrained optimization with a controllable, inexact zero-order oracle.
3.  Providing the first theoretically-backed, convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem, complete with complexity bounds.
This work successfully bridges the gap between recent advances in optimization with inexact oracles and a practical problem in machine learning.

**Significance:**
The paper's contributions are significant for several reasons. First, it addresses an important and practical problem in web search and recommendation systems—learning to rank. The proposed algorithms not only have stronger theoretical foundations but are also shown empirically to yield better results, which could lead to improved ranking quality in practice. Second, the general optimization frameworks for handling inexact oracles in constrained (and potentially non-convex) settings are of broader interest to the machine learning and optimization communities. Many large-scale learning problems involve objectives that are computationally expensive to evaluate exactly (e.g., involving matrix inversions, expectations over large datasets, or solutions to differential equations), and the methods developed here could be applicable in those contexts as well. The paper advances the state of the art in learning graph-based ranking models in a demonstrable way.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first convergence guarantees and complexity analyses for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a major gap in the previous literature.
*   **Generalizable Frameworks:** The proposed optimization methods for inexact oracles are presented in a general form and could be applied to other large-scale learning problems.
*   **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments on a real-world commercial search engine dataset demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the existing state-of-the-art algorithm.
*   **High Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and a complete analysis from theory to practice.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   **Assumption of Convexity:** The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption for a problem that is globally non-convex. This limitation should be made more prominent.
*   **Limited Experimental Baseline:** The comparison is made only against the direct predecessor [21]. Including other families of learning-to-rank algorithms would provide a broader context for the performance of the Supervised PageRank model itself.
*   **Incremental Novelty in Optimization:** While the application is novel, the core optimization methods are extensions of existing work rather than entirely new paradigms.
