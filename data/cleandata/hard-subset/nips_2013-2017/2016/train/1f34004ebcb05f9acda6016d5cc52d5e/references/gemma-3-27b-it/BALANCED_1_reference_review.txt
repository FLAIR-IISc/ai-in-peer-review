This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, one gradient-based and one gradient-free. The core contribution lies in providing convergence guarantees for these methods, addressing a known limitation of the original approach from [21], and demonstrating improved ranking quality in experiments. The work builds upon existing literature in PageRank, supervised ranking, and optimization methods, particularly focusing on inexact oracle settings.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** A significant strength is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is crucial, as the original Supervised PageRank algorithm lacked such guarantees. The analysis appears rigorous, building on existing work in inexact optimization.
* **Novelty:** The adaptation of existing optimization techniques (specifically, methods from [16] and [6]) to the constrained, inexact oracle setting of Supervised PageRank represents a novel contribution.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate the superiority of their methods over the state-of-the-art [21] on a real-world web ranking task using data from Yandex.  The comparison against the original algorithm and the reporting of p-values strengthens the empirical findings.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper includes a thorough analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity. The accompanying lemmas and theorems (though lengthy) provide a strong mathematical foundation for the proposed methods.
* **Well-defined Problem:** The problem of Supervised PageRank is clearly defined, and the paper effectively motivates the need for improved optimization techniques in this context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Readability:** The paper is dense and challenging to follow. The mathematical notation is heavy, and the presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  The numerous lemmas and theorems, while rigorous, can make it difficult to grasp the core ideas. The writing style is somewhat terse.
* **Organization:** The structure feels fragmented. While the sections are logically organized, the flow between them isn't always smooth.  For instance, the detailed presentation of the theoretical framework in Sections 3, 4, and 5 could be streamlined or broken down into more digestible parts.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experimental section mentions the importance of choosing the appropriate value for L (Lipschitz constant), but the details of how this was determined are limited. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to [21] is valuable, it would be insightful to compare the proposed methods against other relevant ranking algorithms or baselines.  For example, comparing against simpler ranking functions or alternative approaches to incorporating node/edge features would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Code Availability:**  The paper would be significantly improved by the inclusion of publicly available code. This would allow for easier reproducibility of the results and facilitate further research in this area.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Supervised PageRank algorithms by providing theoretical convergence guarantees.
* The proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world dataset.
* The work presents a novel adaptation of optimization techniques to a challenging problem with a constrained and inexact oracle.
* The detailed mathematical analysis demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying principles.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from significant clarity and readability issues, making it difficult for a broad audience to understand.
* The experimental section could be more comprehensive and include a broader range of baselines.
* The lack of code availability hinders reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking and optimization. The theoretical results and experimental validation are promising. However, the significant clarity and readability issues are a major concern.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity and organization of the paper, providing more detailed explanations of their methods, and addressing the concerns regarding the experimental evaluation and code availability. If these issues are addressed, the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS. Specifically, I suggest:

* **Rewriting key sections with a focus on clarity.** Use more intuitive explanations and avoid overly dense mathematical notation.
* **Providing a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning.** Explain how the values for L, ε, and other parameters were selected.
* **Adding a comparison against additional baselines.** Consider including simpler ranking algorithms or alternative approaches to supervised PageRank.
* **Making the code publicly available.** This will greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.
* **Consider a more concise presentation of the lemmas and theorems.** Perhaps move some of the detailed proofs to the appendix.
