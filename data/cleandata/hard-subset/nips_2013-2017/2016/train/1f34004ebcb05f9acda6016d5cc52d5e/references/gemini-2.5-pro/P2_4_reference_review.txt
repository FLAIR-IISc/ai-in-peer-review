### Review Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a framework that incorporates node and edge features into the PageRank calculation. The authors identify a critical weakness in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: while it uses a two-level optimization scheme, the inexactness of its lower-level oracle (using the power method to estimate the stationary distribution and its derivatives) means the overall algorithm lacks convergence guarantees.

The core contribution of this work is to provide two new, theoretically-grounded, two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem. Both algorithms are built upon the concept of an "inexact oracle" with controllable accuracy.
1.  A **gradient-free method** that adapts the framework of [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the objective function can only be approximated.
2.  A **gradient-based method** that extends the framework of [7] to constrained, non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors provide rigorous convergence rate analyses and derive overall computational complexity bounds, explicitly accounting for the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the number of upper-level iterations. Experimentally, on a real-world web ranking task, both proposed methods are shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach in terms of the final loss function value.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. Its main strength is the rigorous theoretical treatment of the optimization problem.

**Strengths:**
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis, moving from the general framework for optimization with inexact oracles (Theorems 1 and 3) to the specific application for learning Supervised PageRank (Theorems 2 and 4). This provides the convergence guarantees that were missing from prior work [21], which is a significant contribution.
*   **Well-Justified Methodology:** The choice to replace the standard power method with the linearly convergent method from [17] at the lower level is well-motivated. The key is that this method provides an explicit, provable bound on the number of iterations needed to achieve a desired accuracy (Lemmas 1 and 2), which is essential for the inexact oracle framework.
*   **Careful Evaluation:** The authors are careful in their problem formulation and analysis. The treatment of the trade-off between lower-level oracle precision (`δ`) and upper-level complexity (`M`) is particularly insightful and practical for large-scale applications.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Convexity Assumption:** The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. Given that the loss function (2.4) is generally non-convex due to the composition of the `max` operator and the non-linear dependence of `πq(ϕ)` on `ϕ`, this is a strong assumption. The paper should discuss the implications of this assumption more thoroughly. Is it purely for the sake of the theoretical bound, or does it limit the practical settings where the guarantee holds?
*   **Experimental Metrics:** The experiments compare methods based on the final value of the loss function `f(ϕ)`. While this directly measures optimization performance, it is an indirect measure of ranking quality. The inclusion of standard learning-to-rank metrics like NDCG or MAP would have made the experimental validation much stronger by showing that a lower loss value translates to demonstrably better rankings.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically structured, though it is quite dense.

**Strengths:**
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem with the existing approach—the lack of convergence guarantees—and outlines how the proposed methods will solve it.
*   **Logical Flow:** The paper follows a very clear structure: defining the model, detailing the construction of the inexact oracle, presenting the general optimization frameworks, applying them to the specific problem, and finally, presenting experimental results. The separation between the general optimization theory (Subsections 4.1 and 5.1) and its application is effective.

**Weaknesses/Suggestions:**
*   **Notation Density:** The paper uses a large number of symbols, particularly for parameters related to accuracy (`ε`, `δ`), iteration counts (`N`, `M`), and problem constants (`L`, `R`). A small table summarizing the key notation could significantly improve readability for non-experts in this specific area of optimization.
*   **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** Many crucial proofs are deferred to the supplementary material. While standard practice, this makes a full in-paper verification of the technical claims challenging.

### Originality

The work is highly original, particularly in its contributions to optimization theory and its application.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Optimization Frameworks:** The primary novelty lies in the development and analysis of optimization methods for problems with controllable inexact oracles. The adaptation of a random gradient-free method to the constrained setting and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle are significant contributions. While they build on prior work ([7], [16]), the extensions are non-trivial and address a practical class of problems common in machine learning.
*   **Novel Application:** Applying this rigorous optimization machinery to the Supervised PageRank problem is novel and directly remedies the theoretical shortcomings of the original paper [21]. It elevates the learning of this model from a heuristic procedure to a principled optimization with formal guarantees.

### Significance

The paper's results are significant and likely to be impactful for both the optimization and graph-based learning communities.

**Strengths:**
*   **Impact on Optimization:** The general frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles are broadly applicable to many large-scale machine learning problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are computationally prohibitive (e.g., training models on massive datasets where objectives are estimated on mini-batches).
*   **Impact on Web Search and Ranking:** The paper provides a more reliable and provably convergent method for training a powerful class of feature-based ranking models. For practitioners, this means more stable training and potentially better-tuned models, which can directly impact the quality of systems like search engines.
*   **Bridging Theory and Practice:** The work is an excellent example of combining deep optimization theory with a practical, important application. The analysis of complexity trade-offs provides actionable insights for practitioners implementing such systems.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a very strong paper that makes significant contributions to both optimization theory and its application in graph-based ranking. It addresses a clear and important gap in prior work by providing the first provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results, while they could be strengthened with additional metrics, support the claims. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths. I strongly recommend acceptance.
