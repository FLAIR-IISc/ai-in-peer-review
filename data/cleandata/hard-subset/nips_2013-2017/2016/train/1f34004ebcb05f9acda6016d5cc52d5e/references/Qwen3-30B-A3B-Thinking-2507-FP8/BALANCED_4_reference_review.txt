

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank parameters with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors address a significant gap in the existing literature, as the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks convergence guarantees for the non-convex loss minimization problem. The paper's main contributions are: (1) a gradient-based method with convergence rate analysis for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle; (2) a gradient-free method adapted to constrained optimization with inexact zero-order oracle; and (3) a comprehensive analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The authors establish rigorous complexity bounds for both their gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a clear advancement over the state-of-the-art [21] that lacks such guarantees.

2. **Technical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates strong technical depth in developing the theoretical framework. The use of inexact oracle concepts to handle the computational challenges of estimating stationary distributions is well-justified and appropriately formalized. The complexity analysis (Theorems 2 and 4) is thorough and provides valuable insights for practitioners.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real-world web ranking data demonstrate clear practical improvements over the state-of-the-art. The gradient-free method (GFN) consistently outperforms existing methods, and the gradient-based method (GBN) shows significant improvements on smaller query sets. The results are statistically significant (p<0.005) as reported.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem statement, methodology, and theoretical results are logically presented, with appropriate technical depth for the target audience.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty vs. Incremental Contribution:** While the theoretical guarantees are valuable, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how the proposed methods differ from existing optimization frameworks. The gradient-based method builds on [7], and the gradient-free method extends [16] - the novelty is primarily in applying these frameworks to the Supervised PageRank problem with inexact oracle, rather than in the optimization techniques themselves. A more detailed comparison of the specific innovations in the adaptation to this problem would strengthen the paper.

2. **Technical Verification:** Several key proofs are relegated to supplementary materials without sufficient justification. For example, Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 are critical to the paper's theoretical claims, but their proofs aren't provided in the main text. This makes it difficult for reviewers to verify the correctness of the claims without accessing supplementary materials, which isn't ideal for a conference paper.

3. **Experimental Context:** The experimental setup could be more comprehensive. The paper compares against only one baseline (the state-of-the-art method [21]), but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study of their own methods. It would be valuable to see how the algorithms perform on different graph sizes or with varying numbers of features. Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss the practical implications of the computational complexity results in real-world search engine settings.

4. **Practical Considerations:** While the paper claims the gradient-free method avoids derivative calculations for large matrices (a potential advantage), it doesn't provide clear guidance on when this advantage would outweigh the computational cost of the gradient-free method (which appears to require more iterations based on the complexity bounds). The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of practical trade-offs.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank, providing the first convergence guarantees for this problem. The theoretical results are sound and the experimental validation is convincing. However, the novelty of the optimization frameworks themselves is somewhat incremental, and the paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed comparisons to related optimization techniques, including the proofs of key lemmas in the main text, and a more comprehensive experimental analysis.

The paper meets the quality bar for NIPS, with strong theoretical contributions and empirical validation. It advances the state of the art in the specific context of learning Supervised PageRank, though the broader significance for optimization methods could be articulated more clearly.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the discussion of novelty to more clearly distinguish the specific contributions to the optimization framework versus the application to Supervised PageRank.

2. Include key proofs (Lemma 1 and Lemma 2) in the main text to facilitate verification.

3. Add a more comprehensive experimental analysis comparing the two proposed methods against each other and against additional baselines.

4. Provide a clearer discussion of the practical trade-offs between the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, including computational cost versus accuracy.

5. Clarify the real-world implications of the complexity bounds for large-scale search engine applications.
