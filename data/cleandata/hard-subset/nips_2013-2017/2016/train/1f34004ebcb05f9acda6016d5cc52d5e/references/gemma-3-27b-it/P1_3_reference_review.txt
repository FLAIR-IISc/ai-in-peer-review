## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of the classic PageRank algorithm that incorporates node and edge features. The core challenge lies in the non-convexity of the optimization problem and the difficulty in accurately computing the stationary distribution required for gradient-based methods. The authors propose two two-level methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both leveraging a linearly convergent method (from [17]) to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives. They provide convergence guarantees for both methods and analyze the trade-off between accuracy of the lower-level approximation and overall computational complexity. Finally, they demonstrate empirical improvements over the existing state-of-the-art [21] on a web page ranking task.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21]. It addresses the limitations of the original approach, specifically the lack of convergence guarantees.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of random walk-based ranking algorithms (PageRank, HITS) and acknowledge advancements incorporating node/edge features.  They also draw connections to broader optimization techniques, referencing work on stochastic gradient descent ([7]), gradient-free optimization ([16]), and inexact oracle methods ([6]).  The explicit comparison to power methods ([18, 2, 21]) and highlighting the benefits of [17] is well done.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** A significant strength is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, addressing a critical gap in the original Supervised PageRank work. This is a crucial contribution for establishing the reliability of these algorithms.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The authors cleverly combine a lower-level approximation method ([17]) with upper-level optimization techniques (gradient descent and gradient-free approaches), creating a robust two-level optimization framework.
* **Complexity Analysis:**  The detailed analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity provides valuable insight for practical implementation and parameter tuning.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world web ranking dataset demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the previous state-of-the-art.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and the mathematical notation is reasonably clear. The organization into sections for model description, numerical computation, and optimization methods facilitates understanding.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Analysis:** While the theoretical analysis is a strength, it is also dense and potentially difficult to follow for readers not deeply familiar with optimization theory.  More intuitive explanations of the key results would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithms likely depends on the choice of several parameters (e.g., smoothing parameter τ, step size h, Lipschitz constant L).  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis.  The relatively limited discussion of how L was chosen is a concern.
* **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are performed on a single dataset (Yandex search logs). While this is a realistic dataset, results on additional datasets would further strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Limited Comparison to Baseline:**  The comparison to the state-of-the-art [21] is valuable, but the inclusion of more standard ranking algorithms (e.g., standard PageRank, BM25) as baselines would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Supplementary Materials Reliance:**  Many key details, proofs, and additional experiments are relegated to the supplementary materials. While this is acceptable, it makes it harder to quickly assess the full scope of the work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement by providing convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank.
* The proposed methods demonstrate practical improvements over the existing state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task.
* The detailed complexity analysis offers valuable insights for practical implementation.
* The work addresses a relevant and challenging problem in the field of information retrieval and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope (single dataset, limited baselines).
* The reliance on supplementary materials for key details could hinder a quick understanding of the work.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of Supervised PageRank and optimization algorithms for ranking. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. While the complexity of the analysis and limited experimental scope are weaknesses, they are outweighed by the overall quality and novelty of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors:

* Provide more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical results.
* Conduct a more thorough parameter sensitivity analysis.
* Consider including additional datasets and baselines in the experimental evaluation.
* Improve the accessibility of key details currently in the supplementary materials (e.g., by summarizing key proofs in the main text).
