Summary
This paper tackles learning Supervised PageRank (SPR) with provable guarantees under an inexact-oracle setting. It proposes two two-level procedures: (i) a gradient-free method that extends random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with uniformly bounded function-value noise, and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method that accommodates possibly nonconvex objectives with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. On the lower level, both methods replace the standard power method with the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme to approximate the stationary distribution of the query-dependent Markov chain; the authors also derive an iterative scheme to approximate derivatives. They analyze the trade-off between inner accuracy (number of lower-level iterations) and outer complexity, and give end-to-end complexity bounds. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset suggest both proposed methods improve the loss (and stated ranking quality) over the prior gradient-based SPR learner [21].

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank/Supervised Random Walks frameworks [21, 12], Markov-chain stationary distribution computation [17, 18], gradient-free optimization with randomization [13, 1, 16], and inexact-oracle first-order methods [25, 23]. The novelty lies in: (a) adapting random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with bounded, deterministic function-value errors and sphere randomization; (b) an adaptive projected gradient scheme for nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and backtracking on unknown L; (c) coupling these with a linearly convergent Markov-chain solver to obtain overall complexity bounds and convergence guarantees for SPR learning—something the standard power-method-based SPR learner lacks. This combination, the inexact-oracle analyses, and the derivative-approximation scheme appear to go beyond prior SPR work.

Quality
Theoretical development is substantial: clear oracle models, convergence theorems for both upper-level optimizers, and inner–outer complexity accounting that depends on α, graph sparsity s, and dimensions. The nonconvex case is addressed by an adaptive projected gradient method with a concrete stopping criterion and oracle accuracy scheduling. However, the gradient-free guarantee (Theorem 1) assumes convexity (the authors invoke local convexity in a ball Φ), which is strong for pairwise hinge-like losses composed with stationary distributions; this assumption is not justified empirically or theoretically. The lower-level derivative approximation (Lemmas 1–2) is carefully handled, though some constants (e.g., β1) and their magnitudes are deferred to the supplement, which clouds practical implications.

Empirical evaluation uses a real, nontrivial dataset; both proposed methods outperform the baseline in the reported loss and the authors claim improved ranking quality. Yet the evaluation lacks standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP/ERR), wall-clock runtime, and detailed sensitivity/ablation on inner accuracy vs. outer convergence. Comparisons are limited to one prior SPR learner rather than including mainstream LTR baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) or alternative graph-based supervised random walk methods.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized with explicit algorithms and lemmas. Some exposition is dense; several typographical issues and reference formatting problems remain. Important constants and proof ideas are relegated to the supplement, making it harder to assess practical complexity. The “local convexity” assumption for the gradient-free method is mentioned but not motivated. It would help to state β1 and give an intuitive bound, and to summarize the derivative-approximation method’s cost/effect more succinctly.

Originality
Moderately high. While each building block has antecedents, the constrained inexact-oracle gradient-free result, the adaptive inexact first-order method for nonconvex composite objectives with backtracking, and the full two-level complexity analysis for SPR together constitute a novel and useful contribution. Providing convergence guarantees for learning SPR under inexact oracles is a meaningful step beyond prior practice.

Significance
SPR and supervised random walks remain relevant for graph-based ranking and recommendation. A theoretically grounded, practically implementable framework that handles inexact inner solvers is valuable and could influence subsequent work on learning with Markov-chain-based features. The experimental section, while promising, is not strong enough to fully substantiate impact; including standard ranking metrics, runtimes, and broader baselines would strengthen the case.

Pros
- New convergence guarantees for learning SPR with inexact oracle information, both gradient-free and gradient-based.
- Careful inner–outer complexity analysis and trade-off discussion; use of a linearly convergent Markov-chain solver.
- Practical algorithms that appear to improve over a prior SPR learner on real data.
- Adaptive gradient method that handles nonconvexity and unknown L via backtracking.

Cons
- Gradient-free analysis hinges on (local) convexity of a fundamentally nonconvex problem; assumption not validated.
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard IR metrics, runtime comparisons, and broader LTR baselines.
- Some constants and details crucial to practical complexity are opaque (relegated to supplement).
- Clarity and polish: typos, inconsistent references, and heavy reliance on supplementary material.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report NDCG@k/MAP/ERR alongside the loss; include wall-clock runtime and iterations for inner/outer loops.
- Compare with standard LTR baselines (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM) and other supervised random-walk variants.
- Provide sensitivity to α, R, L, ε; and ablations on inner accuracy (N, N1, N2) vs. performance.
- Justify or empirically probe the local convexity assumption; otherwise discuss how to operate without it.
- Move key constants (β1) and their bounds into the main text; clean up references and notation.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite some empirical and clarity shortcomings, the paper advances a nontrivial, theoretically grounded approach to learning SPR with inexact oracles and offers two distinct methods with provable guarantees. The conceptual novelty and potential impact justify acceptance, provided the authors strengthen the experimental section and clarify assumptions.

Confidence: Medium.
