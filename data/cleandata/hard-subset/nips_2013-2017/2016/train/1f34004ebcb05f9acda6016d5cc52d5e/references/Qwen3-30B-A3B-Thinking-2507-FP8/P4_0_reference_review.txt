

# Review for "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking method that incorporates node and edge features. Previous work [21] on this problem used a two-level gradient-based optimization approach but lacked convergence guarantees due to inexact oracle information (approximate calculations of stationary distributions and their derivatives). The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: (1) a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for non-convex problems with inexact first-order oracle, and (2) a gradient-free method adapted for constrained optimization with inexact zero-order oracle. The paper provides theoretical convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods, demonstrating through experiments that they outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality while providing theoretical guarantees.

The work builds on prior optimization literature [7, 16, 25] but extends it to the specific setting of inexact oracles for Supervised PageRank. The paper correctly cites the relevant prior work on Supervised PageRank [21] and the optimization methods [7, 16], showing awareness of the field's context.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness:** The paper makes a significant technical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning. The authors rigorously prove lemmas and theorems (in supplementary materials) establishing that their methods achieve the desired accuracy with well-bounded complexity. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, covering both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches with appropriate adaptations for constrained optimization and inexact oracles.

**Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature (lack of convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning) with a non-trivial extension of existing optimization frameworks. The two-level approach with controlled inexactness at the lower level is novel for this specific problem domain. The adaptation of random gradient-free methods to constrained problems with known error bounds [16] is also a meaningful contribution.

**Significance:** The experimental results clearly demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality across multiple datasets. The theoretical guarantees provide a significant advantage over existing methods, making the approach more reliable for practical applications in search engines and recommendation systems. The paper effectively demonstrates how the theoretical improvements translate to practical performance gains.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The problem statement and motivation are clearly presented, and the technical contributions are explained with appropriate detail. The experimental setup and results are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Technical Accessibility:** The paper requires a strong background in optimization theory to fully appreciate the contributions, which may limit its accessibility to some NeurIPS readers. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the two-level optimization framework for readers less familiar with advanced optimization concepts.

**Experimental Depth:** While the experiments show clear improvements over the state-of-the-art, they could be strengthened by including more ablation studies (e.g., the impact of different accuracy parameters at the lower level) or comparisons with additional baselines. The paper mentions that the gradient-based method requires a convex neighborhood, but doesn't thoroughly explore the practical implications of this constraint.

**Practical Impact:** The paper claims that the methods "can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine," but doesn't provide specific quantitative estimates of these improvements beyond the ranking quality metrics. More concrete discussion of practical deployment considerations would strengthen the significance argument.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- First paper to provide convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning, addressing a clear gap in the literature.
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates and complexity bounds for both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches.
- Demonstrates clear experimental improvements over the state-of-the-art with meaningful ranking quality metrics.
- Develops a novel two-level optimization framework for problems with inexact oracles, with potential applicability beyond Supervised PageRank.
- Well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- The technical depth may be too specialized for a general NeurIPS audience.
- Experimental section could be strengthened with more detailed comparisons and ablation studies.
- Practical impact claims could be more concrete with specific performance metrics for real-world deployment.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing the first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning. The theoretical results are technically sound and rigorously proven, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. The paper addresses a well-motivated problem with a novel approach that extends existing optimization frameworks to a specific but important application domain. While the technical depth may be challenging for some readers, the contribution is substantial and aligns with NeurIPS' focus on theoretical advances with practical implications.

The paper is clearly within NeurIPS scope, addressing a problem at the intersection of optimization theory and graph-based machine learning. The novelty is significant, the quality is high, and the significance is demonstrated through both theoretical advances and empirical results.

**Recommendation: Accept**
