This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors address the challenges of inexact oracle information and constrained optimization within the Supervised PageRank framework. While the paper presents a substantial body of work and attempts to provide rigorous analysis, the level of detail and precision, coupled with numerous minor flaws, raises significant concerns about its overall quality and readiness for NIPS.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its attempt to provide convergence guarantees for algorithms operating with inexact information, a crucial aspect in practical applications like Supervised PageRank where exact calculations are often intractable. The exploration of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity is also valuable. However, the actual implementation details and practical considerations seem underdeveloped compared to the theoretical analysis. 

Numerous weaknesses detract from the paper's impact. First, the notation is often inconsistent and dense, hindering readability. For example, the use of ϕ for both a parameter vector and a distribution is confusing. The definition of Φ, while attempting to ensure positive probabilities, feels ad-hoc and its impact on the convergence analysis isn’t fully justified. 

Second, the theoretical analysis, while extensive, appears overly optimistic and lacks crucial robustness checks. The reliance on strong convexity within a specific region (Φ) feels like a convenient assumption rather than a principled approach to handling the inherent non-convexity of the problem. The constant factors hidden within the Big-O notation of Theorems 2, 3 and 4 seem suspiciously large and potentially render the proposed methods impractical. A more thorough discussion of these factors and their dependence on problem parameters is essential. The connection between the theoretical bounds and the empirical results is weak; the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate the claimed performance gains over the state-of-the-art, especially given the complexities introduced by the proposed methods.

Third, the experimental setup is insufficiently detailed.  The choice of hyperparameters (L, ε, R) is justified with minimal explanation. The sensitivity of the algorithms to these parameters, particularly L in the gradient-free method, should be analyzed more thoroughly. Figure 2 in the supplementary material, while attempting to illustrate this sensitivity, lacks clear labels and a proper description of the experimental setup.  The explanation of why the state-of-the-art method’s parameters were fixed after an initial search is also inadequate. 

Fourth, the paper suffers from several minor technical inaccuracies and inconsistencies. Equation (2.1) should clarify the condition under which the restart probability is zero. The notation used for matrices and vectors (e.g., use of boldface vs. regular text) is inconsistent throughout.  The claim that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art *in ranking quality* needs a more precise definition of “ranking quality” – what metric is being used, and why is it a suitable measure? A statistical significance test (beyond a vague mention of p-values) for the ranking quality improvements would be required.

Fifth, the paper contains numerous typos and grammatical errors, which, while not fatal, detract from its professionalism. ("concider" instead of "consider", "w.r.t." used inconsistently, awkward phrasing throughout).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant problem with a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The analysis of inexact oracle information and the trade-off between accuracy and complexity is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis relies on potentially unrealistic assumptions and lacks robustness checks.
*   The experimental results are not convincing and lack sufficient detail.
*   The paper suffers from numerous technical inaccuracies, inconsistencies, and stylistic flaws.
*   The complexity bounds, even if theoretically correct, appear impractical due to large constant factors.
*   The writing quality and clarity are significantly below the standard expected for NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential novelty and theoretical contributions, the numerous flaws outlined above lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper requires significant revisions, including a more rigorous analysis, a more thorough experimental evaluation, and a substantial improvement in clarity and presentation before it would be suitable for publication in NIPS. The authors must address the concerns regarding the practical implications of their theoretical results and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods in a robust and statistically significant manner.
