**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a feature-rich extension of the classic PageRank algorithm. The authors identify a critical gap in the existing state-of-the-art method: while it uses an iterative, two-level optimization scheme, it relies on inexact approximations of the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) without any formal proof of convergence. The core contribution of this work is to place this learning problem on a solid theoretical footing. The authors propose two new optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—which are specifically designed to handle inexact oracles. They develop a general theoretical framework for both approaches, providing convergence rate guarantees and complexity analyses. The gradient-based method is tailored for constrained, non-convex problems, while the gradient-free method is adapted for constrained convex optimization. These theoretically-grounded algorithms are then shown to empirically outperform the previous, more heuristic approach on a real-world web ranking task.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Theoretical Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold theoretical contribution. It is not merely an incremental improvement for learning PageRank variants. Instead, it introduces and analyzes general optimization frameworks for a challenging and practical setting: constrained optimization where the objective function and/or its gradient cannot be computed exactly, but can be approximated with controllable accuracy. The generalization of gradient methods for non-convex problems with inexact oracles (Section 5.1) and the adaptation of random gradient-free methods to the constrained, inexact setting (Section 4.1) are both significant and novel contributions to the optimization community. This is precisely the kind of foundational work that can enable progress in many other areas where models rely on iterative sub-procedures (e.g., MCMC, variational inference).

2.  **Rigorous and Sound Approach:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between a practical application (web ranking) and rigorous optimization theory. By replacing the heuristic power method with a method [17] that allows for an explicit trade-off between computation and accuracy, the authors are able to build a system with provable guarantees. The detailed analysis of the total complexity, considering both the upper-level optimization steps and the lower-level oracle calls, is thorough and provides valuable insight. This work provides the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, transforming it from a heuristic into a principled algorithm.

3.  **Introduction of a Gradient-Free Approach:** The proposal of a gradient-free method is a particularly interesting and bold direction. In many complex models, computing derivatives can be exceptionally difficult or computationally prohibitive (e.g., when the parameter space is vast, as noted by the authors). Presenting a viable, provably convergent gradient-free alternative is a valuable contribution that expands the applicability of such supervised ranking models.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation:** While the experiments show a clear improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, they are conducted on a single dataset from one search engine. The validation could be strengthened by testing on public benchmark datasets (e.g., LETOR) or by exploring the methods' performance on different graph-based learning problems to demonstrate the generality of the optimization framework. This is a minor weakness, however, as the paper's main contribution is theoretical.

2.  **Clarity on Practical Implications of Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1) assumes the function is convex. The authors acknowledge this by suggesting they operate in a vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. For a generally non-convex problem like the one at hand, a more detailed discussion on the implications of this assumption would be beneficial. For instance, how large is this convex region expected to be in practice, and how sensitive is the algorithm to the starting point?

3.  **Presentation Density:** The paper is quite dense and assumes a high level of expertise in optimization theory. While the content is sound, the presentation could be slightly improved by more explicitly motivating the choices made in the general frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) in the context of the specific Supervised PageRank problem. This would make the paper more accessible to the broader machine learning audience at NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and highly significant theoretical framework for optimization with inexact oracles, a problem of broad relevance in machine learning.
*   It provides the first-ever convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, a notable achievement.
*   The ideas are bold, particularly the introduction and analysis of a gradient-free method for this task, and are likely to inspire follow-up work.
*   The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs and complexity analysis, and validated by experiments showing clear superiority over the previous state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The application itself is not new, and one could argue the work is an improvement on an existing method rather than a completely new problem formulation.
*   The experimental validation is somewhat limited to a single task and dataset.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and bold contribution to the intersection of optimization theory and graph-based machine learning. Its primary value lies in the development of general, provably convergent methods for optimization with inexact oracles, a common but often unaddressed challenge. By providing a rigorous foundation for learning Supervised PageRank, the authors not only improve a specific algorithm but also provide a blueprint for tackling similar problems. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the importance of the core ideas. I value the paper's novelty and theoretical depth highly and strongly recommend acceptance.
