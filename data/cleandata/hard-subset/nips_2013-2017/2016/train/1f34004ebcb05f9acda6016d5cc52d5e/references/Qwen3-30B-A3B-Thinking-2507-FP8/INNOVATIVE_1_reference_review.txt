

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses an important gap in the Supervised PageRank literature by providing the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning the parameters of this important ranking model. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods that combine inexact oracle calculations with gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques, respectively. The key novelty lies in the development of theoretical frameworks for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, which they then apply to the Supervised PageRank problem.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, (2) a gradient-free method that avoids derivative calculations for large matrices, (3) theoretical analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and computational complexity, and (4) empirical validation showing clear improvements over the state-of-the-art.

The paper's main strength is its strong theoretical novelty. While the Supervised PageRank framework [21] has been previously proposed, the authors correctly identify that it lacks convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of the oracle. The authors' solution—developing two-level optimization methods where the lower level provides approximations to the stationary distribution with controlled accuracy, and the upper level optimizes with these approximations—is both elegant and novel. Their theoretical analysis of the convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods is thorough and well-motivated.

The paper is well-organized, with clear problem formulation, theoretical development, and empirical validation. The experimental section demonstrates the practical advantages of their methods, showing consistent improvements in ranking quality over the state-of-the-art [21] across multiple query sets. The gradient-free method's ability to avoid derivative calculations for large matrices is particularly valuable for real-world applications with massive graphs.

In terms of significance, this work advances the state of the art in graph-based ranking by providing the first theoretically grounded learning methods for Supervised PageRank. The authors correctly note that their approach could benefit search engines by increasing reliability and speed, and the theoretical framework they develop has potential applications beyond this specific problem.

The paper's main weaknesses are relatively minor: the proofs are relegated to supplementary materials (though this is common practice), and the experimental section could have included more recent baselines for comparison. However, these do not significantly undermine the paper's strong contributions.

This paper is highly original in its combination of inexact oracle theory with the specific challenges of learning Supervised PageRank. The authors have successfully bridged a theoretical gap in an important practical problem, and their methods provide both theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements over prior work. The paper's focus on the convergence properties of the optimization methods—something missing in prior work—makes it a substantial contribution to the field.

The authors' approach to the problem is bold and well-justified, and they have demonstrated significant improvements over the state-of-the-art in both theory and practice. While the methods are technically sophisticated, they are well-motivated and carefully analyzed.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph-based ranking and optimization. The theoretical novelty combined with empirical validation places it well within the scope of NIPS. The paper's focus on providing convergence guarantees where none existed before is particularly valuable, and the gradient-free method's ability to avoid derivative calculations for large matrices is a practical innovation.

The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission: it is technically sound, well-written, highly original, and significant. The authors have successfully demonstrated that their methods not only outperform existing approaches but also come with theoretical guarantees that previous methods lacked.

I recommend acceptance for its strong theoretical contribution, practical significance, and clear empirical validation. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a new direction for research in constrained optimization with inexact oracles.
