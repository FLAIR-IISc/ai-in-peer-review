Summary
The paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, where node/edge features parameterize restart and transition probabilities. The authors propose two two-level optimization schemes under inexact oracles: (i) a gradient-free projected method that uses a sphere-smoothing finite-difference estimator and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for composite, possibly nonconvex objectives with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. On the lower level, both methods approximate the stationary distribution via a linearly convergent truncated Neumann-series form (attributed to Nesterov–Nemirovski [17]), and the authors generalize it to compute derivatives. They provide bounds on oracle accuracy vs. iteration counts (Lemmas 1–2) and complexity results (Theorems 2 and 4). Experiments on Yandex browsing graphs with 600 queries compare against a prior gradient-based approach [21] using the power method, reporting lower loss values.

Relation to prior work: The gradient-free framework relates to Nesterov–Spokoiny [13] and Agarwal et al./Ghadimi–Lan on zeroth/first-order methods; the inexact oracle concept follows Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; PageRank computation and derivatives build on PageRank/power methods and derivative computation [18, 20–24]. The truncation scheme for PageRank is standard via geometric series; the derivative series is a natural extension of the fixed-point equation.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant practical problem (learning PageRank with features) and recognizes the ubiquity of inexact lower-level computations.
- Provides convergence/complexity analyses for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods under inexact oracles, including an adaptive stepsize/backtracking scheme without prior L.
- Clear definition of the two-level structure and explicit trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and upper-level complexity (at least qualitatively).
- Uses a real dataset from a commercial engine; claims of empirical improvements over a baseline.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and assumptions
- Convexity assumption in Sec. 4.2: Theorem 2 requires convexity of f on Φ, but the objective is globally nonconvex; the paper assumes “Φ is a small vicinity … in which f is convex” without justification. The complexity bound is thus conditional and potentially vacuous. Please either establish verifiable convexity conditions or reposition the result as local with clear assumptions and diagnostics.
- Smoothness of f: The gradient-based framework (Sec. 5) relies on f ∈ C1,1L. Given π(ϕ) depends on ϕ through normalized positive combinations and f(ϕ)=||(Aπ(ϕ))+||^2, smoothness may hold on R++^m, but no proof or bound on L is provided. The hinge-squared is C1, but explicit Lipschitz constants and dependence on graph/feature bounds are not quantified.
- Inexact oracle inequality (5.1): The reduction from Lemmas 1–2 to (5.1) is asserted “similar to [25]” but not shown. The construction uses different norms (∞-norm for gradient error), and the mapping from δ1,δ2 to a single δ in (5.1) needs a careful, explicit derivation.
- Lower-level derivative computation: The proposed Πk recursion (3.2–3.3) appears to be a truncated series solution of (I−(1−α)P^T)dπ = α dπ0 + (1−α)dP^T π, but it substitutes π̃ for π in the right-hand side. Error propagation and stability of this approximation deserve more detailed analysis, especially wrt α and graph size. The constant β1 is deferred to the Supplement and not summarized.
- Claims that [17] “has the lowest proved complexity bound among methods proposed in [5]” are not substantiated in the main text with a precise comparison.

Algorithmic clarity and reproducibility
- Confusing algorithm numbering: “Algorithm 2” refers both to the gradient-free method in the Supplement (Sec. 4.2) and to the adaptive projected gradient method (Sec. 5.1). Please fix numbering to avoid ambiguity.
- Hyperparameters: The experiments set L=L0=1e−4 and ε=1e−6 with little justification; performance sensitivity is only briefly mentioned. For the gradient-free method, the choice of τ and δ relative to ε (per Theorem 1) is not connected to practical settings.
- Practical computation of dP/dϕ: The cost and memory to compute and store derivatives for all edges are not detailed. Complexity statements (e.g., |Q|(10mps+…)) are difficult to interpret without clarifying what “a.o.” means and what operations dominate in sparse settings.

Experimental evaluation
- Metrics: Only the training objective f is reported; there are no standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP/ERR) on held-out data. Claims that methods “outperform” prior work in ranking quality are not supported by such metrics in the main text.
- Baseline fairness/stop criteria: The GBP baseline description is unclear. “The stochastic matrix and its derivative are raised to the power 100” conflates power-iteration count with stepsize. The stopping rule “differences … are less than −1e−5” appears incorrect (negative threshold). Please clarify stopping conditions and ensure equal computational budgets or wall-clock comparisons.
- Variance: The gradient-free method is randomized; results lack error bars or multiple runs.
- Dataset description: The total edges (≈7.5K) being fewer than vertices (≈11.7K) is unusual; a per-query breakdown, sparsity, and α value used would help. The choice to not tune α should be justified.

Writing, formatting, and references
- Many typos/grammar: “concider”, “probablities”, “conducts the random walk”, “a.o.”, “ĩ”, “Nesterov–Nemirovski method” inconsistently spelled; “Rm++1” likely should be Rm
++. Numerous punctuation/spacing issues.
- Stray header text appears mid-paragraph: “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.”
- Notation inconsistencies: use of 1- vs 2-norm in diameter bounds; inconsistent sub/superscripts; P(ϕ)]̃i,i. Please standardize.
- References are severely incomplete/misformatted (missing years/venues/authors; e.g., “O, A. Agarwal, Dekel and L. Xiao”; duplicates; missing page ranges). This is below NeurIPS standards.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical setting (learning PageRank with features) with inexact computation.
- Provides convergence guarantees under inexact oracles, with adaptive step strategy for unknown L.
- Proposes a plausible derivative-computation scheme aligned with series approximations.

Cons
- Key assumptions (local convexity, Lipschitz smoothness) are unstated/unsupported, weakening theoretical claims.
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient (no ranking metrics, unclear baselines, no variance, limited ablations).
- Numerous writing/formatting/reference issues impede readability and reproducibility.
- Novelty over existing inexact-oracle and zeroth/first-order literature appears incremental; lower-level method is standard truncation.

Questions for authors
1. Can you rigorously establish C1,1 smoothness of f on Φ and provide an explicit L depending on α, feature bounds, and graph sizes?
2. Under what conditions is f convex in your chosen Φ? How is Φ chosen to ensure this condition?
3. Please provide full derivation showing that Lemmas 1–2 imply (5.1), including norm conversions and constants.
4. Clarify the computational cost of dP/dϕ and memory footprint for large graphs.
5. Report NDCG@k/MAP on held-out queries, with multiple runs for GFN, and compare wall-clock/runtime vs GBP under matched budgets.
6. Fix the stopping rule for GBP and explain the “power 100” choice.

Recommendation
Reject. While the direction is interesting and the high-level idea of combining inexact-oracle optimization with PageRank learning is valuable, the paper currently has substantial clarity, referencing, and experimental shortcomings, and key theoretical assumptions are not justified. I encourage a revised submission with strengthened theory, cleaner exposition, and more rigorous empirical evaluation. Confidence: high.
