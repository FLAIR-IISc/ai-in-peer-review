

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking algorithm that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods that provide theoretical convergence guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem of learning the model parameters. The lower level of their approach uses a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, while the upper level employs gradient-based and gradient-free optimization with inexact oracle information. The work builds on previous research in supervised ranking [21] and optimization [16, 7, 25], but provides the first theoretical guarantees for this specific learning problem.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing convergence rate analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods applied to this specific problem. Theorems 1-4 establish rigorous guarantees that the state-of-the-art [21] lacks.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The authors demonstrate that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world web ranking task, providing empirical validation of their theoretical claims. The improvements in ranking quality are statistically significant (p < 0.005).

3. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper provides a well-structured mathematical formulation of the Supervised PageRank learning problem and clearly defines the challenges posed by the inexact oracle.

4. **Comprehensive Complexity Analysis**: The authors carefully analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, providing valuable insights for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (Yandex) with limited comparisons. More extensive experiments across multiple datasets and graph structures would strengthen the claims about practical utility.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Other Optimization Approaches**: The paper only compares with the state-of-the-art [21], but doesn't compare with other relevant optimization methods that could be applicable to this problem (e.g., stochastic gradient methods, other inexact oracle approaches).

3. **Insufficient Detail on Implementation**: The experimental section lacks details on hyperparameter tuning, computational resources, and the exact implementation of the lower-level methods. The paper states "we evaluate both our gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms for different values of the accuracies" but doesn't provide sufficient detail on these evaluations.

4. **Overstated Novelty**: The claim that "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle" appears overstated. Related work [16, 7] has already explored similar concepts for different problems.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, such as sensitivity to the choice of the feasible set Φ or potential issues with very large graphs.

## Constructive Suggestions

1. **Expand Experiments**: Include additional experiments on other benchmark datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. Compare with other optimization methods that could be applied to this problem (e.g., stochastic gradient descent with inexact gradients).

2. **Clarify Methodology**: Provide more details about the implementation of the lower-level method (Section 3). Specifically, explain why the method from [17] is superior to the power method used in [21] for their specific application.

3. **Refine Theoretical Claims**: Re-evaluate the novelty claims in the introduction to better position the work relative to existing optimization literature. Acknowledge the similarities with [16, 7] while clearly articulating the specific contributions to the Supervised PageRank problem.

4. **Add Practical Guidance**: Include a discussion of how to choose the key parameters (e.g., the accuracy δ, the number of iterations N) in practice, based on the problem size and desired trade-offs.

5. **Expand Analysis of Trade-offs**: Provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity trade-offs in different graph regimes (e.g., sparse vs. dense graphs, small vs. large graphs).

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the problem of learning Supervised PageRank by providing convergence guarantees that were previously missing in the literature. The experimental results support the theoretical claims with practical improvements over the state-of-the-art. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more comprehensive experiments, better positioning relative to existing optimization methods, and more detailed implementation guidance.

The work is technically sound and addresses an important problem in graph-based ranking, but the significance is somewhat limited by the single-dataset validation and the incremental nature of the theoretical contribution relative to existing optimization literature. The paper would benefit from more careful articulation of the novelty and a broader experimental validation.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about experimental validation and theoretical positioning in a revised version. The paper has clear value for the NeurIPS community, but needs to better demonstrate its broader impact beyond the specific Supervised PageRank application.
