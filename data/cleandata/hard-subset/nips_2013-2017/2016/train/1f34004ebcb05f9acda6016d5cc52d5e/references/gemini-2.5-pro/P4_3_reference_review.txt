This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that extends the classical PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features into the random walk's restart and transition probabilities. The learning problem is formulated as the minimization of a non-convex loss function. A key challenge is that the loss function and its gradient depend on the stationary distribution of a large, parameter-dependent Markov chain, which cannot be computed in closed form.

The authors identify that the existing state-of-the-art method for this problem [21] relies on the power method to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, but lacks theoretical guarantees on the convergence of the overall learning algorithm. To remedy this, the paper proposes two novel two-level optimization algorithms that are equipped with provable convergence guarantees. At the lower level, both methods replace the power method with a more efficient, linearly convergent method [17] to compute the stationary distribution. The authors show how this method can be used to construct an "inexact oracle" that approximates the loss function value and its gradient with a controllable, pre-specified accuracy. At the upper level, two distinct optimization algorithms are proposed to work with this inexact oracle:
1.  A random gradient-free method, which adapts the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems and an inexact zero-order oracle.
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method for general non-convex constrained optimization, which extends the work of Ghadimi and Lan [7] and Devolder et al. [25] to the setting of an inexact first-order oracle.

For both proposed algorithms, the paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence rate and the total computational complexity. Finally, the authors conduct experiments on a real-world web page ranking task, demonstrating that their proposed methods outperform the existing algorithm [21] in terms of final ranking quality.

### Quality
The paper is technically sound. The central claims regarding the convergence and complexity of the proposed algorithms are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors provide a rigorous treatment of the problem, starting from the construction of a controllable inexact oracle (Lemmas 1 and 2) and building upon it to prove convergence for the upper-level optimization algorithms (Theorems 1-4). The theoretical development appears correct and constitutes a complete piece of work, addressing a significant gap—the lack of convergence guarantees—in the prior literature for this problem. The authors are careful to state their assumptions, such as the local convexity assumption used for the convergence analysis of the gradient-free method. The experimental evaluation is solid, comparing the proposed methods against the relevant baseline on a real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, which lends credence to the practical utility of the work.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized and follows a logical structure. It effectively motivates the problem, describes the model, details the proposed methods, and presents empirical results. However, the manuscript is quite dense with technical details and heavy notation, which can make it challenging to follow at times. For instance, the distinction and interplay between the various accuracy parameters (`ε`, `δ`, `δ1`, `δ2`) and iteration counts (`N`, `N1`, `N2`, `M`, `K`) could be made clearer, perhaps with a summary table. While it is standard practice for a theory-heavy paper, the reliance on supplementary materials for all proofs makes a full and independent verification of the results from the main text alone impossible. The writing is otherwise clear and grammatically correct.

### Originality
The paper presents several original contributions. While the Supervised PageRank model [21] and the lower-level numerical method [17] are based on prior work, their combination to form a provably convergent learning framework is novel. The primary originality lies in the development and analysis of the upper-level optimization algorithms. Specifically:
*   The extension of random gradient-free methods to the setting of constrained optimization with a biased, inexact zero-order oracle (Section 4.1, Theorem 1) is a novel contribution to the optimization literature.
*   The development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (Section 5.1, Theorem 3) is a non-trivial generalization of previous work that considered either convex problems [25] or exact oracles [7].
*   This is the first work to provide a full complexity analysis for learning Supervised PageRank, connecting the accuracy of the low-level oracle to the overall convergence of the high-level optimizer. The related work is adequately cited and the paper's contributions are clearly distinguished from prior art.

### Significance
The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners. For the specific application of learning to rank, the paper provides two algorithms that are not only empirically superior to the existing method but also come with the strong theoretical guarantee of convergence. This increases the reliability of the learning process and provides a principled way to manage the trade-off between computational effort and solution accuracy.

More broadly, the optimization frameworks developed in Sections 4.1 and 5.1 are of general interest. Many problems in machine learning involve objective functions that are expensive or impossible to evaluate exactly, such as those involving expectations over large datasets or solutions to systems of equations. The proposed methods for optimization with an inexact oracle are directly applicable to such settings. The paper successfully demonstrates how to combine ideas from numerical analysis (for the oracle) and modern optimization theory to solve a challenging large-scale learning problem, a template that could be valuable for other problems in the field.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem, filling a crucial theoretical gap.
2.  **Novel Optimization Frameworks:** The paper introduces novel extensions to gradient-free and gradient-based optimization methods to handle inexact oracles in constrained and/or non-convex settings, which is of broader interest.
3.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The proposed methods are shown to outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world web ranking task, demonstrating their practical value.
4.  **Technical Soundness:** The work is technically rigorous, with detailed convergence and complexity analyses.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Density and Readability:** The paper is very dense, which may limit its accessibility. Key proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is common but makes full verification challenging.
2.  **Assumptions:** The analysis for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption, which may not always hold in practice for the non-convex objective. However, the gradient-based method does not require this assumption for its convergence guarantee to a stationary point.
