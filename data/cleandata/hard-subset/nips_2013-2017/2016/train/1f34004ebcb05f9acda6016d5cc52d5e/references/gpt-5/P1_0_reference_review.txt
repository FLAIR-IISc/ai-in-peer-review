Summary
The paper studies learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. It proposes two two-level algorithms:

- Lower level: Instead of the usual power method, it uses the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration to approximate the stationary distribution π and, via a new extension, its Jacobian with respect to parameters. It derives explicit bounds on N, N1, N2 iterations to achieve prescribed accuracies δ1 (function value) and δ2 (gradient), and arithmetic-operation complexity bounds.

- Upper level: 
  (a) A random gradient-free method for constrained problems with an inexact zero-order oracle based on spherical smoothing and projection; a convergence theorem (Theorem 1) provides an expected suboptimality bound that accounts for smoothing and oracle noise.
  (b) An adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle (Theorem 3), using backtracking to estimate an unknown oracle “Lipschitz constant”. For the Supervised PageRank objective (a sum of squared positive parts of pairwise score differences), they instantiate both methods and provide end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4).

Experiments on a proprietary Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries, ≈11.7K pages, ≈7.5K edges, ≈1.7K labeled pairs) compare the proposed gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) learners against the state-of-the-art gradient method (GBP) from prior Supervised PageRank work. The authors report lower loss values for GBN/GFN, statistical significance by paired t-tests, and faster convergence of the Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level solver than the power method.

Quality
- Theory: The lower-level approximation lemmas are useful and, together with the inexact-oracle optimization frameworks, lead to coherent two-level complexity guarantees. The gradient-based method’s stopping criterion via the gradient mapping (‖Mk(xk−xk+1)‖) is appropriate for nonconvex settings. The gradient-free analysis extends prior bandit/smoothing methods to constrained domains with bounded oracle noise; the error terms are carefully tracked.

- Assumptions: For the gradient-free upper level, convexity over the feasible ball Φ is assumed, which weakens the “general nonconvex” positioning. For the gradient-based method, the inexact (δ, L)-oracle assumption is justified via the constructed approximations, but the dependence of L on graph/feature parameters is not explicit. Please clarify conditions ensuring f ∈ C1,1 and bounds on L for the Supervised PageRank loss; while (x+ )^2 is C1 with Lipschitz gradient, the dependence on π(ϕ) and its Jacobian merits a brief argument.

- Experiments: The empirical section shows reduced training loss and some convergence plots. However, evaluation uses only the training objective (pairwise squared hinge-like loss). Standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k) on held-out labels are essential for establishing practical significance. Wall-clock/run-time or per-iteration costs and memory use are not reported; the paper instead uses operation counts. Ablations isolating (i) the lower-level solver (power vs Nesterov–Nemirovski) with the same upper-level optimizer, and (ii) upper-level optimizer (GBP vs GBN) with the same lower-level solver, would clarify where gains come from. Hyperparameter choices (e.g., L=1e−4, ε=1e−6) appear ad hoc; include sensitivity analyses beyond the brief remarks.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized but has numerous typos and some notational inconsistencies (e.g., “Rm++1”, “concider”, references missing years/venues). Definitions of constants (e.g., β1) are deferred; provide at least their dependencies in the main text. It would help to:
- State the lower-level algorithm (Nesterov–Nemirovski) more explicitly and summarize its convergence.
- Add pseudocode for the two-level procedures with the way δ, N, N1, N2 are set from ε.
- Clearly separate complexity in big-O with key parameters (|Q|, p, s, r, m, α) and highlight dominant terms.

Originality
- The extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained domains with bounded inexact oracles and spherical randomization is a meaningful, if incremental, contribution beyond Nesterov–Spokoiny and Ghadimi–Lan.
- The adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex objectives with inexact first-order oracles is a notable synthesis that generalizes inexact-oracle methods (e.g., Devolder et al.) beyond convexity and integrates backtracking.
- The systematic two-level analysis for Supervised PageRank (with explicit δ-to-N mappings for both function and gradient) is useful. Related work on supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec, 2011) and BrowseRank are cited; please compare empirically and conceptually to learning-to-rank baselines widely used at NIPS (e.g., LambdaMART/GBDT), which is currently missing.

Significance
Theoretically, the paper provides practically relevant inexact-oracle optimization schemes with convergence guarantees applicable to Markov-chain-based ranking. For practitioners, end-to-end runtime and ranking-quality evidence are insufficient to judge impact. With stronger empirical evaluation and broader baselines, the work could influence learning on large graphs.

Pros
- Solid two-level framework with clear inexact-oracle control and complexity bounds.
- Adaptive gradient method for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracle.
- Extension of gradient-free methods to constrained, inexact settings.
- Practical lower-level improvement (Nesterov–Nemirovski) over power iteration.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics, broader baselines, and runtime/memory comparisons.
- Convexity assumption for gradient-free method limits generality.
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity not well justified.
- Some presentation and referencing issues; many technical details deferred.

Questions for authors
1. Can you report NDCG/MAP on held-out labeled pairs and compare to Supervised Random Walks and standard learning-to-rank (e.g., LambdaMART)?
2. What are wall-clock times and memory footprints for lower-level (π and Jacobian) computations across methods?
3. How sensitive are GBN/GFN to ε, α, and L, and how do these choices affect N, N1, N2 in practice?
4. Can you provide bounds or estimates for L in terms of graph/feature parameters?
5. How large can |Vq| and |Eq| be before Jacobian computation becomes prohibitive? Any sparsity or automatic differentiation tricks?

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept) for the theoretical contributions and the careful two-level analysis. Strengthen the empirical section with standard ranking metrics, runtime comparisons, and broader baselines; improve clarity and references.
