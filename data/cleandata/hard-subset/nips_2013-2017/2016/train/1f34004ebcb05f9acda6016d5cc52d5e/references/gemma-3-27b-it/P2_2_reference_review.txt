## NIPS Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank that incorporates node and edge features. The authors identify the lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based methods and propose two novel two-level optimization approaches: one gradient-based and one gradient-free. They provide convergence rate analysis for both methods and investigate the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity.  Finally, they empirically demonstrate the superiority of their methods compared to the state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Supervised PageRank is a valuable framework for incorporating rich information into ranking algorithms, and efficient optimization is crucial for its practical application.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides convergence guarantees for both proposed algorithms, a significant improvement over existing work.  The analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and complexity is also a valuable contribution. The derivations seem rigorous, though a quick verification of all lemmas and theorems would require significant effort.
* **Practical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation on a real-world web ranking dataset demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The comparison against a state-of-the-art approach is compelling.
* **Well-defined Framework:** The two-level optimization structure is clearly explained and motivates the development of specialized methods for each level.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper delves into both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches, offering a comprehensive exploration of potential optimization strategies.  The inclusion of supplementary materials further demonstrates this thoroughness.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Organization:**  While the paper is generally well-written, it is *very* dense.  The notation is heavy, and the flow of ideas can be challenging to follow, especially for readers unfamiliar with the specific details of PageRank and related optimization techniques.  Section 3, in particular, feels quite packed.  More intuitive explanations and potentially breaking down complex lemmas into smaller steps would improve readability.
* **Incremental Originality:** While the convergence guarantees are novel, the underlying optimization techniques (gradient descent, gradient-free methods) are not groundbreaking in themselves. The primary originality lies in the *application* of these techniques within the specific context of Supervised PageRank, coupled with the analysis tailored to this problem.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experimental section mentions sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., the choice of L) but doesn’t fully explore this. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and the robustness of the results to different parameter settings would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental results are positive, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset.  Exploring the performance of the algorithms on other ranking tasks or graph types would broaden the impact of the work.
* **Overly Detailed Lemmas/Theorems:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the sheer number of lemmas and theorems can make it difficult to grasp the core ideas. Focusing on the most critical theoretical results and providing more high-level summaries would improve clarity.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous mathematical analysis and a solid experimental evaluation.  The claims are generally well-supported, though verifying every detail would require significant effort.  It’s a complete piece of work addressing a well-defined problem.
* **Clarity:** As noted above, clarity is a significant weakness. The paper is dense and requires considerable effort to understand.
* **Originality:** The originality is moderate. The core optimization techniques are not novel, but the application to Supervised PageRank and the convergence guarantees are significant contributions.
* **Significance:** The results are likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners working on ranking algorithms and graph-based machine learning. The improved convergence guarantees and empirical performance could lead to more reliable and efficient ranking systems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides valuable theoretical guarantees for optimizing Supervised PageRank, a practically important problem.
* Demonstrates superior empirical performance compared to the state-of-the-art.
* Offers a comprehensive exploration of both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization strategies.
*  Addresses a gap in the literature regarding convergence analysis for these algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and challenging to read.
* The originality is incremental rather than revolutionary.
* The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **above average** and should be **accepted** with minor revisions. The theoretical contributions and empirical results are strong, despite the clarity issues. Addressing the clarity concerns by simplifying explanations and providing more high-level summaries would significantly improve the paper’s accessibility.  Further exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and evaluation on additional datasets would further strengthen the conclusions.  

**Confidence:** 7/10 (My confidence is somewhat lower due to the density of the paper and the effort required to fully verify the technical details.)
