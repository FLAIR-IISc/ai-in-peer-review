Summary
The paper studies “Supervised PageRank” where restart and transition probabilities depend on node/edge features via linear weights ϕ. The learning objective is a pairwise squared hinge loss over query-dependent browsing graphs. The computation of the loss and its gradient requires the stationary distribution π(ϕ) and its derivatives w.r.t. ϕ, which are only available approximately. The paper proposes two two-level schemes:

- A gradient-free method (upper level) using a random gradient-free oracle with bounded noise and constraints; the lower level uses the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate π(ϕ) to a prescribed accuracy.

- A gradient-based method (upper level) for composite, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle and backtracking on an unknown “Lipschitz constant”; the lower level approximates both π(ϕ) and its derivatives via an adaptation of [17] and a power-like recursion for derivative propagation.

The authors provide convergence rates and complexity bounds, and study the trade-off between inner accuracy (number of lower-level iterations) and outer complexity. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries) report that both proposed methods outperform a prior gradient-based baseline [21] in terms of the training loss, with additional comments on the faster convergence of the Nesterov–Nemirovski method for stationary distribution computation vs. the power method.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical setting: learning parameters of featureized PageRank with only inexact evaluations of π(ϕ) and its derivatives.
- Provides formal convergence guarantees for both a gradient-free scheme (convex case) with bounded evaluation error and a gradient-based scheme (nonconvex) with an inexact oracle and adaptive step-size.
- Lower-level algorithm choice (Nesterov–Nemirovski) is appropriate and the trade-off analysis between inner accuracy and overall complexity is valuable.
- Attempts to evaluate on a real industrial dataset and compares with a prior “state-of-the-art” method.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity:
- Equation (3.1): π̃q(ϕ,N) includes a normalization factor α/(1−(1−α)^{N+1}). The exact truncated Neumann series for π is α Σ_{k=0}^N (1−α)^k (P^T)^k π0 without this normalization. Your variant enforces the sum to one but introduces a different bias. Please justify this choice and its role in Lemma 1; as written, it seems inconsistent with standard truncation.
- Algorithm numbering and notation are inconsistent: “Algorithm 1” (Sec. 4.1), “Algorithm 2” is used twice (gradient-free in Supp. vs. “Adaptive projected gradient” in Sec. 5.1). This makes cross-references hard to follow.
- Multiple formula typos likely change meaning: in Algorithm 2 (Sec. 5.1) the terms “Mk2” clearly intend “Mk/2” (as in standard smoothness inequality f(w) ≤ f(x)+⟨g,w−x⟩+(Mk/2)||w−x||^2+…); similarly “Lk+1 = Mk2” is ambiguous (Mk/2? Mk^2?). Please correct and unify.
- Inequality (5.1) uses an absolute value around f(y)−(f̃(x,δ)−⟨g̃(x,δ),y−x⟩). This is unusual; most inexact-oracle frameworks give upper and lower bounds separately (e.g., Devolder et al., 2014). Please clarify the precise assumption and whether both sides are needed.
- Lemma 2 depends on an “explicitly computable constant” β1 relegated to the supplement. Since β1 appears inside logarithms in iteration bounds, its magnitude matters. This should be defined in the main text or at least summarized.
- Derivative computation (3.2–3.3) requires dpi(ϕ)/dϕ^T for normalized transition probabilities. Explicit formulas and complexity/conditioning issues (e.g., division by small denominators) are not discussed.
- The convexity requirement for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1) is circumvented in Sec. 4.2 by assuming f is convex on a “small vicinity” Φ around a local minimizer. This is a strong, unverified assumption; no evidence is given that the chosen Φ (R=0.99) is contained in a convex region of f for this problem. Thus the guarantees for the gradient-free method are not applicable as stated.
- The gradient-based method aims at nonconvex settings, but the convergence criterion actually bounds a gradient-mapping-like quantity ‖Mk(xk−xk+1)‖; relate this to stationarity more explicitly.

Experimental methodology:
- Only the loss (2.4) is reported. For ranking, metrics like NDCG/MAP are standard. The abstract/intro claim improved ranking quality; yet no such results are shown in the main text. Table 1 and Figure 1 are not included; thus claims are not verifiable.
- Baselines are weak: only the prior Supervised PageRank training method is considered. No comparisons to standard learning-to-rank methods (RankSVM, LambdaMART), or to PageRank variants in the cited literature.
- Hyperparameter choices appear ad hoc—e.g., L=L0=1e−4 without estimation. For GFN, step size depends on L; unusually small L implies very large steps. Sensitivity is only briefly mentioned (supplement).
- The stopping criterion for GBP: “differences between loss on the next and current step are less than −10−5” (negative threshold) is nonsensical; likely a typo. This calls into question fairness and reproducibility.
- Dataset stats are odd (≈11.7K vertices but ≈7.5K edges total across all graphs), suggesting extremely sparse graphs; more context needed. No train/validation/test split for tuning is described; “sorting by graph size” to form Q1j/Q2j/Q3j may introduce distribution shift.
- Reproducibility is limited: code is not provided; β1 and other constants are unspecified; lower-level iteration parameters (N,N1,N2) chosen from theory or tuned?

Presentation:
- Numerous typos, grammar issues, and formatting glitches (e.g., “concider,” “probablities,” “a.o.”, stray footnote “th Conference on…”, garbled indices like “[Pq(ϕ)]̃i,i”). References are incomplete, inconsistently formatted, and often missing years/titles/authors. These issues significantly impair readability.

Relation to prior work:
- The “inexact oracle” framework for convex optimization (Devolder et al., 2014) and nonconvex stochastic (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013) are closely related. The novelty over these, and over Nesterov–Spokoiny (2017) for zero-order methods, is not crisply delineated. The constrained/Euclidean-sphere randomization variation appears incremental.

Pros
- Addresses a practically relevant learning problem with principled inexact-oracle analysis.
- Provides complexity bounds linking inner and outer accuracies.
- Uses a better lower-level solver than power iteration.

Cons
- Theoretical assumptions (convexity on Φ for GFN) not substantiated; several equations/algorithms contain likely errors/typos.
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient and partially unverifiable (missing tables/figures; no ranking metrics; unclear baselines/tuning).
- Presentation quality and references are well below NeurIPS standards.
- Novelty over established inexact-oracle literature is modest.

Recommendation
Reject. While the problem is important and the general direction is promising, the paper in its current form suffers from significant presentation issues, unclear or incorrect formulas, an unconvincing empirical section, and theoretical assumptions that are not validated for the target application. I encourage the authors to (i) fix all algorithmic and notation errors, (ii) clearly justify Eq. (3.1) and provide full derivative/computation details (incl. β1), (iii) add standard ranking metrics and stronger baselines, and (iv) sharpen the novelty claims relative to prior inexact-oracle and zero-order optimization work.

Confidence: High.
