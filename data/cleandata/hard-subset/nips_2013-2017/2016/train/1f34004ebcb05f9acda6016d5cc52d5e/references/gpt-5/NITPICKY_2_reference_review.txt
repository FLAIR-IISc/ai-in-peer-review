Summary
The paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where node/edge features determine restart and transition probabilities. It proposes a two-level approach: (i) a lower-level algorithm to approximate the stationary distribution π(ϕ) and its derivatives using a linearly convergent method (attributed to Nesterov–Nemirovski) instead of the classical power method; and (ii) upper-level optimization with two alternatives: a gradient-free method for convex problems with an inexact zero-order oracle, and an adaptive projected gradient method for (possibly) nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle. The authors derive convergence and complexity bounds for both upper-level methods under inexact oracles, analyze the accuracy/complexity trade-offs across the two levels, and report experiments on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset, claiming improved loss compared to a baseline gradient method.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant setting: optimization with inexact lower-level evaluations for PageRank-type models.
- Clear two-level decomposition and explicit complexity accounting linking lower-level accuracy to upper-level convergence.
- The gradient-free estimator and the adaptive gradient method are standard but adapted to constrained, inexact-oracle settings; the convergence proofs (if correct) are of interest.
- The lower-level use of a linearly convergent averaging of power iterates for π and its derivatives is reasonable and likely preferable to naive power iteration.
- Some empirical improvements in the training loss on a real dataset.

Major weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: The zero-order approach (spherical smoothing, two-point estimator) and inexact-oracle analysis are well-studied (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny 2015; Ghadimi–Lan 2013; Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov 2014; Duchi et al. two-point estimators). The constrained/inexact extensions and backtracking are not convincingly distinguished from prior art; claims of “first time combining” seem overstated.
- Key assumptions are informal or unrealistic: Section 4.2 assumes f becomes convex in a “small vicinity” Φ around a local minimizer and that x* ∈ int Φ. No guidance on how to choose such Φ in practice, yet the main complexity result (Theorem 2) crucially depends on convexity. This undermines the practical applicability of the gradient-free method.
- The smoothness/“Lipschitz gradient” assumption for f is not rigorously justified. While g(z) = (max(0,z))^2 is C1, ensuring global smoothness of f still requires nontrivial smoothness of π(ϕ) w.r.t. ϕ under the restart/transition parameterization. The paper assumes positivity constraints but does not state conditions ensuring the denominators in (2.1), (2.2) are uniformly bounded away from zero across Φ and queries; this is needed for Lipschitzness and for derivative existence.
- Heavy reliance on Supplementary Materials for essential definitions and constants (e.g., β1), with complexity and error constants opaque in the main text.
- Experimental evaluation is weak:
  - Only reports training loss values; no standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP), despite the ranking context. Claims that methods “outperform in ranking quality” are unsupported.
  - Very small labeled set (~1.7K pairs) and small graphs (~11.7K vertices, ~7.5K edges total). Scalability to realistic web graphs is untested. No wall-clock/runtime or memory comparisons, despite claims of efficiency.
  - Baseline tuning is unclear/unfair: GBP raises matrices to power 100; step sizes chosen ad hoc. No sensitivity to α; unclear whether α is learned or fixed.
  - No ablation on N1/N2 (derivative approximation depth) or lower-level tolerance vs. upper-level convergence; no comparison to a modern two-point gradient estimator baseline for nonconvex problems.
- Clarity/Presentation issues are numerous and severe: pervasive typos (“concider”, “probablities”), broken equations (e.g., [Pq(ϕ)]̃i,i), inconsistent notation (p used for both dimension and |Vq| maxima; n vs p vs r), stray conference footnote in the middle of text, and many incomplete/malformed references (authors truncated, missing years/venues). This is far below NeurIPS standards for readability and reproducibility.
- Reproducibility: Many crucial choices are missing (initialization π0q(ϕ), α, stopping criteria and tolerances for lower-level iterations per query, code/data availability). The Yandex data cannot be shared; no synthetic or public benchmarks.

Specific comments and questions
- Precisely state conditions on features V, E and Φ that guarantee π(ϕ) and dπ/dϕ exist and are Lipschitz on Φ. Provide bounds linking α, feature magnitudes, and minimal denominators in (2.1), (2.2).
- Clarify whether α is optimized or fixed; indicate its chosen value experimentally. If optimized, include it in Φ and revisit smoothness.
- Define β1 in the main text and give the dependence of N1, N2 on problem constants to make Lemma 2 actionable.
- Replace the ad hoc convexity-in-a-ball assumption in 4.2 with either a provable property of f or restrict claims accordingly.
- Provide standard ranking metrics, wall-clock/run-time, and memory usage; compare to modern baselines for zeroth-order and first-order nonconvex optimization under noise.
- Fix references and formatting. Current bibliography is incomplete and in places unintelligible.

Minor issues (non-exhaustive)
- Equation (3.1): define π0q(ϕ) explicitly; clarify whether π0 is query-dependent in experiments. Typo “probablities”.
- Notation s = max outdegree; ensure complexity counts reflect sparse operations; “a.o.” should be “arithmetic operations,” define clearly.
- Clarify projection/prox step in Algorithm 2/4; the prox for h≡0 reduces to projection onto Φ; include closed form for the ball.
- Provide units/scale for Lipschitz constants used in experiments (L = 1e−4) and justify.

Originality: Moderate to low. The core techniques are standard; the constrained/inexact extensions are plausible but incremental.

Quality: Mixed. Theoretical development may be sound but relies on unstated constants and assumptions. Experiments are insufficient to support practical claims.

Clarity: Poor; numerous typos, broken notation, and incomplete references.

Significance: Limited by incremental novelty and weak empirical validation.

Recommendation: Reject.

Confidence: 4/5.
