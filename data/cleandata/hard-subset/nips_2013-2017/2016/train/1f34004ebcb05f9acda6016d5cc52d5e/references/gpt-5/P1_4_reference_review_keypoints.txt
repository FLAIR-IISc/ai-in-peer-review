- Paper proposes a two-level learning framework for Supervised PageRank using a Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration for stationary distributions and derivative estimation, plus two upper-level optimizers (zeroth-order projected method with inexact oracle and an adaptive projected gradient for nonconvex composite problems).  
- Authors derive accuracy–cost trade-offs and overall complexity bounds (Theorems 1–4) and evaluate on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset, reporting faster convergence and lower loss in some settings; Nesterov–Nemirovski beats power iteration.  

Strengths:
- Solid optimization framing for learning with inexact oracles, with well-motivated upper-level algorithms and convergence analysis.  
- Lower-level contribution: sensible adoption and extension of Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration to compute derivatives with useful error controls.  
- Inexact-oracle theory: extensions to constrained zeroth-order methods with deterministic noise and proximal gradient for inexact first-order oracles are of independent interest.  
- Practical relevance: addresses a real bottleneck in learning random-walk rankers and shows promising industrial-data results.  

Weaknesses:
- Empirical validation is limited to a single proprietary dataset (∼1.7K labeled pairs) and reports mainly training loss, lacking standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP, Precision@k), wall-clock time, and memory.  
- Baselines are too narrow (only GBP); missing comparisons to other supervised random-walk methods and strong learning-to-rank baselines, and to power iteration with fair stopping criteria.  
- The zeroth-order theoretical guarantee assumes convexity though the objective is generally nonconvex; convexity assumptions near minima are unverified and may not hold in experiments.  
- Manuscript has clarity issues: typos, inconsistent notation, deferred important constants/parameter choices to supplement, and unclear experimental protocol (e.g., GBP step sizes and unusual early stopping).  
- Reproducibility concerns: no code or public data, unspecified hyperparameters and stopping criteria, and uncontextualized claims about “lowest proved complexity.”  

Quality:
- Theoretical results appear technically sound under stated assumptions, but empirical evidence is insufficient to support practical claims.  

Clarity:
- Moderate; needs proofreading, consistent notation, clearer algorithm descriptions, and more experimental detail including standard metrics and runtimes.  

Originality:
- Incremental combination of known techniques, with novelty in adapting methods to constrained, biased-oracle settings and derivative computation for PageRank-like models.  

Significance:
- Moderate: potentially useful for practitioners if validated more thoroughly; theoretical extensions may interest optimization researchers.  

Key suggestions for authors:
- Report standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP/Precision@k), wall-clock times, and ablations on N, N1, N2 and α sensitivity.  
- Clarify GBP baseline details (step sizes, stopping rules) and avoid test-set based early stopping.  
- Discuss/justify the convexity assumption for the zeroth-order method or restrict claims to stationarity results.  
- Provide pseudocode/details for derivative computation, constants like β1, and practical settings for δ, τ, and backtracking.  
- Include proofs or references supporting complexity claims and contextualize the “lowest proved complexity” statement.  
- Improve writing, complete references, and release code/data for reproducibility.  

Pros:
- Sound optimization framework, faster lower-level algorithm with derivative estimation, explicit accuracy–complexity trade-offs, and promising preliminary experiments.  

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard metrics, broader baselines, and runtime; convexity assumption for zeroth-order method is unverified; and clarity/reproducibility issues remain.  

Recommendation:
- Borderline reject: solid theoretical contributions but insufficient empirical validation and clarity for a strong accept; needs stronger experiments, clearer presentation, and tightened assumption discussion.
