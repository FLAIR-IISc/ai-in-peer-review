Summary
The paper tackles learning a supervised variant of PageRank in which restart and transition probabilities are feature-weighted by parameters to be learned. The central difficulty is that the loss depends on the stationary distributions of query-specific Markov chains, and exact values and derivatives are unavailable. The authors propose two two-level methods: (i) a gradient-based method with an adaptive projected gradient scheme using an inexact first-order oracle; and (ii) a random gradient-free method adapted to constrained optimization with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, they replace the power method with the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate stationary distributions and (via a proposed recursion) their derivatives to any target accuracy. They provide convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for the upper-level methods under inexact oracle assumptions and analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset compare the proposed methods to a prior two-level gradient method that uses power iterations.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank and related learning-from-graphs methods, including HITS and variants that incorporate features via personalized PageRank, BrowseRank, and Supervised Random Walks. On optimization, it connects to inexact oracle frameworks (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), random gradient-free methods (Nesterov–Spokoiny; bandit optimization), and nonconvex first/zeroth-order methods (Ghadimi–Lan). The lower-level stationary distribution approximation leverages Nesterov–Nemirovski’s method for Markov chains.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the two-level structure and the role of inexact oracles; rigorous attention to how numerical errors propagate to upper-level optimization.
- A principled replacement of the power method with a linearly convergent method for both stationary distributions and derivative computations, and corresponding complexity accounting.
- New convergence results: (a) a gradient-free method for constrained problems with bounded zero-order oracle error; (b) an adaptive projected gradient method for (possibly nonconvex) composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz parameter. The complexity of the full two-level pipeline is derived.
- Empirical indication that the proposed methods outperform a state-of-the-art baseline on the given dataset and that the lower-level method converges faster than power iterations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Smoothness vs. the chosen loss: The loss uses the positive part (·)+, which is nonsmooth at zero. Yet the gradient-based theory assumes an inexact oracle satisfying a C1,1-type inequality (and the gradient-free analysis assumes f ∈ C1,1 and convex in the feasible set). The paper computes gradients as if the loss were smooth (g̃ uses (Aπ̃)+). This needs a careful treatment (e.g., subgradient oracle, smoothing, or putting the nonsmoothness into h in a composite formulation). As written, there is a mismatch between assumptions and the stated loss.
- Convexity assumptions: For the gradient-free method, the main theorem assumes convexity of f on Φ. The problem is nonconvex; the paper assumes local convexity in a neighborhood containing the solution but provides no justification. Without verification, the stated global complexity guarantee does not apply.
- Inexact oracle constants: The theorems depend on constants L, β1, R, etc. It is unclear how β1 is computed and how large these constants are in practice; the practical guidance to choose δ, N, N1, N2 is thin. The dependence on r, m, |Q| may be large, and it’s unclear how this compares to power-method-based baselines in realistic scales.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: Only one dataset (600 queries; ~1.7K labeled pairs) and primarily the training loss are reported. IR-standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) on held-out data are not shown, despite being most relevant to ranking quality. Claims of improved ranking quality are not substantiated by such metrics. Baseline tuning appears asymmetric (e.g., step sizes for GBP, unspecified smoothing τ in gradient-free, stopping criteria differences). Ablations (e.g., lower-level method swapped in isolation) are minimal in the main text.
- Clarity and presentation: Numerous typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “We concider,” “probablities,” missing words), inconsistent notation (norms, indices, ĩ), and reliance on the supplement for critical proofs hinder readability. Some definitions (e.g., the exact form and properties of the derivative recursion Πk and constant β1) are not sufficiently self-contained.
- Related work breadth: While several relevant citations are included, the broader literature on learning with PageRank variants and efficient PageRank differentiation is only partially covered. For example, more recent analyses of inexact and stochastic oracles and nonconvex proximal gradient with errors could be discussed. The connection to subgradient or proximal methods for nonsmooth losses is missing.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you reconcile the nonsmooth hinge-like loss with the C1,1 assumptions? Are you using subgradients, a smoothed surrogate, or putting the (·)+ into h in a composite framework? Please formalize the oracle construction and guarantees under nonsmoothness.
2. What concrete bound/formula do you use for β1, and how sensitive are N1, N2 to it in practice?
3. How is τ chosen in the gradient-free method, and how sensitive is performance to τ and δ?
4. Can you provide NDCG/MAP (and statistical tests) on held-out sets and include stronger baselines?
5. How large are the graphs where your lower-level method outperforms power iterations in wall-clock time?

Pros
- Solid theoretical development for inexact-oracle optimization tailored to this application.
- Lower-level improvement with linear convergence and derivative estimation.
- End-to-end complexity accounting and convergence guarantees.
- Promising empirical results versus a reasonable baseline.

Cons
- Theoretical assumptions (smoothness, convexity) do not match the stated loss; local convexity is assumed but unverified.
- Limited and nonstandard evaluation (no NDCG/MAP), small dataset, and potentially unfair baseline tuning.
- Clarity issues and reliance on supplementary material for key definitions and proofs.
- Practical guidance and reproducibility are limited; constants in bounds may be impractically large.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the direction—marrying inexact-oracle optimization with efficient PageRank computations—is valuable. However, the mismatch between theory and the nonsmooth loss, unsubstantiated convexity assumptions for the gradient-free method, and limited empirical evaluation on standard ranking metrics undermine the current submission. Strengthening the theoretical treatment to cover the actual nonsmooth objective (or explicitly smoothing it), verifying assumptions, and expanding/clarifying the empirical study would substantially improve the paper.
