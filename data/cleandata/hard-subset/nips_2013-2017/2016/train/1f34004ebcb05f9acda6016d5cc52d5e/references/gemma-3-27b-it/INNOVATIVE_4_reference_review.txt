This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, focusing on the challenges of non-convex optimization with constraints and inexact oracles. The authors propose two two-level methods – one gradient-based and one gradient-free – and provide convergence guarantees for both. The core novelty lies in adapting optimization techniques to this specific problem, particularly in handling the inexactness arising from estimating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The work builds upon existing Supervised PageRank formulations and addresses the lack of convergence proofs in prior work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The adaptation of gradient-based and gradient-free methods to the constrained, inexact oracle setting of Supervised PageRank is a strong point. While the individual methods (power method, gradient descent, etc.) aren’t new, their combination and theoretical analysis in this context are novel. The handling of inexactness through careful error analysis is commendable.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides convergence rate analysis for both proposed methods. This is a significant advantage, as the original Supervised PageRank work lacked such guarantees. The convergence proofs, even if complex, demonstrate a serious attempt to ground the methods in solid theoretical foundations.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to web page ranking, a real-world problem, lends practical relevance to the work.  The experimental results, showing outperformance over the state-of-the-art, further bolster this relevance.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy, complexity, and convergence rate, showcasing a deep understanding of the methods. The lemmae and theorems are numerous and provide a strong basis for understanding the performance characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The paper is dense and mathematically complex. The notation is heavy, and following the derivations requires significant effort.  While rigor is appreciated, it comes at the cost of readability.
* **Experimental Setup:** While the experiments demonstrate improved ranking quality, the experimental setup feels somewhat limited. The comparison is primarily against a single baseline.  Exploring more diverse datasets or comparing against more recent ranking algorithms would strengthen the results. The description of hyperparameters is somewhat brief.
* **Incremental Improvements:** While the theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements are positive, the magnitude of the improvements isn't always dramatic. It’s unclear if the increased complexity of the proposed methods justifies the gains in performance.
* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The assumptions underlying the convergence proofs (convexity on a small region, strong convexity of the prox-function) are stated, but their practical implications and potential limitations aren't fully discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in Supervised PageRank – the lack of convergence guarantees.
* The novel adaptation of optimization techniques to the problem is a valuable contribution.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for understanding the methods' behavior.
* The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements over the state-of-the-art.
* The careful analysis of trade-offs between accuracy and complexity is insightful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is very complex and may be difficult for a broad audience to understand.
* The experimental results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing.
* The improvements over the baseline aren't always substantial.
* The reliance on strong assumptions might limit the applicability of the methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Supervised PageRank. The authors have taken a bold approach to tackling a challenging optimization problem, providing both theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence of improvement. While the paper is complex and the gains aren't always dramatic, the novelty and rigor are compelling. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should consider adding a section summarizing the key contributions in a more accessible way, expanding the experimental evaluation with more baselines and datasets, and providing a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and their practical implications. The complexity of the paper is a concern but is acceptable given the challenging nature of the problem and the thoroughness of the analysis. It’s a paper that pushes the boundaries of what is known, and that’s something to be encouraged.
