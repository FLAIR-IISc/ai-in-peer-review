### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a framework that incorporates node and edge features into the PageRank algorithm. The authors identify a key weakness in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: while it uses a gradient-based approach, it relies on inexact calculations of the stationary distribution and its derivatives (via the power method) without any formal proof of convergence for the overall optimization process.

The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem that come with theoretical convergence guarantees.
1.  **A Gradient-Free Method:** This method adapts the random gradient-free optimization framework of [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the objective function can only be evaluated with a known error bound (an inexact zero-order oracle). The authors provide a convergence rate for this general method and derive the overall computational complexity for solving the Supervised PageRank problem.
2.  **A Gradient-Based Method:** This method extends the adaptive gradient framework of [7] to general non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle. It provides a convergence guarantee to a stationary point and, again, the authors derive the total complexity for the learning problem.

For both methods, the lower level of the optimization relies on the efficient and provably convergent method of [17] to calculate the PageRank vector and its derivatives up to a desired accuracy. A key part of the theoretical contribution is analyzing the trade-off between the accuracy of this lower-level oracle and the overall complexity of the upper-level optimization. Finally, the authors conduct experiments on a real-world web search dataset, showing that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach in terms of the final loss function value.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound and address a significant gap in the previous literature for this problem.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The primary strength is the provision of the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. By rigorously handling the inexactness of the oracle for the PageRank stationary distribution and its gradient, the authors place the optimization on solid theoretical ground.
    *   The development of general optimization frameworks for constrained problems with inexact oracles (both zeroth and first-order) is a valuable contribution in its own right and could be applicable to other large-scale problems where exact function/gradient evaluations are intractable.
    *   The complexity analysis, which connects the required accuracy of the lower-level oracle to the number of iterations in the upper-level optimization, is thorough and insightful.
    *   The experimental results on a large-scale, real-world dataset convincingly demonstrate that the proposed methods achieve a better solution (lower loss) than the previous state-of-the-art.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set Φ. The paper acknowledges that the loss function is generally non-convex and justifies this by assuming Φ is a "small vicinity of a local minimum". This is a strong assumption that limits the generality of the theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free approach. The gradient-based method does not require this, which is an important distinction that could be highlighted more.
    *   The experiments measure performance solely by the value of the loss function. While this is appropriate for evaluating the optimization algorithm's success, it would be more compelling to also report standard information retrieval metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) on the test set to show that a lower loss value translates to a tangible improvement in ranking quality.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written but very dense.
*   **Strengths:** The problem statement, model description, and motivation are clearly laid out in the introduction. The structure, which separates the general optimization frameworks from their specific application to Supervised PageRank, is logical.
*   **Weaknesses:** The connection between the general frameworks (Sections 4.1, 5.1) and their application (Sections 4.2, 5.2) could be made more explicit for the reader. For instance, a clearer statement on how the derived approximations in Lemmas 1 and 2 satisfy the formal requirements of the inexact oracles (e.g., Inequality 5.1) would improve readability. The proofs are relegated to supplementary materials, which is standard, but the main text is packed with theorems and lemmas that can be challenging to digest in a single pass.

**Originality:**
The paper demonstrates solid originality.
*   The core idea of the Supervised PageRank model itself is not new [21], nor are the underlying optimization concepts [7, 16] or the method for calculating PageRank [17].
*   However, the novelty lies in the synthesis of these elements. The adaptation of gradient-free methods to the constrained, inexact oracle setting and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle are novel contributions. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a rigorous, provably convergent optimization framework for the Supervised PageRank learning problem.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant for both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and information retrieval.
*   The paper advances the state of the art by providing robust, theoretically-backed algorithms that empirically outperform the existing method for an important ranking problem.
*   The general optimization frameworks for inexact oracles are of broader interest and could inspire new approaches for other large-scale learning problems where exact computations are prohibitive.
*   For practitioners (e.g., at search engine companies), having reliable and provably convergent algorithms for tuning ranking functions is highly valuable, potentially leading to better and more stable search results.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first convergence guarantees for training the Supervised PageRank model, resolving a critical weakness in prior work.
2.  **Generalizable Frameworks:** It develops general optimization methods for constrained problems with inexact oracles, which are of independent interest.
3.  **Convincing Empirical Results:** Experiments on a real-world dataset show a clear and statistically significant improvement over the state-of-the-art baseline.
4.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The detailed complexity analysis connecting the lower-level oracle accuracy to the upper-level optimization is a high-quality piece of theoretical work.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Limiting Convexity Assumption:** The theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption, which is not fully justified and may not hold in practice. This is a notable limitation of that part of the analysis.
2.  **Lack of Ranking Metrics:** The experimental evaluation would be stronger if it included standard ranking metrics like NDCG to complement the loss function results.

### Overall Recommendation:
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution by developing two provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are compelling. While the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method is a limitation, the contribution of the gradient-based method (which does not require convexity) and the overall framework for handling inexact oracles are more than sufficient to warrant publication. The paper is well-suited for NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept.**
