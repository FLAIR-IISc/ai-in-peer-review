### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, first introduced by Zhukovskii et al. [21]. This model extends the classic PageRank by parameterizing the restart and transition probabilities as linear functions of node and edge features, allowing the ranking to be learned from data. The authors correctly identify a critical gap in the original work: the proposed gradient-based optimization method lacked a proof of convergence because it relied on the power method, which provides only imprecise estimates of the stationary distribution and its gradients.

The core contribution of this paper is to place the learning of Supervised PageRank on firm theoretical ground. To achieve this, the authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both of which come with formal convergence guarantees. Their approach involves two key ideas: 1) On the lower level, they replace the power method with a more efficient, linearly convergent method by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] to approximate the stationary distribution, which importantly allows them to control the approximation error. 2) On the upper level, they develop general optimization frameworks capable of handling this inexactness. The gradient-based method extends work on non-convex optimization [7, 25] to the constrained setting with an inexact first-order oracle. The gradient-free method adapts the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained problems with a zero-order oracle that returns function values with a known error bound.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The primary contribution is the theoretical analysis that guarantees convergence for a problem where such guarantees were previously absent. The authors derive complexity bounds for their complete two-level algorithms, carefully analyzing the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the number of upper-level iterations. This rigorous treatment is a significant improvement over the prior art. The experimental results, while based on a single real-world dataset, support the theoretical claims by showing that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach [21].

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured logically. It clearly motivates the problem by outlining the shortcomings of the existing method. It then systematically presents its two proposed solutions, first introducing the general optimization framework and then detailing its specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the contributions are clearly articulated.

**Originality:** The true novelty of this work lies not in inventing a new ranking algorithm, but in the bold approach of rigorously connecting the theory of optimization with inexact oracles to a practical, large-scale machine learning problem. While the optimization methods are extensions of existing foundational work, their adaptation to the constrained setting with provable error control is a novel and non-trivial contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a complete, provably convergent framework for learning the Supervised PageRank model. This conceptual bridge is precisely the kind of innovative work that the field needs.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is twofold. First, it makes the Supervised PageRank model far more reliable and principled, which is important for practical applications like web search where stability and predictability are crucial. Second, and perhaps more broadly, the general frameworks for gradient-based and gradient-free optimization with inexact oracles could be highly influential. Many complex machine learning problems, such as meta-learning and bi-level optimization, involve objective functions or gradients that can only be approximated. The methods and analysis presented here could provide a valuable blueprint for developing provably convergent algorithms in these other domains.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper is strong, but there are a few points that could be improved.
1.  The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set Φ. However, the loss function (2.4) is generally non-convex. The authors acknowledge this by stating they assume Φ is a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that isn't empirically verified, and it somewhat limits the generality of this specific theoretical result.
2.  The experimental validation is conducted on a single, proprietary dataset. While the results are compelling, demonstrating the methods' effectiveness on publicly available graph ranking benchmarks would further strengthen the paper's empirical claims.

Despite these points, I view them as minor flaws in an otherwise excellent paper. The primary contribution is the novel theoretical framework, and for a paper of this nature, I am willing to overlook a strong assumption in the analysis or a limited experimental scope, as these can be addressed in future work.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** Provides the first provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank, a significant theoretical leap over the previous heuristic approach.
*   **Generalizable Framework:** The proposed optimization methods for inexact oracles are a substantial contribution in their own right and are likely to be applicable to a wide range of other machine learning problems.
*   **Technical Depth:** The paper presents a rigorous and complete analysis, including complexity bounds that connect lower-level approximation accuracy to overall performance.
*   **Bold Direction:** The work successfully bridges the gap between abstract optimization theory and a practical application, providing a template for how to add rigor to complex learning systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free method rests on a local convexity assumption which may not hold in practice and is not verified.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** Experiments are confined to a single proprietary dataset, which limits the generalizability of the empirical findings.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It embodies the kind of ambitious research that NIPS should champion. It takes an existing, practical model and fundamentally improves it by providing the missing theoretical foundation. The development of general optimization tools for inexact oracles is a significant contribution that pushes the field forward. While the paper is not without minor flaws, its novelty, technical depth, and potential for broader impact are outstanding. This is a complete and important piece of work that other researchers are very likely to build upon.
