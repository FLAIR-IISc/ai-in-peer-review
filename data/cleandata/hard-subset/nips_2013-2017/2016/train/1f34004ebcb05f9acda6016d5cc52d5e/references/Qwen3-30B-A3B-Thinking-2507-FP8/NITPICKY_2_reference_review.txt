

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing the lack of convergence guarantees in the state-of-the-art method [21]. The authors develop theoretical frameworks for both approaches, with convergence rate analysis and complexity bounds, and demonstrate empirical improvements on a web ranking task.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important gap in the Supervised PageRank literature by providing convergence guarantees for optimization methods.
- The two-level approach (with lower-level approximation of stationary distribution and upper-level optimization) is well-motivated.
- The experimental results show consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of loss function values.
- The complexity bounds in Theorems 2 and 4 are theoretically sound and provide useful insights.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Technical Inconsistency:** The paper claims to solve a *non-convex* optimization problem (Section 1, "non-convex loss-minimization problem") but then applies convex optimization techniques throughout (Section 4.1, "Let f ∈ C1,1L(‖·‖) and convex" in Theorem 1). This contradicts the problem statement and undermines the theoretical contribution.

2. **Formatting and Typos:** Numerous minor but distracting errors throughout the paper: "concider" (Section 2), "probablities" (Section 3), "th Conference" (first page), and inconsistent notation (e.g., π0q(ϕ) vs π0(ϕ)). The reference list contains incomplete entries like "O, A. Agarwal, Dekel and L. Xiao, Optimal algorithms for online convex optimization with multi-point bandit feedback, , ".

3. **Theoretical Flaw in Gradient-Free Method:** The biased gradient-free oracle is defined as "gτ(x, δ) = mτ(f̃(x+τξ, δ)−f̃(x, δ))ξ" (Section 4.1). This should be "gτ(x, δ) = (m/τ)(f̃(x+τξ, δ)−f̃(x, δ))ξ" to match standard gradient-free methods, as "mτ" would make the gradient estimate diverge as τ→0. This is a critical mathematical error.

4. **Inconsistent Problem Statement:** The paper claims the problem is non-convex (Section 1), but then in Section 4.2, it states "we assume that there is a local minimum ϕ* and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ*, in which f(ϕ) (2.4) is convex." This creates confusion about the theoretical guarantees.

5. **Lack of Clear Novelty:** The gradient-free method appears to be a straightforward adaptation of [16] to constrained optimization with inexact oracle, which is not sufficiently justified as a novel contribution. The gradient-based method closely follows [7] with modifications for inexact oracle.

6. **Experimental Details:** The paper claims "GFN significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art algorithms on all test sets" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the claimed improvements (beyond a vague mention of "p-values less than 0.005" without providing the actual values or test details).

7. **Reference Incompleteness:** Several references are improperly formatted or incomplete (e.g., [18] is listed as "The PageRank citation ranking: Bringing order to the web, L. Page, S. Brin, R. Motwani, T. Winograd, Stanford InfoLab, , " with missing publication details).

**Summary of Major Concerns:**

The paper presents a well-motivated problem with some interesting theoretical results, but the technical flaws (particularly the contradiction between claiming a non-convex problem and using convex optimization techniques) and numerous formatting errors significantly undermine the contribution. The critical mathematical error in the gradient-free method's definition (mτ instead of m/τ) would invalidate the theoretical guarantees for that method.

**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper has some interesting ideas and empirical results, the critical technical errors and inconsistencies in the theoretical framework, combined with numerous formatting and typographical errors, prevent this from being a sound scientific contribution. The contradiction between the problem statement (non-convex) and the theoretical treatment (convex) is particularly concerning and suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of the problem.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Clarify the convexity/non-convexity of the problem throughout the paper.
2. Correct the critical mathematical error in the gradient-free oracle definition (mτ → m/τ).
3. Fix all formatting inconsistencies and typos.
4. Provide complete reference information.
5. Better justify the novelty of the proposed methods compared to existing literature.

The paper's core idea has merit, but as presented, it contains significant technical flaws that prevent it from being accepted as a sound scientific contribution.
