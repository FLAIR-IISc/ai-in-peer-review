Summary:
The paper tackles learning parameters for Supervised PageRank where node/edge features linearly parametrize restart and transition probabilities. The resulting loss is nonconvex, and computing exact function values and gradients requires stationary distributions and their sensitivities. The authors propose two two-level methods:

- A gradient-free method: extend random zeroth-order optimization to constrained domains with a uniformly bounded, inexact zero-order oracle. They use a sphere-based randomization and give a convergence bound for convex objectives (Theorem 1). On the lower level, they approximate the PageRank stationary distribution using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method [17], with error control (Lemma 1).

- A gradient-based method: an adaptive projected gradient framework for composite, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle (Inequality 5.1), using backtracking on an unknown “Lipschitz” constant (Theorem 3). They provide a way to approximate the gradient of the loss via a generalization of [17] to compute sensitivities (Lemma 2).

They analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity, and provide complexity bounds for both methods (Theorems 2 and 4). Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset (≈600 queries, ~11.7k nodes total) suggest both proposed methods outperform a prior gradient-based method [21]; the gradient-free method shows the strongest empirical gains by their loss metric.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important practical setting: optimization with inexact function/gradient information induced by iterative PageRank computations.
- Provides convergence guarantees and explicit complexity bounds that incorporate oracle inaccuracies; connects upper- and lower-level accuracies.
- Uses the Nesterov–Nemirovski method for stationary distributions to achieve linear convergence at the lower level and derives error bounds for both function and gradient approximations.
- Extends random gradient-free optimization to constrained domains under bounded additive noise, and proposes an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle for nonconvex composite objectives.
- The overall two-level viewpoint (accuracy allocation across levels) is thoughtful and relevant to large-scale, iterative ranking algorithms.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Quality and rigor
- The gradient-free method’s convergence is proved under convexity of f on the feasible set, yet the target problem is nonconvex. The paper assumes local convexity in a neighborhood Φ of a local minimum; this is a strong, unverifiable assumption in practice and undermines guarantees on real data. It is not clear how Φ is chosen (beyond an ad-hoc ball around the all-ones vector) to ensure convexity.
- The inexact first-order oracle condition (5.1) is central. While the paper sketches how Lemmas 1–2 imply (5.1), key constants and assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz smoothness of f wrt parameters given the PageRank map) are relegated to the supplement. The construction of Π̃q and the derivative bounds depend on nontrivial stability properties of stationary distributions; clearer main-text justification is needed.
- The adaptive gradient method’s analysis depends on an unknown constant L, yet in experiments L0 and δ are chosen heuristically (e.g., L0=10^-4), with no guidance on estimation. The sensitivity of results to these choices is not systematically studied.
- Several formulae and notations have typos or are hard to parse (e.g., [P]̃i,i, “Rm++1”, broken sentences). Many references are incomplete or incorrectly formatted, making it hard to verify prior art.

Empirical validation
- The experimental evaluation is limited. The dataset is small (≈600 queries, 1.7k labeled pairs, ~11.7k nodes total), far from “huge-scale” graph settings where PageRank-based learning is typically motivated. Claims about scalability and speed are not substantiated by wall-clock or memory usage comparisons.
- Baselines are weak. Only [21] and an untuned ϕ=1 baseline are compared. There is no comparison to standard learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM), to other supervised random-walk approaches (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011), or to alternative PageRank solvers/derivative approaches. This makes it hard to assess significance relative to established practice.
- Evaluation metrics: results are reported mostly in terms of the training objective or a loss on test sets; standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) are not presented. The paper claims improved “ranking quality” but does not substantiate it with conventional IR metrics.
- Hyperparameter tuning is insufficiently described (e.g., choice of ε=1e-6, step sizes for GBP, N1/N2 settings). No ablations of the accuracy–complexity trade-off are shown beyond a single figure in the supplement.
- Statistical testing is mentioned (p<0.005), but effect sizes, test metrics, and detailed per-query results are not reported.

Originality
- The lower-level use of [17] is known; computing derivatives of stationary distributions has prior work ([20], [21], Andrew 1967/1969). The novelty is mainly in tying those approximations to inexact-oracle upper-level methods and extending existing frameworks (Nesterov–Spokoiny for zeroth-order, Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov and Ghadimi–Lan for inexact/ nonconvex first-order) to constrained and nonconvex settings with specific oracle structures. The contributions seem incremental; clearer positioning versus these established lines of work is needed.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typographical and formatting errors, inconsistent notation, and missing reference details. Important assumptions and constants are deferred to the supplement, making the main paper hard to verify.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a practically relevant problem and provides theoretically grounded methods for inexact-oracle optimization in this context.
- Convergence and complexity analyses that connect lower- and upper-level accuracy choices.
- Some empirical evidence of improvement over a prior Supervised PageRank training method.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong assumptions (local convexity, oracle smoothness) and unclear constants reduce the practical force of the theory.
- Limited and nonstandard empirical evaluation; no comparisons to strong, established baselines or standard ranking metrics; small-scale data.
- Clarity and presentation issues, with incomplete references and typos.
- Incremental novelty relative to established inexact-oracle and nonconvex optimization literature.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments on larger, publicly available datasets; report NDCG/MAP and runtime/memory; include strong learning-to-rank baselines and alternative supervised random-walk methods.
- Clarify and justify oracle assumptions and constants; move key parts of Lemmas 1–2 into the main text; provide intuition and bounds for β1.
- Systematically study sensitivity to ε, L0, τ, N1/N2; report effect sizes and ablations of the accuracy–complexity trade-off.
- Fix notation/typos; complete and standardize references; improve exposition.

Recommendation: Reject. The theoretical framing is interesting, but the empirical validation is insufficient and the assumptions and clarity issues limit confidence. With stronger experiments against established baselines, clearer assumptions, and improved presentation, this work could be much more compelling.
