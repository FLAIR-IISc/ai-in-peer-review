This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, one gradient-based and one gradient-free. The authors address the lack of convergence guarantees for existing methods and aim to improve ranking quality. They focus on approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk efficiently and analyze the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity.

The core idea builds on existing Supervised PageRank formulations [21] and focuses on improving the optimization process. The paper rigorously analyzes the convergence rates of both proposed methods, providing theoretical guarantees lacking in prior work.  The methods utilize an efficient linearly convergent method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution and explore the impact of accuracy on overall performance. The approach leverages recent advancements in gradient and gradient-free optimization techniques, adapting them to the specific constraints and inexact oracle nature of the Supervised PageRank problem.  Related work in power methods for PageRank [18, 2, 21], optimization methods [16, 7], and inexact oracle optimization [25] is acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:**  A significant strength is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a crucial contribution, addressing a key limitation of prior work [21].
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper includes detailed analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy, complexity, and convergence, providing valuable insights into the practical implementation of the algorithms.
*   **Well-Defined Problem:** The Supervised PageRank problem is clearly articulated, and the proposed methods build upon a solid foundation of existing research.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  Empirical results demonstrate improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art gradient-based method [21].
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors thoroughly review related work in PageRank, optimization, and inexact oracle optimization, establishing the novelty of their contribution.
*   **Detailed Complexity Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed complexity analysis of the proposed algorithms, making it easier to assess their scalability and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Notation:** The notation, especially regarding lemmas and equations, is often dense and can be difficult to follow without careful reading.  A more streamlined presentation would improve readability.
*   **Implementation Details:** While the paper provides a theoretical framework, some aspects of the practical implementation (e.g., parameter tuning, specific choices of norms) could be elaborated further.  The explanation of the experimental setup lacks detail.
*   **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset from Yandex. While results are presented for subsets of queries, broadening the experimental evaluation with other datasets would increase confidence in the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison with the state-of-the-art method [21] is central, but a more extensive comparison with other relevant ranking algorithms (e.g., those based on learning-to-rank approaches) would provide a broader context for the results.
*   **Length:** The paper is quite long and detailed. Some of the supplementary material could potentially be integrated more seamlessly into the main text for better flow.
*   **Reproducibility:** While the authors state the algorithms are implementable, providing code or more explicit instructions for replication would greatly enhance the paper's impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank optimization, a crucial advance over existing methods.
*   The rigorous analysis of trade-offs and complexity offers valuable practical insights.
*   The experimental results demonstrate improved performance on a real-world ranking task.
*   The thorough literature review and clear problem definition strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The density of notation and complex presentation can hinder readability.
*   The limited dataset and comparison with other algorithms restrict the generalizability of the findings.
*   The lack of code or more detailed implementation guidance limits reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid and significant contribution to the field of ranking algorithms. The theoretical guarantees and rigorous analysis are particularly noteworthy. While the paper could benefit from improved clarity, broader experimental validation, and increased reproducibility, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity and reproducibility concerns.  Specifically, the authors should consider streamlining the notation, providing more implementation details, and expanding the experimental evaluation.
