

# Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes two optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing the lack of convergence guarantees in the existing method [21]. The authors develop a two-level approach where the lower level uses a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with any required accuracy, and the upper level applies gradient-based or gradient-free optimization. The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for both methods, and demonstrates empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task.

## Quality
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by addressing a gap in the theoretical foundation of Supervised PageRank learning. The authors correctly identify that the existing method [21] lacks convergence guarantees due to the use of inexact oracle information (approximate stationary distributions from power methods). By introducing two-level optimization methods with provable convergence rates, the paper provides a theoretically grounded solution to this problem.

The theoretical analysis is thorough, with clear statements of lemmas and theorems supported by proofs in supplementary materials. The authors correctly analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and the overall computational complexity. The complexity bounds in Theorems 2 and 4 appear well-derived, though the paper could have provided more intuition about the practical implications of these bounds.

However, the experimental evaluation has some limitations. The paper reports modest improvements over the state-of-the-art (Table 1), but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how sensitive the results are to hyperparameter choices (e.g., the impact of different values of L, ε, and the number of iterations in the lower-level method). The comparison with only one baseline (GBP [21]) is insufficient for a NIPS paper, as it doesn't establish how the proposed methods compare to other optimization approaches for similar problems.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the key concepts (e.g., inexact oracle, two-level optimization) are well-explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand the methods.

However, some sections could be clearer. The paper mentions "Supplementary Materials" multiple times but doesn't provide the actual supplementary content in the submission, which is necessary for evaluating the proofs. The experimental section (Section 6) would benefit from more detail about the data preprocessing steps and how the seed set U was constructed. The paper also doesn't clearly explain why the specific choice of Φ = {ϕ ∈ R^m : ‖ϕ - e_m‖_2 ≤ 0.99} was made.

## Originality
The paper's main contribution is in providing convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning, which is a significant gap in the existing literature. The combination of gradient-based and gradient-free methods with an inexact oracle for this specific problem appears novel. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work (e.g., [5], [16], [21]) and clearly articulate how their work differs from it.

The paper claims to be the first to combine random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods with efficient inexact oracle methods for large-scale optimization, which seems plausible given the references provided. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize how their specific approach differs from similar techniques in other domains (e.g., [16] for gradient-free methods), which would strengthen the originality claim.

## Significance
The significance of this work is somewhat limited. While improving web ranking algorithms is important, the paper demonstrates only modest improvements over the state-of-the-art (Table 1). The paper claims "outperform it in the ranking quality in experiments," but doesn't provide sufficient context about how these improvements translate to real-world search engine performance. The practical impact of the theoretical guarantees isn't clearly articulated.

The paper's theoretical contribution (convergence guarantees for an inexact oracle setting) is significant for optimization researchers, but the paper doesn't sufficiently connect this to broader implications in machine learning or web search. The experimental results don't provide enough evidence to support the claim that these methods would "increase the reliability and speed of a search engine" as stated in the conclusion.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical advances for an important problem. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficiently detailed and the significance claims are overstated relative to the modest empirical improvements. The paper would be stronger with more comprehensive experiments comparing against additional baselines and a clearer explanation of the practical impact.

**Strengths:**
- Provides convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning, addressing a significant gap in the literature
- Thorough theoretical analysis with well-justified complexity bounds
- Clear problem formulation and well-structured presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental evaluation (only one baseline, insufficient hyperparameter analysis)
- Overstated significance claims without sufficient evidence
- Missing supplementary materials referenced in the paper
- Insufficient detail about experimental setup and hyperparameter choices

The paper is a solid technical contribution to optimization theory, but its significance for NIPS is limited by the modest empirical improvements and lack of broader impact discussion. I recommend **weak rejection** as it doesn't meet the bar for NIPS in terms of significance for the machine learning community. The authors should strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide more context about the practical impact, and clarify the novelty of their approach relative to existing optimization literature.
