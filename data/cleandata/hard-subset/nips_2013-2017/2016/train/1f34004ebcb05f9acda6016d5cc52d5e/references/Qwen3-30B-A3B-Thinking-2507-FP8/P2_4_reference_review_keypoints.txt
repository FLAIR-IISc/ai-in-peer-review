- Addresses learning parameters for Supervised PageRank with node/edge features and proposes two two-level methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) to handle inexact oracle from stationary distribution estimation.  
- Main contribution: provides convergence guarantees for both methods under inexact oracle, improving on prior work [21].  
- Theoretical analysis includes convergence rates and computational complexity.  
- Empirical results on a Yandex web-ranking task show both methods outperform the state-of-the-art, with the gradient-free method especially strong.  
- Strong technical quality: rigorous proofs (Lemmas 1 and 2) that address accuracy requirements for approximating stationary distributions and derivatives.  
- Evaluation lacks detailed ablation studies on parameter choices and comparisons with other non-convex optimization methods.  
- Practical scalability beyond the tested graphs (~11.7K vertices) is not thoroughly explored.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written with consistent notation and a clear two-level optimization framing.  
- Supplementary materials contain proofs, but key technical steps could be explained more in the main text; hyperparameter choices (e.g., R=0.99) need justification.  
- Originality: novel application of inexact-oracle optimization to Supervised PageRank and a valuable two-level framework, though methods build on prior optimization work.  
- Strong contribution by combining efficient Markov chain solvers with optimization for this specific application, but clarity needed on technique vs. application novelty.  
- Significance: important for search/ranking systems; theoretical guarantees boost practical confidence and may generalize to other graph-based learning problems.  
- Strengths: first theoretical convergence guarantees for this problem, development/analysis of both methods, empirical superiority, and analysis of trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and computation.  
- Weaknesses: limited method comparisons, insufficient scalability analysis, and unclear separation of novelty between optimization techniques and application.  
- Recommendation: accept—paper is novel, technically sound, significant, and meets conference criteria despite minor weaknesses.
