

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking method that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) that provide convergence guarantees for the non-convex loss minimization problem that was previously unsolved with theoretical guarantees. The key innovation lies in their use of inexact oracles (with provable accuracy bounds) and subsequent adaptation of optimization methods to this setting, which they demonstrate outperforms the state-of-the-art [21] on real-world ranking tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors develop a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact first-order and zero-order oracles, which is valuable beyond just this specific application.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art, with statistically significant performance gains (p < 0.005). This makes the theoretical contributions immediately applicable to real-world ranking systems.

3. **Novel Algorithmic Design**: The adaptation of the Nesterov's linearly convergent method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution, combined with the two-level optimization framework, is well-motivated and theoretically sound.

4. **Thorough Analysis**: The authors provide detailed complexity bounds for both algorithms, which is crucial for understanding the practical trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Convexity Assumption**: The paper claims convexity of the problem on a small neighborhood of ϕ* (Section 4.2), but this is not clearly justified. The loss function (2.4) is inherently non-convex due to the nonlinear dependency of πq(ϕ) on ϕ, which could undermine the theoretical guarantees. The authors should clarify under what conditions this local convexity holds.

2. **Complexity Bounds**: The complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) appear extremely high, especially for large-scale applications. For instance, Theorem 4's complexity scales as O(L/ε × r|Q|/α ln(1/ε)), which would be prohibitive for large graphs. The authors should provide more intuition about why these bounds are necessary and whether they can be improved.

3. **Experimental Scope**: The experiments use only 600 queries total, with 50% for training. This is a relatively small dataset for a real-world ranking task, and the results might not generalize to larger, more complex scenarios. The authors should expand the experimental validation.

4. **Gradient-Free Method Justification**: The paper claims the gradient-free method "avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix," but for the specific case of PageRank, the derivative calculations (Lemma 2) appear to be efficiently handled through iterative methods, not direct matrix computations. The authors should clarify why the gradient-free approach is preferable in practice.

5. **Comparison with Other Methods**: The paper compares with [21] but doesn't discuss how their methods relate to other general optimization approaches for graph-based learning (e.g., [6, 12, 20]). A more thorough comparison with related work would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The two-level optimization framework with inexact oracles is well-motivated and theoretically sound, and the experimental results demonstrate practical improvements over the state-of-the-art.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in its theoretical justification (particularly regarding the convexity assumption) and the practical relevance of the complexity bounds. The experimental validation is limited in scope, and the justification for preferring the gradient-free method over the gradient-based approach is not fully convincing.

The paper is technically sound and addresses an important problem in graph-based learning, but the theoretical claims could be strengthened, and the experimental results would benefit from broader validation.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** (or **Borderline Accept**)

The paper makes a genuine contribution to the field by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, which is a significant advancement over the state-of-the-art. The theoretical framework for constrained optimization with inexact oracles is also valuable. However, the theoretical claims require more careful justification, particularly regarding the convexity assumption, and the experimental validation should be expanded to more realistic scenarios.

The authors should address the concerns about the convexity assumption and complexity bounds, and provide a more thorough comparison with other optimization approaches for graph-based learning. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
