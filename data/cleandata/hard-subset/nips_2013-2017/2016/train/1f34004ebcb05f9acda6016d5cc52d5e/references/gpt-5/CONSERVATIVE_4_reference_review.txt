Summary
The paper studies learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: (i) a gradient-based scheme with an inexact first-order oracle and (ii) a random gradient-free scheme with an inexact zero-order oracle. At the lower level, both methods approximate PageRank stationary distributions (and, for the gradient method, their derivatives) using a linearly convergent iterative method attributed to Nesterov–Nemirovski, and the paper provides error/complexity bounds for function and gradient approximations (Lemmas 1–2). At the upper level, the paper develops (a) an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact oracle (Theorem 3) and (b) a constrained random gradient-free method with inexact zero-order oracle (Theorem 1), each with complexity guarantees that account for lower-level approximation accuracy. Experiments on a Yandex browsing graph dataset (600 queries, ~1.7K labeled pairs) compare against a prior gradient-based approach [21]; the authors report improvements in the training loss.

Relation to prior work
The formulation builds on Supervised PageRank [21] and related supervised random walk models. The lower-level estimation uses a known linearly convergent scheme for Markov chains [17], rather than the power method. The upper-level analyses extend inexact-oracle frameworks from Nesterov and coauthors [7,16,25] to constrained settings and to the specific two-level estimation structure. The derivative computation scheme generalizes iterative methods for eigen-derivatives [20,21].

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the two-level structure with explicit upper bounds linking lower-level approximation accuracies to overall complexity.
- The inexact-oracle framing is principled and addresses a shortcoming of earlier work [21] that lacked convergence guarantees due to inexact lower-level computations.
- The use of the Nesterov–Nemirovski method for stationary distribution estimation is sensible and may be practically faster than the power method.
- The paper attempts to quantify the accuracy–cost trade-off and gives explicit iteration bounds (albeit with hidden constants) for both function and gradient approximations.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/methodological soundness:
- The gradient-free method’s theory requires convexity on the feasible set, yet the learning objective is nonconvex. The paper explicitly assumes “a small vicinity of a local minimum where f is convex” but does not verify or diagnose this in practice. This is a strong, untested assumption and undermines the relevance of Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 to the actual problem.
- For the nonconvex gradient method, Theorem 3 ensures a stationarity measure (‖M_K(x_K−x_{K+1})‖^2), but the practical setup relies on an inexact oracle whose constants (e.g., L) and error tolerances are set heuristically (L0=L=1e−4; ε=1e−6) without sensitivity or calibration against true Lipschitz behavior. The adaptive doubling scheme mitigates this, but the gap between theory and the used hyperparameters is not bridged.
- Critical constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement; the main text does not make clear how large they are or how they impact practical complexity. This limits interpretability and reproducibility.
- The derivative approximation (Equations 3.2–3.3) appears plausible but stability/variance and computational cost relative to automatic differentiation or implicit differentiation are not empirically assessed. The claim that the gradient-free upper level avoids “derivative for each element of a large matrix” is true but the overall cost might still be high; wall-clock trade-offs are not reported.

Empirical validation:
- Evaluation focuses on the training loss (Equation 2.4). Standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP, ERR) are not reported, despite the ranking context and availability of graded labels. Without these, it is hard to assess practical impact.
- The dataset is relatively small (~1.7K labeled pairs over 600 queries). There is no test on larger graphs, large label sets, or web-scale scenarios where the proposed complexity benefits would matter most.
- Baselines are limited. The “state-of-the-art” GBP is described with fixed inner power iterations and specific step sizes, but there is no evidence that it was properly tuned or that stronger baselines (e.g., Supervised Random Walks [Backstrom & Leskovec], other learning-to-rank methods such as LambdaMART or GBDT on the same features) were considered. The absence of comparisons to standard learning-to-rank undermines claims of practical significance.
- No ablations on the lower-level accuracy trade-off (N, N1, N2 vs. quality/time) or sensitivity to α, R, or the choice of Φ. Time/memory and scalability are not reported; “significant” improvements are based on loss values, not end-to-end runtime.

Clarity:
- The manuscript has numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., Rm++1, scale-invariance discussion vs. choice of Euclidean ball Φ rather than a simplex; inconsistent use of norms and dimensions). Key constants and algorithmic details are relegated to the supplement.
- The problem setup could better motivate the choice of constraint set Φ and discuss identifiability given the scale-invariance of probabilities.

Originality:
- The lower-level method and the inexact-oracle frameworks are established. The main novelty lies in adapting these to the constrained setting and tying oracle accuracy to overall complexity in this specific PageRank learning context. While useful, the innovations read as incremental extensions rather than conceptually new algorithms.

Significance:
- The theoretical framing is neat, but without strong empirical validation on standard ranking metrics and competitive baselines, it is difficult to judge impact for practitioners. The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method further limits applicability.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide results on NDCG/MAP/ERR and wall-clock time vs. quality trade-offs? How do methods scale with |V|, |E|?
- How was L chosen; did the adaptive scheme backtrack far from L0 in practice?
- Can you justify or empirically verify the local convexity assumption on Φ?
- How large is β1 in practice, and how sensitive are N1, N2 to problem parameters?
- Why constrain ϕ to a ball near the all-ones vector rather than a simplex, given scale invariance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add standard ranking metrics, runtime, and scalability experiments; include stronger baselines.
- Provide ablations on lower-level accuracy, ε, and α.
- Clarify constants and algorithmic details in the main text; improve writing and notation.
- Either validate the local convexity assumption or avoid relying on it for claims about the gradient-free method’s guarantees on the actual task.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The theoretical contributions are incremental adaptations of established inexact-oracle methods, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient to substantiate the claimed practical gains, especially given unverified assumptions (local convexity) and the lack of standard ranking metrics and robust baselines. With stronger experiments and clearer exposition, this work could become more compelling.
