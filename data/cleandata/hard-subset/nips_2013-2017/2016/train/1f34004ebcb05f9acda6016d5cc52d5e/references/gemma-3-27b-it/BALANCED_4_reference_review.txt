This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, with theoretical convergence guarantees and experimental validation. The core idea is to improve upon existing optimization methods [21] by utilizing a more accurate approximation of the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, achieved through a linearly convergent method [17]. The paper also provides complexity analysis and demonstrates improved ranking quality in experiments.

The paper addresses an important problem – efficiently learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a generalization of the classic PageRank algorithm that incorporates node and edge features. The motivation for improving upon existing methods is clear, as the original approach lacked convergence guarantees. The authors correctly identify the key bottleneck as accurately estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives. The proposed methods, leveraging [17] for lower-level approximation, are a reasonable approach to address this challenge. The theoretical contributions – convergence rate estimates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, and the trade-off analysis between accuracy and complexity – are valuable. The experimental results demonstrating improved ranking quality are a positive sign.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides convergence rate analysis for both proposed methods, a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art [21]. This is crucial for understanding the behavior and reliability of the algorithms.
*   **Well-Motivated Problem:**  Supervised PageRank is a relevant and widely studied topic, and the need for more efficient and theoretically sound optimization methods is justified.
*   **Sound Methodology:** Utilizing method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives appears to be a sensible approach, and the authors provide supporting analysis for its efficiency.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper includes complexity analysis, a trade-off study between accuracy and computational cost, and experimental validation.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to the baseline, suggesting practical benefits.
*   **Clear Structure:** The paper is reasonably well-organized, with clear sections and a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** While logically structured, the paper is dense and the writing style can be challenging.  There are numerous technical details presented without sufficient context or intuition.  The equations are often presented without clear explanations of the variables or their significance.  For example, the explanations of Lemma 1 and 2 are terse and require considerable effort to follow.
*   **Lack of Comparison with Alternatives:** The comparison is mainly against [21]. While important, it would be beneficial to compare the performance against more recent and relevant ranking algorithms or optimization methods, especially those employing stochastic gradient descent or other modern techniques.  Are there other methods that achieve similar ranking quality with better efficiency?
*   **Experimental Setup Details:** While the experimental setup is described, there's a lack of detail regarding hyperparameter tuning for the competing methods. The authors mention trying different step sizes for GBP, but don’t provide sufficient information to replicate the results or understand the sensitivity of the performance to these parameters. The choice of L in GFN seems rather arbitrary – a more principled justification or sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
*   **Limited Novelty in Gradient-Free Method:** The adaptation of [16] to constrained optimization is stated as a contribution, but the extension appears relatively straightforward. More emphasis on the specific challenges and solutions encountered during this adaptation would strengthen this contribution.
*    **Tight Coupling with Specific Method [17]:** The paper heavily relies on the properties of the method [17]. If that method has limitations or if a better approximation algorithm becomes available, the entire approach would need to be revisited. A discussion of the robustness to alternative approximation techniques would be beneficial.
*   **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are performed on a specific web browsing dataset from Yandex.  The generalizability of the results to other ranking tasks or graph structures is unclear.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contributions with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated improvement in ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in ranking and graph analysis.
*   Comprehensive analysis of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity and readability issues hinder understanding and reproducibility.
*   Limited comparison to alternative methods.
*   Lack of detail in the experimental setup.
*   Potential limitations due to reliance on a specific approximation algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid technical contribution with valuable theoretical insights and promising experimental results. However, the lack of clarity and the limited comparison to alternative methods are significant weaknesses. While the theoretical guarantees and performance improvements are commendable, the paper would benefit from substantial revisions to improve its readability, provide a more thorough experimental evaluation, and address the concerns regarding generalizability.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Major Revisions.**  I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but strongly encourage the authors to revise and resubmit.  The core ideas are promising, but the paper needs to be significantly more accessible and thoroughly validated to meet the standards for NIPS publication. Specifically, I recommend:

1.  **Improve Clarity:**  Rewrite sections to be more intuitive and accessible, adding more explanations and context.
2.  **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Compare against a wider range of ranking algorithms and optimization methods. Provide a more detailed description of hyperparameter tuning.
3.  **Address Limitations:** Discuss the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research.
4.  **Provide More Insight:** Explain the choices made and the underlying intuition, particularly regarding the parameters used and the justifications for the design decisions.
5.  **Simplify Equations:** Break down complex equations into smaller steps and explain each term.
