Summary
The paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, where edge and node weights are linear in features with parameters ϕ, and ranking scores are the stationary distribution πq(ϕ) of a query-dependent Markov chain. The objective is a squared hinge loss over pairwise order constraints f(ϕ) = (1/|Q|)∑q‖(Aqπq(ϕ))+‖2. The authors observe that prior work [21] uses a two-level gradient-based method with power iterations to approximate π and its derivatives, but without convergence guarantees due to inexactness. They propose two two-level methods:

- Gradient-free (upper level): a constrained random search method with an inexact zero-order oracle, extending Nesterov–Spokoiny [16] to constrained optimization and to uniformly bounded oracle error. They analyze convergence in the convex case and derive complexity when the lower level supplies f̃(ϕ,δ) with accuracy δ via an accelerated PageRank iteration [17].

- Gradient-based (upper level): an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle, generalizing [7] to constrained problems and inexact oracles following the (δ,L)-oracle idea [25]. They provide a bound on a stationarity measure and end-to-end complexity when the lower level supplies both f̃ and g̃ approximations.

On the lower level, they use the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method [17] to approximate πq(ϕ) and extend it to compute derivatives dπq/dϕ via a similar recursion. Lemmas 1–2 bound the errors |f̃−f| and ‖g̃−∇f‖∞ and the arithmetic cost in terms of graph sizes and desired accuracy.

Experiments on Yandex user browsing graphs (600 queries) compare the proposed gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods to the prior gradient-based method (GBP) from [21]. They report lower final loss values and paired t-test p-values < 0.005 in several settings. They also note faster convergence of the lower-level PageRank method versus the power method.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The upper-level algorithms and analyses build on established smoothing/finite-difference ideas [16] and adaptive projected gradient with inexact oracles [25], extended to constraints and nonconvexity. The gradient mapping stopping criterion for nonconvex problems is standard. The lower-level approximation bounds are plausible: the π approximation uses a truncated geometric series with restart and yields an O((1/α) log(1/δ)) dependence; the derivative recursion mimics the stationary equation and is bounded in ∞-norm. The combined complexity theorems (Theorems 2 and 4) follow by composing oracle accuracies with algorithmic parameters.

- Assumptions: The gradient-free convergence requires convexity of f on Φ and x* in int Φ; the nonconvex gradient method assumes existence of an inexact first-order oracle satisfying inequality (5.1) with unknown L, which the paper constructs via Lemmas 1–2. However, the local convexity assumption “Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ* in which f is convex” is strong and unverified; f is generally nonconvex because π(ϕ) depends nonlinearly on ϕ. The interior-point assumption x* ∈ int Φ is also nontrivial in practice. Constants such as β1 in Lemma 2 are only defined in the supplement; their size could materially affect N1,N2.

- Empirics: The experiments demonstrate improvements in the training/test loss metric f, but do not report standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP), runtime, or memory use. Baselines are limited to [21]; broader learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM) are not compared. The choice of α and the exact lower-level termination parameters are not fully specified. Statistical tests are mentioned but effect sizes are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized: modeling in Section 2, lower-level approximations in Section 3, upper-level methods in Sections 4–5, and experiments in Section 6. However, there are numerous typos and minor grammatical issues (e.g., “concider”, “probablities”), and some notation could be streamlined. Important constants (β1) and proofs are deferred to the supplement; a brief intuition in the main text would help. The role of convexity versus nonconvexity is occasionally unclear: Section 4 assumes convexity of f on Φ, while Section 5 targets nonconvexity—clarifying when each upper-level method should be used in practice would improve readability. Reporting the chosen α and explicitly stating whether f is evaluated on held-out queries would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Extending random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with uniformly bounded inexact zero-order oracles appears novel relative to [16]. The adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles generalizes [7,25] to this setting.
- The derivative computation for π(ϕ) via a Nesterov–Nemirovski-style recursion is a tailored contribution. Providing end-to-end complexity for learning Supervised PageRank with inexact oracles strengthens prior work [21].
- The conceptual components are known, but the integration and convergence guarantees for this specific application are nontrivial.

Significance
Theoretical contributions provide convergence guarantees and complexity for a practically relevant class of ranking problems with inexact oracles. However, empirical significance is limited by the scope of evaluation: nonstandard metrics (only f), limited baselines, and missing runtime analyses. Broader impact beyond supervised PageRank is plausible (general constrained inexact-oracle optimization), but the paper does not demonstrate applications beyond this domain.

Pros
- Clear two-level formulation with rigorous inexact-oracle analysis.
- Convergence guarantees for both gradient-free (convex) and gradient-based (nonconvex) settings.
- Lower-level method with linear convergence and derivative computation; explicit accuracy/complexity trade-offs.
- Empirical improvement over [21] on a real dataset.

Cons
- Gradient-free method requires convexity on Φ; the paper does not establish convexity or conditions under which it holds.
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics, runtime, and broader baselines.
- Some assumptions (x* in int Φ) and constants (β1) are opaque; practical sensitivity (e.g., to L, ε) is not fully explored.
- Presentation issues: typos, missing definitions in the main text, and unclear hyperparameter choices (e.g., α).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide conditions or evidence for convexity (or avoid claiming convex guarantees for this application).
- Report standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP), wall-clock times, and memory; add comparisons to standard learning-to-rank baselines.
- Include a brief description and bounds for β1 in the main text and discuss its practical magnitude.
- Clarify the selection of α and stopping criteria for lower-level iterations; add ablations on δ, N, N1, N2.
- Improve writing and fix typographical errors; tighten notation and restate key assumptions prominently.

Overall, the paper offers solid theoretical advances for inexact-oracle optimization applied to supervised PageRank and shows promising empirical results against a specific prior method. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying assumptions would substantially improve the contribution.
