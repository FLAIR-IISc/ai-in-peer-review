Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features. It proposes two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle. The lower level in both cases estimates the stationary distribution and (for the gradient-based approach) its derivatives using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method, rather than the power method used in prior work. The upper level uses newly analyzed optimization procedures that account for inexact oracle information: an adaptive projected gradient scheme for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with unknown Lipschitz constant and an inexact oracle; and a constrained, biased random gradient-free method on the sphere with inexact function values. The authors prove convergence rates for both and derive complexity bounds that balance lower-level accuracy and overall runtime. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset indicate improvements over a state-of-the-art Supervised PageRank learner [21] in terms of the training loss used in this paper.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: unifies inexact-oracle view with learning PageRank parameters, giving end-to-end convergence guarantees where the classic approach lacked them.
- Methodological novelty: extensions of random gradient-free optimization to constrained domains with bounded, unknown errors in function values; and an adaptive projected gradient scheme for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracle and unknown smoothness. These are natural but nontrivial generalizations of prior theory.
- Lower-level improvement: replacing the power method with the Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration for both stationary distribution and its derivatives is sensible; the linear convergence and complexity accounting are valuable for building complexity guarantees for the full procedure.
- Practical angle: the gradient-free upper-level avoids derivative computations over large matrices, which can be advantageous in high dimensions or when derivative computation is burdensome.
- Empirical indication: on the reported dataset, both proposed methods outperform the prior Supervised PageRank learner, sometimes by a noticeable margin in the chosen loss.

Weaknesses
- Novelty vs. prior theory: much of the machinery builds closely on Nesterov–Spokoiny (zero-order), Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov (inexact oracles), and Ghadimi–Lan (nonconvex first-/zeroth-order). The constrained/inexact adaptations are useful but feel incremental rather than a bold new direction. The lower-level switch from power to Nesterov–Nemirovski is also a known alternative; the novelty lies mostly in assembling these components for this application and tightening complexity accounting.
- Empirical evaluation: results are reported primarily in terms of the square hinge loss used for training. For a ranking task, standard IR metrics (NDCG, MAP, ERR) on held-out queries are expected. Without them, it’s hard to assess real ranking impact. Baselines are limited; comparison to Supervised Random Walks (Backstrom & Leskovec 2011) or modern graph-aware LTR baselines is missing.
- Assumptions and scope: the gradient-free analysis in Section 4.2 assumes local convexity of f over Φ, yet the problem is nonconvex in general; the paper does not verify that this holds on the instances used, nor discuss when it might fail. The positivity/nonzero-denominator constraints for restart/transition parameters are handled by a ball inside the positive orthant, but practical detection and handling of degeneracy are not fully addressed.
- Clarity and presentation: there are many typos, broken sentences, and incomplete references (authors/years/venues missing). Several key definitions (e.g., the constant β1) and algorithms are deferred to the supplement; more intuition and a compact self-contained statement would help. The paper would benefit from a clearer separation of what is new vs. what is adapted from prior work.
- Experimental scale and fairness: the dataset is relatively small; the construction of the GBP baseline (e.g., raising the stochastic matrix to fixed powers) may not reflect its best tuning; wall-clock timings and memory usage are not reported, making the practical advantages of the lower-level method hard to gauge.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Report NDCG/MAP/ERR on held-out queries in addition to training loss, and provide statistical significance. Show per-query or per-bucket results to assess robustness.
- Compare against Supervised Random Walks (Backstrom & Leskovec 2011) and at least one strong learning-to-rank baseline on the same features.
- Provide wall-clock timing, number of matrix–vector multiplies, and sensitivity to α and to the ball radius R. Include ablations isolating the impact of the NN method vs. the power method while keeping the upper level fixed.
- Clarify the local convexity assumption in Section 4.2: when does it hold, how is Φ chosen in practice, and what happens if f is nonconvex throughout?
- Make β1 explicit in the main text (not only in the supplement), and give a brief derivation or intuition about its magnitude and dependence on graph parameters.
- Clean up references (complete author lists, years, venues) and fix the typographical issues.
- Discuss scalability: how do the bounds and actual performance scale with |Q|, graph size (p, s), and feature dimension m? Can the lower level be parallelized across queries?

Pros for acceptance
- Provides unified, principled convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank with inexact oracles.
- Extends known optimization frameworks in a practically relevant way (constraints + inexactness), which others may reuse beyond this application.
- Shows empirical improvements over a prior Supervised PageRank learner.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical contributions, while sound, are incremental extensions of well-known frameworks.
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics and broader baselines; scale is modest.
- Presentation issues and reliance on supplemental details hinder accessibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. I appreciate the direction—bringing inexact-oracle optimization rigor to supervised PageRank is worthwhile—and the constrained/inexact extensions may interest the community. However, the novelty is moderate, and the experimental validation (especially the absence of standard ranking metrics and broader baselines) makes it hard to judge practical impact. With stronger empirical evaluation, clearer exposition, and a sharper positioning of what is fundamentally new, this work could be competitive.
