### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes two two-level optimization algorithms for the Supervised PageRank problem, which aims to learn parameters for a personalized PageRank model by minimizing a loss function over relevance-judged data. The key challenge, as identified by the authors, is that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to its reliance on the power method to inexactly compute the stationary distribution (the PageRank vector) and its derivatives.

To address this, the authors propose two new methods—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—that are designed to work with inexact oracles. At the lower level, both methods use the linearly convergent algorithm from [17] to compute the stationary distribution and a novel generalization of it to compute its derivatives, allowing the approximation error to be controlled. At the upper level, the authors develop:
1.  A random gradient-free method, extending the work of [16] to handle constrained optimization problems and a known bound on the function approximation error.
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex constrained problems, extending the framework of [7] to accommodate an inexact first-order oracle.

The paper provides convergence rate analyses and complexity bounds for both algorithms. Experimental results on a real-world web ranking task suggest that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach [21].

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The primary contribution of this paper is placing the optimization of Supervised PageRank on firm theoretical ground. By providing algorithms with explicit convergence guarantees and complexity analyses, the work addresses a significant gap in the prior art [21].
2.  **Generalizable Optimization Frameworks:** Beyond the specific application to PageRank, the paper develops general optimization frameworks for constrained problems with inexact oracles. The adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems (Section 5.1) and the gradient-free method for convex problems (Section 4.1) are potentially of broader interest to the optimization community.
3.  **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall computational complexity of the two-level algorithms. This is a crucial aspect of designing practical algorithms for such problems.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The theoretical contributions are supported by experiments on a real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, showing that the proposed methods not only converge in theory but also achieve better performance in practice.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its strengths, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological details, and clarity, which collectively detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of attention to detail.
    *   The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It is neither alphabetized nor sorted by order of appearance. Many references are incomplete or improperly formatted (e.g., [1], [4], [9], [11]), making it difficult for a reader to locate the cited works. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   There are numerous typographical and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript (e.g., "concider" in Sec 2, "probablities" in Sec 3, the broken sentence at the end of the second paragraph in the Introduction, the superscript "2" after "Yandex" in Sec 6.1). These errors hinder readability.

2.  **Methodological Ambiguity and Strong Assumptions:**
    *   The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method (Section 4.2) is a major limitation. The paper assumes that the objective function f(ϕ) is convex within a small vicinity Φ of a local minimum. This is a very strong assumption for a generally non-convex problem. The paper offers no practical guidance on how to choose Φ to satisfy this condition, which undermines the applicability of Theorem 2.
    *   The output of Algorithm 1 is specified as `yM = arg minx{f(x) : x ∈ {x0, . . . , xM}}`. Since the exact function `f(x)` is intractable, this step is not feasible. The authors likely mean to use the inexact approximation `f̃(x, δ)`, but this is not specified. This ambiguity is a critical flaw in the algorithm's description.
    *   The connection between the separate error bounds for `f̃` and `g̃` and the combined inexact oracle definition in (5.1) is asserted by citing [25] but not explicitly derived. For full technical soundness, this crucial step should be shown in the paper.
    *   The stopping criterion for the baseline method GBP is stated as "the differences between the values of the loss function on the next step and the current step are less than −10−5". A negative difference is a highly unusual criterion; one assumes the authors meant the improvement is less than 10⁻⁵, but this lack of clarity makes the experimental comparison less rigorous.

3.  **Clarity:**
    *   The notation is quite dense. While necessary for the analysis, a table or a more careful introduction of symbols could improve readability.
    *   The description of the derivative estimation method (Eqs. 3.2, 3.3) is presented without much intuition. Calling it a "generalization of the method [17]" is vague. A clearer explanation of how this iterative scheme approximates the derivative matrix would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper successfully tackles an important open problem: providing convergent optimization algorithms for the Supervised PageRank model.
*   The work contains solid theoretical contributions in the form of convergence proofs and complexity analyses for optimization with inexact oracles.
*   The empirical results are positive and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The manuscript is poorly prepared, with a deeply flawed reference section and numerous typos, suggesting a rushed submission that does not meet the high presentation standards of NIPS.
*   The practical applicability of the gradient-free method is questionable due to the strong and unjustified local convexity assumption.
*   Several key methodological details are ambiguous or underspecified (e.g., the output step of Algorithm 1, the baseline stopping criterion), which compromises reproducibility and the rigor of the claims.

**Recommendation:**

Reject.

While the core ideas of this paper are valuable and address an important problem, the execution is unfortunately not up to the standard required for NIPS. The sheer number of presentation flaws, particularly the unacceptable state of the bibliography, is a major red flag. More importantly, the unaddressed strong assumption of local convexity for the gradient-free method and the ambiguities in algorithmic descriptions represent significant technical weaknesses. The paper has strong potential, but it requires a major revision to fix these numerous issues before it can be considered for publication.
