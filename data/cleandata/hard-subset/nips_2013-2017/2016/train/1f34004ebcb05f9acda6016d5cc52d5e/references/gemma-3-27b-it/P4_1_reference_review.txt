This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, one gradient-based and one gradient-free. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for these methods, which were previously lacking in the state-of-the-art approach [21], and demonstrating improved ranking quality in experiments. The paper builds on existing work in power methods for estimating stationary distributions and adapts optimization techniques to handle the constraints and inexactness of the problem.

The paper begins by clearly outlining the Supervised PageRank framework and the challenges associated with optimizing the model parameters. It correctly identifies the lack of convergence proofs for existing methods and the difficulty in applying standard optimization techniques due to constraints and imprecise oracle information. The proposed methods aim to address these issues. The description of the model, including the random walk process and the loss function, is reasonably clear and follows standard notation. The authors motivate their work with references to relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The main strength of the paper is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses a key limitation of previous work in this area.
*   **Novelty:** Adapting and proving convergence for gradient-free methods in a constrained optimization setting with an inexact oracle is novel. The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity adds further value.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach on a real-world web page ranking task. The use of a standard dataset (Yandex) and comparison to a baseline (Supervised PageRank) strengthens the validity of the results.
*   **Detailed Analysis:** The paper provides detailed analysis of the methods, including lemmas and theorems establishing bounds on approximation errors and convergence rates.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The authors clearly define the problem and its challenges, providing a good foundation for their proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** While the technical details are present, the presentation is dense and can be difficult to follow. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while rigorous, interrupt the flow of the main ideas. More intuitive explanations and high-level summaries could improve readability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not fully address the sensitivity of the algorithms to hyperparameter choices (e.g., the smoothing parameter τ, the Lipschitz constant L). While the authors explore some values, a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The stated theoretical complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) are quite complex and potentially difficult to interpret practically. While the analysis is mathematically sound, a more accessible discussion of the practical implications of these bounds would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus solely on web page ranking. It would be interesting to see how the proposed methods perform on other graph ranking tasks or datasets.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper lacks detailed discussion of recent advances in gradient-free optimization and inexact oracle methods beyond the cited references. For example, the work on bandit-based optimization could be relevant.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of Supervised PageRank: the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   The proposed methods are novel and offer a substantial improvement over existing approaches.
*   The experimental results provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The rigorous mathematical analysis adds credibility to the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The dense and technical presentation may limit the accessibility of the paper to a wider audience.
*   The complexity of the theoretical results and limited scope of the experiments could raise concerns about the practical applicability of the methods.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and computational complexity are not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Supervised PageRank. The theoretical guarantees and experimental results are compelling. However, the clarity of presentation and the lack of detailed analysis of practical considerations (hyperparameter sensitivity, computational complexity) are weaknesses that should be addressed. Despite these concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve readability and provide a more practical discussion of the results. Specifically, the authors should consider providing high-level summaries of the key lemmas and theorems and discussing the practical implications of the complexity bounds. A more detailed sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters would also be beneficial.
