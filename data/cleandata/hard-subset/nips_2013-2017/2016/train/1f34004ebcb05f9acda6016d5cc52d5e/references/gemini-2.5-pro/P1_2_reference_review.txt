This paper presents two novel two-level optimization algorithms for learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a problem introduced by Zhukovskii et al. [21]. The core challenge in this problem is that the objective function (a pairwise ranking loss) and its gradient cannot be computed exactly, as they depend on the stationary distribution of a parameterized Markov process. The state-of-the-art method [21] uses power iterations to approximate these quantities but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees.

The main contributions of this work are two-fold. First, the authors propose using a more efficient, linearly convergent method [17] for the lower-level task of approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, providing explicit bounds on the number of iterations needed to achieve a desired accuracy (`δ`). Second, they develop two upper-level optimization frameworks that can provably converge despite this inexactness:
1.  A random gradient-free method, extending the work of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is computed with a known error bound.
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex constrained problems, extending the framework of Devolder et al. [25], that can handle an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates and overall computational complexity bounds that explicitly link the desired final accuracy (`ε`) to the required precision of the lower-level oracle. These theoretical contributions are then validated through experiments on a real-world web page ranking task, demonstrating that the proposed algorithms outperform the previous state-of-the-art method.

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions in Sections 4.1 and 5.1 are significant and well-supported by proofs (provided in the supplementary materials). The authors correctly identify the key limitation of prior work—the lack of convergence guarantees due to the use of an inexact oracle—and systematically build a solution. The analysis connecting the upper-level optimization accuracy (`ε`) with the lower-level oracle precision (`δ`) and the complexity of achieving that precision (Lemmas 1 and 2) is particularly strong.

The experimental evaluation is solid and directly supports the paper's claims. By comparing their gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) methods against the previous method (GBP) on a real-world dataset, they demonstrate a clear improvement in terms of the final loss function value. The authors are careful in their experimental setup, describing hyperparameter choices and the data partitioning scheme.

A potential weakness in the evaluation is the sole reliance on the loss function as the performance metric. While minimizing the loss is the direct goal of the optimization, it would have been more compelling to also show how this translates to improvements in standard ranking metrics like NDCG or MAP on the test set. This would more directly demonstrate the practical impact on search quality.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though mathematically dense. It does a good job of separating the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2). This structure makes the paper's contributions clear and highlights the broader applicability of the proposed optimization methods. The notation is consistent and the problem is formally well-defined.

For improved clarity, the introduction could more explicitly state that the two main contributions are new general optimization frameworks for inexact oracles, which are then *applied* to Supervised PageRank. The current text slightly blurs this distinction. Additionally, providing a brief intuition in the main text as to why the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] is superior to the power method (beyond just citing its complexity bound) would be helpful for readers not deeply familiar with that work.

### Originality
The work is original and makes a novel contribution to the field. While it builds upon several existing lines of work (Supervised PageRank [21], gradient-free methods [16], and optimization with inexact oracles [7, 25]), its novelty lies in the synthesis and extension of these ideas. Specifically:
1.  Adapting gradient-free methods to the *constrained* setting with an inexact zero-order oracle is a new contribution.
2.  Developing an adaptive gradient method for *non-convex, constrained* problems with an inexact first-order oracle is a valuable extension of existing work.
3.  This is the first work to provide a provably convergent algorithm for the Supervised PageRank learning problem.
4.  The rigorous complexity analysis that ties the two optimization levels together is novel and insightful.

The paper correctly positions itself relative to prior work and provides adequate citations.

### Significance
The results presented are significant for both researchers and practitioners. For researchers in optimization, the paper provides two general and provably convergent frameworks for dealing with constrained optimization problems where the objective and/or its gradient can only be approximated. This setting is common in large-scale machine learning, particularly in problems involving equilibrium states or solutions to large linear systems.

For practitioners in information retrieval and graph mining, the paper provides a reliable and more effective method for training a powerful feature-based ranking model (Supervised PageRank). The fact that the algorithms come with convergence guarantees increases their reliability. The experimental results suggest that these methods can lead to better-tuned models, potentially improving search engine performance. The work advances the state of the art in learning-to-rank on graphs.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank problem, filling a critical gap in the literature.
2.  **Generalizable Frameworks:** The core contributions are two general optimization methods for constrained problems with inexact oracles, which are significant beyond the specific application.
3.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper includes a thorough complexity analysis that links the accuracy of the oracle to the overall convergence rate.
4.  **Positive Experimental Results:** The proposed methods are shown to outperform the existing state-of-the-art on a real-world task.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
1.  **Limited Experimental Metrics:** The evaluation relies solely on the optimization loss function, without reporting standard information retrieval metrics (e.g., NDCG), which would better quantify the practical impact.
2.  **Local Convexity Assumption:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption, which may not hold in general for the non-convex Supervised PageRank loss function. (To the authors' credit, they are transparent about this assumption).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The strengths, particularly the novel convergence guarantees for an important problem, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
