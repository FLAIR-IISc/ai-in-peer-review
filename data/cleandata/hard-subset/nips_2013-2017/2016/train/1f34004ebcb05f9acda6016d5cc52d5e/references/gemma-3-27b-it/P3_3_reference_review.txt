## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, a framework for incorporating node and edge features into the classic PageRank algorithm. The core challenge lies in the non-convexity of the loss function and the difficulty of obtaining exact solutions. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free approach, both built upon a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. Importantly, they provide convergence guarantees for both methods, something lacking in prior work [21], and demonstrate improved ranking quality in experiments.  The work focuses heavily on analyzing the trade-off between accuracy on the lower level (stationary distribution approximation) and the overall computational complexity.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], addressing its limitations regarding convergence guarantees. It acknowledges and compares itself against methods for approximating stationary distributions like the power method [18, 2, 21] and highlights the advantages of the method from [17] used as a core component.  The gradient-free optimization is positioned against techniques from [16, 7], and attempts to extend them to handle constraints and inexact oracles.  The connection to online convex optimization and bandit feedback [O, Agarwal, Dekel and L. Xiao] seems distant and isn't explicitly elaborated upon, but the inexact oracle framework is relevant. The paper also draws on literature related to iterative methods for eigensystems [Andrew, 1992; Andrew, 1994].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The most significant strength is the provision of convergence rate analysis for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a critical improvement over the existing state-of-the-art [21], which lacks such guarantees.
* **Rigorous Analysis of Lower-Level Approximation:** The authors perform a detailed analysis of the linearly convergent method [17] for estimating the stationary distribution, establishing complexity bounds and exploring the trade-off between accuracy and computation.
* **Handling of Constraints & Inexact Oracles:**  Extending existing gradient-free and gradient-based methods to accommodate constrained optimization problems and inexact oracle information is a valuable contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the existing state-of-the-art in a real-world web page ranking task.
* **Detailed Complexity Analysis:** The work provides a fairly comprehensive assessment of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-structured, with a clear presentation of the model, the proposed methods, and the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notationally Dense:** The paper is extremely dense with notation. While necessary to some extent for a theoretical paper, it often hinders readability and makes it difficult to grasp the key ideas quickly.  More intuitive notation would be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the complexity analysis is thorough, there's limited discussion about practical considerations for parameter tuning (e.g., how to choose appropriate values for smoothing parameters or step sizes). The experiments offer some insights, but more guidance would be helpful.
* **Experimental Setup Could Be More Comprehensive:** The experimental section is a bit limited. Comparing against a wider range of baseline ranking algorithms (beyond just [21]) would strengthen the results. A more thorough ablation study, examining the impact of different hyperparameters, would also be beneficial. It's also unclear why they limit the radius R to 0.99 instead of 1.
* **Connection to Broader Optimization Literature:** While they cite relevant work, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how their inexact oracle framework relates to broader trends in optimization, such as variance reduction techniques.
* **Lack of Intuition for Algorithm Choices**: The rationale for choosing specific algorithms and their parameters isn't always clear.  More motivation behind these design choices would strengthen the presentation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides theoretical guarantees for a practically relevant problem (learning Supervised PageRank) where such guarantees were previously missing.
* Presents novel and well-analyzed algorithms for solving a challenging non-convex optimization problem.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art.
* Contributes to the understanding of trade-offs between accuracy and complexity in optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is very dense and could benefit from improved clarity and readability.
* The experimental evaluation, while positive, could be more comprehensive.
* The connection to broader optimization literature could be more explicitly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of ranking and optimization. The provision of convergence guarantees is a significant achievement, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. While the paper suffers from some density and could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially expand the experimental evaluation).  I recommend the authors prioritize improving readability and providing more practical guidance for using their algorithms.
