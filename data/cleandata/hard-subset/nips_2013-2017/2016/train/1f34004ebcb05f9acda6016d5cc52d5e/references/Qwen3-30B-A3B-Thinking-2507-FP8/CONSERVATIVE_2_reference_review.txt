

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two-level optimization methods for learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a ranking model based on random walks on graphs. The authors claim their gradient-based and gradient-free methods provide theoretical convergence guarantees and outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in experiments. 

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, where node and edge features are used to weight the random walk. The authors develop two-level optimization approaches: on the lower level, they approximate the stationary distribution using a method from [17], and on the upper level, they apply gradient-based or gradient-free optimization. The paper claims theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, which are novel for this specific problem, and reports experimental improvements over [21].

This work builds on the Supervised PageRank framework [21] and general optimization techniques [7, 16]. However, it does not clearly distinguish between the novel contributions (application of existing optimization techniques to this specific problem) and the standard adaptations of those techniques.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides theoretically sound convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithms, which is a strength.
2. The mathematical derivations are technically correct and well-organized.
3. The paper demonstrates a clear connection between the lower-level approximation methods and the upper-level optimization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Experimental Validation**: The experiments are conducted on a single, relatively small dataset (600 queries) from Yandex. This is inadequate for a ranking task, as large-scale validation is crucial for such methods. The paper fails to demonstrate scalability to larger datasets or real-world search engine applications.

2. **Unfair Comparison**: The comparison with the state-of-the-art [21] is unfair because [21] uses the power method for stationary distribution approximation, while the authors use a different method [17]. The authors do not compare their methods with the same lower-level approximation method, making it impossible to isolate the effect of the upper-level optimization.

3. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims to be the "first time" when gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with "efficient method for huge-scale optimization," but this is an overstatement. Many papers have combined optimization techniques with graph problems, and the authors don't clearly distinguish their contribution from standard applications of existing methods.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The paper claims "GFN significantly outperforms" the state-of-the-art but doesn't provide sufficient statistical evidence. The p-values mentioned (less than 0.005) are not reported in the main text, and the paper doesn't quantify the magnitude of the improvement.

5. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The claim that "these algorithms can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine" is unsupported by any large-scale experiments or production data.

6. **Missing Context**: The paper references [5] as containing "the lowest proved complexity bound" but doesn't properly cite or discuss [5], which undermines the novelty claim.

7. **Inadequate Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach, particularly the fact that Supervised PageRank is inherently non-convex, and their convergence guarantees only apply to finding stationary points, not global minima.

## Quality and Significance

The paper is technically sound in its mathematical derivations, but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of superiority. The improvements reported are marginal (the paper doesn't quantify them), and the experimental setup doesn't convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed methods for real-world ranking applications.

The significance of the work is limited because the paper doesn't address how the proposed methods would be used in large-scale search engines, which is the primary application context for ranking algorithms. The claimed advantages (e.g., avoiding derivative calculations) are not supported by evidence that this is a meaningful bottleneck in practice.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically correct but incremental contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank. The proposed methods are standard adaptations of existing optimization techniques to a specific problem, without sufficient evidence of superiority over established approaches. The experimental validation is inadequate for the claims made, and the paper overstates the novelty and significance of the work.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be strengthened by: 1) more comprehensive experiments on larger datasets, 2) a fair comparison with the state-of-the-art using identical lower-level approximation methods, 3) a clearer distinction between novel contributions and standard applications of existing techniques, and 4) more detailed statistical analysis of the reported improvements.

The work is interesting but does not meet the high standards of NIPS for methodological novelty, rigorous experimental validation, and significant impact on the field.
