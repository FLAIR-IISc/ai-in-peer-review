Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. Prior work [21] optimized a nonconvex loss using a two-level procedure: on the lower level, the stationary distribution and its derivatives were approximated via power methods; on the upper level, gradient descent updated parameters—but without convergence guarantees due to inexactness of the lower-level computations.

This paper proposes two alternative two-level schemes with theoretical guarantees:
- A gradient-free method: extends random gradient-free optimization (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny) to constrained problems with an inexact zero-order oracle. It uses spherical randomization and shows expected error bounds depending on smoothing, diameter, and oracle accuracy.
- A gradient-based method: an adaptive projected gradient framework for composite, possibly nonconvex problems, with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. It establishes a bound on the (scaled) proximal-gradient norm, hence convergence to a stationary point.

On the lower level, both methods replace power iteration with a linearly convergent PageRank estimator [17] to approximate the stationary distribution, and they generalize it to approximate derivatives w.r.t. parameters. They quantify the accuracy/complexity trade-offs (Lemmas 1–2) and propagate these through upper-level complexity (Theorems 2 and 4). Experiments on a real (Yandex) query-dependent browsing graph compare their methods to the state-of-the-art method [21], reporting improved loss and better ranking quality.

Quality
- Theory: The upper-level frameworks are carefully developed. The gradient-free analysis (Theorem 1) adapts bandit-style smoothing to a constrained, inexact-oracle setting and provides explicit dependence on dimension, smoothing radius, and oracle error. The gradient-based analysis (Theorem 3) generalizes inexact oracle conditions (akin to Devolder et al.) to a nonconvex composite setting with an adaptive line search, returning a point with small prox-gradient norm. These are sound and useful contributions.
- Lower-level accuracy: Lemmas 1–2 provide explicit choices of N, N1, N2 for the lower-level linear-convergence estimator and its derivative generalization to guarantee prescribed accuracies on f and ∇f. The coupling of oracle accuracy with upper-level progress is clearly reasoned, and the final complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) integrate both levels.
- Assumptions: The gradient-free upper-level guarantees require convexity of f on the feasible ball Φ and x∗ ∈ int Φ. The problem is generally nonconvex; the paper assumes there exists a small vicinity where f is convex. This is a strong, unverified assumption that limits the scope of the gradient-free guarantees to local regions and weakens claims of general applicability.
- Experiments: The empirical section shows consistent improvements over the previous state-of-the-art [21], with some statistical testing. However, it is limited in several respects:
  - Evaluation focuses mostly on the training loss; standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) are not reported in the main text.
  - Baselines are narrow: no comparison to strong learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART) or to other supervised random walk variants (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec).
  - Scale: the dataset (≈600 queries, ≈11.7K vertices total, ≈7.5K edges) is relatively small for claims about “huge-scale” applicability; wall-clock times and memory are not reported to substantiate speedups.
  - Hyperparameters: the choice of L, ε, and α and their sensitivity are only lightly explored.
- Reproducibility: The algorithms are described, but several constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement, and code or detailed implementation notes are not provided in the main text.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized with a clear separation between lower- and upper-level methods. The explicit oracles and accuracy propagation are helpful.
- There are many typos, formatting issues, and incomplete references (missing years, venues), which hinder readability. Some notational switches (norms, diameters) could be clarified earlier to avoid confusion.
- Important practical details (choice of α, computation of derivatives dpi/dϕ, complexity constants) would benefit from brief sketches in the main text.

Originality
- The combination of: (i) constrained, inexact zero-order optimization with spherical randomization; (ii) adaptive projected gradient for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles; and (iii) tight coupling with a linearly convergent PageRank estimator and its derivative generalization, appears novel. The work builds on Nesterov/Nemirovski/Devolder frameworks but extends them in ways directly relevant to supervised PageRank learning with provable end-to-end guarantees.

Significance
- Theoretical significance: solid. The techniques for handling inexact oracles in constrained settings and translating lower-level accuracy into upper-level convergence rates are broadly useful beyond PageRank.
- Practical significance: promising but under-demonstrated. The current empirical evidence (single dataset, limited baselines/metrics) does not fully establish advantages in real-world, large-scale ranking pipelines.

Pros
- Clear two-level architecture with provable convergence and complexity bounds.
- Sensible and efficient lower-level estimator; derivative computation generalized with accuracy control.
- Extends inexact oracle theory to constrained zero-order and nonconvex first-order settings.
- Empirical improvements over the prior supervised PageRank training method.

Cons
- Gradient-free method relies on unverified local convexity; guarantees may not hold in general.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale, baselines, and metrics; lacks runtime reports.
- Clarity suffers from typos and incomplete references; some constants and assumptions left opaque.
- No public code or detailed implementation guidance.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you empirically verify or characterize regions where f is convex to justify applying the gradient-free method?
- Please report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k) and wall-clock runtimes for all methods.
- Include comparisons to strong learning-to-rank baselines and to supervised random walks (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec, BrowseRank variants).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for α, L, ε, and the lower-level iteration counts; and discuss robustness to these choices.
- Clarify the constant β1 and key steps of Lemmas 1–2 in the main text; fix references and typos.

Overall assessment
This paper presents meaningful theoretical advances for learning with inexact oracles and a principled application to supervised PageRank. The theory is solid and potentially impactful. However, the empirical evaluation is not yet commensurate with NeurIPS standards for significance, and the gradient-free guarantees rest on a strong local convexity assumption. With a stronger experimental section (metrics, baselines, scale, runtimes) and improved clarity, this would be a compelling contribution. As it stands, I view the work as borderline, with strong theory but insufficient empirical validation.
