### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a framework where node and edge weights in a graph are parameterized by feature vectors. The authors correctly identify a key weakness in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: its two-level optimization procedure, which uses the power method to approximate the stationary distribution and its gradients, lacks theoretical convergence guarantees.

The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem that come with formal guarantees. The first is an adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex problems, and the second is a random gradient-free method that assumes local convexity. Both methods rely on a more robust lower-level procedure [17] to compute the stationary distribution, which allows the authors to precisely bound the error of the function value (zero-order oracle) and its gradient (first-order oracle). Based on these bounded-error oracles, the paper provides convergence rate analysis and complexity bounds for both proposed algorithms. Experimental results on a real-world web ranking task show that the proposed methods outperform the previous method [21] in terms of final loss function value.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its focus on theoretical guarantees. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the Supervised PageRank learning problem in the context of optimization with an inexact oracle. By deriving convergence rates and complexity bounds, they place the optimization procedure on a much firmer theoretical footing than the prior heuristic approach in [21]. This is a valuable and necessary contribution to the topic.

2.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the deficiency in the existing literature—the lack of a provably convergent algorithm for a non-convex problem with an inexact oracle. The proposed solutions directly and effectively address this gap.

3.  **Clear Connection Between Theory and Practice:** The authors do a good job of connecting the theoretical analysis to the practical algorithm design. The trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation (controlled by N) and the overall complexity of the upper-level optimization is explicitly analyzed, which is crucial for any practical implementation of such two-level schemes.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty in Optimization Frameworks:** While the application to Supervised PageRank is novel, the core optimization algorithms themselves are extensions or adaptations of well-established methods. The gradient-free method builds upon the framework of [16], and the gradient-based method generalizes the approach of [7] to a constrained setting. The contribution lies more in the careful application and analysis within the inexact oracle setting for this specific problem, rather than in the introduction of fundamentally new optimization principles.

2.  **Strong Convexity Assumption for the Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1) requires the objective function to be convex. The authors acknowledge that the Supervised PageRank loss function is generally non-convex and justify their approach by assuming that the optimization is performed in a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function behaves as if convex. This is a significant theoretical caveat that limits the generality of the guarantees provided for this method. The analysis for the gradient-based method, which does not require convexity, is therefore more compelling.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation Against Broader Baselines:** The experiments are limited to a comparison against the single, direct predecessor method from [21]. While demonstrating an improvement over this baseline is necessary, it is not sufficient to establish the broader significance of the work. The field of learning-to-rank has evolved significantly, with methods like Gradient Boosted Decision Trees (e.g., LambdaMART) and various neural models often serving as standard, powerful baselines. Without a comparison to at least one such established ranking method, it is difficult to assess whether the Supervised PageRank model, even when optimized with a superior algorithm, is competitive in the current landscape. The focus is on *how* to optimize, but the *why* (i.e., that this model is worth optimizing in the first place) is not fully substantiated against the state of the art in the wider field.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is technically dense and at times challenging to parse. While the formalism is necessary for the theoretical results, the exposition could be improved with more intuition, particularly around the setup of the inexact oracles and the parameter choices in the final complexity bounds. The practical implications of these complex theoretical results could be stated more plainly.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   Provides the first provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem, addressing a significant theoretical gap in prior work.
*   The work is methodologically sound, presenting a rigorous analysis of optimization with an inexact oracle.
*   The empirical results, though limited in scope, demonstrate a clear and statistically significant improvement over the existing state-of-the-art method for this specific problem.

### **Arguments for Rejection**

*   The novelty of the core optimization methods is incremental, consisting mainly of adapting existing frameworks.
*   The theoretical guarantees for the gradient-free method rely on a strong and potentially unrealistic local convexity assumption.
*   The experimental evaluation is narrow, failing to compare against standard, non-PageRank-based learning-to-rank baselines, which makes it difficult to judge the practical impact of the work.

### **Recommendation**

This paper presents a solid, technically sound contribution by adding much-needed theoretical rigor to the optimization of Supervised PageRank. The lack of convergence guarantees in prior work is a real problem, and the authors' solution is well-founded. However, the work's overall impact is tempered by the limited novelty of the underlying optimization techniques and, more importantly, by an experimental evaluation that fails to contextualize the method within the broader field of learning-to-rank. The assumption of local convexity for the gradient-free method is also a notable weakness. While the theoretical contribution is clear, the paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could either relax the convexity assumption or provide a more comprehensive empirical evaluation against stronger, more diverse baselines. Given the high standards of NIPS, I am on the borderline, but lean towards rejection due to the narrow scope of the experimental validation and the reliance on a strong convexity assumption for one of the main contributions.
