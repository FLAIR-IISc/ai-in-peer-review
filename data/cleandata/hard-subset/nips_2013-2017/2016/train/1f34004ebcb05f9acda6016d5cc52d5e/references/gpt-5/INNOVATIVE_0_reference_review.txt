Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank when restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The core difficulty is that the loss and its gradient require solving query-specific Markov chains and (for gradients) differentiating through them; both are only available approximately. The authors propose two two-level algorithms:

- Gradient-free: extend random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with an inexact zero-order oracle; use a linearly convergent PageRank computation (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to obtain function values with controllable accuracy and analyze overall complexity.

- Gradient-based: develop an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (unknown Lipschitz-like constant), and couple it with approximate PageRank and its derivative computation to get gradient estimates with controllable accuracy and overall complexity bounds.

They provide lemmas connecting inner-loop iterations to oracle accuracy, analyze trade-offs between inner accuracy and total complexity, and give convergence/complexity theorems for both upper-level methods. On a Yandex user-browsing dataset (600 queries, ~11.7K pages total, ~1.7K labeled pairs), both methods outperform a prior gradient-based Supervised PageRank approach; the Nesterov–Nemirovski PageRank estimator converges faster than the power method.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank [21] and addresses gaps in prior learning procedures, which relied on power-method approximations without convergence guarantees to a stationary point in the outer loop. It adapts random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny) to constrained/inexact settings, and generalizes adaptive gradient methods to inexact first-order oracles in a nonconvex composite setting. It also leverages Nesterov–Nemirovski’s linearly convergent PageRank computation. To my knowledge, the constrained/inexact extensions and their use in a two-level supervised random-walk learning framework with end-to-end complexity accounting are new.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean unification of inexact-oracle optimization with approximate Markov chain solvers for learning Supervised PageRank. The constrained, inexact gradient-free extension and the adaptive gradient method with inexact first-order oracle are both useful beyond this application.

- Theoretical rigor: Clear oracle-accuracy lemmas for f and ∇f with computable iteration budgets; convergence and complexity theorems that explicitly capture the accuracy/complexity trade-off across levels.

- Practical insight: The gradient-free variant avoids derivative computation through large stochastic matrices; the use of the Nesterov–Nemirovski PageRank estimator is a well-motivated, faster alternative to the power method.

- Empirical signal: On real logs, both proposed methods improve the training objective over the published baseline; the inner solver converges faster than the power method.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments are small-scale, report only the training loss (pairwise squared hinge on Aπ), and omit standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP/P@k) and comparisons to stronger learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., supervised random walks, RankSVM/LambdaMART on the same features) and to alternative supervised graph-ranking methods. Wall-clock time and scalability on larger graphs are not reported.

- Assumptions and practicality: The gradient-free analysis in Sec. 4.2 assumes local convexity on Φ, which weakens the nonconvex message; guidance on choosing Φ to ensure convexity is not provided. Some constants (e.g., L set to 1e-4) appear ad hoc; the theorems rely on Lipschitz-like parameters and oracle accuracies that may be hard to calibrate in practice. Sensitivity analyses are minimal.

- Clarity/polish: The paper has typos and rough edges (e.g., stray “th Conference…”, several incomplete references), dense notation, and key technical details relegated to the supplement (e.g., β1 and derivative computation) that would help reader confidence in the main text.

- Evaluation focus: Reporting only arithmetic operation counts is of limited utility for practitioners; end-to-end runtime and memory, along with ablations on inner-loop iteration budgets (N, N1, N2), would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
1) Please report NDCG@k/MAP improvements and compare against standard LTR baselines using the same features. How do your methods perform at top ranks?

2) How are α, R, and especially L chosen in practice? Can you provide adaptive strategies for τ, δ, and L that avoid manual tuning and still preserve guarantees?

3) For the nonconvex gradient-based method, can you characterize convergence to an ε-stationary point without assuming convexity on Φ? Theorem 3 gives a stationarity measure; please clarify how Sec. 5.2 leverages it in the nonconvex case.

4) What values of N, N1, N2 were actually used per query in experiments, and how do they scale with graph size? Can you provide wall-clock comparisons to the power method pipeline?

5) Can you clarify the derivative computation (Eqs. 3.2–3.3) and the constant β1 in the main text (not just the supplement)?

Pros
- Novel and generally useful inexact-oracle optimization frameworks adapted to this learning setting.
- Theoretical guarantees that the prior method lacked.
- Reasonable empirical improvements and faster inner convergence.

Cons
- Empirical scope and metrics are too limited for NeurIPS; few baselines.
- Practical tuning and scalability guidance is thin.
- Clarity and references need significant polish.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a meaningful and reasonably bold direction—marrying inexact-oracle optimization (both gradient-based and gradient-free) with supervised random walks—backed by convergence/complexity results and encouraging initial experiments. With stronger empirical validation (standard IR metrics, broader baselines, runtime) and improved presentation, this could be a solid contribution to graph-based ranking and optimization with inexact oracles.

Confidence
Moderate.
