Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a framework where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features, shared across queries. The objective is a pairwise square loss over query-specific graphs. The authors propose two two-level algorithms:

- Lower level: For any parameter vector, approximate the stationary distribution πq(ϕ) and, for the gradient-based variant, its derivatives. They replace the standard power method by the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method for computing stationary distributions, and provide a generalization for derivative computation. They prove that the loss and gradient can be approximated to arbitrary accuracy, with explicit complexity bounds.

- Upper level: 
  (i) A gradient-free method based on random smoothing with projection onto a convex feasible set under an inexact zero-order oracle; they provide a convergence guarantee (for convex objectives) and complexity bounds, carefully tracking the impact of the oracle inaccuracy δ induced by the lower level.
  (ii) An adaptive projected gradient method for composite, possibly nonconvex, constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle, with convergence to a stationary point measured via a norm of the prox-gradient mapping. They also quantify how the lower-level accuracy controls the upper-level convergence.

They further analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy (number of iterations) and total complexity.

Empirically, they evaluate on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset (600 queries; ~11.7K pages, ~7.5K edges total; ~1.7K labeled pairs), comparing: (a) their gradient-free (GFN); (b) their gradient-based (GBN); and (c) a “state-of-the-art” gradient method (GBP) using the power method for π and derivatives. They report improvements in the loss and show fewer upper-level iterations versus some GBP settings, and suggest the Nesterov–Nemirovski method converges faster than the power method.

This work relates to: supervised/random-walk ranking methods (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011; BrowseRank; semi-supervised ranking with metadata), PageRank computation methods (e.g., Haveliwala; Nesterov–Nemirovski), inexact oracles for first-order methods (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny), and nonconvex stochastic/first-order methods (Ghadimi–Lan). The novelty lies in adapting constrained, inexact-Oracle gradient-free and gradient-based schemes to this two-level Supervised PageRank setting with explicit complexity guarantees, and providing a derivative computation scheme consistent with the lower-level solver.

Strengths
- Clear and useful formalization of learning Supervised PageRank as a two-level inexact-oracle optimization problem.
- Solid theoretical contributions: 
  • A gradient-free method with projection and explicit handling of uniform oracle bias/noise, with convergence bounds.  
  • An adaptive projected gradient method for composite, constrained, nonconvex problems under an inexact first-order oracle, with provable convergence to stationarity.  
  • Explicit coupling between lower-level accuracy and upper-level complexity, including constructive iteration bounds for computing π and its derivatives.
- Derivative computation based on a principled extension of the Nesterov–Nemirovski approach; the lemmas quantify errors and costs.
- Empirical indication that the proposed solvers can outperform a baseline gradient method using the power method, and that the lower-level solver is faster than power iteration.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited: one proprietary dataset with 600 queries and modest labeling; no public datasets; no reproducibility artifacts (code/data).
- Evaluation metrics focus on training loss only; standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, P@k) on held-out sets are not reported, making it hard to assess ranking quality impact.
- Baseline coverage is thin: no comparison to established learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART), nor to other supervised random-walk approaches (e.g., Supervised Random Walks; Semi-supervised ranking with rich metadata) under the same features.
- The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method (Section 4.2) is strong and unverified; the objective is generally nonconvex. Guarantees thus hinge on assuming a convex neighborhood around a local minimizer without evidence.
- Practical guidance on tuning lower-level accuracies (δ, N, N1, N2) and constants (e.g., β1) is limited to the supplement; it is unclear how sensitive performance is to these constants, and whether bounds are numerically conservative.
- Clarity issues: numerous typos/grammar problems; some definitions and constants are deferred; references are incomplete and inconsistently formatted.
- Significance: while the theoretical synthesis is sound, the experimental scope may be insufficient for NeurIPS, and the incremental novelty relative to known inexact-oracle methods may be viewed as moderate.

Quality
- The technical development appears sound; assumptions are generally stated, and the two-level error propagation is carefully handled. However, the convexity assumption needed by the gradient-free guarantees is not justified empirically, and the nonconvex analysis targets the gradient-based method only.
- Experimental evidence is not adequate to substantiate claims about search engine reliability/speed; no runtime or wall-clock benchmarks are provided.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading, clearer notation (especially around q-dependent objects), and a more accessible exposition of the derivative computation.
- References need to be fixed (complete bibliographic details). Some statements (e.g., “lowest proved complexity bound among methods proposed in [5]”) should be substantiated more explicitly.

Originality
- Adapting random gradient-free and inexact first-order methods with explicit projection to this setting, with tight coupling to PageRank solvers and derivative computation, is a useful contribution. The core ideas leverage known techniques; the novelty is in integration, extension to constrained/inexact settings, and application to Supervised PageRank.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners working on feature-augmented random-walk ranking. However, without broader baselines, standard IR metrics, and public datasets, the impact is difficult to assess.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical framework for inexact-oracle, constrained optimization (both zero- and first-order) applied to a relevant problem.
- Explicit complexity/error trade-offs across levels, including derivative computation.
- Initial empirical gains over a reasonable baseline.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and non-standard empirical evaluation; lack of standard ranking metrics and baselines.
- Strong, unjustified convexity assumption for gradient-free guarantees.
- Clarity and referencing issues.
- Limited evidence of practical efficiency improvements (runtime).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Report standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, P@k) on held-out data; include CIs and statistical tests.
- Compare against strong LTR baselines (e.g., LambdaMART) and supervised random-walk methods under the same features. Include ablations for lower-level methods and α sensitivity.
- Provide wall-clock runtime, memory, and iteration counts for π/∇π computations and upper-level methods; include convergence plots.
- Justify or relax the convexity assumption in Section 4.2; alternatively, provide a nonconvex analysis for the gradient-free approach.
- Elaborate on practical selection of δ, τ, N, N1, N2 and the role/magnitude of β1; discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Improve writing and complete references; move key constants/definitions from the supplement into the main text.

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical integration is well executed, but the empirical evaluation is too limited for NeurIPS, the convexity assumption for the gradient-free guarantees is problematic, and clarity/references need substantial improvement. Strengthening experiments (public datasets, IR metrics, baselines, runtime) and clarifying assumptions would make this a much stronger submission. Confidence: medium.
