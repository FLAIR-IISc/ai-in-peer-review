

# Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking method that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both designed to handle the inexact oracle problem inherent in estimating stationary distributions for Markov random walks. The key contribution is providing convergence guarantees for these methods, which the previous state-of-the-art [21] lacked. The paper includes theoretical analysis of convergence rates and computational complexity, along with empirical results showing superior performance on a real-world web ranking task.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors successfully establish convergence guarantees for both optimization methods under the challenging inexact oracle setting. The proofs of Lemmas 1 and 2 (referenced to supplementary materials) properly address the accuracy requirements for approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, which is central to solving the problem. The experimental results on the Yandex dataset convincingly demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21], with the gradient-free method showing particularly strong results.

However, the paper could strengthen its evaluation by providing more detailed ablation studies on how different parameter choices affect performance. The comparison with other optimization methods for non-convex problems is limited, and the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the practical limitations of the proposed approaches (e.g., how they scale to extremely large graphs beyond the 11.7K vertices tested).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the problem statement is carefully defined. The distinction between the two-level optimization approach (lower-level for stationary distribution estimation, upper-level for parameter optimization) is clearly articulated. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation is well-maintained throughout.

The supplementary materials referenced for proofs are appropriately used, though some readers might benefit from more detailed explanations of the key technical steps in the main paper. The experimental setup is clearly described, though the paper could better explain why certain hyperparameters (like the choice of R=0.99) were selected.

## Originality
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the application of optimization techniques to graph-based ranking problems. While the core optimization methods (gradient methods with inexact oracles) build on existing work by Nesterov and others, their application to Supervised PageRank with the specific challenges of inexact oracle for Markov chains is novel. The two-level framework for handling the inexact oracle problem is particularly valuable.

The paper properly situates itself in the literature, citing relevant work on Supervised PageRank [21] and optimization methods [16, 7, 25]. However, it could better clarify how much of the novelty comes from the optimization techniques versus the application to this specific problem. The combination of efficient Markov chain solvers with optimization methods for this particular application is the paper's strongest original contribution.

## Significance
The problem of learning effective ranking algorithms is highly significant for search engines and information retrieval systems. The paper's theoretical guarantees provide much-needed confidence in the optimization process, which is particularly valuable for practical applications where convergence behavior directly impacts ranking quality. The empirical results showing improved ranking quality over the state-of-the-art demonstrate clear practical impact.

The paper's significance is further enhanced by its potential to generalize to other graph-based learning problems where similar inexact oracle issues arise. The authors' observation that "this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle" is well-supported by the paper's contributions.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank
- Develops and analyzes both gradient-based and gradient-free methods for this specific problem
- Demonstrates practical superiority over the state-of-the-art in real-world experiments
- Carefully analyzes the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and computational complexity

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with other optimization methods for non-convex problems
- Insufficient exploration of practical limitations and scalability beyond the tested datasets
- Could better distinguish between the novelty of the optimization techniques versus their application to Supervised PageRank

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a solid contribution to the optimization of graph-based ranking algorithms with clear theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. While the core optimization techniques aren't completely novel, their application to this specific problem with the inexact oracle challenge is both novel and significant. The paper advances the state of the art in learning Supervised PageRank and provides valuable theoretical insights that can benefit the broader optimization and machine learning communities.

The paper meets NIPS's quality criteria with sound theoretical analysis and convincing experimental results. It addresses a practically important problem with a theoretically grounded approach, and the results have clear potential for impact in search engine ranking systems. The writing is clear and well-organized, and the paper properly situates itself within the relevant literature.

The minor weaknesses in the comparison with other optimization methods and exploration of limitations do not significantly detract from the paper's strong contribution. The work is sufficiently novel, technically sound, and significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
