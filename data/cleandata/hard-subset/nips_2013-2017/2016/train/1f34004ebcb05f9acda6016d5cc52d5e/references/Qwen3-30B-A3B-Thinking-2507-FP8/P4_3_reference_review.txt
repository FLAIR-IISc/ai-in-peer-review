

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, an extension of traditional PageRank that incorporates node and edge features to improve ranking quality. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning these parameters. The first method uses gradient-based optimization with a linearly convergent lower-level method for approximating the stationary distribution, while the second method adapts a gradient-free optimization approach to handle the inexact oracle setting. The paper builds upon previous work on Supervised PageRank [21], extends gradient-based optimization methods [7, 21], and leverages efficient methods for computing stationary distributions [17, 23, 24]. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity, and demonstrate superior performance on a real web ranking task compared to the state-of-the-art method [21].

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound methods with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide lemmas and theorems with proofs in supplementary materials, establishing convergence rates for both optimization methods. The experimental results demonstrate that both proposed algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art method [21] on a web ranking task, with statistical significance confirmed through t-tests. The paper properly acknowledges limitations, noting that the gradient-based method requires the problem to be convex in a small vicinity of the optimum, which is a standard assumption for non-convex optimization methods. However, the paper does not thoroughly address the computational complexity of the proposed methods in practice beyond theoretical bounds, and the experiments are limited to a single dataset (Yandex), which may not generalize to other domains.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methods to experiments. Mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The supplementary materials provide detailed proofs, which is helpful for verification. The theoretical sections, while mathematically dense, are presented in a way that is accessible to readers familiar with optimization theory. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is mentioned but not thoroughly explored.

## Originality
The paper makes a novel contribution by combining two-level optimization with gradient-based and gradient-free methods for the Supervised PageRank problem, providing convergence guarantees that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks. The specific application to Supervised PageRank with the trade-off analysis between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity is novel. The authors properly cite related work and clearly distinguish their contributions from previous methods. The extension of gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with inexact oracle information is also a significant contribution to the optimization literature.

## Significance
The problem of learning ranking algorithms for web pages is highly significant for search engine applications. The proposed methods provide theoretical guarantees that the state-of-the-art method lacks, which is a substantial contribution to the field. The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements in ranking quality, which could potentially increase the reliability and speed of search engines. The paper also represents the first time that random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with efficient methods for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle. However, the paper does not thoroughly address how the proposed methods scale to very large graphs, which is crucial for web-scale applications.

## Strengths
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in the context of Supervised PageRank
- Demonstrates superior performance on real-world ranking data compared to state-of-the-art
- Clearly defines the problem and distinguishes contributions from previous work
- Provides detailed complexity analysis for the proposed methods
- Properly acknowledges limitations and assumptions

## Weaknesses
- Experiments limited to a single dataset (Yandex), potentially limiting generalizability
- Does not thoroughly address computational complexity in practice beyond theoretical bounds
- Assumes convexity in a small vicinity of the optimum for the gradient-based method
- Lacks clear comparison of computational complexity between the two proposed methods
- Does not discuss scaling to very large graphs, which is crucial for web-scale applications

## Recommendation
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing convergence guarantees for optimization methods in the context of Supervised PageRank. The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical improvements. The work is well-written and properly contextualized within the literature. While the experiments are limited to a single dataset, the theoretical contributions are strong and the paper addresses an important problem in graph-based ranking.

The main weakness is the lack of discussion about scaling to very large graphs, which is particularly relevant for the web ranking application. However, the theoretical guarantees and clear presentation of the methods make this a valuable contribution to the field.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: Strong theoretical contribution with convergence guarantees, clear problem formulation, demonstrated practical improvements over state-of-the-art, proper citation of related work.

**Contra-acceptance arguments**: Limited experimental validation on a single dataset, lack of discussion about scaling to very large graphs, assumption of local convexity for the gradient-based method.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based learning problems, with both theoretical and practical significance. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with meaningful improvements over previous methods. I recommend acceptance.
