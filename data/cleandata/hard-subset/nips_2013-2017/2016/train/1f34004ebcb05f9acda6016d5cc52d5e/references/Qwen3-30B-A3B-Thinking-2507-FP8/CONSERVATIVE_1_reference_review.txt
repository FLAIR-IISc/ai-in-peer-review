

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank, claiming to provide theoretical convergence guarantees that previous work lacks. The authors develop a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method for optimizing the non-convex loss function in Supervised PageRank, with theoretical complexity bounds and empirical validation on a web ranking task.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound in its theoretical development, with detailed convergence proofs for both methods. The authors clearly identify limitations in prior work (lack of convergence guarantees for the state-of-the-art [21]) and address them with their proposed methods. The experimental results show consistent improvements over the baseline [21] on the web ranking task, which is a relevant application domain. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with proper mathematical notation.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical claims, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the novelty or significance of its contributions. The two-level approach is not fundamentally new - it builds directly on established optimization frameworks (e.g., adapting [16] for constrained optimization, extending [7] for inexact oracles) rather than introducing a novel paradigm. The authors claim "the lowest proved complexity bound" but don't adequately justify why their specific implementation is superior to alternatives that could have been applied to this problem.

The experimental validation is insufficient for a NeurIPS submission. The paper only evaluates on one dataset (Yandex web browsing graphs), which is limited in scope. Crucially, they don't compare against more recent optimization methods that might have been applicable to this problem (e.g., stochastic optimization methods that have become standard for large-scale problems). The improvement over [21] is marginal (though statistically significant), but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this marginal improvement justifies the additional complexity of their methods.

The theoretical analysis is quite technical but lacks clear practical insights. For instance, the paper claims "the main advantage of the first gradient-based algorithm: the guarantees of a convergence do not require the convexity," but the problem is explicitly stated to be non-convex, and the authors only assume convexity in a local vicinity of the minimum - a common but not particularly strong assumption. The gradient-free method's claimed advantage ("avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix") is questionable since their method still requires derivative approximations through the inexact oracle.

Most significantly, the paper doesn't adequately address why these methods are better suited for the specific challenges of web ranking than standard optimization techniques. The problem is framed as "huge-scale optimization," but the complexity analysis doesn't convincingly show that their methods scale better than simpler alternatives. The computational complexity bounds provided are quite high, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that their methods actually perform better in practice on large-scale instances.

**Comparison to prior work:** While the paper cites relevant work on Supervised PageRank ([21], [2], [6], [12]), it doesn't properly contextualize its contribution relative to recent advances in optimization for machine learning. For instance, the gradient-free method is a straightforward adaptation of [16], which was already presented at NeurIPS 2015. The gradient-based method builds on [7], which was published in SIAM Journal on Optimization in 2015. The paper fails to explain why these standard techniques aren't sufficient for the problem at hand.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to optimization theory, the novelty is limited and the experimental validation is insufficient for a NeurIPS submission. The marginal improvements over existing methods don't justify the additional complexity of the proposed approaches, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these methods are better suited for the problem than established optimization techniques. The theoretical claims are well-supported, but the practical significance is unclear, which is a critical criterion for NeurIPS. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized optimization journal rather than a venue like NeurIPS that prioritizes both theoretical and practical significance.

**Summary of key concerns:**
- Limited novelty (builds directly on existing optimization frameworks)
- Insufficient experimental validation (only one dataset, no comparison to recent optimization methods)
- Marginal performance gains without clear practical significance
- Theoretical claims not adequately justified as superior to alternatives
- Failure to demonstrate scalability benefits for large-scale web ranking tasks
