

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking method that incorporates node and edge features. The authors identify a key limitation in existing approaches: the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of computing the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The paper proposes two two-level optimization methods that combine a lower-level approximation of the stationary distribution (using a linearly convergent method) with an upper-level optimization algorithm (either gradient-based or gradient-free). The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for both methods, and demonstrate empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task.

## Strengths

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters, addressing a critical gap in the literature. The two-level framework is well-motivated and clearly articulated, with careful analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are technically sound and build upon established optimization frameworks (Nesterov, etc.) while adapting them to the specific challenges of the inexact oracle setting.

The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] across multiple query sets. The authors thoroughly investigate the impact of hyperparameters (ε, L) and provide meaningful comparisons with the untuned Supervised PageRank baseline. The use of real-world web browsing data from Yandex adds practical relevance to the work.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the experimental section could be strengthened. The paper doesn't compare with other relevant optimization methods for similar problems (e.g., stochastic gradient methods), which would better contextualize the relative advantages of the proposed approaches. The assumption that f(ϕ) is convex in a local neighborhood of the optimum is somewhat restrictive for a non-convex problem, and the paper doesn't explore how this assumption affects practical performance.

The complexity analysis is thorough, but the paper doesn't provide concrete computational time comparisons between the proposed methods and the state-of-the-art. The empirical results show that GBN requires fewer iterations than GBP, but without timing data, it's unclear whether this translates to practical speedups.

## Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and thorough experimental validation. The proofs in the supplementary materials are well-structured, though some of the more complex derivations might benefit from additional intuition. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the local convexity assumption), though they could better discuss how these might impact real-world applications.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation and logical flow. The two-level framework is particularly well-explained. The supplementary materials are comprehensive, though some of the most technical details might be better placed in the main paper for accessibility. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution—applying inexact oracle optimization methods to the Supervised PageRank learning problem—is novel. While the individual components (inexact oracles, gradient methods) are not new, their combination for this specific problem is original. The paper appropriately cites related work, though it could better distinguish its contributions from the broader literature on graph-based ranking algorithms.

**Significance:** The problem of learning graph-based ranking algorithms is highly relevant to search engines and recommendation systems, making this work practically significant. The theoretical guarantees provide a strong foundation for practitioners who need reliable optimization methods. The empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art demonstrate clear progress in the field. This work advances the state of the art by providing a principled approach to learning Supervised PageRank with convergence guarantees.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to both the optimization and graph-based learning communities. The theoretical guarantees fill an important gap in the literature, and the empirical results demonstrate practical value. While the experimental section could be slightly strengthened with additional comparisons and timing data, the paper's technical merits and significance outweigh these limitations.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel optimization approach that could be applied to other graph-based learning tasks. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the field, with both theoretical rigor and practical relevance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions (which could be addressed in the camera-ready version), specifically to strengthen the experimental comparison with other optimization methods and provide more concrete computational time comparisons.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
