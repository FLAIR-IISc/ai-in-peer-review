

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank, a ranking algorithm based on random walks on graphs. The authors claim their methods have theoretical convergence guarantees, unlike the state-of-the-art method [21], and demonstrate improved ranking quality in experiments.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on existing work on supervised ranking methods [21] and optimization with inexact oracles [16, 7]. The authors extend the Supervised PageRank framework by introducing two-level optimization approaches where the lower level approximates the stationary distribution using a linearly convergent method [17], and the upper level uses either gradient-based or gradient-free optimization. While the paper claims novelty in combining these techniques for this specific problem, the core optimization methods are slight adaptations of existing approaches rather than fundamentally new contributions.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both proposed methods, which is a positive contribution.
2. The experimental results on a real-world ranking task demonstrate improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of loss function values.
3. The paper clearly articulates the trade-offs between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and the overall computational complexity.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of rigorous validation**: The paper claims theoretical guarantees but fails to adequately demonstrate that these guarantees translate to practical advantages. The convergence theorems rely on restrictive assumptions (e.g., convexity in a neighborhood of the solution), while the problem is inherently non-convex. The paper does not provide evidence that the methods converge to meaningful solutions in the non-convex regime.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The experiments are limited to a single dataset (Yandex) with no ablation studies to isolate which components of the proposed methods drive the improvements. The paper mentions "p-values of the paired t-tests" but doesn't provide these values in the main paper, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the claimed improvements.

3. **Questionable novelty**: The gradient-free method is a slight adaptation of [16], and the gradient-based method builds on [7]. The paper's claim that "this is the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization" is overstated, as similar combinations have been explored in other contexts.

4. **Inadequate comparison with alternatives**: The paper compares only with the state-of-the-art method [21] but fails to benchmark against other standard optimization approaches that might be more practical for this problem (e.g., standard stochastic gradient descent, which is commonly used for large-scale optimization).

5. **Lack of real-world scalability evidence**: The paper's complexity bounds are asymptotic and don't provide meaningful insights into how the methods would perform on graphs of varying sizes in production environments. The experiments use only small subsets of the data (100-300 queries), making it unclear how the methods would scale to the full dataset.

6. **Unjustified claims about advantages**: The paper claims the gradient-based method "has less input parameters than gradient-free one" and the gradient-free method "avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix." However, it doesn't provide evidence that these claimed advantages translate to practical benefits in terms of implementation complexity or computational efficiency.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed methods.
- The experimental results show improvements over the state-of-the-art on a real dataset.
- The paper clearly articulates the trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The theoretical guarantees are limited to restrictive settings (convex neighborhoods) while the problem is fundamentally non-convex.
- The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claimed advantages.
- The novelty of the approach is overstated, as it primarily adapts existing optimization techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new methods.
- The paper lacks evidence that the proposed methods would scale to real-world search engine applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a reasonable attempt to address a practical problem, it falls short of the rigorous validation expected at NIPS. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported by practical evidence, and the experimental results are limited and inadequately analyzed. The paper presents a slight adaptation of existing optimization techniques rather than a significant methodological advance. The lack of comparison with standard optimization approaches and the insufficient evidence for the claimed advantages undermine the paper's contribution.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen the experimental validation (including statistical significance testing, ablation studies, and comparison with standard optimization methods) and provide more convincing evidence that the theoretical guarantees translate to practical advantages in real-world ranking applications before this work would be suitable for NIPS.
