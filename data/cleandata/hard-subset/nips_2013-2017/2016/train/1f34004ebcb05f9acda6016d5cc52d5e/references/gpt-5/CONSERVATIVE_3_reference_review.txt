Summary
The paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. It proposes two two-level optimization schemes: (i) a gradient-free method for convex problems with an inexact zero-order oracle (built via random smoothing on the sphere and projection), and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle. On the lower level, both methods rely on the Nesterov–Nemirovski approach to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov chain, and a generalized iterative scheme to approximate derivatives of the stationary distribution. The authors give convergence guarantees under oracle inaccuracies and derive complexity bounds that couple the lower-level approximation accuracy with upper-level iteration complexity. Experiments on a Yandex browsing graph dataset compare their methods against a prior gradient method for Supervised PageRank, reporting improved loss values and claims of faster convergence.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical setting in graph ranking where exact stationary distributions and derivatives are expensive and only approximate oracles are available.
- Provides convergence guarantees for both a zeroth-order projected method with biased/inexact function values and a first-order adaptive method with inexact gradients, including complexity bounds that account for lower-level approximations.
- The lower-level use of Nesterov–Nemirovski for PageRank computation (and its extension for derivatives) is sound and preferable to the power method in theory.
- The paper attempts to quantify the trade-off between oracle accuracy and total computational complexity.
- Empirical results on a real (though proprietary) dataset suggest improvements over a specific prior baseline for Supervised PageRank.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited and nonstandard for ranking: only the training loss (pairwise squared hinge) is reported. No widely used IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) are presented, making it hard to assess practical ranking gains.
- Baseline coverage is narrow. The only competitor appears to be the earlier gradient-based Supervised PageRank method. Missing are comparisons to established learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM), supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec 2011), and modern zeroth-order/derivative-free optimizers (SPSA, CMA-ES, and more recent gradient-free nonconvex methods). Given the conservative novelty of the upper-level methods, stronger baselines are needed.
- Claims that existing methods [7,16] “cannot be applied” are not fully convincing. Prior work considers constrained problems with projections and handles stochastic/bias issues under various oracle models. The contribution relative to Nesterov–Spokoiny (random gradient-free), Ghadimi–Lan (nonconvex stochastic), and Duchi et al. (smoothing/bandit) needs more precise positioning. The proposed gradient-free oracle is biased; related results with biased/noisy function evaluations exist and should be discussed.
- The gradient-free method’s guarantees require convexity; the paper admits the loss is generally nonconvex and invokes “local convexity” on Φ without establishing or diagnosing it in the experiments. This undermines the relevance of its theoretical guarantee to the application.
- The first-order method’s guarantee controls a proximal residual MK(xK−xK+1); a clearer connection to stationarity (e.g., gradient mapping norm) would help interpretability. Assumptions and constants (e.g., β1, L) are hidden in the supplement; practical measurability is unclear.
- Experimental setup is small (≈600 queries, ≈1.7K labeled pairs total). Results are reported only for loss values, with limited robustness analysis. Wall-clock time, memory, and derivative computation costs are not reported, despite the paper’s emphasis on computational trade-offs.
- Reproducibility is limited: data is proprietary; many implementation details (α, stopping criteria for both levels, numerical tolerances, initialization) are only briefly sketched. Reported p-values lack accompanying effect sizes and multiple-testing considerations.
- Presentation issues: numerous typos and inconsistent notation; several references are incomplete or improperly formatted; some claims (e.g., “lowest proved complexity bound among methods in [5]”) need careful backing.

Questions for the authors
- Please report standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) on the test sets and provide per-query variance. Do improvements in f translate into ranking quality?
- How does wall-clock time compare across methods, including lower-level iterations (N1, N2) and derivative computation? Provide time-accuracy trade-offs as δ varies.
- Justify the “local convexity” assumption on Φ for the gradient-free method. Did you verify convexity empirically (e.g., via Hessian sampling) or choose Φ small enough to ensure it?
- Provide comparisons to stronger baselines: LambdaMART, RankSVM/RankNet, Supervised Random Walks (Backstrom & Leskovec 2011), and derivative-free optimizers (SPSA, CMA-ES).
- Clarify the relation between the residual ‖MK(xK−xK+1)‖ and a standard stationarity measure for constrained nonconvex problems.
- Describe how β1 and L are estimated in practice, and the sensitivity to their misspecification.
- Elaborate on ensuring positivity constraints and non-zero denominators; what happens with features that are zero or sparse?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Theoretical development for inexact oracles in both zeroth- and first-order settings, with application to a relevant graph ranking problem.
- Lower-level algorithmic choice is principled and arguably improves over power iteration.
- Some empirical indications of benefit over a specific prior Supervised PageRank optimizer.

Cons:
- Limited and nonstandard empirical validation; missing key baselines and metrics.
- The main guarantees either do not apply to the nonconvex application (gradient-free) or are hard to interpret practically (first-order residual), with unclear constants.
- Novelty over existing optimization literature appears incremental and not convincingly positioned.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical contributions are of interest, but the experimental section is too limited to validate the practical relevance, and the positioning relative to established optimization and learning-to-rank literature is insufficient. A stronger empirical study with standard metrics, broader baselines, clear run-time analyses, and better exposition would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence: 4/5
