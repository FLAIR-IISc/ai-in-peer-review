Summary
The paper tackles learning parameters for Supervised PageRank in a two-level framework where the lower level computes approximate stationary distributions (and their derivatives) of query-dependent random walks and the upper level optimizes the loss over parameters with inexact oracle information. The lower level replaces the standard power iteration with the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent iteration, and generalizes it to compute derivatives via a weighted-averaging scheme. On top of this, the authors propose two upper-level optimizers:

- A random gradient-free (zeroth-order) projected method for constrained convex problems under a uniformly bounded inexact oracle, with a convergence guarantee (Theorem 1) that accounts for oracle bias and smoothing.
- An adaptive projected gradient method for composite, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant (Theorem 3), providing a stationarity-type guarantee.

They derive explicit accuracy–cost trade-offs linking the lower-level iteration counts (N, N1, N2) to upper-level tolerances and present overall complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4). Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset compare their gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods to the prior gradient-based approach (GBP) used in Supervised PageRank. They report lower loss and faster convergence in some settings and show that the Nesterov–Nemirovski method converges faster than power iteration.

Strengths
- Solid optimization perspective: The paper formulates learning as constrained optimization with inexact oracles, which is natural for PageRank-like quantities computable only approximately. The upper-level algorithms are well-motivated and accompanied by convergence analyses.
- Lower-level advances: Adopting the Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration for stationary distribution computation (and extending it to derivatives) is sensible; the error controls (Lemmas 1–2) and complexity accounting are useful for end-to-end guarantees.
- Inexact oracle theory: Extending gradient-free methods to constrained settings with bounded deterministic noise and adapting a proximal gradient framework to nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles and backtracking are of independent interest.
- Practical relevance: The two-level design addresses a real bottleneck in learning random-walk based rankers; experiments on an industrial dataset suggest potential improvements.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and metrics: Evaluation is confined to a single proprietary dataset with ~1.7K labeled pairs and reports primarily the training loss (pairwise squared hinge-like). Standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP, Precision@k) are not reported, making it hard to judge practical ranking quality. The paper should also report wall-clock times and memory, since the central claim is improved efficiency/complexity.
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to one variant (GBP) from the Supervised PageRank literature. It would strengthen the case to compare to (a) other supervised random-walk methods (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec, WSDM’11), (b) strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) on the same graphs, and (c) power iteration with more appropriate stopping criteria.
- Guarantees vs. problem nonconvexity: The gradient-free upper-level guarantee (Theorem 1/2) requires convexity; yet the objective is nonconvex in general. The application section assumes f is convex in a small neighborhood around a local minimum, which is neither verified nor testable. The gradient-based method addresses nonconvexity (stationarity measure), but the paper should clarify which guarantee applies to which experiments and how the convexity assumption is used in practice.
- Clarity and presentation: There are many typos and formatting issues, inconsistent notation, and incomplete references. Important constants (e.g., β1) and specific parameter choices for N, N1, N2 are deferred to the supplement; brief intuition and practical guidance in the main text would help. The experimental protocol (e.g., choice of step sizes “50, 100, 200, 500” for GBP) is unclear; early stopping “when the difference … is less than −1e−5 on the test sets” is unusual and may bias evaluation.
- Reproducibility: No code or public data; several hyperparameters and stopping criteria are unspecified. The claims that the proposed lower-level method has the “lowest proved complexity” among [5] options should be contextualized with concrete constants and practical implications.

Quality
The theoretical components appear technically sound given the stated assumptions. The inexact oracle modeling and bounds are consistent with known analyses (e.g., Devolder et al., Nesterov–Spokoiny), and the projection/sphere-randomization choices are reasonable. However, the empirical section is too limited to fully support the practical claims.

Clarity
Moderate. The overall structure is clear, but the manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading, consistent notation (norms, constants), clearer algorithm descriptions (unique numbering, explicit inputs/outputs), and more experimental detail. Reporting standard ranking metrics and runtime would improve clarity and usefulness.

Originality
The paper combines and extends known techniques: linearly convergent Markov chain solvers; gradient-free optimization with smoothing; proximal gradient with inexact oracles. The main novelty is the adaptation to constrained, biased-oracle settings and the derivative computation scheme for PageRank-like models, together with end-to-end complexity accounting. This is incremental but meaningful.

Significance
Moderate. If validated more thoroughly, the approach could be useful for practitioners training feature-parameterized random-walk rankers. The theoretical developments, while incremental, may interest the optimization community working with inexact oracles.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report NDCG/MAP/Precision@k on test sets, and include wall-clock time and iterations for lower- and upper-level components. Provide ablations on N, N1, N2 and sensitivity to α.
- Clarify the GBP baseline: what exactly are the step sizes “50, 100, 200, 500”? How is power iteration stopped? Avoid using test sets for early stopping criteria.
- Discuss the convexity assumption used for the gradient-free method. Can you provide conditions under which f is convex on Φ, or otherwise restrict claims to stationarity?
- Provide more details (or pseudocode) for derivative computation (3.2–3.3), the constant β1, and practical settings for δ, τ, and backtracking parameters.
- Include or reference proofs for complexity claims and the “lowest proved complexity” statement.
- Improve writing and references (complete bibliographic info).

Pros
- Sound optimization framework for inexact-oracle learning.
- Lower-level algorithm with faster convergence and derivative estimation.
- Explicit accuracy–complexity trade-offs and convergence guarantees.
- Promising experimental indications.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics, broader baselines, and runtime.
- Convexity assumption for zeroth-order method is unverified; guarantees may not apply.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are solid and relevant, but the experimental evaluation and clarity fall short of NIPS standards. With stronger empirical validation (standard metrics, runtime, fair baselines), clearer presentation, and tighter discussion of assumptions, this could become a compelling submission.
