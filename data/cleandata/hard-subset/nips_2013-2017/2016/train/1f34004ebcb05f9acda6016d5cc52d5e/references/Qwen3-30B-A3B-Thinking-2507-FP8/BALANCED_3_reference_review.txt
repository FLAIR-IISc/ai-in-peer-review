

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary

This paper addresses the learning problem in Supervised PageRank [21], which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features through linear combinations of features with learned parameters. The authors identify a critical gap in existing work: the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of the lower-level Markov chain stationary distribution calculations. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) that provide convergence guarantees while maintaining practical efficiency. The work builds on recent advances in optimization with inexact oracles [7, 16, 25] but adapts them specifically to the constrained non-convex optimization problem in Supervised PageRank.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes strong theoretical contributions with rigorous convergence analysis for both proposed methods. The authors provide tight complexity bounds for both algorithms (Theorems 2 and 4) and establish the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity. The experimental results on real-world web ranking data support the theoretical claims, demonstrating consistent improvement over the state-of-the-art method [21] across multiple query sets. The paper carefully addresses the challenges of constrained optimization with inexact oracles, which is a non-trivial extension of existing work.

**Originality:** The two-level framework with inexact oracle specifically designed for Supervised PageRank is novel. While there has been work on optimization with inexact oracles (e.g., [7, 16, 25]), the authors' adaptation to the specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank problem—particularly the constraints on the parameter space and the nature of the inexact oracle—represents a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by addressing the convergence guarantees gap in [21].

**Significance:** The problem of learning Supervised PageRank is important for web ranking applications, and the authors demonstrate practical improvements in ranking quality. The theoretical guarantees provide a stronger foundation for using this approach in real-world search engines, potentially increasing both reliability and speed. The framework could be extended to other graph-based learning problems, suggesting broader applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The supplementary materials provide necessary details for the proofs, which is helpful for the reader.

## Weaknesses

**Experimental Design:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about the specific parameter choices for the baselines. The paper mentions that "GBP" was evaluated for different step sizes, but doesn't provide a clear justification for the chosen step sizes or how they compare to standard practices. It would be helpful to see a comparison with additional baselines, such as stochastic gradient descent with the same lower-level approximation.

**Practical Implementation:** While the paper provides complexity bounds, it doesn't discuss the practical implementation challenges of the proposed methods. For example, how do the algorithms behave with very large graphs (beyond the 11.7K vertices studied)? The paper mentions "huge-scale optimization" in the abstract, but the experiments don't adequately demonstrate scalability.

**Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper cites [25] as relevant to the inexact oracle framework, but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from recent advances in optimization with inexact oracles, such as those in [26, 27] (not cited in the paper). The authors should clarify how their framework improves upon these.

**Theoretical Assumptions:** The convergence guarantees for the gradient-based method require that the objective function is convex in a neighborhood of the solution (Section 5.2). This is a strong assumption for a non-convex problem, and the paper doesn't provide empirical evidence that this condition holds in practice for the ranking problem.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Introduces two novel optimization methods with tight complexity bounds
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art
- Well-written with clear theoretical contributions

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges
- Insufficient comparison to recent optimization methods with inexact oracles
- Strong theoretical assumptions (convexity in a neighborhood)
- Limited scalability analysis beyond the current dataset

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Expand the experimental section to include a more comprehensive comparison with recent optimization methods for inexact oracles (e.g., [26, 27]), explaining how the proposed methods differ and why they're better suited to this specific problem.

2. Provide more details about the practical implementation of the algorithms, including memory requirements and how the algorithms behave with larger graphs. Consider adding experiments on progressively larger subsets of the dataset to demonstrate scalability.

3. Discuss the practical implications of the convexity assumption in Section 5.2. Provide empirical evidence (e.g., a plot of the objective function around the solution) to show that this condition is reasonable for the ranking problem.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the framework might extend to other graph-based learning problems, highlighting the broader applicability of the two-level optimization approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in the Supervised PageRank literature. The paper makes valuable theoretical advances with rigorous convergence guarantees, and the empirical results support the claims. While there are some limitations in the experimental design and theoretical assumptions, these can be addressed with minor revisions. The paper meets the quality standards of NIPS and makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based learning problems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
