Summary
The paper studies learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where node and edge features are linearly combined to form restart and transition probabilities. The authors propose two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-free method that uses a zero-order inexact oracle obtained by approximating the stationary distribution via a linearly convergent PageRank routine (Nesterov–Nemirovski) and applies a randomized gradient-free projected method with spherical smoothing under constraints; (ii) a gradient-based method that uses an inexact first-order oracle (approximating both function and gradient via the same PageRank routine and a derivative iteration) and an adaptive projected gradient algorithm with backtracking for a composite (potentially nonconvex) objective. They provide convergence results and complexity bounds accounting for the lower-level approximation accuracy, and empirically compare against the prior two-level gradient-based approach that uses power iteration for PageRank/derivatives. On a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries; ~11.7k nodes total), their methods reduce the training loss more than the baseline, and the Nesterov–Nemirovski routine converges faster than power iteration.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant class of problems (learning parameters of feature-augmented PageRank/HITS variants) with a clear two-level structure and inexact oracle.
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both zero-order and first-order upper-level methods in the presence of deterministic approximation errors from the lower level; includes explicit dependence on approximation accuracy.
- Lower-level: adopts a linearly convergent algorithm for PageRank computation and extends it to derivative estimation, with error control and arithmetic operation counts.
- The adaptive projected gradient method handles unknown Lipschitz constants via backtracking and yields a stationarity criterion in terms of the gradient mapping.
- Empirically shows improved optimization loss versus a prior power-iteration-based method; demonstrates that the chosen PageRank routine is faster than power iteration.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. Randomized gradient-free methods with smoothing and inexact oracles (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny, Devolder et al.) and adaptive gradient methods for composite objectives are well-established. The main extensions claimed—handling constraints, spherical smoothing, and deterministic bounded errors—are standard or straightforward adaptations. The nonconvex case is treated by measuring stationarity, which is consistent with prior work (e.g., Ghadimi–Lan), and the paper relies on local convexity assumptions for the gradient-free method, which substantially narrows the claim of generality.
- Theoretical development leaves key constants (e.g., β1) unspecified in the main text; complexity bounds are difficult to interpret and to compare to alternatives. The claim of “lowest proved complexity bound among methods proposed in [5]” is asserted without a transparent side-by-side comparison.
- Experimental validation is limited:
  - Dataset scale is small relative to web-graph settings; “huge-scale” claims are not demonstrated. Derivative computations for large graphs (memory/time) are not convincingly evaluated; the gradient-free method’s practical benefit (avoiding per-entry derivatives) is not shown on truly large instances.
  - Evaluation focuses on the training loss (pairwise squared hinge on PageRank scores). There is no reporting of ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP) on held-out data, which are standard for learning-to-rank and necessary to assess practical impact.
  - Baselines are narrow; comparison is only to a specific two-level gradient method [21]. No comparisons to broader supervised random walk approaches (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011), learning-to-rank baselines, or modern graph ranking methods.
  - Runtime/wall-clock and memory comparisons are missing; only iteration counts or arithmetic operation estimates are given. Sensitivity to approximation accuracy vs. runtime is not systematically explored.
  - Several experimental details are unclear or concerning: step-size/“power” parameters for GBP are confusing; the stopping rule “difference less than −1e−5” seems incorrectly signed; hyperparameter choices (ε=1e−6, L=1e−4) appear arbitrary and over-precise without justification; no cross-validation; significance tests are reported only for loss, not for IR metrics.
- Clarity and presentation need work:
  - Numerous typos and grammar issues; some equations and notation are hard to parse.
  - References are incomplete and in several cases missing years/venues; related work coverage around inexact oracles and nonconvex zeroth-/first-order methods could be more thorough and precise.
  - Important assumptions are buried or not critically discussed: e.g., for the gradient-free method, convexity of f over the feasible ball around a local minimum is assumed without guidance on verifying or enforcing it; the role of positivity constraints and feasibility of projections are glossed over.
  - Proofs delegated to supplementary; main text provides limited intuition.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you report standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k/MAP) on test queries, with confidence intervals, and compare to stronger baselines (e.g., supervised random walks, learning-to-rank on the same features)?
2. How does wall-clock time and memory footprint compare between your GBN/GFN and GBP across varying graph sizes and accuracies? Please provide plots showing the trade-off between lower-level accuracy (N, N1, N2), upper-level convergence, and time.
3. How is β1 defined and bounded in practice? Are your complexity bounds numerically meaningful on the provided dataset?
4. The gradient-free method assumes convexity on Φ. How is Φ chosen in practice to satisfy this assumption? What happens if the assumption fails?
5. For derivative computation, what is the computational and memory complexity in terms of |V|, |E|, and m? How does this scale on graphs larger than those used here?
6. Please clarify the GBP baseline configuration: what exactly are “step size (50, 100, 200, 500)” and “raising to the power 100”? Also, is the stopping criterion sign correct?

Pros
- Addresses an important problem; clear two-level formulation with inexact oracle.
- Provides convergence guarantees with explicit error propagation from lower to upper level.
- Uses a faster PageRank routine and adapts it to derivative estimation.
- Some empirical improvement in optimization loss.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established inexact-oracle and zero- / first-order frameworks.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: small scale, limited baselines, lack of standard ranking metrics and runtime/memory comparisons.
- Several assumptions (local convexity) and constants are not practically grounded.
- Presentation and references need significant polish.

Overall assessment
While the paper’s goal—bringing rigor to supervised PageRank learning with inexact lower-level computations—is worthwhile, the methodological contributions appear incremental, and the empirical validation does not meet NeurIPS standards for demonstrating significance and robustness. Given the emphasis on established methods and rigorous evidence, I am not convinced that the paper advances the state of the art sufficiently in either theory or practice.

Recommendation: Reject

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen empirical evaluation substantially: larger datasets, strong baselines, ranking metrics, runtime/memory, and sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify and tighten theoretical contributions, including explicit constants and comparisons to prior bounds.
- Discuss and empirically probe the convexity assumption and its practical implications.
- Improve clarity, fix references, and provide more implementation details for reproducibility.
