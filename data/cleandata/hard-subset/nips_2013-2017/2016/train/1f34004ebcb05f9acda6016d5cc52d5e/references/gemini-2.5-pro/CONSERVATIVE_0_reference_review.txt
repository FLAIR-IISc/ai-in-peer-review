This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of a Supervised PageRank model. The problem is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization task. A key difficulty is that the objective function and its gradient, which depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov process, cannot be computed exactly. The authors propose to treat this as an optimization problem with an inexact oracle. They introduce two two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—and provide rigorous proofs of their convergence rates, a feature lacking in the previous state-of-the-art method for this problem. The lower level of their algorithms uses an established method to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives to a desired accuracy. The upper level uses this inexact information within either an adaptive gradient method or a random gradient-free search. Experiments on a large-scale web ranking dataset show that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free variant, achieve a lower final loss value than the baseline approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its careful and theoretically sound approach to the optimization problem. The authors correctly identify that prior work lacked convergence guarantees due to the use of an uncontrolled approximation of the stationary distribution. By formally modeling this as a problem with an inexact oracle and providing algorithms with explicit convergence rate guarantees, the paper places the learning of Supervised PageRank on a much firmer theoretical footing. This is a significant contribution.

2.  **Soundness of Approach:** The choice of components for the proposed framework is well-justified. Using the Nesterov-Nemirovski method for calculating the stationary distribution provides the necessary accuracy control for the inexact oracle. The adaptation of recent optimization frameworks for non-convex (gradient-based) and convex (gradient-free) problems to handle these controlled errors is a solid piece of technical work.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The algorithms are evaluated on a real-world, large-scale dataset from a commercial search engine, which adds practical relevance to the theoretical findings. The comparison is made against the appropriate prior art [21], and the results in Table 1 suggest the new methods are effective at finding solutions with lower objective function values.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Mismatch Between Theory and Practice for the Gradient-Free Method:** The gradient-free method (GFN) is shown to be the best-performing algorithm in the experiments. However, the accompanying theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) is for convex functions. The paper's problem formulation is explicitly non-convex. The authors attempt to justify this by assuming that the optimization occurs within a convex region around a local minimum. This is a strong assumption that is not verified and may not hold in practice, which significantly weakens the theoretical claims for the paper's most successful method. While local convexity is a common assumption, it requires more justification in a paper whose central claim is providing rigorous guarantees.

2.  **Marginal Empirical Gains for the Gradient-Based Method:** The gradient-based method (GBN), which has a more appropriate non-convex convergence guarantee, shows less impressive empirical results. According to Table 1, its performance is often only slightly better than the baseline gradient-based method (GBP), and on the Q32 set, the baseline with a step size of 500 achieves a comparable loss. Given the increased complexity of the proposed adaptive method, the practical benefit over a simpler, well-tuned gradient descent seems modest. The experiments could be strengthened by including a comparison against more robust, standard baselines like Adam, which are also heuristic but often perform very well in practice.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is based entirely on the value of the loss function. While this is the direct target of the optimization, it is not a standard measure of ranking quality. Including metrics like NDCG or MAP would provide a clearer picture of whether the reduction in the loss function translates to a meaningful improvement in the ultimate ranking task. Without this, it is difficult to assess the true significance of the empirical results.

4.  **Practical Justification:** The paper is motivated by the baseline's lack of a convergence guarantee. However, the experiments do not demonstrate a clear failure mode for the baseline; it converges to a solution that is only moderately worse than those found by the new methods. This raises the question of the practical necessity of the proposed, more complex algorithms. An established, simple method like power iteration combined with gradient descent is often sufficient. The paper does not make a compelling case that the theoretical deficiencies of the baseline lead to significant practical problems that only their new methods can solve.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, a valuable theoretical contribution.
    *   The work is methodologically sound, correctly framing the problem as optimization with an inexact oracle and applying appropriate techniques.
    *   The proposed methods are shown to find better solutions (in terms of loss) than the previous state-of-the-art on a real-world dataset.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   A significant mismatch exists between the convexity assumption in the theory for the best-performing method (GFN) and the non-convex nature of the problem.
    *   The empirical advantage of the theoretically sound non-convex method (GBN) over the simpler baseline is marginal.
    *   The practical impact is not fully demonstrated, as the baseline does not fail catastrophically and evaluation is limited to the loss function, not standard ranking metrics.

Overall, this paper presents a solid piece of theoretical work that improves our understanding of how to solve the Supervised PageRank learning problem rigorously. However, its practical contributions are less convincing. The gap between the theory and the empirical results for the gradient-free method is a notable weakness. While I value the emphasis on methodological soundness, the evidence presented does not fully justify the adoption of these more complex methods over simpler, established approaches.
