

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank, a framework for ranking nodes in graphs that incorporates node and edge features. The key contribution is providing the first theoretical convergence guarantees for this important problem, which previously lacked such guarantees in the state-of-the-art method [21].

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by addressing a fundamental gap in the Supervised PageRank literature. While prior work [21] used a two-level approach without convergence proofs, the authors introduce rigorous theoretical guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods. They develop a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, which they adapt to the specific challenges of Supervised PageRank learning. The gradient-based method provides convergence guarantees without requiring convexity, while the gradient-free method avoids expensive derivative calculations for large matrices.

The theoretical analysis is strong, with careful attention to the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity. The paper provides detailed complexity bounds for both algorithms, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21], with statistically significant performance gains across multiple query sets.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel approach to handling the inexact oracle problem in Supervised PageRank learning. By adapting gradient-free methods [16] to constrained optimization with inexact oracles and developing a new gradient method for non-convex problems, the authors provide a comprehensive solution to a long-standing problem in graph-based ranking. The theoretical contributions are significant and not merely incremental - they provide the first convergence guarantees for this important class of problems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation throughout. The supplementary materials contain the detailed proofs, which adds credibility to the theoretical claims. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about the dataset (Yandex user browsing graphs) and evaluation metrics, making the results reproducible.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of graph-based ranking and optimization. The combination of theoretical rigor with practical applicability is particularly compelling. The authors successfully address a fundamental gap in the literature while providing methods that outperform existing approaches.

The paper's significance is clear: it provides the first theoretically guaranteed methods for learning Supervised PageRank, which has direct applications in search engine ranking. The methods could also be adapted to other graph-based learning problems, potentially having broader impact beyond the specific application studied here.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The gradient-free method analysis assumes local convexity around the minimum, though the problem is non-convex in general (though this is a common assumption in optimization).
2. The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (Yandex), though the results are statistically significant across multiple query subsets.
3. The paper could have provided more discussion on how the proposed methods compare to other optimization approaches that might be applicable to this problem.

However, these are minor limitations that do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution. The theoretical novelty is significant, and the practical results demonstrate clear advantages over the state-of-the-art.

In terms of originality, this paper stands out by providing the first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning, which is a major step beyond previous work. The combination of two-level optimization with inexact oracles is novel and addresses a critical gap in the literature.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it combines theoretical innovation with practical application in a relevant machine learning domain. The problem is important, the solution is novel, and the results are significant.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field by providing theoretically guaranteed methods for a problem that previously lacked such guarantees, and their experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages. This work represents a significant step forward in the area of graph-based ranking and optimization with inexact oracles.

**Summary of strengths:**
- First theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Novel two-level optimization framework for inexact oracles
- Rigorous complexity analysis with practical trade-off insights
- Clear experimental demonstration of superiority over state-of-the-art
- Theoretical framework that could be applied to other problems

**Summary of weaknesses:**
- Limited to one dataset (though results are statistically significant)
- Local convexity assumption for gradient-free method
- Some technical depth that might limit accessibility to practitioners

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field with significant theoretical and practical advances. The novelty and significance of the work outweigh the minor limitations, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
