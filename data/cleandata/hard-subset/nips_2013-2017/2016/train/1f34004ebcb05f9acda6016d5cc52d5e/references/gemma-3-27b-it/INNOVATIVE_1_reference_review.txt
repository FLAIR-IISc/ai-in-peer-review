This paper tackles the problem of Supervised PageRank, focusing on the optimization challenges inherent in learning the node and edge weights. The core idea – developing and analyzing two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) with convergence guarantees – is a strong one. The authors correctly identify a gap in the existing literature regarding convergence proofs for these types of algorithms and attempt to address it. The connection to existing PageRank literature and the motivation behind improving upon the existing Supervised PageRank approach are clearly articulated.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical contributions. The authors provide convergence rate analysis for both proposed methods, a significant advancement over the existing state-of-the-art. The detailed complexity analysis, investigating the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational cost, is valuable. The inclusion of both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches expands the toolkit available for addressing this problem.  Furthermore, the experimental validation on a real-world web ranking task demonstrates practical improvements over the baseline.  The thoroughness in analyzing the algorithms (Lemmas 1 & 2, Theorems 1-4) and their complexities is commendable. 

However, the paper also exhibits several weaknesses. While theoretically sound, the presentation is extremely dense and difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the proofs, though presumably correct, are relegated to supplementary materials making a quick assessment of their validity difficult. The experimental section, while showing improvements, feels somewhat limited.  More extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with a wider range of hyperparameter settings would strengthen the claims. The gains over the existing method, while statistically significant, appear modest in some cases (Table 1), raising the question of whether the theoretical advancements fully translate into practical benefits. The justification for choosing the specific prox-function `d(ϕ) = 1/2||ϕ||^2` in the gradient-based method section is minimal; a more detailed discussion of its impact would be helpful. Additionally, the discussion surrounding the choice of the Lipschitz constant 'L' and its impact on performance feels somewhat underdeveloped. The leap from the complexity analysis to a concrete estimate of runtime for realistic problem sizes is missing.  Finally, the paper lacks a discussion of potential failure modes or limitations of the proposed methods. For example, what happens when the graph is disconnected or highly sparse?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents novel optimization algorithms with convergence guarantees for a challenging problem in graph ranking.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and complexity bounds, is a significant contribution.
*   **Practical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements over existing methods.
*   **Addresses a Gap:** The work directly addresses a gap in the literature regarding convergence proofs for Supervised PageRank algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to read and understand due to its density and heavy notation.
*   **Modest Gains:** The experimental improvements, while statistically significant, are sometimes modest.
*   **Limited Evaluation:** The experimental section lacks extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and hyperparameter settings.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:**  The paper fails to adequately discuss potential limitations and failure modes.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in clarity and experimental scope, I lean towards **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contributions are substantial and address an important open problem in the field. The convergence guarantees and complexity analysis are genuinely valuable. While the paper could benefit from a more accessible presentation and more extensive experimentation, the novelty and theoretical depth are compelling.  I believe the core ideas presented here have the potential to significantly impact future research in graph ranking and optimization.  I recommend the authors focus on improving the clarity and presentation of the paper, particularly by expanding the discussion of experimental results and limitations, if given the opportunity for revision.
