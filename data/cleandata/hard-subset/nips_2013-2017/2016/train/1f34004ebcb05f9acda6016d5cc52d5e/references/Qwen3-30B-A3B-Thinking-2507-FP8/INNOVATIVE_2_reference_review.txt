

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on graph-based ranking algorithms by introducing two novel two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors identify that while Supervised PageRank [21] has been widely used for learning node rankings in graphs, its existing optimization approach lacks convergence proofs - a critical limitation for a method that is already being applied in practical settings like search engines.

The main novelty of this work lies in developing a theoretical framework for two-level optimization methods that can handle the specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank problem: non-convexity, constraints, and the inexactness of the oracle (since exact calculation of the stationary distribution and its derivatives is computationally intractable). The paper makes two key contributions: a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, and a gradient-free method adapted for constrained optimization with inexact zero-order oracle. Crucially, the authors provide theoretical analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is a significant practical consideration for large-scale graph problems.

The paper is well-structured and the theoretical development is rigorous. The authors build on recent advances in optimization theory [7, 16] but extend them specifically to the inexact oracle setting required by the Supervised PageRank problem. Their approach to handling the inexact oracle through carefully designed lower-level algorithms (using the linearly convergent method [17] for stationary distribution approximation) is particularly elegant. The experimental validation on a real-world web ranking task demonstrates clear improvements over the state-of-the-art [21], with statistically significant gains in ranking quality across multiple test sets.

This paper stands out from typical incremental optimization work because it identifies a fundamental limitation in an existing approach (lack of convergence guarantees) and provides a principled solution that combines theoretical rigor with practical applicability. The authors have successfully bridged a gap between theoretical optimization and a concrete, important application domain. The two-level framework they propose could potentially be applied to other problems with similar inexact oracle structures.

The work is well-positioned to be impactful for both the optimization and information retrieval communities. While the theoretical results are somewhat technical, the authors have clearly articulated the practical implications (e.g., the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and computational complexity), making the work accessible to practitioners. The experimental results are convincing and the comparison to the state-of-the-art is thorough.

One minor limitation is that the paper doesn't explicitly connect to recent NIPS work on graph neural networks or other modern ranking approaches, though the focus on the specific Supervised PageRank framework is justified given the problem statement. The authors could strengthen the significance argument by more clearly explaining why the specific Supervised PageRank framework is still relevant despite the rise of deep learning approaches for ranking.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first theoretically grounded optimization approach for Supervised PageRank learning. The novelty of the two-level framework with convergence guarantees, combined with strong experimental results, makes this work stand out from typical incremental optimization papers. The authors have identified a real limitation in existing practice and provided a principled solution with both theoretical and empirical validation.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the two-level optimization framework with convergence guarantees for this specific problem is substantial, and the practical impact of the approach (improved ranking quality with theoretical guarantees) is compelling. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound, clearly written, significantly advances the state of the art, and addresses an important problem with a bold new approach. The minor limitations noted do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the field.
