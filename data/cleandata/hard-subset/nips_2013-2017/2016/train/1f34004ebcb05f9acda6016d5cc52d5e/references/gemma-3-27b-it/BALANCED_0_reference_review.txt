This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, focusing on optimization methods for the involved non-convex loss minimization. The authors propose two two-level methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, aiming to address the convergence issues of the state-of-the-art method [21] and improve ranking quality. They provide convergence guarantees for both methods and analyze the trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity. The empirical evaluation demonstrates improved performance compared to the existing approach.

The core idea – improving the optimization landscape for Supervised PageRank – is valuable and builds upon prior work in random walk-based ranking algorithms [18, 11, 8, 9, 19] and their extensions incorporating node/edge features [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21]. The explicit focus on convergence guarantees, a weakness of prior methods [21], is a strong positive aspect. The paper carefully details the complexities of calculating the loss function and its gradients, and the proposed methods attempt to address these challenges. The connection to existing optimization methods like those in [16, 7, 25] is also well-established. The experimental validation on a real-world web ranking dataset is a key strength.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The presentation is often dense and difficult to follow. The notation is heavy and requires significant effort to unpack. While the authors claim to address weaknesses of existing methods, the improvements in experimental results are not always dramatically significant and require further analysis. The analysis of the complexity tradeoffs, while present, is not presented in a highly accessible manner. 

More specifically:

* **Clarity:** The paper is overly verbose and the mathematical notation is dense. Equations are often presented without sufficient explanation. For instance, the numerous definitions in Section 2 (Eqs. 2.1-2.5) are presented rapidly without enough intuitive explanation. A more gradual introduction of concepts and clear definitions would significantly improve readability.
* **Technical Soundness:** While the authors claim convergence guarantees, the proofs are largely relegated to Supplementary Materials. This makes it difficult to assess the validity of the claims within the core paper. The assumptions made for Theorem 1, 3, and 4 need to be explicitly stated and their relevance to the specific problem of Supervised PageRank discussed. The conditions relating to the convexity of f(ϕ) on Φ are crucial but need stronger justification.
* **Originality:** The core idea of applying gradient and gradient-free methods to Supervised PageRank isn’t entirely novel, but the specific convergence analysis and the detailed analysis of computational complexity are valuable contributions. However, the paper doesn't always sufficiently highlight the *unique* aspects of its contributions relative to the broader optimization literature. The connection to Nesterov’s work [24, 25] is good, but could be expanded.
* **Significance:** The reported performance improvements are modest. While statistically significant, the practical impact of these improvements needs further discussion. It's unclear how much improvement in ranking quality translates to a tangible benefit for users. Exploring the sensitivity of the methods to different parameter settings and dataset variations would strengthen the significance of the findings. The fact that the improvements are most significant on Q12 warrants a deeper investigation.  Why does GBN outperform GBP only on that subset?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in graph ranking.
* Provides convergence guarantees for proposed algorithms, addressing a key limitation of previous work.
* Demonstrates improved performance on a real-world dataset, even if the improvements are modest.
* Detailed analysis of computational complexity and accuracy trade-offs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper is difficult to read due to dense notation and verbose presentation.
* The mathematical analysis is largely in supplementary materials, hindering assessment of technical soundness.
* The performance improvements, while statistically significant, are not always substantial.
* Limited discussion of the practical implications of the improvements.



**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Improve Clarity:** Simplify the notation, provide more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts, and restructure the presentation for better flow.
* **Strengthen Technical Validation:** Provide a high-level summary of the key proofs in the main paper, or at least highlight the core ideas and assumptions.
* **Enhance Experimental Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough analysis of the experimental results, including sensitivity analysis, ablation studies, and a discussion of the practical implications of the improvements. Consider visualizing the ranking results beyond just the loss function values.
* **Address Limitations:** Acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the proposed methods and potential avenues for future research.
* **Expand Related Work:** Provide a more comprehensive overview of the related work in optimization, particularly highlighting the unique aspects of the proposed approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Supervised PageRank optimization, the clarity issues and modest performance improvements prevent acceptance in its current form. Addressing the suggested improvements could significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to NIPS.
