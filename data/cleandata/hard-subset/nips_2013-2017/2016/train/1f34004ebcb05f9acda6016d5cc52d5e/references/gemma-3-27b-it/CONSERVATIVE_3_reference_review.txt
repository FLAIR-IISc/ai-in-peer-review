This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors address a known issue with existing methods – lack of convergence proof – and aim to improve ranking quality. While the problem statement is reasonable, and the attempt to provide theoretical foundations is commendable, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling novelty and a somewhat inflated assessment of its contributions.

The core idea – applying existing optimization techniques (gradient descent and a coordinate descent-like approach) to the Supervised PageRank problem with an inexact oracle – feels incremental. The authors correctly identify the challenges posed by the non-convexity and constraints, and the inexactness of the lower-level oracle. However, the adaptation of established optimization methods doesn’t represent a breakthrough. The theoretical results, while mathematically sound (though the proofs are relegated to supplementary materials, hindering thorough evaluation), largely consist of convergence rate analyses for well-known algorithms *applied* to this specific problem, rather than fundamentally new algorithmic innovations. The focus on bounding the complexity and trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost is positive, but again, not entirely novel in the optimization literature.

A significant weakness is the somewhat disconnected presentation. The paper jumps between describing the model, the numerical calculations, the optimization methods, and the experimental setup, making it difficult to follow the overall narrative. The introduction promises contributions beyond convergence guarantees – "investigating the trade-off" and "estimating the complexity" – but these feel somewhat buried in the technical details and lack clear, insightful conclusions. 

The experimental evaluation is also unconvincing. The use of a single dataset (Yandex) and a limited comparison to the existing state-of-the-art (GBP) is insufficient to strongly support the claim of “outperforming” the prior work. The experimental setup is described, but details about hyperparameter tuning (beyond mentioning a range of values for L in GBN) are lacking, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison. The statement that differences in loss are less than 10^-7 for different ε is a trivial observation. More importantly, the performance gains, while reported, are not dramatic enough to justify the complexity of the proposed methods, especially given the established baseline. The assertion that the power method is “less efficient” than the method in [17] is also not clearly substantiated with sufficiently detailed comparisons.

**Strengths:**

*   Theoretical convergence guarantees for both proposed methods.
*   Analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity.
*   Addresses a valid problem – the lack of theoretical grounding for Supervised PageRank optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – largely an application of existing optimization techniques.
*   Incremental improvements – the performance gains are not substantial.
*   Unclear presentation – disjointed flow of ideas.
*   Insufficient experimental evaluation – limited dataset, hyperparameter details lacking, and comparison to a single baseline.
*   Overstated claims – the paper sometimes overstates the significance of its contributions.
*   Proofs in Supplementary Materials – hinders review.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical justification for a commonly used technique (Supervised PageRank optimization).
*   The analysis of convergence rates and complexity is potentially useful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks substantial novelty to warrant publication at NIPS.
*   The experimental results are not compelling enough to justify the theoretical work.
*   The presentation could be significantly improved for clarity.
*   The reliance on supplementary materials for crucial proofs makes a thorough evaluation difficult.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the work is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, it lacks the originality and significance expected of a NIPS publication. The incremental nature of the contributions, combined with the limited experimental evaluation and somewhat unclear presentation, do not justify acceptance. The authors would benefit from focusing on more novel algorithmic developments or providing a more comprehensive and convincing experimental validation of their methods. A more detailed comparison to other relevant baselines and a more thorough ablation study would also strengthen the paper.
