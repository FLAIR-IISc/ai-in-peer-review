This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model where node and edge features are used to define the transition and restart probabilities of a random walk. The learning task is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization problem. The authors correctly identify a key weakness in the prior state-of-the-art method [21]: while it uses a two-level gradient descent approach, it relies on inexact estimations of the stationary distribution (PageRank vector) and its gradient via the power method, and consequently lacks any formal convergence guarantees.

The central contribution of this work is to place the optimization of Supervised PageRank on a solid theoretical foundation. The authors propose two new two-level algorithms, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both designed to explicitly handle the inexactness of the oracle (i.e., the inability to compute the loss function and its gradient exactly). For the lower-level oracle, they replace the standard power method with a more efficient, linearly convergent method from [17]. The main novelty lies in the upper-level optimization:
1.  A gradient-based method is developed for general constrained non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle, providing convergence rate guarantees to a stationary point. This adapts the framework of [7] to handle constraints and a deterministically bounded error.
2.  A random gradient-free method is developed for constrained convex problems where the function value is computed with known accuracy. This extends the work of [16] to a constrained setting and provides a valuable alternative when gradients are too expensive to compute.

For both proposed methods, the authors provide a full complexity analysis that elegantly connects the required accuracy of the lower-level oracle to the overall number of iterations needed for the upper-level algorithm to converge. This trade-off analysis is itself a valuable contribution. Experiments on a real-world web ranking dataset show that both proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength is its novelty and theoretical significance. It addresses a critical and often overlooked gap in machine learning on graphs: many learning problems involving equilibrium states (like PageRank) rely on iterative solvers, leading to inexact oracles for the outer learning loop. Prior work often ignored this, using standard optimization methods heuristically without guarantees. This paper provides the *first provably convergent algorithms* for the Supervised PageRank problem. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a foundational contribution that adds much-needed rigor.

The introduction of a theoretically-grounded, gradient-free optimization method for this problem is particularly bold and interesting. In many complex graph models, computing gradients can be prohibitively expensive or complex. Showing that a zeroth-order method can be adapted to this setting with formal guarantees opens up a promising new direction for related problems.

The technical quality of the work is high. The paper successfully bridges the gap between the theory of optimization with inexact oracles and a practical, important application in web search. The analyses are thorough, culminating in explicit complexity bounds that are of both theoretical and practical interest.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness lies in the theoretical assumptions and experimental validation. The convergence guarantee for the gradient-free method requires a local convexity assumption on the objective function. While the authors are transparent about this, the overall problem is non-convex, which limits the scope of this specific theoretical result. However, the proposed gradient-based method does not require convexity and applies to the general problem, which mitigates this concern significantly.

The experimental evaluation, while showing positive results, is conducted on a single, proprietary dataset from a commercial search engine. This makes the results difficult to reproduce and raises questions about their generalizability to other types of graphs or ranking tasks. While I appreciate the use of real-world data, validation on one or two public benchmark datasets (e.g., from LETOR) would have substantially strengthened the paper's empirical claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for a well-known and important learning problem. This is a significant conceptual leap over prior heuristic approaches.
*   **Novel Framework:** The work presents a principled framework for combining iterative solvers (as inexact oracles) with modern optimization theory. This idea is broadly applicable beyond PageRank to other learning problems involving equilibrium or fixed-point computations.
*   **Bold Approach:** The successful application of a gradient-free method with convergence guarantees to a problem of this nature is novel and opens a new avenue for research.
*   **Strong Theory:** The theoretical contributions are solid, providing convergence rates and full complexity analyses.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The empirical validation on a single, proprietary dataset weakens the claims of practical superiority.
*   **Restricive Assumption:** The local convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's analysis is a theoretical limitation.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contribution of providing the first provably convergent methods for learning Supervised PageRank is highly significant and novel. It moves the field from heuristic approaches to a principled and rigorous optimization framework. The introduction of both gradient-based (for the general non-convex case) and gradient-free alternatives is a major strength. While the experimental validation could be broader, the demonstrated improvement over the prior state-of-the-art on a large-scale, real-world task is compelling. This work prioritizes a significant new direction—rigorous optimization with inexact oracles for graph learning—over perfecting every methodological detail, and for that, it should be commended. It is a paper that others in the field are likely to build upon.
