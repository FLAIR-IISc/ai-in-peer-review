This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, addressing the lack of convergence guarantees in existing approaches. The authors present a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method, both leveraging a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. They provide convergence rate analysis for both methods and demonstrate improved ranking quality in experiments compared to a state-of-the-art baseline.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to Supervised PageRank and its limitations, motivating the need for new optimization techniques. The model description (Section 2) is comprehensive, defining the random walk process and the loss function to be minimized. Sections 3 and 4 detail the numerical calculation of the loss function and its gradient, and the gradient-free optimization methods respectively, with accompanying lemmas and theorems establishing convergence properties and computational complexity. Section 5 extends this to gradient-based methods. The experimental setup and results are presented in Section 6.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** A key strength is the provision of convergence rate analyses for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a significant contribution, addressing a major shortcoming of previous work in this area. The lemmas and theorems establishing these guarantees are well-defined and appear rigorous.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the Supervised PageRank problem and the associated challenges, providing sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the area.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper offers a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed methods, considering the trade-offs between accuracy and complexity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach on a real-world dataset.
* **Inexact Oracle Handling:** The framework for handling inexact oracles is a valuable contribution, making the methods more practical for large-scale problems where exact computations are infeasible.
* **Detailed Lemmas and Theorems**:  The numerous lemmas and theorems with proofs (mostly in the supplementary material) support the claims made and add to the paper's rigor.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Overhead:** The paper employs a significant number of symbols and notations, which can be overwhelming for readers. While generally well-defined, the density of notation hinders readability.
* **Limited Novelty in Core Ideas:** While the combination of techniques and the theoretical guarantees are valuable, the underlying concepts (power method, gradient descent, random gradient-free optimization) are well-established. The novelty lies more in the application and analysis within the specific context of Supervised PageRank and inexact oracles.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments primarily focus on a single dataset from Yandex. Expanding the evaluation to other datasets or ranking tasks would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
* **Comparison Detail:** While the paper states the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art, the presentation of experimental results could be more detailed.  Providing more granular results and error bars would enhance the clarity and reproducibility of the findings. The reasoning for choosing specific hyperparameter values (e.g., L = 10^-4) is also not fully explained.
* **Supplementary Material Reliance**: Much of the crucial proof details are relegated to the supplementary materials. This makes a full assessment challenging without dedicating significant time to reviewing the supplement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in ranking with theoretical guarantees, a significant improvement over existing methods.
* The proposed methods demonstrate improved performance in experiments.
* The framework for handling inexact oracles has broader applicability beyond Supervised PageRank.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by lemmas and theorems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core ideas are not fundamentally novel, relying on well-established optimization techniques.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The notational complexity and reliance on supplementary materials may hinder accessibility and understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, and the framework for handling inexact oracles is a valuable addition. While the novelty is incremental, the paper’s rigor and practical implications make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on improving readability by streamlining the notation and providing more detailed experimental results, as well as better integrating key proofs into the main body of the paper where feasible.
