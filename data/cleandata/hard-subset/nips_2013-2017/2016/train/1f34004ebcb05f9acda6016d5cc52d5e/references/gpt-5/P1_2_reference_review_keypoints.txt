- Proposes two two-level methods to learn Supervised PageRank parameters that explicitly handle inexact lower-level Markov chain computations.  
- Replaces the power method with a Nesterov–Nemirovski-style accelerated averaging to approximate the stationary distribution π and its derivatives with provable accuracy and complexity bounds.  
- Develops a constrained randomized gradient-free upper-level method that tolerates uniformly bounded errors in function evaluations.  
- Proposes an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz-like constant.  
- Derives convergence theorems and end-to-end complexity bounds that couple lower-level accuracy with upper-level iteration complexity.  
- Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset show both methods reduce training loss more than the prior gradient-based Supervised PageRank baseline, with the gradient-free method avoiding derivatives.  
- Situates work relative to prior Supervised PageRank and supervised random-walk literature and adapts known randomized zero-order and inexact-oracle first-order optimization techniques to constrained and noisy settings.  
- Extends accelerated averaging methods to derivative estimation with complexity guarantees.  
- Theoretical strengths: nontrivial error and complexity bounds for approximating f(ϕ) and ∇f(ϕ), a bounded-noise constrained gradient-free method, and an adaptive inexact-oracle projected gradient method with stopping criteria.  
- Two-level complexity analysis explicitly trades off lower-level accuracy and upper-level convergence, which is practically relevant.  
- Empirical weakness: evaluation limited to a surrogate pairwise squared loss without standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k) on held-out queries.  
- Baselines are narrow, missing strong learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART, neural rankers), other supervised random-walk variants, and ablation studies isolating estimator vs optimizer effects.  
- Theoretical-practical gap: convexity assumptions for the gradient-free theorem are not reconciled with the actual nonconvex problem and no empirical landscape diagnostics or safeguards are provided.  
- Stationarity measure returned by the nonconvex method (‖Mk(xk−xk+1)‖) is not clearly tied to standard constrained stationarity notions.  
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for critical constants, proofs, and algorithmic details; some constants and practical magnitudes are opaque in the main text.  
- Presentation issues: typos, inconsistent norm usage, notational overload, and incomplete references hinder readability.  
- Requests to improve clarity: define constants in main text, include pseudocode for two-level algorithms in the main paper, unify norm choices, and clarify the stationarity measure.  
- Originality: novel integration of bounded-noise gradient-free and adaptive inexact-oracle first-order methods into a two-level Supervised PageRank learning framework.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for large-graph learning where exact gradients are costly, but practical impact is not convincingly demonstrated due to weak empirical evaluation.  
- Pros: solid theoretical advances in inexact-oracle optimization, careful lower-level approximation analysis, and real-world dataset experiments showing loss improvement.  
- Cons: missing ranking metrics and broader baselines, unverified assumptions and stationarity criteria, and presentation/replicability shortcomings.  
- Authors are asked to report standard ranking metrics with statistical tests, sensitivity to α and Φ, practical τ and δ choices, clarify stationarity relation to projected gradient, and provide wall-clock time and lower-level iteration counts with ablations.  
- Reproducibility: moderate — proprietary dataset limits reproducibility; code, synthetic data, and detailed hyperparameters should be released and stopping rules/practical constants clarified.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject — strong theoretical contributions but insufficient empirical evidence and presentation issues prevent acceptance in current form.
