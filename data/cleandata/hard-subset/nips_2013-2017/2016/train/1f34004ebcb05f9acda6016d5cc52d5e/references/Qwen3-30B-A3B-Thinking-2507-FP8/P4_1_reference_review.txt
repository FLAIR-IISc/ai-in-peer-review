

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods for learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a framework for node ranking in graphs that incorporates node and edge features. The authors address the key limitation of previous work [21], which lacked convergence guarantees for its gradient-based method due to the inexact oracle problem arising from approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The paper introduces a gradient-based method that provides theoretical convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, and a gradient-free method adapted for constrained optimization with inexact oracle. The authors prove convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods, and demonstrate their superiority over the state-of-the-art method [21] on a real-world web page ranking task.

The work relates directly to previous NIPS contributions on optimization methods (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated methods [16, 7, 25]) and graph-based ranking algorithms (e.g., PageRank [18], HITS [11], Supervised PageRank [21]). The authors clearly identify the gap in existing work (lack of convergence guarantees) and position their contribution as addressing this gap through the development of methods specifically designed for the inexact oracle setting in Supervised PageRank.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence proofs and complexity bounds for both optimization methods. The authors carefully address the challenges of the inexact oracle setting by developing novel adaptations of existing optimization frameworks. The proofs in the supplementary materials appear sound and well-structured.

2. **Novelty:** The paper makes a clear contribution by adapting gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to the specific inexact oracle problem in Supervised PageRank. The two-level approach (with the lower level solving the stationary distribution approximation and the upper level performing optimization) represents a novel framework for this problem. The authors properly contextualize their work against related contributions in the literature.

3. **Experimental validation:** The experiments on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of ranking quality. The paper provides sufficient experimental details, including dataset description, evaluation metrics, and comparison with a baseline (untuned Supervised PageRank).

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow from problem statement to methods to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The supplementary materials provide necessary details for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited comparison with recent work:** While the paper compares with [21], it does not discuss how the proposed methods relate to or compare with more recent optimization approaches for similar problems. The authors should explicitly address whether their inexact oracle framework could be applied to other graph-based learning problems.

2. **Quantification of practical gains:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art, but the magnitude of improvement is not clearly quantified. For instance, the paper states "GFN significantly outperforms" but does not provide specific percentage improvements or statistical significance beyond p-values.

3. **Scalability discussion:** The paper provides complexity bounds, but doesn't thoroughly discuss how the methods scale with extremely large graphs (beyond the experimental dataset size), which is particularly relevant for web ranking applications.

4. **Parameter sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the choice of L in the gradient-free method influences the output (referencing supplementary Figure 2), but doesn't provide guidance on how to select optimal hyperparameters for different problem sizes.

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature for graph-based ranking problems by providing the first theoretical guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The two-level approach with inexact oracle handling is novel and well-motivated. The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical improvements. While the paper could strengthen its contribution by better contextualizing the work against more recent optimization methods and providing clearer quantification of practical gains, these are minor issues given the paper's strong theoretical foundation and experimental validation.

**Pro arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides the first theoretical guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank with convergence rates and complexity bounds
- Introduces two novel optimization methods specifically designed for the inexact oracle problem in Supervised PageRank
- Demonstrates clear experimental improvements over the state-of-the-art
- Well-structured and clearly written with sufficient technical detail

**Con arguments for acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with more recent optimization methods for similar problems
- Quantification of practical gains could be more explicit
- Limited discussion of scalability for extremely large graphs

The paper addresses a significant problem in graph-based learning with theoretically sound methods that have practical value in real-world ranking applications. It makes a clear contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, which aligns with NIPS scope. The quality of the technical content, clarity of presentation, and significance of the problem addressed strongly support acceptance.
