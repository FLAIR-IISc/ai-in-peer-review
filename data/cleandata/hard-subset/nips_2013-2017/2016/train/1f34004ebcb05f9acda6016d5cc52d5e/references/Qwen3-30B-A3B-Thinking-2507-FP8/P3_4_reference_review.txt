

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in the Supervised PageRank framework by proposing two two-level optimization methods with rigorous convergence guarantees. The authors recognize that existing methods (e.g., [21]) lack theoretical convergence guarantees due to the use of imprecise estimates of the stationary distribution and its derivatives. They develop both gradient-based and gradient-free methods that work with an "inexact oracle" - a framework where the loss function and its gradient are approximated with controlled error. The paper provides theoretical convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods, and demonstrates empirically that their approaches outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality.

## Quality
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed proofs for their convergence theorems (Theorems 1-4) and complexity bounds, demonstrating a strong understanding of optimization theory. The complexity analysis (Theorems 2 and 4) is particularly thorough, explicitly connecting lower-level approximation accuracy to overall computational cost.

However, I have concerns about the convexity assumptions. The problem is explicitly stated as non-convex, but Theorem 1 assumes convexity for the gradient-free method. The authors claim their methods handle non-convex problems, yet the convergence guarantees for the gradient-based method (Theorem 3) are only for non-convex problems with a specific structure (f(x) with inexact first-order oracle). This discrepancy needs clarification.

The experimental design is appropriate, using a real-world web ranking dataset from Yandex. The results show consistent improvements over [21] across multiple query sets. However, the paper lacks comparison with other state-of-the-art ranking algorithms beyond [21], making it difficult to assess the relative significance of their improvements.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem formulation, theoretical contributions, and experimental results are logically organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors effectively explain the two-level optimization framework and the role of the inexact oracle.

A minor weakness is that the relationship between the approximation parameters (δ, N) and the final accuracy ε is somewhat abstract. While the theoretical bounds are provided, the practical implications for choosing these parameters could be made more concrete for readers implementing the methods.

## Originality
The core contribution - applying inexact oracle methods to the Supervised PageRank learning problem - is novel. The authors extend existing work on gradient-free optimization ([16]) and adaptive gradient methods ([7]) to handle the specific challenges of graph-based ranking with inexact oracle.

However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach from related work on inexact oracle methods. For instance, the framework for constrained optimization with inexact oracle (Section 4.1) builds on [16] and [25], but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their constrained version differs from these works. The use of the method from [17] for approximating the stationary distribution is also not particularly novel, as it's already employed in [21].

## Significance
The significance of this work lies in providing the first theoretically guaranteed methods for learning Supervised PageRank parameters. This is important for practitioners who need reliable algorithms for web ranking and similar applications. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality.

However, the paper doesn't adequately address scalability to very large graphs. The Yandex dataset used in experiments has only ~11.7K vertices in total, which is modest compared to web-scale graphs. The authors should discuss how their methods would perform on graphs with billions of nodes, which is a major practical concern for ranking applications.

## Strengths
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods
- Comprehensive complexity analysis connecting lower-level approximation accuracy to overall computational cost
- Clear empirical demonstration of outperformance over the state-of-the-art [21]
- Well-structured presentation of the problem and proposed methods

## Weaknesses
- Convexity assumptions in theorems don't align with the stated non-convex problem
- Limited comparison with other state-of-the-art ranking algorithms
- Insufficient discussion of scalability to web-scale graphs
- Loss function choice (square loss) doesn't align with standard ranking evaluation metrics

## Recommendation
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization of graph-based ranking algorithms. While the theoretical framework is sound and the empirical results are promising, the paper would benefit from:

1. Clarifying the relationship between convexity assumptions and the non-convex problem setting
2. Expanding the experimental comparison to include other ranking algorithms
3. Discussing scalability to larger graphs
4. Providing more practical guidance on choosing parameters (δ, N, ε) for real-world applications

The paper addresses a well-motivated problem with a technically sound approach, but needs to better contextualize its contribution within the broader literature on graph optimization and ranking algorithms. With these improvements, it would be a strong candidate for publication at NeurIPS.

**Overall Rating: 7 (Accept)** - The paper makes a valuable contribution with solid theoretical foundations and empirical validation, though it would benefit from addressing the scalability concerns and clarifying the convexity assumptions.
