Summary
The paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The core challenge is that the objective is nonconvex and the loss and gradients can only be computed approximately via Markov chain stationary distributions and their derivatives. The authors propose two two-level methods: (i) a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, they replace the power method with the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent estimator for the stationary distribution and extend it to estimate derivatives, providing accuracy-complexity trade-offs. On the upper level, they develop convergence analyses for constrained optimization with inexact oracles: an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems and a constrained gradient-free method using spherical smoothing. They provide convergence and complexity bounds and report experiments on a proprietary Yandex user-browsing-graph dataset, showing improvements over the prior gradient-based approach [21] in terms of the training loss and reduced iterations.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on supervised random walks/learning link-based ranking (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011; BrowseRank; supervised/nested PageRank) and on optimization with inexact oracles (Nesterov, Spokoiny; Ghadimi & Lan; Devolder et al.). The novelty lies in (a) adapting linearly convergent stationary distribution computation to gradient estimation with error control; (b) extending random gradient-free optimization to constrained, inexact-zero-order settings; and (c) an adaptive projected gradient framework for nonconvex composite problems under an inexact first-order oracle, and tying the oracle accuracy to lower-level iteration counts. The prior state-of-the-art for Supervised PageRank [21] lacked convergence guarantees due to imprecise lower-level computations; this paper aims to close that gap.

Strengths
- Technical: Clear two-level design with rigorous error propagation from lower-level approximations to upper-level optimization. Theorems give explicit dependencies on accuracy, dimension, and Markov chain parameters (α).
- Contributions to optimization: Constrained, inexact-oracle variants of gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods are useful beyond this application. The inexact first-order oracle model (inequality (5.1)) is well-motivated here.
- Lower-level advances: Using the Nesterov–Nemirovski method for stationary distribution estimation and generalizing it for derivative estimation, with provable linear convergence and complexity bounds, is valuable in large graphs.
- Empirical: On the Yandex dataset, both proposed methods outperform the prior approach in the reported loss; the lower-level method converges faster than the power method.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is limited: results are reported in terms of the surrogate pairwise squared loss only; standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP) and wall-clock/runtime comparisons are missing. This weakens practical significance.
- Baselines: No comparisons to broader learning-to-rank or supervised random walk baselines (e.g., LambdaMART, Backstrom & Leskovec’s method), nor ablations isolating the impact of the lower-level method vs. the new upper-level solvers.
- Assumptions: The gradient-free method’s application assumes local convexity of f within Φ, which is strong and unverified. It is unclear when this holds in practice.
- Clarity/reproducibility: Many constants (e.g., β1) and key details are deferred to the supplement; several references are incomplete; there are typos. The derivative estimation recurrence (Πk) would benefit from clearer derivation and implementation guidance. Proprietary data limits reproducibility; no code is provided.
- Fairness of comparisons: The prior method’s lower-level is fixed to 100 power iterations; it’s not clear whether that baseline was tuned for comparable accuracy/time, nor whether stopping tolerances were aligned across methods.

Quality
- The theoretical developments are mostly sound and well-justified. The inexact-oracle analyses are careful about error terms and how δ, τ, N, N1, N2 interact. However, the strength of the claims relies on (5.1) holding with the constructed oracles, which depends on constants defined in the supplement. Without those details in the main text, it’s hard to fully verify.
- Empirical support is suggestive but not comprehensive. Practical efficiency claims would be stronger with time/memory metrics and standard ranking measures.

Clarity
- The high-level structure is clear, but presentation is dense, with heavy notation, some formatting issues, and missing constants/references. Adding pseudocode for the derivative estimator, a summary table of all parameters/complexities, and clearer guidance for setting ε, τ, δ in practice would help.

Originality
- Moderate to good. The adaptation to constrained, inexact-oracle settings and derivative estimation for PageRank are nontrivial. The upper-level algorithms build on known frameworks but extend them in useful ways.

Significance
- Potentially significant for practitioners needing principled learning of random-walk-based rankers with guarantees. The broader optimization contributions are of interest. However, the limited empirical evaluation and lack of public benchmarks reduce immediate impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report NDCG/MAP@k and wall-clock times, and include comparisons to supervised random walks and standard LTR baselines on a public dataset?
- How sensitive are results to α, R, and feature scaling? Provide ablations for N, N1, N2, and tolerances.
- Please clarify and make explicit β1 and all constants needed to instantiate δ, τ, and iteration counts.
- For the gradient-free method, when (empirically) is f convex on Φ? Any diagnostics or guarantees?
- How is the derivative estimation implemented efficiently for large m and p? Any vector-Jacobian tricks?

Pros
- Sound and useful theory for inexact-oracle optimization with constraints.
- Lower-level PageRank and derivative estimation with provable accuracy and complexity.
- Demonstrated improvements over [21] on a real task.

Cons
- Limited and partially unfair empirical evaluation; lack of standard ranking metrics and runtime.
- Strong local convexity assumption for one method; reproducibility concerns.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (6/10), contingent on strengthening experiments and clarifying constants/assumptions.

Confidence: 3/5.
