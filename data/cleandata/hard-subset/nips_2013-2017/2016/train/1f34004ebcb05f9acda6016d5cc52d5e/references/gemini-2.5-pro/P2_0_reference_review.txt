### Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a framework where node and edge features are used to guide a random walk for ranking tasks. The authors identify a key weakness in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: its two-level optimization procedure lacks convergence guarantees because the lower level (calculating the PageRank vector and its derivatives via the power method) is inexact.

The main contribution of this work is to provide two new two-level optimization algorithms with rigorous theoretical guarantees. The core idea is to replace the power method at the lower level with a provably convergent method [17], which provides approximations of the loss function and its gradient with controllable accuracy. This turns the optimization into a problem of minimization with an "inexact oracle." The authors then develop and analyze two upper-level algorithms to handle this setting:
1.  A gradient-free method, adapted from [16] to handle constrained optimization problems, with convergence guarantees for convex loss functions.
2.  A gradient-based method, extending the framework of [7] to constrained non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors provide a full complexity analysis, linking the desired final accuracy of the optimization to the required precision of the inexact oracle at the lower level. Experimental results on a real-world web ranking task show that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach in terms of final loss function value.

**Evaluation**

**Quality: High**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions are the main strength.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The formalization of the Supervised PageRank learning problem as optimization with an inexact oracle is elegant and principled.
    *   The authors provide rigorous convergence proofs for both proposed methods (Theorems 1 and 3) and derive the overall computational complexity (Theorems 2 and 4). This is a significant improvement over the prior work [21], which lacked such guarantees.
    *   The analysis of the trade-off between the lower-level accuracy (number of iterations `N`) and the upper-level convergence is thorough and provides a clear recipe for implementing the algorithms with a desired precision.
    *   The gradient-based method is developed for the general non-convex case, which is appropriate for the loss function in question and makes the result broadly applicable.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the loss function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. The paper acknowledges that the function is generally non-convex but justifies the assumption by considering a small vicinity of a local minimum. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice, and its validity is not empirically verified. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the implications if this assumption is violated.
    *   The experiments are conducted on a proprietary dataset from Yandex. While understandable for industry research, this makes the results non-reproducible, which is a limitation.

**Clarity: Good**

The paper is well-organized and clearly structured. The motivation is well-laid-out in the introduction, followed by the theoretical development and experimental validation.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The problem statement and model description in Section 2 are clear.
    *   The distinction between the lower-level oracle computation and the upper-level optimization is maintained consistently throughout the paper, which helps the reader follow the logic.
*   **Suggestions for Improvement:**
    *   The paper is theoretically dense, with a large amount of notation (e.g., `δ`, `δ1`, `δ2`, `N`, `N1`, `N2`, `M`, `L`, `ε`). A table summarizing the key parameters and their roles might improve readability.
    *   The connection between the required accuracy `ε` in the upper-level algorithms and the resulting oracle precisions (`δ1`, `δ2`) and iteration counts (`N`, `N1`, `N2`) is complex. A diagram illustrating this flow of dependencies could make the overall framework more intuitive.

**Originality: High**

The paper makes several novel contributions.
*   While the components build on existing work (e.g., [7], [16], [17], [21]), their synthesis is novel and impactful. To my knowledge, this is the first work to apply this rigorous inexact oracle framework to the problem of learning Supervised PageRank.
*   The extensions to existing optimization frameworks are valuable contributions in their own right. Specifically, adapting the random gradient-free method of [16] to the constrained setting and developing the adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with this particular inexact first-order oracle are significant.
*   The paper clearly situates its contributions with respect to prior art, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and explaining how the new proposals overcome them. Related work is adequately cited.

**Significance: High**

The results presented in this paper are important for both researchers and practitioners.
*   The problem of optimization with inexact function values or gradients is fundamental and appears in many areas of machine learning beyond ranking (e.g., reinforcement learning, variational inference, large-scale optimization). The proposed methods and analysis are general enough to be of interest to a broad audience.
*   For the specific application of learning to rank, the paper provides algorithms that are not only theoretically sound but also practically superior to the existing method. Improving ranking quality is of paramount importance to search engines, making this a high-impact application.
*   The work advances the state of the art by providing a robust and provably convergent solution to a challenging non-convex optimization problem. Other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Strong and rigorous theoretical contributions with proofs of convergence for two novel optimization algorithms in an inexact oracle setting.
2.  Addresses a clear and important limitation (lack of convergence guarantees) in prior state-of-the-art work.
3.  The proposed methods are shown to be practically effective, outperforming the baseline in experiments.
4.  The problem and methods are of broad interest to the NIPS community, connecting optimization theory with a practical application in information retrieval.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method is a notable theoretical limitation that is not fully justified.
2.  The experiments rely on a proprietary dataset, hindering reproducibility.

**Overall Recommendation:**

The strengths of this paper, particularly its rigorous theoretical analysis and the significance of its contributions to both optimization theory and a key application, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It is a high-quality, original, and impactful piece of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
