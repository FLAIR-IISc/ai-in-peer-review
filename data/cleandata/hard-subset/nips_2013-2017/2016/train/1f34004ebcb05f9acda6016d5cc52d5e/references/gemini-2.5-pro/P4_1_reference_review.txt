### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank where restart and transition probabilities in a random walk are parameterized functions of node and edge features. The learning task is formulated as a non-convex, constrained optimization problem aimed at minimizing a loss function that reflects the agreement between the model's ranking scores and human-provided relevance labels.

The authors identify a key limitation in the state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], which uses a gradient descent algorithm where the stationary distribution and its gradients are approximated via the power method. This approach lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to the use of an inexact, biased oracle for the gradient.

The main contributions of this paper are two novel, two-level optimization algorithms that overcome this limitation. Both methods are built on the principle of an inexact oracle, where the lower level computes approximations of the objective function and/or its gradient with controllable accuracy. For this lower level, the authors employ the method of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] for approximating the stationary distribution, which they show to be efficient and provides the necessary accuracy bounds. They also generalize this method to estimate derivatives.

The two upper-level algorithms are:
1.  A random gradient-free method, which extends the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the objective function value is computed with a known error bound.
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method for general non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle. This method builds upon the work of Devolder et al. [25] and Ghadimi and Lan [7] but extends it to the constrained setting and incorporates an adaptive procedure for the Lipschitz constant.

For both proposed algorithms, the authors provide rigorous theoretical analyses, proving convergence rates and deriving the total computational complexity required to solve the problem to a specified accuracy. Finally, they conduct experiments on a real-world web page ranking dataset from a commercial search engine, showing that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of the final loss value.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its strong theoretical foundation. It directly addresses a critical weakness in prior work by providing the first provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank. The analysis is thorough, establishing explicit convergence rates for both the gradient-free and gradient-based methods under the challenging setting of a non-convex objective with an inexact oracle. The derivation of complexity bounds that connect the accuracy of the lower-level oracle to the overall optimization performance is a significant and complete piece of theoretical work.

2.  **Originality and Generalizability:** The paper makes novel contributions to the optimization literature beyond the specific application of PageRank. The proposed adaptive gradient method for constrained, non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle is a valuable generalization of existing techniques. Similarly, adapting the random gradient-free framework to handle constraints and a provably inexact zero-order oracle is a non-trivial extension. These optimization frameworks are general and could be applied to other machine learning problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are intractable.

3.  **Significance and Impact:** The work is significant for researchers and practitioners working with graph-based ranking models. By providing theoretically-grounded optimization methods, the paper increases the reliability and trustworthiness of learned Supervised PageRank models. The analysis of the trade-off between oracle accuracy and computational cost provides practical guidance for implementation. The broader contribution to optimization with inexact oracles is also likely to be influential.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors do a good job of separating the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2). The problem statement and the motivation for new methods are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation Metrics:** The experiments exclusively use the value of the loss function (Eq. 2.4) as the performance metric. While this directly measures the success of the optimization procedure, it does not show whether a lower loss value translates to a tangible improvement in ranking quality. The inclusion of standard ranking metrics such as Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) on the test set would have made the experimental results much more compelling and easier to interpret in a practical context.

2.  **Assumptions for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. The authors acknowledge the function is generally non-convex and justify this by assuming `Φ` is a small neighborhood around a local minimum where convexity holds. This is a strong, potentially restrictive assumption, and its practical validity is not discussed or verified in the paper. The gradient-based method's analysis, which holds for non-convex functions, is more general and robust in this regard.

3.  **Clarity of Experimental Details:** Some details of the experimental setup could be clarified. For instance, the choice of the Lipschitz constant `L` for the gradient-free method is stated, but the process for selecting this value is not well-justified. Reporting results for a range of `L` values is helpful, but guidance on how to set it in practice would be beneficial. Furthermore, while the baseline method (GBP) is evaluated for different step sizes, it is not clear how these compare to the original implementation in [21] or if they were tuned fairly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem, filling a significant theoretical gap.
*   The developed optimization frameworks for constrained problems with inexact oracles are novel and of general interest to the machine learning community.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous, complete, and provides clear complexity bounds.
*   Experimental results on a real-world dataset demonstrate that the proposed methods achieve better objective values than the existing state-of-the-art.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical impact of the improved optimization is not demonstrated, as experiments lack standard ranking evaluation metrics (e.g., NDCG).
*   The convergence proof for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption that may not hold in practice.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to both the field of graph-based learning and the broader area of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical results are strong, novel, and rigorously proven, addressing a clear shortcoming in previous work. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including standard ranking metrics, the demonstrated improvement on the optimization objective on a real-world task is convincing. The paper's strengths, particularly its theoretical rigor and the general applicability of its proposed methods, substantially outweigh its weaknesses.
