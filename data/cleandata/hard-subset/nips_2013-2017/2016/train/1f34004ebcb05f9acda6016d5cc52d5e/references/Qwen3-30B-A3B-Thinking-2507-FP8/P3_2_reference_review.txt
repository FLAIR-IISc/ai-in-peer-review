

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a framework for ranking nodes in graphs that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle and a gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle. They provide theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods and demonstrate empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art [21] in web page ranking tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant contribution by providing convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, which previous work lacked. The authors properly identify the limitation of [21] (no convergence proof) and address it through rigorous theoretical analysis.

2. **Problem Relevance**: Web page ranking is a practical and important problem in information retrieval, and the paper demonstrates clear empirical improvements over existing methods.

3. **Methodological Soundness**: The two-level framework (lower-level approximation of stationary distribution, upper-level optimization) is well-motivated and properly justified. The use of Nesterov's method [17] for the lower-level approximation is appropriate.

4. **Rigorous Analysis**: The convergence rate analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is thorough, with well-constructed theorems and proofs (referenced to supplementary materials).

## Weaknesses

1. **Non-Convexity Treatment**: The paper claims the objective function is non-convex but "convex on Φ" (a small vicinity of ϕ*), which is not sufficiently justified. The authors should provide more insight into why this local convexity assumption holds for their problem and how it affects the practical applicability of their convergence guarantees.

2. **Computational Practicality**: While the paper provides complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), they contain terms like ln(1/ε) that may make the methods impractical for very large-scale problems. The experimental section doesn't provide sufficient detail about computational time or memory requirements compared to the state-of-the-art, which is crucial for assessing real-world applicability.

3. **Gradient-Free Method Claim**: The paper claims "the main advantage of the second gradient-free algorithm is that it avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix." However, Theorem 2 shows the complexity still involves terms proportional to m (the dimension of the parameter vector), suggesting the computational burden remains substantial.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites [19] (Fresh BrowseRank) but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with this and other recent methods for learning graph-based ranking algorithms. This weakens the claim of being "state-of-the-art."

5. **Empirical Details**: The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., how the choice of L affects the gradient-free method) and doesn't compare the computational efficiency of the proposed methods with the baseline.

## Critical Analysis

The paper's core theoretical contribution is strong and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The two-level optimization framework is well-justified, and the convergence analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is rigorous. However, the practical implications of the theoretical results are not fully explored.

A particularly concerning issue is the claim that the gradient-free method "avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix." The complexity analysis in Theorem 2 shows the method still requires O(m) operations per iteration, which may not be significantly better than gradient-based methods for large m. The paper should clarify this point and provide more concrete evidence of computational advantages.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More detailed discussion of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational practicality
2. A clearer explanation of why the local convexity assumption is reasonable
3. More comprehensive computational efficiency comparisons with the state-of-the-art
4. A more thorough comparison with recent related work (e.g., [19])

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes an important contribution to the theory of optimization for graph-based ranking algorithms. The combination of inexact oracle methods with the specific requirements of Supervised PageRank is novel. The theoretical results could potentially be applied to other problems with similar inexact oracle structures.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of clear computational advantages over existing methods, despite the theoretical guarantees. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why practitioners would prefer these methods over existing approaches in real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical contributions that addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The empirical results support the theoretical claims, showing clear improvements over the state-of-the-art. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with more detailed discussion of computational practicality and clearer justification of the local convexity assumption.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical optimization advances with a practical application. The main weaknesses relate to practical implications rather than theoretical soundness.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to optimization methods for graph-based ranking algorithms. With minor revisions to address the computational practicality concerns and provide more clarity on the local convexity assumption, this would be a strong NIPS paper. The empirical results are convincing, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous.

Specific revisions needed:
1. Clarify the computational advantages of the gradient-free method
2. Provide more details on the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational practicality
3. Strengthen the comparison with recent related work
4. Justify the local convexity assumption more thoroughly
