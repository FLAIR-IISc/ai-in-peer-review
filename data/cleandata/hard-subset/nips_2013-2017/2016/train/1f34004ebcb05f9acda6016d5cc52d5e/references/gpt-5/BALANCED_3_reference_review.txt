Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The learning objective is a pairwise squared hinge-like loss over query-dependent graphs. The key challenge is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires stationary distributions and their derivatives, which are only accessible approximately.

The paper proposes two two-level algorithms:
- A gradient-free method that extends random gradient-free optimization (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny; Agarwal et al.) to constrained problems with an inexact zero-order oracle. A two-point estimator on the sphere is used, and the bias due to bounded function-evaluation errors is analyzed. The lower level computes approximate stationary distributions using a linearly convergent method of Nesterov–Nemirovski [17] instead of the power method.
- A gradient-based method that generalizes projected gradient methods with inexact oracles (building on Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov for convex problems and Ghadimi–Lan for nonconvex) to constrained, possibly nonconvex composite objectives with an adaptive estimate of the “Lipschitz constant” of the inexact oracle. The lower level approximates both the stationary distributions and their derivatives via a generalized [17]-style iteration.

For both methods, the paper derives error/computation trade-offs that connect inner accuracies (iterations N, N1, N2) to outer convergence guarantees, and global complexity bounds. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (600 queries, ~11.7K pages, ~7.5K edges) compare the proposed methods to the prior gradient-based Supervised PageRank approach [21] that uses the power method and its derivative. The proposed methods achieve lower training loss and show faster convergence of the stationary distribution computation than the power method.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-motivated and practically relevant problem: learning random-walk–based ranking with feature-dependent restarts/transitions.
- The lower-level use of a linearly convergent PageRank solver and its extension to derivative computation is sensible and likely beneficial in practice; the analysis quantifies how inner accuracy impacts outer convergence.
- The gradient-free extension to constrained problems with bounded evaluation error is carefully analyzed, with explicit dependence on smoothing/stepsize and error budgets.
- The adaptive gradient method with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz parameter is a useful generalization; the convergence is stated in terms of a standard stationarity measure.
- Theoretical results are tied to implementable procedures (δ determines N, N1, N2), yielding end-to-end complexity bounds.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality/technical soundness
- The gradient-free outer method is analyzed under convexity. The learning objective here is nonconvex; the paper circumvents this by assuming a small convex neighborhood around a local minimum. This is a strong and unverifiable assumption, and it weakens the applicability of the guarantees. A nonconvex analysis for the zero-order case (e.g., expected gradient-norm bounds as in Ghadimi–Lan’s nonconvex zeroth-order methods) would be more convincing.
- The inexact first-order oracle framework is well motivated, but the novelty relative to Devolder et al. (inexact oracle, convex) and Ghadimi–Lan (nonconvex, exact or stochastic) should be made crisper. As written, Algorithm 2 resembles known adaptive projected methods; it would help to clarify precisely what is new (e.g., the acceptance test, complexity with bounded oracle error).
- Some proofs are deferred; given the number of technical lemmas (e.g., derivative iteration and β1), providing at least proof sketches in the main text would aid evaluation.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typos and style issues (e.g., “concider,” “probablities”) and several incomplete references. Notation is heavy and occasionally inconsistent (e.g., switching between norms and spaces without reminders).
- The construction and guarantees for Π̃q (derivative iteration) are central but only summarized; intuition and stability considerations (e.g., conditioning, storage) are missing.
- The role of the constraint set Φ is a bit ad hoc (ball around 1-vector of radius 0.99). Please clarify positivity enforcement, projection details, and how zero-outdegree nodes are handled in learning.

Originality
- Lower-level use of [17] for PageRank and its derivative appears underexploited in prior supervised PageRank learning and is a neat contribution.
- The gradient-free extension to constrained, inexact-oracle settings and the adaptive inexact first-order method for nonconvex composite objectives are incremental but useful. Positioning relative to Nesterov–Spokoiny (two-point estimators), Agarwal et al. (bandit convex optimization), and Ghadimi–Lan (nonconvex zeroth-/first-order) should be strengthened.

Significance/empirical validation
- Experiments are limited. Only training loss is reported (Table 1); no ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, P@k) are provided, which makes it hard to judge practical impact on ranking quality.
- Comparisons are restricted to one baseline (GBP [21]) with a few stepsizes; no comparisons to other supervised random-walk methods (e.g., Backstrom–Leskovec), standard learning-to-rank baselines (RankSVM, LambdaMART), or to alternative PageRank solvers.
- No wall-clock/runtime or memory results are reported. Since a major claim is improved efficiency, end-to-end time, inner iteration counts, and sensitivity to ε, τ, N1, N2 are essential.
- Data are proprietary; no code or synthetic replication is provided, limiting reproducibility.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid and relevant problem; principled two-level design.
- Theoretical treatment of inexact-oracle optimization tied to PageRank computations.
- Clear potential to improve practical learning of supervised PageRank variants.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation insufficient (no ranking metrics, limited baselines, no runtime).
- Gradient-free theory assumes convexity; applicability to the nonconvex problem is tenuous.
- Clarity and presentation issues; several references incomplete.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@1/3/10, MAP) on held-out queries, with statistical tests.
- Include runtime and memory comparisons for lower-level solvers and end-to-end training; provide ablations on τ, δ, N, N1, N2 and sensitivity to ε and R.
- Compare against broader baselines (supervised random walks; classic LTR methods).
- Either provide a nonconvex analysis for the zero-order method (e.g., expected gradient norm) or clearly delimit when the convexity assumption holds in practice.
- Improve writing and fix references; add proof sketches for main lemmas in the body; clarify projection/constraint choices and positivity enforcement.
- Consider releasing code and a synthetic or public dataset for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The theoretical contributions are interesting and potentially useful, but the current empirical validation and clarity do not meet the bar for NeurIPS. Strengthening experiments, clarifying novelty relative to closely related optimization literature, and tightening the presentation would make this a much stronger submission.
