This paper presents two new two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, aiming to address the convergence issues of existing gradient-based approaches and limitations of other optimization techniques. The core contributions lie in employing a linearly convergent method for stationary distribution approximation, developing a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, adapting a gradient-free method for constrained problems, and analyzing the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity. The authors also provide theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate empirical improvements over a state-of-the-art method.

While the problem addressed – improving the optimization of Supervised PageRank – is relevant, and the authors demonstrate a considerable amount of mathematical rigor, I find myself skeptical of the practical significance and overall novelty of this work, given the extensive existing literature in optimization and graph ranking. My primary concern stems from the incremental nature of the contributions and the lack of compelling evidence demonstrating substantial real-world impact beyond the specific experimental setup.

The primary strength lies in the theoretical analysis. The authors diligently attempt to provide convergence guarantees for their proposed methods, addressing a known limitation of the original Supervised PageRank approach. The detailed complexity analysis is also commendable. However, the theoretical sophistication doesn’t necessarily translate to a significant practical advancement. The reliance on a linearly convergent method (Method 3.1) on the lower level, while theoretically sound, might limit scalability for very large graphs.  The authors acknowledge this to some degree but don’t provide sufficient analysis of the impact on practical runtimes for realistically sized datasets.

A significant weakness is the experimental evaluation. The chosen dataset, while real, appears relatively small (≈ 11.7K vertices and ≈ 7.5K edges) and specifically from a commercial search engine (Yandex).  The performance gains over the baseline (GBP) are presented but aren’t dramatically large. Crucially, the experimental section lacks a comparison to more widely used and established optimization libraries or algorithms commonly employed for large-scale graph processing.  For instance, comparing to distributed optimization frameworks (e.g., using Spark or Dask) or state-of-the-art solvers for non-convex optimization would offer a more informative benchmark. The impact of parameter tuning (specifically L in GFN) is briefly discussed, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results. The claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art method is presented, but the margin is not overwhelmingly convincing, and the limited dataset size casts doubt on the generalizability of the findings.

The originality of the work is questionable. The adaptation of existing optimization techniques (gradient and gradient-free methods) to the specific context of Supervised PageRank, while correct, doesn’t represent a fundamentally novel algorithmic contribution. The theoretical extensions – providing convergence guarantees for inexact oracles – are valuable but build heavily on existing results in optimization theory. The paper doesn’t sufficiently distinguish itself from the vast body of work on inexact optimization methods.

The significance of this work is limited. While the proposed algorithms may offer improvements in certain scenarios, the lack of scalability analysis and the modest experimental gains suggest that they are unlikely to have a broad impact on the field. The problem being addressed – optimizing Supervised PageRank – is a specialized one, and the proposed solutions seem tailored to this specific context.  A broader investigation of the applicability of these methods to other graph ranking or learning problems would be necessary to demonstrate their wider significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees.
*   Well-defined problem and clear presentation of the proposed methods.
*   Demonstrated performance improvement (albeit modest) over a baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – primarily an adaptation of existing techniques.
*   Limited experimental evaluation with a relatively small dataset.
*   Lack of comparison to widely used optimization libraries or algorithms.
*   Questionable scalability for large graphs.
*   Limited demonstration of practical significance beyond the specific experimental setup.



**Overall assessment:** Reject.  While technically sound and rigorously analyzed, this paper lacks the originality and practical significance necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The contributions are incremental, and the experimental evaluation is insufficient to convince me that the proposed methods represent a substantial advancement over existing approaches. I encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and conduct a more comprehensive evaluation on larger and more diverse datasets before resubmitting. A clearer demonstration of the methods’ applicability to other graph learning problems would also strengthen the paper.
