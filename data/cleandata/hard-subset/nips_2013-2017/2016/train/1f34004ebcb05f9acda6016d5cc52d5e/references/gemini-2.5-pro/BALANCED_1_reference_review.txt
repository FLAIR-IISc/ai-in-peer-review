### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are parameterized functions of node and edge features. The learning task is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization problem. A key challenge is that the loss function depends on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain, which cannot be computed exactly. This places the problem in the framework of optimization with an "inexact oracle," where function values and gradients are only available as approximations.

The authors identify that the state-of-the-art method for this problem [21] relies on a heuristic gradient-based approach using the power method, which lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. The main contributions of this work are to provide two new two-level optimization algorithms with rigorous theoretical backing:

1.  A **random gradient-free method** that adapts the framework of [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is approximated with a known error bound. The authors provide a convergence rate analysis for the convex case.
2.  An **adaptive gradient-based method** for constrained, possibly non-convex problems. This method extends the work on inexact first-order oracles [7, 25] to handle constraints and adaptively tunes its parameters, avoiding the need to know the Lipschitz constant of the gradient a priori. The analysis proves convergence to a stationary point.

For the lower level (the oracle), the paper proposes using a linearly convergent method from [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, which is shown to be more efficient than the standard power method. The paper provides a full complexity analysis for both proposed algorithms, linking the required accuracy of the low-level oracle to the overall computational cost. Experiments on a large-scale web page ranking task demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art method in terms of the final objective value.

### **Strengths of the Paper**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical treatment of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. It provides the first known convergence guarantees for this problem, filling a critical gap left by previous work [21]. The development of optimization algorithms for constrained problems with inexact oracles is a valuable contribution to the broader optimization community, with potential applications beyond this specific ranking task.

2.  **Novel and Well-Founded Optimization Algorithms:** The paper introduces two distinct and well-motivated algorithms. The adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex problems is particularly strong, as it addresses the practical challenge of not knowing the smoothness properties of the objective function in advance. The careful analysis that connects the accuracy of the low-level oracle (`δ1`, `δ2`) to the complexity of the high-level optimization is thorough and provides a complete picture of the algorithm's performance.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It effectively motivates the problem, explains the shortcomings of prior work, and details its proposed solutions. The distinction between the upper-level optimization and the lower-level oracle approximation is maintained clearly throughout the paper. The theoretical results (Lemmas 1-2, Theorems 1-4) are stated precisely.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, conducted on a large, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, are convincing. They clearly demonstrate that both the gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) methods achieve a lower loss value than the existing state-of-the-art method (GBP). This provides strong evidence that the theoretical improvements translate into practical benefits.

### **Weaknesses of the Paper and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Convexity Assumption for the Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and its application in Theorem 2) assumes that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex. However, the authors state that the loss function (2.4) is generally non-convex. They justify this by assuming the optimization occurs in a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that may not hold and weakens the theoretical guarantee.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should either provide stronger justification for this local convexity assumption (e.g., empirical evidence of the Hessian's properties) or, preferably, extend their analysis to the non-convex setting. Recent advances in zeroth-order optimization for non-convex functions could be leveraged to prove convergence to a stationary point, which would align better with the non-convex nature of the problem and the analysis provided for the gradient-based method.

2.  **Lack of Practical Performance Metrics:** The experimental evaluation relies exclusively on the value of the loss function `f(ϕ)`. While minimizing the loss is the direct objective, it is not a standard measure of ranking quality.
    *   **Suggestion:** To demonstrate the practical significance of the work, the authors should report standard information retrieval metrics such as Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) on the test set. This would show that achieving a lower loss value translates into a tangible improvement in ranking quality, which is the ultimate goal.

3.  **Incomplete Computational Cost Comparison:** The paper reports that the proposed GBN method takes fewer upper-level iterations than the baseline GBP but does not provide a direct comparison of total wall-clock time. The per-iteration cost of the proposed methods might be higher due to the more complex oracle computations (e.g., derivative calculations in Section 3).
    *   **Suggestion:** A plot showing the loss function value versus wall-clock time for all methods would provide a much clearer picture of their practical efficiency. This would help readers understand the trade-offs between convergence rate and per-iteration complexity.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The naming and referencing of algorithms could be improved. For example, "Algorithm 2" is used for the general gradient-based method in Section 5.1, but "Algorithm 4" is used for its specific application in Section 5.2 (with both apparently detailed in the supplementary material). A more consistent naming scheme would improve readability.
    *   The claim that the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] is faster than the power method is a key motivation for the lower-level oracle design. While a figure is mentioned to be in the supplementary materials, highlighting this result more prominently in the main paper's experimental section would strengthen the motivation for this design choice.

### **Decision**

I recommend **Acceptance** for this paper.

The paper makes a strong and well-supported theoretical contribution by providing the first provably convergent algorithms for learning the Supervised PageRank model. This is a significant advance over prior heuristic approaches. The proposed optimization frameworks for inexact, constrained problems are of independent interest. While there are some weaknesses, particularly the strong convexity assumption for one of the methods and the scope of the experimental evaluation, these do not undermine the core contributions. The suggestions provided above should be considered by the authors to further strengthen the paper for the camera-ready version. Overall, this is a high-quality paper that fits well within the scope of NIPS.
