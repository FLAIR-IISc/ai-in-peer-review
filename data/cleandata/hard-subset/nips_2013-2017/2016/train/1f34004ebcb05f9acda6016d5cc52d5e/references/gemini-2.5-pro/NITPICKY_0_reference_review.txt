### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a non-convex optimization problem where the objective function and its gradients cannot be computed exactly. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-free method and a gradient-based method. Both methods are designed to work with an "inexact oracle" that provides approximations of the function value (and gradient) with a controllable accuracy. The core contributions are the theoretical convergence guarantees for these methods in a constrained, non-convex (for the gradient-based method) or convex (for the gradient-free method) setting with such an oracle. The authors claim this is a significant improvement over the existing state-of-the-art method [21], which lacks convergence proofs. The proposed methods are experimentally evaluated on a web page ranking task using a proprietary dataset, where they reportedly outperform the baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is the rigorous theoretical analysis of optimization algorithms in the presence of a controllable inexact oracle. Providing convergence guarantees (rate of convergence and complexity bounds) for learning Supervised PageRank is a valuable contribution, addressing a noted gap in the existing literature [21].
2.  **Novel Frameworks:** The paper develops two distinct optimization frameworks. The adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems (Section 5) is particularly interesting, as it does not require prior knowledge of the Lipschitz constant. The extension of random gradient-free methods to the constrained, inexact oracle setting (Section 4) is also a solid contribution.
3.  **Problem Relevance:** Learning to rank is a fundamental problem with significant practical applications. Applying advanced optimization theory to a specific, non-trivial ranking model like Supervised PageRank is a worthy endeavor and of potential interest to the NIPS community.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is beset by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor typos to significant clarity issues, which collectively undermine its quality and readiness for publication.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The manuscript is littered with typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "concider" instead of "consider" in Section 2; the negative stopping threshold "-10^-5" in Section 6.2; awkward phrasing like "guarantees of a convergence").
    *   A footnote indicating the NIPS conference appears directly in the body of the introduction, which is a major formatting error.
    *   The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted, with inconsistent styles, missing information (e.g., conference names, years), and a general lack of adherence to any standard citation format. This suggests a lack of care in the paper's preparation.
    *   Notation is sometimes confusing or inconsistently introduced. For example, in Section 2, the constraint set `Φ` is said to lie in `Rm++1`, where the trailing `1` is undefined and likely a typo. The introduction of multiple graph size parameters (`nq`, `p`, `n`, `s`) at the end of Section 2 feels abrupt and their relationships are not immediately clear.
    *   The paper refers to "Algorithm 2" and "Algorithm 4" in the main text, but these algorithms are relegated to the Supplementary Materials. This makes the main paper difficult to follow as a self-contained document. The text should at least clearly state that these are in the appendix.

2.  **Technical and Methodological Concerns:**
    *   The analysis of the gradient-free method in Theorem 1 and 2 relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. The authors correctly state that the problem is generally non-convex, which makes this a strong and potentially limiting local assumption. The practical implications of this assumption are not sufficiently discussed.
    *   In the adaptive gradient method (Algorithm 2 in supplementary, described in Section 5.1), the line search condition includes a term `ε/8Mk`, where `ε` is the *global stopping tolerance*. It is highly unconventional to use the stopping criterion's tolerance within the logic of a single iteration's line search. This requires a much more thorough justification than is provided.
    *   The complexity bounds, particularly in Theorem 4, result in expressions that are difficult to parse and interpret practically (e.g., the monstrous term inside the logarithm). While formally correct, more intuition about the scaling with key parameters would be beneficial.
    *   The paper states that standard global optimization methods cannot be applied because they require unbiased estimations. However, the proposed gradient-free oracle `gτ(x, δ)` is itself biased. While the analysis accounts for this, the initial claim should be phrased more carefully to clarify why their biased approach is valid while others are not.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The experiments are conducted on a proprietary dataset from Yandex, which makes the results entirely non-reproducible. While access to real-world data is valuable, the lack of a reproducible benchmark on a public dataset (e.g., LETOR) is a significant weakness for a scientific publication.
    *   The evaluation is based solely on the value of the loss function. For a ranking task, it is critical to report performance using standard ranking metrics like NDCG or ERR. Improvement on a surrogate loss function does not guarantee improvement in the actual ranking quality perceived by a user.
    *   The description of the baseline method (`GBP`) is sparse. The choice of its parameters (e.g., raising a matrix to the power 100) is stated without justification. The comparison could be made stronger by providing more details or exploring a wider range of parameters for the baseline.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   Provides the first (to my knowledge) formal convergence guarantees for optimizing the Supervised PageRank model.
*   The proposed optimization frameworks for inexact oracles are general and could be applicable to other problems beyond PageRank.
*   The paper tackles a non-trivial, non-convex optimization problem of practical importance.

### Arguments for Rejection:

*   The manuscript suffers from poor presentation, including numerous typos, severe formatting errors, and an unacceptably formatted reference list.
*   Key assumptions, such as the local convexity required for the gradient-free method's analysis, limit the generality of the theoretical claims.
*   Experimental validation is weak due to the use of a non-reproducible dataset and the absence of standard task-specific evaluation metrics (e.g., NDCG).
*   Critical details, such as algorithm descriptions, are relegated to supplementary material, harming the clarity and self-contained nature of the main paper.

### Recommendation: **Reject**

While the theoretical ideas at the core of this paper are interesting and potentially significant, the execution is deeply flawed. The sheer volume of errors in formatting, clarity, and presentation suggests a rushed and careless preparation that is not up to the standards of NIPS. Furthermore, the limitations of the theoretical assumptions and the weaknesses in the experimental evaluation prevent me from confidently endorsing the paper's conclusions. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous issues before it can be considered for publication at a top-tier venue.
