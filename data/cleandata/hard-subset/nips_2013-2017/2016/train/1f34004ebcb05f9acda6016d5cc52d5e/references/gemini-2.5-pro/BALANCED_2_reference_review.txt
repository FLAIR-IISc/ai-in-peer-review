This paper tackles the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are parameterized functions of node and edge features. This learning task is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization problem. The core difficulty, which the authors identify, is that the objective function and its gradient depend on the stationary distribution of a Markov chain, which can only be approximated. Consequently, standard optimization algorithms with exact oracles are not applicable, and the prior state-of-the-art method [21] lacked convergence guarantees.

The main contribution of this work is the development and analysis of two novel two-level optimization algorithms with provable convergence guarantees for this problem. The authors first propose using a more efficient method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, providing explicit complexity bounds to reach a desired accuracy. Building on this "inexact oracle", they propose:
1.  A random gradient-free method, which extends the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization and a known bound on the oracle's error.
2.  An adaptive gradient-based method, which generalizes the work of Devolder et al. [25] to non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of their convergence rates and overall computational complexity. The algorithms are then validated experimentally on a real-world web page ranking task, demonstrating superior performance over the previous state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Rigorous Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's primary strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. It formally addresses the "inexact oracle" setting, which is fundamental to many large-scale learning problems, but is often handled heuristically. Providing explicit convergence rates and complexity bounds for both a gradient-free and a gradient-based method in this setting is a significant contribution. The extensions of existing optimization frameworks ([16], [25]) to new problem classes (constrained, non-convex, inexact oracles) are novel and valuable to the optimization community.

2.  **Significance and Practical Relevance:** The work addresses a practical and important problem: learning to rank on graphs. Supervised PageRank is a powerful model, and the ability to train it with provable guarantees increases its reliability. The proposed algorithms could have a direct impact on applications like web search and recommendation systems. The general optimization frameworks developed are also potentially applicable to a wider range of problems beyond PageRank.

3.  **Completeness and Soundness:** The work is a complete research contribution. It clearly defines the problem, develops new algorithms, provides solid theoretical backing, and validates the results with empirical experiments on a real-world dataset. The analysis connecting the accuracy of the lower-level approximation (Lemmas 1 and 2) to the upper-level optimization convergence (Theorems 2 and 4) is particularly well-executed and crucial for the overall soundness of the approach.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while focused on a single task, effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods. Both new algorithms, and particularly the gradient-free one (GFN), are shown to achieve a lower loss than the existing baseline (GBP). This provides compelling evidence that the theoretical improvements translate into better real-world performance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and its application in Theorem 2) assumes the objective function is convex. However, the paper states that the Supervised PageRank loss function is generally non-convex. The authors should clarify this point. Is the assumption that the optimization is performed in a local region around a minimum where the function is convex? If so, this should be stated more explicitly, and a discussion on the validity and potential limitations of this assumption would be valuable. This is especially important as the gradient-based method is analyzed for the general non-convex case, and the contrast should be highlighted.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The analysis and implementation rely on hyperparameters like the Lipschitz constant `L` and the desired accuracy `ε`. The gradient-free method, in particular, seems to require an estimate of `L` for setting the step size. While the adaptive nature of the proposed gradient-based method is a strong advantage in this regard, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on how to set `L` for the GFN method in practice and its sensitivity to misspecification. A brief justification for the choice of `L=10^{-4}` in the experiments would be helpful.

3.  **Discussion of GFN vs. GBN Trade-offs:** The paper proposes two distinct methods, GFN and GBN. Empirically, GFN appears to be the clear winner. The paper could be strengthened by adding a more explicit discussion on the theoretical and practical trade-offs between the two approaches. For instance, GBN has stronger theoretical guarantees for non-convex problems, while GFN avoids the potentially complex and expensive computation of derivatives. Why does GFN perform better in this specific application? A more in-depth comparison would provide readers with better guidance on which method to choose in different scenarios.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:**
    *   The algorithms for the final Supervised PageRank learning procedure (referred to as Algorithm 2 and 4 in the text) appear to be in the supplementary materials. This should be explicitly stated, or the algorithms should be included in the main text for better readability.
    *   The citation style seems inconsistent and appears as a raw list in some places rather than as proper in-text citations. This is likely a formatting artifact that should be corrected in the final version.

**Decision:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong, well-supported contribution. It bridges the gap between optimization theory and a practical machine learning problem by developing novel, provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank with an inexact oracle. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results validate its practical utility. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed with modest revisions. I recommend acceptance.
