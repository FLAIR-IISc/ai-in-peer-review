This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing the lack of convergence guarantees in the original approach [21]. The authors leverage a linearly convergent method for stationary distribution approximation and develop gradient-based and gradient-free optimization algorithms with convergence analysis. They also investigate the trade-off between accuracy and complexity. The experimental results demonstrate improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for a method previously lacking them. The convergence analysis for both gradient and gradient-free methods is a notable contribution, particularly in the context of inexact oracle information. The detailed complexity analysis is also valuable. The experimental validation, while limited in scope, suggests performance improvements over existing methods.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that significantly impact its overall quality. First, the problem setting itself – Supervised PageRank – feels somewhat niche and its broader relevance isn't convincingly established. While the authors cite motivations related to incorporating node/edge features, the benefits over simpler, well-established ranking methods (e.g., Learning to Rank approaches) aren't clearly articulated. The paper primarily focuses on *how* to optimize the existing Supervised PageRank framework, rather than questioning its fundamental suitability.

Second, the theoretical contributions, while present, are heavily reliant on lemmas and theorems presented without sufficient contextualization. The reader is frequently referred to “Supplementary Materials,” which is a poor practice for a NIPS submission. Key definitions and proofs should be included within the main text or significantly summarized. The constant factors in the convergence rates are not discussed, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of the theoretical results.

Third, the experimental evaluation is limited. The dataset, while real-world, appears relatively small, and the comparison is primarily against a single baseline method [21]. A comparison with more standard ranking algorithms (e.g., RankBoost, LambdaMART) would be crucial to demonstrate the true value of the proposed approach. The presented figures are small and lack sufficient detail.  The claim that "GFN significantly outperforms" requires more rigorous statistical testing and a more in-depth analysis of the results. The analysis of the effect of parameter choice (L in GFN) relegated to the supplementary materials is also suboptimal. 

Fourth, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity and motivation in certain sections. The discussion surrounding the choice of the parameter space Φ, and the justification for choosing a specific norm, feels somewhat arbitrary. The connection between the theoretical convergence rates and the practical performance observed in the experiments isn’t explicitly made.

Finally, the writing style, while generally clear, is occasionally dense and cumbersome, hindering understanding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for a previously unanalyzed method.
*   Presents a detailed complexity analysis.
*   Demonstrates improved ranking performance in a limited experimental setting.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The underlying problem (Supervised PageRank) is relatively niche and lacks strong justification.
*   Theoretical contributions are presented without sufficient context and rely heavily on supplementary materials.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comparisons to more standard ranking algorithms.
*   Lack of clarity in motivation and parameter choices.
*   Writing quality could be improved.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents some valuable theoretical contributions, its limitations in terms of problem relevance, experimental validation, and clarity outweigh its strengths. The work feels more like an engineering effort to improve an existing algorithm than a significant scientific advancement. The paper needs substantial revisions, including a more compelling justification for the chosen problem, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and a clearer presentation of the theoretical results, before it would be suitable for NIPS.
