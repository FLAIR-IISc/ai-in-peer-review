- Proposes two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank parameters with convergence analysis.  
- Main contributions: convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, a gradient-free method for constrained settings with inexact oracle, and complexity analysis for both methods.  
- Strength: Provides the first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank and analyzes the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity.  
- Strength: Clearly formulates Supervised PageRank learning as constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle, justified by large-graph stationary distribution challenges.  
- Strength: Empirical results on Yandex data show consistent, statistically significant improvements over prior work [21] across multiple graph sizes.  
- Strength: Addresses practical relevance and computational challenges, offering methods with theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited to a single dataset and query graph structure, raising questions about generalizability to other graph types.  
- Weakness: Novelty claims are overstated given related recent work ([7, 16]) on combining random gradient-free and gradient methods for non-convex optimization.  
- Weakness: Complexity bounds are theoretically thorough but include large constants and lack numeric evaluation or clear scalability discussion for million-node graphs.  
- Weakness: Gradient-based method’s convergence relies on local convexity assumptions, so guarantees apply only to local minima in a fundamentally non-convex problem.  
- Overall assessment: Well-written and technically strong paper with meaningful theoretical and empirical contributions, but would benefit from broader experiments and clearer novelty positioning.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Key arguments for acceptance: first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank, rigorous complexity analysis, consistent empirical improvements, and well-motivated practical problem formulation.  
- Key arguments for rejection: limited experimental validation across graph types, overstated novelty claims, and insufficient practical scalability discussion.
