### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model. The authors correctly identify a key weakness in the state-of-the-art method [21]: a lack of theoretical convergence guarantees due to the use of inexact gradient information derived from the power method. To remedy this, the paper proposes two new two-level optimization algorithms. At the lower level, both algorithms leverage a linearly convergent method [17] to approximate the PageRank stationary distribution and its derivatives, allowing for precise control over the approximation error. At the upper level, the paper develops and analyzes two distinct approaches: 1) a gradient-based method for constrained, non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle, and 2) a gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization, adapted from [16] to handle an inexact zero-order oracle. For both methods, the authors provide theoretical analyses of convergence rates and overall computational complexity. Experimental results on a real-world web ranking task suggest that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free variant, outperform the existing baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The primary strength of this work is its rigorous theoretical foundation. By formalizing the learning problem within the framework of optimization with inexact oracles, the authors provide the first convergence guarantees for training the Supervised PageRank model. This is a significant and necessary contribution to the literature on learnable graph ranking algorithms.
2.  **Novel Methodological Contributions:** The paper presents valuable extensions to existing optimization theory. The adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact oracle (Section 5.1) and the analysis of the random gradient-free method under similar constraints (Section 4.1) are general contributions that could be of interest beyond the specific application of PageRank.
3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The complexity analysis, which investigates the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall iterations required by the upper-level optimizer, is thorough and insightful.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental comparison against the state-of-the-art method [21] on a real-world dataset demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed algorithms, showing superior performance in terms of the final loss value.

### Weaknesses

Despite the paper's strong theoretical contributions, it is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and methodological details. These issues, in aggregate, detract significantly from the quality of the manuscript and make a recommendation for acceptance difficult.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally dense and difficult to read. The prose is often convoluted, and key concepts are not introduced with sufficient clarity.
    *   Notation is heavy and at times inconsistent. For instance, `a.o.` is used for "arithmetic operations" without prior definition. The vector `em` is informally defined as "the vector of all ones."
    *   The algorithms central to the paper's contribution (referred to as Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 4) are relegated to the supplementary materials. A top-tier conference paper must be self-contained; the core algorithms should be presented in the main text.
    *   The manuscript contains numerous typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "we concider," "outputs of the algorithms differ insufficiently"). This suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

2.  **Methodological and Technical Concerns:**
    *   A critical inconsistency exists in the treatment of convexity. The paper is motivated by the non-convex nature of the loss function `f(ϕ)`. However, Theorem 1, which provides the convergence guarantee for the proposed gradient-free method, explicitly requires `f` to be convex. The subsequent application in Section 4.2 relies on a strong and potentially impractical assumption: "We assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) ... is convex". This undermines the generality of the gradient-free approach and contradicts the initial problem framing. The analysis for the non-convex case is what would be truly novel here.
    *   The justification for hyperparameter choices in the experiments is thin. The Lipschitz constant `L` is set to `10^-4` with little motivation other than a brief mention of testing a few values for `L0` in the gradient-based method. The relationship between the chosen `L` and the true (but unknown) Lipschitz constant of the objective function is not discussed.
    *   The definition of the inexact first-order oracle in (5.1) is non-standard and combines errors in the function value and the gradient into a single inequality. While it is a valid generalization, its implications and relationship to more standard definitions of inexact gradients are not sufficiently explored.

3.  **Formatting and Professionalism:**
    *   The paper retains a template footer ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.") on the first page, which is highly unprofessional.
    *   The reference section is poorly formatted. Many entries are incomplete (e.g., missing conference or journal names, as in [1], [4], [5]), and the citation style is inconsistent. For example, the introduction cites [11] for HITS, but the reference list attributes [9] to Kleinberg's HITS paper. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable and hinders the reader's ability to consult prior work.
    *   A footnote for "Yandex" is included without a number.

### Decision

**Reject.**

While the core theoretical ideas are strong and address an important gap in the literature, the execution of the paper is deeply flawed. The significant contradiction in the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method, the omission of the main algorithms from the body of the paper, the poor clarity of the writing, and the numerous formatting and referencing errors collectively prevent this manuscript from meeting the high standards of NIPS. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address these extensive issues before it can be considered for publication.
