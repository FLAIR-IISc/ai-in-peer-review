This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank that incorporates node and edge features. The authors identify a critical gap in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: while it employs a gradient-based approach, it relies on inexact estimations of the stationary distribution and its derivatives (via the power method) without any formal proof of convergence. The core contribution of this work is to introduce two new, two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—that are equipped with theoretical convergence guarantees, even in the presence of such an "inexact oracle."

The key idea is to meticulously analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (which approximates the PageRank vector and its derivatives) and the complexity of the upper-level optimization algorithm. On the lower level, they employ a more efficient, linearly convergent method [17] to ensure the approximation error can be bounded by any desired tolerance. On the upper level, the paper makes two distinct and novel contributions to optimization theory. First, it extends the framework of random gradient-free methods [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is calculated with a known error bound. Second, it generalizes adaptive gradient methods [7] to constrained, non-convex problems where the first-order oracle is inexact. For both proposed algorithms, the authors provide a full complexity analysis, linking the overall number of operations to the desired final accuracy ε.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold and novel approach to the optimization problem. Instead of proposing yet another incremental variant of PageRank, the authors tackle a fundamental, and often overlooked, issue: how to rigorously train such models when core components (like the stationary distribution) can only be approximated. Providing the *first* theoretically-grounded optimization framework with convergence guarantees for this problem is a significant advance. This moves Supervised PageRank from a heuristic-based method to a principled learning algorithm.

2.  **Important Contribution to Optimization Theory:** The paper's contributions extend beyond the specific application. The development of a gradient-free method for constrained problems with a biased zero-order oracle and an adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle are valuable additions to the optimization literature. The concept of explicitly managing the oracle's precision as a parameter of the optimization algorithm is powerful and could be highly influential for other two-level or bi-level optimization problems prevalent in machine learning.

3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical work is comprehensive. The authors provide detailed convergence theorems and complexity bounds for both of their proposed methods (Theorems 2 and 4). This level of rigor is commendable and provides a solid foundation for the work.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments, though conducted on a single large-scale dataset, effectively demonstrate the practical value of the proposed methods. Both the gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) algorithms are shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art [21] in terms of final loss on the test set. This confirms that the theoretical soundness translates into tangible performance gains.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Theoretical Assumptions:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set Φ. While this is a common technique for proving local convergence, the Supervised PageRank loss function is non-convex in general. This represents a minor gap between the theory and the general problem setting, though it does not invalidate the novelty of handling the inexact oracle. The analysis for the gradient-based method, which applies to non-convex problems, is stronger in this regard.

2.  **Focus on Optimization over Modeling:** The contribution is entirely algorithmic. The paper does not introduce any new modeling ideas for ranking. While I view this as a strength—a deep dive into a hard, foundational problem—reviewers focused primarily on new models for ranking or graph representation learning might perceive this as incremental.

3.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is theoretically dense and assumes significant familiarity with advanced optimization concepts. While precise, this may limit its accessibility to the broader machine learning community. A slightly more intuitive explanation of the core ideas in the main body could have broadened its impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and theoretically sound approach to a known, important problem.
*   Provides the first convergence guarantees for training Supervised PageRank.
*   The contributions to optimization theory for inexact oracles are significant and broadly applicable.
*   The approach is a bold departure from heuristic-driven methods, emphasizing mathematical rigor.
*   Empirical results confirm the superiority of the proposed methods over the existing state-of-the-art.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's analysis is a limitation.
*   The work is an improvement on an existing model's training procedure, not a new model itself.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It exemplifies the kind of foundational work that NIPS should champion. The authors address a difficult and important theoretical problem with a novel and rigorous approach. The willingness to tackle the messy reality of inexact oracles in iterative algorithms and provide formal guarantees is a bold step forward. While the modeling contribution is null, the algorithmic and theoretical novelty is exceptionally high. This paper provides not just a better way to solve a specific problem, but also a methodological blueprint for analyzing other complex, two-level learning systems. The minor theoretical gaps do not detract from the significance of the core contribution.
