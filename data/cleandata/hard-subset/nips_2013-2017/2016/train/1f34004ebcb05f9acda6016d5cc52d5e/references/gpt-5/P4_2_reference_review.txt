Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where node and edge weights are linear functions of features, and ranking scores are given by the stationary distribution of a query-dependent Markov chain. The learning objective is a pairwise hinge-like squared loss over query-specific graphs. The authors propose two two-level optimization schemes:

- Lower level: approximate the stationary distribution πq(ϕ) and, for the gradient-based variant, its derivatives w.r.t. ϕ. They advocate the linearly convergent method of Nesterov–Nemirovski [17] to compute π, and present a natural generalization for computing sensitivities via a power-like recursion (Equations 3.2–3.3). Lemma 1 bounds the error of the approximate loss f̃(ϕ, δ1), and Lemma 2 bounds the error of the approximate gradient g̃(ϕ, δ2), both with explicit iteration-complexity in terms of desired accuracy.

- Upper level: (a) a random gradient-free method under a constrained domain and inexact zero-order oracle, extending the sphere-smoothing estimator literature [16] to bounded (potentially adversarial) oracle error and constraints. Theorem 1 provides expected suboptimality bounds under convexity. (b) An adaptive projected gradient method for composite nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz parameter (generalizing [7] beyond exact oracles). Theorem 3 provides a stationarity measure bound and counts oracle calls. Theorems 2 and 4 compose lower- and upper-level complexities to obtain end-to-end guarantees.

Empirically, the methods are evaluated on Yandex user-browsing graphs for 600 queries (about 11.7k vertices, 7.5k edges across all graphs; ~1.7k labeled pairs). The proposed gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) algorithms are compared against the state-of-the-art gradient-based procedure (GBP) from [21]. On held-out subsets, GFN consistently achieves lower training loss than GBP; GBN improves on GBP on some subsets and typically uses fewer upper-level iterations. The authors also illustrate faster lower-level convergence of [17] vs. the standard power method.

Quality
- Theory: The lower-level approximation bounds (Lemmas 1–2) are plausible and useful for orchestrating inexact oracles. The gradient-free upper-level analysis (Theorem 1) is rigorous for convex f with bounded evaluation error, using a uniform-on-sphere estimator and projection. The adaptive gradient method (Theorem 3) provides a standard stationarity guarantee for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracles and backtracking. The end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2, 4) transparently track dependence on accuracy and graph parameters.

Potential technical concerns:
- Smoothness of the objective: The loss uses (·)+ on pairwise score differences, making f non-smooth (kinks where margins are zero). Yet several analyses assume f ∈ C1,1 (e.g., Section 4.1, inequality 5.1 with δ=0 references the smooth case). While the inexact-oracle framework can accommodate errors, the claimed smoothness-based rates should be reconciled with the non-smooth structure, e.g., by treating the (·)+ as part of h in the composite model or establishing that the inexact oracle satisfies (5.1) despite non-smoothness. As written, Section 5.2 sets h ≡ 0, which appears inconsistent with the loss.
- Convexity assumption for gradient-free method: Section 4.2 assumes f is convex in a neighborhood Φ around a local minimum to apply Theorem 1. No evidence is provided that this holds for the proposed f. Thus the gradient-free guarantees may not apply in general nonconvex settings, although the method can still be used heuristically.
- Constants and practicality: Some constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement, and the “a.o.” complexity counts are informative but do not translate to wall-clock or memory. The derivative computation via (3.2)–(3.3) may be heavy in high dimensions; practical memory/time trade-offs are not discussed.

- Empirics: The evaluation reports the optimization loss but not ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) commonly used in learning-to-rank. This makes it unclear whether improvements in f translate to better retrieval effectiveness. The dataset is modest, proprietary, and not public; reproducibility is limited (no code). Baselines beyond [21] (e.g., other learning-to-rank or graph-based methods) are not included. Runtime and resource usage are not reported, despite claims of speed advantages.

Clarity
The paper is generally well structured, with a clear two-level decomposition and explicit algorithms. However:
- There are several typos and formatting issues (e.g., “th Conference ...” line, inconsistent notation for norms and dimensions, unexplained “a.o.”).
- Some assumptions are only loosely justified (positivity of denominators; convexity in Φ).
- Important constants and proofs are deferred to the supplement; brief sketches would help.
- The relationship between non-smooth loss and the inexact-oracle model should be clarified (e.g., by treating (·)+ inside h or by smoothing).

Originality
- Extending random gradient-free methods to constrained domains with bounded evaluation noise and analyzing a sphere-based estimator under inexact oracle is a meaningful refinement of [16].
- The adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant is a useful generalization of [7]/[25].
- Generalizing [17] to derivative computation for PageRank-like stationary distributions, and integrating it into an inexact-oracle learning pipeline, appears novel in this context.

Significance
The work advances principled learning for graph-based ranking by providing end-to-end convergence guarantees under inexact oracles, a realistic setting for PageRank-type computations. If broadly validated, the methods could be useful for large-scale graph ranking and related problems. However, the current empirical validation is limited (loss-only, small proprietary dataset, few baselines), tempering claims about impact on ranking quality and speed.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem setting with practical importance (learning Supervised PageRank).
- Solid theoretical contributions for inexact-oracle optimization (both zero- and first-order).
- Lower-level algorithm choice is justified; derivative computation scheme is explicit.
- End-to-end complexity bounds and discussion of accuracy–cost trade-offs.
- Empirical evidence of improved optimization loss over a published baseline.

Cons for acceptance
- Mismatch between assumed smoothness and the non-smooth loss; unclear integration into the composite framework (h ≡ 0 vs. (·)+).
- Gradient-free guarantee relies on local convexity not demonstrated for this objective.
- Limited empirical evaluation: no standard ranking metrics, runtimes, or broader baselines; proprietary data; reproducibility concerns.
- Missing implementation details and constants reduce practical interpretability.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references/formatting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reconcile the analysis with the non-smooth loss: either incorporate (·)+ into h and use the composite framework, or introduce a smooth surrogate (e.g., pairwise logistic/squared hinge with smoothing) and adjust proofs accordingly.
- Either justify local convexity empirically/theoretically or state guarantees explicitly for nonconvex f (e.g., stationarity) in the gradient-free case.
- Report ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP) and runtime/memory, and compare to additional baselines.
- Provide more implementation details, release code if possible, and include constants like β1 in the main text or a concise appendix.
- Correct typos/formatting and clarify notation and assumptions.

Overall, the paper offers solid theoretical advances and a well-motivated application, but the empirical evaluation and some theoretical assumptions (smoothness/convexity) should be strengthened to meet the standards of the venue.
