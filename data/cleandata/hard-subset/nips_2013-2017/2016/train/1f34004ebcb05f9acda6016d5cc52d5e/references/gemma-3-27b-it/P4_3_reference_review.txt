This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, leveraging both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques. The core contribution lies in providing convergence guarantees for these methods, which were lacking in previous work [21], and demonstrating improved ranking quality in experiments. The authors address the challenges of inexact oracle information, parameter constraints, and the non-convex nature of the loss function.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of the problem, related work, and the authors’ contributions. The mathematical formulation of Supervised PageRank is presented clearly, building upon existing literature [21]. The numerical methods for calculating the loss function and its gradient are described in detail, along with complexity analysis. The inclusion of theoretical results on convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches is a significant strength. The experimental section demonstrates improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] on a real-world web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides convergence analysis for both proposed methods, addressing a crucial gap in the existing literature [21]. This is a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of gradient-based and gradient-free approaches with inexact oracle information for the Supervised PageRank problem appears novel.  The convergence results for inexact oracles are also valuable.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The authors provide complexity bounds for the proposed algorithms, offering insights into their computational efficiency.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the state-of-the-art in ranking quality.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** While the theoretical contributions are significant, the practical improvement over the existing method [21] (especially in the main results of Table 1) appears modest. The paper should further discuss the scenarios where the proposed methods offer a substantial advantage.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (L, ε, R) can significantly impact performance. The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. While the supplementary materials provide some exploration (Figure 2), a more systematic analysis is needed.
*   **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are performed on a single dataset from a specific search engine (Yandex). The results might not generalize to other datasets or ranking tasks.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with the state-of-the-art method [21] could be more thorough. A wider range of parameter settings for [21] and a more detailed ablation study could strengthen the conclusions. The discussion around step sizes for GBP is quite limited.
*   **Notation:** The paper occasionally uses overloaded notation (e.g., ϕ) which can create confusion.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in web ranking and provides valuable theoretical contributions in the form of convergence guarantees for novel optimization methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, even if the improvements are incremental.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical improvements over the state-of-the-art are relatively modest, raising questions about the significance of the results.
*   The lack of a comprehensive parameter sensitivity analysis and the limited scope of the experiments weaken the generalizability of the findings.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of web ranking and optimization. The theoretical results are valuable, and the experimental validation is encouraging. However, the modest practical improvements and the limitations in parameter analysis and dataset diversity raise some concerns.  Given the strong theoretical contributions and the clear presentation, I lean towards **acceptance**, but with the caveat that the authors should address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, a more in-depth analysis of parameter sensitivity, a wider range of experimental evaluations, and a discussion of the practical implications of the incremental improvements are crucial for strengthening the paper.
