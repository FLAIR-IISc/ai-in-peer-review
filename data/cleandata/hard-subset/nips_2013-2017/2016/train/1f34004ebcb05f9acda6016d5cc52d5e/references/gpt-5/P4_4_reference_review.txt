Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a framework in which node and edge weights are linear functions of features, with parameters shared across queries. The objective is a pairwise squared hinge-like loss over judged document pairs per query. The core challenge is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires stationary distributions of query-specific Markov chains and their parameter derivatives, which are only accessible via iterative approximations.

The authors propose two two-level algorithms. At the lower level, they use a linearly convergent PageRank solver (attributed to Nesterov–Nemirovski [14] and related work [17]) to approximate stationary distributions with arbitrary accuracy, and extend it to compute derivatives by propagating sensitivities through the Markov operator (building on iterative eigen-derivative methods [20, 21]). At the upper level, they develop:
- A gradient-free (zeroth-order) algorithm for constrained optimization with an inexact, biased oracle (bounded additive error), using randomized sphere smoothing and projected updates. They provide a convergence bound for convex f, adapting and extending results of Nesterov–Spokoiny [13] and related bandit/zeroth-order works [1, 7] to constrained and inexact settings.
- An adaptive projected gradient method for (possibly) nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle satisfying a generalized (δ, L)-oracle condition [25], with backtracking on the unknown Lipschitz-like constant. They prove convergence to a stationary point measured via the gradient mapping norm.

They analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and total complexity for both methods, yielding overall arithmetic operation bounds as functions of graph/query dimensions and desired accuracy. Experiments on a Yandex query-dependent browsing graph (600 queries, ~11.7K vertices, ~7.5K edges, ~1.7K labels) compare their methods against the prior two-level gradient method [21] that uses power iterations for both stationary distributions and derivatives. The proposed methods achieve lower loss and provide theoretical convergence guarantees absent in [21].

Quality (technical soundness):
- Lower-level approximations: Lemma 1 provides an explicit N ensuring |f̃ − f| ≤ δ1 with arithmetic complexity accounting for graph sparsity and dimensions. Lemma 2 provides a bound on the gradient approximation error in infinity norm, with explicit iteration counts N1, N2 and complexity. The derivative recursion (Πk) and averaging scheme mirror the stationary distribution approximation and are plausible; details are deferred to the supplement via constant β1.
- Gradient-free method: Theorem 1 gives an expected suboptimality bound for convex f under bounded oracle error, with bias from both smoothing and additive noise; the result aligns with known dependencies on m, τ, L, δ and constrained projection. The adaptation to a ball constraint and sphere randomization is reasonable.
- Gradient-based method: The inexact first-order oracle condition (5.1) is standard (generalizing [25]). The adaptive backtracking and projected update are well-motivated; Theorem 3 bounds the gradient mapping norm, appropriate for nonconvex problems. The derivation relies on having f ∈ C1,1L and on constructing oracle outputs f̃, g̃ with compatible errors, which the authors argue using Lemmas 1–2. While [25] treats convex settings, the presented analysis targets nonconvex composite problems; full proofs are in the supplement.
- End-to-end complexity: Theorems 2 and 4 combine oracle costs with iteration counts to produce overall operation bounds; the dependencies on α, r, m, p, s, |Q| are explicit.

Potential technical limitations:
- The gradient-free upper-level analysis assumes convexity of f on Φ, while the underlying learning objective is generally nonconvex. The method is therefore analyzed for local convex regions; in practice Φ is chosen as a fixed Euclidean ball around the all-ones vector, but no convexity verification is provided.
- For the gradient-based method, the nonconvex analysis depends on the (δ, L)-oracle condition; the paper references “similar to [25]” to justify that Lemmas 1–2 induce (5.1), but [25] is convex. The supplement presumably fills this gap; including a brief proof sketch in the main text would strengthen confidence.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear separation of model, lower-level approximations, and upper-level algorithms. Some notation-heavy sections (e.g., constants like β1) defer essential definitions to the supplement, which can impede readability. There are occasional typos and minor grammatical issues. Experimental descriptions lack some details (e.g., value of α, exact stopping criteria for the baseline) and present results primarily in terms of loss rather than standard ranking metrics.

Originality:
The work combines and extends several strands: (i) using the Nesterov–Nemirovski PageRank solver for both values and derivatives in a supervised setting, (ii) adapting zeroth-order methods to constrained settings with bounded additive oracle error, and (iii) proposing an adaptive inexact-oracle gradient method for nonconvex composite problems. While each component builds on prior literature [13, 14, 7, 25], their integration, the derivative computation scheme, and the explicit end-to-end complexity trade-off for Supervised PageRank appear to be novel and useful.

Significance:
The problem is relevant to graph-based ranking and learning to rank on large graphs. Providing convergence guarantees where prior practice relied on inexact power iterations is valuable. The empirical improvements over [21] on a real dataset support practical utility. However, the experimental scope is modest, comparisons are limited to a single baseline in the same family, and ranking-quality metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) are absent, which limits the demonstrated impact for practitioners.

Pros:
- Clear two-level design with controllable lower-level accuracy and explicit complexity bounds.
- Rigorous convergence analyses for both zeroth- and first-order methods under inexact oracles.
- Practical derivative computation scheme avoiding full Jacobians in large graphs.
- Empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art Supervised PageRank training method [21].
- Discussion of accuracy–complexity trade-offs relevant for large-scale deployment.

Cons:
- Zeroth-order analysis requires convexity on Φ; no evidence that the chosen Φ satisfies this in practice.
- Nonconvex inexact-oracle gradient analysis references convex prior work; key technical steps are deferred.
- Experiments report only loss values; no standard ranking metrics or broader baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART).
- Several experimental details are under-specified (α, stopping rules, wall-clock/runtime comparisons).
- Some constants (e.g., β1) and assumptions appear only in the supplement, affecting readability.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report NDCG/MAP on test sets and compare against additional learning-to-rank baselines and unsupervised baselines (PageRank, BrowseRank).
- Provide wall-clock runtime and per-iteration cost comparisons between the proposed lower-level method and the power method, including derivative computation overhead.
- Clarify experimental settings: α, choice of Φ and projection behavior, stopping criteria for all methods, selection of N, N1, N2 from target δ, and sensitivity analyses.
- Move definitions of key constants and a brief proof sketch showing that Lemmas 1–2 imply (5.1) into the main paper.
- Discuss the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method, including empirical checks or conditions under which it is expected to hold, and guidance on choosing Φ to contain a good solution while preserving feasibility and positivity.

Scope:
The paper is well within NeurIPS scope (optimization methods, learning on graphs, ranking).
