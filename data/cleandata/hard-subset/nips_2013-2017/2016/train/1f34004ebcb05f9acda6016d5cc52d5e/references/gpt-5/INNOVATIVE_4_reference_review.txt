Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank by framing it as a two-level optimization problem with inexact oracles. On the lower level, instead of the classical power method, the authors use the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution and (via an extension) its derivatives to any prescribed accuracy. On the upper level, they propose two alternatives: (i) a gradient-free, randomized sphere-smoothing method that handles constrained optimization with a uniformly bounded oracle error, and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. They prove convergence guarantees and complexity bounds, analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity, and show empirical improvements over the prior Supervised PageRank training method [21] on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and combines several strands: random-walk based ranking (PageRank, HITS, Supervised PageRank), random gradient-free optimization via smoothing (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny; bandit-style methods), first-order nonconvex methods (e.g., Ghadimi–Lan) and inexact-oracle frameworks (e.g., Devolder et al.). The lower-level computation leverages Nesterov–Nemirovski for stationary distributions; derivative computation builds on iterative methods for eigenderivatives. The paper’s claimed novelties are adapting gradient-free methods to constrained problems with bounded, biased zero-order noise, and an adaptive projected gradient scheme with inexact first-order oracle for nonconvex composite problems, and applying these to Supervised PageRank with end-to-end complexity guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear problem decomposition with carefully controlled inexact oracles at the lower level; practical insight into accuracy–cost trade-offs.
- Convergence guarantees: the gradient-free method for convex objectives with bounded oracle error under constraints, and an adaptive inexact projected gradient method guaranteeing decrease in a gradient-mapping surrogate for nonconvex problems.
- Lower-level use of Nesterov–Nemirovski provides explicit accuracy control and improved complexity over power iteration; extension to derivative computation is useful.
- Application to Supervised PageRank provides, for the first time (to my knowledge), learning algorithms with provable convergence guarantees rather than heuristic stopping rules.
- Empirical evidence indicates improvements over the state-of-the-art training procedure [21]; gradient-free avoids expensive matrix-derivative computations.

Weaknesses
- Novelty feels incremental. The upper-level methods are largely adaptations of known frameworks (random smoothing, inexact oracle, backtracking/prox-grad) to a fairly standard constrained setting; the theory is useful but not conceptually bold. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning against closely related bandit/zeroth-order constrained methods and inexact nonconvex composite methods.
- Experimental evaluation is thin and does not use standard IR metrics. Results are primarily on the training loss; there is no report of NDCG/MAP@k or click-based metrics, and only a single (relatively small) dataset is used. Comparisons are limited to [21]; there is no comparison to strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) or to Supervised Random Walks (Backstrom & Leskovec).
- Practical impact of the complexity bounds is unclear: constants are large, and there is little wall-clock/runtime/memory profiling. It would help to see scalability to larger graphs, and sensitivity to α, R, τ, and lower-level iteration counts N, N1, N2.
- Assumptions and choices (e.g., local convexity for the gradient-free method, Euclidean ball constraints despite scale invariance, selection of L and ε) feel somewhat ad hoc; more guidance or adaptivity would strengthen the practical story.
- Presentation: numerous typos and incomplete references make the paper harder to follow; some proofs are deferred with limited intuition in the main text.

Questions for the authors
- Please report test-set ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) and compare against standard LTR baselines and Supervised Random Walks, not only the loss in Eq. (2.4).
- Provide wall-clock/runtime and memory comparisons for the lower-level methods (power vs Nesterov–Nemirovski), and sensitivity plots for α, τ, R, and oracle accuracies.
- Clarify the novelty relative to existing constrained zeroth-order methods under biased noise; how does your bound compare in dimension and noise dependence?
- For the nonconvex method, can you report gradient-mapping norms across iterations to substantiate convergence to a stationary point?
- Given scale invariance, why constrain Φ as an ℓ2-ball around 1 rather than a simplex-like set? Any impact on identifiability?

Pros for acceptance
- Brings theoretical rigor (inexact-oracle convergence and complexity) to learning Supervised PageRank, a practically relevant problem.
- Useful combination of lower-level accurate PageRank solvers with upper-level methods; gradient-free alternative is appealing for very large models.
- Solid analysis and sensible algorithms; some empirical improvements.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty from a conceptual standpoint; mostly adaptations and combinations of established techniques.
- Empirical evaluation is not up to NIPS standards (no standard ranking metrics, limited baselines, small dataset, no scalability study).
- Presentation/refs need substantial polishing.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses a relevant problem and provides clean theoretical guarantees and a practical algorithmic recipe; however, the conceptual novelty is modest and the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently compelling for NIPS. Strengthening experiments (standard IR metrics, stronger baselines, larger datasets, scalability) and clarifying the theoretical novelty relative to closely related zeroth-order and inexact-oracle literature would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: medium.
