This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, an extension of the classic PageRank algorithm that incorporates node and edge features. The authors identify a key weakness in the existing state-of-the-art learning algorithm [21]: its use of the power method for estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives provides no formal guarantee of convergence for the overall optimization procedure. To remedy this, the paper proposes two new two-level optimization algorithms, one gradient-based and one gradient-free. The core idea is to replace the power method with a numerically stable, linearly convergent method [17] that provides an "inexact oracle" with controllable error bounds. The paper then develops optimization frameworks that can provably converge to a stationary point despite relying on these inexact function and gradient evaluations.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength is its focus on methodological soundness and theoretical rigor. It makes a clear and valid criticism of the prior art's lack of convergence guarantees and builds a comprehensive theoretical framework to address it.

1.  **Convergence Guarantees:** The main contribution is the provision of formal convergence proofs for learning the Supervised PageRank model. The analysis for both the gradient-based method (for non-convex problems) and the gradient-free method (under a local convexity assumption) is detailed and appears to be technically sound. This provides a solid theoretical foundation that was previously missing.

2.  **Principled Use of Inexact Oracles:** The paper does a good job of framing the problem in the context of optimization with inexact oracles. It thoughtfully connects the number of lower-level iterations (N) required to achieve a certain accuracy (δ) with the upper-level algorithm's convergence rate, resulting in overall complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4). This analysis of the trade-off between computational effort and precision is well-executed.

3.  **Grounded in Established Theory:** The proposed methods are built upon a strong foundation of established optimization research, including work by Nesterov, Nemirovski, and Lan. This grounding lends credibility to the approach, as it extends well-understood principles to a new and challenging setting involving constrained optimization with inexact, biased oracles.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the theoretical strengths, the paper is significantly lacking in its experimental validation and fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of its contributions.

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments compare the proposed methods (GFN, GBN) only against the single baseline from which the work is derived (GBP [21]). While this is the direct state-of-the-art for this specific problem, the evaluation is inadequate for a top-tier venue like NIPS. Most critically, the evaluation is based entirely on the value of the loss function (2.4). The ultimate goal of a ranking algorithm is to produce a high-quality ranking, which is typically measured by standard information retrieval metrics like NDCG, MAP, or ERR. The paper provides no evidence that achieving a lower loss value translates to any improvement in these standard metrics. The claim in the introduction that the proposed methods "outperform it in the ranking quality in experiments" is not substantiated by the results presented, which only show loss values. Without this, the practical benefit of the proposed methods remains unproven.

2.  **Limited Practical Significance:** The paper focuses exclusively on the Supervised PageRank model. While an interesting model, its widespread adoption and impact are not well established. The contributions are therefore tied to a relatively niche problem. The broader claims about increasing "the reliability and speed of a search engine" are highly speculative and not supported by the evidence provided. The work would have been much more compelling if the proposed optimization methods were tested on a wider range of machine learning problems that fit the inexact oracle framework.

3.  **Moderate Novelty of Optimization Methods:** While the application and analysis are novel, the core optimization algorithms themselves represent incremental extensions of existing work. Algorithm 1 adapts random gradient-free methods [16] to a constrained setting, and Algorithm 2 (referred to as Algorithm 4 in the text) is an adaptive gradient method for handling an unknown Lipschitz constant, a well-studied area. The primary novelty is in the careful integration and analysis within the specific inexact oracle setting derived from the PageRank problem, rather than a breakthrough in optimization methodology itself.

4.  **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set Φ. The authors acknowledge the function is generally non-convex but justify this by assuming Φ is a small vicinity around a local minimum. This is a strong and convenient assumption that may not hold in a general setting, limiting the applicability of the theoretical result.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a theoretically rigorous treatment of an important problem: provably convergent optimization for a feature-rich PageRank model. The theoretical analysis is the work's main accomplishment. However, the paper's claims of superiority are not backed by sufficient empirical evidence. The lack of standard ranking metrics in the evaluation is a major flaw, making it impossible to assess whether the proposed methods offer any real-world benefit over the baseline. While the theoretical contributions are sound, the unproven practical utility and limited scope of the application make it a borderline case. I am skeptical of endorsing a new optimization approach whose practical advantages have not been demonstrated according to the standard practices of the target domain (i.e., information retrieval).
