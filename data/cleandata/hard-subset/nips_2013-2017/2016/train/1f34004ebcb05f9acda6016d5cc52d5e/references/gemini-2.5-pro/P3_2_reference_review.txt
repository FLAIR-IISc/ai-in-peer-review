This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that enriches the classic PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features into the random walk's restart and transition probabilities. The core challenge is that learning these parameters involves solving a non-convex optimization problem where the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly. The value of the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) must be approximated, leading to an "inexact oracle." The paper's primary contribution is the development of two novel two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—that, unlike previous work, come with formal convergence guarantees in this inexact setting.

The proposed methods build upon existing optimization literature. The gradient-free method adapts the random search approach of Nesterov [16] to a constrained setting with a biased, inexact zeroth-order oracle. The gradient-based method extends the adaptive gradient framework of Ghadimi and Lan [7] to handle constrained non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle. A key element for both approaches is the use of a linearly convergent method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution, which allows the authors to precisely control the oracle's error (`δ`) and analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of this inner-loop approximation and the overall complexity of the outer-loop optimization.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Rigor and Significance:** The main strength of this paper is its strong theoretical contribution. The prior state-of-the-art method for Supervised PageRank [21] used a gradient descent approach with an inexact gradient computed via the power method, but lacked any proof of convergence. This paper fills that critical gap by providing the first (to my knowledge) optimization algorithms for this problem with explicit convergence rates and complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4). Establishing convergence for non-convex problems with a controllable but inexact (and potentially biased) oracle is a non-trivial and important result, connecting theoretical optimization with practical machine learning on graphs.

2.  **Novelty and Generalizability:** While the underlying optimization schemes are adapted from prior work ([7], [16]), their application and analysis in this specific context are novel. The framework for handling the inexact oracle, where the error `δ` is strategically chosen based on the main algorithm's desired accuracy `ε`, is elegant and potentially generalizable to other large-scale learning problems where the objective or gradient depends on the solution of an inner iterative process (e.g., models involving matrix inversions, fixed-point solutions, or eigenvector problems).

3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The choice of the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] for the lower-level problem is well-justified, as it provides a linear convergence rate and allows for the precise error control needed for the theoretical analysis. The development of an adaptive gradient method (Algorithm 2 in the paper) that does not require prior knowledge of the Lipschitz constant is a significant practical advantage for the gradient-based approach.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized. It clearly separates the model description, the methods for oracle approximation (Section 3), and the two proposed optimization frameworks (Sections 4 and 5). This structure makes the contributions easy to follow, despite the technical density of the material.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Assumptions and Scope:** The analysis of the gradient-free method (Section 4.2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex within the optimization domain `Φ`. The paper states, "We assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) is convex." While this is a standard technique for local convergence analysis, it feels somewhat limiting for a problem that is globally non-convex. The gradient-based method does not require this assumption, which is a major advantage that could have been emphasized more strongly in the comparison.

2.  **Experimental Depth:** The experiments successfully demonstrate that the proposed methods (GFN and GBN) achieve lower final loss values than the baseline (GBP). However, the evaluation could be strengthened in several ways:
    *   **Baseline Comparison:** The only baseline is the method from [21]. While this is the most direct competitor, including other feature-based learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART, or early graph neural network models) would provide a broader context for the performance of Supervised PageRank itself on this task.
    *   **Computational Cost:** The paper provides theoretical complexity analysis but lacks an empirical comparison of wall-clock time. The proposed methods require careful control of the inner-loop iterations (`N`, `N1`, `N2`), which could be computationally expensive. A comparison of the total runtime required for each method to reach a certain level of performance would be highly valuable.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The gradient-free method requires setting a Lipschitz constant `L`. While the paper mentions testing different values for the gradient-based method's `L0`, a more thorough analysis of how the choice of `L` affects GFN's performance and convergence would be welcome, as this is a key hyperparameter.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While generally clear, the paper is extremely dense. Some key algorithmic details are deferred to the supplementary materials (e.g., "Algorithm 2" and "Algorithm 4"). Including high-level pseudocode for the main two-level algorithms in the main text would significantly improve readability and help the reader grasp the interplay between the upper- and lower-level methods.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution by introducing two novel optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank parameters, backed by rigorous theoretical guarantees of convergence. It successfully bridges the gap between theoretical optimization for non-convex problems with inexact oracles and the practical needs of large-scale graph-based learning. The theoretical results are solid, and the experiments, while limited in scope, demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approaches over the existing state-of-the-art. The weaknesses are primarily related to the strength of some assumptions and the depth of the experimental evaluation, but they do not undermine the core contribution. The work is original, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem. It is a good fit for NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem.
*   The theoretical framework for handling inexact oracles is novel in this context and of broader interest.
*   The adaptive gradient-based method is particularly strong, as it works for non-convex functions and does not require tuning a Lipschitz constant.
*   Experimental results show a clear improvement in solution quality over the previous method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's analysis is a notable limitation.
*   The experimental comparison could be broader, particularly concerning baselines and practical runtime analysis.

**Final Verdict:** Lean Accept. The theoretical contributions are significant and address a clear and important gap in the literature.
