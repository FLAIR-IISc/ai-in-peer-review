This paper presents two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, one gradient-based and one gradient-free. The authors aim to address the convergence issues of existing methods and provide theoretical guarantees. While the paper tackles a relevant problem and attempts to provide theoretical foundations, the sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies significantly detract from its overall impact and raise concerns about the thoroughness of the work.

The introduction sets the stage reasonably well, highlighting the limitations of traditional PageRank and the potential of Supervised PageRank. However, the writing is often convoluted and lacks conciseness. For example, the statement “Despite undeniable advantages of PageRank and its mentioned modifications, these algorithms miss important aspects of the graph that are not described by its structure” is vague and doesn’t offer concrete examples. The claim that existing optimization methods cannot solve the problem due to constraints and the impossibility of calculating the exact loss function is a strong statement that requires more rigorous justification – simply stating it isn’t sufficient.

Section 2 provides a clear description of the Supervised PageRank model. However, the notation is inconsistent. For example, π0 is initially defined as a vector and then used as a distribution. The assumptions regarding non-zero outdegrees are also somewhat restrictive and should be discussed in terms of their practical implications. Moreover, the definition of Φ as a ball around ϕ̂ with radius R, while reasonable, lacks a justification for the choice of ϕ̂ and R. Why is ϕ̂ chosen as a vector of ones? This seems arbitrary.

Section 3 details the numerical calculation of f(ϕ) and its gradient. While the authors claim their method [17] has the lowest proven complexity bound, this claim isn’t adequately substantiated. A comparative table outlining the complexity of different methods would be beneficial. Lemma 1’s proof is relegated to supplementary materials, which is acceptable, but the statement itself feels somewhat detached from the main text. The analysis of computational complexity appears incomplete, lacking a clear accounting of all operations. The jump between stating Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 feels abrupt.

Section 4 on gradient-free methods builds upon existing work [1, 16, 7] but introduces additional assumptions and modifications. The extension to constrained optimization is a valuable contribution. However, the justification for choosing a Euclidean sphere for randomization feels superficial. The theorem (Theorem 1) is presented without sufficient context. What is ΞM? What is τ? These should be defined clearly within the theorem itself. The discussion of the computational complexity in Theorem 2 is unclear and relies on unexplained variables.

Section 5 develops a gradient-based approach. The generalization of [7] is a positive aspect, but the notation becomes increasingly cumbersome. Again, the justification for certain choices (e.g., the prox-function) feels lacking. Theorem 3 also suffers from undefined variables and a lack of clarity in its presentation. The relationship between the inexact oracle error (δ) and the choice of N1 and N2 is not sufficiently explained.

Section 6 presents experimental results using a real-world dataset. While the comparisons to the state-of-the-art method [21] are welcome, the experimental setup is described inadequately. What specific parameters were used for GBP? Why were different step sizes chosen? The explanation of Figure 1 is minimal. The claim that “GFN significantly outperforms” requires statistical significance testing beyond just stating p-values less than 0.005. The observation that the power method converges faster than the method in [17] is interesting but feels somewhat disconnected from the main focus of the paper. The final statement regarding the increase in search engine reliability is overly ambitious, given the scope of the work.

Throughout the paper, there is a consistent pattern of minor errors and omissions. Equations are often unlabeled, making it difficult to refer to them. The use of abbreviations (e.g., a.o.) is inconsistent. There are several instances of grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The supplementary materials are mentioned frequently but their accessibility and completeness are uncertain.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed (learning Supervised PageRank) is relevant.
*   The authors attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for convergence.
*   The gradient-free method offers an alternative to gradient-based approaches.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is riddled with minor errors, inconsistencies, and omissions.
*   The theoretical analysis is often incomplete and lacks sufficient justification.
*   The experimental setup is inadequately described, and the results are presented without sufficient statistical rigor.
*   The writing is often convoluted and difficult to follow.
*   The claims of novelty and superiority are not always convincingly supported.
*   The lack of clear explanations for choices of parameters and methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem, the numerous flaws and inconsistencies in this paper significantly diminish its quality. The lack of thoroughness and attention to detail raises concerns about the reliability of the results and the validity of the theoretical claims. While the authors demonstrate some technical skill, the overall presentation and execution fall short of the standards expected for a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**. A major revision addressing the issues outlined above, along with a more rigorous experimental evaluation, might be considered for future submission.
