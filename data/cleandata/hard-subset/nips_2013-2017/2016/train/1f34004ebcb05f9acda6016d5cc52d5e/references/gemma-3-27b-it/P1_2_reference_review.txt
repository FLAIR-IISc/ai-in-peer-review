## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, where node and edge weights are learned from features to improve ranking quality. The authors identify a lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based methods and propose two novel two-level algorithms: one gradient-based and one gradient-free. Both utilize a more efficient method for approximating the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk and provide convergence guarantees, a key contribution.  The paper also analyzes the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity and demonstrates empirically that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21]. It positions itself against the limitations of existing methods, specifically the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees. The work references and compares itself to other methods for approximating stationary distributions (e.g., power method [18, 2, 21]), and acknowledges related optimization techniques (e.g., [16, 7]). The authors position their work as filling a gap by providing *provably* convergent methods for this specific problem, combined with practical improvements in ranking quality.  They also connect to broader literature on inexact optimization [1, 25].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** This is the primary strength.  The authors provide convergence analysis for both proposed methods, addressing a crucial shortcoming of the existing state-of-the-art. This is a significant contribution for a field often reliant on heuristics.
* **Efficiency Improvements:**  The use of [17]'s method for approximating the stationary distribution is claimed to be more efficient than previous approaches. The analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and complexity is valuable.
* **Dual Approach:** Offering both gradient-based and gradient-free methods broadens the applicability of the work, catering to different scenarios where derivative calculations might be prohibitive.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over the existing approach.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the Supervised PageRank problem and motivates the need for improved optimization methods.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper includes detailed lemmas and theorems, alongside complexity analysis, demonstrating a rigorous approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and mathematically rigorous, potentially making it difficult to follow for readers not deeply familiar with Markov chains and optimization theory. While detail is good, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The experimental section doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the methods to different hyperparameter settings.  While some parameters are mentioned, a more systematic analysis would strengthen the empirical claims. Specifically, how sensitive are the results to the choice of 'L' in the gradient-free method?
* **Limited Comparison:** While the paper outperforms [21], comparisons with other state-of-the-art ranking algorithms beyond Supervised PageRank are limited. Adding comparisons with established methods like LambdaMART or RankBoost would provide valuable context.
* **Dataset Specificity:** The results are demonstrated on a specific web ranking dataset. It's unclear how well these methods would generalize to other graph ranking tasks (e.g., social network influence maximization).
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of convexity of f(ϕ) on Φ is a strong one and may not hold in all practical scenarios. The authors acknowledge this but it would be beneficial to discuss the potential impact of non-convexity.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation in the field of Supervised PageRank – the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees.
* The proposed methods are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrably outperform the existing state-of-the-art.
* The rigorous analysis of complexity and trade-offs adds significant value to the work.
* The work presents a practical contribution with potential real-world impact in web search and ranking.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high level of mathematical complexity might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more detailed hyperparameter tuning and comparisons to a wider range of ranking algorithms.
* The reliance on a specific dataset raises questions about the generalizability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The authors successfully address a key limitation of existing Supervised PageRank methods by providing provably convergent optimization algorithms. While the paper's density and limited experimental scope are minor concerns, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept**.

I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results make a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. I would suggest the authors consider simplifying the presentation slightly and expanding the experimental evaluation to further strengthen their claims.
