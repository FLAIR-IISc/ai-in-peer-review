Summary and relation to prior work
The paper revisits Supervised PageRank (SPR) where restart and transition probabilities are parameterized as linear functions of node/edge features. Learning is cast as minimizing a pairwise hinge-squared loss over query-dependent graphs, with the stationary distribution π(ϕ) defined by a random walk with restarts. The central difficulty is that both f(ϕ) and ∇f(ϕ) require solving (and differentiating) a Markov chain fixed-point per query, which can only be approximated.

The authors propose two two-level learning schemes. At the lower level, they approximate π using the Nesterov–Nemirovski geometrically weighted power series (linear convergence with rate 1−α), and extend it to approximate the Jacobian dπ/dϕ via a related recursion. At the upper level, they instantiate:
- A gradient-free (zeroth-order) projected method with random directions on the unit sphere and an inexact oracle (additive error δ), providing an expected suboptimality bound (Theorem 1) and an overall complexity when combined with the lower-level approximation (Theorem 2).
- An adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex, composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle (Theorem 3), yielding a stationarity guarantee (in terms of gradient mapping norm) and total complexity when linked to the lower-level approximations (Theorem 4).

Compared to the earlier SPR training in [21], which used standard power methods for π and its derivatives and a simple gradient descent without convergence guarantees under inexactness, this paper brings inexact-oracle analysis (related to Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov for convex problems and Ghadimi–Lan for nonconvex) and a lower-level solver with explicit error control. The paper also relates to Nesterov–Spokoiny and Agarwal–Dekel–Xiao for gradient-free optimization, adapting to constrained domains and bounded additive oracle error.

Strengths
- Lower-level computation: Using the Neumann-series-like estimator (Eq. 3.1) with explicit geometric weights is a principled way to control error in π with linear convergence and tighter complexity than plain power iteration. The derivative recursion (Eqs. 3.2–3.3) is natural via differentiating the fixed-point and approximating the associated Neumann series.
- Inexact-oracle optimization: The gradient-free method handles bounded additive errors and constraints with a clean expected-error decomposition in τ and δ, and the gradient-based method provides an adaptive backtracking scheme with guarantees for nonconvex composite objectives under an inexact oracle of the form (5.1).
- End-to-end complexity accounting: Lemmas 1–2 tie the inner approximation accuracies (N, N1, N2) to outer tolerances, enabling overall arithmetic complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4). This addresses a key gap in [21].
- Empirical study on real query-dependent browsing graphs from Yandex, with featureized nodes/edges and multiple query subsets, shows that the methods improve the training loss over the baseline and over the prior SPR training, with some statistically significant gains.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumptions for gradient-free method: Theorem 1 requires convexity of f on Φ and x* in the interior. The problem is generally nonconvex; the paper assumes Φ is a “small vicinity” where f is convex. This local convexity assumption is not verified empirically or theoretically and weakens the guarantee for the gradient-free method in the intended setting.
- Nonconvex theory vs practice: For the gradient-based method, the analysis builds on the inexact oracle inequality (5.1), which requires f to be L-smooth. The loss uses s(x)=(x_+)^2, which is C^1 with Lipschitz derivative, but the composition with π(ϕ) needs smoothness and Lipschitzness of ∇π(ϕ). While Lemmas 1–2 control approximation errors, the smoothness and Lipschitz constants of f (and value of L used in theorems) are not characterized; β1 is left “explicitly computable” but unspecified. Some discussion/derivation of bounds on L in terms of α, feature norms, and graph degrees would strengthen the claims.
- Derivative recursion correctness: The Π-recurrence (3.2–3.3) implicitly solves for (I−(1−α)P^T)^{-1} acting on α dπ0/dϕ + (1−α)∑_i dpi/dϕ [π]_i. A concise derivation and conditions under which interchanging differentiation and series truncation is valid would help; currently this is deferred to the supplement.
- Experimental evaluation is limited:
  - Metrics: Results are reported primarily in terms of the training loss. For ranking, standard IR metrics (NDCG, MAP, ERR) on held-out data are essential, especially since the loss is only a surrogate. The claim “outperform it in the ranking quality” is not substantiated in the provided text.
  - Baselines: Beyond [21], comparisons to alternative supervised random walks (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011) or learning-to-rank baselines that do not require stationary distributions (RankSVM/RankNet with the same features) would contextualize gains.
  - Fairness/tuning: The GBP baseline is set to a fixed number of power iterations (100) for both π and its derivatives. A stopping criterion based on residual tolerance would be more equitable; likewise, wall-clock times and per-iteration costs for all methods should be reported.
  - Scale: The dataset (≈600 queries, ≈11.7K vertices total) is relatively small for web-scale claims, and r (pair counts) can be large; the complexities depend on r, s, and |Q| with sizable constants. Demonstrating scalability on larger graphs or using sampling strategies for pairs would be valuable.
- Clarity/presentation: There are numerous typographical issues and inconsistent notation (e.g., “we concider,” “probablities,” missing indices), and some definitions (e.g., β1, details of Algorithm numbering vs text) are only in the supplement. Several references are incomplete (years/venues missing). A more careful editorial pass would improve readability.

Quality
Technically solid overall with plausible derivations and appropriate use of inexact-oracle analysis. Some key smoothness constants and proofs are relegated to the supplement. The empirical section does not fully validate the practical advantages or scalability.

Clarity
The main ideas are understandable, but exposition needs polishing. Provide a short derivation of the derivative recursion, state assumptions clearly (feature nonnegativity, positivity of denominators), and define all constants in the main text.

Originality
Moderate. The lower-level estimator and its linear convergence are known; the derivative approximation via truncated series is a natural extension. The main novelty lies in knitting together (i) controlled-accuracy lower-level computations, (ii) constrained gradient-free optimization with bounded additive noise, and (iii) an adaptive inexact first-order method for nonconvex composite problems, and then providing end-to-end complexity bounds for SPR learning.

Significance
Potentially useful contribution to learning on graphs where stationary distributions must be computed approximately. The theoretical guarantees address a gap in prior SPR work. However, practical significance would be stronger with more comprehensive and scalable experiments and standard ranking metrics.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide explicit bounds (in terms of α, max feature norms, maximum outdegree s, and graph size p) for L and β1? This would make Theorems 2 and 4 more interpretable.
- How sensitive are results to the choice of Φ (radius, center em) given the scale invariance of ϕ? Would projecting to the simplex (‖ϕ‖1=1, ϕ≥0) remove degeneracy more naturally?
- Report test NDCG@k and MAP, alongside loss, and include wall-clock comparisons with matched tolerances for the lower-level solvers.
- Consider stochastic approximations for r (pair sampling) to reduce dependence on potentially quadratic pair counts.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical issue (inexact lower-level computations) with rigorous analysis.
- Provides both zeroth- and first-order methods with convergence guarantees under inexact oracles.
- Demonstrates improved loss over prior SPR training on real data.

Cons
- Local convexity assumption for gradient-free method is unverified.
- Experimental evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics and broader baselines; limited scale.
- Some theoretical constants and assumptions are under-specified in the main text.
- Presentation issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10), if the authors can strengthen the experimental section with standard ranking metrics, fair baseline tuning, and clearer constants/assumptions. Otherwise, borderline. Confidence: 0.7
