- Paper proposes learning Supervised PageRank parameters via linear feature-based transition/restart models and a pairwise squared hinge loss over query-specific stationary distributions.  
- Novelty: uses Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme to approximate π and extends it to approximate dπ/dϕ, producing anytime inexact oracles with controllable accuracy.  
- Novelty: develops two upper-level optimizers that provably handle inexact oracles — a constrained random gradient-free method with biased zero-order oracle and an adaptive projected first-order method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with unknown Lipschitz constant.  
- Theoretically couples lower-level approximation accuracy and upper-level convergence with complexity bounds.  
- Lower-level extension of [17] to estimate π and dπ/dϕ with linear convergence is reasonable; estimators achieve geometric error decay O((1−α)^N).  
- Critical practical dependence on constants (notably β1 defined only in supplement) may heavily affect actual complexity.  
- Theorem 1 meaningfully extends random gradient-free optimization to constrained domains with biased, inexact function values, but convexity and interior-solution assumptions limit applicability.  
- Algorithm 2 generalizes adaptive projected gradient methods to inexact first-order oracles and unknown L in constrained, possibly nonconvex settings, with stationarity guarantees via a gradient-mapping norm.  
- Clarification needed on how the gradient-mapping stopping measure corresponds to KKT conditions for Euclidean-ball constraints.  
- The objective is C^{1,1} (gradient Lipschitz) given positivity assumptions on π(ϕ), but this should be explicitly stated.  
- Two-level complexity bounds are informative but involve many heavy constants (m,p,s,r,|Q|,α) and lack numerical illustrations for practical guidance.  
- Experiments use a small dataset (600 queries, ~11.7K pages, ~7.5K edges, ~1.7K labeled pairs), limiting scalability claims.  
- Evaluation reports loss values but omits standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k) needed to substantiate ranking improvements.  
- Baselines are limited to the prior gradient-based [21]; missing comparisons to learning-to-rank baselines (RankSVM, LambdaMART) and other PageRank/stationary-computation schemes.  
- Hyperparameter tuning and fairness of comparisons are unclear; sensitivity analyses, wall-clock time, and memory usage are not reported.  
- Need runtime breakdown and lower-level cost reporting (N, N1, N2) to clarify trade-offs between gradient-free and gradient-based methods.  
- Paper is generally well organized but has typos, grammar issues, and omits key constants/assumptions from the main text (e.g., β1, positivity region for Φ).  
- Recommend adding brief proof sketches for Lemmas 1–2 and practical guidance for selecting ε and δ.  
- Contributions are mostly careful adaptations and integrations rather than fundamentally new theory, but the end-to-end inexact-oracle framework is useful.  
- Theoretical framework for inexact-oracle optimization is broadly valuable, but modest empirical evaluation limits practical impact.  
- Pros: clear two-level design, controllable inexact oracles, convergence guarantees for adapted zero- and first-order methods, evidence of improvement over [21].  
- Cons: small-scale experiments, missing standard metrics and baselines, opaque constants in supplement, restrictive assumptions for gradient-free method, clarity issues.  
- Suggestions: report NDCG/MAP, add larger or synthetic scalability tests, include strong baselines, provide runtime/memory and sensitivity analyses, surface key constants and proof sketches in main text.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid theoretical contributions but empirical evaluation needs strengthening to increase practical impact.
