

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in the literature on Supervised PageRank by proposing two novel two-level optimization methods with convergence guarantees. The authors recognize that existing methods for learning Supervised PageRank parameters lack theoretical convergence proofs due to the use of approximate calculations of the stationary distribution and its derivatives. They develop a framework where the lower level computes approximations to the stationary distribution and its derivatives with controllable accuracy, while the upper level uses these approximations to optimize the parameters. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for non-convex problems, and (2) a gradient-free method adapted to handle constrained optimization with inexact oracle information. The authors provide theoretical convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods and validate them on a real-world web ranking task, demonstrating superior performance over the state-of-the-art.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novelty**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, which was previously missing in the literature. The two-level optimization framework with inexact oracle is well-motivated and properly positioned against prior work (e.g., [21], [5]). The authors' adaptation of gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact oracle is particularly noteworthy.

2. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper provides thorough complexity analysis for both methods, including precise bounds on the number of iterations required at both the lower and upper levels to achieve a given accuracy. The theoretical results (Lemmas 1-2, Theorems 1-4) are well-supported with detailed proofs in the supplementary materials.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental results on a real-world web ranking dataset demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality. The paper also provides a thoughtful analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.

4. **Clarity of Contribution**: The paper clearly articulates the advantages of each method (gradient-based vs. gradient-free) and provides concrete guidance for practitioners regarding parameter selection and implementation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: While the experiments demonstrate improved ranking quality, they are conducted on a single dataset (Yandex) with limited scale (≈11.7K vertices, ≈7.5K edges across 600 queries). The paper would benefit from additional experiments on larger or more diverse datasets to better establish the scalability of the methods.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that the gradient-free method avoids "calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix" but doesn't provide a concrete comparison of computational complexity between the gradient-based and gradient-free approaches. A more detailed discussion of when each method would be preferable in practice would strengthen the paper.

3. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The paper assumes that the objective function is locally convex in a small vicinity of the optimal solution. While this is reasonable for practical optimization, the paper could better discuss how common this assumption is in practice for Supervised PageRank learning problems.

4. **Comparison with Alternative Methods**: The paper compares only with the state-of-the-art method [21], but doesn't discuss how their methods compare with other standard optimization approaches (e.g., stochastic gradient descent, L-BFGS) that might be applicable to this problem.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong technical contribution that addresses a real gap in the literature on Supervised PageRank. The paper's theoretical framework is sound, its complexity analysis is thorough, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based ranking problems.

The main weaknesses are the limited experimental scope and insufficient discussion of when practitioners should choose one method over the other. However, these are relatively minor concerns compared to the paper's substantial theoretical contribution and validation.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications for web search and ranking applications. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and provides valuable convergence guarantees for a problem that previously lacked such guarantees. While the experimental section could be strengthened with additional datasets, the theoretical contributions and validation on a real-world dataset are sufficient to establish the paper's significance.

The paper advances the state of the art in optimization for graph-based learning problems and provides a framework that could be applied to other similar problems in network analysis. The clarity of the exposition, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis, makes this an excellent contribution to NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the experimental section, include a comparison with standard optimization methods (e.g., SGD, L-BFGS) to better contextualize the performance gains.

2. Add a discussion of the practical trade-offs between the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, including computational complexity comparisons for large-scale problems.

3. Consider adding a brief analysis of how the methods scale to extremely large graphs (e.g., millions of nodes) based on the complexity bounds provided.

4. Clarify the assumption of local convexity and discuss how common this is in practice for Supervised PageRank learning.

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and graph-based learning, and I believe it would be a significant addition to the NIPS proceedings.
