- Proposes two-level gradient-based and gradient-free methods for learning Supervised PageRank using inexact oracles with provable convergence guarantees and shows empirical improvement over prior work.

- Theoretical contribution: first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank and a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact first- and zero-order oracles.

- Practical relevance: experimental results on a real-world web ranking task show statistically significant gains (p < 0.005) over the state-of-the-art.

- Algorithmic novelty: adapts Nesterov’s linearly convergent method for approximating stationary distributions within a two-level optimization framework.

- Analysis: provides detailed complexity bounds for both proposed algorithms, clarifying trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.

- Convexity concern: claim of local convexity near ϕ* is not clearly justified given the inherent non-convexity from πq(ϕ), risking the validity of theoretical guarantees.

- Complexity concern: derived complexity bounds (e.g., Theorem 4) scale very poorly for large graphs and need more intuition or potential improvements.

- Experimental scope concern: experiments use only 600 queries (50% training), which is small for real-world ranking and may limit generalizability.

- Gradient-free justification weak: claim that gradient-free avoids expensive derivative computations is unconvincing since PageRank derivatives can be computed efficiently via iterative methods.

- Comparison gap: limited comparison to prior work ([21]) and insufficient discussion of relations to other optimization approaches for graph learning (e.g., [6,12,20]).

- Overall: solid and valuable theoretical advance with practical promise, but theoretical justifications (convexity, complexity) and experimental validation need strengthening.

- Recommendation: Weak Accept / Borderline Accept contingent on addressing convexity justification, complexity concerns, broader experiments, and fuller comparisons with related methods.
