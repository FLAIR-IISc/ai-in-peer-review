This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, a task with clear relevance to ranking problems in various domains. The core idea – investigating and improving optimization methods for learning the parameters within this framework – is promising. The authors identify a gap in existing work regarding convergence guarantees and attempt to address this with both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches. This focus on theoretical underpinnings, alongside empirical validation, is commendable. The exploration of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity is also a valuable contribution.

However, while the paper presents a substantial body of work, it feels somewhat… dense. The theoretical development is extensive, and while rigorous, it lacks a clear narrative thread. The presentation jumps between general frameworks and specific applications, making it difficult to grasp the key innovations without significant effort.  The numerous lemmas and theorems, while likely correct, aren’t always motivated sufficiently, leaving the reader unsure *why* a particular result is crucial. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, addressing a significant limitation of prior work.
* **Novelty:**  The extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact oracles, and the corresponding convergence analysis, is a novel contribution. Adapting these techniques to Supervised PageRank is also a valuable step.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper analyzes the trade-offs between accuracy, generalization, and computational complexity, offering practical insights.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is difficult to follow. The abundance of mathematical notation and lemmas, coupled with a somewhat disjointed presentation, hinder understanding. A more streamlined and motivated exposition is needed.
* **Incremental Nature:**  While theoretically sound, the improvements over the existing state-of-the-art (as presented) seem somewhat incremental. The empirical gains, while present, aren’t dramatic enough to fully justify the significant theoretical investment. It's unclear if the added complexity is warranted by the performance boost.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate improvement on the chosen dataset, the breadth of evaluation is limited. It would be helpful to see results on a wider variety of graph structures and ranking tasks to assess the generalizability of the proposed methods.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices (e.g., L in the gradient-free method) but doesn’t provide sufficient guidance on how to select these parameters in practice.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Supervised PageRank – the lack of theoretical guarantees.
* The proposed methods are novel and offer a rigorous approach to optimization in this context.
* The empirical results, though modest, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* The analysis of trade-offs is insightful and potentially valuable for practitioners.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper’s clarity and presentation significantly hinder its impact.
* The improvements over existing methods are incremental, and the added complexity may not be justified.
* The scope of experimental evaluation is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid piece of technical work, but its impact is diminished by its presentation. The theoretical contributions are valuable, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature. However, the paper feels overly complex and lacks a compelling narrative. 

I am leaning towards **acceptance**, but with a strong recommendation for revision. The authors should prioritize clarity and streamlining the presentation, focusing on the core innovations and their practical implications. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation would also strengthen the paper considerably. If the authors can address these concerns, this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field. The boldness of tackling the theoretical underpinnings is appreciated, and I believe the underlying ideas are strong enough to warrant a chance for improvement.
