

# Review for "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank parameters, which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features to weight random walks. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) to solve the non-convex optimization problem with inexact oracle information, which previous work [21] lacked theoretical guarantees for. The paper makes significant contributions by providing convergence rate analysis and complexity bounds for both methods, demonstrating superior performance on real-world web ranking tasks compared to the state-of-the-art.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs of convergence rates and complexity bounds. The authors carefully analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy (determined by the number of iterations in the power method) and overall computational complexity. The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task consistently demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21], with statistical significance (p < 0.005). The paper properly acknowledges limitations and carefully addresses the non-convex nature of the problem.

**Originality:** This work combines two important concepts in novel ways: (1) Supervised PageRank learning, which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features, and (2) optimization with inexact oracle information. The authors provide the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters, which is a significant theoretical advance. The adaptation of random gradient-free methods [16] and gradient methods [7] to handle the specific constraints and inexactness in this problem is original.

**Significance:** The problem addressed has direct applications in search engines and web ranking systems. The proposed methods not only outperform existing approaches but also provide theoretical guarantees, which is crucial for practical implementation. The paper demonstrates that the new algorithms can improve ranking quality while being theoretically sound, which has clear practical significance for the search engine industry.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity:** While the paper is well-structured, some technical details in the supplementary materials could be better explained. The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation choices (e.g., why L = 10^-4 was chosen for the gradient-free method) could be clearer. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method would scale to extremely large graphs (beyond the 11.7K vertices tested).

**Theoretical Contribution Nuance:** The gradient-based method appears to be the stronger contribution, as it provides convergence guarantees without requiring convexity and has fewer input parameters. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the gradient-free method is necessary or advantageous in practice, given these advantages. The paper states that "the main advantage of the second gradient-free algorithm is that it avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix," but doesn't provide evidence that this is a significant bottleneck in practice.

**Experimental Details:** The experimental section lacks details about hyperparameter tuning. For example, how were the values for L (Lipschitz constant) and R (radius) determined? The paper mentions that "the differences are less than 10^-7" when varying L, but doesn't explain why the authors chose the specific value used in the final experiments. More detailed ablation studies on parameter sensitivity would strengthen the empirical contribution.

## Recommendations

1. **Clarify the practical advantages of the gradient-free method:** The paper should provide more concrete evidence that the gradient-free approach is preferable in real-world scenarios, particularly for very large graphs where derivative calculations might be prohibitive. A comparison of computational time between the two methods would help establish this.

2. **Expand experimental details:** Include more information about hyperparameter selection and sensitivity. A table showing results for different values of key parameters (L, R, ε) would be valuable for practitioners.

3. **Strengthen connections to broader NIPS community:** While the paper is clearly relevant to optimization and graph-based learning, it could better position itself within recent NIPS advances in these areas. A more detailed discussion of how this work relates to recent optimization methods (e.g., from NIPS 2020-2023) would enhance its significance.

4. **Improve clarity in the theoretical presentation:** The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the inexact oracle setting is challenging for convergence guarantees, and how the authors' approach specifically addresses these challenges.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and clear empirical validation. The authors successfully address a well-motivated problem in graph-based ranking with novel optimization methods that provide theoretical guarantees previously missing from the field. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in clarity and justification of the gradient-free method, the overall contribution is substantial and advances both the theory and practice of learning graph-based ranking algorithms.

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The theoretical advances are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the problem is significant for both academic and industrial applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues noted above.
