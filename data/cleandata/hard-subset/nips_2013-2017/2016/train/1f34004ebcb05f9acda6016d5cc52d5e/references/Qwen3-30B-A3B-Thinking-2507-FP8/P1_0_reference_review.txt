

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, an extension of traditional PageRank that incorporates node and edge features through parameterized transition probabilities. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods (one gradient-based, one gradient-free) to solve the non-convex optimization problem involved in learning these parameters. Unlike the state-of-the-art method [21], which lacks convergence guarantees, the authors provide theoretical convergence rates and complexity bounds for their approaches by carefully analyzing the trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.

The paper builds upon previous work on Supervised PageRank [21] and related optimization methods [16, 7, 5], but makes significant contributions by providing convergence guarantees for the learning problem. The authors demonstrate that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art in ranking quality on a real-world web page ranking task while providing theoretical guarantees that the existing method lacks.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters. The authors develop a rigorous theoretical framework for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with inexact oracles, which is a significant contribution to the optimization literature.

2. **Novel Two-Level Framework**: The paper's two-level approach (lower-level approximation of stationary distribution, upper-level optimization) is well-motivated and effectively addresses the computational challenges of the problem. The analysis of trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity is particularly valuable.

3. **Rigorous Analysis**: The paper provides detailed complexity bounds for both methods (Theorems 2 and 4), which is rare in the machine learning literature for similar problems. The convergence rate analysis for both methods is comprehensive and well-supported.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on a real-world Yandex dataset demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality, with statistical significance confirmed through t-tests. The authors also provide insightful analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity.

5. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient mathematical detail to understand the contributions. The separation of theoretical development from empirical validation is particularly effective.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (Yandex). While this is sufficient to demonstrate the method's efficacy, including results on other standard benchmark datasets (e.g., from the LETOR collection) would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. **Lack of Computational Time Analysis**: The paper provides complexity bounds but doesn't report actual wall-clock times for the algorithms. It would be valuable to know how the computational complexity translates to actual runtime performance.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the methods are to hyperparameters (e.g., the choice of Lipschitz constant L, accuracy parameters δ). The supplementary materials mention some sensitivity analysis (Figure 2), but this should be included in the main paper.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper could provide more details about practical implementation considerations, such as how the authors handle the large-scale nature of the graphs in practice.

5. **Broader Impact Discussion**: While the paper mentions applications to search engines, it doesn't discuss how these methods might impact other ranking problems beyond web search, limiting the perceived significance of the work.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for ranking problems. The paper makes a significant step forward by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical validation is convincing, and the methods are clearly presented.

The paper's main limitations are the single-dataset experimental validation and lack of detailed computational time analysis. However, these do not undermine the theoretical contribution, which is substantial and novel.

## Recommendation

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing theoretically sound optimization methods for a practically important problem. The theoretical guarantees are a significant advancement over the state-of-the-art, and the empirical results confirm the practical benefits. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the core theoretical contribution is robust and significant.

## Pro/Con Checklist

**Pro:**
- Provides first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with complexity bounds
- Well-motivated two-level optimization framework
- Clear experimental validation showing consistent improvement over state-of-the-art
- Addresses a practical problem with real-world applications in search engines

**Con:**
- Limited to a single dataset for empirical validation
- Lacks detailed analysis of computational time performance
- Insufficient discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity
- Limited discussion of broader applicability beyond web search

This paper represents a solid contribution to the NIPS community, advancing both the theory of optimization for ranking problems and providing practical algorithms that outperform existing methods. The theoretical framework could be applied to other similar learning problems involving Markov chains, making it potentially influential beyond the specific application to PageRank.
