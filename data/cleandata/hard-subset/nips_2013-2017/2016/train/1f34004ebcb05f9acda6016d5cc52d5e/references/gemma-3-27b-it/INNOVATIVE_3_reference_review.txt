This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, focusing on optimization methods to estimate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, critical for parameter learning. The authors identify a gap in the convergence guarantees of existing methods and propose two novel two-level approaches: a gradient-based and a gradient-free method. They provide convergence analysis for both, considering inexact oracle information, and investigate the trade-offs between accuracy and complexity. The experimental evaluation on a web ranking task demonstrates the superiority of their methods over the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution lies in providing *convergence guarantees* for learning Supervised PageRank – a significant missing piece in the existing literature. The adaptation of existing optimization methods (gradient descent and a derivative-free approach) to this specific problem with inexact oracle information is a clever and potentially impactful direction. The exploration of trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost is also valuable.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides theoretical analysis, including convergence rate estimation for both proposed methods. The lemmas and theorems, while potentially dense, demonstrate a serious attempt at grounding the methods in solid mathematical foundations.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addressing the optimization challenges in Supervised PageRank has direct relevance to real-world ranking problems, particularly in web search and recommendation systems.
*   **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors consider both gradient-based *and* gradient-free approaches, offering flexibility and catering to different computational constraints. The inclusion of complexity analysis further strengthens the contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world dataset show a clear performance improvement over the existing state-of-the-art method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is very dense and reads like a technical report. The notation is heavy and can be challenging to follow. More intuitive explanations and a clearer organization would significantly improve readability. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while important, disrupt the flow and could be relegated to the appendix with concise summaries in the main text.
*   **Experimental Detail:** While the experiments demonstrate improvement, the details regarding hyperparameter tuning (beyond mentioning a few values tested) and the sensitivity of the results to these parameters are limited. A more thorough ablation study would strengthen the empirical validation. The description of the dataset could be more detailed.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation focuses solely on web page ranking. Demonstrating the applicability of the methods to other graph-based ranking tasks would broaden the impact.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of convexity within the feasible set Φ is a strong one. The justification for this assumption should be more clearly articulated, or the analysis extended to handle non-convex cases more generally.
*   **Code availability:** The lack of code availability hinders reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant open problem – providing convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning.
*   The proposed methods are novel and demonstrate promising theoretical and empirical results.
*   The analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the trade-offs involved.
*   Even with some flaws in presentation, the technical contribution is substantial.
*   The potential impact of the work on real-world ranking applications is high.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and difficult to follow.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more thorough and broader in scope.
*   The strong assumption of convexity may limit the generalizability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling contribution to the field of graph-based ranking. While the presentation could be improved significantly, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and promising experimental results make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The convergence guarantees are a major step forward and address a critical gap in the existing literature. I am willing to overlook the flaws in presentation and limited experimental scope given the significance of the theoretical contributions. This paper embodies the spirit of NIPS – a bold approach to a challenging problem with a clear potential for impact.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with encouragement to improve clarity and expand experimental evaluation in a post-acceptance revision, if possible).
