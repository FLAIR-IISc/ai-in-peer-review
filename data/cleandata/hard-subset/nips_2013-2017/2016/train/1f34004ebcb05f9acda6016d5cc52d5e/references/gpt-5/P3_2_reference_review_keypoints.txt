- Paper tackles learning Supervised PageRank parameters linear in node/edge features via two two-level algorithms: inexact first-order (gradient-based) and inexact zero-order (random gradient-free).
- Lower level uses a linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method to approximate the stationary distribution π(ϕ) and a new recursion to approximate its derivatives ∂π/∂ϕ.
- Upper level presents (a) an adaptive projected first-order method for constrained, possibly nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracle and (b) an extension of random gradient-free optimization with biased function evaluations and explicit parameter–accuracy trade-offs.
- Provides end-to-end complexity bounds that include costs of lower-level approximations and reports improved training loss on a Yandex browsing graph dataset (600 queries) versus a prior power-method-based learner.
- Builds on Supervised PageRank and BrowseRank; uses known NN solver but extends it to derivative computation and integrates inexact-oracle ideas from Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov and Ghadimi–Lan.
- Combination with the specific supervised PageRank loss is novel, but many optimization components are incremental extensions of existing techniques.
- Lower-level error bounds (Lemmas 1–2) that propagate π and ∂π errors to bounded errors in f and ∇f are plausible and technically valuable.
- The derivative recursion mirrors Neumann-series sensitivity and sensibly generalizes prior derivative power methods.
- Upper-level results quantify dependence on smoothing τ and oracle error δ; adaptive method yields standard gradient-mapping guarantees and informative complexity results (Theorems 1–4).
- Gradient-free method’s guarantee requires convexity on Φ, but the target problem is nonconvex; authors assume local convexity near a local minimizer, weakening global significance.
- Parameter set Φ is chosen as a Euclidean ball in the positive orthant to ensure valid probabilities; the constraint’s effect on expressivity is not analyzed.
- Experimental evaluation is narrow: single proprietary dataset, only training loss reported (pairwise squared loss with hinge), and no standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP).
- Baselines are limited to a prior Supervised PageRank learner; no comparisons to mainstream learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) to contextualize impact.
- Hyperparameter tuning and stopping criteria for the baseline appear ad hoc; statistical testing is mentioned but detailed tables/figures are missing from the main text.
- Paper is generally readable but contains typos, imprecise notation, and defers key constants (e.g., β1) to the supplement, reducing self-containment.
- Exposition would benefit from clearer intuition for the derivative recursion, explicit assumptions near main theorems, and a crisper discussion of when f is convex on Φ.
- Positive originality: principled combination of NN lower-level solvers with inexact-oracle learning and tailored derivative computation with explicit complexity accounting.
- Limited novelty: upper-level methods and analyses largely adapt established frameworks to constrained settings and bounded deterministic errors.
- Significance: methodologically useful for supervised random-walk learning, but practical significance is undermined by limited empirical validation and lack of strong baselines.
- Pros: clear two-level design, sound π and ∂π approximations with provable bounds, adaptive inexact first-order method, and empirical suggestion that NN lower-level outperforms power method.
- Cons: narrow empirical evaluation and reproducibility issues, gradient-free method depends on local convexity, several theoretical pieces are modest extensions, and clarity/presentation problems.
- Suggested improvements: report standard ranking metrics and ablations (ε, N1, N2, α); compare to strong learning-to-rank baselines; isolate NN vs power-method effects; move key constants/assumptions into main text; fix writing and typos.
- Recommendation: borderline reject—technically sound but incremental on optimization side and experimentally insufficient for conference standards; needs stronger experiments and clearer novelty claims.
