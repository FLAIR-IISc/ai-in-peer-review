

# Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a method for ranking nodes in graphs that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free approach, both designed to handle the inexact oracle setting where the loss function and its gradient can only be approximated with controlled error. The key contribution is providing theoretical guarantees for convergence rates and complexity bounds, which the previous state-of-the-art method [21] lacked. The authors demonstrate empirically that their methods outperform the existing approach on a real-world web page ranking task.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully establish the problem's challenges, particularly the inexact oracle setting, and provide thorough proofs for their key lemmas and theorems (with complete proofs in the supplementary materials). The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity is particularly well-executed.

The experimental results on a real-world dataset (Yandex web browsing data) support the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] across multiple query subsets. The authors properly acknowledge limitations, such as the convexity assumption in the vicinity of the minimum, and provide a clear analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.

## Clarity

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation and consistent terminology. The problem definition and model description are thorough, providing sufficient background for readers familiar with Markov random walks and ranking problems. The connection between the theoretical contributions and the practical application is well-maintained throughout.

The presentation of the two-level optimization framework is logical and easy to follow. The supplementary materials provide the necessary technical details for the proofs, which is appropriate for a theoretical contribution. The experimental setup is well-described, including dataset characteristics, evaluation metrics, and implementation details.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by extending gradient-free and gradient-based optimization methods to the specific problem of learning Supervised PageRank with inexact oracle. While the underlying optimization techniques (from [16, 7]) are not entirely new, their application to this specific problem with rigorous analysis of the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational complexity is novel.

The authors properly distinguish their work from previous contributions, particularly noting that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks convergence guarantees. The paper's key innovation is the theoretical analysis of the two-level optimization framework with controlled error, which represents a meaningful advancement over prior work.

## Significance

The problem of learning node rankings in graphs is highly significant for applications like web search, social networks, and recommendation systems. The paper advances the state of the art by providing theoretically sound methods with convergence guarantees for a problem that previously lacked such guarantees.

The empirical results demonstrate practical improvements over the state-of-the-art, which could directly benefit real-world search engines. The paper's approach could potentially be applied to other problems involving Markov random walks with inexact oracle information, broadening its potential impact.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Rigorous theoretical guarantees for convergence rates and complexity bounds
2. Clear analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity
3. Strong empirical results demonstrating consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art
4. Properly contextualized within related work with appropriate citations
5. Well-structured presentation that connects theory to practice

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited discussion of scalability to extremely large graphs beyond the tested dataset
2. The convexity assumption in the vicinity of the minimum might not hold for all datasets
3. The paper could provide more detailed analysis of computational efficiency compared to the state-of-the-art
4. The comparison with other recent optimization methods for similar problems could be expanded

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing theoretically sound methods for learning Supervised PageRank with convergence guarantees, addressing a gap in the literature. The combination of rigorous theoretical analysis with compelling empirical results demonstrates significant scientific merit.

The paper meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS submissions: it's technically sound, well-written, original in its specific application, and significant for both theoretical and practical applications. While there are minor limitations regarding scalability and convexity assumptions, these do not undermine the paper's overall contribution.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing both theoretical guarantees and practical improvements over previous work. The authors have clearly identified a problem with important applications and provided a novel solution with rigorous analysis.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based ranking problems and demonstrates how theoretical advances can lead to practical improvements in real-world applications.
