This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, focusing on optimization methods to estimate the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk. The authors propose two two-level methods, a gradient-based and a gradient-free approach, aiming to address the convergence issues of existing methods and improve ranking quality. The core contribution lies in employing a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution and providing convergence guarantees for both proposed algorithms.

The paper demonstrates a commendable level of mathematical rigor. The authors attempt to address a challenging problem—optimizing parameters in Supervised PageRank—and provide theoretical analysis of convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, a significant undertaking. The effort to establish convergence guarantees, especially in the presence of inexact oracles, is noteworthy. The inclusion of complexity analysis adds further weight to the theoretical contributions.

However, despite the mathematical sophistication, several concerns impede a positive assessment. The primary issue is a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating substantial practical advantage over established methods. While the authors claim to outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality, the experimental results, as presented, are not entirely convincing. The gains appear marginal and the description of the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the significance of these improvements. For instance, the choice of hyperparameters for the compared methods (especially L0 in GBN) is briefly mentioned but a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the motivation for exploring gradient-free methods isn't fully justified. The authors acknowledge the additional input parameters of the gradient-free approach and the avoidance of derivative calculations as advantages, but these don’t necessarily translate into practical benefits. Given the computational overhead associated with achieving sufficient accuracy in the zero-order oracle, it's unclear if the gradient-free method offers a worthwhile trade-off. The claim that the gradient-free method "avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix" feels somewhat overstated, as the approximation of the derivative *is* a significant computational cost within the proposed method.

The paper also relies heavily on existing work, primarily [21], and the contributions, while mathematically sound, feel incremental. The theoretical results, while interesting, do not dramatically shift our understanding of optimization in this context. The connection between the theoretical convergence guarantees and the observed practical performance needs to be more clearly articulated. The assumption of convexity within a local neighborhood of the solution, while potentially valid, is not rigorously justified and relies on the empirical observations.

The discussion of related work, while present, feels somewhat cursory. More thorough comparison with other ranking algorithms and optimization techniques, beyond the immediate lineage of Supervised PageRank, would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous mathematical analysis of convergence for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in graph ranking with theoretical guarantees.
*   Potential for improvement in ranking quality compared to existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Marginal improvements in experimental results, lacking a compelling demonstration of practical advantage.
*   Insufficiently detailed experimental setup and sensitivity analysis.
*   Incremental contributions that build heavily on existing work.
*   Unclear justification for the gradient-free approach given its complexity.
*   Lack of a thorough comparison with a broader range of relevant ranking algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper demonstrates technical competence and a solid understanding of optimization principles, it falls short of the NIPS standard for originality and significance. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the limited practical impact and lack of a compelling empirical demonstration weaken the overall contribution. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, a clearer articulation of its advantages over existing methods, and a more robust justification for the proposed approaches. I encourage the authors to consider simplifying the presentation, focusing on the most compelling results, and conducting more extensive experiments to validate their claims.
