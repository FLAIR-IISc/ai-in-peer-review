

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank parameters, addressing a significant gap in the existing literature where the state-of-the-art method lacks convergence guarantees. The authors formalize the problem as a constrained non-convex optimization problem with inexact oracle information (due to approximations in calculating the stationary distribution and its derivatives). Their key contributions include: (1) a gradient-based optimization method with theoretical convergence guarantees using a linearly convergent lower-level method; (2) a gradient-free optimization method adapted to constrained problems with inexact oracle; and (3) a thorough analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity.

The paper builds upon prior work on Supervised PageRank [21], which introduced the framework for incorporating node and edge features into PageRank, but whose optimization method lacked convergence proofs due to the inexact nature of the oracle. The authors correctly identify that standard optimization methods [7, 16] cannot be directly applied due to the constraints on the parameter vector and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. They also appropriately reference related work on random walk models [18, 11], feature-based ranking [2, 3, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21, 22], and optimization methods [5, 17, 23, 24].

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for both proposed methods, including convergence rates and complexity bounds. Lemmas 1 and 2 establish precise relationships between the accuracy of lower-level approximations (via Method 3.1) and the resulting error in the loss function and its gradient. Theorems 1-4 formally prove convergence properties for both optimization frameworks under appropriate assumptions. The experimental evaluation on a real-world web page ranking task demonstrates clear performance improvements over the state-of-the-art [21], with statistically significant results (p < 0.005). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the need for local convexity in the gradient-based method.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the problem formulation is well-motivated. The description of the two-level optimization framework is particularly clear, with well-defined lower-level and upper-level components. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about the dataset (Yandex web browsing graphs), evaluation metrics, and hyperparameter choices. The supplementary materials contain the full proofs, which are helpful for verifying the theoretical claims.

**Originality:** The paper makes several novel contributions to the field. The adaptation of gradient-free methods [16] to constrained optimization with inexact oracle is a significant extension of prior work. The development of a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle (Theorem 3) represents a meaningful advance over existing methods [7, 25]. The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity is novel and practically valuable. The authors effectively differentiate their work from prior Supervised PageRank methods [21] and other optimization approaches [7, 16] by highlighting the lack of convergence guarantees in existing methods.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a practical problem in web ranking with clear real-world applications. The proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in terms of ranking quality while providing theoretical guarantees, which is particularly valuable for applications like search engines where reliability matters. The complexity analysis provides practical insights for implementing these methods at scale. The paper demonstrates how to combine ideas from random gradient-free optimization and gradient optimization with efficient methods for large-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle, which is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature.

**Strengths:** Theoretical rigor with convergence guarantees, clear experimental validation on a real-world dataset, thorough analysis of computational complexity, effective adaptation of existing optimization techniques to the specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank learning problem.

**Weaknesses:** The paper assumes local convexity for the gradient-based method, which may limit its applicability to certain problem instances. The experimental section could benefit from a more detailed ablation study of the hyperparameters (though some sensitivity analysis is provided). The comparison with other recent non-convex optimization methods could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive context.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

Pros:
- Provides the first convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Develops two distinct optimization frameworks (gradient-based and gradient-free) with theoretical guarantees
- Offers practical insights through complexity analysis and real-world experiments
- Well-structured paper with clear mathematical presentation

Cons:
- Limited discussion of applicability beyond the specific web ranking task
- Some assumptions (local convexity for gradient-based method) may limit generality
- Comparison with other non-convex optimization approaches could be more comprehensive

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing theoretically sound optimization methods for a practically important problem. The theoretical advances are significant, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the proposed methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a clear gap in the literature. It represents a meaningful advancement over the state-of-the-art and provides valuable tools for practitioners working on graph-based ranking problems.
