### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a non-convex optimization problem where the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly. The authors frame this as a problem of optimization with an inexact oracle. The primary contribution is the development of two two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—that, unlike the previous state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], come with formal convergence guarantees.

The proposed methods operate on two levels. On the lower level, responsible for approximating the oracle, the paper leverages a linearly convergent method from [17] to compute the stationary PageRank distribution and its derivatives. Crucially, the authors provide a theoretical analysis (Lemmas 1 and 2) that bounds the approximation error as a function of the computational budget, allowing them to control the oracle's accuracy. On the upper level, two distinct optimization strategies are presented. The first is a random gradient-free method, which adapts the framework of [16] to handle constrained optimization problems and the inexact zero-order oracle. The second is an adaptive gradient-based method that generalizes the work in [7] to non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle. For both methods, the authors provide a full theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and overall computational complexity. Experimental results on a large-scale web ranking task show that the proposed algorithms outperform the existing method from [21].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The primary strength is the rigorous theoretical treatment of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. By formalizing the optimization in an inexact oracle framework, the authors provide the first convergence guarantees for this problem, addressing a significant theoretical gap in the prior work [21].
    *   The analysis is thorough, connecting the accuracy of the lower-level oracle approximation (Lemmas 1 and 2) to the convergence rate of the upper-level optimization algorithm (Theorems 1-4). This provides a complete picture of the algorithm's complexity.
    *   The proposed adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex optimization is particularly strong, as it does not require prior knowledge of the function's Lipschitz constant, making it more practical.
    *   The experimental results, though on a single dataset, are positive and support the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods achieve a lower loss value than the baseline.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within the optimization domain Φ. The paper acknowledges that the Supervised PageRank loss is generally non-convex and justifies this by assuming Φ is a small vicinity of a local minimum. This is a strong assumption that limits the generality of the theoretical guarantee for this specific method. The gradient-based method, which does not require convexity, is therefore more broadly applicable.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized but can be dense for non-experts in optimization theory.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The structure is logical, clearly separating the model description, the oracle approximation, the general optimization frameworks, their application to the specific problem, and the experiments.
    *   The motivation for the work is clearly articulated: the lack of convergence guarantees in existing methods for Supervised PageRank.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The presentation is very dense with mathematical notation. A table summarizing the key variables and parameters (e.g., ε, δ, N, M, R, L) would significantly improve readability.
    *   Key algorithmic descriptions (Algorithm 2 and 4) are deferred to the supplementary materials. While this is common practice due to space constraints, it hinders a complete understanding of the proposed methods from the main paper alone.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and careful synthesis of existing ideas to provide a new, theoretically-grounded solution.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The main novelty is the successful integration of efficient PageRank approximation techniques [17] with modern optimization frameworks for inexact oracles [7, 16]. Applying this combined approach to solve the Supervised PageRank learning problem with formal guarantees is a new and valuable contribution.
    *   The development of general frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) is a contribution of independent interest. Specifically, the adaptation of a random gradient-free method for constrained problems and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems with inexact first-order oracles appear to be novel extensions.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The core components—the Supervised PageRank model, the lower-level approximation method, and the foundational optimization algorithms—are based on prior work. The originality lies in the combination and the rigorous analysis, rather than the invention of fundamentally new techniques.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper provides robust, reliable, and theoretically-sound algorithms for learning graph-based ranking models. This is a problem of significant practical importance in areas like web search and recommendation systems.
    *   The general optimization frameworks for inexact oracles have broad potential applicability across machine learning, particularly in settings where exact function or gradient evaluations are computationally prohibitive (e.g., large-scale learning, variational inference, certain reinforcement learning settings).
    *   The work advances the state of the art in learning Supervised PageRank by providing algorithms that are not only provably convergent but also demonstrate superior empirical performance.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The experimental validation is conducted on a single, proprietary dataset. While realistic, this limits the ability of other researchers to reproduce the results and compare against them. Evaluation on publicly available ranking benchmarks would strengthen the paper's empirical claims.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  Provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem, a significant theoretical contribution.
2.  Develops general and novel frameworks for constrained/non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, which are of broader interest to the NIPS community.
3.  The technical analysis is rigorous and complete, bridging the gap between lower-level approximation error and upper-level optimization convergence.
4.  Empirical results on a large-scale, real-world task demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods over the existing state-of-the-art.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

1.  The convergence guarantee for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption, which may be difficult to verify in practice for the non-convex objective.
2.  The experimental evaluation is limited to a single proprietary dataset, which hinders reproducibility.
3.  The paper's clarity could be improved, as it is notation-heavy and defers key algorithm descriptions to the supplement.

**Conclusion:** This is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical and practical contributions to the problem of learning graph-based ranking models. The development of provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank model is a notable achievement. Despite minor weaknesses in the scope of the experimental validation and the local convexity assumption for one of the methods, the strengths in technical quality, originality, and significance are substantial. The paper is a solid contribution to the field of large-scale optimization and its applications.
