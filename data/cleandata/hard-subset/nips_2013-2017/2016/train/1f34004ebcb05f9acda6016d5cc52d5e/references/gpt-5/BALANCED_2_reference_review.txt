Summary
The paper addresses learning a query-dependent PageRank variant (Supervised PageRank) where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features. The main difficulty is that evaluating the loss and its gradients requires computing stationary distributions of query-specific Markov chains and their sensitivities with respect to parameters, which can only be approximated. The authors propose two two-level methods:

- Lower level: Replace the standard power method with the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski iterative scheme to approximate the stationary distribution πq(ϕ), and a generalization thereof to approximate dπq/dϕ. They establish error bounds translating iteration counts (N, N1, N2) into accuracies for the objective and gradient.
- Upper level: 
  - A gradient-free method that adapts random zeroth-order optimization (sphere smoothing and finite differences) to constrained problems with inexact function values. They prove a convergence rate under convexity and bounded oracle error, and provide an overall complexity result for the two-level scheme.
  - A gradient-based method that extends adaptive projected gradient methods to constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracles and unknown Lipschitz constants, yielding convergence to a stationary point and an overall complexity bound.

Experiments on a proprietary Yandex browsing-graph dataset (600 queries; ~11.7k vertices and ~7.5k edges across all graphs; ~1.7k labeled pairs) compare the proposed methods against the prior supervised PageRank learner that used the power method and lacked convergence guarantees. The proposed methods reduce the optimization loss and, in some settings, use fewer outer iterations.

Relation to prior work
This builds on and integrates: personalized PageRank and HITS; supervised/random-walk-based ranking and BrowseRank; learning PageRank parameters via pairwise losses; iterative methods for PageRank and derivatives; random gradient-free optimization (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny) and inexact oracle frameworks (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov); and gradient methods for nonconvex problems with inexact oracles (Ghadimi–Lan). The main novelty is adapting these pieces to a constrained inexact-oracle setting and tying lower-level approximation accuracy to upper-level convergence guarantees for supervised PageRank learning.

Strengths
- Sound and useful formalization of the two-level inexact-oracle setting, with clear lemmas translating lower-level iteration counts into objective/gradient accuracy.
- Upper-level algorithms are well-motivated: the gradient-free method adapts sphere smoothing to constraints and biased/inexact function values; the gradient-based method offers an adaptive line-search style procedure and provides stationarity guarantees for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles.
- Complexity statements are explicit (albeit with large constants), clarifying the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and total cost.
- Empirical results indicate the proposed training schemes improve the supervised PageRank loss over the prior approach and can be more efficient in outer iterations.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The gradient-free method’s main convergence guarantee requires convexity on the feasible ball. The paper acknowledges f is generally nonconvex and assumes local convexity near a putative minimum, but there is no empirical or theoretical support that this holds in practice. This limits the strength of the guarantee for the intended setting.
- Several constants (e.g., β1) and key proof steps are deferred to the supplement. The main text would benefit from tighter, interpretable bounds or brief proof sketches to build intuition.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: only one baseline (the older supervised PageRank learner using power method); no comparisons to other supervised random walk or learning-to-rank baselines; no reporting of ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP, precision@k). Reporting only the training loss makes it hard to assess practical impact on retrieval effectiveness.
- No wall-clock runtime, memory usage, or scalability analysis; results focus on iteration counts and arithmetic operations rather than measured efficiency on larger graphs.

Clarity
- Writing quality is uneven, with grammatical issues and occasional ambiguity. Some notation is heavy and could be streamlined; assumptions (e.g., positivity constraints and choice of Φ) deserve clearer motivation.
- The references are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years, venues), and connections to closely related NeurIPS work are not always discussed in depth.

Originality
- The lower-level method is adopted from prior work; the derivative estimation builds on classical techniques. The upper-level frameworks are extensions/adaptations of existing inexact-oracle and gradient-free methodologies. The synthesis for supervised PageRank with end-to-end complexity guarantees is novel but incremental.

Significance
- Providing convergence guarantees for supervised PageRank training is valuable. However, the data scale is modest, reproducibility is limited (proprietary data, no code), and the lack of standard ranking metrics and broader baselines undercuts the impact for the IR/graph-ML community.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical development tying lower-level approximation error to upper-level convergence for both gradient-free and gradient-based schemes.
- First convergence guarantees for learning supervised PageRank in this two-level, inexact-oracle setting.
- Empirical indication of improved loss over a known baseline.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and proprietary empirical evaluation without standard ranking metrics or diverse baselines.
- Reliance on unverified local convexity for the gradient-free method in the target nonconvex problem.
- Clarity and presentation issues; key constants/assumptions are opaque, and references are incomplete.
- No runtime/scalability evidence to substantiate practical efficiency claims.

Recommendation
Reject.

Constructive feedback
- Expand experiments: report NDCG/MAP/Precision@k on held-out queries; include additional baselines such as Supervised Random Walks, BrowseRank variants, and standard learning-to-rank methods using the same features. Provide wall-clock runtime, memory, and scalability analyses on larger graphs.
- Clarify and justify assumptions: either remove the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method or empirically validate local convexity; discuss implications when it fails. Consider nonconvex zeroth-order convergence to stationary points.
- Improve clarity: provide brief proof sketches and interpretations of key bounds in the main text; define β1 and other constants; clean up notation and grammar; fix and complete references.
- Reproducibility: release code and, if possible, a synthetic or public dataset. Detail hyperparameter selection, stopping criteria, and sensitivity analyses (e.g., α, R, L, ε).
- Broaden modeling: consider learning α; explore different feasible sets (e.g., simplex with positivity) aligning with scale invariance; compare alternative pairwise loss functions.
- Add ablations: quantify the benefit of the Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level method vs. power method in wall-clock; show how N, N1, N2 affect accuracy and overall cost.
