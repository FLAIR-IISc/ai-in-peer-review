- Paper addresses learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart/transition probs are linear in features and loss depends on stationary distributions, yielding only inexact function/gradient information.  
- Proposes two two-level approaches: (i) gradient-based method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle; (ii) random gradient-free method for constrained convex problems with an inexact zero-order oracle.  
- Uses Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method on the lower level to approximate stationary distributions and generalizes it to approximate derivatives with explicit error–iteration tradeoffs.  
- Provides convergence and complexity bounds for both upper-level algorithms under inexact-oracle assumptions and analyzes the tradeoff between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity.  
- Builds on prior supervised/random-walk ranking work and replaces power method with Nesterov–Nemirovski averaging/acceleration at the lower level.  
- Extends random gradient-free optimization and inexact oracle frameworks to constrained settings with bounded oracle errors and an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives.  
- Positions advantageously against prior two-level gradient method for Supervised PageRank that lacked convergence guarantees due to inexact power-method oracles.  
- Strong lower-level analysis: explicit N bound for loss approximation (Lemma 1) and generalized derivative computation with infinity-norm gradient error bound (Lemma 2).  
- Upper-level theoretical results include gradient-free convergence for convex case (Theorem 1), adaptive projected gradient stationarity measure for nonconvex composite problems (Theorem 3), and end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4).  
- Explicit reasoning about lower/upper-level accuracy tradeoff, important for practical tuning.  
- Gradient-free method assumes convexity of f over Φ or a “small vicinity” of convexity in nonconvex case; this is a strong, unverified assumption without theoretical or empirical justification.  
- Inexact oracle condition for gradient-based method depends on constants (e.g., β1) placed in supplement, making practical tightness of bounds hard to assess.  
- Experimental validation is limited: single real dataset (600 queries, ~1.7k labeled pairs), one baseline, and evaluation primarily on surrogate loss rather than standard ranking metrics.  
- Missing standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP), lack of wall-clock or large-scale iteration comparisons, and limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis weaken practical claims.  
- GBP stopping criterion is unclear and baseline power-method parameters may be under-tuned, raising fairness concerns in comparisons.  
- Paper is generally well-structured but contains grammatical issues, typos, confusing notation (norm switching), and missing details (β1, step-size/accuracy schedules, stopping rules).  
- Contribution is incremental but nontrivial: adaptation of gradient-free methods to constrained inexact-oracle settings and an adaptive gradient method for nonconvex composite problems; Πk derivative recursion is a useful generalization.  
- Differences from existing inexact-oracle methods (Devolder et al., Ghadimi & Lan) could be made clearer.  
- Theoretical significance is solid, offering end-to-end guarantees for practical two-level graph-based learning-to-rank problems.  
- Practical significance is limited by sparse experiments, small scale, and limited baselines/metrics.  
- Pros: sound inexact-oracle formulation, clear lower/upper-level accuracy coupling, convergence and complexity guarantees, and empirical improvement over an earlier baseline.  
- Cons: strong convexity-in-vicinity assumption, limited experiments and baselines, and opaque constants/assumptions needing clarification.  
- Requests for authors: report NDCG@k/MAP, sensitivity to α/R/ε and lower-level parameters (N, N1, N2), disclose β1 and constants, provide practical recipes for δ/τ/L0, demonstrate scalability/wall-clock comparisons, and clarify GBP stopping rule.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10) — solid theoretical contributions but empirical section and clarity need strengthening.
