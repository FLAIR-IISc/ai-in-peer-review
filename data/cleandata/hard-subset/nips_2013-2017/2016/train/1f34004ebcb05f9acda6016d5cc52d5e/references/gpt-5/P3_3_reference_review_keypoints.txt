- Summary: Learns a supervised PageRank variant with feature-weighted restart/transition probabilities where loss depends on stationary distributions of query-specific Markov chains.
- Summary: Proposes two two-level methods — (i) adaptive projected gradient with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) constrained random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle.
- Summary: Uses Nesterov–Nemirovski’s linearly convergent method (instead of power iterations) to approximate stationary distributions and their derivatives to arbitrary accuracy.
- Summary: Provides convergence guarantees and complexity bounds under inexact-oracle assumptions and empirically compares methods on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Supervised PageRank, BrowseRank, Supervised Random Walks and connects to inexact-oracle and zeroth-/first-order nonconvex optimization literature.
- Strength: Clear two-level formulation with rigorous tracking of numerical error propagation to the upper-level optimization.
- Strength: Principled replacement of the power method with a linearly convergent solver for both distributions and derivative computations, with complexity accounting.
- Strength: New theoretical results: constrained gradient-free with bounded zero-order error and adaptive projected gradient for inexact first-order oracles without known Lipschitz constant.
- Strength: Empirical indication that proposed methods beat a prior two-level gradient baseline and that the lower-level solver converges faster than power iterations.
- Weakness: Mismatch between assumed smoothness (C1,1) and the nonsmooth hinge-like loss (positive part), with no formal treatment (subgradient/smoothing/composite) provided.
- Weakness: Gradient-free method assumes convexity on Φ though the problem is nonconvex; local convexity is assumed without justification, so global complexity guarantees may not hold.
- Weakness: Theorems depend on constants (L, β1, R, etc.) with unclear computation and practical sizes; guidance for choosing δ, N, N1, N2 is thin and scaling with r, m, |Q| may be large.
- Weakness: Limited experiments — single small dataset (600 queries, ~1.7K pairs), mostly training loss reported, no held-out NDCG/MAP or statistical tests to substantiate ranking claims.
- Weakness: Potentially asymmetric baseline tuning and minimal ablation (e.g., isolating lower-level method) hinder fair empirical comparison.
- Weakness: Clarity issues — numerous typos, inconsistent notation, reliance on supplement for key proofs/definitions, and insufficiently self-contained definitions (e.g., derivative recursion Πk, β1).
- Weakness: Related work coverage incomplete; missing recent analyses on inexact/stochastic oracles, nonconvex proximal methods, and PageRank differentiation literature.
- Questions: How is the nonsmooth hinge reconciled with C1,1 assumptions — subgradients, smoothing, or composite formulation?
- Questions: What concrete bound/formula is used for β1, and how sensitive are N1/N2 to it in practice?
- Questions: How is τ chosen in the gradient-free method, and how sensitive are results to τ and δ?
- Questions: Can the authors provide NDCG/MAP on held-out sets with statistical tests and stronger baselines?
- Questions: For what graph sizes does the lower-level method outperform power iterations in wall-clock time?
- Pros: Solid theoretical development tailored to inexact-oracle optimization and end-to-end complexity accounting.
- Pros: Lower-level improvement with linear convergence and derivative estimation.
- Cons: Theory assumptions (smoothness, convexity) mismatch the stated loss and lack verification of local convexity.
- Cons: Limited, nonstandard empirical evaluation and potential unfair baseline tuning reduce practical credibility.
- Recommendation: Weak reject — important direction and promising methods, but theory-practice mismatch, unverified assumptions, and limited experiments need substantial improvement.
