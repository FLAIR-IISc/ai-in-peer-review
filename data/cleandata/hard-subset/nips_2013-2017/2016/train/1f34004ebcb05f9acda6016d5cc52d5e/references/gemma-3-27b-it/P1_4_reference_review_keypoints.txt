- Summary: Proposes two two-level SPR optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) with linearly convergent stationary distribution estimation and provides convergence rate analysis and experiments showing improved ranking.

- Related work: Situates the work among PageRank/HITS, supervised ranking, and optimization literature with relevant citations and discussion of prior limitations.

- Strength: Theoretical guarantees: Provides convergence rate analysis for both methods addressing inexact-oracle issues.

- Strength: Novelty: Adapts gradient-based and gradient-free methods to constrained, inexact-oracle SPR and combines them with linear stationary-distribution solvers.

- Strength: Practical improvement: Empirical results on Yandex data show improved ranking quality over prior SPR methods.

- Strength: Detailed analysis: Thorough examination of computational complexity and trade-offs between accuracy, generalization, and cost.

- Strength: Clear presentation: Well-written, organized, and mathematically clear with defined algorithmic steps.

- Weakness: Complexity of implementation: Two-level nested approach and accurate distribution estimation appear computationally intensive for very large graphs.

- Weakness: Parameter sensitivity: Performance likely depends on multiple hyperparameters, with insufficient sensitivity analysis or tuning strategies.

- Weakness: Limited experiments: Evaluation on a single dataset (Yandex) limits evidence of generalizability across graph types and features.

- Weakness: Convexity assumptions: Assumes local convexity near the optimum without sufficient justification or guarantees that solutions lie in that region.

- Weakness: Reproducibility: Lacks detailed implementation details or code/pseudocode for the lower-level stationary distribution estimation.

- Argument for acceptance: Addresses a key limitation of SPR by providing rigorous convergence guarantees and validated empirical improvement.

- Argument for acceptance: Theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle optimization are substantial and broadly valuable.

- Argument against acceptance: High computational complexity may impede practical use on very large graphs.

- Argument against acceptance: Limited experimental scope raises questions about method robustness and generality.

- Argument against acceptance: Insufficient hyperparameter analysis weakens confidence in practical deployment.

- Overall assessment: Strong paper with compelling theoretical and empirical contributions, despite concerns about scalability and experimental breadth.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Required revisions: Provide detailed computational cost breakdowns for each algorithmic step.

- Required revisions: Add discussion or experiments on hyperparameter sensitivity and selection strategies.

- Required revisions: Include more detailed pseudocode or code for the stationary distribution estimation to aid reproducibility.

- Required revisions: Offer a more thorough justification or evidence for the local convexity assumption.
