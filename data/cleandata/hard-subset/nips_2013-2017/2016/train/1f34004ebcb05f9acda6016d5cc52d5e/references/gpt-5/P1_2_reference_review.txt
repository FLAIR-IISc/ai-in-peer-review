Summary
The paper tackles learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank (a query-dependent random walk on a graph whose restart and transition probabilities are linear in features) by proposing two two-level methods that explicitly accommodate inexact oracle information from the lower-level Markov chain computations. On the lower level, the authors replace the standard power method by a linearly convergent scheme (Nesterov–Nemirovski-style convex combination of powers) to approximate the stationary distribution π and its derivatives with provable accuracy and complexity bounds. On the upper level, they (i) develop a constrained, random gradient-free method that tolerates uniformly bounded errors in objective values, and (ii) propose an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz-like constant. They derive convergence theorems and end-to-end complexity bounds that couple the lower-level accuracy and the upper-level iteration complexity. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries; ≈11.7k nodes total) indicate that both methods outperform the prior gradient-based approach used for Supervised PageRank in terms of the training loss, with the gradient-free method avoiding costly derivative computations.

Relation to prior work
- Supervised PageRank and supervised random walks have a substantial literature (e.g., Richardson & Domingos; Backstrom & Leskovec; Dai & Davison; Yandex work by the same group). Prior work typically relied on power-method-based approximations and gradient-based learning, without convergence guarantees under inexact lower-level computations.
- On the optimization side, the paper builds on randomized gradient-free smoothing methods (e.g., Nesterov & Spokoiny) and inexact-oracle first-order methods (e.g., Devolder, Glineur, Nesterov; Ghadimi & Lan). The authors adapt these ideas to constrained domains and explicitly bounded, unknown noise in the zero-order oracle, and to nonconvex composite problems with an adaptive backtracking line-search-like scheme.
- The lower-level estimator for π is related to accelerated averaging of power iterates (Nesterov–Nemirovski), and the paper extends it to derivative estimation with complexity bounds.

Quality
Strengths:
- Theoretical contributions are clearly nontrivial: (a) error and complexity bounds for approximating f(ϕ) and ∇f(ϕ) via finite sums of power iterates, (b) a constrained gradient-free method with bounded inexact function evaluations and convergence guarantees, and (c) an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracle, including a stopping criterion tied to the gradient mapping.
- The two-level complexity analysis explicitly trades lower-level accuracy against upper-level convergence, which is important in practice.
- The empirical section uses a real industrial dataset and compares multiple configurations, reporting that the proposed methods reduce the training loss more than the baseline algorithm.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and metrics. The paper optimizes and reports only the surrogate pairwise squared loss. There are no standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k) on held-out queries. Given the application (ranking), this is a notable omission; without ranking metrics and statistical tests on them, it is hard to assess practical significance.
- Baselines are narrow. The only algorithmic baseline appears to be the previous gradient-based Supervised PageRank approach (GBP). Missing are: (i) strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART, neural rankers) trained on the same features; (ii) other supervised random-walk methods (Backstrom & Leskovec 2011, BrowseRank variants); (iii) ablations isolating the effect of the lower-level estimator vs. the upper-level optimizer.
- Assumptions for theory vs. practice are not reconciled. The gradient-free convergence theorem assumes convexity on Φ; for the actual problem the authors assume local convexity near a local minimum but give no empirical evidence (e.g., landscape diagnostics) nor safeguards against failure when the iterate leaves the locally convex region. The nonconvex gradient method returns a point with small gradient-map norm; however, the stationarity measure used (‖Mk(xk−xk+1)‖) should be explicitly tied to a standard notion of stationarity for constrained problems, and this connection should be clarified.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for critical constants (e.g., β1), proofs, and algorithmic details. Some constants and bounds (and their practical magnitudes) are opaque in the main text, making it hard to judge practical complexity. The description of stopping criteria and parameter sensitivity is terse.
- Clarity issues: numerous typos/grammar problems, inconsistent norm usage (switching between ℓ1 and ℓ2 when bounding diamΦ), and notational overload make the paper harder to follow. Several references are incomplete.

Clarity
The paper’s structure is logical, but presentation needs polishing:
- Clearly define all constants in the main text (at least their dependencies), especially β1 and the role of r, p, s in complexity.
- Provide pseudocode for the two-level algorithms in the main paper, not only in the supplement.
- Unify norm choices and diameter bounds; explain the stationarity measure returned by Algorithm 2 (Section 5).
- Correct typos and complete references.

Originality
The combination is novel: adapting random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with bounded inexact function values, and developing an adaptive inexact-oracle gradient method for nonconvex composite problems, both embedded in a two-level learning framework for Supervised PageRank. While components build on known ideas, their integration with lower-level stationary-distribution/gradient estimation and full complexity accounting is a valuable contribution.

Significance
Potentially impactful for learning on large graphs where exact gradients are costly or unavailable. The theoretical guarantees for end-to-end learning under inexact oracles are useful beyond this application. However, the empirical evidence needs to be strengthened with standard ranking metrics and broader baselines to convincingly demonstrate practical gains.

Pros
- Solid theoretical advances in inexact-oracle optimization (zero- and first-order) with constrained domains.
- Careful lower-level approximation and complexity analysis; derivative estimation scheme.
- Real-world dataset; methods appear to converge faster in loss than the prior baseline.

Cons
- Missing ranking metrics and broader baselines; unclear practical significance.
- Assumptions (local convexity) and stationarity criterion not fully justified empirically.
- Presentation issues; heavy dependence on supplement; incomplete references.

Questions for authors
1) Please report NDCG@k/MAP on held-out queries, with statistical significance, and compare to strong LTR baselines (e.g., LambdaMART).
2) How sensitive are results to α and to the choice of Φ (center and radius)? Did you try learning α as well?
3) For the gradient-free method, what τ and δ were used in practice, and how do they scale with m and |Q|?
4) Can you clarify the relationship between ‖Mk(xk−xk+1)‖ and a standard projected gradient norm? What ε implies in terms of stationarity?
5) Provide wall-clock time and lower-level iteration counts, and an ablation isolating the benefit of the Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level method vs. the power method under your upper-level solvers.

Reproducibility
Moderate. The dataset is proprietary; please release code, synthetic data, and detailed hyperparameters to improve reproducibility. Theoretical details are thorough, but practical constants and stopping rules should be clarified.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject
While the theoretical contributions are strong and relevant, the empirical evaluation does not yet meet the NeurIPS bar for demonstrating practical impact in ranking. Strengthening experiments with standard metrics and baselines, clarifying assumptions, and improving clarity would move this to a clear accept.

Confidence: High on optimization aspects; moderate on graph-ranking practice.
