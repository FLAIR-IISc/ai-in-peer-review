- Paper addresses optimization challenges in Supervised PageRank (SPR) and proposes two-level gradient-based and gradient-free methods.  
- Authors identify lack of convergence guarantees and difficulty of applying standard optimization due to constraints and inexact loss evaluation.  
- Proposes improved iterative method to approximate the stationary distribution and integrates it into both optimization approaches.  
- Provides theoretical convergence analysis and rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.  
- Demonstrates empirical superiority over prior SPR methods on a real-world web ranking task.  
- Builds on classic PageRank and prior SPR work, situating contributions within optimization under inexact/noisy oracles.  
- Adapts linearly convergent stationary-distribution approximation (Dhamankar et al.) and gradient-free techniques to constrained SPR.  
- Theoretical contribution: convergence guarantees for methods that many existing SPR implementations lack.  
- Novel methodology: combination of improved stationary-distribution solver with two-level optimization frameworks.  
- Practical impact: experimental results on Yandex web browsing data show better performance than state-of-the-art.  
- Detailed complexity analysis links lower-level accuracy to overall computational cost, with supporting lemmas/proofs.  
- Presentation is generally clear with consistent notation, though mathematically dense.  
- Weakness: heavy mathematical density and involved derivations reduce readability for non-experts.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited to one dataset and task, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., Lipschitz constant) is noted but not thoroughly explored.  
- Weakness: insufficient implementation details (parameter choices, software) hinder reproducibility.  
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to other contemporary ranking algorithms and alternative optimization methods.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical/practical contributions, convergence guarantees, strong empirical results, and useful complexity analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: high mathematical complexity, limited experimental scope, and missing implementation details.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with important contributions; recommend accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, broaden experiments, and add implementation details.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
