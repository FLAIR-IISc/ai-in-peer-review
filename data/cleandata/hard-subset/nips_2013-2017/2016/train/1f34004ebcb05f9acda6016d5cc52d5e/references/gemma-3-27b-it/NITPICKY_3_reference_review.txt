This paper proposes two two-level methods for solving the Supervised PageRank problem, combining gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques with a novel analysis of the lower-level approximation accuracy. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and attempts to provide theoretical guarantees, the review reveals a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to substantial concerns regarding the novelty and practical impact of the proposed methods.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], aiming to improve the optimization process. It acknowledges the challenges of applying existing methods to this problem due to constraints and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. The core contribution lies in leveraging an improved lower-level approximation of the stationary distribution using a method from [17] and developing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free upper-level optimization algorithms. The work positions itself within the broader context of non-convex optimization and inexact oracle methods, referencing relevant papers like [1, 6, 7, 16, 25].

**Strengths:**

*   The attempt to provide theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is commendable.
*   The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity is a valuable consideration.
*   The experimental evaluation, although limited (discussed below), demonstrates some improvement over the baseline method [21].

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity Issues:** Numerous minor notational inconsistencies exist throughout the paper. For instance, the definition of *U* changes between Section 2 and the subsequent sections. The meaning of subscript 'q' is not always clear, especially when it appears with parameters α and ϕ, which are stated as not query-dependent. The notation `a.o.` (arithmetic operations) is used without proper definition in the main body, making it hard to interpret complexity claims.
*   **Incremental Novelty:** The proposed methods largely appear as applications of existing techniques ([17] for lower-level approximation, standard gradient descent/gradient-free methods) to a specific problem. The claimed novelty of adapting [16] to constrained optimization lacks substantial justification; constrained optimization is a well-studied area and adapting existing methods is often straightforward. The contribution of extending [17] for derivative calculation appears incremental as well, and its necessity isn’t convincingly demonstrated.
*   **Overly Optimistic Complexity Analysis:** The complexity estimates presented (Theorems 2 & 4) appear inflated and rely on numerous parameters, making them difficult to interpret in practice. The expressions are unwieldy and it's unclear whether the proposed methods would actually be computationally efficient compared to existing approaches.
*   **Experimental Limitations:** The experimental setup is inadequate. The dataset description is brief and lacks details about the size of the graph structures and features. The comparison is only against the original [21] method, and there’s no comparison with other relevant ranking algorithms or optimization techniques. The use of a single dataset limits the generalizability of the findings. The reliance on p-values without effect sizes weakens the conclusions. The statement about convergence rate on Figure 1 lacks any quantitative metrics for comparison.
*   **Assumptions and Convexity:** The strong assumption of local convexity in Section 4.2 feels unjustified and limits the applicability of the method. The claim that the set Φ can be chosen close to the simplex is vaguely described and its practical implications are unclear.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks a discussion of practical challenges such as parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis, making it difficult to assess the real-world usability of the proposed methods.
*   **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper contains significant redundancy, particularly in the descriptions of the methods and the proofs. Sections are often overly verbose and lack conciseness.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Incremental novelty and lack of substantial theoretical breakthroughs.
*   Overly complex and potentially impractical complexity analysis.
*   Insufficient experimental validation and limited scope.
*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and clarity issues.
*   Strong and potentially unrealistic assumptions.

**For:**

*   Attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for the proposed methods.
*   Analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and complexity.
*   Demonstrated improvement over the baseline method in a limited setting.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem, this paper suffers from numerous weaknesses that outweigh its strengths. The contributions are incremental, the theoretical analysis is complex and potentially impractical, the experimental evaluation is insufficient, and the presentation is marred by notational inconsistencies and verbosity. While the authors’ attempt to provide theoretical guarantees is laudable, the overall quality of the work falls short of NIPS standards.

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**

The authors need to significantly revise the paper, focusing on simplifying the analysis, strengthening the experimental validation, and improving the clarity of the presentation. A more compelling demonstration of the practical advantages of the proposed methods is crucial for reconsideration. A detailed comparison with state-of-the-art ranking algorithms and optimization techniques is also required.
