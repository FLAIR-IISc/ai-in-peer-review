## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of standard PageRank allowing node and edge features to influence the ranking. The core contribution lies in proposing two two-level optimization methods—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—to solve the non-convex loss minimization problem inherent in learning these parameters. The authors focus on addressing the challenges posed by inexact oracle information (due to approximations of the stationary distribution) and provide convergence guarantees for both methods, alongside a complexity analysis. The paper demonstrates empirically that these methods outperform the existing state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21]. It acknowledges the limitations of existing optimization techniques (e.g., [6, 16, 7]) when applied to this problem, specifically constraints, the inability to calculate the exact loss function, and lack of convergence guarantees. The authors draw upon existing methods for approximating the stationary distribution [17, 5, 23, 24] and utilize concepts from inexact optimization [1, 16, 25].  The work is positioned as improving upon [21] by providing theoretical convergence guarantees and achieving better empirical performance.  The use of two-level optimization with inexact oracles is a common theme in large-scale machine learning, and this paper contributes to the analysis and application of this technique in the context of graph ranking.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  A significant strength of this paper is the provision of convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a notable improvement over previous work on Supervised PageRank [21], which lacked such guarantees.
* **Inexact Oracle Handling:** The rigorous analysis of optimization with inexact oracle information (due to approximations of the stationary distribution) is well-done and addresses a practical challenge in applying these methods to large graphs. The convergence rate estimations are particularly valuable.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed complexity analysis of the proposed algorithms, which is essential for understanding their scalability.  The trade-off analysis between accuracy and computational cost is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to the state-of-the-art. The provided tables and figures give a good overview of the results.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-defined, and the mathematical notation is generally clear. The extensions to existing frameworks for inexact optimization are logically presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and mathematically involved. While the rigor is commendable, it can make the paper challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the relevant optimization techniques.  More intuitive explanations of the key concepts would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions experiments with different step sizes and Lipschitz constants, a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters (e.g., α, R, τ) would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to a single dataset and task (web page ranking). While this provides a proof-of-concept, it would be valuable to see the performance of the algorithms on other graph ranking problems or different types of graphs.
* **Minor Notation Issues:** There are a few instances of inconsistent notation (e.g., the use of subscripts and superscripts for similar quantities). While not critical, these can add to the cognitive load.
* **Assumed Convexity:** The claim that the problem is convex *on Φ* feels somewhat weak.  A stronger justification or discussion of the potential impact of non-convexity on the convergence guarantees would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in graph ranking with solid theoretical foundations.
* The provision of convergence guarantees is a significant contribution.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on inexact optimization and its application to large-scale machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is quite dense and mathematically challenging, which might limit its accessibility.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The assumption of local convexity requires more justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of graph ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. While the paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and more extensive experimentation, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – I suggest the authors consider simplifying the presentation where possible and expanding the discussion of the convexity assumption).  The paper is a valuable contribution to NIPS and is likely to be of interest to researchers working on graph ranking, optimization, and machine learning.
