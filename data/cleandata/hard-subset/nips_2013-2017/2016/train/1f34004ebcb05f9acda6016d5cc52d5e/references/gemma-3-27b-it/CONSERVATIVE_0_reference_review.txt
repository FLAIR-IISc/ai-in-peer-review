This paper proposes two two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing the lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based approaches. The authors present both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods, accompanied by theoretical convergence analyses and complexity estimations. They demonstrate empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task.

While the problem addressed – improving the optimization of Supervised PageRank – is a valid one, and the authors attempt a rigorous theoretical treatment, the paper falls short of convincing me of its significance or originality relative to established methods. My primary concern is the incremental nature of the contributions. The core idea of applying more robust optimization techniques (specifically, a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution and more careful analysis of convergence rates) to an existing framework feels evolutionary rather than revolutionary. The existing Supervised PageRank framework itself, while valuable, isn't presented as a particularly groundbreaking advance, and improvements to its optimization don't necessarily translate to a major impact. 

The theoretical contributions, while detailed, appear largely focused on tightening existing bounds and providing convergence guarantees – laudable goals, but not sufficient to justify acceptance at NIPS in the absence of substantially novel ideas. The convergence analysis relies heavily on existing methods (e.g., [17], [7]) and largely adapts them to the specific context of Supervised PageRank. The claims of improved convergence rates and complexity bounds need to be substantially more compelling, especially given the inherent complexities of analyzing non-convex optimization problems. The derivation of Lemma 1 and 2, while presented, are referenced to be found in supplementary materials making proper review difficult. 

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating some performance improvements, isn’t particularly striking. The gains over the state-of-the-art are presented without a thorough ablation study to understand *why* the proposed methods are better. For example, how sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? What is the impact of different accuracy levels (N and N1, N2)? The comparison to the untuned Supervised PageRank serves as a weak baseline; comparison against more sophisticated ranking algorithms would strengthen the claims. The claim that the "state-of-the-art" method's performance is limited by the power iteration steps highlights a potential issue but doesn't fully justify the complexity of the proposed solutions. Moreover, the results rely heavily on a single dataset from Yandex, limiting generalizability.

The paper is reasonably well-written and organized, though the sheer density of mathematical notation can be challenging. The inclusion of lemmas and proofs in the supplementary materials is appropriate. However, the lack of clarity regarding the interplay between the different approximations (e.g., the accuracy of the stationary distribution approximation and its impact on the gradient estimates) hinders full comprehension.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis, providing convergence guarantees for the proposed methods.
*   Demonstrated empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in the field of web ranking and graph analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; the contributions largely consist of applying existing optimization techniques to an existing framework.
*   The experimental results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   Complexity of the theoretical analysis may outweigh the practical benefits.
*   Limited generalizability of the experimental evaluation.
*   Reliance on supplementary material for key proofs limits proper review.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and well-intentioned, it lacks the originality and significance necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The improvements offered are incremental, and the theoretical contributions, while rigorous, don't fundamentally change our understanding of optimization in this context. The paper would be more suitable for a venue focused on more specialized applications of optimization techniques. A major revision, demonstrating substantially more novel ideas or a significantly broader impact, would be needed for reconsideration.
