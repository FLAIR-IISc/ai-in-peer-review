Summary
The paper tackles learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where transition and restart probabilities depend on node/edge features via linear models. The objective is a pairwise squared hinge-style loss over query-dependent stationary distributions. The novelty is twofold: (i) using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent scheme to approximate stationary distributions (and a new extension to approximate their derivatives), yielding anytime inexact oracles with controllable accuracy; (ii) two upper-level optimizers that provably handle inexact oracles: a constrained, random gradient-free method with biased zero-order oracle, and an adaptive projected first-order method for (possibly nonconvex) composite optimization with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. The authors provide complexity bounds that couple lower-level accuracy and upper-level convergence, and report improvements over the prior gradient method of [21] on a Yandex browsing-graph ranking task.

Quality and technical correctness
- Lower level: Using [17] to estimate π and extending it to estimate dπ/dϕ with linear convergence is reasonable; the constructed estimators π̃q and Π̃q with geometric weights lead to errors O((1−α)N). Lemmas 1–2 assert bounds on function and gradient approximation with explicit N depending on α, r, β1. However, β1 is only defined in the supplement, and the dependence and magnitude of constants (especially β1) are critical for practical complexity.
- Upper level (gradient-free): Theorem 1 adapts random gradient-free optimization to constrained domains with biased, inexact function values. The smoothing/finite difference on the sphere with projection is standard, but the handling of a bounded unknown error δ and the constrained setting is a useful extension. The convexity and x*∈int(X) assumptions limit applicability; in the application they assume local convexity of f on Φ.
- Upper level (gradient-based): Algorithm 2 (adaptive projected gradient) with inexact first-order oracle (inequality (5.1)) is a meaningful generalization of [7,25] to the constrained, possibly nonconvex setting with unknown L. The guarantee is in terms of the gradient mapping norm ‖M_k(x_k−x_{k+1})‖, a standard stationarity measure. Clarity about how this corresponds to KKT conditions for the Euclidean ball would help.
- Objective smoothness: f(ϕ) uses (·)+ squared. This is C^{1,1} (its gradient is Lipschitz), so the smoothness assumptions are plausible given the positivity constraints that avoid non-differentiabilities in π(ϕ). This is worth stating explicitly.
- Complexity: The two-level complexity bounds are informative and reflect the trade-off between N, N1, N2 and ε. However, they involve heavy constants (m, p, s, r, |Q|, α), and practical guidance is limited without numerical illustrations of these constants.

Empirical evaluation
- Dataset: 600 queries, ~11.7K pages and 7.5K edges across query graphs, and ~1.7K labeled pairs. This is small for web ranking; scalability claims would benefit from larger graphs or synthetic stress-tests.
- Metrics: The paper mainly reports loss values; for a ranking task, standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k) on the labeled sets are essential to substantiate “ranking quality” improvements.
- Baselines: The main baseline is the earlier gradient-based method [21] using power iterations (GBP). It would strengthen the case to include other learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) using the same features and to report untuned vs tuned PageRank variants (e.g., BrowseRank) and alternative stationary computation schemes (push-based, Monte Carlo).
- Fairness of comparison: Hyperparameter choices (e.g., step sizes/powers for GBP, ε and L/L0 for the proposed methods) are fixed but not tuned equivalently; report sensitivity and wall-clock time and memory. The claim that the Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme converges faster than power is plausible; include time-to-ε plots and derivative computation costs.
- Practicality: The gradient-free method requires two function evaluations per step; the gradient-based method requires a costly gradient oracle via Π̃. A direct runtime comparison (and a breakdown of lower-level costs N, N1, N2 achieved) would clarify when each method is preferable.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well organized, but there are typos and grammar issues that make some parts hard to follow. Key constants (β1) and assumptions (e.g., positivity via Φ around 1) should be stated in the main text. Brief proof sketches for Lemmas 1–2 would help readers assess the gradient estimator.
- The relationship between the stopping criterion ‖M_k(x_k−x_{k+1})‖ and a standard stationarity measure should be clarified. Provide guidance for selecting ε and δ in practice.

Originality
- The contributions are largely careful adaptations and integrations: using [17] for π and extending to dπ/dϕ; extending random gradient-free optimization to constrained inexact oracles; an adaptive projected gradient with inexact oracle for nonconvex problems. While incremental, the end-to-end treatment of inexact oracles for this application is useful.

Significance and impact
- The theoretical framework for dealing with inexact oracles in both zero- and first-order methods is valuable beyond PageRank learning. The application to supervised PageRank with convergence guarantees addresses a gap in [21]. However, the current empirical evaluation is modest and lacks standard ranking metrics and broader baselines, which limits practical impact.

Pros
- Clear two-level design with controllable inexact oracles and end-to-end complexity bounds.
- Extension of gradient-free and gradient-based methods to constrained, inexact settings with convergence guarantees.
- Practical application to supervised PageRank and evidence of improvement over [21].
- Attention to the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and total complexity.

Cons
- Experiments are small-scale, lack standard ranking metrics and strong baselines, and do not report runtime/memory.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary proofs and opaque constants (β1); limited insight into practical parameter choices.
- Assumptions for gradient-free method (convexity in Φ, interior solution) may not hold; discussion of when they do is brief.
- Some clarity issues and incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report NDCG/MAP in addition to loss; add larger datasets or synthetic scalability tests; include runtime and memory.
- Add baselines (RankSVM/LambdaMART; other PageRank variants) and a tuned power-method optimizer.
- Provide proof sketches and quantify constants in the main text; give practical guidance for setting ε, δ, N, N1, N2.
- Discuss sensitivity to α and feature scaling; provide ablations on Nesterov–Nemirovski vs power for π and dπ/dϕ.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle optimization in a two-level PageRank learning setting are solid and relevant, but the empirical section needs strengthening. With added metrics, baselines, and runtime analysis, this could be a strong contribution.
