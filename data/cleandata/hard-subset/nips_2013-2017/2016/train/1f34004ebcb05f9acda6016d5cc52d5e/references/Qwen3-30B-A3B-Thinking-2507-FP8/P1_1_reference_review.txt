

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes two novel optimization methods for learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, a framework for ranking nodes in graphs that incorporates node and edge features. The authors develop both gradient-based and gradient-free two-level optimization approaches, providing theoretical convergence guarantees and complexity analysis. The methods address a key limitation of the state-of-the-art [21], which lacked convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters. The authors demonstrate through experiments that both methods outperform the previous approach in ranking quality on a real-world web page ranking task.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide rigorous theoretical proofs for their methods (Lemmas 1-2, Theorems 1-4) and thorough complexity analysis. The experimental results on a real-world dataset from Yandex convincingly demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21]. The paper correctly identifies the key limitation of previous work: the lack of convergence guarantees due to the use of approximate values for the stationary distribution and its derivatives. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly the assumption of local convexity for the gradient-based method.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the notation is consistent throughout. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. One minor improvement would be to provide more detailed explanation of why the previous method [21] lacks convergence guarantees beyond stating that it uses imprecise values for the stationary distribution and its derivatives.

## Originality
This work makes a significant contribution by providing the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The combination of gradient-free optimization with the specific inexact oracle setting for PageRank is novel. The two-level approach with analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and computational complexity is particularly insightful. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly [21], by addressing the lack of convergence guarantees.

## Significance
The results are highly significant as they provide the first theoretically guaranteed method for learning Supervised PageRank, which has important applications in web search and recommendation systems. The empirical results showing improved ranking quality over the state-of-the-art are convincing. The gradient-free method's ability to avoid computing derivatives for large matrices is a practical advantage for real-world applications. This work bridges a critical gap between optimization theory and graph-based ranking algorithms.

## Strengths
1. First theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank (a significant gap in the literature)
2. Novel two-level optimization framework with comprehensive complexity analysis
3. Both methods provide practical advantages (gradient-based has fewer parameters, gradient-free avoids large matrix derivatives)
4. Strong empirical results on a real-world ranking task from Yandex
5. Clear and well-organized presentation of the problem, methods, and results
6. Insightful analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity

## Weaknesses
1. The justification for the local convexity assumption in Theorem 2 is limited (only mentioned as an assumption without sufficient justification for why it's reasonable in practice)
2. The scalability to extremely large graphs (beyond the tested sizes) is not thoroughly discussed
3. The paper could provide more details on hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the gradient-free method
4. Limited comparison with other graph-based ranking methods beyond Supervised PageRank

## Comparison with Previous Work
The paper properly cites related work, including [21] as the state-of-the-art method for Supervised PageRank, and correctly identifies its lack of convergence guarantees. The authors also cite relevant optimization literature, including [16] for gradient-free methods and [7] for gradient methods. However, the paper could better connect to other graph-based ranking approaches beyond Supervised PageRank (e.g., DeepWalk, Node2Vec) to better position the contribution within the broader field.

## Recommendation
I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The theoretical contributions are significant, the empirical results are convincing, and the work is well-written and properly situated in the literature. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor compared to the substantial contributions of the paper.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of optimization theory and graph-based ranking algorithms, and I believe it would be of interest to the NIPS community. The authors have successfully bridged a gap between theoretical optimization and practical graph-based ranking problems with meaningful results. The work provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, which is a strong combination for NIPS.

The paper demonstrates how theoretical optimization advances can directly benefit practical applications in search engines and recommendation systems, which is particularly relevant for NIPS. The gradient-free method's ability to avoid large matrix derivatives is a practical advantage that could make this work widely applicable.

Overall, this is a strong NIPS submission that advances the state of the art in both optimization theory and graph-based ranking algorithms.
