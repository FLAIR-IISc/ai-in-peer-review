### Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a generalization of PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are learned functions of node and edge features. The core challenge, identified from the original work by Zhukovskii et al. [21], is that the optimization problem is non-convex, and the objective function (a loss over ranking pairs) and its gradients cannot be computed exactly. This is because they depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain, which can only be approximated. The existing method [21] uses a power method-based gradient descent but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to the inexactness of the oracle.

The authors make several key contributions to solve this problem. First, they propose using a provably linearly convergent method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives. This method provides explicit bounds on the approximation error, which is crucial for their theoretical analysis. Second, they develop two novel two-level optimization algorithms. The first is a gradient-free method that adapts the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems with an inexact zero-order oracle. The second is an adaptive gradient-based method that extends the work of Ghadimi and Lan [7] to constrained, non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle. For both methods, the authors provide rigorous proofs of convergence and derive the overall computational complexity, linking the required accuracy of the inner-loop oracle to the desired accuracy of the outer-loop optimization. Finally, they demonstrate experimentally on a large-scale web ranking task that their proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free one, outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of the final loss value.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem in both graph-based learning and large-scale optimization. Providing the first provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank is a significant step forward. Supervised PageRank is a powerful model, and making it trainable with theoretical guarantees increases its reliability and applicability for practitioners, for instance in search engines or recommendation systems.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contributions (Originality & Quality):** The paper's core strength lies in its rigorous optimization framework. The authors don't just apply off-the-shelf solvers; they carefully extend state-of-the-art methods to their specific problem setting.
    *   The adaptation of random gradient-free methods [16] to the constrained setting with a controllable, inexact oracle is a novel and valuable contribution.
    *   Similarly, the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle (generalizing [7]) is a solid theoretical result in its own right.
    *   The complexity analysis (Theorems 2 and 4) that explicitly connects the lower-level approximation accuracy (controlled by `N`) to the upper-level optimization convergence (controlled by `ε`) is thorough and provides practical insights into the trade-offs involved.

3.  **Methodological Soundness (Quality):** The choice to use the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] for the lower-level problem is well-justified. Unlike the standard power method used in prior work, it provides explicit error bounds, which are the cornerstone upon which the entire theoretical framework is built. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's structure.

4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments are conducted on a large, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, which lends strong credibility to the results. The comparison against the previous state-of-the-art [21] is direct and fair. The results clearly show that the proposed methods achieve a lower loss, with the gradient-free method (GFN) performing particularly well. The inclusion of convergence plots (Figure 1) effectively visualizes the optimization dynamics.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against Acceptance / for Revision)**

1.  **Evaluation Metrics (Significance):** A major weakness of the experimental section is the exclusive reliance on the loss function value (Eq. 2.4) for evaluation. While minimizing this loss is the direct goal of the optimization, it is a proxy for ranking quality. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it also reported standard information retrieval metrics like Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) on the test set. It is crucial to show that a lower loss value actually translates to a better ranking of documents as judged by human assessors.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is technically dense and presents a large amount of complex material in a limited space. While the formalism is necessary for rigor, the exposition could be improved.
    *   The connection between the required oracle accuracies (`δ`, `δ1`, `δ2`) and the parameters of the optimization algorithms (`ε`, `Mk`, etc.) is complex. A small table or a more intuitive explanation could help the reader navigate the interplay between the different components.
    *   The convexity assumption required for the gradient-free method's convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is a strong one for a generally non-convex problem. The authors state it holds in a "small vicinity of ϕ∗". Some discussion on the practical implications of this assumption or why it might be reasonable in this application would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scope of Comparison:** The comparison is limited to the single baseline from Zhukovskii et al. [21]. While this is the most direct competitor, the field of learning-to-rank on graphs has evolved. A brief discussion situating this work relative to more recent graph neural network (GNN) based approaches for ranking would provide valuable context, even if a full experimental comparison is beyond the scope.

**Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and solid experimental backing. It solves a well-defined and important open problem: how to train Supervised PageRank with convergence guarantees. The technical quality of the optimization analysis is high, and the results are convincing. The main weaknesses are in the experimental evaluation (lack of ranking metrics) and clarity of presentation, which are addressable. The strengths, particularly the novel and rigorous theoretical framework, far outweigh the weaknesses.

**Decision: Accept**
