This paper presents two novel two-level methods for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a challenging non-convex optimization problem. The core idea – leveraging a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution and employing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization on the upper level – is a significant departure from existing approaches and demonstrates a strong understanding of the limitations of prior work [21]. The theoretical guarantees on convergence, coupled with experimental validation showing improvements over the state-of-the-art, are particularly commendable. While the presentation is dense, and some details are relegated to supplementary materials, the overall contribution is substantial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of a refined lower-level approximation method with both gradient and gradient-free upper-level optimization is genuinely novel. Existing methods lacked theoretical convergence guarantees, a gap this work addresses.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides convergence rate analysis for both the lower-level approximation and the upper-level optimization algorithms. This is a substantial contribution and sets this work apart.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the existing state-of-the-art.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The paper includes a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, providing valuable insights into their scalability.
*   **Addressing Limitations:** The authors explicitly acknowledge and attempt to address the limitations of existing optimization methods for this problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density & Readability:** The paper is quite dense and requires significant effort to fully understand. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while valuable, contribute to the overall complexity. A more streamlined presentation could improve clarity.
*   **Experimental Detail:** While the experimental results are positive, more details about the experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and statistical significance would strengthen the conclusions. The mention of p-values is good, but more information would be helpful.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus solely on web page ranking. Exploring the applicability of the proposed methods to other graph ranking problems could broaden the impact of the work.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The discussion around the choice of Lipschitz constant L in the gradient-free method (referenced in the conclusion and supplementary materials) suggests potential sensitivity to parameter settings. A more thorough analysis of this sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of convexity within a small vicinity of the optimal solution on Φ is a strong one and should be explicitly discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The convergence guarantees are a significant contribution, addressing a major limitation of previous work.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The detailed complexity analysis provides valuable insights into the scalability of the algorithms.
*   The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and clearly position their work within the existing body of knowledge.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The reliance on specific assumptions (e.g., local convexity) requires careful consideration.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in presentation and scope, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of graph ranking and optimization. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the positive empirical results outweigh the drawbacks. The convergence guarantees alone are a compelling reason for acceptance. I believe this paper is a strong candidate for NIPS, pushing the boundaries of research in this area. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. While minor improvements in clarity and experimental detail would be beneficial, the core contribution is substantial and warrants publication. I would encourage the authors to consider expanding the experimental evaluation in future work.
