## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters in a Supervised PageRank model, where node and edge features influence the random walk process. The core issue is the non-convexity and constraint satisfaction of the optimization problem, making traditional methods challenging. The authors propose two two-level methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both aiming to address the limitations of previous approaches like [21]. They provide convergence guarantees for both methods and analyze the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity. Experimental results on a web ranking task demonstrate improvements over the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** A significant strength is the provision of convergence guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a notable improvement over the prior work [21]. This is particularly valuable in a field where convergence is often difficult to prove for complex, non-convex optimization problems.
* **Novelty of Approach:** Adapting gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact oracles, and developing a gradient method with similar properties, represents a novel contribution. The analysis of the accuracy vs. complexity trade-off is also insightful.
* **Practical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation on a real-world web ranking task demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over the existing state-of-the-art.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper offers a thorough analysis of the computational complexity and provides lemmas to back up claims about the efficiency of the methods. The connections to existing optimization literature (e.g., [1, 7, 16, 25]) are well-articulated.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors explicitly acknowledge the limitations of existing methods and clearly position their work as a response to these challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and mathematically involved. While rigorous, the sheer volume of notation and lemmas can make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific optimization techniques to follow the core ideas. Improved visual aids or a more streamlined presentation could enhance accessibility.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate improvement, they are limited to a single dataset and task. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other graph types (e.g., social networks) and ranking tasks would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not adequately address the sensitivity of the algorithms to hyperparameter settings. A more detailed discussion of how the choice of parameters (e.g., step size, accuracy parameters) affects performance would be valuable.
* **Incremental Improvement:** While theoretically sound and practically beneficial, the improvements presented seem incremental rather than transformative. The leap in performance over the existing method, while statistically significant, is not dramatic.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-supported claims. The methods are well-defined, and the proofs appear correct (though a thorough verification is beyond the scope of this review). The completeness of the work is good, although, as noted above, the experimental evaluation could be expanded.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The writing is often dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation. While precise, this makes the paper less accessible to a broader audience. Improved organization and more intuitive explanations would enhance clarity.
* **Originality (7/10):** The combination of gradient and gradient-free methods for this specific problem, along with the convergence analysis, represents a novel contribution. However, many of the underlying techniques (e.g., power method, gradient descent) are well-established.
* **Significance (7/10):** The results are important for the specific problem of Supervised PageRank learning. The methods address a genuine challenge in this area and offer practical improvements. While not a breakthrough, the work represents a solid advance in the field and could be useful to researchers and practitioners working with graph ranking algorithms.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides valuable theoretical guarantees for optimization algorithms in a challenging setting.
* Offers a novel and practical solution to a relevant problem.
* Demonstrates improvements over the state-of-the-art through empirical evaluation.
* The work is well-motivated and addresses limitations of previous approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and requires significant mathematical background to fully understand.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The performance improvements are incremental.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The theoretical contributions and practical improvements are valuable, and the work is well-executed. However, the authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, expanding the experimental evaluation, and providing more discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithms to hyperparameter choices. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and make it more accessible to a wider audience.
