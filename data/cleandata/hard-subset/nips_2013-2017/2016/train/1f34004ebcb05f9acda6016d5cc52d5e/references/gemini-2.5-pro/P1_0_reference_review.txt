### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a framework for ranking nodes in a graph by incorporating node and edge features into the transition and restart probabilities of a random walk. The authors identify a critical gap in the existing state-of-the-art method [21]: while it uses a gradient-based approach, it relies on the power method to approximate the stationary distribution (PageRank vector) and its derivatives. This results in an "inexact oracle" for the objective function and its gradient, for which no convergence guarantees were previously established.

The core contribution of this work is to provide two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem with formal theoretical guarantees. The key ideas are:
1.  **Lower-Level Oracle:** On the lower level, the authors propose using a more efficient and provably accurate method from Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] to compute the PageRank vector. They derive explicit bounds (Lemmas 1 and 2) that connect the desired accuracy of the function value (δ₁) or its gradient (δ₂) to the number of iterations required by this lower-level method.
2.  **Upper-Level Optimization:** On the upper level, they develop two distinct optimization schemes that explicitly account for the inexact oracle:
    *   A **gradient-free method** that adapts the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is known only up to a bounded error δ.
    *   A **gradient-based method** that generalizes the adaptive gradient descent approach of [7] to non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle.
3.  **Complexity Analysis:** For both proposed algorithms, the authors provide a full computational complexity analysis that accounts for the work done at both the lower and upper levels, establishing a clear trade-off between the desired final accuracy (ε) and the total number of arithmetic operations.
4.  **Experimental Validation:** The proposed methods are evaluated on a real-world web page ranking task using data from a commercial search engine, showing they outperform the previous state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of final loss function value.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The main strength lies in its rigorous theoretical treatment of an important practical problem.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The formalization of the Supervised PageRank learning problem within the framework of optimization with an inexact oracle is excellent. Providing explicit bounds in Lemmas 1 and 2 that make the oracle's error controllable is a key contribution.
    *   The convergence and complexity analyses (Theorems 1-4) are thorough and provide the first formal guarantees for solving this problem. The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level and upper-level computation is particularly valuable.
    *   The experimental results, while on a single dataset, clearly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods achieve a better objective value than the baseline.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set Φ. The authors acknowledge that the function is generally non-convex but assume convexity in a local region. While a common technique, it would be beneficial to discuss the practical implications of this assumption—e.g., how to verify it or how sensitive the method is to its violation.
    *   The proofs are deferred to the supplementary material. While standard practice, this makes it difficult to fully assess the technical details (e.g., the value of constants like `β1`) from the main paper.

#### **Clarity**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though it is very dense.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly stating the problem, the gap in the prior art, and a summary of the paper's contributions.
    *   The structure, separating the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1, 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2, 5.2), is logical and helps the reader understand the contributions at different levels of abstraction.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The paper is heavily reliant on complex notation and assumes significant familiarity with advanced optimization theory. A less expert reader might struggle to follow the derivations.
    *   The experimental section could be more detailed. For the baseline method (GBP), it is mentioned that several step sizes were evaluated, but it is not explicitly stated which one was used for the final comparison in Table 1. Clarifying this would strengthen the comparison.

#### **Originality**
The paper's originality lies not in inventing entirely new components but in the novel synthesis and rigorous analysis of existing ones to solve a previously unaddressed theoretical problem.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a complete, theoretically-grounded optimization framework for the Supervised PageRank problem. Previous work [21] had proposed the model but lacked a sound optimization procedure.
    *   The adaptation of general-purpose optimization methods for inexact oracles ([7], [16]) to the specific structure of this graph-based learning problem is a novel and meaningful contribution.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The core components—the Supervised PageRank model [21], the PageRank computation method [17], and the optimization frameworks [7, 16]—are based on prior work. The contribution is the "glue" and the associated analysis, which is solid but less foundational than inventing a new algorithm from scratch.

#### **Significance**
The results presented are significant for both researchers in optimization and practitioners working on graph-based ranking.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper provides a principled and reliable methodology for training a class of important ranking models. For any application relying on learning parameters for a stationary distribution of a Markov chain (not just PageRank), this framework is directly applicable.
    *   The broader idea of analyzing the complexity of two-level optimization schemes by controlling the inner-loop accuracy is highly relevant to many areas of machine learning, such as bi-level optimization and meta-learning.
    *   The demonstrated performance improvement over the existing method, combined with the new theoretical guarantees, makes this work a clear advance in the state of the art for this problem.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first rigorous convergence and complexity analysis for learning Supervised PageRank, filling an important theoretical gap.
2.  **Novel Synthesis:** It successfully combines techniques from graph theory, numerical methods, and advanced optimization to create a complete and sound solution.
3.  **Practical Relevance:** The problem is practically important in web search and recommendation systems. The proposed methods are shown to outperform the existing state-of-the-art.
4.  **Generality:** The optimization frameworks developed for inexact oracles have potential applications beyond the specific PageRank problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Novelty of Components:** The core algorithmic building blocks are adaptations of existing methods rather than entirely new inventions.
2.  **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The analysis for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption for a non-convex problem, which could be seen as a limitation.
3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are conducted on a single (albeit real-world) dataset against a single baseline.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the problem of learning graph-based ranking functions. The weaknesses are minor compared to the main strength, which is the rigorous and complete analysis of a problem that was previously only addressed heuristically. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported, and the results advance the state of the art. I recommend acceptance.
