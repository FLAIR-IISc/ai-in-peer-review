Summary
The paper tackles learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The core difficulty is that the objective (pairwise hinge-squared loss over query-dependent graphs) depends on the stationary distributions πq(ϕ), whose exact values and gradients are unavailable. The authors propose two two-level methods:

- Lower level: approximate πq(ϕ) and its derivatives using a linearly convergent method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) based on a truncated and renormalized Neumann series, instead of the classical power method. They also derive a forward-propagation scheme to approximate ∂π/∂ϕ with prescribed accuracy.
- Upper level: (i) a random gradient-free method using two-point spherical smoothing with bounded additive oracle error, adapted to constrained optimization; (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle (unknown Lipschitz constant), with convergence to stationarity measured by the gradient mapping.

They provide complexity bounds that explicitly trade off lower-level accuracy (number of inner iterations) and outer-loop convergence, and they apply the methods to learning on a query-dependent browsing graph built from Yandex logs. Experiments compare the gradient-based (GBN), gradient-free (GFN), and a prior gradient method (GBP) that uses power iterations and unguaranteed inexact gradients. Reported results show improved objective values, with GFN consistently outperforming GBP and GBN often better as well.

Quality
- Theory: The inexact-oracle frameworks are reasonable extensions of Nesterov–Spokoiny (zero-order), Ghadimi–Lan/Nesterov (first-order), and Devolder et al. (inexact oracle) to constrained settings and bounded, deterministic oracle error. The random-sphere estimator, bias/error decomposition, and adaptive linesearch logic are standard but carefully adapted. The lower-level truncation schemes for π and ∂π/∂ϕ align with differentiating the Neumann series and are plausible, with linear convergence in α and explicit δ-dependent inner iteration counts. However, key constants (e.g., β1) and full proofs are deferred to the supplement, making it hard to validate tightness.
- Correctness of application: The gradient-free upper-level analysis assumes convexity of f on Φ, but f is generally nonconvex; the paper “assumes” Φ is a small convex neighborhood around a local minimizer where f is convex, without verification. This undermines the relevance of Theorem 2 to the practical setting. The gradient-based method does not require convexity and is more appropriate.
- Experiments: The evaluation is limited. The primary metric reported is the training objective f (pairwise hinge-squared). There is no reporting of standard IR ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP) on held-out test sets, despite claims that ranking quality improves. Baselines are limited: only the prior supervised PageRank method is compared; no comparison to strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM) or supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec, WSDM’11). Moreover, stopping criteria based on test-set objective (for GBP) risk data leakage. Wall-clock time and scalability are not thoroughly reported; the claimed lower-level speedup over power iterations needs empirical backing in runtime vs. accuracy.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable but has numerous typos and stylistic issues, incomplete references, and reliance on supplementary material for essential proofs/algorithms (Algorithms 2 and 4). Important assumptions (e.g., positivity of feature-weighted sums, choice of Φ ensuring all denominators positive) are scattered. The experimental section lacks sufficient details (e.g., exact train/validation/test protocol, computational environment, wall-clock timing, confidence intervals).

Originality
- The lower-level use of Nesterov–Nemirovski’s scheme for π and its differentiation in this learning context is a useful adaptation, though the idea of differentiating the Neumann series is standard. 
- The upper-level contributions are incremental but valuable: adapting zero-order randomized methods to constrained problems with bounded inexactness, and an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracles and unknown Lipschitz constants.
- The overall integration and complexity trade-off analysis for supervised PageRank learning appears novel within this niche.

Significance
Learning graph-structured ranking with provable convergence under inexact computation is important. If empirical gains were demonstrated convincingly on ranking metrics and against stronger baselines, the impact would be higher. As is, the theoretical contributions are incremental, and the empirical section does not yet establish practical significance beyond the chosen loss.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical issue: reliable learning when only inexact stationary distributions/gradients are available.
- Nonconvex projected gradient method with inexact oracle and adaptive stepsize is broadly useful.
- Lower-level derivative computation scheme is principled and may be more efficient than power-method-based differentiation.
- Complexity bounds explicitly capture inner/outer accuracy trade-offs.

Cons
- Gradient-free method’s theoretical guarantees rely on local convexity assumed but not verified.
- Experiments are insufficient: no ranking metrics, limited baselines, potential test leakage in stopping, no runtime/scale analysis.
- Clarity issues: missing/incomplete references, reliance on supplement for essential constants/proofs, numerous typos.
- Lack of ablations (e.g., sensitivity to α, feature scales, accuracy parameters δ, τ).
- Practical relevance of gradient-free method for m≈O(10^2) is not established.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide NDCG/MAP on held-out test sets and compare to standard LTR baselines and supervised random walks. Use a proper validation set for hyperparameter/early stopping.
- Report wall-clock times and memory for lower-level methods (power vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski) at matched accuracy; include scaling with |Vq|, |Eq|, and |Q|.
- Justify or empirically verify the local convexity assumption underlying GFN’s use; otherwise, clarify that guarantees don’t apply.
- Include sensitivity analysis for δ, τ, ε, and L0; show how inner accuracy affects outer convergence and runtime.
- Clarify and include explicit definitions/constants (e.g., β1) in the main text or an accessible appendix.
- Clean up references (missing years/venues/authors), fix typos, and include pseudo-code for Algorithms 2 and 4.

Recommendation: Reject in current form, primarily due to experimental weaknesses and clarity issues. With stronger empirical validation, clearer exposition, and removal or justification of the convexity assumption for GFN, this work could be competitive.
