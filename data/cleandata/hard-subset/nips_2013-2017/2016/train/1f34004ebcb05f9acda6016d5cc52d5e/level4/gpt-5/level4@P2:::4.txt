Summary
The paper tackles learning Supervised PageRank models via nonconvex loss minimization when node and edge features influence restart and transition probabilities. The authors propose two two-level algorithms:

- A gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle: the lower level computes approximate stationary distributions and their derivatives using a linearly convergent scheme (Nesterov–Nemirovski), and the upper level runs an adaptive projected gradient method designed for nonconvex composite objectives with inexact oracles. They prove convergence to a stationary point and provide complexity bounds that account for the interplay between oracle accuracy and computation.
- A random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle: the lower level again approximates stationary distributions to a prescribed accuracy; the upper level uses a constrained, sphere-randomized, finite-difference estimator with guarantees under bounded function-evaluation error. They extend prior gradient-free frameworks to constrained domains and biased, inexact oracles, and give complexity guarantees.

They also analyze the trade-off between the inner accuracy (number of iterations for stationary distribution and derivative approximation) and overall complexity. Experiments on a query-dependent user browsing graph from a commercial search engine (Yandex) show that both methods improve the supervised ranking loss over a prior two-level gradient method that relied on power iterations and lacked convergence guarantees. The gradient-free method often achieves the best loss; the new gradient method converges in fewer upper-level iterations than the baseline.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on supervised random walks/Supervised PageRank, where weights are linear in features and parameters are learned by minimizing a pairwise loss. Prior work used power iterations for both scores and derivatives and did not establish convergence under inexact oracles. The authors instead use a linearly convergent scheme to approximate PageRank and develop optimization methods that explicitly model oracle inexactness. They connect to inexact oracle theory (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), random gradient-free methods (Nesterov–Spokoiny), and nonconvex stochastic optimization (Ghadimi–Lan), adapting these ideas to constrained settings with biased, bounded errors.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: two upper-level optimizers designed for inexact lower-level computations, with convergence-rate guarantees and explicit complexity accounting for accuracy-iteration trade-offs.
- Technical novelty: constrained, biased, gradient-free oracle analysis; adaptive gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracles and unknown Lipschitz constant.
- Practical relevance: replaces power iterations with a faster scheme for stationary distributions and derivative propagation; avoids explicit derivatives at upper level in the gradient-free variant.
- Empirical gains: on a real dataset, both proposed methods reduce the training loss relative to the previous gradient method; the gradient-based method converges in fewer outer iterations for some settings.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation limited in scope: experiments use 600 queries and ~1.7K labeled pairs from a single search engine. Only the training loss is reported; standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) are absent, making it hard to judge end-task impact.
- Runtime/memory not reported: wall-clock time, number of arithmetic operations realized in practice, memory footprint, and parallelization details are missing. Given the emphasis on complexity trade-offs, empirical runtime comparisons (including derivative computation cost) are essential.
- Assumptions need clearer justification: for the gradient-free method, the convexity-in-a-ball assumption around a local minimum is pivotal for the stated complexity result; the paper should discuss when this holds and how Φ is chosen in practice. Similarly, constants such as L and β1 and the choice of α should be explained or estimated empirically.
- Baselines: comparison is restricted to a prior supervised PageRank learner. Additional baselines (e.g., learning-to-rank methods like LambdaMART on the same features, or alternative supervised random-walk learners) would contextualize the gains.
- Reproducibility details: hyperparameter selection, stopping criteria, and sensitivity (e.g., to ε, R, L) could be elaborated; code availability is not mentioned.

Quality
The theoretical results appear sound and are aligned with inexact oracle literature. The construction of derivative approximations and the convergence theorems are appropriate for the problem structure. However, empirical validation should include runtime and quality metrics beyond the loss to substantiate practical significance.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized. Some sections (definitions of constants, assumptions for Theorem 2, parameter choices for α, L, R, β1) would benefit from clearer exposition. There are minor typographical issues and inconsistent references. Adding diagrams summarizing the two-level structure and the inexact-oracle dataflow would help.

Originality
Adapting random gradient-free methods to constrained, biased inexact oracles and developing an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives with inexact oracles are novel in this application. Using the Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme at the lower level and coupling accuracy to overall complexity is well thought out.

Significance
Supervised PageRank is widely used; providing convergence guarantees and improved loss with practical approximation schemes is valuable. The broader frameworks for inexact-oracle optimization may interest a wider NIPS audience. Demonstrated improvements on standard ranking metrics and runtime would elevate the impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical development with clear relevance to large-scale, inexact-oracle learning problems.
- Practical algorithms with demonstrable improvements over a commonly used baseline in supervised random walks.
- Careful analysis of accuracy–cost trade-offs and end-to-end complexity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics and runtime/memory comparisons.
- Limited baselines and dataset diversity; uncertain generalization to larger or different graphs.
- Some assumptions and constants are under-motivated or opaque to practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report wall-clock time, number of inner iterations (N, N1, N2), and memory usage for all methods; include scaling with |V|, |E|, and feature dimensionality.
- Add NDCG/MAP on held-out queries, and clarify whether improvements in loss translate to better ranking quality.
- Justify convexity-in-Φ and provide diagnostics for L and β1; include sensitivity analyses for α, R, ε.
- Compare with additional baselines, including modern learning-to-rank methods using the same features.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos.

Overall
A thoughtful and technically strong paper that advances optimization for supervised PageRank under inexact computations. With stronger empirical evaluation (runtime and ranking metrics) and clearer practical guidance on assumptions and parameter selection, this would be a compelling contribution. I lean toward acceptance contingent on addressing the experimental and reporting gaps.
