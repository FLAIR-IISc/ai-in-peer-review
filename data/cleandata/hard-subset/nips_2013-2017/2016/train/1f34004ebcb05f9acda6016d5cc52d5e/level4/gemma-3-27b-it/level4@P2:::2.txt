This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, framing it as a constrained non-convex optimization challenge that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two distinct solution approaches: a gradient-free method leveraging an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method utilizing an inexact first-order oracle. A key contribution is the provision of convergence rate analyses for both methods. However, a significant concern is the omission of experimental results from the main text, rendering the paper incomplete and hindering a full assessment of the proposed methods’ practical performance.

The paper effectively situates its work within the existing literature on PageRank and supervised ranking methods, referencing key works such as Page [18], Backstrom & Leskovec [2], and Zhukovskii et al. [21]. The motivation for extending PageRank with node and edge features is clearly articulated, highlighting the limitations of structure-based approaches and the benefits of incorporating richer information. The problem formulation, presented in Section 2, is well-defined and builds logically upon prior work in Supervised PageRank [21]. The proposed methods are presented with a reasonable level of detail, and the theoretical contributions, namely the convergence guarantees, are noteworthy.

Strengths of the paper include the theoretical convergence analysis for both the gradient-free and gradient-based methods, a valuable contribution given the non-convex nature of the problem. The adaptation of existing optimization techniques ([16, 17]) to the constrained optimization setting with inexact oracles is also a positive aspect. The authors also investigate the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity, adding a practical dimension to the work.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall quality of the submission. The most critical issue is the placement of experimental results exclusively in the supplementary material. This makes it difficult for the program committee (and the reader) to evaluate the empirical performance of the proposed methods without significant effort. A concise presentation of key results within the main text is essential. Furthermore, the assumption of convexity in Theorem 1 seems inconsistent with the explicitly stated non-convexity of the objective function (2.4). Clarification regarding how the parameter space Φ is chosen to ensure local convexity is required. While Theorem 3 provides a bound on the distance between successive iterates, it doesn’t explicitly demonstrate convergence to a stationary point. A clearer connection between the bound and convergence criteria would strengthen the analysis. Finally, the slow convergence of the gradient-free method observed in Figure 1 (supplementary material) suggests a need for further investigation into its efficiency, ideally with a comparison of computational cost.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical convergence guarantees for both proposed methods, addressing a challenging non-convex optimization problem.
*   Novel adaptation of existing optimization techniques to a constrained setting with inexact oracles.
*   Analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and complexity.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Omission of experimental results from the main text, making it incomplete and hindering evaluation.
*   Apparent inconsistency between the stated non-convexity of the problem and the convexity assumption in Theorem 1.
*   Lack of a direct link between the convergence bound in Theorem 3 and convergence to a stationary point.
*   Potentially slow convergence of the gradient-free method, requiring further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents theoretically sound methods with promising analytical results, the lack of experimental results in the main text is a significant drawback. Addressing this issue, clarifying the convexity assumption, and strengthening the convergence analysis would substantially improve the paper's quality. As it stands, the paper feels incomplete and falls short of the standards expected for NIPS publication. I currently recommend **rejecting** the paper, but would be open to reconsidering a revised version that addresses the aforementioned concerns.
