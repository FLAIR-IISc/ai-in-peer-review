- Paper studies learning a feature-parameterized PageRank (restart/transition depend on node/edge features) with a nonconvex pairwise-ranking square loss over query-dependent graphs.  
- Proposes two two-level optimization approaches: (i) gradient-based and (ii) random gradient-free, both using Nesterov–Nemirovski to approximate stationary distributions (inexact oracle).  
- Upper-level contributions: (a) biased spherical smoothing gradient-free algorithm with convergence for constrained problems with inexact zero-order evaluations, (b) adaptive projected gradient for constrained nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracle and unknown smoothness.  
- Provides iteration and arithmetic complexity bounds and analyzes the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity.  
- Uses a faster lower-level stationary distribution approximation than the power method and generalizes it to derivative computation.  
- Empirical results on a Yandex browsing dataset show improved loss and convergence versus a baseline that uses the power method.  
- Strength: addresses a practically important feature-based PageRank variant and frames it clearly as a two-level problem with inexact oracles.  
- Strength: convergence guarantees are provided for both gradient-free and gradient-based upper-level methods.  
- Strength: explicit complexity bounds and discussion of accuracy–cost trade-offs are given.  
- Strength: empirical improvements over a commonly used baseline are reported.  
- Weakness: paper has many grammatical errors, incomplete/misattributed references, and needs careful copyediting.  
- Weakness: unclear relationship and novelty relative to prior “Supervised PageRank” and semi-supervised ranking work; novelty over [21] not made explicit.  
- Weakness: key analytical assumptions (local convexity for gradient-free, specific inexact oracle inequality for gradient method) may not hold for the nonconvex loss in practice and need justification.  
- Weakness: constraint choice (L2 ball ∩ positive orthant) may limit expressivity; selection and sensitivity of ϕ̂ and R are not justified or reported.  
- Weakness: main text experimental reporting is sparse; key results, metrics, confidence intervals, and ablations are deferred to the appendix.  
- Weakness: experiments use a single dataset and limited query subsets; unclear generality across datasets or standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP) beyond training loss.  
- Quality: theoretical components are interesting but lack proof sketches and intuitive explanations in the main text.  
- Quality: empirical section lacks reproducibility details (hyperparameters, stopping criteria, seeds) and wall-clock/time-per-iteration comparisons.  
- Clarity: notation reuse, undefined symbols, and inconsistent terminology (restart/teleport, τ, δ) reduce readability and should be standardized.  
- Originality: methods are combinations of known ideas, but adapting Nesterov–Nemirovski and providing inexact-oracle guarantees and a biased spherical gradient-free oracle appear to offer novel, coherent contributions.  
- Significance: potential impact for learning-to-rank on graphs if assumptions hold and gains generalize, but current empirical evidence in the main text is insufficient.  
- Questions: how were ϕ̂ and R chosen and how sensitive are results to R and positivity constraints?  
- Questions: what α values were used and how sensitive are results to α?  
- Questions: what were the practical step counts N, N1, N2, their relation to δ, and the time/iteration trade-offs?  
- Questions: were multiple datasets/graph types evaluated and do improvements translate to held-out ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP)?  
- Suggestions: include concise proof sketches and clear assumptions in the main text, add a main-body results table with mean±std and ranking metrics, provide ablations/sensitivity analyses, and supply implementation details and code.  
- Arguments for acceptance: provides convergence-guaranteed algorithms with explicit complexity trade-offs and promising empirical results vs a standard baseline.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental breadth/detail in the main text, heavy reliance on appendix, and unconvincing justification of assumptions for the target application.
