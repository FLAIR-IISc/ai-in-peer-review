- Summary: Studies learning feature-based node/edge weights for a PageRank-style random walk, combining a stationary-distribution approximation with a biased zeroth-order optimizer, formalizing the problem and proving convergence, with experiments on a single web graph (full results in appendix).

- Strength: Aims to provide performance guarantees in a setting where prior approaches lacked them.

- Strength: Clearly separates a generic biased zeroth-order framework from its PageRank instantiation.

- Strength: Nontrivial theoretical contributions: bounds for an approximate gradient oracle and convergence guarantees under bias.

- Strength: Topic relevance: PageRank parameter learning is important for ranking and graph mining.

- Weakness: Novelty/positioning unclear—appears to reproduce prior work [21] under different terminology (“Supervised” vs “semi-supervised”) without clearly articulating differences.

- Weakness: Empirical evidence is limited in the main text, with core results relegated to the appendix and lacking effect sizes, significance, and external validity across multiple graphs.

- Weakness: Missing experimental details (choice of φ0 and R, smoothing parameters, iteration budgets, stopping criteria), harming reproducibility.

- Weakness: Technical assumptions may be restrictive or unjustified in practice (R-ball constraint, Lipschitz/smoothness, bounded approximation errors, mixing properties).

- Weakness: The cumulative impact of multiple bias sources (stationary-distribution approximation and zeroth-order smoothing) is not quantified in realistic regimes.

- Weakness: Presentation issues—grammatical/typographical errors, inconsistent citations, and confusing terminology that should not relegate proofs/experiments entirely to the appendix.

- Clarification request: Precisely delineate novelties relative to [21] and other prior art with a side-by-side comparison of assumptions and guarantees.

- Clarification request: Detail the biased gradient oracle—express bias and variance as functions of smoothing radius, sample size, and stationary-distribution approximation accuracy.

- Clarification request: Report how φ0 and R were chosen and provide sensitivity analyses for these hyperparameters.

- Clarification request: Describe datasets (number of graphs, sizes/topologies), train/validation/test splits, metrics used, and how ground-truth labels were obtained.

- Clarification request: Provide computational costs: wall-clock time, number of oracle evaluations, convergence curves, and comparisons to gradient-based baselines.

- Clarification request: Empirically check whether stated assumptions (smoothness, bounded features, mixing rates) hold on the datasets.

- Suggestion: Improve positioning and credit by using consistent terminology with prior work and clearly stating novel contributions.

- Suggestion: Move a central theorem (with proof sketch) to the main text and map generic assumptions to the PageRank instantiation with constant translations.

- Suggestion: Expand the empirical section in the main body with multiple graphs, error bars, ablations (approximation accuracy, φ0, R, smoothing), and runtime/scalability comparisons.

- Suggestion: Discuss practical impact and when the method is preferable over alternatives, quantifying performance–compute trade-offs.

- Suggestion: Improve reproducibility by listing all hyperparameters, initialization, stopping criteria, preprocessing, and releasing code or detailed pseudocode.

- Suggestion: Proofread and fix writing, citation inconsistencies, and the “Supervised vs semi-supervised” terminology discrepancy.

- Overall assessment: The work is relevant and theoretically interesting but lacks clear novelty relative to prior work, defers key empirical evidence, and relies on strong assumptions without empirical validation—substantial revisions are needed.
