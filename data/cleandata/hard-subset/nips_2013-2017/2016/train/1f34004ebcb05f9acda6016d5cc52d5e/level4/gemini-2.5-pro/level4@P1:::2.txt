This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models. The authors formulate this as a constrained, non-convex optimization problem that accounts for node and edge features. The core contribution is the proposal of two new optimization algorithms designed to handle this problem: a random gradient-free method based on an inexact zero-order oracle, and an adaptive gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle. A key aspect of this work is that, unlike previous methods for this problem, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms. The methods are evaluated empirically on a web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its strong theoretical foundation. It formally addresses the Supervised PageRank learning problem within a modern optimization framework (non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle) and provides convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithms. This is a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art method, which lacked such guarantees.
*   **Novel Application of Methods:** The paper successfully adapts and extends recent optimization techniques—specifically, random gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods—to the specific challenges of learning a graph-based ranking model. The handling of the inexact oracle, where the objective and its gradient can only be approximated, is particularly relevant to this problem.
*   **Completeness:** The work is well-rounded, presenting the problem formulation, the development of two distinct algorithms, their theoretical analysis, and an experimental comparison against a relevant baseline.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Self-Containment:** The paper's completeness is undermined by the placement of key experimental results, such as figures illustrating the convergence of the algorithms (e.g., Figure 1), in the supplementary material. A reader should be able to grasp the core empirical performance of the proposed methods from the main text alone. For the paper to be self-contained, crucial figures and tables supporting the main claims should be included in the main body.

2.  **Convexity Assumption for Theorem 1:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1) assumes that the objective function is convex. However, the loss function in (2.4) is explicitly stated to be non-convex. In Section 4.2, the authors justify this by assuming that the optimization is performed over a set Φ that is a "small vicinity of a local minimum" where the function happens to be convex. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice. The authors should provide more justification for this. For instance, is there any practical guidance on how to choose the set Φ to ensure this condition is met? Is there any empirical evidence to suggest that the loss landscape for Supervised PageRank typically exhibits such locally convex regions around minima?

3.  **Interpretation of Convergence in Theorem 3:** Theorem 3 provides a bound for the gradient-based method on the quantity `‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖^2`, which is used as a stopping criterion. While this is a standard approach in non-convex optimization, the connection to stationarity could be made more explicit. Could the authors clarify how this guarantee relates to the convergence of the iterates `xk` to a stationary point of the objective function? Explicitly connecting this criterion to the norm of the gradient would strengthen the theoretical claim.

4.  **Evaluation of Computational Efficiency:** The experimental results focus on the final value of the loss function. However, a crucial aspect of optimization algorithms is their efficiency. Based on Figure 1 in the supplement, the gradient-free method (GFN) appears to converge more slowly (in terms of iterations) than the gradient-based methods. The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison of the algorithms' practical efficiency. The authors should consider reporting metrics such as wall-clock time or the total number of arithmetic operations required to achieve a certain loss value on the test set. This would provide a clearer understanding of the trade-offs between the proposed methods.
