This paper studies the convergence of a non-convex loss minimization problem that arises when learning parameters for a general graph-based ranking model. The ranking is defined via a Markov random walk whose transition weights depend on node and edge weights, which in turn are functions of node and edge features (constructed through additional random walks). Because the exact objective value is unavailable, standard optimization methods are not directly applicable.

The authors propose a two-level approach. At the first level, they use a linearly convergent algorithm to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. They show that, under this scheme, the loss can be approximated to any prescribed accuracy. At the second level, they develop a gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle and prove convergence to a stationary point. The main contribution is the adaptation of inexact-oracle techniques to constrained problems where the function value can be computed only up to a known precision. They establish convergence guarantees for this method and apply it as the outer optimization procedure.

The paper is clearly written, and the proofs appear sound, although I did not verify all details.

My main concern is novelty. Several optimization components seem to be adaptations of known techniques, particularly those related to inexact oracles and zeroth-order methods, as in Nesterov and Spokoiny (Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015). In addition, variants of supervised PageRank have been proposed previously. The paper would benefit from a more explicit positioning relative to this prior work, clearly delineating what is new (e.g., specific convergence rates under constraints, new error-control mechanisms, or problem-specific insights) versus what is adapted.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- A brief concluding section would help summarize the theoretical and practical takeaways and clarify limitations and future directions.
- Present the optimization framework in a more general form first, and then show the studied algorithm as a concrete instance. This would better situate the contribution with respect to the state of the art and make the reuse of general techniques more transparent.
- Where possible, provide a direct comparison (assumptions, oracle models, rates, and constraints) with closely related methods to highlight the distinct advantages of the proposed approach.
