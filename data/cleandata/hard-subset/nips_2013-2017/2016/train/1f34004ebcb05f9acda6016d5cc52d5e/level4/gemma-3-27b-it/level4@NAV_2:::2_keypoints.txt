- Addresses learning Supervised PageRank as a constrained non-convex optimization using node and edge features.
- Proposes two solution methods: a gradient-free approach with an inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based approach with an inexact first-order oracle.
- Main contribution: provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, unlike prior work [21].
- Experimental comparisons to state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task report performance improvements.
- Well-motivated extension building on random-walk models and prior work integrating features into restart/transition probabilities.
- Follows the two-level optimization framework of Supervised PageRank [21] but fills its gap by supplying convergence guarantees.
- Introduction and analysis of inexact oracle methods are significant for large-scale graphs where exact computations are infeasible.
- Offering both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches gives a practical trade-off between computational cost and convergence.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis and consideration of lower-level estimation accuracy and its effect on complexity.
- Strength: successful adaptation of existing optimization techniques to a constrained, inexact setting.
- Weakness: experimental results are placed only in the supplementary material, leaving the main text incomplete.
- Weakness: Theorem 1’s convexity assumption conflicts with the stated non-convexity of the problem and needs clarification (choice of feasible set Φ/local convexity).
- Weakness: Theorem 3 bounds successive-iterate differences but does not explicitly establish convergence to a stationary point.
- Weakness: observed slow convergence of the gradient-free method (Figure 1) raises concerns about practical efficiency.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel application of inexact oracle methods, rigorous guarantees, empirical improvements, and addresses a key limitation of [21].
- Arguments against acceptance: missing main-text experiments, unjustified convexity assumption, incomplete convergence proof to stationary points, and slow gradient-free convergence.
