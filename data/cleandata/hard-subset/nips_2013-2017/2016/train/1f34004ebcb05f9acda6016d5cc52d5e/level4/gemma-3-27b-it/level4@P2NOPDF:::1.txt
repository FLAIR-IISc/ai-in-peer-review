This paper investigates the convergence properties of a non-convex loss minimization problem arising in the context of learning parameters for a general graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk process where node and edge weights are determined by random walks based on node and edge features, respectively. Due to the intractability of directly optimizing this loss function with existing methods that require exact function values, the authors propose a two-level approach.

The first level focuses on estimating an approximation to the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk using a linearly convergent method. The authors demonstrate that this approximation can achieve any desired level of precision.  Furthermore, they develop a gradient method for constrained, non-convex optimization problems that utilizes an inexact oracle – a crucial component given the approximate loss function. They prove the convergence of this method to a stationary point. The core contribution lies in extending this approach to the setting of constrained optimization with function values known only to a certain precision, and subsequently leveraging this theoretical result within the second level of their algorithm. 

The paper is well-written and the proofs appear sound, though a complete verification was not possible within the scope of this review. However, a significant concern is that the optimization techniques presented largely build upon prior work, particularly “Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015.” Additionally, the concept of supervised PageRank, a related algorithm, has been explored previously.

Several minor improvements could strengthen the paper. Primarily, the inclusion of a concluding section is recommended to summarize the findings and discuss potential future directions.  Moreover, framing the optimization problem in a broader, more general context would be beneficial. Presenting the studied algorithm as a specific instance of a more general framework would better clarify its contribution relative to existing state-of-the-art methods and highlight its unique aspects.
