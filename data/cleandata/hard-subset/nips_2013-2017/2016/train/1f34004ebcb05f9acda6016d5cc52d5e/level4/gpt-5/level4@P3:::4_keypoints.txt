- Problem: learning query-dependent Supervised PageRank with restart and transition probabilities linear in node/edge features and a pairwise squared hinge loss over preferences.  
- Main challenge: outer loss/gradient evaluation requires stationary distributions of ϕ-parameterized Markov chains that can only be computed approximately.  
- Methods: two-level frameworks — a constrained gradient-free (zero-order) method extending Nesterov–Spokoiny randomization with bounded function-value errors, and a gradient-based nonconvex composite method with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant.  
- Lower-level: use a linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method (instead of power method) to approximate stationary distributions and derive a procedure to approximate derivatives to prescribed accuracy.  
- Analysis: explicit complexity bounds tying lower-level oracle accuracy (number of iterations) to outer optimization convergence and total arithmetic complexity.  
- Experiments: on a Yandex user-browsing graph (~600 queries) the proposed methods improve test loss over prior two-level gradient method [21], but standard IR metrics are not reported.  

- Strength: clear technical formulation of inexact zero- and first-order oracles with convergence-rate guarantees that incorporate oracle inaccuracies.  
- Strength: novel extensions — constrained inexact random gradient-free methods, adaptive projected gradient for nonconvex inexact oracles, and tailored derivative approximation for Markov chain stationary distributions.  
- Strength: completeness of analysis with upper bounds on oracle accuracy vs cost and scheduling guidance for lower-level accuracy.  
- Strength: empirical loss improvements over prior supervised PageRank approach.  

- Weakness: empirical reporting omits wall-clock time, memory, and parallelization details despite an efficiency focus.  
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope — small dataset (~600 queries), no standard IR metrics (NDCG, MAP), and only one baseline comparison.  
- Weakness: theoretical claim mismatch — gradient-free guarantees require convexity on the feasible ball, which contradicts the paper’s general nonconvex framing and is not emphasized.  
- Weakness: gradient-based guarantee is phrased in terms of prox-gradient stationarity but this target should be made explicit.  
- Weakness: manuscript clarity issues — many typos, inconsistent notation, undefined constants (e.g., β1), and incomplete/inconsistent references.  
- Weakness: practical guidance is limited — hyperparameter defaults and sensitivity analyses are sparse; treatment of α and zero-outdegree nodes needs more discussion.  

- Quality: theoretical development appears technically sound though several proofs/constants are in the supplement; more intuition in the main text would help.  
- Clarity: overall structure is reasonable but requires significant editing for grammar, notation consistency, and completeness of definitions and references.  
- Originality: novel and relevant extensions of inexact gradient-free and adaptive inexact first-order methods, plus a useful lower-level derivative approximation for this application.  
- Significance: potentially impactful if paired with stronger empirical efficiency (wall-clock) and standard ranking metrics on larger datasets; currently promising but not fully demonstrated.  

- Key requests: report wall-clock runtimes, memory and implementation details; add NDCG/MAP and comparisons to more baselines; clarify convexity assumptions and stationarity targets; provide ablations on α and features; define β1 and other constants in the main text; fix grammar and references.  

- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contributions on optimization with inexact oracles, empirical loss improvements, and broad applicability beyond this ranking task.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacking wall-clock/IR metric validation at scale, convexity requirement for the gradient-free method conflicts with nonconvex claims, and presentation issues hinder readability.  

- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid theory and promising results but needs stronger empirical validation and polishing for full impact.
