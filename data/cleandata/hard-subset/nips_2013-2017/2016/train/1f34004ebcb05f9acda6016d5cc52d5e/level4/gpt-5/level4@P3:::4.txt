Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of learning query-dependent Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities depend linearly on node and edge features. The learning objective is a pairwise squared hinge loss over labeled preferences. The key challenge is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires stationary distributions of Markov chains parameterized by ϕ, which can only be computed approximately. The paper proposes two two-level optimization frameworks that account for this inexactness:

- A gradient-free (zero-order) method that extends Nesterov–Spokoiny-style random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with bounded function-value errors and uniform sphere randomization. It yields complexity bounds under convexity on the feasible ball Φ and uses a smoothed finite-difference estimator based on two function calls per iteration.

- A gradient-based method for nonconvex composite optimization with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, generalizing Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov to constrained, potentially nonconvex problems via an adaptive projected step with Bregman distance.

On the lower level, instead of the power method used in prior supervised PageRank work [21], the authors use a linearly convergent method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate stationary distributions and derive a new procedure to approximate derivatives with respect to ϕ, each to a prescribed accuracy. They quantify the trade-off between the oracle accuracy (set by the number of lower-level iterations) and overall complexity of the outer optimization.

Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset (≈600 queries) compare the proposed methods against the state-of-the-art two-level gradient method [21]. The proposed methods show better test loss and, according to the paper, improved ranking quality (although standard IR metrics are not reported).

The work is connected to supervised PageRank [21], gradient-free optimization [16], first-order methods for nonconvex problems [7], inexact oracles [25], and iterative computation of stationary distributions and their derivatives [17, 20, 21].

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Clear formulation of inexact zero- and first-order oracles for this learning problem; convergence-rate guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based outer methods that explicitly incorporate oracle inaccuracies.

- Novelty: Extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained settings with bounded function-value errors; adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact oracles and unknown Lipschitz constant; a derivative approximation scheme tailored to the Markov chain stationary distribution.

- Completeness: Upper bounds provided for oracle accuracy vs. computational cost, and for total arithmetic complexity (with problem-dependent constants). The analysis clarifies how lower-level accuracy should be scheduled to ensure outer convergence.

- Empirical: Demonstrates consistent improvements over the prior two-level gradient approach that relied on power method approximations.

Weaknesses
- Empirical reporting: The paper does not report wall-clock time, memory, or parallelization details; results are shown primarily in terms of loss reduction and iteration counts. Given the emphasis on efficiency, wall-clock time and throughput on large graphs are essential.

- Evaluation scope: Limited dataset (≈600 queries) and no reporting of standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP, AUC). “Ranking quality” is mentioned, but quantitative IR metrics are missing. Comparison is only against one baseline (GBP) from [21]; stronger learning-to-rank baselines or additional PageRank variants would strengthen the case.

- Assumptions vs claims: The gradient-free method’s guarantee requires convexity on the feasible ball around the optimum, which contradicts the general nonconvex framing of the problem; this limitation should be highlighted more prominently. For the nonconvex case, the gradient-based method measures stationarity via the prox-gradient norm, which is appropriate but should be made explicit as the main target of the guarantee.

- Clarity/organization: The manuscript contains numerous typos and grammatical issues, uneven notation (e.g., varying norms and constants), and a few undefined constants in the main text (e.g., β1 deferred to the supplement). The references are incomplete and inconsistently formatted.

- Practical guidance: Hyperparameter selection (τ, δ, N, N1, N2, L0) is theoretically motivated but practical defaults and sensitivity analyses are limited. The choice of α and treatment of zero-outdegree nodes deserve more discussion.

Quality
The theoretical development appears technically sound, with appropriate use of inexact-oracle analysis and careful accounting of lower-level approximation error. However, some proofs and constants are relegated to the supplement; more intuition in the main text would help. Empirical validation demonstrates improvements in the proposed loss, but lacks wall-clock analyses and standard IR metrics.

Clarity
The overall structure is reasonable, but the presentation requires significant editing for grammar, notation consistency, and readability. Suggestions:
- Clearly state in the main text where convexity is assumed (gradient-free) vs nonconvexity (gradient-based).
- Define all constants and norms on first use; avoid switching between ∥·∥∞ and ∥·∥2 without reminder.
- Replace “a.o.” with “arithmetic operations” and expand all abbreviations on first use.
- Provide an algorithm box for the derivative approximation (3.2–3.3).
- Fix reference formatting and ensure complete bibliographic entries.

Originality
The extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained, inexact-function settings and the adaptive gradient method with inexact first-order oracle for nonconvex problems are novel and relevant. The lower-level derivative approximation for stationary distributions is a useful contribution in this application domain.

Significance
If the guarantees can be coupled with strong empirical efficiency (wall-clock) and ranking metrics on larger datasets, the work could impact graph-based learning-to-rank and more broadly optimization with inexact oracles. As presented, the significance is promising but not fully demonstrated empirically.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report wall-clock runtime, memory usage, and implementation details (e.g., sparse operations, vectorization, parallelism) for all methods. Include time–accuracy trade-offs and sensitivity to N, N1, N2.
- Add standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) on held-out data, and compare to additional baselines (e.g., learning-to-rank with features alone, other supervised random walk variants).
- Clarify the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method and its practical scope; discuss whether a nonconvex analysis (e.g., convergence to stationary points) is possible.
- Provide ablations on α and on feature subsets; discuss robustness to noisy labels.
- Define β1 and other constants in the main text or provide a succinct bound that conveys dependence on key problem parameters.
- Improve grammar and referencing throughout; ensure that all cited works have complete entries.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and relevant theoretical contributions on optimization with inexact oracles.
- Clear advances over prior supervised PageRank training in both guarantees and empirical loss.
- Broad applicability of the methods beyond this specific ranking problem.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical section lacks wall-clock benchmarks and standard IR metrics on larger-scale data.
- Gradient-free method’s guarantees hinge on convexity on Φ, which conflicts with the nonconvex narrative.
- Presentation issues (typos, missing details) hinder readability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are strong and relevant to NIPS, and the application to supervised PageRank is timely. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and polishing the presentation would make this a clear accept.
