This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which incorporate node and edge features into the ranking process. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of traditional PageRank and its variants, which rely solely on graph structure. They aim to develop optimization methods with theoretical convergence guarantees for learning the parameters of these feature-enriched PageRank models, a challenge not fully addressed by prior work [21]. The proposed approach combines an existing method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with adaptations of gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques.

The paper presents a two-level optimization framework. The lower level focuses on efficiently approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, leveraging a method from [17] and analyzing its accuracy and complexity. The upper level employs these approximations within a gradient-based or gradient-free optimization loop to learn the model parameters. The authors provide convergence rate analyses for both methods, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches. They also investigate the trade-off between accuracy in the lower-level approximation and the overall computational complexity of the algorithm. Finally, the paper includes experimental results on a web page ranking task, demonstrating improved performance compared to a state-of-the-art method [21].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** A major strength of this work is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods. This is a significant contribution, as existing approaches often lack such assurances.
*   **Inexact Oracle Framework:** The authors effectively utilize the concept of an inexact oracle, which is practical for large-scale problems where exact computations are intractable.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and trade-offs involved in the proposed algorithms.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on a real-world dataset.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of learning Supervised PageRank and its challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Polish:** The writing contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, which hinders readability. While the technical content is sound, the presentation could be significantly improved.
*   **Limited Empirical Detail:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail. The paper states that the methods outperform the state-of-the-art, but fails to provide comprehensive tables or figures showcasing the extent of the improvement. Key experimental parameters (e.g., the choice of  ϕ₀ and R) are not fully explained. 
*   **Dataset Diversity:** It is unclear whether the algorithms were tested on a diverse set of graphs. The experiments focus on a single dataset, which limits the generalizability of the results.
*   **Impactful Problem?:** While the paper tackles a well-defined problem, its practical significance isn’t fully conveyed. The authors should better articulate how improvements in Supervised PageRank translate to tangible benefits in real-world applications.
*   **Citation Accuracy:** There appears to be a discrepancy in the citation of [21] relating to Supervised PageRank.
*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The assumption of constraining the parameter space Φ to an R-ball around ϕ₀ could limit the algorithm’s applicability and should be discussed further.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound approach with novel theoretical contributions.
*   The convergence guarantees provide valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithms.
*   The experimental results, despite limited detail, demonstrate promising performance.
*   The work addresses a relevant problem in the field of graph ranking.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The writing quality needs significant improvement.
*   The lack of detailed empirical results hinders a full assessment of the algorithms' performance.
*   The limited dataset diversity raises concerns about generalizability.
*   The practical impact of the work is not clearly articulated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of graph ranking, particularly due to its theoretical rigor and convergence guarantees. However, the writing quality and limited empirical detail detract from its overall impact. While the paper is technically sound, it requires significant polishing and expansion of the experimental section to meet the standards of NIPS. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **conditional acceptance** with major revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, focusing on improving the clarity of the writing, providing more detailed experimental results, and justifying the assumptions made in their analysis. Specifically, the paper should include tables and figures presenting the experimental results, a discussion of the parameter selection process, and an analysis of the sensitivity of the algorithms to different parameter settings. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to the NIPS community.
