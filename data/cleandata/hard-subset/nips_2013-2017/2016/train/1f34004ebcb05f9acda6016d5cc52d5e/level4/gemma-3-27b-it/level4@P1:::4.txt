This paper proposes gradient-based and gradient-free methods for solving a non-convex loss-minimization problem in the context of learning Supervised PageRank models. The authors address a challenging problem where existing methods lack convergence guarantees. They propose two two-level methods, one gradient-based and one gradient-free, building upon the concept of an inexact oracle. A key contribution is providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both algorithms, a feature missing in prior work like [21]. The paper also includes experimental validation on a web page ranking task, demonstrating improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art.

The core idea of learning Supervised PageRank, as introduced in [21], involves incorporating node and edge features into the ranking process by adjusting restart and transition probabilities. The existing optimization approach [21] and the methods presented here share a two-level structure: a lower level estimates the loss function value (or its derivatives), and an upper level uses these estimations to update the model parameters. The authors highlight that the state-of-the-art gradient-based method [21] lacks convergence proofs due to the imprecise values obtained from the power method used for estimating the stationary distribution. The proposed methods aim to address this limitation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The provision of theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant strength. This addresses a key limitation of the existing state-of-the-art [21].
*   **Inexact Oracle:** The use of an inexact oracle is a practical approach for this problem, where exact calculations are often intractable. The analysis of the accuracy needed for convergence is valuable.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper presents a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the accuracy of the lower-level algorithm, its computational complexity, and the overall performance of the two-level approach.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in ranking quality, providing empirical support for the theoretical results.
*   **Detailed Technical Contributions:** The paper contains a number of detailed technical contributions relating to convergence analysis for both gradient and gradient-free methods within the context of inexact oracles and constrained optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and mathematically involved. While the depth is commendable, it may make the material less accessible to readers not intimately familiar with the field.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** It's not entirely clear how sensitive the performance of the methods is to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., step size, accuracy parameters). More discussion on parameter tuning and robustness would be helpful.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with [21] is important, it would be beneficial to compare against other relevant ranking algorithms, even if they don’t explicitly utilize a Supervised PageRank framework.
*    **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are conducted on a specific dataset from Yandex. It would be useful to evaluate the methods on other datasets to assess their generalizability.
*   **Redundancy:** Some sections contain substantial overlap in the presentation of ideas. For example, the introduction to the model description in Section 2 feels repetitive.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem with a clear gap in the existing literature (lack of convergence guarantees).
*   The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art.
*   The use of inexact oracles is a pragmatic approach for large-scale problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including comparisons to a wider range of algorithms and datasets.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The provision of convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a valuable addition to the literature on Supervised PageRank. While the paper could benefit from increased clarity and more comprehensive experiments, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should consider streamlining the presentation, providing more guidance on parameter tuning, and exploring the generalizability of the methods to other datasets. It would be useful to explicitly state the computational time of the proposed algorithms.
