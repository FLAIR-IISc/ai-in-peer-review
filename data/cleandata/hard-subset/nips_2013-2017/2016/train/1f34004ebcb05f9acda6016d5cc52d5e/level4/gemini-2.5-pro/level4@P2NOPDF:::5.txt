This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a personalized PageRank model where node and edge weights are derived from feature vectors. The authors correctly point out that a prior method for this problem lacks performance guarantees. To address this, they propose a new algorithm that combines a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of this approach, including performance guarantees under a set of technical assumptions.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical contribution. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of their proposed gradient-free optimization algorithm, establishing performance bounds under specific assumptions (e.g., Lemmas 3.3, 3.4 and Theorem 4.2). The approach of combining stationary distribution approximation with a biased gradient-free oracle to solve this learning problem is technically interesting and appears to be sound.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

Despite the theoretical promise, the paper has significant weaknesses in its presentation, empirical evaluation, and framing that prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

1.  **Clarity, Presentation, and Scholarship:** The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors that hinder readability. The scholarship also appears to be lacking in certain areas. For instance, the paper refers to the problem as "Supervised PageRank" and cites [21], but that work seems to focus on semi-supervised PageRank. More importantly, the proposed framework appears to be a direct reproduction of the one in [21], simply presented with different notation. This lack of clear attribution to prior work and differentiation of the novel contributions is a serious concern.

2.  **Over-reliance on the Appendix:** A critical flaw of this paper is that nearly all substantive content—both theoretical proofs and empirical results—is relegated to the appendix. The main body of the paper omits proof outlines, key derivations, and any concrete data from the experiments. A conference paper must be a self-contained unit that provides sufficient evidence to support its claims. As it stands, the main text makes assertions but fails to provide the evidence, making it extremely difficult for a reviewer to assess the validity and significance of the contributions.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is currently too brief to be convincing.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** The authors state that experiments were run on a web graph, but they do not report any specific results, tables, or figures in the main paper. Key experimental parameters, such as how the initial parameter vector `phi0` and the search radius `R` were chosen, are not mentioned. This information is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the results.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper claims that the proposed algorithm "outperformed other algorithms," but this claim is unsubstantiated. There is no discussion of the margin of improvement, the specific metrics used for comparison, or the practical implications of this outperformance.
    *   **Limited Scope:** It is unclear whether the method was evaluated on more than one graph. Testing on a single dataset is insufficient to demonstrate the generalizability and robustness of the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis rests on several strong technical assumptions. While necessary for the proofs, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of whether these assumptions are likely to hold for real-world empirical data. Some assumptions seem particularly limiting; for example, constraining the search space of `phi` to an R-ball around a chosen `phi0` could greatly restrict the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm. The potential impact of this constraint is not discussed.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please include tables in the main paper that report the specific performance metrics of your algorithm against the baselines?
2.  How did you select the initial parameter vector `phi0` and the radius `R` for your experiments? How sensitive is the algorithm's performance to these choices?
3.  Were experiments conducted on a diverse set of graphs beyond the single one mentioned? If not, claims about the algorithm's general effectiveness are premature.
4.  Can you provide justification or empirical evidence to suggest that your key technical assumptions hold on common real-world graph datasets?
5.  Could you clarify the relationship between your framework and that of [21] and more precisely state your novel contributions?

In summary, while the theoretical ideas presented in the paper may be powerful, the paper fails to communicate them effectively and does not provide the necessary empirical evidence to support its claims. The core content is almost entirely in the appendix, the experimental section lacks crucial details, and the practical relevance of the theoretical assumptions is not discussed.
