## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - Please Insert]

This paper presents two novel optimization methods – a gradient-based (GBN) and a gradient-free (GFN) approach – for solving the supervised PageRank problem. A key strength of this work lies in its theoretical guarantees: the GBN method offers a provable convergence rate to a specified accuracy, while the GFN method guarantees a decrease in the loss function with each iteration. Importantly, both methods avoid the computational burden of requiring exact objective function values, and provide estimates of their convergence rates. Experimental results demonstrate superior ranking quality compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.

The authors build upon the foundational work of Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref:17) to formulate a two-level optimization process. By circumventing the need to compute large matrices, the proposed methods offer a significant practical advantage. The paper provides a detailed explanation of both methods, with proofs relegated to the supplementary material – a reasonable approach given the technical depth. The inclusion of hyperparameter settings enhances reproducibility. While the datasets used are not publicly available, the authors offer a clear description, suggesting reasonable feasibility for replication.

However, the paper suffers from a significant issue regarding notational clarity. The numerous symbols used throughout the equations, particularly in Sections 2 through 5, make the derivations difficult to follow. A symbol table would substantially improve readability and comprehension. For instance, the variable *N* is introduced in Line 139 and Equation 3.1 without explicit definition; its meaning (number of steps) is not clarified until Line 193. This lack of immediate definition creates unnecessary cognitive load for the reader and hinders understanding.

The experimental evaluation also presents areas for improvement. The authors primarily compare their methods to GBP solely based on the loss function, omitting a direct comparison of ranking quality – the ultimate objective of PageRank. A more comprehensive evaluation should include metrics directly assessing ranking performance. Furthermore, given that this work generalizes reference [17], a direct comparison with the original method would provide valuable insight into the advancements achieved. Demonstrating performance gains over its predecessor would strongly reinforce the contribution of this paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** Provable convergence rates and loss function decrease offer a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Avoiding the computation of large matrices is a significant practical benefit.
*   **Clear Method Descriptions:** The paper details the proposed methods and includes comprehensive proofs in the supplementary material.
*   **Hyperparameter Transparency:** Providing hyperparameter settings promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Clutter:** Excessive use of symbols without clear, immediate definition significantly hinders readability. A symbol table is strongly recommended.
*   **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:**  Comparison limited to loss function, lacking a direct assessment of ranking quality.
*   **Missing Baseline:** Absence of a direct comparison to the method generalized (ref:17).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of supervised PageRank optimization. The theoretical guarantees and potential for computational efficiency are substantial. However, the lack of notational clarity and the limited experimental evaluation detract from the overall impact. Addressing these weaknesses, particularly by adding a symbol table and expanding the experimental results, would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions. The core ideas are solid and promising, but the issues outlined above must be addressed before publication. Specifically, the authors *must* include a symbol table. The experimental section should be expanded to include ranking quality metrics and a comparison to ref:17.
