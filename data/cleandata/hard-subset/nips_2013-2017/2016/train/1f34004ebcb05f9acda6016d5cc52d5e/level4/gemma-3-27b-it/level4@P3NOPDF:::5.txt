This paper introduces a framework for learning node and edge weights in a PageRank-like model, motivated by the limitations of existing algorithms which lack performance guarantees. The authors address this by combining a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with a gradient-free optimization technique suitable for loss functions that can only be estimated. While the theoretical development is substantial, the paper suffers from presentation issues and a lack of compelling empirical validation within the main body.

The authors correctly identify the challenge of parameter learning in weighted PageRank models. Their proposed approach builds upon existing work, specifically reproducing (under a different notation) a framework presented in [21], which they refer to as “Supervised PageRank.” However, this attribution is inaccurate; [21] describes semi-supervised PageRank, raising concerns about the authors' familiarity with related literature. The paper thoroughly discusses the technical assumptions underlying their method and defines a loss function, clearly articulating the difficulties inherent in solving the learning problem. Several lemmas are presented to bound the approximate gradient, and a general theorem concerning the performance of the utilized gradient-free method is stated. 

The subsequent sections detail the adaptation of the gradient-free method to their specific learning problem, accompanied by further bounds. However, the experimental evaluation, presented primarily in the appendix, is insufficient. Results are shown on a single web graph used in prior work, but critical details – including the specific parameter settings – are omitted. While the authors claim their algorithm outperforms existing methods, the extent and statistical significance of this improvement are not discussed. It remains unclear whether the observed performance generalizes to a wider range of graph structures. The limited presentation of results in the main body, with crucial details relegated to the appendix, hinders a clear understanding of the practical impact of this work.

My primary concern is the substantial reliance on the appendix for essential content. The paper lacks both proof sketches for the theoretical results and a comprehensive presentation of the empirical findings. Specifically, the choices of  φ₀ and R in the experimental setup are not explained, and it is not stated whether the algorithm was tested on more than one graph. The absence of tables or specific results within the main text makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed method.  Furthermore, the authors do not sufficiently address the practical consequences of their theoretical improvements. 

Several technical assumptions also warrant further scrutiny. The restriction of the search space for φ to an R-ball around φ₀, while potentially simplifying the optimization problem, may significantly limit the algorithm’s efficacy and applicability. This constraint should be explicitly discussed and its potential impact analyzed.  While the theoretical bounds presented are strong, I have reservations about the validity of the stated assumptions when applied to real-world data.

Ultimately, despite the potentially powerful theoretical results and the algorithm itself, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate its practical benefits. The significant amount of core material confined to the appendix, the incomplete reporting of experimental details, and the questionable attribution of related work raise substantial concerns. The impact of the solved problem remains unpersuasive, and the limited empirical validation does not inspire confidence in the method’s generalizability.
