This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model, defined by a random walk conducted by weights of nodes and edges, which are in turn defined by random walks defined by nodes' and edge's features. The optimization problem cannot be solved by existing optimization methods, which require exact values of the objective function. The proposed approach operates in two levels: at the first level, a linearly convergent method is used to estimate an approximation to the stationary distribution of Markov random walk, and at the second level, a gradient method is developed for general constrained non-convex optimization problems using an inexact oracle. The authors prove the convergence of this method and exploit it on the second level of the proposed algorithm.

The paper relates to previous work on PageRank, HITS, and their variants, as well as supervised ranking frameworks. The authors' approach builds upon existing methods, including the power method and Nesterov's method for computing the stationary distribution of Markov chains. However, the authors' contribution lies in adapting these methods to the case of constrained optimization problems when the value of the function can be calculated with some known precision.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs seem to be correct. However, the main optimization points made here are adaptations of those proposed in previous work, such as "Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015, pp. 1–40". Additionally, the supervised PageRank algorithm has been proposed previously.

The strengths of the paper include its clear presentation of the optimization problem and the proposed solution. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the convergence of their method and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments. However, the paper could benefit from a more general presentation of the optimization problem, showing the studied algorithm as a specific case. This would allow for a better placement of the contribution with respect to the state-of-the-art.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research.

The originality of the paper lies in its adaptation of existing methods to the case of constrained optimization problems with inexact oracles. However, the paper builds upon previous work, and the authors could have done a better job of highlighting the novelty of their contribution.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of ranking algorithms in web search and other applications. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments, and the results are promising.

In conclusion, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of optimization and ranking algorithms. However, the authors could have done a better job of highlighting the novelty of their contribution and placing it within the broader context of the state-of-the-art.
