This paper introduces two two-level optimization methods, a gradient-based (GBN) and a gradient-free (GFN) approach, to solve the supervised PageRank problem. Unlike existing methods, the proposed approaches do not require the exact value of the objective function. The gradient-based method is presented with a theoretical convergence rate guarantee to achieve a specified accuracy, while the gradient-free method ensures a guaranteed decrease in the loss function. The authors demonstrate that their methods achieve state-of-the-art performance in terms of ranking quality.

The core technical contribution lies in the development of two-level optimization methods, based on the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17], that can provably converge without needing to compute the exact objective function. This allows the methods to avoid computationally expensive operations, such as large matrix inversions, while still achieving strong results. The theoretical details are thoroughly explained from Section 2 to Section 5, with proofs provided in the supplementary material. The authors also provide the hyperparameters used, and while the datasets are not public, their clear descriptions should facilitate the reproduction of the results.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed:

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper's readability would be significantly improved by the inclusion of a symbol table. There are many symbols used throughout the equations, making it difficult for the reader to keep track of them. For example, the symbol $N$ is used in Line 139 and Equation 3.1, but its meaning (number of steps) is not defined until Line 193. Clarifying notation upfront would make the technical sections much easier to follow.

2.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
    *   In the experiments, the authors only compare their work with the GBP method with respect to the loss function, but not in terms of ranking quality. A comparison on this key metric is necessary for a complete evaluation.
    *   Since this work is presented as a generalization of the method in reference [17], a direct experimental comparison against that baseline would be valuable to empirically demonstrate the performance benefits of the proposed generalization.
