This paper introduces two novel two-level optimization methods, one gradient-based (GBN) and one gradient-free (GFN), to address the supervised PageRank problem. The core contribution lies in proposing algorithms that possess theoretical convergence guarantees and can achieve a pre-specified accuracy. A key advantage of these methods is that they do not require the exact value of the objective function, thereby avoiding the computationally prohibitive task of inverting a large matrix. The authors report that their methods outperform state-of-the-art approaches in terms of ranking quality. The framework is presented as a generalization of the work by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide convergence rate guarantees for their proposed methods, a crucial aspect often missing in heuristic-based approaches. The ability to attain a given accuracy without needing the exact objective function value is a significant and practical contribution.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed methods are designed to be scalable. By circumventing the need to explicitly compute the inverse of a large matrix, the algorithms are well-suited for the large-scale datasets typical of ranking problems.

3.  **Performance:** The experimental results suggest that the proposed GBN and GFN methods achieve superior ranking quality compared to existing state-of-the-art techniques.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors provide the hyperparameters used in their experiments. While the datasets are not publicly available, their descriptions are sufficiently clear to facilitate the reproduction of the results by other researchers.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper's clarity could be significantly improved, particularly concerning the mathematical notation. The text is dense with symbols, which can make it difficult for the reader to follow the derivations and the overall logic.
    *   **Suggestion:** I strongly recommend that the authors include a symbol table or a dedicated notation section at the beginning of the technical exposition. This would serve as a valuable reference for the reader.
    *   **Example:** The symbol 'N' is used in Line 139 and Equation 3.1 but is not defined as the number of steps until Line 193. Defining symbols immediately upon their first use is crucial for readability and would greatly enhance the quality of the paper.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section, while showing promising results, could be strengthened in two key areas.
    *   **Suggestion:** The comparison with the GBP method is currently limited to the loss function value. To provide a more complete and compelling evaluation, this comparison should be extended to include the primary metric of interest: ranking quality. This would allow for a direct assessment of the practical impact of the optimization performance.
    *   **Suggestion:** Given that this work is explicitly framed as a generalization of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17], a direct empirical comparison with the method from [17] would be highly valuable. Such a comparison would clearly demonstrate the practical benefits and performance gains achieved by the proposed generalization.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking with well-motivated and theoretically-grounded optimization methods. The main strengths are the convergence guarantees and computational efficiency. The primary weaknesses lie in the clarity of the presentation and the scope of the experimental comparisons. Addressing the suggestions above, particularly regarding the inclusion of a symbol table and the expansion of the experimental evaluation, would substantially strengthen the paper.
