This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk, where the weights of nodes and edges are determined by their respective features. A key challenge is that the objective function cannot be computed exactly, which precludes the use of standard optimization methods that require precise function values.

To address this, the authors propose a two-level approach. At the first level, a linearly convergent method is used to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The authors show that this allows the loss function to be approximated with any given precision. At the second level, they develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems that operates with an inexact oracle, and they prove its convergence to a stationary point. The primary contribution lies in adapting this optimization framework to the case of constrained problems where the function value is only available up to a known accuracy. The authors prove the convergence of this method and utilize it in the second level of their proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the theoretical analysis appears to be sound, although I did not verify every proof in detail. My main concern, however, relates to the novelty of the contributions. The core optimization techniques appear to be adaptations of those proposed by Nesterov and Spokoiny ("Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions," Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015), and the supervised PageRank algorithm has also been proposed previously. The paper should more clearly delineate its novel contributions from these existing foundations.

On a minor note, the paper would benefit from a conclusion section, for which there appears to be sufficient space. Furthermore, I would suggest presenting the optimization problem in a more general context, positioning the studied algorithm as a specific instance of a broader class of problems. This reframing would help to better contextualize the paper's contribution with respect to the state-of-the-art.
