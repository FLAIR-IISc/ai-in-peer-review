This paper introduces two novel optimization algorithms, a gradient-based (GBN) and a gradient-free (GFN) method, for addressing the supervised PageRank problem. Distinguishing themselves from existing approaches, the proposed gradient-based method comes with a theoretical guarantee on its convergence rate and is capable of reaching a pre-specified accuracy. Similarly, the gradient-free method ensures a quantifiable decrease in the loss function. A significant advantage of both algorithms is that they operate without needing the exact value of the objective function and possess proven estimates for their convergence rates. Empirically, the methods demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art techniques in terms of ranking quality.

The authors have provided the necessary hyperparameters for both methods. While the datasets are not publicly accessible, their descriptions are sufficiently detailed to likely permit the reproduction of the results. The core of the contribution lies in two two-level optimization methods built upon the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref:17). This framework allows the methods to circumvent the computationally expensive task of forming and manipulating a large matrix while achieving strong performance.

The theoretical underpinnings and methodological details are elaborated from Section 2 to Section 5, with complete proofs available in the supplementary material. However, the paper's clarity could be significantly enhanced by the inclusion of a symbol table. The high density of notation makes it challenging for the reader to follow the mathematical derivations. A specific example is the symbol $N$, which appears in Line 139 and Equation 3.1, but its meaning (the number of steps) is not clarified until Line 193.

Regarding the experimental evaluation, the comparison against the GBP baseline is limited to the loss function. A more thorough analysis would also compare the final ranking quality achieved by the methods. Moreover, since the proposed work is framed as a generalization of reference [17], a direct empirical comparison with this key baseline would be valuable to definitively demonstrate the performance benefits of the new approach.
