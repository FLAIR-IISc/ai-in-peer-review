- Paper studies learning parameters of a supervised PageRank model with feature-dependent restart/transition probabilities using a nonconvex pairwise square loss over query graphs.
- Authors propose two two-level methods: an inexact first-order (gradient-based) algorithm and an inexact zero-order (random gradient-free) algorithm.
- Lower-level solves stationary distributions and derivatives with the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent scheme, argued superior to power methods.
- Upper-level develops an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle.
- Upper-level also develops a constrained random gradient-free method using a biased spherical finite-difference oracle handling inexact function values.
- Theoretical contributions include inexact zero- and first-order oracle formulations, convergence-rate guarantees, and complexity bounds tracking graph size and feature sparsity.
- Authors quantify trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall complexity, with complexity expressions in graph and feature dimensions.
- Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing dataset (~600 queries, ~11.7K pages, ~7.5K edges, ~1.7K labeled pairs) compare GFN, GBN, and a gradient-based baseline (GBP).
- Reported empirical claim: both proposed methods outperform the baseline in ranking quality, with GFN performing best.
- Strength: clear theoretical framing and explicit control of approximation errors for π and its derivatives.
- Strength: practical motivation and two-level perspective relevant to ranking applications with query-dependent graphs.
- Weakness: many grammatical errors, inconsistent notation, and incomplete/sloppy citations harming readability and verification.
- Weakness: strong or insufficiently justified assumptions (local convexity, interior solution, ℓ2-ball constraints) with unclear practical implications.
- Weakness: experimental reporting lacks key details (choice of ϕ̂ and R, α, stopping criteria, hyperparameter sensitivity, runtime/iterations, standard ranking metrics) and omits main-text tables/figures.
- Weakness: limited baselines and datasets; missing comparisons to supervised random walks and stronger first-order optimizers.
- Weakness: reproducibility is weak due to missing methodological and bibliographic details.
- Quality: theoretical results appear technically sound but rely heavily on supplement; main-text proof sketches are needed.
- Quality: empirical section underdeveloped—no clear effect sizes, variance/statistical significance details, or runtime comparisons.
- Clarity recommendation: fix language/notation, define assumptions where used, add intuition for key lemmas, and move key results/tables into main text.
- Originality: extending gradient-free methods to constrained inexact settings and adapting inexact first-order composite methods to nonconvex constraints are novel and useful.
- Significance: potentially valuable for large-scale training of feature-augmented random-walk models, but current experiments do not convincingly demonstrate practical impact.
- Requested clarifications: justify Φ = {ϕ: ||ϕ−ϕ̂||2 ≤ R} and sensitivity to ϕ̂/R; discuss local convexity assumption size; report NDCG@k means/SEs/effect sizes and runtimes; compare additional baselines; provide a main-text proof sketch; fix bibliography and reconcile related work.
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical contributions, clear two-level approach, potential applicability.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient empirical evidence, presentation/citation issues, and strong assumptions not validated.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject pending substantial clarity, experimental, and reproducibility improvements.
