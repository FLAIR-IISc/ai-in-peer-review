Summary
The paper studies learning feature-based node and edge weights for a PageRank-style random walk. The authors combine (i) an existing approximation of the stationary distribution for a feature-parameterized Markov chain with (ii) a gradient-free (zeroth-order) optimization method suitable when the loss is only accessible via noisy, biased estimates. They formalize the learning problem, state assumptions, derive bounds for an approximate gradient oracle, present a general biased zeroth-order optimization procedure with a convergence theorem, and then instantiate it for the PageRank setting. Experiments are reported on a web graph used in prior work, with fuller results in the appendix.

Strengths
- Ambition to provide performance guarantees for a setting where prior learning approaches reportedly lacked them.
- Clear separation between a generic biased zeroth-order framework and its instantiation to PageRank learning.
- Nontrivial theoretical development: bounds for an approximate gradient oracle and convergence guarantees under bias.
- Topic relevance: learning parameters for PageRank variants remains of interest in ranking and graph mining.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty/positioning: The framework appears to reproduce, under different terminology and notation, prior work (cited as [21]). The paper calls the problem “Supervised PageRank,” whereas [21] discusses “semi-supervised PageRank.” The relationship and genuine differences (algorithmic, theoretical, or empirical) are not clearly articulated. Renaming without clarifying incremental contributions raises concerns.
- Empirical evidence: The main text contains only a brief experimental description; core results, tables, and analyses are deferred to the appendix. The paper claims the proposed method outperforms baselines, but effect sizes, statistical significance, and practical implications are not discussed. It is unclear whether evaluation spans multiple and diverse graphs; using a single web graph limits external validity.
- Missing details: Key parameters and choices are not reported in the main body (e.g., selection of φ0 and radius R for the feasible set, smoothing parameters for the zeroth-order method, iteration budgets, stopping criteria). Without these, reproducibility and interpretation suffer.
- Assumptions and practicality: While the bounds are strong, several technical assumptions seem restrictive or may fail in practice. In particular:
  - Restricting φ to an R-ball around φ0 may unduly constrain the solution and hides the complexity of model selection (how φ0 and R are chosen).
  - Assumptions required for the biased gradient oracle (e.g., Lipschitzness/smoothness of the loss in φ, bounded approximation error in stationary distribution estimates, mixing properties of the Markov chain) are not empirically justified.
  - The cumulative impact of bias from stationary-distribution approximation and from gradient-free smoothing is not quantified in realistic regimes.
- Clarity and presentation: The writing contains grammatical errors and typographical issues, and citations are inconsistent. The terminology (“Supervised PageRank” vs what is actually cited) is confusing. Important proof ideas and experimental results should not be relegated entirely to the appendix.

Requests for clarification
- Precisely delineate what is new relative to [21] and other prior art: Is it the oracle model, tighter rates, broader losses, new approximation schemes, or improved empirical performance? Provide a side-by-side comparison of assumptions and guarantees.
- Detail the construction and analysis of the biased gradient oracle: What are the bias and variance as functions of the smoothing radius, sample size, and approximation accuracy of the stationary distribution? How are these controlled in practice?
- Report how φ0 and R were chosen in experiments. Was there validation, cross-validation, or problem-specific heuristics? Provide sensitivity analyses to these choices.
- Describe datasets: How many graphs, their sizes/topologies, and task definitions? Are there train/validation/test splits? What metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) and how are ground-truth labels obtained?
- Provide computational costs: wall-clock time, number of oracle evaluations, convergence curves, and comparisons to gradient-based or heuristic baselines.
- Do the stated assumptions (smoothness, bounded features/weights, mixing rates) hold approximately on your datasets? Any empirical checks?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Positioning and credit: Use consistent terminology with prior work, and clearly state the paper’s novel contributions. If the framework is largely from [21], emphasize what is substantively different (e.g., new guarantees under bias, broader class of losses, improved algorithms).
- Theory presentation: Move at least one central theorem (with a proof sketch) to the main text. Explicitly map generic assumptions to the PageRank instantiation, showing how each is verified and how constants translate.
- Empirical section in the main body: Include a concise but complete set of results (tables/figures) with:
  - Multiple graphs (web, citation, social, synthetic with known properties).
  - Effect sizes with error bars over multiple runs.
  - Ablations on approximation accuracy vs performance, and sensitivity to φ0, R, smoothing radius, and sample counts.
  - Runtime and scalability comparisons.
- Practical impact: Discuss when the method should be preferred over baseline learning approaches (e.g., when gradients are unavailable/expensive), and quantify the performance–compute trade-offs.
- Reproducibility: Provide all hyperparameters, initialization strategies, stopping criteria, and data preprocessing steps; consider releasing code or pseudocode detailed enough to implement.
- Writing and citations: Proofread for grammar and typographical errors; ensure consistent use of PageRank capitalization; fix the “Supervised vs semi-supervised” discrepancy; and tidy references.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a relevant problem and offers theoretically interesting results for biased zeroth-order learning in a PageRank setting. However, the current submission lacks clear novelty relative to prior work, defers essential empirical evidence to the appendix, and relies on strong assumptions whose practical validity is not demonstrated. Substantial revisions to clarify contributions, strengthen and surface empirical results, justify assumptions, and improve presentation are necessary before the work can be considered competitive.
