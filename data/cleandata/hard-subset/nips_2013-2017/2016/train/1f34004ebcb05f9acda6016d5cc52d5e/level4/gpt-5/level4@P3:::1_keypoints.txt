- Tackles supervised PageRank with linear feature-parametrized transition/restart probabilities using a two-level optimization with inexact stationary-distribution and derivative oracles.  
- Novelty: integrates inexact-oracle techniques into both zero-order and first-order methods and analyzes the inner-approximation vs outer-complexity trade-off.  
- Builds on prior supervised PageRank and random-walk learning work, replacing the power method with a linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme for stationary distribution approximation.  
- Theoretical contribution: defines precise inexact zero-/first-order oracles and provides convergence rates for a gradient-free method (convex-in-ball) and an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite objectives.  
- Analysis links inner tolerances (δ), smoothing (τ), and target accuracy (ε) to overall computational complexity bounds.  
- Extends controlled-accuracy derivative computation, adapting prior results to obtain provable gradient approximations.  
- Algorithmic clarity: clear two-level design with proofs tying inner iteration counts (N, N1, N2) to outer performance and an explicit discussion of precision vs cost.  
- Experiments on Yandex user-browsing graphs (600 queries) show GFN and GBN reduce training loss more than the prior GBP baseline and converge in fewer outer iterations.  
- Experimental limitations: evaluation reports only surrogate training loss; missing standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP), wall-clock/runtime, memory, scalability, and stronger learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART).  
- Clarity issues: heavy notation, some typos, and needs clearer assumptions on positivity/normalization, explicit definitions of constants (β1), and algorithm boxes with inputs/outputs.  
- Originality is incremental but meaningful: adapts known optimization frameworks to constrained, inexact-oracle settings and provides a practical two-level application with provable rates.  
- Significance: provides valuable convergence guarantees and complexity analysis for supervised random-walk learning, with broader applicability beyond the specific task.  
- Strengths: principled inexact-oracle control, convergence guarantees where prior work lacked theory, improved stationary-distribution/derivative approximations, and empirical gains with significance tests.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: justify or relax the convexity-in-Φ assumption for GFN; report IR metrics on held-out queries; include broader baselines; provide runtime/memory/scalability and sensitivity analyses; improve presentation and motivations.  
- Key questions for authors: how often is f convex within Φ in practice; do f reductions translate to NDCG/MAP gains; wall-clock times and scaling with graph/features; sensitivity to α and inner iteration counts?  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theoretical gap-filling, useful inexact-oracle adaptations, and empirical improvements over prior supervised PageRank methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, insufficient empirical evaluation (metrics, baselines, runtime), and unverified/local-convexity assumptions.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — strong theory and relevant contributions, but experimental evaluation needs to be substantially strengthened.
