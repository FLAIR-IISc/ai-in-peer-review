- Paper extends PageRank to learn node/edge weights from features and motivates the Supervised PageRank setup.
- Notes prior training lacked convergence guarantees and proposes a fix combining a linearly convergent stationary-distribution estimator with a gradient-free optimizer for inexact function values.
- Much of the Supervised PageRank formulation is restated with altered notation relative to prior work [21].
- The authors state technical assumptions and use a squared pairwise loss, arguing standard optimizers struggle on this problem.
- Lemmas are given that bound error in approximate gradients.
- A random gradient-free method based on a biased sphere-smoothing oracle is presented along with a performance theorem.
- Additional assumptions are introduced to tailor the method to the learning problem, yielding an algorithm and complexity bounds.
- Experimental section is sketchy: main results and detailed analyses are deferred to the appendix.
- Several experimental parameters are not reported, undermining reproducibility and interpretation.
- The claim that the new method outperforms alternatives is not supported by detailed metrics or discussion of practical impact.
- It is unclear whether experiments cover diverse graphs or only a single dataset used in prior work.
- Writing has numerous grammatical issues and citation inconsistencies (e.g., referring to “Supervised PageRank” but citing a semi-supervised source).
- Reviewer is unconvinced the problem addressed is impactful in practice.
- Concerns that several technical assumptions may not hold empirically or are overly restrictive (e.g., restricting search to an R-ball around phi0).
- Primary substantive content (proofs, empirical results) is placed in the appendix rather than the main text.
- Key experimental design choices are unexplained: how phi0 and R were selected, and whether tests were run on multiple graphs.
- No tables or specific numerical outcomes are reported in the main body.
- Overall, while analysis and bounds appear strong, the presentation and empirical validation do not convincingly demonstrate practical benefit.
