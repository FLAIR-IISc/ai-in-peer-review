

This paper introduces two novel two-level optimization methods—gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN)—for solving the supervised PageRank problem. The key contributions are a gradient-based approach with a theoretically guaranteed convergence rate for achieving specified accuracy, and a gradient-free method that ensures monotonic decrease in the loss function. Crucially, both methods avoid requiring exact evaluations of the objective function, instead relying on estimates that enable efficient computation without explicit large-matrix operations. The authors demonstrate superior ranking quality compared to state-of-the-art methods, with hyperparameters clearly specified. While the datasets are not publicly available, the experimental descriptions are sufficiently detailed to facilitate replication.

The theoretical foundation builds on Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work (Ref. 17), with the lower- and upper-level optimizations structured accordingly. The convergence rate analysis is provided with precise bounds for a given accuracy tolerance, which is a significant advantage over methods needing exact objective evaluations. The paper’s organization is logical, with comprehensive proofs included in the supplementary material (Sections 2–5). However, the heavy use of symbols across equations—such as the undefined $N$ in Line 139 and Equation 3.1 (referenced as "number of steps" only in Line 193)—creates unnecessary confusion. A dedicated symbol table would greatly enhance readability and reduce cognitive load for readers.

A critical weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The authors compare their methods solely against GBP based on loss function values, but fail to assess ranking quality—the primary metric for supervised PageRank. This omission undermines the claim of "superior performance," as ranking quality is the core objective in this context. Additionally, since the work generalizes Ref. 17, a direct comparison with this baseline is essential to validate the improvement. Without such a comparison, it remains unclear whether the proposed methods offer meaningful gains over the foundational approach.

The paper’s technical claims are well-supported, but the presentation could be strengthened. The convergence guarantees and computational efficiency are compelling, yet the experimental section’s focus on loss function rather than ranking quality misaligns with the problem’s goals. The absence of a symbol table also hinders the reader’s ability to follow the mathematical derivations. While the methodology is sound and the empirical results promising, these gaps prevent a fully convincing assessment of the work’s contribution. Addressing the symbol clarity and expanding the evaluation to include ranking quality (and a direct comparison with Ref. 17) would significantly bolster the paper’s impact.
