

This paper investigates the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning parameters in a graph-based ranking model. The model is defined via a Markov random walk, where node and edge weights (determined by node and edge features) govern the walk dynamics. Standard optimization methods are inapplicable here, as they require exact evaluations of the objective function. The authors propose a two-level approach to address this challenge. At the first level, they employ a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, demonstrating that the loss function value can be approximated to any desired precision. At the second level, they develop a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization using an inexact oracle—proving its convergence to a stationary point—and apply this to the main problem. The key contribution lies in adapting this inexact oracle framework to constrained settings where function evaluations are known to within a specified precision. The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear sound, though I have not verified all details.  

My primary concern is that the core optimization techniques closely adapt methods from Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), *Foundations of Computational Mathematics*, and the supervised PageRank algorithm itself has been previously proposed. The novelty of the approach thus appears limited to its application within this specific graph-ranking context, rather than in the underlying optimization methodology. Additionally, the paper lacks a formal conclusion despite ample space for one. I recommend framing the optimization problem within a broader theoretical framework to explicitly position the contribution relative to existing work. For instance, clarifying how this algorithm generalizes or extends prior inexact oracle methods would strengthen the context and highlight the specific advancement over Nesterov & Spokoiny’s work. This would better contextualize the technical contribution and address the perceived novelty gap.
