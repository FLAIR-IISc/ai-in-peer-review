- Paper proposes learning PageRank node/edge weights from features but largely reproduces the setup of [21] under different name/notation.  
- Authors combine a stationary-distribution approximation for Markov chains with an adapted gradient-free optimization for estimated-loss settings.  
- Paper introduces technical assumptions, a loss function, lemmas bounding the approximate gradient, a biased gradient-free oracle, and a performance theorem.  
- Additional assumptions produce a learning algorithm and further theoretical bounds; the theoretical development yields nontrivial results.  
- Empirical and expository components are weak: experiments are terse and defer most details to the appendix.  
- Key experimental parameters are not reported, and claims of outperforming baselines lack discussion of magnitude, consistency, or practical significance.  
- It is unclear whether experiments were run on more than one graph or whether the dataset suite is diverse enough to support general claims.  
- Writing has many grammatical issues and citations are sloppy; the paper mislabels prior work (calling it “Supervised PageRank” while citing a semi-supervised paper).  
- Renaming an existing framework without clearly delineating novelty and with inconsistent citation undermines the paper’s contribution.  
- The practical impact is doubtful because several technical assumptions may not hold on real data and some are overly restrictive.  
- Constraining the parameter vector phi to an R-ball around phi0 may severely limit applicability and needs explicit justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Most substantive material is relegated to the appendix; the main text lacks proof sketches and complete empirical findings, hindering assessment of contributions.  
- Reviewer requests the main paper include core lemmas/theorems with proof sketches and comprehensive experimental reporting (datasets, parameter choices, metrics, baselines, ablations).  
- Concrete unanswered questions: how phi0 and R were chosen and their sensitivity; which graphs were used and their characteristics; exact performance numbers and variability; and practical benefits of the theoretical improvements.  
- Needed fixes: strengthen exposition, clarify relationship to [21] and prior art, justify and test assumptions (especially the R-ball), and provide thorough, transparent experiments on diverse datasets.
