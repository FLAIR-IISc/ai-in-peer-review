This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods—a gradient-based (GBN) and a gradient-free (GFN) approach—for the supervised PageRank problem. The methods build upon the foundational work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17].

The primary contribution is a pair of algorithms with strong theoretical properties. Unlike many existing methods, the proposed GBN algorithm comes with a theoretical guarantee on its convergence rate to a specified accuracy, while the GFN algorithm is guaranteed to decrease the loss function. A significant practical advantage is that neither method requires the exact objective function value, thereby avoiding the costly computation of a large, dense matrix. The paper provides convergence rate estimates and presents experimental results showing that the proposed methods outperform state-of-the-art techniques in terms of ranking quality. Sections 2 through 5 detail the proposed methods, with all theoretical proofs provided in the supplementary material.

Despite the strong technical contributions, the paper has weaknesses in its presentation and experimental evaluation that should be addressed. First, the manuscript's readability suffers from dense notation. The authors introduce many symbols, making it difficult to follow the equations. For instance, the symbol $N$ appears in Line 139 and Equation 3.1, but its definition (number of steps) is not provided until Line 193. The inclusion of a symbol table would greatly improve clarity.

Second, the experimental comparison could be strengthened. The authors compare their work against the GBP baseline only in terms of the loss function, not the final ranking quality, which is a more meaningful metric for this problem. Furthermore, since the proposed framework is presented as a generalization of the work in [17], a direct empirical comparison with this foundational method is essential to demonstrate the practical benefits of the generalization.

On a positive note for reproducibility, the authors clearly list the hyper-parameters used in their experiments. Although the datasets are not publicly available, they are well-described, which should facilitate the reproduction of the results.
