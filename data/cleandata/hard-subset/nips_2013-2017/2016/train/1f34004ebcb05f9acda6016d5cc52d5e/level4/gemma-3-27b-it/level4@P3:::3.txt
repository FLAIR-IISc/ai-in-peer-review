This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing both gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) optimization methods. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in prior work, particularly the state-of-the-art approach [21]. The authors highlight that their methods operate effectively with inexact oracle information and demonstrate superior ranking quality in experiments. This work builds upon established techniques like the power method [18, 23, 24] and adapts existing optimization frameworks [6, 16, 17] to this specific problem. 

The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem, proposed solutions, and experimental validation. The mathematical derivations are extensive, and the authors diligently provide proofs in the supplementary materials. The experimental setup is reasonably detailed, employing a real-world web browsing dataset. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work and position their contributions within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant strength, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches.
*   **Inexact Oracle:** The ability to operate with inexact oracle information is practical and important, as calculating exact values can be computationally prohibitive.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper is comprehensive, covering the theoretical foundations, algorithmic details, and experimental validation in a detailed manner.
*   **Clear Problem Definition**: The paper clearly explains the Supervised PageRank problem and the challenges associated with its optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Symbol Table/Notation:** The paper uses a large number of symbols, making it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations without repeatedly referring back to definitions. A symbol table would significantly improve readability.  For example, the meaning of 'N' isn't immediately clear (Line 139, Eq. 3.1) and is only fully explained much later (Line 193).
*   **Limited Comparison in Experiments:** While the authors compare against the state-of-the-art [21] using the loss function, a direct comparison of ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) would provide a more convincing evaluation of performance.
*   **Missing Baseline:** Given the methods’ connection to Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work [17], a direct comparison to their original approach would strengthen the paper and clearly demonstrate the advancements made.
*   **Dataset Accessibility:** While the dataset is described, its unavailability hinders reproducibility. Providing access or detailed instructions for recreating the dataset would improve the paper's impact.
*   **Overly Detailed Sections:** Some sections, particularly those presenting lemmas and theorems, contain a high density of mathematical notation and could benefit from a more concise presentation, potentially moving some details to the supplementary material.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in ranking with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The provision of convergence guarantees is a significant contribution to the field.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The paper is well-written and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive ranking quality comparison weakens the empirical validation.
*   The absence of a comparison to the foundational work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] leaves a gap in the evaluation.
*   The dense notation and lack of a symbol table may hinder understanding.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, and the authors have demonstrated a solid understanding of the existing literature. While the paper could benefit from addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the comparison to related baselines and the clarity of notation, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised. Specifically, I recommend adding a section presenting ranking quality metrics, including a comparison to [17], and creating a symbol table to improve readability.
