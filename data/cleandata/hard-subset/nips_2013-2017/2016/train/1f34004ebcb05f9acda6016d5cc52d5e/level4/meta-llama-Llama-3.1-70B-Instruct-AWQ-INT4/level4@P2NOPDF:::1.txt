This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk conducted by weights of nodes and edges, which are in turn defined by random walks based on nodes' and edges' features. The optimization problem cannot be solved by existing optimization methods, which require exact values of the objective function. To address this, the proposed approach operates on two levels. 

At the first level, a linearly convergent method is employed to estimate an approximation to the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The authors validate this approach and demonstrate that the value of the loss function can be approximated with any given precision. Furthermore, they develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems using an inexact oracle and prove its convergence to the stationary point of the problem. The key contribution lies in adapting this approach to the case of constrained optimization problems, where the value of the function can be calculated with some known precision. The authors prove the convergence of this method and exploit it on the second level of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be correct, although a thorough examination of all proofs was not conducted. However, there are some concerns regarding the originality of the main optimization methods presented. These methods seem to be adaptations of those proposed by Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny in their 2015 paper "Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions" (Foundations of Computational Mathematics, pp. 1-40). Additionally, the supervised PageRank algorithm has been previously proposed. 

On a minor note, a conclusion section is expected, especially given the available space. It would be beneficial to present the optimization problem in a more general context, positioning the studied algorithm as a specific case. This would enable a clearer placement of the contribution within the state-of-the-art landscape, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the paper's significance.
