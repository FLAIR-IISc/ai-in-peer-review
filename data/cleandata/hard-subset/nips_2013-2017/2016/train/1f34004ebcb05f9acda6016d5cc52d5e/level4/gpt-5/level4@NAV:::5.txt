The paper motivates PageRank and introduces a framework where node and edge weights are derived from a feature-based model. The authors note that a prior algorithm for learning the model parameters lacks performance guarantees. Their approach combines an existing method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique designed for settings in which the loss function is only estimable. They effectively reintroduce the framework from [21], albeit under a different name and with different notation. They list several technical assumptions, define a loss function, and explain why the problem is challenging. Lemmas bounding the approximate gradient are provided. A general gradient-free method leveraging a biased gradient-free oracle is discussed, along with a theorem establishing its performance. With additional assumptions, they construct an algorithm for the learning problem, adapt the gradient-free methods to their setting, and state further bounds. They present a brief overview of experiments on a web graph used in related work, but detailed results and analyses are deferred to the appendix. Several parameters are not reported, and although the proposed algorithm is said to outperform alternatives, there is no discussion of the magnitude or practical significance of the improvement. It is also unclear whether the evaluation includes a diverse set of graphs. The writing contains numerous grammatical errors. Citations are imprecise: the problem is termed “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], but [21] appears to address only semi-supervised PageRank. I am not convinced that the solved problem is impactful. The bounds are strong, but I doubt that the stated technical assumptions will hold on empirical data. Some assumptions seem restrictive; for example, constraining the search space of phi to an R-ball around a chosen phi0 may substantially limit the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm and should be discussed. My primary concern is that most of the substantive content resides in the appendix: the main text includes neither proof sketches nor comprehensive empirical results. How were phi0 and R chosen in the experiments? Were multiple graphs evaluated? The main body reports no tables or specific quantitative outcomes. What are the practical consequences of the claimed improvements? While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be strong, the paper in its current form does not persuade me.
