- Paper addresses non-convex optimization for learning Supervised PageRank by providing a two-level framework with controllable inexact oracle for stationary distribution and derivatives.  
- Lower-level uses Nesterov & Nemirovski’s linearly convergent method to approximate stationary distribution and derivatives to a pre-specified accuracy.  
- Proposes two upper-level algorithms using the inexact oracle: a random gradient-free method for constrained problems with known function-value error and a gradient-based adaptive method for constrained non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle.  
- Provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees and complexity analyses linking lower-level accuracy to upper-level iterations (Theorems 2 and 4).  
- Empirical validation on large real-world web-ranking data shows both proposed methods (GFN, GBN) outperform prior state-of-the-art (GBP), with the gradient-free method performing best.  
- Main strength: first rigorous theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, filling a gap in prior work.  
- Methodological novelty: extends gradient-free and adaptive gradient frameworks to constrained problems with biased/inexact oracles, with broader applicability.  
- Strong empirical results on a large commercial dataset support practical effectiveness.  
- Problem is well-motivated: learning-to-rank and feature-based PageRank are important and practically relevant.  
- Weakness: no wall-clock runtime comparison, making real-world efficiency and training-time trade-offs unclear.  
- Weakness: paper is technically dense and could better map general optimization notation to the Supervised PageRank application for readability.  
- Weakness: gradient-free method’s convergence (Theorem 2) assumes convexity on the feasible set, but the objective is generally non-convex; this limitation should be emphasized.  
- Weakness: limited experimental baselines—lacks comparison to standard learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) using the same features.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical guarantees, well-analyzed optimization methods, and clear empirical improvements on a real-world task.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing practical runtime analysis and reliance on a convexity assumption for one method’s guarantees.  
- Conclusion: overall strong paper with significant theoretical and empirical contributions; weaknesses are addressable, recommendation to accept.
