This paper formulates the Supervised PageRank problem as a constrained non-convex optimization problem, incorporating features associated with both nodes and edges. To solve this problem, the authors propose two distinct algorithmic approaches: a gradient-free method that uses an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method that relies on an inexact first-order oracle. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed formulation is interesting. However, the paper suffers from several major issues in its current form, primarily concerning its presentation and the clarity of its theoretical claims.

**Major Concerns**

My primary concern is the complete relegation of all experimental results to the supplementary material. The empirical validation of a proposed method is a critical component of a research paper, not an optional addendum. By omitting all results from the main text, the paper feels incomplete and its central claims are left unsubstantiated. A reader must be able to assess the paper's contributions and the performance of its methods based on the main body of the work. As it stands, it is impossible to evaluate the practical significance of the proposed algorithms without consulting a separate document, which is a significant flaw in the paper's structure. I strongly urge the authors to integrate a representative summary of their key experimental findings into the main paper.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

1.  **Clarity on Convexity Assumption:** There appears to be a disconnect between the theoretical analysis and the problem formulation. Theorem 1 is presented under the assumption of convexity. However, the objective function defined in Equation (2.4) is non-convex. This raises a critical question about the applicability of the theorem. Could the authors please clarify under what conditions the objective function becomes convex? Specifically, how must the set Φ be chosen to guarantee the convexity required by Theorem 1? If the theorem does not apply to the general non-convex problem addressed in the paper, its relevance and the context in which it holds should be made much clearer.

2.  **Convergence Guarantees:** Regarding the convergence analysis for the gradient-based method, Theorem 3 provides a bound for the change between successive iterates, ||x_k - x_{k+1}||. While this indicates that the steps eventually become small, it is not immediately obvious whether this bound is sufficient to guarantee that the sequence {x_k} converges to a stationary point of the non-convex objective. For non-convex optimization, establishing convergence to a stationary point is a crucial theoretical result. Can the authors provide a more detailed argument or formal proof that connects the bound in Theorem 3 to such a guarantee?

3.  **Algorithm Efficiency and Performance:** Based on Figure 1 in the supplementary material, the gradient-free method (GFNM) appears to exhibit slow convergence. This observation highlights the need for a more thorough analysis of the practical efficiency of the proposed algorithms. To provide a complete picture of their utility, the authors should report on computational complexity and empirical runtimes in their experiments. A comparative analysis of efficiency, alongside accuracy, is essential for readers to understand the trade-offs and determine which method is more suitable for practical applications.

In summary, while the paper introduces an interesting formulation for Supervised PageRank, its significant presentational issues and the lack of clarity on key theoretical points prevent a positive recommendation. The inclusion of experimental results in the main text is non-negotiable for a paper of this nature. Furthermore, the concerns regarding the convexity assumption and convergence guarantees need to be thoroughly addressed to strengthen the paper's theoretical contributions.
