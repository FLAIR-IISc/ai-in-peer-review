This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, which incorporate node and edge features into the ranking process. The authors formulate this as a constrained, non-convex loss-minimization problem. The central challenge is that the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly, as they depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain. Consequently, any optimization algorithm must operate with an "inexact oracle."

The authors identify that the state-of-the-art method for this problem [21] uses a gradient-based approach but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to its use of an inexact gradient. The main contribution of this paper is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. The first is a random gradient-free method designed for constrained problems with an inexact zero-order oracle (i.e., inexact function values). The second is an adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex problems that works with an inexact first-order oracle (i.e., inexact function and gradient values). For the lower-level oracle, both methods leverage an efficient iterative scheme [17] that provides controllable accuracy for the stationary distribution and its derivatives. The proposed methods are evaluated on a web page ranking task, where they are shown to outperform the existing baseline.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Theoretical Significance:** The primary strength of this work is that it provides the first formal convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. This is a significant theoretical advance over the previous state-of-the-art, which lacked such guarantees. The development of general optimization frameworks for constrained problems with inexact oracles is a valuable contribution in its own right and could be of interest to the broader optimization community.
*   **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper originally combines several advanced optimization concepts: inexact oracles, random gradient-free methods, and adaptive gradient methods for non-convex problems. The theoretical analysis appears sound and carefully derives complexity bounds that account for the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall performance of the upper-level algorithm.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the problem formulation, the challenges associated with the inexact oracle, and the proposed two-level solution structure. The motivation for each component of the proposed algorithms is well-argued.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while limited, demonstrate that the proposed methods achieve better ranking quality (lower loss) than the existing baseline on a real-world dataset.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Strong Convexity Assumption for the Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the proposed gradient-free method (Theorem 1) assumes that the objective function is convex within the optimization domain Φ. However, the Supervised PageRank loss function is generally non-convex. The authors suggest choosing Φ as a small vicinity of a local minimum where the function is convex, but it is unclear how one would identify such a region in practice. This strong assumption limits the applicability of the theoretical guarantees for the gradient-free method to the actual problem.
*   **Clarity of Convergence Criterion for the Gradient-Based Method:** The convergence guarantee for the gradient-based method (Theorem 3) is provided for the quantity `‖M_K(x_K − x_{K+1})‖^2`. While this is a valid stopping criterion for proximal gradient methods, its relationship to more standard measures of stationarity (e.g., the norm of the gradient of the objective) is not explicitly discussed. Clarifying this connection would make the theoretical result more accessible and its implications easier to understand.
*   **Incomplete Evaluation of Efficiency:** The experimental results focus on the final loss value and the number of upper-level iterations. However, Figure 1 suggests that the gradient-free method (GFN) converges quite slowly in terms of iterations. A more complete empirical evaluation should include a comparison of wall-clock time or total arithmetic operations to convergence. This would provide crucial insight into the practical trade-offs between the proposed methods and the baseline, especially since the proposed methods may require more complex computations per iteration at the lower level.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  The authors should explicitly address the practical limitations of the convexity assumption in Theorem 1. A discussion on why the method is still expected to work well in non-convex settings (as the experiments might suggest) or an empirical study on the sensitivity to the choice of the domain Φ would strengthen the paper.
2.  In Section 5.1, it would be helpful to briefly explain how the convergence criterion `‖M_K(x_K − x_{K+1})‖^2 ≤ ε` relates to finding an approximate stationary point of the problem.
3.  The experimental section would be significantly improved by reporting runtimes or another measure of computational cost. This would allow for a fair comparison of the overall efficiency of the algorithms.
4.  For better readability and self-containment, it would be beneficial to include the main convergence plot (Figure 1) in the main body of the paper rather than in the supplementary material.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence-guaranteed algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank. The technical quality is high, but the practical implications of a key theoretical assumption and the evaluation of computational efficiency could be further clarified. Despite these points, the paper's strengths, particularly its theoretical novelty and importance, make a compelling case for acceptance.
