

The paper introduces a supervised PageRank framework that incorporates node and edge features through linear combinations of feature vectors with model parameters. The authors identify a gap in the existing work [21], which lacks theoretical convergence guarantees for the parameter learning procedure, and propose two two-level optimization methods to address this limitation. Their approach leverages an inexact oracle framework, combining a linearly convergent method for approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk with adaptations of gradient-free and gradient-based optimization techniques. The paper provides convergence rate guarantees for both algorithms, which the previous state-of-the-art method [21] lacks, and demonstrates empirical improvements on a web ranking task.

Strengths: The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous convergence rate analysis for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. The authors provide detailed complexity bounds for the two-level algorithms and carefully analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art method [21] on the ranking task.

Weaknesses: The main body of the paper lacks sufficient empirical details, with key results relegated to the appendix, including critical parameters such as the choice of ϕ₀ and R in the experiments. The problem is mislabeled as "Supervised PageRank" when [21] describes "semi-supervised" PageRank, creating confusion in the citation context. The assumption of restricting the parameter space to a Euclidean ball around a chosen ϕ₀ may limit practical applicability, and this constraint is not adequately justified or discussed. The paper contains multiple grammatical errors that affect readability, and the impact of the proposed method on real-world search engine applications is not sufficiently elaborated. The experimental section does not provide details about whether diverse graph structures were tested or how the improvements translate to practical benefits.

Arguments for acceptance: The theoretical contributions are significant and well-supported, with the convergence guarantees addressing a clear limitation in prior work. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art.

Arguments against acceptance: The lack of detailed empirical results in the main paper, the mislabeling of the problem with inconsistent citations, the potentially limiting technical assumptions, and the poor writing quality significantly undermine the paper's presentation and impact. The reviewers require more substantial evidence of the method's practical utility in the main body of the paper.
