In this paper, the authors propose two optimization methods to solve the supervised PageRank problem. Unlike existing methods, this paper proposes a gradient-based method with a theoretically guaranteed convergence rate and the ability to achieve a given accuracy. The proposed gradient-free method also exhibits a guaranteed loss function decrease. These two methods do not require the exact value of the objective function and provide an estimate of the convergence rate, while also demonstrating improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art. The hyperparameters used in both methods are clearly stated. While the datasets are not publicly available, the description is detailed enough to facilitate reproduction of the results. 

The authors propose two two-level optimization methods – gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) – to solve the supervised PageRank problem. The lower and upper level optimization are based on the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. The proposed methods avoid the need for exact objective function values and offer proven estimates of the convergence rate, potentially circumventing the computational burden of large matrix operations while still achieving good results. Sections 2 through 5 provide detailed explanations of the methods, including all proofs in the supplementary material.

However, the paper would greatly benefit from a symbol table, as the numerous symbols in the equations can be difficult to track. For example, the meaning of ‘N’ (representing the number of steps) is introduced on line 193, but is used in equation 3.1 much earlier (line 139) without prior definition. Additionally, the experimental section primarily compares the methods based on the loss function, rather than directly evaluating ranking quality. Given that this work generalizes [17], a direct comparison to that method would be valuable to assess the performance improvements.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods, a significant advantage over previous work [21].
*   **Inexact Oracle:** The methods effectively address the challenge of non-convex optimization by working with inexact oracle information, making them practical for large-scale problems.
*   **Improved Performance:** Experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in terms of ranking quality.
*   **Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a comprehensive explanation of the methods and their theoretical foundations, with proofs included in the supplementary material.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Symbol Table:** The abundance of symbols and mathematical notation without a reference table hinders readability and understanding.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments primarily focus on loss function values, with insufficient emphasis on direct ranking quality metrics.
*   **Missing Comparison to Baseline:** A direct comparison to the method generalized from ([17]) is absent, making it difficult to fully assess the improvements.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in graph-based ranking.
*   The theoretical contributions, particularly the convergence guarantees, are significant.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive explanation of the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a symbol table and the limited experimental evaluation detract from the paper's clarity and impact.
*   The absence of a comparison to the baseline method [17] weakens the argument for the proposed methods' improvements.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised PageRank and optimization methods for graph-based ranking. The theoretical guarantees and experimental results are promising. While the paper would benefit from addressing the noted weaknesses, the strengths outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points regarding clarity and completeness in a revised version. Specifically, adding a symbol table and including a direct comparison to the method in [17] would significantly strengthen the paper.
