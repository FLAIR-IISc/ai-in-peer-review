This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models with node and edge features, motivated by the limitations of existing methods that lack theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors propose gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods built upon inexact oracle approximations, aiming to overcome these limitations and improve ranking performance. They establish convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods, and empirically demonstrate improvements over a state-of-the-art approach on a web page ranking task.

The paper builds on the foundation of PageRank and its extensions [18, 11, 8, 9, 19], recognizing their shortcomings in capturing rich node and edge properties. It correctly identifies Supervised PageRank [21] as a framework to address this, while highlighting the lack of convergence analysis for the original optimization method. The proposed approach leverages approximations of the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, utilizing a method from [17] deemed more suitable than existing alternatives [5]. The theoretical contributions—convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods—are a significant strength. The adaptation of the gradient-free approach from [16] to a constrained optimization setting with inexact function evaluations is also noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides convergence rate guarantees for both proposed optimization methods, a crucial aspect often missing in similar works.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of inexact oracle approximations with gradient-based and gradient-free methods in the context of Supervised PageRank appears novel. The adaptation of existing methods to the constrained optimization setting is also a valuable contribution.
*   **Potential Impact:**  Improving the performance and theoretical understanding of Supervised PageRank could have practical benefits in ranking applications, such as web search and recommendation systems.
*   **Well-Defined Problem:** The problem statement is clear, and the authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from poor writing quality, including grammatical errors and convoluted phrasing. This hinders readability and understanding. The organization could be improved; the extensive reliance on "Supplementary Materials" for key details (proofs, full experimental results) diminishes the paper's self-containedness.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While experiments are conducted, the presentation of results in the main body is insufficient. Crucial details – parameter choices (specifically, the selection of  ϕ̂ and R), the diversity of graphs tested, and detailed quantitative comparisons – are relegated to the appendix. The lack of comprehensive results makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the reported improvements.
*   **Questionable Assumptions:** The constraint of the parameter space Φ to an R-ball around a chosen point ϕ̂ could be limiting and requires further justification. The potential impact of this assumption on the algorithm’s performance and applicability should be discussed.
*   **Citation Accuracy:** The paper contains inaccuracies in its citations; for example, citing [21] for "semi-supervised page rank" when the paper introduces "Supervised PageRank".
*   **Impactful Problem?** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the overall impact of solving this specific problem isn’t strongly articulated.  A more compelling argument for the importance of the learned ranking algorithm is needed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents novel optimization methods for a relevant problem with rigorous theoretical guarantees.
*   The empirical results, although presented incompletely in the main paper, suggest performance improvements over the state-of-the-art.
*   The work could stimulate further research in the area of Supervised PageRank and inexact optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper's clarity and presentation are significantly hampered by poor writing and a lack of self-containedness.
*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed methods.
*   The potential limitations of key assumptions are not adequately addressed.
*   Citation inaccuracies undermine the paper’s credibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents technically sound work with potentially valuable contributions. However, its current form is significantly hindered by clarity issues, an incomplete empirical evaluation, and questionable assumptions. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize improving the writing quality, providing a comprehensive presentation of experimental results (including detailed tables and statistical analysis), addressing the limitations of their assumptions, and verifying the accuracy of their citations.  Moving key material from the supplementary materials into the main body will also substantially improve the paper. A clearer articulation of the problem's importance and potential impact is also necessary.
