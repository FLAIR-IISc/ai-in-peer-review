The authors motivate their work by revisiting PageRank and introducing a framework incorporating node and edge weights derived from a feature model. They identify a lack of performance guarantees in a previously proposed algorithm for learning the parameters of this model, and propose a solution combining a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique suitable for loss functions that can only be estimated. Their framework largely reproduces existing work from [21] under different notation. The paper details several technical assumptions, defines a loss function, and explains the challenges inherent in solving the resulting optimization problem. Several lemmas bounding the approximate gradient are stated, followed by a discussion of a general gradient-free method utilizing a biased gradient-free oracle, and a corresponding theorem outlining its performance. Further assumptions are made, an algorithm is constructed to address the learning problem, and the gradient-free methods are adapted to their specific context, accompanied by additional bounds. 

Experimental results are presented using a web graph from related work, but detailed reporting and analysis are largely relegated to the appendix. Crucially, several parameters are not reported, and while the authors claim their novel algorithm outperforms existing approaches, there is no substantive discussion of the *extent* or *implications* of this improvement. It’s unclear whether the evaluation encompassed a diverse set of graph structures. The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors and inconsistencies in citation style. For instance, the problem is referred to as “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], which actually addresses semi-supervised PageRank. 

I am not fully persuaded of the practical impact of the problem addressed. While the theoretical bounds are strong, I harbor doubts regarding the validity of the stated technical assumptions when applied to empirical data. Some assumptions appear particularly limiting; for example, restricting the search space of φ to an R-ball around a chosen φ₀ may significantly curtail the algorithm’s efficacy and applicability—a limitation that should be thoroughly discussed.

My primary concern lies with the fact that the core contributions—detailed experimental results and proofs—are primarily located in the appendix. The paper lacks both proof outlines and comprehensive empirical results within the main text. Specifically, how were φ₀ and R chosen in the empirical evaluation? Were tests conducted on more than one graph? No tables or specific results are included in the main body of the paper. Ultimately, what are the practical consequences of the observed improvements? While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm itself may be powerful, the paper, in its current form, fails to convincingly demonstrate their value.
