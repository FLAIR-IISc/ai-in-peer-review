### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a feature-based PageRank model. The authors correctly point out that a prior algorithm for this task lacks performance guarantees. To address this, they propose a new method that combines an approximation for the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with a gradient-free optimization method designed for scenarios where the loss function can only be estimated. The paper presents a theoretical analysis of this approach, providing performance bounds for their learning algorithm. Experimental results on a single web graph are briefly mentioned, with full details deferred to the appendix.

**Strengths**

The primary contribution of this work is theoretical. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of their proposed algorithm, establishing performance guarantees for learning in a feature-based PageRank model where previous methods lacked such guarantees. The theoretical bounds derived for the gradient-free optimization method appear strong, and the approach of adapting a biased gradient-free oracle to this problem is sound.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the theoretical promise, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its presentation, experimental validation, and discussion of practical relevance, which collectively undermine the contribution.

1.  **Clarity, Presentation, and Structure:** The paper is difficult to read due to numerous grammatical errors and sloppy citations. For instance, the problem is referred to as "Supervised PageRank" with a citation to [21], but that reference appears to discuss "semi-supervised PageRank." More critically, the paper's structure makes it nearly impossible to evaluate. The main body of the paper lacks essential content; nearly all technical substance, including proof outlines and the entirety of the empirical results and analysis, is relegated to the appendix. A strong paper should be self-contained, with the main text providing sufficient evidence—including key theoretical insights and summary results—to support its claims. As it stands, the paper reads as an extended abstract for the work presented in the appendix.

2.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** While the theoretical bounds are the paper's main strength, their relevance is contingent on a set of strong technical assumptions. The authors provide no justification or empirical evidence to suggest these assumptions would hold on real-world data. Some assumptions seem particularly limiting. For example, the constraint that the search space for the parameter `phi` is an R-ball around a pre-selected `phi0` could greatly restrict the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm. The potential negative impact of this constraint is not discussed. Without a discussion of the validity of these assumptions, the practical value of the theoretical guarantees is unclear.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation presented in the main paper is wholly inadequate. The authors briefly mention experiments on a single web graph previously used in related work, but they provide no tables, figures, or specific quantitative results.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** Critical hyperparameters, such as how `phi0` and `R` were chosen for the experiments, are not reported. This makes the results irreproducible.
    *   **Vague Claims:** The paper proclaims that the proposed algorithm "outperformed other algorithms," but this claim is unsubstantiated in the main text. There is no discussion of the *extent* of the improvement (e.g., by 1% or 50%?), its statistical significance, or its practical implications. What real-world benefit does this improvement provide?
    *   **Limited Scope:** It is unclear if a diverse set of graphs was considered for testing. Reporting results on a single graph is not sufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.

4.  **Impact and Contribution:** The paper does not convincingly argue for the impact of the problem being solved. Further, the framework is presented as a reproduction of the one in [21] under different notation, which may limit the perceived novelty of the problem formulation itself. The core contribution lies in the new learning algorithm and its analysis, but as detailed above, the paper fails to demonstrate its practical value.

**Conclusion**

In its current form, the paper is not ready for publication. While the theoretical direction is interesting, the work is severely undermined by a combination of poor presentation, the relegation of all essential content to the appendix, strong and unevaluated assumptions, and a superficial experimental section. The paper fails to convince the reader of the practical value and empirical validity of its theoretically-grounded algorithm. I would recommend rejection, but I would be willing to reconsider a substantially revised version that integrates the key technical and empirical results into the main paper, provides a thorough and reproducible experimental evaluation on diverse datasets, and seriously discusses the practical implications and limitations of the theoretical assumptions.
