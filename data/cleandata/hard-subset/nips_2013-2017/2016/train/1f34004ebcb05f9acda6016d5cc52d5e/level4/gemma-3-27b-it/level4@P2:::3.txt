This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing both gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) optimization methods. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods – a key distinction from prior work [21] on this problem, which lacked such assurances. The authors highlight that their approaches operate effectively with inexact function evaluations and offer estimated convergence rates, while also demonstrating superior ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art [21] in their experiments. 

The work builds upon the foundational two-level optimization framework introduced in [21], extending it by leveraging results from Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. A significant advantage of the proposed methods is their ability to avoid calculating exact derivatives or dealing with large matrices, making them potentially more scalable. The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, with proofs relegated to the supplementary material, and presents experimental results on a web page ranking task using a dataset from Yandex.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The provision of convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant contribution, addressing a limitation of previous approaches.
* **Inexact Oracle:** The ability to operate with inexact function evaluations makes the methods more practical for large-scale problems where exact computations are prohibitive.
* **Improved Performance:** Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including lemmas and theorems, and offers complexity estimations.
* **Well-defined Experiment:** The experimental setup is clearly described, though the dataset is not publicly available.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Symbolism & Clarity:** The paper suffers from a high density of symbols and equations, making it difficult to follow the derivations without repeated reference to the supplementary material.  A symbol table would significantly improve readability. For instance, the meaning of 'N' (number of iterations) is not immediately clear and requires searching through the text (e.g., line 193).
* **Limited Comparison in Experiments:** The experimental comparison primarily focuses on the loss function.  A more compelling evaluation would include direct comparisons of ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) to better demonstrate the practical impact of the proposed methods.
* **Missing Baseline Comparison:** Given the reliance on and generalization of the work in [17], a direct comparison with the original method [17] would strengthen the paper’s contribution and clearly illustrate the improvements achieved.
* **Dataset Availability:** The lack of public availability of the dataset hinders reproducibility. While the description is adequate, open access would greatly benefit the research community.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically effective solution to a challenging optimization problem in supervised ranking.
* The convergence guarantees and ability to handle inexact evaluations are valuable contributions to the field.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art.
* The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of relevant prior work and builds upon it in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high level of mathematical notation and lack of a symbol table may hinder understanding for some readers.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, particularly with respect to ranking metrics and comparison to relevant baselines.
* The lack of public dataset availability limits reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions. While the paper could benefit from improved clarity and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, the significance of the results – particularly the convergence guarantees – outweighs the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity concerns and considering a more thorough evaluation of ranking quality.  Specifically, I suggest adding a symbol table and including standard ranking metrics in the experimental results.
