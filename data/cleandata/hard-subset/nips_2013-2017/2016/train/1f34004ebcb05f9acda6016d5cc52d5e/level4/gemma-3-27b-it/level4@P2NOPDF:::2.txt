This paper introduces a Supervised PageRank model formulated as a constrained non-convex optimization problem, leveraging node and edge properties to enhance the ranking process. The authors propose two distinct solution approaches: a gradient-free method employing an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method utilizing an inexact first-order oracle. My primary concern is the omission of experimental results from the main text, rendering the paper currently incomplete and hindering a full assessment of its contributions.

Here are more detailed comments:

1. The placement of experimental results solely within the supplementary material significantly detracts from the paper’s comprehensibility. Key findings should be integrated into the main text to provide a complete picture of the proposed methods' performance and to support the authors' claims. Without this integration, the paper feels preliminary.

2. Theorem 1 assumes convexity, yet the function defined in equation (2.4) is explicitly stated as non-convex. The paper needs to clearly address this discrepancy. Specifically, how is the set $\Phi$ chosen to ensure the convexity of the objective function, or what justification is provided for proceeding with a non-convex optimization despite the initial assumption? A discussion of the implications of this non-convexity is also warranted.

3. Theorem 3 provides a bound on $MK(xK - x{K+1})$. It is unclear whether this bound is sufficient to guarantee convergence of the sequence $x_k$ to a stationary point. The authors should elaborate on the relationship between this bound and convergence properties, perhaps by stating necessary conditions for convergence or discussing the practical implications of the bound’s limitations.

4. The results presented in Figure 1 of the supplement suggest that the Gradient-Free Newton Method (GFNM) converges relatively slowly. While the authors demonstrate feasibility, a more thorough evaluation of algorithmic efficiency – including runtime comparisons with existing methods or detailed scalability analysis – is necessary. Reporting computational complexity and potentially providing performance profiles beyond convergence speed (e.g., iterations required for a certain level of accuracy) would strengthen the evaluation.
