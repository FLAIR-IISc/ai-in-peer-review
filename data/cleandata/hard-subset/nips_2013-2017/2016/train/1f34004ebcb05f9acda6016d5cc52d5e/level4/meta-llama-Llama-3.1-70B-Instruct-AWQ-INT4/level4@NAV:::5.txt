The authors introduce the concept of PageRank and propose a framework incorporating node and edge weights derived from a feature model. They highlight the limitations of a previous algorithm for learning model parameters, which lacked performance guarantees. To address this, they aim to combine a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with a modified gradient-free optimization technique. The authors recreate a framework, previously presented in [21], under a different name and notation. They discuss technical assumptions, a loss function, and the challenges associated with solving the problem. The paper includes lemmas bounding the approximate gradient and outlines a general gradient-free method utilizing a biased gradient-free oracle, accompanied by a theorem on its performance. The authors make additional assumptions and construct an algorithm for the learning problem, adapting the gradient-free method and providing further bounds. Experimental results on a web graph are briefly mentioned, but a detailed analysis is relegated to the appendix. Notably, some parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform others, the extent and implications of this improvement are not discussed. Furthermore, it is unclear whether the testing was conducted on a diverse set of graphs. The writing is marred by grammatical errors, and citations are inconsistent, with the problem being referred to as Supervised PageRank despite only semi-supervised PageRank being mentioned in [21]. The impact of the problem solved is questionable, and the technical assumptions may not hold in empirical data. Some assumptions, such as the restriction of the search space of phi to an R-ball around phi0, may limit the algorithm's efficacy and applicability. A major concern is that the majority of the content is relegated to the appendix, with no proof outlines or comprehensive empirical results provided in the main body. The choice of phi0 and R in the empirical results section is not explained, and it is unclear whether tests were conducted on multiple graphs. The absence of tables and specific results in the main body of the paper raises questions about the practical consequences of the improvements. While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be powerful, the content presented in the paper fails to convince.
