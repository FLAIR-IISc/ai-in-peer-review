This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss minimization problem arising in the learning of parameters for a general graph-based ranking model. This model is defined by a random walk process governed by node and edge weights, which are themselves determined by random walks on the nodes' and edges’ respective feature spaces. The optimization problem posed is challenging as existing methods require exact function values, which are intractable in this setting. The authors propose a two-level approach to address this difficulty. 

At the first level, a linearly convergent method is employed to estimate an approximation of the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The authors validate this approximation and demonstrate that the loss function can be approximated to any desired precision. Complementing this, they develop a gradient method for constrained, non-convex optimization problems utilizing an inexact oracle, and rigorously prove its convergence to a stationary point. The core contribution lies in adapting this methodology to the scenario of constrained optimization where function values are known only to a specified precision. This convergence analysis is then leveraged in the second level of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written and the proofs appear sound, although a complete verification was beyond the scope of this review. However, a primary concern is that many of the optimization techniques presented appear to be adaptations of those found in Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015) – specifically, their work on random gradient-free minimization of convex functions. Furthermore, the concept of supervised PageRank, which shares similarities with this approach, has been previously explored in the literature. 

While the adaptation to constrained optimization with inexact oracles is a valuable extension, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive contextualization of the optimization problem. Presenting the problem in a more general framework, and positioning the proposed algorithm as a specific instantiation within that framework, would better illuminate the novelty and significance of the contribution relative to the existing state-of-the-art. 

A concluding section is notably absent. Such a section would be beneficial to summarize the key findings, discuss potential limitations, and outline avenues for future research. This could include exploring the practical performance of the algorithm on larger, more complex datasets, or investigating alternative methods for approximating the stationary distribution.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a challenging optimization problem. While building upon existing work, the adaptation to inexact oracles and constrained optimization represents a worthwhile contribution. Strengthening the discussion of related work and adding a concluding section would significantly enhance the paper's impact and clarity.
