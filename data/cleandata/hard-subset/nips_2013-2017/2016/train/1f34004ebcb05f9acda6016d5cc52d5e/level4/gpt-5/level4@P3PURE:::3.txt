This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for the supervised PageRank problem: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN). The lower- and upper-level optimizations are based on Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work (ref:17). The gradient-based method comes with a theoretical convergence-rate guarantee and can achieve a prescribed accuracy. The gradient-free method guarantees a decrease in the loss function. Importantly, both methods do not require the exact value of the objective function and provide convergence-rate estimates. As a result, the approach avoids computing a large matrix while still delivering strong results. The hyper-parameters used in both methods are provided. Although the datasets are not publicly available, the descriptions are clear enough that reproducing the results should be feasible. From Sections 2 to 5, the authors detail the methods, with all proofs included in the supplementary material.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contributions: GBN has a provable convergence rate to a given accuracy, and GFN guarantees a decrease in the loss.
- The methods do not require exact objective function values and avoid expensive large-matrix computations.
- Practical details, such as hyper-parameter settings, are included.
- The reported performance appears better than the state of the art in terms of ranking quality.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Notation clarity: There are many symbols, making it easy to lose track. A symbol table would significantly improve readability. For example, N is used in Line 139 and Equation (3.1) but is not defined as the number of steps until Line 193.
- Experimental comparisons: In the experiments, the authors compare to GBP primarily in terms of the loss function rather than ranking quality. Given that ranking quality is the ultimate goal, a direct comparison to GBP on ranking metrics would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
- Relation to prior work: Since this work generalizes ref:17, a direct empirical comparison with that baseline would help quantify the gains and clarify the contribution.

Overall, the paper presents theoretically grounded and practically relevant methods for supervised PageRank, with solid convergence guarantees and promising empirical results. Clarifying the notation and strengthening the experimental comparisons—especially on ranking metrics and against ref:17—would further improve the paper.
