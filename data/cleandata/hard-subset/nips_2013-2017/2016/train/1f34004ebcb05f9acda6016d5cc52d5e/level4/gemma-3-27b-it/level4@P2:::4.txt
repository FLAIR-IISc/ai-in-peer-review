This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a non-convex optimization challenge, by proposing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods. The core idea is to leverage an “inexact oracle” approach, aiming to provide theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods—a feature lacking in previous state-of-the-art gradient-based approaches [21]. The authors also investigate the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity. 

The paper builds upon the established foundations of PageRank [18] and its extensions [8, 9, 19], which traditionally focus on graph structure.  It acknowledges the limitations of these methods and connects to approaches that incorporate node and edge features into ranking via modified restart and transition probabilities [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21].  The work explicitly frames its contribution within the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], addressing the convergence issues present in that method. It distinguishes itself from other optimization techniques [16, 7] due to the constraints inherent in the problem and the difficulty of exact loss function calculation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** A significant strength is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a notable improvement over the existing state-of-the-art [21] which lacks such guarantees.
*   **Inexact Oracle Approach:** The use of an inexact oracle is a practical and well-motivated choice, allowing for efficient computation and theoretical analysis. The analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and complexity is valuable.
*   **Comprehensive Comparison:** The experimental evaluation compares the proposed methods against the existing state-of-the-art [21] on a real-world ranking task. The paper reports improvements in ranking quality.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem and provides a well-structured mathematical formulation.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a reasonably comprehensive overview of relevant literature in PageRank, supervised ranking, and optimization methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Detail:** While experiments are presented, the level of detail regarding the parameter tuning and computational resources used could be improved. Reporting the *time* taken for each algorithm to converge, in addition to the final loss, would be highly valuable.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The complexity analysis, while present, is somewhat dense and difficult to readily interpret. A more intuitive explanation of the practical implications of the complexity bounds would be beneficial.
*   **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset from Yandex. Evaluating the methods on additional datasets with different characteristics would increase confidence in their generalizability.
*   **Discussion of Convexity:** The assumption of local convexity, invoked when applying Theorem 1, feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed justification for this assumption, or a discussion of the method's behavior in non-convex regions, would be helpful.
* **Redundancy:** The paper repeats similar information in multiple sections (e.g. description of the model), leading to some redundancy. 



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to learning Supervised PageRank with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The convergence guarantees are a significant contribution to the field.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The work addresses a well-defined and important problem in ranking and information retrieval.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and detailed.
*   The complexity analysis is not easily accessible.
*   The assumption of local convexity warrants further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical guarantees and the improved performance in experiments make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. While the experimental details could be improved and the complexity analysis simplified, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors providing more detailed information regarding experimental setup (especially runtime) and clarifying the complexity analysis. Addressing the points raised regarding the convexity assumption would also strengthen the paper.
