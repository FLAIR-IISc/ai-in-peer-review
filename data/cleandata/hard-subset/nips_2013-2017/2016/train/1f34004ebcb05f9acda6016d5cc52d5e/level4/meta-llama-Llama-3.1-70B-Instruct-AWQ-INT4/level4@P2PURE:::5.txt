The authors introduce PageRank and propose a framework incorporating node and edge weights from a feature model. They highlight the limitations of a previous algorithm for learning model parameters, which lacks performance guarantees. To address this, they combine a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique, suitable for scenarios where the loss function can only be estimated. Although the framework is similar to that presented in [21], the authors employ different notation and terminology. They discuss several technical assumptions, define a loss function, and explain the challenges associated with solving the problem. The authors provide lemmas bounding the approximate gradient, describe a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle, and state a theorem on its performance. Following additional assumptions, they construct an algorithm for the learning problem, adapting the gradient-free method to their specific problem, and derive further bounds. Experimental results on a web graph are briefly outlined, but a detailed analysis of the results is relegated to the appendix. However, some parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform others, the extent and implications of this improvement are not discussed. It is also unclear whether a diverse set of graphs were considered for testing. Furthermore, the writing is marred by grammatical errors, and citations are sloppy, with the problem being referred to as Supervised PageRank, despite [21] only discussing semi-supervised PageRank. I remain unconvinced about the impact of the problem being solved. Although the bounds presented are strong, I have doubts about the validity of the technical assumptions in the context of empirical data. Some assumptions, such as restricting the search space of phi to an R-ball around a chosen phi0, may significantly limit the algorithm's efficacy and applicability, warranting further discussion. My primary concern with the paper is that the majority of the content, including proof outlines and empirical results, is relegated to the appendix. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how phi0 and R were chosen in the empirical results section, nor do they report on whether tests were conducted on multiple graphs. The main body of the paper lacks specific results, tables, and an analysis of the practical consequences of the improvements. While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be powerful, the content presented in the paper fails to convince me of its significance.
