This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning a graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk where transition probabilities are parameterized by features of the graph's nodes and edges. A key challenge is that the objective function, which depends on the stationary distribution of this random walk, cannot be computed exactly. Consequently, standard optimization methods that require exact function values are not applicable.

The authors propose a two-level approach to address this challenge. At the first level, they employ a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, demonstrating that the loss function's value can be estimated to any desired precision. At the second level, they develop a gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems that can operate with an inexact oracle. The core theoretical contribution is proving the convergence of this method to a stationary point when the objective function can only be calculated with a known, controllable precision. This method is then applied to optimize the ranking model's parameters.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be sound, although I have not verified every detail.

My primary concern relates to the novelty of the contributions. The core optimization techniques for non-convex problems using an inexact oracle seem to be a direct adaptation of the methods proposed in "Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions" by Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015). While the authors extend this to the constrained case, the fundamental idea seems inherited. Furthermore, the underlying supervised PageRank model is also based on existing work. The paper would be significantly stronger if it more clearly delineated its novel theoretical contributions from these established foundations.

To improve the paper and better highlight its contributions, I suggest the following:

1.  **Generalize the Problem Formulation:** The paper would benefit from presenting the optimization problem in a more general context first. This would allow the authors to position the studied ranking algorithm as a specific instance of a broader class of problems. Framing it this way would help to better contextualize the contribution with respect to the state-of-the-art in optimization with inexact oracles, particularly in relation to the work of Nesterov and Spokoiny.

2.  **Add a Conclusion:** The paper currently lacks a conclusion. Adding a dedicated section to summarize the findings, reiterate the main contributions, and perhaps suggest avenues for future work would strengthen the paper's structure and impact. There appears to be sufficient space to do so.
