Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning parameters of a Supervised PageRank model in which restart and transition probabilities depend on node and edge features. The learning objective is a nonconvex pairwise square loss over query-specific graphs. Existing practice computes approximate stationary distributions and their gradients via power methods and then runs gradient descent, but without convergence guarantees due to inexactness of the oracle.

The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: (1) a gradient-based algorithm using an inexact first-order oracle, and (2) a random gradient-free algorithm using an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, both methods approximate the stationary distribution (and, for the gradient method, its derivatives) using the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme, arguing it has a better proven complexity than power methods. On the upper level, they develop: (i) an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle (generalizing inexact-oracle frameworks such as Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov and constrained variants of Nesterov’s composite gradient methods), and (ii) a constrained random gradient-free method with a biased spherical finite-difference oracle, extending Nesterov–Spokoiny/Ghadimi–Lan style estimators to handle inexact function values and constraints.

They prove convergence-rate bounds for both algorithms, quantify the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall complexity, and provide complexity expressions in terms of graph and feature dimensions. Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing dataset (≈600 queries, ≈11.7K pages, ≈7.5K edges, ≈1.7K labeled pairs) compare the proposed methods (GFN, GBN) with a prior gradient-based baseline (GBP). They report that both methods have theoretical guarantees and outperform the baseline in ranking quality, with GFN performing best.

Strengths
- Theoretical development: Clear formulation of inexact zero- and first-order oracles for this application; convergence-rate guarantees for both gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods under inexactness; complexity bounds that explicitly track graph sizes and feature sparsity.
- Lower-level computation: Sensible choice of the Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme; explicit control of approximation errors for π and its derivatives.
- Practical motivation: Learning parameters of feature-augmented random-walk models is relevant to ranking; the two-level perspective and error-accuracy trade-offs are well motivated.
- Empirical use case on real search logs, with query-dependent graphs.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The paper has numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent notation, and many incomplete/sloppy citations (missing venues/years; some references appear mismatched). This significantly hinders readability and verification.
- Assumptions and scope: Several theoretical results rely on assumptions that are strong or not clearly justified in practice (e.g., local convexity in a small ball; x* in the interior; positivity constraints via an ℓ2 ball around a chosen vector). The implications of these assumptions for real data are not discussed.
- Experimental reporting: Key experimental details (choice of ϕ̂ and R, α, stopping criteria, hyperparameter sensitivity, runtime/iteration complexity, and evaluation metrics like NDCG/MAP) are missing from the main text. Claims of “outperforming” the baseline are not supported with tables/figures in the body; quantitative effect sizes are largely deferred to the supplement.
- Baselines and breadth: Comparison is limited; it would be informative to include other learning-to-rank baselines and supervised random walk methods (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec), and to evaluate on multiple and more diverse graphs.
- Reproducibility: The current draft lacks enough detail for reproduction; even the bibliography is incomplete.

Quality
- The theoretical contributions appear technically sound at a high level and are well aligned with prior work on inexact oracles and gradient-free methods. However, the paper relies heavily on the supplement for proofs; providing clear proof roadmaps in the main text would strengthen confidence.
- The empirical section is underdeveloped: no main-text tables/metrics, limited discussion of variance/statistical significance (beyond a brief mention of p-values), and no runtime comparisons. Practical consequences (e.g., how much improvement in ranking quality) are not convincingly established.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from careful editing for English, consistent notation, and a streamlined presentation. Define all assumptions clearly where used; explain the intuition behind the key lemmas; and move at least one detailed result table and a learning-curve figure into the main text.

Originality
- Extending random gradient-free methods to constrained problems with inexact function values and adapting an inexact first-order composite method to the nonconvex constrained setting are worthwhile and, to my knowledge, not standard in the context of learning PageRank parameters. The lower-level derivative approximation via a generalized Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration is also a useful contribution.

Significance
- Potentially significant for practitioners who train feature-augmented random walk models at scale, as the guarantees and complexity control are valuable. However, the current empirical section does not convincingly demonstrate impact (dataset size is modest; effect sizes and runtimes are unclear). Strengthening experiments would raise the paper’s significance.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please justify the constraint set Φ = {ϕ: ||ϕ − ϕ̂||2 ≤ R} beyond positivity: How were ϕ̂ and R chosen? How sensitive are results to these choices? Do they unduly restrict the solution?
- Discuss the assumption that f is convex in a neighborhood of the solution (used for the gradient-free algorithm). How large is this neighborhood in practice?
- Report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k) with means, standard errors, and effect sizes; include main-text tables for Q2 splits. Provide wall-clock runtime and iteration counts for all methods.
- Compare against additional baselines: supervised random walks, stronger first-order optimizers (e.g., Adam/Adagrad) using automatic differentiation of π(ϕ), and alternative PageRank derivative approximations.
- Provide a proof sketch in the main text for at least one core theorem and clarify the role of biased gradient-free estimators under inexact function values.
- Fix and complete the bibliography (venues, years, accurate titles) and reconcile references to “Supervised PageRank” vs related literature (e.g., Supervised Random Walks, BrowseRank).

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and relevant theoretical contributions on optimization with inexact oracles.
- Clear formulation of a two-level approach and complexity/error trade-offs.
- Potential applicability to large-scale ranking tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient empirical evidence in the main text; unclear practical impact.
- Presentation and citation quality issues impede evaluation.
- Strong assumptions not adequately justified or validated empirically.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper likely contains publishable theoretical advances and a promising application, but the current presentation and experimental evidence are too weak for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to (1) substantially improve clarity and citations, (2) provide thorough, main-text empirical results with standard ranking metrics, effect sizes, and runtimes, and (3) openly discuss assumptions and sensitivity to Φ. With these improvements, the work could merit acceptance.
