The authors introduce the concept of PageRank and propose a framework incorporating node and edge weights from a feature model. They highlight the limitations of a previous algorithm for learning the model's parameters, which lacked performance guarantees. To address this, they combine a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with a gradient-free optimization technique that can handle estimated loss functions.

The framework presented is similar to the one described in [21], albeit with different notation. The authors discuss various technical assumptions and the loss function, explaining the challenges associated with solving the problem. They provide lemmas that bound the approximate gradient and introduce a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle. A theorem is stated to demonstrate the performance of this method.

The authors make additional assumptions and construct an algorithm for the learning problem, adapting the gradient-free method to their specific needs. They provide bounds for this algorithm and outline the experimental results on a web graph used in related work. However, the detailed reporting of results and analysis are relegated to the appendix.

Several concerns arise from the paper's content and presentation. Firstly, some parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform others, the extent and implications of this improvement are not discussed. It is unclear whether a diverse set of graphs was considered for testing. Furthermore, the writing is marred by grammatical errors, and the citations are sloppy. Specifically, the problem is referred to as Supervised PageRank with a citation to [21], but only semi-supervised PageRank appears in that reference.

I am not convinced that the problem solved is impactful, despite the strong bounds presented. The technical assumptions stated raise doubts about their validity in empirical data. Some assumptions, such as the restriction of the search space of phi to an R-ball around a chosen phi0, may greatly limit the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm. This limitation should be discussed in more detail.

My primary concern with the paper is that the majority of the content, including proof outlines and full empirical results, is relegated to the appendix. The main body of the paper lacks essential information, such as tables and specific results. The authors do not provide insight into how they chose phi0 and R in the empirical results section or whether they conducted tests on multiple graphs.

Ultimately, the practical consequences of the improvements presented in the paper are unclear. While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be powerful, the content in the paper itself fails to convince me of its significance.
