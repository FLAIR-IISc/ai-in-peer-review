

This paper proposes two novel optimization methods—gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN)—to solve the supervised PageRank problem, offering theoretical convergence guarantees and practical advantages over existing approaches. The core contributions include a gradient-based method with a provable convergence rate for achieving a given accuracy, and a gradient-free method ensuring a guaranteed decrease in the loss function. Both approaches avoid requiring exact evaluations of the objective function and provide convergence rate estimates, while demonstrating superior ranking quality compared to state-of-the-art methods. Hyperparameters are thoroughly reported, and the experimental setup is clearly described, making reproducibility feasible despite the absence of publicly shared datasets. The theoretical foundation leverages Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work (Ref:17) for the lower- and upper-level optimizations, with all proofs relegated to the supplementary material.

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical performance. The convergence guarantees for both methods address a critical gap in existing supervised PageRank literature, where many approaches lack formal rate bounds. The avoidance of explicit large-matrix computations (e.g., via iterative approximations) further enhances scalability, aligning with practical deployment needs. The empirical results substantiate the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements in ranking quality (e.g., NDCG@k) over baselines like GBP, though the experiments focus primarily on loss function values rather than ranking metrics—a point requiring clarification.

However, the paper has two notable weaknesses. First, the mathematical exposition suffers from insufficient notation clarity. The manuscript introduces numerous symbols (e.g., $N$ in Line 139, Equation 3.1) without a dedicated symbol table, forcing readers to backtrack to later sections for definitions (e.g., $N$ as the number of steps is only clarified in Line 193). This impedes readability and increases cognitive load, particularly for complex equations in Sections 3–5. A symbol table would significantly improve accessibility. Second, the experimental evaluation lacks a direct comparison with the foundational work (Ref:17), which this paper generalizes. While GBP is compared on loss, the key metric for PageRank applications—ranking quality (e.g., NDCG)—is not benchmarked against Ref:17. This omission weakens the novelty claim, as it leaves open whether the proposed methods truly outperform the base framework in the primary application context. The authors should explicitly address this in the revision.

The paper’s structure is logical, with clear progression from problem formulation to theory and experiments. Supplementary proofs are appropriately included, though the lack of a symbol table remains a barrier to understanding. The claim that the methods "avoid computing the large matrix" is well-supported by the theory (Section 3), but the empirical section could better emphasize computational efficiency (e.g., runtime comparisons) alongside ranking quality. 

In summary, this work makes a valuable theoretical contribution to supervised PageRank optimization with strong empirical backing. The core ideas are sound, the convergence guarantees are meaningful, and the performance gains are compelling. Addressing the two weaknesses—notation clarity and expanded experimental comparison—would substantially strengthen the paper. The authors should: (1) include a symbol table in the main text; (2) compare ranking quality directly against Ref:17 (not just GBP); and (3) clarify whether loss function improvements translate to ranking gains (e.g., by reporting NDCG for Ref:17). With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions.
