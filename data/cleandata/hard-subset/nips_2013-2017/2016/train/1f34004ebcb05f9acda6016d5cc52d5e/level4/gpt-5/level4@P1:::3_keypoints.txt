- Proposes two two-level optimization methods for Supervised PageRank: gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN).  
- Uses Nesterov–Nemirovski method for lower-level stationary distribution approximation instead of the power method.  
- Upper level uses an adaptive inexact first-order oracle (GBN) or a random inexact zero-order oracle (GFN).  
- Provides convergence-rate guarantees and complexity bounds for both methods under inexact oracle assumptions.  
- Avoids explicit computation of derivatives for large matrices, improving practical scalability.  
- Extends prior work on PageRank, HITS, and supervised random walks and addresses a limitation of prior two-level methods [21].  
- Theoretical contributions include handling nonconvex composite constrained problems with unknown Lipschitz constants and a biased gradient-free oracle analysis.  
- Algorithmic design quantifies the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and total complexity and uses a linearly convergent lower-level solver.  
- Empirically outperforms the state-of-the-art gradient-based GBP in terms of the training loss on a Yandex web-ranking task.  
- GBN requires fewer upper-level iterations than GBP for certain step sizes.  
- Paper is notation-heavy and difficult to follow; many symbols (e.g., N, N1, N2, α) are defined late — a symbol table is recommended.  
- Experimental evaluation focuses on loss rather than standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP), weakening claims about ranking quality.  
- Lacks direct empirical comparison to [17], despite using it centrally for lower-level computation.  
- Dataset is not public and implementation details (restarts, edge feature construction, stopping criteria, sensitivity analyses) are insufficient for full reproducibility.  
- Theorems and proofs are provided and the paper appears technically sound with explicit assumptions.  
- The approach is original in combining a linearly convergent lower-level method with upper-level optimization under inexact oracles in the supervised PageRank setting.  
- The work is significant for large-scale ranking where exact derivatives are infeasible, but empirical evaluation should be strengthened.  
- Suggested improvements: add a symbol table, report standard IR metrics, compare empirically to [17] and power-method baselines, and release code or more implementation details.
