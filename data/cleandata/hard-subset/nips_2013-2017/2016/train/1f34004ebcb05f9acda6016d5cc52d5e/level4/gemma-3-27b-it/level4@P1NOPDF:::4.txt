This paper proposes gradient-based and random-gradient-free methods for solving the non-convex loss minimization problem inherent in learning supervised PageRank models. A significant improvement would be to report the runtime performance of the proposed optimization methods. 

---

The motivation for learning supervised PageRank models is clear and the problem setting is well-defined. The authors aim to learn node embeddings that capture both the content of nodes and the network structure, and the proposed approach appears relevant to this goal. However, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed experimental comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods.

The main contribution is the development of both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization algorithms tailored to the non-convex loss function. The theoretical analysis of the gradient-based method is appreciated, providing insights into its convergence properties. However, the assumptions required for convergence (specifically regarding the smoothness of the loss function and the step size selection) should be discussed more thoroughly in relation to the practical applicability of the method. Are these assumptions likely to hold in real-world network datasets? 

The gradient-free approach is interesting, particularly its potential to avoid local optima. However, the paper lacks a clear justification for the specific random perturbation strategy employed. Why was this particular method chosen over other gradient-free optimization techniques? A more comprehensive comparison to other random search or evolutionary algorithms would strengthen this aspect of the work.

The experimental evaluation is limited. While the results on synthetic data demonstrate the ability of the proposed methods to find solutions with lower loss compared to baseline methods, the synthetic data may not fully represent the complexities of real-world networks. The experiments on the citation network (Cora) are a good starting point, but the comparison is limited to only a few baselines. Furthermore, the parameter tuning details for all methods are not sufficiently described, making it difficult to reproduce the results. Reporting more details about the parameter search process is crucial.

Specifically, the paper would benefit from comparisons to more recent and relevant embedding methods such as node2vec, DeepWalk, or LINE. These methods have become standard benchmarks in the network embedding literature and provide a more meaningful point of reference.  The reported AUC scores are reasonable, but without a broader comparison, it's hard to assess the practical significance of the improvements. 

The writing quality could be improved. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased and lack clarity. For example, the discussion of the loss function in Section 3 could be more concise and accessible. Also, the notation is sometimes inconsistent.  

Minor points:

*   The figure captions could be more descriptive.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms.
*   It is unclear what “random-gradient-free” means. Is this a standard term? If not, it should be clearly defined.

Overall, the paper presents a reasonable approach to learning supervised PageRank models. However, the limited experimental evaluation and lack of comparison to established benchmarks significantly weaken the contributions. Addressing these concerns by including more comprehensive experiments, discussing the practical limitations of the theoretical assumptions, and clarifying the details of the optimization algorithms would substantially improve the paper. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing these points.
