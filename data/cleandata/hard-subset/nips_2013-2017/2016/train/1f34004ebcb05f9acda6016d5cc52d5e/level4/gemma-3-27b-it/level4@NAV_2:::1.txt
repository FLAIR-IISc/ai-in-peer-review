This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model, which utilizes a random walk weighted by node and edge features. The optimization problem is challenging due to the inability to directly apply existing methods requiring exact objective function values. The proposed approach employs a two-level strategy: first, a linearly convergent method approximates the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, allowing for loss function approximation with arbitrary precision; second, a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle is developed and proven to converge to a stationary point. A key contribution lies in adapting this framework to constrained optimization problems where function values are calculated with known precision, with corresponding convergence theorems. The paper is well-written and the proofs appear sound, though a complete verification was not undertaken. A primary concern is that the core optimization techniques build heavily on prior work, particularly “Yurii. Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015, pp. 1–40”, and the supervised PageRank algorithm itself has been previously introduced. A minor suggestion is to include a concluding section, which is currently missing. Furthermore, framing the optimization problem in a more general context, demonstrating the presented algorithm as a specific instance, would better position its contribution relative to the state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for optimization algorithms applied to a relevant problem (supervised PageRank).
*   The authors address a practical challenge—dealing with inexact oracle information—in the context of non-convex optimization.
*   The experimental results demonstrate performance improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core optimization techniques are largely adaptations of existing methods.
*   The supervised PageRank algorithm itself is not novel.
*   The paper lacks a concluding section summarizing the contributions and future work.
*   The generalizability of the approach could be better highlighted by presenting it within a broader optimization framework.
