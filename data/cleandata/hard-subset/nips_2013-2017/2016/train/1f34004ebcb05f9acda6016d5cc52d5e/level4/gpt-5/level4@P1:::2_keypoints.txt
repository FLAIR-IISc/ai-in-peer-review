- Paper formulates Supervised PageRank as a constrained, generally non-convex optimization with node/edge features via restart and transition probabilities.  
- Proposes two two-level methods using inexact oracles: (i) random gradient-free (inexact zero-order) and (ii) adaptive projected gradient (inexact first-order).  
- Lower-level: approximate stationary distribution and derivatives with a linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme instead of the power method.  
- Upper-level: analyzes convergence rates and complexity bounds that incorporate lower-level approximation accuracy.  
- Experiments: evaluated on Yandex query-dependent browsing graph vs. GBP, claiming improved ranking quality and convergence guarantees, but most results are in the supplement.  
- Theory strengths: provides lemmas bounding function/gradient estimation error and theorems for convergence in constrained and non-convex inexact-oracle settings.  
- Missing theoretical details: important derivations and explanations deferred to the supplement.  
- Convexity concern: Theorem 1 assumes f is convex on Φ, yet f in (2.4) is generally non-convex; unclear how Φ is chosen or convexity ensured.  
- Experimental reporting issue: main text omits key empirical results and runtime/efficiency metrics (wall-clock time, oracle calls, dependence on N, N1, N2).  
- Clarity: overall understandable but dense; key choices (e.g., selection of Φ) not clearly justified and some references incomplete.  
- Reproducibility: explicit complexity bounds and parameterizable accuracies given, but practical tuning guidance for τ, L, and oracle tolerances and their relation to N, N1, N2 is insufficient.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of random gradient-free to constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracle and adaptive projected gradient method for unknown Lipschitz/non-convex composite cases.  
- Contribution value: careful lower/upper-level error–complexity trade-off analysis yields convergence guarantees absent in prior GBP due to its power-method approximations.  
- Significance: relevant for learning-to-rank on graphs; providing inexact-oracle convergence guarantees is important for large-scale settings.  
- Request to move experiments: move final loss, IR metrics (NDCG/MAP), convergence plots, and computational cost summaries from supplement into main paper.  
- Convexity clarification requested: provide conditions ensuring f is convex on a ball around ϕ̂ or clarify that GFN aims for local minima and how convexity is checked/enforced.  
- Stationarity clarification requested: explain whether Theorem 3 implies convergence to stationary points and what stationarity measure or additional assumptions are needed.  
- Efficiency quantification requested: report wall-clock times, oracle evaluations, and dependence on lower-level tolerances; compare runtimes explicitly to GBP and GBN.  
- Metric suggestion: report standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP) in addition to squared pairwise loss to strengthen practical relevance.  
- Parameter guidance suggestion: provide recommendations and robustness analysis for choosing ε, τ, L, and initial L0, and offer similar guidance for GFN.  
- Notation/assumptions suggestion: clearly state norms used in each theorem, conditions for Lemmas 1–2, and justify boundedness/positivity assumptions for (2.1)–(2.2).  
- References: several citations are incomplete and should be fixed (authors, venues, years).  
- Pros: solid theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle optimization, careful error-control and complexity bounds, relevant large-scale application with claimed empirical improvements.  
- Cons: main text lacks experimental detail, convexity assumption for GFN unclear, stationarity implications not explicit, and practical tuning/runtime analyses are insufficient.  
- Overall: promising theoretical and application contributions, but needs main-text experiments, clarification of convexity and stationarity results, and stronger empirical efficiency reporting.
