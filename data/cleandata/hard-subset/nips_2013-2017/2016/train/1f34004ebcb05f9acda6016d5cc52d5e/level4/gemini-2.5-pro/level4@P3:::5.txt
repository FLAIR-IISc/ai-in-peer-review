This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that enriches the standard PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features. The authors identify a key limitation in the state-of-the-art learning algorithm [21]: a lack of theoretical convergence guarantees. To remedy this, the paper introduces two novel optimization methods—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—designed to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem associated with learning the model parameters.

The core technical contribution is the development and analysis of these algorithms within a framework of optimization with an "inexact oracle." This framework acknowledges that the loss function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly but can be approximated with controllable accuracy. The proposed methods combine an efficient iterative scheme [17] for approximating the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov chain with novel adaptations of general-purpose optimization techniques. For both the gradient-based and gradient-free approaches, the authors provide rigorous theoretical analyses, deriving convergence rates and complexity bounds. The paper concludes with an empirical comparison against the method from [21] on a web page ranking task.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this work lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. By providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models, the paper places the problem on a much firmer theoretical footing than prior work. The framework for optimization with an inexact oracle for constrained, non-convex problems is well-developed and could be of interest to the broader optimization community.

2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Algorithms:** The paper proposes two distinct and principled algorithms. The gradient-free method is particularly valuable for complex models where gradients are difficult or computationally expensive to derive and compute. The adaptive gradient-based method is also a strong contribution, especially its ability to handle an inexact first-order oracle without prior knowledge of the Lipschitz constant.

3.  **Problem Importance:** Learning feature-based ranking models for graphs is a well-established and important problem. Supervised PageRank is a powerful framework, and developing reliable and efficient methods to train it has clear practical significance for applications like web search and recommendation systems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Self-Containment:** The most significant weakness is the paper's heavy reliance on the supplementary materials. The main body of the paper omits crucial details, making it difficult to fully evaluate the work. Key elements such as proof sketches, detailed algorithm specifications (Algorithms 2 and 4 are referenced but absent), and a complete presentation of experimental results are relegated to the appendix. A strong paper should be largely self-contained, providing the reader with enough information to understand the contributions and be convinced of their validity without extensive reference to supplementary files.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation and Discussion:** The experimental section is too brief. While the results in Table 1 and Figure 1 suggest that the proposed methods (GFN and GBN) outperform the baseline (GBP), the paper lacks a detailed discussion of these results.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** Critical experimental parameters, such as the methodology for choosing the initial parameter vector `ϕ̂` and the radius `R` for the search space `Φ`, are not specified. This choice seems crucial to the algorithm's success and its omission hinders reproducibility.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The evaluation is conducted on a single dataset (albeit with subsets). While valuable, testing on graphs with more diverse characteristics would significantly strengthen the empirical claims.
    *   **Practical Significance:** The paper does not discuss the practical implications of the observed improvements. How does a lower loss function value translate to better search ranking quality (e.g., using metrics like NDCG)? A discussion on this point would better motivate the significance of the work.

3.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The paper makes several technical assumptions, such as the local convexity of the objective function and the constraint of the parameter space `Φ` to a Euclidean ball. The practical implications of these assumptions are not adequately discussed. For instance, constraining the search space to a small ball around a predefined `ϕ̂` might unduly restrict the model's learning capacity. A discussion of the sensitivity to these choices would be beneficial.

4.  **Writing and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit from a thorough round of proofreading to correct numerous grammatical errors and improve phrasing. For example, the citation to [21] ("Supervised Nested PageRank") is referred to simply as "Supervised PageRank," which could cause minor confusion. Polishing the language would improve readability and allow the strong technical contributions to shine through more clearly.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for a relevant and challenging learning problem.
*   The proposed optimization framework with inexact oracles is novel and rigorously analyzed.
*   Preliminary experimental results suggest the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained; essential details (proofs, full results, algorithm descriptions) are missing from the main text.
*   The experimental section is underdeveloped, lacking crucial details for reproducibility and a thorough discussion of the results' significance.
*   The practical implications of key theoretical assumptions are not discussed.

### Recommendation

This paper presents strong theoretical contributions that advance the understanding of how to learn feature-rich PageRank models. However, the current presentation significantly obscures these contributions. The paper reads more like an extended abstract than a complete conference paper due to its over-reliance on the appendix.

I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors revise the paper to be more self-contained. Specifically, I would recommend:
1.  Including concise proof sketches for the main theorems (Theorems 1-4) in the main paper.
2.  Moving a more detailed summary of the experimental results, including a table with final loss values and a discussion of the practical implications, from the appendix into Section 6.
3.  Clarifying the experimental setup, particularly the choice of `ϕ̂` and `R`.
4.  Adding a brief discussion on the validity and potential limitations of the key assumptions.

Without these changes, the paper in its current form is difficult to fully assess and does not meet the standards for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
