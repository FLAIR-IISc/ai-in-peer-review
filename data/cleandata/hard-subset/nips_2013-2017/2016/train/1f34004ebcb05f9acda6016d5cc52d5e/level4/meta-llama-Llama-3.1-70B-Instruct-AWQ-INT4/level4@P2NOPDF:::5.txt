The authors introduce the concept of PageRank and propose a framework that incorporates node and edge weights from a feature model. They highlight the limitations of a previous algorithm for learning the model's parameters, which lacks performance guarantees. To address this, they combine a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with a gradient-free optimization technique that can handle loss functions that can only be estimated.

The authors reproduce a framework from [21] under a different name and notation, discussing technical assumptions and a loss function that make the problem challenging to solve. They provide lemmas that bound the approximate gradient and outline a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle. The authors then construct an algorithm for the learning problem, adapting the gradient-free method to their specific problem and stating bounds on its performance.

Experimental results on a web graph are briefly outlined, but the detailed reporting of results and analysis are relegated to the appendix. However, some parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform other algorithms, the extent and implications of this improvement are not discussed. It is also unclear whether a diverse set of graphs were considered for testing. Furthermore, the writing is marred by grammatical errors, and citations are sloppy, with the problem being referred to as Supervised PageRank despite only semi-supervised PageRank being discussed in [21].

I have reservations about the impact of the problem solved in this paper. While the bounds presented are strong, I question whether the technical assumptions will hold in empirical data. Some assumptions, such as the restriction of the search space of phi to an R-ball around a chosen phi0, may greatly limit the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm. This should be discussed in more detail.

My primary concern with the paper is that the majority of the content is relegated to the appendix, with neither proof outlines nor full empirical results presented in the main body. The authors do not report how they chose phi0 and R in the empirical results section, nor do they provide information on whether tests were run on more than one graph. The absence of tables or specific results in the main body of the paper makes it difficult to fully understand the practical consequences of the improvements presented. While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be powerful, the content in the paper itself fails to convince me of its significance.
