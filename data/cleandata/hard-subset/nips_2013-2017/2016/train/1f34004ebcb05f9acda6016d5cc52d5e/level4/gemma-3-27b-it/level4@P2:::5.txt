This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors motivate their work by noting the lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based methods [21] and propose two-level optimization approaches—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—based on inexact oracle estimations. These methods aim to address the challenges of constrained optimization and the difficulty of calculating the exact loss function. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms and demonstrating improved ranking quality in experiments.

The paper builds upon the framework introduced in [21], which utilizes a two-level optimization strategy. The lower level focuses on approximating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, while the upper level tunes the model parameters.  The authors leverage a method from [17] for approximating this stationary distribution and develop a gradient-based method along with an adaptation of a gradient-free approach from [16] tailored for constrained optimization.  A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to detailed theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates and complexity bounds for the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, including convergence rate guarantees. This is a valuable contribution, as the existing method [21] lacked such guarantees.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of inexact oracle estimations with gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques, adapted for the specific Supervised PageRank problem, represents a novel approach.
*   **Potential Impact:** Improvements to PageRank and related ranking algorithms can have practical implications for search engines and other applications relying on graph-based ranking.
*   **Addressing a Known Limitation:** The authors directly address the known lack of convergence proofs for the existing state-of-the-art method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing contains numerous grammatical errors and can be difficult to follow. The presentation of the mathematical derivations and lemmas could benefit from improved clarity and organization.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail. Critical parameters (e.g., the selection of ϕ₀ and R) are not adequately explained, and the results are largely relegated to an appendix.  The extent and statistical significance of the performance improvements are not thoroughly discussed within the main body of the paper.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (user web browsing graph from Yandex) and it’s unclear whether the results generalize to other graph types or applications. A broader empirical evaluation would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Assumptions and Applicability:** The assumptions made regarding the problem setting, such as constraining the search space of ϕ to an R-ball, might limit the algorithm's applicability in practice. This restriction and its potential impact are not fully discussed.
*   **Citation Accuracy:** The citation of [21] for “Supervised PageRank” is inaccurate; the work primarily focuses on *semi*-supervised ranking. This suggests a potential lack of careful attention to detail in reviewing related work.
*   **Impact and Significance:** While the problem is well-defined, the paper doesn’t persuasively demonstrate the broader impact or significance of solving it. The improvements, while present, lack contextualization in terms of real-world benefits.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees.
*   Novelty of the proposed approach.
*   Demonstrated improvement in ranking quality over the existing state-of-the-art.
*   Addresses a known limitation of prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and numerous grammatical errors.
*   Insufficiently detailed empirical evaluation and analysis.
*   Limited scope of experiments.
*   Potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   Inaccurate citation.
*   Unclear demonstration of broader impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of ranking algorithms. However, the presentation is hindered by clarity issues and the empirical evaluation is insufficient to fully support the claims made. While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and detailed analysis leaves me unconvinced of the practical significance of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity of the writing, providing a more thorough and well-documented experimental evaluation, addressing the limitations of their assumptions, and ensuring the accuracy of their citations. A more compelling discussion of the practical implications of their work would also significantly strengthen the paper.  Moving key results and analyses from the appendix into the main body of the paper is critical.
