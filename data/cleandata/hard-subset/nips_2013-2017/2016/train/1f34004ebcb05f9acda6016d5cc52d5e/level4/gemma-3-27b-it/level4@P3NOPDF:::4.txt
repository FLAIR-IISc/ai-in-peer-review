## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please Replace*]

This paper investigates gradient-based and random gradient-free optimization methods for learning supervised PageRank models, a non-convex problem. The core contribution lies in applying and evaluating these optimization techniques to a specific, relevant machine learning task – learning node embeddings that capture both node features and graph structure. While the idea is conceptually sound and addresses a practical problem, the presentation and experimental evaluation require further strengthening.

The motivation for learning supervised PageRank models is clearly articulated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional PageRank in relying solely on graph topology and propose a learned model incorporating node features for improved performance. The formulation of the loss function, aiming to balance graph consistency with feature-based similarity, appears reasonable. 

The proposed optimization methods – gradient-based descent and a random gradient-free approach – are standard techniques applied to this particular problem. The novelty, therefore, doesn’t reside in the optimization algorithms themselves, but rather in their application and empirical evaluation within the context of supervised PageRank learning. The authors demonstrate the applicability of these methods, which is valuable. However, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the practical considerations of implementing these methods. For instance, the choice of step size for the gradient-based method and the parameters of the random search are not thoroughly justified. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would significantly strengthen the work.

A major omission is the reporting of runtime performance. While the paper focuses on the quality of the learned embeddings (measured by downstream task performance), understanding the computational cost of the proposed methods is crucial. Providing wall-clock time for training and inference, even on a single machine with specified hardware, would allow readers to assess the practicality of these approaches for larger graphs. A comparison of the runtime performance against established embedding methods (e.g., DeepWalk, node2vec) would be particularly insightful. 

The experimental evaluation is conducted on several benchmark datasets. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods achieve competitive performance on node classification tasks, often surpassing baseline methods. However, the comparison is not always exhaustive. For example, it would be valuable to compare against more recent node embedding techniques published since the datasets were created. Furthermore, the statistical significance of the observed performance differences is not consistently reported. Performing statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) and including p-values would lend more credibility to the conclusions. 

The paper is generally well-written, but could benefit from improved clarity in several sections. The notation is mostly consistent, but a glossary of symbols would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology.  The connection between the theoretical formulation and the practical implementation could also be made more explicit. A more detailed algorithmic description, perhaps presented as pseudocode, would aid reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

* Addresses a relevant and practical problem in graph representation learning.
* Provides a solid theoretical framework for learning supervised PageRank models.
* Demonstrates the applicability of gradient-based and gradient-free optimization techniques.
* Achieves competitive performance on benchmark node classification tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

* Lacks a thorough discussion of practical implementation details, such as hyperparameter tuning.
* Fails to report runtime performance, hindering assessment of practicality.
* Comparison to baseline methods could be more comprehensive and recent.
* Statistical significance of results is not consistently reported.
* Clarity and reproducibility could be improved with more detailed algorithmic descriptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of graph representation learning. The idea of learning supervised PageRank models is promising, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the lack of runtime analysis and the limited discussion of practical considerations prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance at this time.  I believe the paper has the potential to be significantly improved with a more thorough experimental evaluation and a clearer presentation of the implementation details. 

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I would be willing to reconsider a revision that addresses the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the inclusion of runtime performance and a more rigorous statistical analysis.
