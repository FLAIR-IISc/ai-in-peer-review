This paper addresses the non-convex loss-minimization problem associated with learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, a feature-aware graph ranking algorithm previously introduced in [21]. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in the state-of-the-art learning method for this model: its reliance on power methods for estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives provides no formal proof of convergence to a stationary point.

The core contribution of this work is a principled two-level optimization framework built upon the concept of an inexact oracle, for which the authors provide full convergence analysis. At the lower level, they employ a linearly convergent method from [17] to approximate the stationary distribution. This allows them to precisely control the approximation error for both the loss function value (a zero-order oracle) and its gradient (a first-order oracle). At the upper level, this inexact oracle is used to drive two different optimization schemes:
1.  A novel gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization, which is shown to converge.
2.  A random gradient-free method that adapts the framework of Nesterov and Spokoiny [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the objective function can only be evaluated with a known, bounded error.

The paper provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms and analyzes the computational complexity, considering the trade-off between the precision of the lower-level oracle and the overall performance. Experiments on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Quality:** The paper tackles an important theoretical gap in the training of Supervised PageRank models. Providing the first formal convergence guarantees for this problem is a significant contribution that places the learning procedure on a much more solid theoretical foundation than prior work. The analysis is rigorous and complete.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The development of optimization methods for constrained problems with inexact oracles is technically sound. The paper carefully derives complexity bounds that account for both the upper-level optimization and the lower-level oracle computations, which is a key aspect for practical application.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed two-level approach is explained logically and in sufficient detail for an expert to understand.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The theoretical contributions are supported by empirical results on a real-world dataset, which show that the proposed methods achieve better final loss values than the established baseline.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Originality:** While the application and analysis are novel for this problem, the core optimization algorithms are adaptations of existing powerful frameworks. The gradient-free approach extends the work of Nesterov and Spokoiny [16], while the gradient-based method for inexact oracles builds upon concepts from works like [7, 25]. The main novelty lies in the careful adaptation of these methods to the constrained, non-convex setting of Supervised PageRank and the corresponding integrated convergence analysis, rather than the invention of fundamentally new optimization principles.
*   **Positioning of Contribution:** The contribution could be framed more broadly to increase its perceived impact. The proposed optimization framework for constrained problems with an inexact oracle is potentially applicable to a wider class of machine learning problems beyond Supervised PageRank. Restructuring the paper to first present the general optimization algorithms and then instantiate them for the specific ranking problem might better highlight the generality and significance of the methodological contribution.
*   **Minor Point:** The manuscript would benefit from a dedicated conclusion section to summarize the key contributions, reaffirm the results, and perhaps suggest avenues for future work.
