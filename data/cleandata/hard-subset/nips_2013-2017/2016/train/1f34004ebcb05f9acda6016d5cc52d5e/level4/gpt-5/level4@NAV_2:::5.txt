The paper begins by motivating PageRank and then introduces a framework in which node and edge weights are derived from feature representations. The authors note that a prior training procedure for this model lacked convergence guarantees. They propose to address this by combining an existing, linearly convergent estimator for the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method suitable for settings where only inexact function values are available. Much of the Supervised PageRank setup is restated with altered notation relative to [21]. The paper specifies a set of technical assumptions and a squared pairwise loss, and explains why the resulting problem is challenging for standard optimization techniques. Lemmas are provided that bound the error in approximate gradients. A general random gradient-free method based on a biased sphere-smoothing oracle is presented, along with a performance theorem. Additional assumptions are introduced to instantiate the approach for the learning problem, yielding an algorithm and accompanying complexity bounds. The experimental section sketches results on a web browsing graph dataset used in related work, but the detailed analyses and results are deferred to the appendix. Several parameters are not reported; although the new method is claimed to outperform alternatives, the extent and implications of the improvements are not discussed. It is also unclear whether the evaluation covers a diverse set of graphs. The writing contains numerous grammatical issues. The citations are inconsistent; the work refers to “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], but that source appears to focus on semi-supervised PageRank. I am not persuaded that the problem addressed is impactful. While the bounds are strong, I have reservations about whether the stated technical assumptions hold on empirical data. Some assumptions appear restrictive—for example, limiting the search over ϕ to an R-ball around a chosen ϕ0 may substantially constrain effectiveness and should be discussed. My primary concern is that most substantive content is placed in the appendix: the main text provides neither proof sketches nor comprehensive empirical results. How were ϕ0 and R selected in the experiments? Were tests conducted on more than one graph? There are no tables or specific numerical outcomes reported in the main body. What are the practical consequences of the reported improvements? Although the analysis, bounds, and algorithm may be strong, the current presentation does not convince me.
