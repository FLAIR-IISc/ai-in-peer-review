Summary
The paper studies a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning parameters of a general graph-based ranking model defined via a Markov random walk. Node and edge weights depend on features, and the resulting stationary distribution underlies the ranking. Because the objective cannot be evaluated exactly, the authors propose a two-level approach. At the first level, they approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov chain using a linearly convergent method and show that the loss can be approximated to any prescribed precision. At the second level, they develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle and prove convergence to a stationary point. They adapt this method to the case where the function value can be computed only up to a known accuracy, and use it to optimize the ranking parameters.

Strengths
- Clear separation between the stochastic approximation layer (stationary distribution estimation) and the outer optimization layer.
- Convergence analysis for the inexact-oracle setting, with guarantees to stationary points in the constrained, non-convex case.
- Theoretical results appear sound; the presentation is generally clear and well organized.
- The linearly convergent inner solver enables arbitrarily precise objective approximation, which is practically important in graph-based learning where exact evaluations are infeasible.

Weaknesses and concerns about novelty
- Several core optimization elements seem to adapt known techniques, notably work on zero-order/inexact-oracle methods such as Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015). The paper would benefit from a crisper delineation of what is new versus what is inherited (e.g., assumptions, oracle models, rates).
- The supervised PageRank framework has prior art. It is not fully clear what conceptual or algorithmic advances beyond existing supervised PageRank formulations are being made here, as opposed to applying known inexact-oracle optimization tools to that setting.

Technical questions and requests for clarification
- Assumptions: Please state precisely the smoothness/regularity assumptions on the loss (e.g., Lipschitz continuity of gradients, bounded domain/constraints, properties of the projection operator), and the noise model for the inexact oracle (bias and variance, dependence on the inner solver tolerance).
- Rates and complexity: Make explicit the dependence of the outer-level convergence rate and total complexity on the target accuracy epsilon, the spectral properties of the Markov chain (e.g., spectral gap affecting linear convergence of the inner solver), and problem size. If there are trade-offs in allocating accuracy between inner and outer levels, provide a clear schedule and resulting overall complexity bound.
- Error propagation: Quantify how approximation errors from the inner level propagate to gradient and function-value errors in the outer level. If your method requires controlling both function and gradient inaccuracies, specify the required tolerances to ensure descent and convergence, and how these tolerances are enforced adaptively.
- Constraints: Clarify the treatment of constraints—do you rely on projected gradients, proximal steps, or barrier/penalty methods? How does the inexact oracle affect feasibility and the guarantees for constraint satisfaction?
- Conditions for linear convergence of the inner solver: State the conditions (e.g., ergodicity, spectral gap) under which the Markov-chain solver is linearly convergent, and how these conditions relate to feature parametrization and graph structure. If preconditioning or restart strategies are needed, please specify.

Positioning relative to prior work
- Carefully compare your inexact-oracle framework and convergence proofs to Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015) and other related zero-order or inexact-gradient methods (e.g., gradient methods with deterministic/probabilistic inexactness). Highlight concretely what is new:
  - Handling of specific constraint sets?
  - Tighter dependence on oracle accuracy?
  - A different oracle model induced by Markov chain approximations?
  - New complexity bounds or error-coupling analysis across the two levels?
- For the application to supervised PageRank, clearly articulate the differences from previous supervised PageRank algorithms (modeling choices, optimization machinery, or theoretical guarantees). If the model class or training objective is broader, emphasize this explicitly and provide a succinct side-by-side comparison.

Presentation suggestions
- Introduce the optimization problem in a general, self-contained form first (objective, constraints, oracle model), then present the graph-ranking instantiation as a special case. This will better situate the contribution within the broader optimization literature and make the novelty more apparent.
- Summarize the main theorems with their assumptions and implications up front, with proof sketches highlighting the new ideas. Move technical lemmas to the appendix.
- Add a short conclusion highlighting: (i) the main theoretical contribution, (ii) limitations (e.g., dependence on spectral gap, non-convexity), and (iii) future directions (e.g., variance reduction, adaptive inner-outer accuracy schedules, stochastic settings).

Overall assessment
The paper is well written, and the theoretical development appears correct and practically relevant for models where only approximate objective values are available. However, the current presentation makes it difficult to judge the novelty of the optimization contributions, which seem closely related to existing inexact-oracle and gradient-free methods, and the supervised PageRank setting is not entirely new. Strengthening the positioning with a clear statement of what is genuinely novel—both in the optimization framework and in the ranking application—would substantially improve the paper. I encourage the authors to add a concise conclusion, present the optimization problem in a more general context before specializing to the graph-ranking case, and make the relationship to prior work explicit and precise.
