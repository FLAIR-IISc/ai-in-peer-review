

This paper introduces two novel two-level optimization methods—gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN)—for solving the supervised PageRank problem. The key contributions lie in their theoretical guarantees and practical efficiency. Unlike prior approaches, GBN provides a gradient-based optimization scheme with a proven convergence rate that achieves a specified accuracy, while GFN ensures a guaranteed decrease in the loss function without requiring exact objective function evaluations. Both methods avoid explicit computation of large matrices, maintaining strong performance while offering convergence rate estimates. The authors demonstrate that these methods outperform state-of-the-art baselines in ranking quality, with hyperparameters clearly specified. Although datasets are not publicly available, the experimental description is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

The theoretical foundation leverages Nesterov and Nemirovski’s framework (ref:17) for lower-level optimization, with the upper-level problem addressed via the proposed methods. The convergence guarantees for both GBN and GFN are rigorously established, highlighting their advantage in scenarios where exact objective evaluation is infeasible. The paper’s structure is logical, with comprehensive methodological details and proofs relegated to the supplementary material (Sections 2–5). This organization effectively separates core contributions from technical proofs.

Two minor concerns warrant attention. First, the manuscript contains numerous symbols without a dedicated symbol table, making equation tracking challenging. For instance, the symbol $N$ (used in Line 139 and Equation 3.1) is defined only later (Line 193) as "number of steps," creating confusion. A symbol table would greatly enhance readability. Second, the experimental comparison in Section 6 focuses solely on loss function values against GBP (a baseline method), but ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) are critical for supervised PageRank. The authors should explicitly compare their methods against GBP using these ranking-oriented metrics to validate their performance claims. Additionally, since the work generalizes ref:17, direct performance comparisons with this baseline would strengthen the evaluation by quantifying the improvement over the foundational approach.

The paper’s theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated, particularly the convergence guarantees that avoid matrix inversion. The empirical results align with the theoretical claims, showing consistent gains in ranking quality. However, the experimental section would benefit from a clearer alignment between the claimed advantages (e.g., avoiding large matrix computations) and the reported runtime or scalability metrics. Including such data would further justify the method’s practicality.

Overall, this work presents a meaningful advancement in supervised PageRank optimization with rigorous theory and competitive empirical results. Addressing the minor points above—specifically adding a symbol table, expanding the experimental comparison to ranking metrics, and clarifying the link between theoretical guarantees and empirical efficiency—would significantly strengthen the paper. The contribution is novel, technically solid, and relevant to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance pending these clarifications.
