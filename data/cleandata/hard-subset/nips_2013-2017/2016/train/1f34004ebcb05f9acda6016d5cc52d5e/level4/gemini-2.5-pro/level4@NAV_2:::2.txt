This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank models by formulating it as a constrained, non-convex loss-minimization problem. The authors' primary contribution is the proposal of two distinct optimization algorithms: a random gradient-free method based on an inexact zero-order oracle, and an adaptive gradient-based method utilizing an inexact first-order oracle. A significant aspect of this work is that, unlike the state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], the proposed algorithms are accompanied by theoretical convergence rate guarantees. The paper also provides an empirical comparison of these new methods against the existing approach on a web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**

*   The main strength of the paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. It provides the first convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models, addressing a notable gap in prior work and establishing a stronger theoretical foundation for this class of ranking algorithms.
*   The development of general frameworks for constrained optimization with inexact oracles (both gradient-free and gradient-based) is a valuable contribution that could have applications beyond the specific problem of PageRank.
*   The paper claims that the proposed methods empirically outperform the state-of-the-art baseline. If substantiated by the results, this demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical developments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Completeness:** A significant weakness is that the experimental results are not included in the main text and are instead relegated to the supplementary material. A scientific paper should be self-contained, and the absence of key figures and tables supporting the central claims makes it difficult to fully evaluate the work's empirical contributions.
*   **Theoretical Assumptions:** There appears to be a disconnect between the theoretical assumptions and the problem setting. Theorem 1, which underpins the gradient-free method, requires the objective function to be convex. However, the loss function in (2.4) is explicitly stated to be non-convex. The authors suggest addressing this by choosing a set $\Phi$ where the function is locally convex, but it remains unclear how to identify such a set in practice. This assumption needs stronger justification.
*   **Clarity of Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence guarantee for the gradient-based method in Theorem 3 establishes a bound on the norm of the gradient mapping, $\|M_K(x_K - x_{K+1})\|$. While this is a standard criterion, the paper could be improved by explicitly explaining how this guarantees convergence to a stationary point for the non-convex problem.
*   **Practical Efficiency:** The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive. Based on Figure 1 (in the supplement), the gradient-free method (GFN) appears to converge slowly. The authors should consider reporting on the practical efficiency of the algorithms, for instance, by comparing wall-clock time or the total number of arithmetic operations required to reach a certain accuracy.

In summary, this paper presents a solid theoretical contribution by introducing principled optimization algorithms with convergence guarantees for an important ranking problem. However, the presentation would be significantly improved by making the paper self-contained with in-text experimental results and by providing more detailed justifications for key theoretical assumptions and their practical implications.
