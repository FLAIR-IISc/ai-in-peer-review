

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank parameters by formulating it as a non-convex optimization problem, where node and edge features are used to weight graph structure. The authors note that while Supervised PageRank [21] provides a framework for incorporating node/edge features into ranking, its existing optimization method lacks convergence guarantees. The paper proposes two two-level optimization methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, both leveraging an inexact oracle approach to handle the computational challenges of estimating the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) providing convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is lacking in the state-of-the-art [21]; (2) developing a theoretical framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles; and (3) demonstrating empirical improvements on a web ranking task. The authors analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is particularly relevant for large-scale applications.

Strengths:
- The paper makes a technically sound contribution by providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both optimization methods, which is a significant advancement over [21].
- The authors carefully analyze the complexity trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational cost, which is valuable for practical implementation.
- The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality, with statistically significant differences (p<0.005) on multiple test sets.

Weaknesses:
- The paper's main technical contributions and most of the empirical results are relegated to the supplementary materials, while the main body contains only brief mentions and lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the choice of ϕ₀ and R in the empirical section is not justified, and the paper does not report specific results or tables in the main text.
- The problem formulation claims to address "Supervised PageRank" but cites [21] which actually describes "Supervised Nested PageRank" - this inconsistency needs clarification.
- The constraint Φ = {ϕ ∈ Rᵐ : ‖ϕ - ϕ̂‖₂ ≤ R} may be too restrictive for practical applications, as the choice of ϕ̂ and R could significantly impact performance. The authors should discuss how these parameters were selected in practice and how sensitive the results are to these choices.
- The experimental section does not provide sufficient detail about the graph diversity used in testing. The paper mentions sorting queries by graph size but doesn't clarify whether the results generalize across different graph structures or are specific to the Yandex dataset.
- The paper claims to be the "first time when random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with efficient methods for huge-scale optimization using inexact oracle," but this claim needs stronger justification relative to related work [16, 7].

The writing contains grammatical errors and lacks clarity in several places, particularly in the problem formulation and technical descriptions. The references to related work are inconsistent (e.g., citing [21] for "Supervised PageRank" when it actually describes "Supervised Nested PageRank"). The paper would benefit from more precise mathematical notation and clearer explanations of the technical contributions.

While the theoretical contributions are sound and the empirical results suggest practical improvements, the lack of detailed results in the main paper and insufficient discussion of practical implementation choices significantly weaken the paper's impact. The authors should provide more information about parameter selection, include key results in the main text, and clarify the relationship between their work and prior publications.

The paper makes a technically interesting contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank, but the presentation and reporting of results fall short of NIPS standards. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for acceptance.
