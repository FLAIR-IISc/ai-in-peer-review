This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of a Supervised PageRank model, a framework where node and edge weights are determined by features. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in prior work [21]: the existing gradient-based learning algorithm lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. The central contribution of this paper is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms, one gradient-based and one random gradient-free, which come with formal proofs of their convergence rates and complexity bounds. The core idea is to treat the calculation of the PageRank stationary distribution and its derivatives as an "inexact oracle" whose accuracy can be controlled. This allows the authors to leverage and adapt general frameworks for optimization with inexact information [7, 16] to solve the constrained, non-convex learning problem. The theoretical work is complemented by an empirical comparison against the state-of-the-art method on a web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness (Quality, Originality):** The primary strength of this work is its rigorous theoretical foundation. By providing convergence guarantees for the learning problem, it addresses a significant and acknowledged gap in the state-of-the-art method [21]. The development of general frameworks for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact first-order oracles, and for constrained convex optimization with inexact zeroth-order oracles, may be of broader interest to the optimization community.
*   **Novel Application of Optimization Theory (Originality):** The paper successfully combines recent advances in optimization under inexact oracles with the specific problem of learning a graph ranking model. This combination appears to be novel and provides a principled approach to a problem that was previously handled with more heuristic methods.
*   **Empirical Validation (Significance):** The authors validate their proposed algorithms on a real-world dataset from a commercial search engine. The experimental results presented in Table 1 suggest that the new methods, particularly the gradient-free approach, can achieve a lower loss value than the existing baseline, demonstrating their practical relevance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper's readability is hampered by numerous grammatical and stylistic errors. A thorough proofreading pass by a native English speaker would significantly improve clarity.
    *   While space is limited, the complete relegation of all proofs to the supplementary material makes the main body less self-contained. Including brief proof sketches or high-level intuitions for the main theorems (e.g., Theorem 1 and Theorem 3) would help the reader better appreciate the technical contributions.
    *   The experimental section could be more detailed. The rationale for choosing specific hyperparameters (e.g., the center `em` and radius `R=0.99` for the feasible set Φ, or the initial Lipschitz constant `L0`) is not explained. Clarifying how these were selected would strengthen the reproducibility and interpretation of the results.

*   **Assumptions and Scope (Quality, Significance):**
    *   The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set Φ. However, the paper initially introduces the loss function as non-convex. The authors should more explicitly discuss the implications of this assumption. How restrictive is it in practice? Is there a way to verify or ensure this condition holds?
    *   The practical significance of the improved loss function values is not fully explored. It would be highly beneficial to report standard information retrieval metrics (e.g., NDCG) on the test set to demonstrate how the lower loss translates to tangible improvements in ranking quality.
    *   The evaluation is conducted on a single type of graph (user browsing graphs). The paper's claims would be more robust if the algorithms were tested on a more diverse set of graph ranking problems to demonstrate broader applicability.

In summary, the paper presents a strong theoretical contribution by introducing optimization methods with guaranteed convergence for learning Supervised PageRank models. However, its impact is currently limited by issues in presentation clarity and a need for a more thorough discussion of the experimental setup and the practical implications of its results.
