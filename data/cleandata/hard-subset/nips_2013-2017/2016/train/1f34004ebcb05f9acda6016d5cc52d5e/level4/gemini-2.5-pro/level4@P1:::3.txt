This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a random gradient-free method (GFN). A key contribution is that both methods are designed to work with an "inexact oracle," meaning they do not require precise calculations of the objective function or its gradient. This is a significant practical advantage for large-scale graphs where such calculations are computationally prohibitive.

Unlike the state-of-the-art method [21], the authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms. The lower-level oracle, which approximates the PageRank stationary distribution and its derivatives, is based on the efficient method of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. The paper demonstrates that the proposed methods outperform the existing baseline in terms of the final loss value on a real-world web page ranking task.

### Strengths

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. By providing convergence rate guarantees for the non-convex Supervised PageRank problem, the paper addresses a critical gap in the existing literature and places the learning process on a more solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Novel Optimization Framework:** The development of gradient-based and gradient-free methods that explicitly handle an inexact oracle is a valuable and significant contribution. This framework allows for a principled analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level computations and the overall complexity of the learning algorithm, which is highly relevant for practical applications.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments show that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free algorithm (GFN), achieve a significantly lower loss value on the test data compared to the state-of-the-art baseline. This suggests the methods are not only theoretically sound but also practically effective.
*   **Clarity of Experimental Setup:** The paper provides sufficient detail about its experimental setup and hyperparameter choices. While the dataset is proprietary, the clear description should facilitate future attempts at reproduction on other datasets.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Clarity and Notation:** The paper is mathematically dense and uses a large number of symbols, which can make it difficult for readers to follow the technical details.
    *   To improve readability, I strongly recommend adding a table of notation that defines all major symbols used throughout the paper. With numerous variables representing accuracies, parameters, and iteration counts (e.g., N, N1, N2, δ, δ1, δ2, ε, M, K), a centralized reference would be invaluable.
    *   Furthermore, some symbols are used before they are formally defined. For example, the parameter `N` appears in Equation 3.1, but its meaning as the number of steps is not clarified until much later (Section 4.2). Consistently defining symbols upon their first appearance would greatly enhance the paper's clarity.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The experiments focus on minimizing the loss function (Equation 2.4). While this is the direct objective of the optimization, the ultimate goal is to improve ranking performance. The abstract states that the new methods "outperform it in the ranking quality in experiments." To fully substantiate this claim, it would be beneficial to report results using standard information retrieval metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) on the test set, in addition to the loss function values. This would provide a more direct measure of the practical impact on ranking quality.
    *   The paper argues for the superiority of the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] over the standard power method for the lower-level oracle. While a comparison is shown in the supplementary material, the main experimental comparison is against the full GBP algorithm from [21], which differs in both its upper- and lower-level methods. To isolate and highlight the benefit of the chosen lower-level method, it would be insightful to include a direct comparison against a variant of the proposed GBN algorithm that uses the power method as its oracle.
