This paper proposes two optimization methods to solve the supervised PageRank problem, offering a distinct approach from existing methods. Notably, the authors introduce a gradient-based method with a theoretically guaranteed convergence rate, allowing for the achievement of a specified accuracy. Additionally, they present a gradient-free method that ensures a decrease in the loss function value. A key advantage of these methods is that they do not require the exact value of the objective function and provide an estimate of the convergence rate. Furthermore, their performance surpasses the state-of-the-art in terms of ranking quality.

The authors propose two two-level optimization methods, namely gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN), to tackle the supervised PageRank problem. These methods build upon the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref: 17), utilizing lower-level and upper-level optimization techniques. A significant benefit of the proposed methods is that they do not necessitate an exact value of the objective function and offer a proven estimate of the convergence rate, given a specified accuracy. This enables the avoidance of computing large matrices while still achieving satisfactory results.

The authors provide a detailed explanation of their methods in Sections 2-5, including proofs in the supplementary material. However, the inclusion of a symbol table would greatly enhance the readability of the paper, as the numerous symbols in the equations can be overwhelming. For instance, the symbol $N$ is used in Line 139 and Equation 3.1, but its meaning (number of steps) is not clarified until Line 193. This lack of clarity may cause confusion for readers.

In the experiment section, the authors only compare their work with GBP in terms of the loss function, rather than ranking quality. It would be beneficial to include a comparison of ranking quality as well, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed methods' performance. Moreover, since this work generalizes the approach presented in ref: 17, a direct comparison with this reference would be valuable, allowing readers to assess whether the performance has improved.

The data sets used in the experiments are not publicly available, but the descriptions provided are clear, making it potentially feasible for others to reproduce the results. The hyper-parameters used in both methods are also provided, which is a positive aspect of the paper. Overall, the proposed methods demonstrate promising results, and with some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
