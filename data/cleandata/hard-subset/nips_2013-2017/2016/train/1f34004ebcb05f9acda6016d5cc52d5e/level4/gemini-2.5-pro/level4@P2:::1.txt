This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking model where node and edge weights are parameterized functions of their features. The core challenge is that this leads to a non-convex loss-minimization problem where the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly. The authors propose tackling this with a two-level optimization scheme.

**Summary of Contributions**

The paper's main contribution is to develop and analyze two optimization algorithms for this problem that, unlike the previous state-of-the-art method, come with theoretical convergence guarantees. The proposed methods operate in a two-level framework:
1.  **Lower Level (Inexact Oracle):** At this level, the algorithm computes an approximation of the PageRank stationary distribution and its derivatives. The authors analyze several methods and select a linearly convergent one [17] that allows them to control the approximation error with a known bound. This provides a formal "inexact oracle" for the upper-level optimizer.
2.  **Upper Level (Parameter Update):** The authors propose two methods for this level:
    *   A **random gradient-free method** that adapts the framework of Nesterov and Spokoiny [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is only available with a known error bound.
    *   A **gradient-based method** for general constrained non-convex problems that also operates with an inexact first-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors provide a formal analysis of the convergence rate and the overall computational complexity, linking the precision required at the lower level to the number of iterations needed at the upper level. The methods are validated experimentally on a web page ranking task, where they are shown to outperform the existing gradient-based approach for Supervised PageRank.

**Strengths**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength of this work is its formal treatment of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. By framing it within the context of optimization with an inexact oracle, the authors provide the first convergence guarantees for this problem. This is a significant improvement over the prior state-of-the-art [21], which lacked such proofs.
*   **Methodological Contribution:** The paper presents clear frameworks for both gradient-free and gradient-based optimization in a constrained setting with inexact information. While based on existing ideas, the explicit adaptation and analysis for this setting are valuable and could potentially be applied to other machine learning problems where exact objective/gradient calculations are prohibitive.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The separation of the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to Supervised PageRank makes the contributions easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods are not only theoretically sound but also practically effective, achieving better loss values on test data compared to the established baseline.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Originality:** My main concern lies with the originality of the core optimization techniques. The proposed gradient-free and gradient-based methods are presented as adaptations and extensions of well-known prior work (e.g., [16], [7]). The novelty is primarily in applying and analyzing these methods for the specific constrained, inexact oracle setting dictated by the Supervised PageRank problem. While this is a valid contribution, it is more of an expert-level application and extension rather than the development of fundamentally new optimization principles.
*   **Framing of Contribution:** The contribution could be positioned more effectively. The paper focuses heavily on the Supervised PageRank application, but the developed optimization frameworks in Sections 4.1 and 5.1 are more general. Highlighting their broader applicability could strengthen the paper's significance.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   I recommend adding a conclusion section. The paper ends abruptly after the experiments. A conclusion would provide an opportunity to summarize the key findings, reiterate the importance of convergence guarantees in this domain, and suggest avenues for future research.
*   To better highlight the paper's contribution, I suggest reframing parts of the introduction and the proposed methods sections. By presenting the optimization problem in a more general context first, and then showing Supervised PageRank as a key motivating application, the authors could better emphasize the broader utility of their theoretical analysis. This would help distinguish the work from a purely application-focused paper.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Provides the first formal convergence analysis for learning Supervised PageRank, a significant theoretical gap in prior work.
    *   The proposed methods are shown to outperform the existing state-of-the-art empirically.
    *   The paper is technically sound, clear, and well-executed.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The core optimization algorithms are adaptations of existing methods, which may limit the paper's perceived novelty from a pure optimization perspective.

Overall, I find the paper to be a solid contribution. The rigorous theoretical analysis and strong empirical results for an established problem are valuable. While the novelty in terms of fundamental algorithm design is limited, the work of formally connecting modern optimization theory with inexact oracles to a practical and challenging learning problem is a worthwhile contribution for the NIPS community. I lean towards acceptance.
