### **Summary**

This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into the PageRank algorithm. The core difficulty is that the loss function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly, a setting the authors frame as optimization with an "inexact oracle." The main contributions are the proposal and analysis of two two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method and a random gradient-free method.

Building on prior work in optimization theory, the authors adapt a gradient method [7] for constrained non-convex problems and a random gradient-free method [16] for constrained problems with function value errors. Crucially, and in contrast to the previous state-of-the-art method for Supervised PageRank [21], this work provides the first theoretical convergence rate guarantees for this learning problem. The authors analyze the complexity of their methods, considering the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (which computes the stationary distribution) and the number of upper-level optimization steps. Experiments on a large-scale web ranking dataset demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the existing baseline in terms of final loss function value.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical contribution. It is the first work to provide formal convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models. By framing the problem in terms of an inexact oracle and carefully applying and adapting established optimization frameworks, the authors add a much-needed layer of theoretical justification to what was previously a more heuristic optimization procedure [21]. The derived complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), which explicitly connect the upper-level convergence to the lower-level approximation accuracy, are a significant and novel result in this specific application domain.

2.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** Supervised PageRank is an important and practical model for ranking on graphs. The development of more reliable and provably convergent optimization algorithms is a valuable contribution to the field. The experimental results, conducted on a real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, are compelling. They show that both proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free one, can achieve a better final ranking quality (lower loss) than the previously used method. This demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed algorithms.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and logically organized. It clearly introduces the Supervised PageRank model, defines the optimization problem, discusses the challenges of the inexact oracle, presents the general optimization frameworks, and then applies them to the specific problem. This clear separation makes the paper's contributions easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** While the experiments effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods in terms of final loss value, they lack a crucial element for practical assessment: runtime comparison. The paper provides theoretical complexity in terms of arithmetic operations, but a comparison of wall-clock time is essential to understand the practical trade-offs. For example, the gradient-free method (GFN) avoids costly derivative calculations but may require more iterations than the gradient-based method (GBN). Without timing results, it is difficult to assess the overall practical efficiency of GFN, GBN, and the baseline GBP.

2.  **Theoretical Assumptions:** The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within the optimization domain Φ. However, the overall Supervised PageRank loss function is stated to be non-convex. The authors acknowledge this by assuming they are operating in a small vicinity of a local minimum where convexity holds. While this is a common technique in optimization analysis, it represents a gap between the provided theory and the general non-convex problem. This limitation could be highlighted more explicitly.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Reporting Runtime:** I strongly recommend that the authors add wall-clock timings to their experimental results in Table 1. This would provide a much more complete picture and allow readers to evaluate the trade-off between computational cost and solution quality for each method.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The gradient-free method requires setting a Lipschitz constant `L`. The paper sets `L = 10^-4` but does not discuss how this value was chosen or how sensitive the algorithm's performance is to this choice. A brief discussion on this, or an ablation study in the supplementary material, would strengthen the experimental section. One of the stated advantages of the adaptive gradient-based method is that it does not require `L` to be known, and it would be useful to reinforce this point by discussing the challenges of setting `L` for the gradient-free method.

*   **Clarity of Notation:** The paper introduces a large number of parameters related to accuracy and complexity (e.g., ε, δ, N, N1, N2, M, L). While the context generally makes their meaning clear, the density of notation can be challenging. The authors might consider adding a small table summarizing these key parameters and their roles to improve readability.

*   **Minor Typo in Section 2:** The sentence "We concider the following random walk..." should be "We consider...".

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a solid contribution by providing the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for the important problem of learning Supervised PageRank. The theoretical analysis is the main highlight, and the positive experimental results on a real-world task validate the approach. Addressing the weakness in the experimental evaluation by including runtime comparisons would make this an even stronger and more complete work.
