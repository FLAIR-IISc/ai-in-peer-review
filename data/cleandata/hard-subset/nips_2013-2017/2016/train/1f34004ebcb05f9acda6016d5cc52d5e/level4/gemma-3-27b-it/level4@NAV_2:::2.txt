This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, formulating it as a constrained non-convex optimization problem that incorporates node and edge properties. The authors propose two methods for solving this problem: a gradient-free method leveraging an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method employing an inexact first-order oracle. A key contribution is providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, unlike prior work [21]. The paper concludes with experimental comparisons to state-of-the-art techniques on a web page ranking task.

The core idea of extending PageRank to incorporate node and edge features is well-motivated, building upon established work in random walk models for graph analysis [18, 11, 8, 9, 19] and previous attempts to integrate feature information into restart and transition probabilities [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21, 22]. The two-level optimization framework mirrors that of Supervised PageRank [21], but crucially, the authors address the lack of convergence guarantees in the original method. The introduction of inexact oracle methods and theoretical analysis of their convergence rates is a significant contribution, particularly in the context of large-scale graph problems where exact computations are intractable. The development of both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches provides flexibility and allows for a trade-off between computational cost and convergence properties.

Strengths of the paper include the rigorous theoretical analysis, providing convergence guarantees for both proposed methods, and the consideration of practical issues such as the accuracy of lower-level estimations and its impact on overall algorithm complexity. The adaptation of existing optimization techniques [16, 17] to the constrained, inexact setting demonstrates a solid understanding of the relevant literature. The experimental evaluation, while relegated to the supplementary material, demonstrates promising performance improvements over the state-of-the-art [21].

Weaknesses of the paper primarily concern presentation and completeness. The most significant issue is the placement of experimental results solely in the supplementary material, rendering the main text incomplete and hindering a comprehensive assessment of the work’s practical impact.  The assumption of convexity in Theorem 1 appears to conflict with the stated non-convexity of the problem (2.4); clarification regarding the choice of the feasible set Φ to ensure local convexity is needed. Additionally, while Theorem 3 provides a bound on the norm of the difference between successive iterates, it doesn't explicitly establish convergence to a stationary point – a connection would strengthen the result. Finally, the slow convergence observed for the gradient-free method in Figure 1 (supplementary material) warrants further discussion regarding its practical efficiency and potential mitigation strategies. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of inexact oracle methods to Supervised PageRank learning.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees for both proposed methods.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over the state-of-the-art in experiments (though relegated to supplementary material).
*   Addresses a key limitation of previous work [21] by providing convergence analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are only presented in the supplementary material, making the paper feel incomplete.
*   The assumption of convexity in Theorem 1 requires justification given the non-convex nature of the overall problem.
*   The convergence analysis in Theorem 3 could be strengthened by explicitly demonstrating convergence to a stationary point.
*   Slow convergence of the gradient-free method raises concerns about its practical efficiency.
