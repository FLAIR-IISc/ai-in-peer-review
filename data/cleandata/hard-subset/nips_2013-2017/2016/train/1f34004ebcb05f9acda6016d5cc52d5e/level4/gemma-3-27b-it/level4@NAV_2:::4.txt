This paper proposes gradient-based and gradient-free methods for solving a non-convex loss-minimization problem in the context of learning Supervised PageRank models. The core idea is to leverage inexact oracle information to develop algorithms with theoretical convergence guarantees, a feature lacking in the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach [21]. The paper addresses a significant problem in ranking and information retrieval, building upon the foundations of PageRank [18], HITS [11], and related work that incorporates node and edge features [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21]. The problem formulation, based on Supervised PageRank [21], involves a two-level optimization process, where the authors aim to improve upon existing methods by providing convergence rate guarantees.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical contributions. The authors successfully demonstrate convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a notable improvement over the existing literature [21]. The use of an inexact oracle is a clever approach to address the challenges of solving this non-convex problem, and the analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity is valuable. Furthermore, the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in a real-world ranking task. The detailed complexity analysis provided throughout the paper and in the supplementary materials is commendable.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the convergence analysis is strong, the practical implications of the theoretical convergence rates are not fully explored. The experiments, while demonstrating superior performance, could be more extensive. A comparison of the runtime performance of the proposed methods against the state-of-the-art would be valuable, as noted by the authors themselves. The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow at times, even for readers familiar with the field. The reliance on supplementary materials for proofs and detailed explanations, while common, makes it harder to quickly assess the full impact of the work. Moreover, the discussion of the choice of parameters (e.g., L in the gradient-free method) could be more detailed; while sensitivity analysis is mentioned, it is relegated to supplementary material.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees for convergence of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
*   Empirically demonstrated improvement over the state-of-the-art [21] on a relevant ranking task.
*   Addresses a challenging non-convex optimization problem with practical applications.
*   Detailed complexity analysis and thoughtful discussion of trade-offs.
*   Novel application of inexact oracle concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of the practical implications of the theoretical convergence rates.
*   Experiments could be more comprehensive, including runtime comparisons.
*   Dense notation and reliance on supplementary materials can hinder readability.
*   Parameter selection discussion could be expanded within the main text.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical results are strong, and the experimental validation is promising. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the points raised regarding runtime performance, notation clarity, and parameter selection in a revised version.
