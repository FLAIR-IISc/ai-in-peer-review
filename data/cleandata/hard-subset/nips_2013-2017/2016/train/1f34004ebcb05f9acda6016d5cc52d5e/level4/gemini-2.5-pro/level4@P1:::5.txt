This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a framework that incorporates node and edge features into the standard PageRank model. The authors correctly identify a significant limitation in prior work [21], namely that the existing learning algorithm lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. The central contribution of this work is to provide two new optimization methods—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—that come with formal performance guarantees. These methods are designed to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem by treating the calculation of the loss function and its gradient as an inexact oracle. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates and complexity bounds for both proposed algorithms.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical treatment of the learning problem. By developing optimization algorithms that provably converge, the authors place the training of Supervised PageRank models on a much firmer theoretical foundation than previous work. The formal analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the inexact oracle (i.e., the precision of the stationary distribution estimate) and the overall computational complexity of the learning algorithm is a valuable contribution. The theoretical results and the proposed algorithms appear to be sound.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper has several weaknesses in its presentation and evaluation that undermine its overall impact.

1.  **Clarity and Completeness:** My main criticism is that the most essential content needed to evaluate the paper's claims—namely, the proofs and the detailed empirical results—has been relegated to the supplementary materials. The main body of the paper does not provide proof sketches, detailed experimental tables, or sufficient analysis to be self-contained. A reader should be able to understand and feel convinced by the paper's core contributions without having to rely entirely on the appendix.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is too brief to be persuasive.
    *   **Missing Details:** Several crucial hyperparameters are not specified. For instance, the paper constrains the parameter search space to a Euclidean ball defined by a center `ϕ̂` and a radius `R`. The choice of these parameters is critical to the algorithm's performance, yet the paper does not explain how they were selected for the experiments.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The evaluation appears to be based on a single type of user browsing graph from one data source. To demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed methods, the evaluation should include a more diverse set of graphs with varying characteristics.
    *   **Insufficient Reporting:** The paper claims that the proposed algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art, but it fails to present any specific results (e.g., tables or detailed figures) in the main text to substantiate this. The magnitude of the improvement is not quantified, and there is no discussion of the practical consequences. For example, how does the observed reduction in the loss function translate to a meaningful improvement in search ranking quality?

3.  **Validity of Assumptions and Practical Limitations:** The analysis rests on several technical assumptions whose validity in real-world scenarios is not discussed or justified.
    *   For instance, the convergence analysis for the gradient-free method assumes local convexity of the loss function. It is unclear how often this assumption holds for this type of problem.
    *   More importantly, the constraint that the parameter vector `ϕ` must lie within a predefined ball seems highly restrictive. This could limit the model's ability to find the true optimal parameters, thereby reducing its practical efficacy. A discussion of this limitation and its potential impact is warranted.

4.  **Originality and Citations:** The problem formulation appears to be a direct reimplementation of the "Supervised Nested PageRank" framework from [21]. The paper could do a better job of positioning its contribution relative to this prior work. Furthermore, there are minor inaccuracies in the citations; for example, the paper consistently refers to the problem as "Supervised PageRank" while citing [21], which is titled "Supervised Nested PageRank." While related, greater precision would improve the paper's scholarly quality.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the paper presents a potentially powerful set of algorithms with strong theoretical foundations. However, as it stands, the manuscript fails to convince me of the practical significance and robustness of its contributions. The lack of detailed results and methodological descriptions in the main text, combined with the un-discussed limitations of the core assumptions, makes the work difficult to fully assess. To be acceptable for publication, the paper would need to be substantially revised to integrate the key experimental results and provide a more thorough discussion of the experimental setup and the practical implications of its assumptions.
