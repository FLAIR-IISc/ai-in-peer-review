This paper presents a convergence analysis for a non-convex loss-minimization problem that arises in learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk on a graph where the transition probabilities (weights on nodes and edges) are themselves parameterized by features. A key challenge in this setting is that the objective function, which depends on the stationary distribution of the random walk, cannot be computed exactly, precluding the use of standard optimization methods.

To address this, the authors propose a two-level optimization framework. At the inner level, a linearly convergent method is used to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. The authors show that this allows the loss function to be estimated with any given precision. At the outer level, they develop a gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization that is designed to work with an inexact oracle—in this case, the approximated objective function and its gradient from the inner level. The paper's main theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence for this method to a stationary point of the problem.

The paper is well-written, and the problem it tackles is significant. The theoretical analysis appears sound, and the proofs, while not verified in full detail, seem correct. The primary contribution lies in adapting and proving the convergence of a gradient method for constrained non-convex problems where the objective function and its gradients are only available with a certain known precision. This is a valuable contribution for a class of problems where exact function evaluations are computationally prohibitive.

My main concern, however, relates to the novelty of the paper's core components. The optimization techniques appear to be a direct adaptation of the framework for random gradient-free minimization proposed by Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015). Furthermore, the supervised PageRank algorithm, which serves as the primary application, has also been proposed in previous work. The paper would be significantly strengthened if it could more clearly articulate its unique contributions in light of this prior art. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the theoretical hurdles involved in extending the existing frameworks to the constrained, non-convex setting with an inexact oracle and how their analysis overcomes them.

To better position the contribution, I would suggest presenting the optimization problem in a more general context first, showing that the specific graph-ranking algorithm is an instance of this broader problem class. This would help clarify the paper's placement with respect to the state-of-the-art in optimization theory and better highlight the generality of the proposed method.

On a minor note, the paper currently lacks a conclusion. Given that there appears to be sufficient space, adding a conclusion to summarize the key findings, discuss limitations, and outline potential avenues for future research would improve the paper's completeness and impact.
