This paper provides a convergence analysis for a non-convex loss-minimization problem in the context of learning parameters for a general graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk, where the weights of nodes and edges are determined by random walks based on node and edge features. The optimization problem at hand cannot be solved using existing methods, which require exact values of the objective function.

To address this challenge, the authors propose a two-level approach. At the first level, they employ a linearly convergent method to estimate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. This approach is validated through various means, and the authors demonstrate that the value of the loss function can be approximated with any desired precision. Furthermore, they develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems using an inexact oracle, and prove its convergence to the stationary point of the problem.

The primary contribution of this work lies in adapting this approach to the case of constrained optimization problems, where the value of the function can be calculated with known precision. The authors prove the convergence of this method and exploit it on the second level of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be correct, although I did not thoroughly examine each one. My main concern is that the key optimization insights presented here are adaptations of those proposed in "Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015, pp. 1–40". Additionally, the supervised PageRank algorithm has been previously proposed.

On a minor note, a conclusion section would be beneficial, especially given the available space. I would suggest presenting the optimization problem in a more general context, positioning the studied algorithm as a specific case. This would allow for a clearer placement of the contribution within the state-of-the-art landscape, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the work's significance.

Overall, the paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed approach, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in solving the non-convex loss-minimization problem. However, the novelty of the work could be more clearly highlighted by situating it within the broader context of optimization research.
