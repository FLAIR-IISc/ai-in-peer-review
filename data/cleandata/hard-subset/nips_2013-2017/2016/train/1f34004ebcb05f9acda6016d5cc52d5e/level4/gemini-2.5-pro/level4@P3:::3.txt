### Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models. The core challenge is that the objective function and its gradients are computationally expensive and generally impossible to calculate exactly. The authors propose two two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a random gradient-free method (GFN).

Both methods are built upon the concept of an "inexact oracle," which allows the optimization algorithms to work with approximations of the loss function and its gradient. On the lower level, the authors employ an efficient method by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] to approximate the PageRank stationary distribution and its derivatives to a controllable accuracy. On the upper level, they develop and adapt optimization schemes that can leverage this inexact information.

The key contribution is providing, for the first time, theoretical convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The proposed gradient-based method is guaranteed to converge to a stationary point, while the gradient-free method is shown to achieve a guaranteed decrease in the loss function value. This is a significant advance over the previous state-of-the-art method [21], which lacked such theoretical guarantees. Experiments on a real-world web page ranking task show that the proposed methods outperform the baseline in terms of the final loss function value.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical contribution. The authors successfully develop a framework for solving a constrained, non-convex optimization problem using an inexact oracle. By providing convergence rate analysis for both a gradient-based and a gradient-free method, they place the learning of Supervised PageRank on a much firmer theoretical footing than previous work. The adaptation of general optimization frameworks [7, 16] to this specific problem is novel and well-executed.

2.  **Practical Significance:** The proposed methods are practically well-motivated. By avoiding the need for exact calculations of the PageRank vector and its derivatives, the algorithms are more scalable and efficient for large graphs. The ability to control the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall computational complexity is a valuable feature.

3.  **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete piece of work, covering theoretical development (Sections 4.1 and 5.1), specific application to the learning problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2), complexity analysis (Theorems 2 and 4), and experimental validation (Section 6). The authors are careful to state their assumptions and provide detailed proofs in the supplementary material.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is technically dense and introduces a large number of mathematical symbols, which can make it challenging to follow.
    *   **Suggestion:** The manuscript's clarity would be significantly enhanced by the inclusion of a symbol table. It is easy for a reader to lose track of the many variables introduced across different sections.
    *   **Suggestion:** Care should be taken to define variables before they are used. For example, the parameter `N` appears in Equation 3.1 but its meaning as the "number of steps" is not explicitly stated until much later (Section 4.2, line 193). Defining such terms upon their first appearance would improve the flow.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Suggestion:** The experiments in Table 1 and Figure 1 compare the methods based on the final value of the loss function. While this is a valid comparison for an optimization paper, the ultimate goal of a ranking model is to improve *ranking quality*. The abstract claims the methods "outperform it in the ranking quality," but this is not directly substantiated with standard ranking metrics like NDCG or MAP. Including such metrics would provide a more compelling demonstration of the practical impact of the improved optimization.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper builds on the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] for the lower-level computation. The authors state at the end of Section 6.2 that this method converges faster than the power method used by the baseline. It would strengthen the paper to present a more direct and detailed comparison of the efficiency of these lower-level methods (e.g., a plot of error vs. computation time), as this is a key component of the overall algorithm's performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art.
*   The novel application of inexact oracle optimization methods to this problem is a solid contribution to the field.
*   Experimental results, though limited to the loss function, demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over the existing baseline.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The paper's clarity suffers from a high density of notation, which could be improved with better organization and a symbol table.
*   The experimental evaluation would be much stronger if it included standard ranking metrics to directly support the claims of improved ranking quality.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical contribution. The authors address a practical problem with rigorous and novel optimization techniques. While there is room for improvement in clarity and the breadth of the experimental evaluation, the strengths of the paper—particularly the novel convergence guarantees—outweigh the weaknesses. This work is a valuable addition to the literature on large-scale learning on graphs and optimization. I recommend acceptance.
