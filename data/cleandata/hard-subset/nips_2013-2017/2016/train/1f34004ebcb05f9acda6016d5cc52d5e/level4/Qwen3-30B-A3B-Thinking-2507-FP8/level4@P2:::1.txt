

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models via non-convex loss minimization, where node and edge weights are linear combinations of features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free method, both leveraging an inexact oracle framework to handle the computational challenges of estimating stationary distributions and derivatives. Unlike prior work [21], which lacks convergence guarantees due to imprecise lower-level approximations, the proposed methods provide theoretically sound convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free settings. The key innovation lies in adapting Nesterov & Spokoiny's (2015) gradient-free framework to constrained optimization with known function approximation accuracy, while developing a constrained non-convex gradient method with inexact oracle guarantees. Theoretical contributions include complexity bounds for the two-level algorithms and a thorough analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational cost.

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Significance*: The paper makes a strong technical contribution by resolving a critical gap in Supervised PageRank optimization. The convergence proofs for both algorithms (Theorems 1–4) are rigorous and address the core limitation of prior work [21]. The experimental validation on a real-world ranking task (Yandex web browsing graphs) demonstrates consistent performance gains over the state-of-the-art (Table 1, Figure 1), with statistically significant improvements (p < 0.005). The analysis of computational complexity (Theorems 2–4) provides actionable insights for practitioners, showing how lower-level accuracy choices impact overall efficiency. The problem formulation is well-motivated by the limitations of structure-only graph models (e.g., standard PageRank), and the experiments convincingly show that incorporating node/edge features improves ranking quality.  

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The two-level framework is introduced logically, with precise definitions of the inexact oracle (Lemmas 1–2) and the optimization algorithms (Algorithms 2, 4). The experimental setup (Section 6.1) is transparent, and the results are presented with appropriate statistical validation. The supplementary materials provide sufficient technical depth for reproducibility.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Originality*: While the theoretical framework builds on Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), the paper makes non-trivial extensions to constrained optimization and the specific Supervised PageRank problem. However, the connection to prior NIPS work on similar optimization techniques (e.g., Ghadimi & Lan, NIPS 2013; Gasnikov et al., NIPS 2014) is not sufficiently contrasted. The paper should explicitly clarify how the constrained setting and application to Supervised PageRank differentiate it from existing inexact oracle methods. The Supervised PageRank model itself (Zhukovskii et al., NIPS 2016) is correctly cited, but the novelty of *applying* optimization methods with convergence guarantees to this model requires stronger emphasis.  

*Minor Issues*: The conclusion section is underdeveloped (missing a concise summary of contributions and implications), which is surprising given the paper’s technical depth. The draft review’s phrasing about "adaptations" of Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015) is misleading; the paper extends their framework to constrained optimization and a specific application, which is a meaningful contribution. The claim that "the main optimization points are adaptations" overlooks the novel complexity analysis and constrained optimization treatment.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory and practice of graph-based ranking. The theoretical guarantees fill a critical gap in Supervised PageRank learning, and the empirical results validate the approach. The paper meets all NIPS criteria:  
- *Quality*: Rigorous proofs, sound experiments, and careful treatment of limitations.  
- *Clarity*: Exceptional organization and exposition.  
- *Originality*: Novel application of inexact oracle methods to Supervised PageRank with constrained optimization extensions (distinct from prior NIPS work on similar optimization techniques).  
- *Significance*: Provides a reliable, theoretically grounded method for ranking systems, with clear potential for improving search engines (Section 7).  

**Recommendation**  
Accept. The paper’s theoretical advances and empirical validation outweigh the minor concerns about novelty framing and conclusion depth. The authors should address the originality point by explicitly contrasting their constrained optimization framework with prior inexact oracle work (e.g., Ghadimi & Lan, NIPS 2013) and add a brief conclusion summarizing the key contributions. As is, the paper is ready for publication at NIPS.
