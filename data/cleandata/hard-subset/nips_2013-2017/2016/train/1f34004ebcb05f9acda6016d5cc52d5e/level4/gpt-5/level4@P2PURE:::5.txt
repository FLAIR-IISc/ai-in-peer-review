The authors motivate PageRank and introduce a framework in which node and edge weights are derived from a feature model. They note that a prior algorithm for learning the model’s parameters lacked performance guarantees. They propose to address this by combining an existing method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method suited to settings where the loss can only be estimated. They essentially reproduce the framework of [21], but under a different name and notation.

The paper lays out several technical assumptions and defines a loss function, arguing that the learning problem is challenging. It states lemmas that bound the approximate gradient. A general gradient-free method is discussed that relies on a biased gradient-free oracle, and a performance theorem is provided. The authors then introduce additional assumptions, construct an algorithm for the learning problem, and adapt the gradient-free methods to their setting, giving further bounds.

The experimental section briefly describes results on a web graph previously used in related work, but detailed results and analysis are deferred to the appendix. Some parameters are not reported. Although the authors claim their new algorithm outperforms others, they do not discuss the magnitude or implications of the improvement. It is also unclear whether a diverse set of graphs was evaluated.

The writing contains numerous grammatical errors. Citations are sloppy: the problem is labeled “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], yet [21] only discusses semi-supervised PageRank. I am not convinced of the impact of the problem addressed. While the bounds appear strong, I have doubts that the stated technical assumptions will hold on empirical data. Some assumptions also seem restrictive—for example, constraining the search space of phi to an R-ball around a chosen phi_0 may significantly limit the algorithm’s efficacy and applicability, and this should be discussed.

My biggest concern is that most of the substantive content is relegated to the appendix. The main body provides neither proof outlines nor full empirical results. How were phi_0 and R selected in the experiments? Were tests conducted on more than one graph? No tables or specific quantitative results are reported in the main text. What are the practical consequences of the reported improvements? Although the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be strong, the presentation in the paper does not persuade me.
