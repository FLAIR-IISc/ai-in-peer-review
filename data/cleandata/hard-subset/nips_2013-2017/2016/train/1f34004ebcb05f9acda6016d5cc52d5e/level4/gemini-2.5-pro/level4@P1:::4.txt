This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, which involves solving a non-convex loss-minimization problem. The core challenge is that the loss function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly, as they depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain. The authors propose two two-level optimization algorithms to tackle this: a gradient-based method and a random gradient-free method. Both algorithms are designed to work with an "inexact oracle"—that is, they use numerical methods that approximate the loss function and its gradient to a controllable degree of accuracy.

The main contribution of this work is providing, for the first time, theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods in the context of learning Supervised PageRank. This is a significant advance over the previous state-of-the-art method [21], which was also gradient-based but lacked a convergence proof due to the use of an inexact oracle. The authors adapt existing general optimization frameworks ([7], [16]) to this specific constrained, non-convex problem setting and analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall computational complexity of the learning algorithm. The methods are evaluated empirically on a real-world web page ranking task, demonstrating improved performance over the existing baseline.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness (Quality & Originality):** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical analysis. It formally addresses the issue of using an inexact oracle for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization of the Supervised PageRank objective. Providing explicit convergence rate guarantees and complexity bounds (Theorems 1-4) for this problem is a novel and important contribution that fills a critical gap left by prior work. The adaptation of general optimization frameworks to this specific problem is non-trivial and well-executed.

2.  **Empirical Validation (Significance):** The authors validate their proposed algorithms on a large-scale, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine. The experiments convincingly show that both the gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach (GBP) in terms of the final loss function value achieved on test data (Table 1). This demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and structured. It clearly motivates the problem, describes the Supervised PageRank model, details the general frameworks for the optimization methods, and then applies them to the specific learning task. The distinction between the upper-level optimization and the lower-level oracle is well-explained.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Missing Computational Cost Comparison:** The most significant weakness of the experimental evaluation is the absence of a comparison of computational costs. While the paper provides theoretical complexity bounds, it does not report practical metrics like wall-clock training time or the total number of arithmetic operations. This makes it difficult to assess the real-world trade-offs between the methods. For example, do the proposed GBN and GFN methods achieve a better loss value at the cost of a significantly longer runtime compared to the baseline GBP? Including a comparison of training times until convergence would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and provide practitioners with crucial information for choosing an appropriate algorithm.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Protocol:** The convergence plot in Figure 1 shows the loss function on the test set (`Q32`) at each iteration. This is unconventional. Typically, convergence is shown on the training set to monitor the optimization process, while the test set is used only for a final evaluation to report generalization performance. Plotting performance on the test set during training can raise concerns about whether the test set was used for model selection (e.g., for determining the number of iterations). The authors should clarify this procedure or, preferably, plot training loss over iterations and report final test performance separately, as they do in Table 1.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the chosen values for the Lipschitz constant `L` and accuracy `ε` were selected based on experiments, but a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity to these hyperparameters would be beneficial. For instance, Figure 2 in the supplement touches on the effect of `L` for the GFN method; incorporating a summary of this sensitivity analysis into the main paper would be valuable for reproducibility and practical application.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a solid theoretical and practical contribution to the problem of learning feature-based ranking models on graphs. The introduction of optimization methods with formal convergence guarantees is a significant step forward for Supervised PageRank. The primary suggestions for improvement relate to strengthening the experimental evaluation by including a practical comparison of computational efficiency and clarifying the reporting protocol.
