This paper investigates the convergence of optimization algorithms for learning parameters in a supervised PageRank model, a graph-based ranking approach where node and edge weights are determined by their features via a random walk process. The core challenge addressed is the non-convexity of the loss function and the difficulty of obtaining exact solutions, leading the authors to propose a two-level optimization scheme. The lower level employs a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, guaranteeing a user-defined level of precision. Building upon this, the authors develop a gradient method tailored for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle, proving its convergence to a stationary point. A key contribution lies in adapting this approach to handle constrained problems where function values are calculated with known accuracy, demonstrating convergence and applying it to the upper level of their algorithm. The paper is clearly written and the provided proofs appear sound, though a complete verification was not performed. 

A primary concern is that much of the optimization methodology builds heavily upon existing work, particularly “Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015.”  Furthermore, the foundation of supervised PageRank itself has been previously established.  While the authors demonstrate convergence guarantees and present empirical results, the novelty of the proposed methods relative to existing literature could be more clearly articulated. 

The experimental results demonstrate improvements over the state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task, which is a valuable contribution. However, a concluding section summarizing the key findings and potential future directions is missing and would enhance the paper’s completeness. 

To strengthen the paper's positioning within the field, I suggest framing the optimization problem in a more general context. Demonstrating how the proposed algorithm represents a specific case within a broader class of optimization techniques would better highlight its contribution relative to existing methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in graph-based ranking.
*   The proposed algorithms have theoretical convergence guarantees, which is a significant strength.
*   Empirical results show improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The core optimization techniques are largely adaptations of existing work.
*   The supervised PageRank framework itself is not novel.
*   The paper lacks a concluding section summarizing results and future work.
*   The novelty relative to Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015) isn’t fully elucidated.
