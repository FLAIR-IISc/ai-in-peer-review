Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning a Supervised PageRank model that incorporates node and edge features by optimizing a loss over the parameters controlling restart and transition probabilities. The optimization is posed as a constrained, generally nonconvex problem. The main technical contribution is a two-level framework with inexact oracles: (i) a gradient-free method that uses a zero-order inexact oracle, and (ii) an adaptive gradient-based method that uses a first-order inexact oracle. On the lower level, both methods approximate the stationary distribution of a query-dependent Markov chain and, for the gradient method, its derivatives. The authors adopt a linearly convergent iteration (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate stationary distributions, and they extend it to compute derivatives. On the upper level, they adapt Nesterov–Spokoiny-style random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with inexact function values, and develop an inexact-oracle projected gradient scheme with backtracking that applies to nonconvex composite objectives. They provide convergence rate bounds and complexity estimates that explicitly trade off lower-level accuracy and upper-level progress. Experiments on a web-page ranking task built from Yandex user browsing graphs compare the proposed methods to the earlier Supervised PageRank training procedure based on power iterations.

This work builds on Supervised PageRank and Supervised Random Walks, on inexact-oracle first-order methods (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny), and on iterative methods for Markov chain stationary distributions and their derivatives (Nesterov–Nemirovski; Andrew). The distinction from prior Supervised PageRank training is the provision of convergence guarantees under inexact oracles and the use of faster lower-level iterations.

Strengths
- Clear optimization-centric framing of learning Supervised PageRank with explicit inexact-oracle modeling.
- Two complementary upper-level methods: a constrained gradient-free algorithm with inexact zero-order oracle, and an adaptive projected gradient scheme with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant.
- Convergence rate results for both methods, with complexity that exposes the trade-off between oracle accuracy and overall cost.
- Lower-level use of a linearly convergent iteration for stationary distributions, with an extension to derivative computation and error control.
- Empirical evaluation indicates improved ranking loss relative to the previous gradient-based approach.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Experimental results are mostly relegated to the supplement. The main text lacks the core tables/figures necessary to assess significance, efficiency, and robustness (datasets, metrics, wall-clock time).
- The random gradient-free method (Theorem 1) assumes convexity in the feasible region, whereas the training loss (involving squared positive parts of pairwise differences) is generally nonconvex and even nonsmooth at the hinge points. The paper informally assumes local convexity in a neighborhood Φ but does not show how to choose Φ to ensure convexity or verify it empirically.
- The projected gradient method (Theorem 3) presents a bound on the norm of MK(xK − xK+1). It would help to make explicit that this quantity is proportional to the projected gradient mapping and thus is a standard first-order stationarity measure; otherwise, the connection to convergence to a stationary point remains opaque.
- The derivation of the gradient oracle appears to differentiate through the positive-part operator; a clear statement about using subgradients and how nondifferentiable points are handled would improve rigor.
- Efficiency reporting is incomplete. Figure 1 (supplement) suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly in iterations. Without wall-clock times, number of lower-level iterations (N, N1, N2), and total oracle calls, it is difficult to judge practical significance.
- Several constants (e.g., β1) and assumptions are deferred to the supplement with little intuition; notation and typos reduce readability.

Quality
The theoretical development is largely sound: the inexact-oracle frameworks and lower-level error control are well motivated, and the complexity bounds are informative. However, key assumptions (convexity in Φ for the zero-order method; handling nonsmoothness; stationarity measure) should be stated more explicitly and justified. Experimental design is reasonable but underreported in the main text.

Clarity
The paper would benefit from tighter organization and proofreading. Suggestions:
- Move the primary experimental tables and at least one convergence plot into the main text; report wall-clock time, number of oracle calls, and lower-level iteration counts alongside loss values.
- Clearly define the stationarity measure used by the gradient method and state the precise convergence notion (approximate first-order stationarity).
- Explain how nonsmoothness due to the positive-part operator is handled (subgradient vs. smoothing), and under what conditions Lemma 2 yields a valid inexact first-order oracle.
- Make the “local convexity” assumption explicit and provide guidance on choosing Φ (e.g., small Euclidean balls around a warm start, with empirical Hessian checks).
- Collect constants (e.g., β1) and their meanings in a short “Notation and constants” subsection.
- Correct typos and unify notation.

Originality
The algorithms adapt known ideas (random gradient-free optimization; inexact-oracle projected gradient methods) to a constrained, inexact setting and apply them to Supervised PageRank with a carefully constructed lower-level oracle. The combination, the derivative computation variant, and the end-to-end complexity analysis are useful, though not entirely novel individually.

Significance
If supported by stronger empirical evidence, these methods could be valuable for learning graph-based ranking models where exact gradients are expensive. The theoretical guarantees address a gap in prior Supervised PageRank training. Demonstrated runtime gains and broader baselines (e.g., other learning-to-rank methods and supervised random walks) would strengthen impact.

Specific questions and requests for the authors
1) Experiments in the main text: Please include key results (loss values, NDCG/precision if available), convergence plots, and wall-clock times. Report both training and test metrics, plus standard deviations or confidence intervals.
2) Convexity assumption for Theorem 1: How do you choose Φ to ensure convexity of f on Φ in practice? Can you provide empirical evidence (e.g., local Hessian PSD checks) or a principled selection procedure?
3) Stationarity in Theorem 3: Clarify that MK(xK − xK+1) corresponds to a projected gradient mapping, and state explicitly that the bound ensures approximate first-order stationarity.
4) Nonsmoothness: Explain how gradients are computed through the positive-part operator and whether your guarantees apply to subgradients. If a smoothing is used, state it and adjust the analysis accordingly.
5) Efficiency: Report the number of lower-level iterations (N, N1, N2), total oracle calls, and wall-clock time per upper-level iteration for each method, in addition to iteration counts. Include a comparison against the power-method-based baseline on both accuracy and time.
6) Sensitivity: Provide sensitivity to α, R, and L/L0, and discuss robustness across query subsets. Consider adding comparisons to other supervised random walk or learning-to-rank baselines.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical problem with a principled optimization framework.
- Provides convergence guarantees missing in prior Supervised PageRank training.
- Thoughtful analysis of inexact oracles and lower/upper-level trade-offs.
- Empirical results suggest improved ranking loss.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key experimental evidence (including efficiency) is not presented in the main text.
- Assumptions underlying the zero-order method (local convexity) and treatment of nonsmoothness are insufficiently justified.
- Practical convergence of the gradient-free method appears slow; impact is unclear without time-based comparisons.
- Clarity issues and missing definitions/intuition hinder accessibility.

Overall, this is a promising optimization-centric contribution to learning supervised PageRank. Strengthening the empirical section in the main text and clarifying the theoretical assumptions would substantially improve the paper.
