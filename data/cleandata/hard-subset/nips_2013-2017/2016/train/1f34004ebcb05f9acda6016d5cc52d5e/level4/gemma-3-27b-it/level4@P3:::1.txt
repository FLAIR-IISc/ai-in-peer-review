This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning parameters in a supervised PageRank model – a graph-based ranking model where node and edge weights are determined by their features and a random walk process. Existing optimization methods struggle with this problem due to the difficulty in calculating the objective function exactly. The authors propose a two-level approach: a lower level utilizing a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, and an upper level employing gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with an inexact oracle. They provide convergence guarantees for both methods, building upon and adapting techniques from existing literature. The paper is generally well-written and the proofs appear sound, though a complete verification was not possible within the scope of this review.

The core idea of approximating the stationary distribution and utilizing inexact oracles is a reasonable approach given the challenges of the optimization problem. This work builds on the foundation laid by prior research on Supervised PageRank [21] and related optimization techniques, particularly those by Nesterov and Spokoiny [16, 25] concerning random gradient-free minimization and inexact oracle methods.  The paper clearly articulates how their methods differ – by adapting these techniques to constrained optimization problems and providing convergence guarantees in that setting.  Related work in PageRank computation, such as power methods [18, 23, 24] and efficient computation methods [8], is also appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a significant contribution, especially for non-convex problems. This distinguishes the work from the original Supervised PageRank formulation [21], which lacked such guarantees.
*   **Inexact Oracle Approach:** The use of inexact oracles addresses the practical challenges of computing the loss function and its gradients accurately, making the method more applicable to large-scale graphs.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and the mathematical derivations are presented with reasonable clarity.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to the state-of-the-art, suggesting practical benefits.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate awareness of relevant literature in ranking, optimization, and numerical linear algebra.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the convergence guarantees and application to constrained optimization are valuable, the core ideas rely heavily on existing work, particularly from Nesterov and Spokoiny [16, 25].  The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of these techniques to the specific problem of Supervised PageRank with constraints and inexact oracles, rather than fundamentally new algorithmic innovations.
*   **Limited Contextualization:** The problem statement could benefit from broader contextualization. Framing the supervised PageRank problem as a specific instance of a more general optimization class would highlight the broader impact of the proposed methods.
*   **Missing Conclusion:** The paper lacks a concluding section that summarizes the key findings, discusses limitations, and outlines potential future research directions.
*   **Dataset Description:** While a dataset is described, more information on its size and characteristics would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in graph-based ranking with a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The convergence guarantees are a valuable contribution, improving upon existing approaches.
*   The experimental results provide evidence of practical effectiveness.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the proposed methods is incremental, primarily consisting of adaptations of existing techniques.
*   The lack of a conclusion and broader contextualization weakens the overall impact of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the core ideas are not entirely novel, the rigorous analysis, convergence guarantees, and experimental validation are significant. The authors have effectively applied existing optimization techniques to a challenging problem in graph-based ranking. The paper, with minor revisions (specifically the addition of a conclusion and some broadening of the problem context), is worthy of acceptance.  I recommend a score of **6/10** – a solid paper, but not groundbreaking.
