This paper formulates Supervised PageRank as a constrained, generally non-convex optimization problem that incorporates node and edge features via restart and transition probabilities. The authors propose two two-level methods that use the concept of an inexact oracle: (i) a random gradient-free method relying on an inexact zero-order oracle, and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle. On the lower level, they approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives using a linearly convergent scheme (following Nesterov–Nemirovski), rather than the power method used in prior work. On the upper level, they analyze convergence rates for both the gradient-free and gradient-based procedures under inexactness, including complexity bounds that account for the accuracy of the lower-level approximations. Experiments on a query-dependent browsing graph from Yandex compare the proposed methods to the gradient-based approach of [21] (GBP), reporting improved ranking quality and convergence guarantees.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper provides lemmas that bound the errors in the function and gradient estimates (via controllable lower-level iterations), and theorems establishing convergence rates for both the constrained gradient-free scheme (under convexity of f on the feasible set) and an adaptive gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle (for possibly non-convex composite problems). These are meaningful extensions of inexact-oracle methods to the constrained setting and to non-convex problems. However, important details are deferred to the supplement. The reliance on local convexity for the gradient-free method raises practical questions about how Φ is chosen to justify convexity in the application, since f in (2.4) is non-convex in general.
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation appears to be substantial (Yandex data, several query subsets, comparisons to GBP), but essentially all results are in the supplement. This makes it difficult to assess empirical significance from the main text alone. Moreover, efficiency (wall-clock time, number of oracle calls, dependence on N, N1, N2) is not clearly reported in the main paper.
- Clarity: The main ideas are understandable, but the presentation is dense. Several key choices (e.g., how Φ is selected to ensure convexity for the gradient-free method) are not clearly justified. Some references appear incomplete.
- Reproducibility: The paper claims that all accuracy parameters can be set to meet target ε, with explicit complexity bounds. Still, practical guidance on tuning τ, L, and oracle tolerances, and their relationship to the lower-level iteration counts N, N1, N2, could be clearer.

Originality:
- Adapting random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with a uniformly bounded inexact zero-order oracle, and developing an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle (unknown Lipschitz constant) for possibly non-convex composite problems, are novel and relevant contributions.
- The careful lower/upper-level error–complexity trade-off analysis for Supervised PageRank, along with convergence guarantees that the prior approach [21] lacked due to power-method approximations, is valuable.

Significance:
- Supervised PageRank remains relevant for learning to rank on graphs when features are available. Providing optimization methods with convergence guarantees under inexact oracles is significant for large-scale applications where exact stationary distributions and gradients are infeasible.
- The empirical improvements over GBP would be compelling, but they need to be visible in the main text, together with runtime analyses.

Detailed comments:
1) Experiments in the main text: As presented, the paper is incomplete. Please move key experimental results from the supplement into the main paper: final loss values and IR metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP), convergence plots, and summary of computational costs (wall-clock time, number of lower-level iterations, and oracle calls). Report sensitivity to ε, τ, L, and to N, N1, N2.

2) Convexity for Theorem 1 vs. non-convex f in (2.4): Theorem 1 assumes convexity of f on Φ, but the objective in (2.4) is non-convex in general. How is Φ selected in practice to ensure f is convex on Φ? Please provide either (a) conditions under which f is convex on a ball around the chosen ϕ̂, with a principled way to set R, or (b) clarify that the gradient-free method targets a local minimum and explain how convexity is verified or enforced (e.g., via diagnostics or regularization).

3) Stationarity for Theorem 3: Theorem 3 bounds ‖M_K(x_K − x_{K+1})‖. Does this imply convergence to a stationary point (e.g., that the projected gradient or a suitable measure of first-order optimality tends to zero)? Please clarify the stationarity measure implied by the bound, and state what additional assumptions (if any) ensure that accumulation points are stationary.

4) Convergence speed and efficiency: In the supplement (Figure 1), GFN appears to converge slowly. Please quantify efficiency: report wall-clock times, number of oracle evaluations, and dependence on lower-level tolerances (N, N1, N2). Since a key claimed advantage is that the gradient-free method avoids computing derivatives for large matrices, explicit runtime comparisons to GBP and GBN are important.

Additional suggestions:
- Metrics: Beyond the squared pairwise loss (2.4), please report standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP) on the labeled pairs to strengthen the practical relevance.
- Parameter choices: Provide guidance for selecting ε, τ, L, and initial L0, and discuss robustness. The current text notes little sensitivity for L0 in GBN; similar guidance for GFN would help.
- Notation and assumptions: Clearly state the norm used in each theorem (especially where switching between l2 and l1 bounds) and the exact conditions under which Lemmas 1–2 hold. Briefly justify the boundedness and positivity assumptions ensuring (2.1)–(2.2) are well-defined.
- References: Several references are incomplete; please fix authors, venues, and years.

Pros:
- Solid and relevant theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle optimization (gradient-free and gradient-based) for constrained and non-convex settings.
- Careful lower/upper-level error-control mechanisms and complexity bounds.
- Application to an important problem with large-scale data; claimed improvements over prior work.

Cons:
- Main text lacks experimental results; limited insight into empirical efficiency and practical impact.
- Reliance on convexity of f over Φ for Theorem 1 without a clear method to ensure or verify it for (2.4).
- Stationarity implications of Theorem 3 are not fully clarified.
- Practical tuning guidelines and runtime analyses are insufficiently detailed.

Overall, the paper has promising theoretical contributions and a relevant application, but it would benefit greatly from moving key experiments into the main text, clarifying the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method, making the stationarity guarantee explicit, and reporting empirical efficiency.
