Summary
The paper tackles a nonconvex loss-minimization problem for learning Supervised PageRank models that incorporate node and edge features via linear parameterizations. The authors propose a two-level framework with provable convergence rates in an inexact-oracle setting. At the lower level, they approximate the stationary distribution of a query-dependent Markov random walk (and its derivatives) using a linearly convergent method, which they argue has the best proven complexity among alternatives reviewed in prior work. This yields controllable-accuracy estimates of the loss and gradient. At the upper level, they present (i) a random gradient-free method adapted to constrained optimization with bounded oracle noise, and (ii) an adaptive gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. They analyze the accuracy–complexity trade-offs between lower and upper levels, derive overall complexity bounds, and validate on a real ranking task, reporting better loss values than a prior gradient-based approach for learning Supervised PageRank.

Relation to previous work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank and related random-walk-based ranking (e.g., PageRank, HITS, BrowseRank), and on optimization frameworks for inexact oracles and zeroth-/first-order methods (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny; Ghadimi–Lan; Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov). The lower-level solver uses the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent scheme instead of standard power iteration, and the upper-level methods adapt known gradient-free and gradient-based techniques to a constrained, inexact-oracle setting. The novelty lies in providing end-to-end convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank under realistic oracle inaccuracies, extending the gradient-free framework to constraints with bounded noise, generalizing derivative computation for the stationary distribution, and quantifying complexity trade-offs across levels.

Strengths
- Clear two-level design with rigorous control of oracle errors; convergence-rate guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based variants.
- Careful treatment of constrained optimization with inexact zero- and first-order oracles, including adaptive step-size for the gradient method without assuming known Lipschitz constants.
- Lower-level analysis identifies a practically useful method with favorable complexity, and extends it to derivative estimation.
- Complexity bounds for the full training procedure, including the accuracy/iteration trade-off (N, N1, N2).
- Empirical results on a real query-dependent browsing graph demonstrating improved loss relative to a commonly used gradient-based baseline.

Weaknesses and questions
- Originality is somewhat incremental: core optimization ideas are adaptations of established methods (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny for zeroth-order, inexact oracle techniques) to this constrained, inexact setting.
- Experimental evaluation focuses on the training loss. Standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) on held-out data would better connect to ranking quality and practical significance.
- Dataset scale and diversity are limited (≈600 queries, ≈1.7K labeled pairs). It would help to report generalization across larger or more heterogeneous query sets and include runtime/wall-clock comparisons for lower-level solvers.
- Several assumptions could be clarified: when and where convexity is required (e.g., local convexity for the gradient-free analysis), how β1 and other constants are instantiated, and sensitivity to the choice of radius R and hyperparameters.
- Reproducibility would benefit from more implementation details (initialization, stopping criteria, random seeds) and code availability.

Quality
- The theoretical components are well-structured, with explicit lemmas and theorems connecting lower-level accuracy to upper-level convergence. I did not verify all proofs in detail, but the arguments appear sound and consistent with the cited frameworks.
- The end-to-end complexity accounting is a valuable contribution. Clarifying constants and assumptions would further strengthen the presentation.
- Empirical comparisons support the claims on loss reduction; broader metrics and stronger baselines would improve the case for practical impact.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, though there are grammatical issues and occasional notation overload. Unifying notation early (especially for query-dependent quantities) and summarizing the algorithmic flow would enhance readability.
- It would help to present the general optimization frameworks first (inexact zero-/first-order, constrained setting), then instantiate them for Supervised PageRank.
- A concise conclusion summarizing contributions, limitations, and future work is expected but currently missing.
- Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted and should be cleaned up.

Originality
- Moderate. The main ideas adapt known methods to a constrained, inexact-oracle context and apply them to Supervised PageRank, providing convergence guarantees that prior work lacked. The derivative-computation generalization and two-level complexity analysis are useful, but much of the methodology builds on established literature.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners training feature-aware random-walk ranking models at scale, where exact oracles are impractical. The guarantees add reliability to a commonly used learning setup. The current empirical scope limits the strength of claims about real-world impact; broader evaluation would raise the significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical development with clear convergence guarantees and complexity bounds.
- Practical two-level training scheme that improves over a standard baseline in experiments.
- Careful handling of inexact oracles and constraints; extensions likely applicable beyond this specific ranking task.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to existing optimization literature.
- Limited experimental scope and evaluation metrics; missing standard IR measures and broader baselines.
- Some presentation issues (grammar, missing conclusion, reference formatting) and assumptions that need clearer articulation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add NDCG/MAP results on held-out queries; include variance across random seeds and runtime/iteration counts for lower-level solvers.
- Provide ablations: effect of N, N1, N2; sensitivity to R, α, and oracle accuracies; comparison to power iteration in both accuracy and time.
- Clarify assumptions (local convexity for gradient-free analysis, constants like β1), and provide guidance for setting hyperparameters.
- Reorganize to present the general frameworks first, then the Supervised PageRank instantiation; add a concise conclusion.
- Fix grammatical issues and complete/standardize references.

Overall, this is a well-executed application and refinement of inexact-oracle optimization methods to learning Supervised PageRank, with sound analysis and promising results, but the novelty is moderate and the empirical evaluation could be stronger.
