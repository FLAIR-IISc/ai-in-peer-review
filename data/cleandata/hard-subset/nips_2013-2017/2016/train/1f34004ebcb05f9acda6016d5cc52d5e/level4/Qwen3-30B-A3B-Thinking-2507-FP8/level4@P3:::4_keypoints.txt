- Proposes two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank models.  
- Addresses non-convex loss-minimization that incorporates node and edge features in graphs.  
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, improving over Zhukovskii et al. (NIPS 2015) which lacked such guarantees.  
- Builds on and extends inexact-oracle methods, connecting to Nesterov (2015) and Nesterov & Spokoiny (2011).  
- Contribution 1: First convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank with gradient-based optimization.  
- Contribution 2: Development of a gradient-free method for constrained optimization with an inexact oracle.  
- Contribution 3: Analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.  
- Contribution 4: Empirical demonstration of superiority over state-of-the-art on a real-world web ranking task.  
- Theoretical analysis is strong, with precise complexity bounds and careful convergence-rate proofs.  
- Experiments are well-designed, use real Yandex data, and show statistically significant improvements (p < 0.005).  
- Practical relevance demonstrated by potential to improve search engine reliability and speed.  
- Weakness: Lacks detailed computational time analysis and real running-time comparisons to practitioners.  
- Weakness: Provides arithmetic operation counts but does not convert them to wall-clock time or practical time complexity.  
- Weakness: Assumes convexity of f(ϕ) on feasible set Φ without discussing how restrictive this is in practice.  
- Weakness: Does not compare with other applicable optimization methods (e.g., stochastic gradient methods).  
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, but some technical proof details (supplement) could be more accessible.  
- Originality: Novel extension of inexact-oracle framework to Supervised PageRank and combined gradient-based/gradient-free approach.  
- Significance: Strong implications for both optimization theory and practical web ranking applications.  
- Argument for acceptance: Rigorous theory, first guarantees for this problem, and consistent empirical improvements.  
- Argument against acceptance: Missing timing analysis, potentially restrictive convexity assumption, lacks broader optimizer comparisons, and some technical clarity issues.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept, contingent on adding timing information in the camera-ready version.
