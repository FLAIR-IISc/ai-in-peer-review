Summary:
The paper studies learning in a Supervised PageRank framework where node and edge importance is parameterized by feature-weighted random walks. The authors consider a non-convex loss-minimization problem over parameters that control restart and transition probabilities via node and edge features. They propose a two-level approach. At the lower level, they use a linearly convergent method (following Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov chain and to compute approximate derivatives, with explicit control of the approximation error. At the upper level, they develop two optimization schemes that can operate with inexact oracles: (i) a random gradient-free method adapted to constrained problems with bounded zero-order oracle error, and (ii) an adaptive gradient method for constrained, possibly non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle. For both methods they provide convergence guarantees (to an ε-accurate solution in the convex/locally convex setting for the gradient-free method and to a stationary point for the gradient-based method) and complexity bounds that account for lower-level approximation. Empirically, on a real web-ranking task built from Yandex browsing data, both methods are compared against a prior gradient-based approach; the proposed methods achieve competitive or improved ranking quality while providing theoretical convergence guarantees that the previous approach lacked.

Relation to prior work:
The work builds on Supervised PageRank and related supervised random walk models, where feature-based restart and transition probabilities have been explored previously. On the optimization side, it adapts techniques from Nesterov and Spokoiny (Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, FoCM, 2015) to constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracles, and leverages inexact first-order oracle ideas for non-convex composite optimization. The proposed lower-level stationary distribution approximation is based on a linearly convergent method rather than the classical power method, with supporting error and complexity analyses.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of a two-level learning procedure with explicit control of inexactness at the lower level (for both function values and gradients).
- Convergence guarantees for both the gradient-free and gradient-based upper-level methods in the presence of inexact oracles, which the prior gradient-based approach did not provide.
- Complexity analyses that quantify the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational cost.
- Empirical evaluation on real query-dependent browsing graphs demonstrating practical relevance and improved ranking performance over a state-of-the-art baseline.
- The paper is generally well written, and the proofs appear correct (though I did not verify all details).

Weaknesses:
- The main optimization contributions are largely adaptations of existing techniques (notably Nesterov–Spokoiny for gradient-free methods), rather than fundamentally new algorithms. Supervised PageRank itself has also been proposed previously.
- The presentation could better situate the contribution by first posing the optimization problem in a more general framework and then showing the studied algorithm as a specific instance. This would help clarify the novelty relative to the state of the art.
- A concluding section would be useful; there seems to be space for one, and it would help summarize takeaways and limitations.

Quality:
The technical development is solid: the lower-level approximation scheme is justified, and the resulting inexact oracles are integrated into upper-level methods with appropriate convergence analyses. The claims are supported by theoretical results and empirical comparisons. My only caveat is that I did not check every proof in detail.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. However, the exposition would benefit from a more general optimization framing before diving into the Supervised PageRank specialization. A brief, explicit conclusion would also improve readability.

Originality:
Moderate. While the application to Supervised PageRank with inexact-oracle-driven upper-level methods is well executed, the optimization techniques themselves are primarily adaptations of known methods to the constrained, inexact setting. The supervised random-walk modeling is also known.

Significance:
The results are meaningful for learning graph-based ranking models in settings where exact function values and gradients are unavailable or expensive. Providing convergence guarantees in this inexact-oracle context is valuable, and the empirical results indicate practical benefit.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Recast the optimization problem first in a general constrained inexact-oracle framework, then present Supervised PageRank as a specific instantiation. This would clarify the scope and novelty.
- Add a concise conclusion summarizing theoretical guarantees, empirical findings, and limitations.
- Briefly discuss the assumptions under which the gradient-free method is applied (e.g., local convexity of f over Φ) to make applicability conditions explicit.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides convergence-guaranteed learning methods for Supervised PageRank with inexact function/gradient information.
- Careful complexity analysis and control of oracle accuracy.
- Demonstrated empirical gains over a state-of-the-art baseline on real data.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Optimization contributions are mostly adaptations of existing methods; limited algorithmic novelty.
- The modeling component (Supervised PageRank) is not new.
- Presentation could better highlight the generality and distinctiveness of the contribution.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed application of inexact-oracle optimization techniques to a practically important ranking problem, with theoretical guarantees and supportive experiments, albeit with limited novelty in the optimization methodology itself.
