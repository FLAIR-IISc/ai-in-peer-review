- Addresses learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a non-convex problem with expensive/impractical exact objective and gradient computation.  
- Proposes two two-level algorithms: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a random gradient-free method (GFN).  
- Uses an inexact oracle framework and Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level method to approximate PageRank stationary distribution and derivatives to controlled accuracy.  
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the first time in this setting: GBN converges to a stationary point; GFN guarantees a decrease in the loss.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvement over a baseline in final loss value on a real-world web ranking task.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: rigorous convergence analysis and novel adaptation of general optimization frameworks to Supervised PageRank.  
- Practically significant: avoids exact PageRank computations, improving scalability and allowing a trade-off between oracle accuracy and computation.  
- Complete presentation: includes theory, application details, complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), experiments, and supplementary proofs.  
- Clarity/readability issues: paper is mathematically dense with many symbols, risking reader confusion.  
- Suggestion: include a symbol table and define variables (e.g., N) at first use to improve flow.  
- Experimental limitation: comparisons focus on final loss rather than standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP), so claims about ranking quality are not fully substantiated.  
- Suggestion: report standard ranking metrics and provide a direct efficiency comparison (error vs. time) between the Nesterov–Nemirovski method and the power method used by the baseline.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first theoretical guarantees for this problem, novel inexact-oracle application, and empirical loss improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy notation harming clarity and incomplete experimental evidence for claimed ranking-quality improvements.  
- Overall conclusion: high-quality, novel theoretical work with practical relevance; recommend acceptance with improvements to clarity and experimental evaluation.
