Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank (SPR) [21], where node and edge features parametrize restart and transition probabilities. The authors consider the standard two-level formulation: (i) a lower-level routine approximates the stationary distribution πq(ϕ) and, for the gradient-based approach, its derivatives with respect to model parameters; (ii) an upper-level optimizer updates ϕ to minimize a nonconvex pairwise loss over queries. The key contribution is to treat the lower-level approximations as an inexact oracle and to provide convergence-rate guarantees for both a gradient-based and a gradient-free upper-level method. For the lower level, they use the Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme [17] to approximate the stationary distribution (and a generalization to approximate derivatives), arguing it has favorable complexity among methods reviewed in [5] and compared to the power method used in PageRank/HITS variants [8, 9, 18] and prior SPR work [21]. For the upper level, they develop:
- A gradient-free algorithm that extends random gradient-free optimization (e.g., [1, 13, 7, 16]) to constrained problems with uniformly bounded zero-order oracle errors.
- An adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle, extending [7, 25].

They analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity, derive complexity bounds for both two-level methods, and evaluate on a Yandex web ranking task, reporting improved loss and claiming improved ranking quality over the prior gradient-based SPR [21].

Strengths
- Quality: The inexact-oracle frameworks are carefully formulated, with clear statements of assumptions and convergence guarantees. The extension of gradient-free methods to constrained domains with bounded oracle error and the adaptive gradient method with inexact first-order oracle are technically solid and broadly useful. The lower-level use of [17] is well-motivated, and Lemmas 1–2 quantify how approximation tolerances propagate to the upper-level oracles.
- Originality: Combining an inexact-oracle perspective with two-level SPR learning, and providing convergence-rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free variants, is, to my knowledge, novel in this context. The constrained, inexact zero-order extension and the adaptive line-search with inexact first-order oracle go beyond prior analyses [16, 7, 25].
- Significance: Learning PageRank-like models remains relevant for large graph ranking with rich features (e.g., BrowseRank [10], supervised random walks [2]). Having provable convergence under realistic inexactness is valuable for large-scale deployments. The gradient-free variant is attractive when derivative computation is prohibitive.
- Clarity (partial): The high-level structure and motivation are clear; the two-level decomposition and the role of inexact oracles are well explained. Hyperparameters and implementation choices are stated, and assumptions on Φ (positivity via a ball around 1) are explicit.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity of notation: The manuscript is heavy with symbols and indices across queries and graphs. A symbol table would greatly improve readability. In particular, parameters like N (number of iterations in Eq. 3.1) should be defined upon first use (e.g., before Eq. 3.1 rather than later). Please standardize norm notation and clearly distinguish query-specific vs. global quantities.
- Experimental evaluation: The experiments mainly report the training objective f (pairwise hinge-like squared loss) and iteration counts. To substantiate the claim of improved “ranking quality,” standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, ERR) on held-out data should be reported, with statistical significance, not just loss values. Runtime and wall-clock comparisons (including lower-level costs N, N1, N2, and total matrix–vector products) vs. the power-method-based SPR [21] would also help. Finally, comparisons to strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) would contextualize the practical impact, even if the focus is SPR.
- Assumptions and scope: The gradient-free analysis assumes f is convex in a neighborhood Φ containing a local minimizer (Section 4.2). This is a strong assumption for the nonconvex SPR loss; please make this explicit earlier, discuss when it is expected to hold, and clarify how Φ is chosen in practice. For the gradient-based method, the stationarity measure ‖Mk(xk − xk+1)‖ is used; please relate this to standard measures (e.g., projected gradient norm) to aid interpretation.
- Reproducibility: The Yandex data are proprietary. Consider releasing code and synthetic or anonymized data, along with precise details on feature construction and query splits, to facilitate reproduction.
- Presentation and minor issues: There are several typos (“concider,” “probablities,” “th Conference”), inconsistent reference formatting, and occasional missing context (e.g., define β1 when first introduced, ensure all constants are either specified or bounded). Algorithm names (GBN, GFN, GBP) should be consistently used and defined where they first appear.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong theoretical development; proofs deferred to the supplement appear to align with standard techniques. Some assumptions (convexity of f on Φ for the gradient-free method) should be highlighted and, if possible, relaxed or empirically justified. Experimental evidence is promising but would benefit from richer metrics and baselines.
- Clarity: Generally well organized but notation-heavy. Adding a glossary, tightening definitions around Eq. 3.1–3.3, and cleaning typos would help. Include high-level pseudocode for the two-level algorithms in the main text (not just in the supplement).
- Originality: Good. The inexact-oracle framing for constrained zero- and first-order methods and its application to SPR with derivative approximations is a meaningful extension beyond [17, 21, 16, 7, 25].
- Significance: Potentially high for large-scale ranking and for practitioners who must operate with inexact computations. Stronger empirical validation against standard IR metrics and external baselines would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contributions with clear convergence guarantees under inexact oracles, for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
- Relevant application to a widely used graph-based ranking paradigm, with demonstrated improvements over prior SPR training.
- Careful analysis of the accuracy–complexity trade-off between lower- and upper-level procedures.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is limited: lack of standard ranking metrics, few external baselines, and limited runtime analysis.
- Some assumptions (local convexity for gradient-free analysis) are strong and not empirically verified.
- Clarity suffers from heavy notation and minor editorial issues; a symbol table and earlier definition of key quantities would help.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report NDCG@k/MAP/ERR on Q2, along with wall-clock times and total matrix–vector products for all methods?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of Φ (center and radius)? Does the convexity assumption for f on Φ hold empirically?
- Could you compare directly to [17] as a baseline lower-level method vs. the power method in terms of both accuracy vs. iterations and speed?
- What is the practical stopping criterion for the gradient-based method in terms of a standard stationarity measure?

Overall recommendation: promising and theoretically sound, with room for strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarity.
