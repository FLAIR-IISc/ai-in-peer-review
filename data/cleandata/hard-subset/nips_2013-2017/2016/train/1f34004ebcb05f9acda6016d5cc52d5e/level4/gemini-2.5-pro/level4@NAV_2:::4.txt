This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that enriches the classic PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features. The authors formulate this as a non-convex, constrained loss-minimization problem. A key challenge, inherited from prior work [21], is that the loss function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly, necessitating methods that operate with an "inexact oracle." The primary contribution of this work is the development of two novel two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—to solve this problem. Crucially, and in contrast to the state-of-the-art method in [21], the authors provide a full theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms. The theoretical framework adapts and extends existing work on optimization with inexact oracles ([7], [25]) and random gradient-free methods ([16]) to the specific constraints and non-convex nature of the Supervised PageRank problem. The proposed methods are empirically validated on a real-world web page ranking task, where they are shown to outperform the existing baseline.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The main strength of this paper is providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The previous state-of-the-art method [21] lacked such a proof. The authors rigorously analyze the complexity of their two-level algorithms by studying the trade-off between the accuracy of the low-level oracle (approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives) and the convergence of the high-level optimization routine. This is a solid and important theoretical advancement for this class of problems.
*   **Technical Quality and Completeness:** The work is technically sound and presents a complete contribution. The paper develops general optimization frameworks for non-convex constrained problems with inexact first-order oracles (Section 5) and for convex constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracles (Section 4). It then successfully applies these frameworks to the target problem, providing full complexity analyses (Theorems 2 and 4). This theoretical work is complemented by a convincing experimental evaluation.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free algorithm (GFN), achieve a lower final loss value on test data compared to the state-of-the-art gradient-based method (GBP). This indicates that the new algorithms not only have stronger theoretical backing but can also lead to better-performing ranking models in practice.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly defines the problem, details the proposed algorithms, and presents the theoretical results in an understandable manner, separating the general optimization frameworks from their specific application to Supervised PageRank.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Lack of Practical Efficiency Comparison:** While the paper provides a theoretical complexity analysis in terms of arithmetic operations, the experimental section lacks a comparison of wall-clock time or computational cost. Figure 1 shows that the proposed gradient-based method (GBN) may require fewer *upper-level* iterations, but each iteration, especially with the adaptive line search procedure, could be significantly more expensive than an iteration of the baseline (GBP). Reporting the training time required for each method to reach convergence would provide a much clearer picture of their practical trade-offs and is essential for a complete comparison.
*   **Discussion on Convexity Assumption:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within the chosen optimization domain Φ. While the authors acknowledge that the problem is non-convex in general and state this is a local assumption, a brief discussion on the practical implications would be beneficial. For example, how sensitive is the algorithm's performance if this local convexity is not strongly pronounced?
*   **Minor Clarifications:**
    *   The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., L = 10^-4) seems crucial. While the paper mentions testing different values for L0, some intuition on how these parameters were chosen or could be set in practice would be helpful.
    *   The description of the baseline GBP method is quite brief. Expanding slightly on its implementation details (e.g., how the step size was tuned beyond the four values tested) would strengthen the experimental comparison.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable and much-needed theoretical contribution to the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models. The provided convergence guarantees address a key gap in prior work, and the empirical results are promising. The primary weakness is the omission of a practical runtime comparison in the experiments, which I strongly encourage the authors to include in the final version. Despite this, the paper's strengths in theory and quality make it a solid contribution to the field.
