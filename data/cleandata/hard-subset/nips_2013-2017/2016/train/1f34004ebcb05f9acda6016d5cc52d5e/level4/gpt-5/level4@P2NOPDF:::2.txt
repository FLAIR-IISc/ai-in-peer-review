This paper formulates Supervised PageRank as a constrained, non-convex optimization problem that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose two solution strategies: (i) a gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle, and (ii) a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle. While the problem setting and algorithms are interesting, the paper feels incomplete because all experimental results are relegated to the supplement.

Major comments:
- The absence of experimental results in the main text significantly weakens the paper. At least the core findings (datasets, baselines, main metrics, and key plots) should be presented in the main body to substantiate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical relevance.

Detailed comments:
1) Placement of experiments. All experimental results are in the supplement, leaving the main text without empirical evidence. Please include a concise but informative experimental section in the main paper: a brief description of datasets and features, baselines, evaluation metrics, primary quantitative results, and a summary of computational costs.

2) Assumption of convexity vs. non-convex objective. Theorem 1 assumes convexity, but the objective defined in (2.4) is non-convex. Under what conditions on the feature set or parameter set Φ does the objective become convex? Please clarify how Φ should be chosen (or restricted) to satisfy the convexity assumptions required by Theorem 1, or explicitly separate the convex and non-convex regimes and state which results apply in each case.

3) Convergence implications of Theorem 3. Theorem 3 provides a bound for M_K(x^K − x^{K+1}). Does this bound imply that the sequence {x^k} converges, and in the non-convex case, does it ensure convergence to a stationary point (e.g., in terms of vanishing gradient norm or a suitable proximal residual)? If not, what additional assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity, Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz property, bounded variance of the oracle) are needed to establish convergence to stationarity?

4) Efficiency and practical performance. Figure 1 in the supplement suggests that GFNM converges slowly. Please report and compare:
   - Wall-clock time, iteration counts, and oracle calls for both methods.
   - Sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., step sizes, smoothing/regularization parameters).
   - Performance vs. accuracy trade-offs (e.g., objective value or evaluation metric vs. time).
   - Comparisons to standard baselines for learning-to-rank on graphs, if applicable.
Presenting these results in the main text would clarify the practical usefulness of the proposed methods.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Clear problem motivation; integration of node/edge features into a principled optimization framework; consideration of inexact oracles for both zero- and first-order methods; formal analysis of iteration progress.
- Weaknesses: Lack of in-text empirical validation; unclear reconciliation between convexity assumptions and a non-convex objective; limited discussion of convergence-to-stationarity guarantees; insufficient reporting on computational efficiency.

Recommendations for improvement:
- Move key experimental results into the main paper, including representative plots and tables, with the full details left to the supplement.
- Clarify the role of Φ in ensuring convexity for Theorem 1, or explicitly delineate the scope of the theorem to convex subproblems only.
- Strengthen convergence claims in the non-convex setting by either proving stationarity results under stated assumptions or clearly limiting the claims to decrease-type guarantees.
- Provide comprehensive efficiency metrics and discuss why GFNM is slow in practice (e.g., oracle noise, dimension, stepsize selection), along with any accelerations or heuristics that improve performance.

With these changes, the paper would present a more complete and convincing contribution.
