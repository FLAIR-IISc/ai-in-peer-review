This paper introduces two optimization approaches for the Supervised PageRank problem. In contrast to prior work, the gradient-based method comes with a theoretical convergence-rate guarantee and can attain a prescribed accuracy. The proposed gradient-free method provides a certified amount of decrease in the loss. Both approaches operate without access to exact objective values (inexact oracle setting), include convergence-rate estimates, and empirically outperform the state of the art on ranking quality. The hyperparameters for both methods are specified. Although the datasets are not public, the description is sufficiently detailed that reproducing the results appears feasible.

The authors present two two-level procedures, a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN), to learn Supervised PageRank. The lower and upper levels build on Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work (ref: 17). The methods do not require exact objective evaluations and have proven convergence-rate bounds for a target accuracy. Consequently, they avoid expensive computations on large matrices while maintaining strong performance. Sections 2–5 clearly lay out the technical details, with proofs deferred to the supplement. That said, a notation table would greatly aid readability: there are many symbols, and it is easy to lose track. For instance, in Equation 3.1 the parameter N appears without immediate clarification (its meaning as the number of iterations is only explained later, e.g., much further down the text). In the experimental section, the comparison to GBP emphasizes the loss rather than ranking metrics; additionally, since this work generalizes ref: 17, a direct empirical comparison against it would be valuable to assess performance gains.
