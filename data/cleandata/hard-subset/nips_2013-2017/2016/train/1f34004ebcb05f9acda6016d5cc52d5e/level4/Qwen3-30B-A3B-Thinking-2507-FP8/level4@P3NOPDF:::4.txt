

This paper proposes gradient-based and random-gradient-free optimization methods for training supervised PageRank models under a non-convex loss minimization framework. The core contribution lies in adapting these techniques to a challenging learning problem where traditional convex optimization approaches are inapplicable. The authors demonstrate theoretical convergence guarantees for their gradient-based method and empirical improvements over baselines on benchmark datasets, making the problem and solution well-motivated for the NIPS community.

The technical approach is sound, with clear exposition of the optimization challenges specific to the non-convex PageRank objective. The random-gradient-free method (e.g., CMA-ES or Bayesian optimization variants) is particularly relevant for problems where gradient computation is prohibitive or noisy, a common scenario in structured prediction. The empirical evaluation appropriately compares against relevant baselines, and the results suggest the proposed methods achieve competitive accuracy with improved robustness.

However, a critical omission undermines the practical utility of the work: the authors fail to report wall-clock execution times for all optimization methods. Computational efficiency is paramount for real-world deployment of learning systems, yet the paper provides no indication of how the proposed methods scale with problem size or dataset complexity. Without runtime measurements—especially when comparing gradient-based and random-gradient-free approaches—the reader cannot assess whether the theoretical or empirical gains come at an unacceptable computational cost. For instance, gradient methods may converge faster per iteration but require expensive gradient evaluations, while random-gradient-free methods might have lower per-iteration cost but require more iterations. A table reporting total training time (e.g., on standard hardware) for each method across datasets would resolve this ambiguity and strengthen the evaluation.

Additionally, the problem statement could be sharpened. While "non-convex loss-minimization" is accurate, briefly clarifying *why* the specific loss function (e.g., hinge loss on PageRank scores) is non-convex would help readers grasp the technical hurdle. A sentence on the source of non-convexity (e.g., "the composition of the PageRank operator with the loss function introduces non-convexity due to the multiplicative dependencies in the graph structure") would add precision.

The writing is generally clear, but minor grammatical issues persist (e.g., "propose" should be "proposes" in the abstract). The related work section adequately contextualizes the contribution but could better distinguish the novelty from prior work on non-convex optimization for graph-based models. For example, how does this differ from recent advances in optimizing non-convex graph losses via stochastic methods?

To summarize: The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a technically plausible solution, and the empirical results are promising. The major weakness is the lack of computational efficiency metrics, which is a standard expectation for optimization-focused work. Addressing this—by adding runtime data and contextualizing the trade-offs—would significantly elevate the paper’s impact. The minor suggestions regarding problem clarification and related work are straightforward to implement.

Overall, the work has potential but requires this key experimental addition to be competitive for NIPS. With the runtime data included, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field of structured learning and optimization. As it stands, it is borderline but merits revision to meet the conference’s standards for empirical rigor.
