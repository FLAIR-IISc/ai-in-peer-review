This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model, defined by a random walk on the graph where the weights of nodes and edges are determined by random walks on the nodes' and edges' features. The proposed optimization problem cannot be solved using existing optimization methods that require exact values of the objective function. To address this challenge, the authors propose a two-level approach. At the first level, they employ a linearly convergent method to estimate an approximation of the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, which they validate through experiments. They demonstrate that the value of the loss function can be approximated with any given precision. Furthermore, they develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems using an inexact oracle and prove its convergence to the stationary point of the problem. The primary contribution lies in adapting this approach to the case of constrained optimization problems, where the value of the function can be calculated with some known precision. They prove the convergence of this method and utilize it on the second level of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be correct, although a thorough examination of all the proofs was not conducted. However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the proposed optimization methods. The main optimization methods presented in this paper seem to be adaptations of those proposed in "Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015, pp. 1–40". Additionally, the supervised PageRank algorithm has been previously proposed. On a minor note, a conclusion section is expected, especially since there is sufficient space to include one. It is suggested that the authors present the optimization problem in a more general context, positioning the studied algorithm as a specific case. This would enable a clearer understanding of the contribution and its relation to the state-of-the-art.
