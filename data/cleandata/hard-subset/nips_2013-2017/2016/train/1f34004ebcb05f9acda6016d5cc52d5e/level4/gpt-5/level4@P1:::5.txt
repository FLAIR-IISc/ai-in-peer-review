Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies learning a Supervised PageRank model that assigns weights to nodes and edges via linear feature combinations. The authors consider a non-convex loss minimization problem over the model parameters and develop two two-level optimization schemes. On the lower level, they approximate the stationary distribution of a query-dependent Markov random walk, using the linearly convergent method of Nesterov–Nemirovski [17], and extend it to approximate derivatives. On the upper level, they propose: (i) a gradient-free method based on a biased gradient-free oracle with inexact function values, and (ii) a gradient-based method for constrained, possibly non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle and adaptive Lipschitz estimation. They provide convergence-rate guarantees for both methods, which were missing for the prior gradient-based approach in the Supervised PageRank literature [21]. They also analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.

Empirically, they apply the methods to a web-ranking task using user browsing graphs from a commercial search engine, reporting that both proposed methods outperform a state-of-the-art gradient-based baseline.

Strengths
- Clear two-level design that isolates stationary distribution estimation from parameter optimization.
- Use of a linearly convergent lower-level method [17], with complexity bounds and extensions to compute approximate gradients.
- Development of a constrained, inexact-oracle gradient-free method with theoretical guarantees, and an adaptive projected gradient method for inexact first-order oracles in non-convex settings.
- Convergence-rate theorems and complexity bounds are provided for both algorithms.
- Application to a realistic ranking setting and comparison to a known baseline method.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The organization pushes most of the key technical details and empirical evidence to the appendix, leaving the main text thin on proof sketches and on concrete experimental results (e.g., no detailed tables in the main body).
- Citations and attribution: Citations are sloppy. The problem is referred to as Supervised PageRank with citation to [21], but it appears that only semi-supervised PageRank is discussed there; please clarify. The paper also “reproduces a framework under a different name and notation from [21],” and the degree of novelty relative to that prior framework is insufficiently delineated.
- Assumptions and scope: Several technical assumptions seem strong and may not hold in practice. In particular, the restriction of the parameter search to an Euclidean ball Φ around a chosen ϕ0 may limit applicability and performance; this needs discussion and empirical justification.
- Experimental reporting: The main text does not report specific numbers (tables/plots) to substantiate the claimed improvements, nor does it discuss the magnitude or practical implications of the gains. Details such as how ϕ0 and R were selected are missing. It is unclear whether multiple, diverse graphs were tested beyond the single dataset described.
- Impact: While the theoretical bounds are strong, I am not convinced the empirical problem setting and evaluation demonstrate high practical impact without more thorough experiments and analysis.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical development appears careful, with convergence guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods under inexact oracles. However, proof outlines are largely absent from the main text, and key constants/assumptions (e.g., local convexity within Φ for the gradient-free method, and the oracle accuracy requirements) need clearer justification relative to empirical settings. Experimental evaluation is underreported in the main body.
- Clarity: The paper is hard to read due to grammatical issues and occasional imprecise terminology. Important algorithmic and experimental details are deferred to the appendix; the main text should include at least high-level proof sketches and core empirical tables.
- Originality: The combination of inexact-oracle optimization methods with the Supervised PageRank learning framework is useful, but some components closely follow existing ideas (e.g., [17], [16], [7]). The novelty lies mainly in adapting these to constrained, inexact settings and in providing end-to-end complexity bounds. The relationship to [21] should be made more precise to avoid confusion about what is new.
- Significance: Potentially significant if the methods demonstrably improve ranking quality and efficiency in practice. As presented, the empirical section does not convincingly establish practical gains or robustness across datasets.

Questions for the authors and suggestions
- How were ϕ0 and R chosen in practice? Please report sensitivity analyses showing how performance varies with different centers/radii of Φ, and discuss the risk of excluding good solutions.
- Did you evaluate on more than one graph or dataset? Please test across diverse graphs (sizes, structures) and report variability.
- Please move core empirical results (tables and plots) into the main text: absolute loss values, relative improvements, standard deviations, and statistical tests. Also report standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) and runtime/iteration counts for all methods.
- Clarify the extent of improvement: what is the magnitude and practical significance on the test sets?
- Provide high-level proof sketches for the main theorems in the paper body, and clearly list all assumptions with commentary on their realism.
- Clarify the citation to [21] and the precise differences between your framework and prior “Supervised” or “semi-supervised” PageRank variants.
- Discuss the choice of the inexact oracle accuracies and how they were set in experiments; include ablations on the lower-level iteration counts (N, N1, N2).
- Improve writing quality and fix grammatical errors; ensure all references are complete and consistently formatted.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle optimization for this application.
- Convergence guarantees for methods commonly used in practice but previously lacking such analysis in this context.
- Potentially useful for large-scale ranking tasks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical evidence in the main text to support claims of practical superiority.
- Ambiguity about novelty relative to prior Supervised PageRank work; sloppy citations.
- Strong assumptions (e.g., local convexity within Φ) and restrictive parameter domain not adequately justified.
- Poor writing and organization impede understanding.

Overall assessment
Promising theory with potentially useful algorithms, but the current presentation and empirical validation fall short. Strengthening the experimental section, clarifying originality and assumptions, and improving writing would substantially increase the paper’s impact.
