### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models. The authors introduce two novel two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a random gradient-free method (GFN). The core contribution lies in developing these methods within an "inexact oracle" framework, where the objective function and its gradient do not need to be computed exactly. This is particularly relevant for the Supervised PageRank problem, where exact calculations are computationally prohibitive. On the lower level, the algorithms use an efficient method from Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] to approximate the PageRank stationary distribution. On the upper level, this information is used to update the model parameters.

Crucially, and in contrast to the existing state-of-the-art method [21], the authors provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms. The GBN method is guaranteed to converge to a stationary point for non-convex problems, while the GFN method has a proven convergence rate for convex problems. Experiments on a large-scale, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine show that the proposed methods achieve a lower loss value than the previous state-of-the-art approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Originality:** The primary strength of this work is its rigorous theoretical underpinning. It is the first paper to provide formal convergence guarantees for the problem of learning Supervised PageRank. By framing the problem using the concept of an inexact oracle and developing algorithms with provable convergence rates, the authors move the field from heuristic approaches towards a more principled optimization framework.

2.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed methods are well-suited for large-scale applications. The inexact oracle approach directly addresses the computational infeasibility of calculating the exact stationary distribution and its derivatives in large graphs. The gradient-free method is particularly appealing as it avoids the expensive computation of the gradient matrix, which can be a significant bottleneck.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The algorithms are evaluated on a large, real-world web browsing graph dataset, which lends credibility to the experimental results. The paper demonstrates that both GBN and GFN consistently achieve lower final loss values on test data compared to the established baseline method (GBP). The inclusion of hyperparameters also aids in understanding the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is technically dense, and the notation can be difficult to follow. The frequent introduction of new symbols across Sections 2-5 makes it challenging for the reader to keep track of all the variables and their meanings.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should consider adding a symbol table at the beginning of the technical sections to improve readability.
    *   **Specific Example:** The parameter `N` is introduced in Equation 3.1 without an immediate definition. Its meaning as the "number of steps" is only clarified much later in the context of Algorithm 1 (line 193). Defining such crucial parameters upon their first appearance would greatly enhance the paper's clarity.

2.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The paper's abstract and conclusion claim that the proposed methods "outperform [the state-of-the-art] in the ranking quality." However, the experimental results presented in Table 1 and Figure 1 are exclusively based on the value of the loss function (Eq. 2.4). While minimizing this loss is the optimization objective, it is not a direct measure of ranking quality. A lower loss value does not automatically guarantee better performance on standard information retrieval metrics.
    *   **Suggestion:** To fully substantiate the claims of improved ranking performance, the authors should evaluate their models using standard ranking metrics such as nDCG, MAP, or Kendall's Tau on the held-out test set. This would provide a more direct and convincing demonstration of the practical impact of their methods.

3.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The paper's lower-level optimization is based on the method proposed by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17], which is chosen over the power method used in the baseline [21]. The authors provide a comparison in the supplementary materials (Figure 2) demonstrating the faster convergence of their chosen method.
    *   **Suggestion:** This comparison is a key justification for the paper's design choices. It would be beneficial to briefly summarize or include this result in the main paper's experimental section to strengthen the argument for using method [17].

4.  **Reproducibility:** The dataset used is proprietary and not publicly available. While the authors provide a clear description of its construction, this prevents the broader research community from being able to reproduce the results or build directly upon this work. This is understandable given the commercial source, but it remains a limitation.

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the field of learning-to-rank on graphs. The development of optimization methods with convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank problem is a valuable achievement. The main weaknesses lie in the clarity of the presentation and the scope of the experimental evaluation. Addressing the suggestions above, particularly by reporting standard ranking metrics, would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and impact.
