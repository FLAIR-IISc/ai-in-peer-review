

This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based GBN and gradient-free GFN) for learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors build upon Nesterov and Nemirovski's work [17] to develop methods that address the non-convex loss-minimization problem in Supervised PageRank [21], which previous approaches lacked theoretical convergence guarantees for. Unlike the state-of-the-art gradient-based method [21], the authors provide convergence rate guarantees for both their proposed methods, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The methods operate through a two-level structure: the lower level uses a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, while the upper level employs adaptive gradient methods to optimize the parameters.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations, with detailed proofs provided in supplementary materials. The authors establish convergence rate guarantees for both methods, showing that the computational complexity is well-controlled and that the methods avoid the need to compute large matrices directly. The experimental results on a real web ranking task demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art GBP algorithm in ranking quality, with GFN showing particularly strong results across all test sets. The hyperparameters are clearly specified, and the experimental setup is well-documented, though the dataset is not publicly available.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in the context of Supervised PageRank. The two-level approach is well-motivated and effectively leverages existing methods for approximating the stationary distribution. The experimental results are compelling, showing consistent improvements in ranking quality over the state-of-the-art. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in graph-based ranking with practical applications in search engines and social networks.

Weaknesses: The paper would benefit significantly from a comprehensive symbol table to help readers navigate the extensive mathematical notation. Several key symbols (such as N in Equation 3.1) are used without definition until later in the paper (Line 193), creating unnecessary confusion. The experimental evaluation focuses primarily on the loss function (2.4) rather than ranking quality metrics, which is the ultimate goal of the ranking task. A direct comparison with the foundational work [17] would provide valuable context for understanding the performance improvements achieved by the proposed methods. While the authors note that their methods outperform GBP, they do not explicitly compare with the underlying optimization framework that their work extends.

The paper addresses all four NIPS criteria effectively:

Quality: The technical content is sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-justified experimental results. The authors carefully address both the strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and logically organized, though the excessive mathematical notation without a symbol table significantly impedes readability. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, but could improve clarity by defining all symbols upon first use.

Originality: This work extends Nesterov and Nemirovski's foundational methods [17] to a novel application domain (Supervised PageRank) while providing convergence guarantees that previous work lacked. The combination of gradient-free and gradient-based methods with the inexact oracle concept represents a meaningful advancement over the state-of-the-art [21].

Significance: The results have practical implications for search engines and ranking systems, as the authors demonstrate improved ranking quality while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The methods could potentially increase both the reliability and speed of search engine ranking algorithms. The theoretical contributions to optimization methods with inexact oracles also have broader applicability beyond the specific ranking task.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with convergence guarantees that previous work lacked, provides strong empirical evidence of improved performance, and addresses a meaningful problem with practical applications. The two-level optimization framework is well-motivated and effectively leverages existing methods.

Arguments against acceptance: The lack of a symbol table creates unnecessary barriers to understanding, and the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by directly comparing with the foundational work [17] and focusing more on ranking quality metrics rather than just the loss function. The paper would benefit from clearer presentation of the mathematical notation.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances both the theory and practice of optimization for graph-based ranking problems. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, though the paper would be significantly improved with better organization of its mathematical notation. Given the importance of the problem and the quality of the technical contribution, I recommend acceptance.
