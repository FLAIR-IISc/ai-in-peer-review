

The authors motivate PageRank and introduce a framework incorporating node and edge weights derived from a feature model. They note that prior work on parameter learning for this model lacks performance guarantees. The authors propose to address this by combining an existing method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique suitable for loss functions that can only be estimated. The framework is reformulated under new notation, closely mirroring the approach in [21]. The paper details several technical assumptions, defines a loss function, and explains the problem's inherent difficulty. Key lemmas bound the approximate gradient, and a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle is presented with a performance theorem. Additional assumptions are made, leading to a tailored algorithm for the learning problem, accompanied by further bounds. Experimental results on a web graph from prior work are briefly outlined, but detailed results and analysis are deferred to the appendix. Critical details such as parameter choices and specific performance metrics are omitted, and the claim that the novel algorithm outperforms competitors lacks discussion of the magnitude or significance of the improvement. It is unclear whether diverse graph types were evaluated. The writing contains numerous grammatical errors, and citations are inconsistent—e.g., the problem is labeled "Supervised PageRank" with a citation to [21], which only discusses semi-supervised PageRank. I find the problem's impact limited. While the theoretical bounds are strong, I question whether the stated technical assumptions hold in practice; some appear restrictive, such as constraining the search space for φ to an R-ball around a fixed φ₀, which may significantly limit the algorithm's applicability and should be addressed. My primary concern is that the substantive content is relegated to the appendix: no proof outlines or full empirical results are provided in the main text. How was φ₀ and R selected in experiments? Were tests conducted on multiple graphs? The main paper omits tables, specific results, and discussion of practical implications. Though the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be theoretically sound, the paper itself fails to convincingly demonstrate their value.
