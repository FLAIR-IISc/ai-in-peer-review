- Paper studies learning a Supervised PageRank model via non-convex parameter optimization with a two-level scheme (stationary-distribution estimation + parameter optimization).
- Lower-level: uses Nesterov–Nemirovski’s linearly convergent method to approximate stationary distributions and extends it to compute approximate derivatives.
- Upper-level: proposes a biased gradient-free method with inexact function values and a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle and adaptive Lipschitz estimation.
- Provides convergence-rate guarantees and complexity bounds for both proposed algorithms, filling gaps in prior work.
- Empirical evaluation on a commercial user-browsing web-ranking task reports outperforming a state-of-the-art gradient-based baseline.
- Strength: clear two-level design separating distribution estimation from parameter optimization.
- Strength: use of a linearly convergent lower-level method with complexity analysis and gradient approximations.
- Strength: development of constrained, inexact-oracle gradient-free and adaptive projected gradient methods with theoretical guarantees.
- Strength: provides convergence theorems and complexity bounds for both methods.
- Strength: application to a realistic ranking setting with baseline comparison.
- Weakness: manuscript has many grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and poor organization with key technical details relegated to the appendix.
- Weakness: sloppy citations and unclear novelty/relationship to prior Supervised/Semi-supervised PageRank work (notably [21]).
- Weakness: strong technical assumptions and restricting parameter search to an Euclidean ball Φ may limit applicability; lacks discussion/justification.
- Weakness: experimental reporting in the main text is inadequate—no detailed tables, plots, or sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: unclear whether evaluation covers multiple diverse graphs/datasets beyond a single dataset.
- Impact concern: theoretical results are strong but empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate practical impact or robustness.
- Quality (NIPS): careful theoretical work but lacks main-text proof outlines and justification of key constants/assumptions relative to experiments.
- Clarity (NIPS): hard to read due to writing issues and deferred algorithmic/experimental details.
- Originality (NIPS): useful adaptation of existing methods to inexact/constrained settings with end-to-end bounds, but parts closely follow prior work and relationship to [21] must be clarified.
- Significance (NIPS): potentially significant if empirical gains hold broadly, but current presentation doesn’t establish this.
- Requests: report how ϕ0 and R were chosen and provide sensitivity analyses for Φ center/radius.
- Requests: evaluate on multiple, diverse graphs and report variability across datasets.
- Requests: move core empirical results (tables/plots, loss values, IR metrics, runtimes, std devs, significance tests) into the main text.
- Requests: clarify magnitude and practical significance of improvements on test sets.
- Requests: include high-level proof sketches and explicitly list assumptions with commentary on realism.
- Requests: clarify citation to [21] and precise differences from prior PageRank variants.
- Requests: explain choice of inexact oracle accuracies, include ablations on lower-level iteration counts (N, N1, N2).
- Requests: improve writing quality and fix references/formatting.
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theoretical contributions, convergence guarantees for widely used methods, potential utility for large-scale ranking.
- Arguments against acceptance: weak empirical evidence in main text, unclear novelty relative to prior work, restrictive assumptions, and poor writing/organization.
- Overall assessment: promising theoretical work with useful algorithms but needs stronger experiments, clearer novelty/assumptions, and much improved presentation.
