This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, which enhance traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors formulate this learning task as a constrained non-convex loss-minimization problem. The main contribution is the proposal of two novel two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem: a random gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle and an adaptive gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle. A key theoretical advance is that, unlike the previous state-of-the-art method [21], both proposed algorithms are accompanied by formal convergence rate guarantees and complexity analyses. The methods build on and extend existing optimization frameworks [7, 16] to handle constrained problems in the presence of inexact oracle information, which is a practical necessity for this large-scale problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality and Originality:** The paper's primary strength lies in its rigorous theoretical treatment of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. By providing convergence guarantees for their proposed algorithms, the authors address a significant gap in the prior work [21], which lacked such theoretical validation. The development of a gradient method for constrained non-convex problems with an inexact oracle (Section 5.1) and the adaptation of a random gradient-free method to a similar constrained setting (Section 4.1) represent solid, novel contributions to the optimization literature.
*   **Significance:** The work is well-motivated and tackles an important, practical problem in web-page ranking. Developing robust and theoretically-grounded algorithms for learning ranking functions has the potential for significant impact. The proposed methods, which outperform the previous state-of-the-art in experiments, could lead to more reliable and effective ranking models. The general framework for optimization with inexact oracles could also be valuable to researchers in other areas facing similar large-scale learning challenges.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The most critical weakness of the current manuscript is that the experimental results are entirely absent from the main body of the paper and are deferred to the supplementary materials. A scientific paper should be self-contained, and the experiments are essential for validating the practical performance and utility of the proposed algorithms. Without them, the main text feels incomplete and is difficult to assess thoroughly. I strongly urge the authors to include a summary of the experimental setup, key results (such as Table 1 and Figure 1), and a discussion of the findings in the main paper, even if some details remain in the supplement.
*   **Quality (Theoretical Assumptions):** There appears to be a potential mismatch between the theoretical assumptions and the problem formulation.
    *   Theorem 1, which provides the convergence analysis for the gradient-free method, assumes that the objective function `f` is convex. However, the loss function in Equation (2.4) is non-convex. The authors state (Section 4.2) that they assume the feasible set Φ is chosen as a small vicinity of a local minimum where `f` is convex. This is a very strong assumption that may not hold in practice. Could the authors provide more justification for this assumption or discuss how one would select such a set Φ? How does the method behave if this local convexity assumption is violated?
    *   For the gradient-based method, Theorem 3 establishes a bound for `‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖^2`. While this criterion ensures the algorithm makes progress, it is not immediately clear how this relates to convergence to a stationary point (e.g., `‖∇f(x)‖ -> 0`). Could the authors clarify the connection between their stopping condition and convergence to a stationary point for the non-convex problem?
*   **Quality (Evaluation of Efficiency):** While the experiments in the supplement show that the proposed methods achieve a lower final loss value, Figure 1 suggests that the gradient-free method (GFN) converges much more slowly (in terms of upper-level iterations) than the gradient-based methods. Computational efficiency is a crucial aspect of any practical algorithm. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a discussion of the algorithms' practical efficiency. Reporting wall-clock times or the total number of arithmetic operations required to reach a certain solution quality would provide a more complete comparison and better guide practitioners in choosing between the methods.

**Summary of Pro and Con Arguments:**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Addresses an important problem with a rigorous and novel theoretical approach.
    *   Provides the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank.
    *   Makes sound contributions to the general theory of optimization with inexact oracles.
    *   Experimental results (in the supplement) demonstrate improved performance over the state-of-the-art.

*   **Con:**
    *   The main paper is incomplete as it lacks any experimental results.
    *   The convexity assumption required for the gradient-free method's convergence proof seems inconsistent with the non-convex problem statement.
    *   The practical efficiency and runtime of the proposed algorithms are not discussed.

In conclusion, this paper presents valuable theoretical contributions to both machine learning and optimization. However, its current presentation as a self-contained scientific work is hampered by the exclusion of experimental results from the main text. The questions regarding the theoretical assumptions also need to be addressed to solidify the paper's claims. If the authors can integrate their experimental results into the main paper and clarify the theoretical points raised, I believe this would be a strong contribution to the conference.
