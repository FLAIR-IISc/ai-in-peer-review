This paper addresses the formulation of Supervised PageRank models as a constrained non-convex optimization problem by incorporating properties of nodes and edges. The authors propose two approaches to solve this problem: a gradient-free method leveraging an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method employing an inexact first-order oracle. A major concern is the placement of experimental results exclusively in the supplementary material, which leaves the main text feeling incomplete and hinders a full assessment of the contributions.

Detailed comments are as follows:

1.  The experimental results are presented only in the supplement. This significantly detracts from the paper’s completeness and makes it difficult to evaluate the practical impact of the proposed methods directly from the main text. Including a concise summary of key findings within the main body would greatly strengthen the paper.

2.  Theorem 1 assumes convexity, while the objective function in (2.4) is inherently non-convex. The authors state they choose the set Φ to ensure local convexity, but the explanation lacks detail. A clearer justification of how Φ is selected to guarantee local convexity within a region around the solution is needed.

3.  Theorem 3 provides a bound for ‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖2. It would be helpful to clarify if, and how, this bound guarantees the convergence of the sequence xk to a stationary point. A more direct link between the bound and convergence criteria would improve the theorem's clarity.

4.  Figure 1 in the supplementary material suggests that the gradient-free method (GFN) converges relatively slowly. The authors should also report the computational efficiency (e.g., runtime, number of iterations) of both methods to provide a more complete comparison and contextualize the convergence rate.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant problem in ranking with a focus on incorporating node and edge features, extending traditional PageRank.
*   The theoretical analysis, particularly the convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, is a significant contribution.  The handling of inexact oracle information is a valuable aspect of the work.
*   The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing methods and motivate the need for new approaches.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   The exclusion of experimental results from the main text substantially weakens the paper’s impact.
*   The explanation regarding the local convexity assumption and the selection of the set Φ needs further elaboration.
*   The connection between the provided convergence bound and actual convergence to a stationary point is not explicitly stated.
*   The lack of efficiency comparisons (e.g., runtime) limits the assessment of practical usability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents novel theoretical results with convergence guarantees for two optimization methods applied to a relevant problem.
*   The approach of handling inexact oracle information is well-motivated and contributes to the field of optimization.
*   The experimental results (though in the supplement) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The incomplete presentation of experimental results in the main text hinders a comprehensive evaluation of the work.
*   The lack of clarity surrounding the convexity assumption and the convergence guarantee reduce confidence in the theoretical results.
*   The absence of efficiency comparisons makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank models. However, the incomplete presentation of experimental results and the lack of clarity in certain theoretical aspects significantly detract from its impact. Addressing these concerns, particularly by including a concise summary of the experimental findings within the main text, would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance.  I would lean towards a **weak reject** at this stage, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the aforementioned weaknesses.
