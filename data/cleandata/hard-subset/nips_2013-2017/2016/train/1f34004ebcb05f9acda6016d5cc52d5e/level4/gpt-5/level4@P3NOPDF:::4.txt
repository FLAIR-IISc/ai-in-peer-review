Summary
The paper studies the problem of learning supervised PageRank models with a non-convex loss and proposes two optimization approaches: a first-order (gradient-based) method and a randomized gradient-free (zeroth-order) method. The goal is to enable training when gradients are available but difficult to compute accurately or when only function evaluations are accessible. The topic is timely and relevant to large-scale link analysis and ranking, where non-convex objectives and complex features are common.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important problem: optimizing supervised PageRank parameters under non-convexity.
- Presents complementary algorithms for different information regimes (with and without reliable gradients), which increases applicability.
- The randomized gradient-free approach can be useful when gradients are noisy, costly, or unavailable, and may leverage parallelization over function evaluations.
- The paper is positioned at the intersection of large-scale optimization and ranking, with potential impact on IR and graph mining applications.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation needs clearer reporting of computational efficiency. For methods meant to scale, wall-clock training time, time per iteration, and convergence speed are essential to assess practicality.
- It is unclear how the proposed methods compare to strong baselines (e.g., established first-order methods, stochastic variants, or existing techniques for supervised PageRank), both in terms of final performance and efficiency.
- The paper would benefit from a more precise description of the objective, assumptions, and differentiability properties (e.g., how gradients are computed through PageRank, handling of damping factors, personalized restarts, and feature parametrization).
- The convergence behavior and stopping criteria are not sufficiently discussed. For non-convex problems, details on stationarity measures, tolerance thresholds, and iteration counts are important.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., step sizes, smoothing parameters for the zeroth-order estimator, batch sizes if stochastic sampling is used) appears underexplored.
- Reproducibility details are limited or missing (datasets, preprocessing, exact model specifications, random seeds, hardware).

Requests and suggestions
- Report runtime comprehensively:
  - Wall-clock time to reach a target objective value or validation metric.
  - Time per iteration and number of iterations until convergence or stopping.
  - Hardware details (CPU/GPU type, memory) and implementation framework.
  - Per-iteration complexity with respect to number of nodes, edges, and features, and the cost of PageRank/vector–matrix operations.
- Compare both proposed methods against:
  - Strong gradient-based baselines (e.g., SGD/Adam/Adagrad with tuned schedules).
  - Any existing supervised PageRank or learning-to-rank approaches that optimize similar objectives.
  - Include runtime and final performance for all baselines.
- Provide ablations:
  - Initialization strategies and their effect on convergence.
  - Gradient accuracy vs. performance (if approximate gradients are used).
  - For the gradient-free method, the effect of sampling dimension, smoothing/noise level, and number of function evaluations.
- Clarify optimization details:
  - Step-size selection (fixed, line search, adaptive), momentum, and stopping criteria.
  - How gradients are computed through the PageRank computation (e.g., power iteration differentiation, implicit differentiation, or automatic differentiation) and any approximations used.
  - Handling of constraints or regularization on parameters.
- Expand the experimental section:
  - Use standard benchmarks (or clearly describe proprietary datasets) with sizes (#nodes, #edges, #queries).
  - Evaluation metrics relevant to ranking (e.g., NDCG, MAP) and statistical significance testing.
  - Scalability plots showing performance and runtime as graph size grows.
  - Memory usage, especially for large graphs.
- Improve clarity of presentation:
  - Precisely define the supervised PageRank model, feature map, and loss function.
  - Summarize theoretical properties, if any, and the assumptions under which they hold.
  - Streamline notation and include a short algorithmic sketch for both methods.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a meaningful and challenging problem and proposes methods that could be useful in practice, especially the randomized gradient-free approach for settings with unreliable gradients. However, the current version does not provide sufficient evidence of computational efficiency or comparative advantage. Strengthening the empirical evaluation—particularly by reporting detailed runtimes and scalability, expanding baseline comparisons, and clarifying optimization details—would substantially improve the paper’s credibility and impact. Pending these additions, I view the contribution as promising but not yet conclusively validated for large-scale, real-world deployment.
