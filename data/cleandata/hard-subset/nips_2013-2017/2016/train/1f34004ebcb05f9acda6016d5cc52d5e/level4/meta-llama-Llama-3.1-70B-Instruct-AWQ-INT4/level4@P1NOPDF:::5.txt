The authors motivate PageRank and then introduce a framework that incorporates node and edge weights from a feature model. They point out that a previous algorithm for learning the parameters of the model lacked performance guarantees. To address this, they propose combining a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique, which is suitable for scenarios where the loss function can only be estimated. They reformulate a framework under a different name and notation from [21]. The authors discuss several technical assumptions, a loss function, and explain why the problem is challenging to solve. They state some lemmas that bound the approximate gradient and discuss a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle, accompanied by a theorem on its performance. After making additional assumptions, they construct an algorithm for the learning problem and adapt the gradient-free method to their problem, stating further bounds. The authors outline some details of their experimental results on a web graph used in related work, but relegate the detailed reporting of results and analysis to the appendix. However, some parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform other algorithms, there is no discussion of the extent or implications of this improvement. It is unclear whether a diverse set of graphs were considered for testing. The writing is marred by grammatical errors, and citations are sloppy – the problem is referred to as Supervised PageRank with a citation to [21], but only semi-supervised PageRank appears in that reference.

I am not convinced that the problem solved has a significant impact. While the bounds presented are strong, I have doubts about whether the technical assumptions stated will hold in empirical data. Some assumptions, such as the restriction of the search space of phi to an R-ball around a chosen phi0, may greatly limit the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm, and this should be discussed. My primary concern with the paper is that the majority of the content appears in the appendix. The authors neither provide proof outlines nor full empirical results. It is unclear how phi0 and R were chosen in the empirical results section, nor whether tests were run on more than one graph. The main body of the paper lacks specific results, including tables, and does not discuss the practical consequences of the improvements. While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be powerful, the content in the paper itself fails to convince me of its significance.
