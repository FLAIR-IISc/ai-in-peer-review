

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, where node and edge features influence the ranking process. The authors build upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which generalizes traditional PageRank by incorporating features of nodes and edges through linear combinations with model parameters. The key contribution is the development of two two-level optimization methods (one gradient-based and one gradient-free) for solving the non-convex loss-minimization problem of learning these parameters, with theoretical convergence guarantees that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks.

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, and (2) a gradient-free method adapted for constrained optimization with function value calculated with known accuracy. The authors provide theoretical analysis of both methods, including complexity bounds, and demonstrate empirical improvements over the existing method on a web ranking task.

Quality: The paper presents technically sound methods with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully establish convergence rate guarantees for both algorithms, addressing the key limitation of previous work [21]. The theoretical results are well-motivated and properly contextualized within the literature on optimization with inexact oracles. The empirical evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over the state-of-the-art, though the paper would benefit from more detailed reporting of results in the main body (the full experimental details are relegated to the appendix). The authors carefully address the challenges of calculating the loss function and its gradient in the Supervised PageRank framework, providing appropriate complexity bounds.

Clarity: The paper would benefit from significant improvements in clarity. The writing contains several grammatical errors and inconsistent notation (e.g., using both "ϕ" and "phi" for the parameter vector). The presentation of the mathematical framework is dense and could be better organized, particularly in the sections describing the two-level optimization methods. While the theoretical results are well-developed, the connection between the abstract optimization framework and the specific Supervised PageRank application could be made more explicit. The paper's main contributions are well-identified, but the presentation of the algorithms (especially Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 4) would be clearer with more intuitive explanations.

Originality: The paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature by extending gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact oracles, and by providing a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for the specific problem of Supervised PageRank learning. While the core ideas (two-level optimization, inexact oracles) are not entirely novel, their application to Supervised PageRank with rigorous convergence analysis is novel. The paper adequately references related work, though it would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent advances in optimization for machine learning (e.g., [7, 16, 25] are cited but not sufficiently contrasted with the current work).

Significance: The problem addressed is significant for web ranking applications, where accurate ranking of web pages based on user behavior is crucial. The authors demonstrate empirical improvements on a real-world dataset, suggesting potential practical impact for search engines. However, the paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the theoretical guarantees or the extent to which the improvements translate to real-world performance. The authors claim their methods "increase the reliability and speed of a search engine," but this is not substantiated with specific performance metrics or comparisons to alternative ranking approaches. The limited discussion of the practical consequences of the improvements weakens the significance argument.

Strengths:
- Theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which the state-of-the-art lacks
- Careful analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy of lower-level approximation and overall computational complexity
- Empirical demonstration of performance improvements over existing methods on a real ranking task
- Rigorous complexity analysis of both algorithms

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient detail in the main paper; critical results and experimental details are relegated to the appendix
- Ambiguous presentation of key parameters (e.g., the choice of ϕ̂ and R in the constraint set Φ)
- Lack of discussion regarding the practical implications of the theoretical improvements
- Grammatical errors and unclear writing throughout the paper
- The paper fails to adequately address how the technical assumptions (particularly the choice of Φ as a ball around ϕ̂) might impact real-world applicability

Arguments for acceptance:
- The theoretical contributions are significant for the optimization community, providing convergence guarantees for a non-convex optimization problem with inexact oracle that previous work lacked
- The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task
- The paper makes a valuable contribution to the Supervised PageRank literature by providing theoretically grounded optimization methods

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper's main contributions (the algorithms and their theoretical analysis) are presented in a way that requires readers to consult the appendix for critical details
- The practical significance of the improvements is not clearly articulated or supported with sufficient evidence
- The writing quality is poor, with numerous grammatical errors that undermine the clarity of the presentation
- The paper fails to adequately address how the technical assumptions might impact real-world applicability

Overall, this paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis, but the presentation is weak and the practical significance is not well-established. The main value lies in the theoretical guarantees for optimization methods with inexact oracles, but these are not sufficiently connected to the practical impact for the target application. The paper would be significantly strengthened by moving critical details from the appendix to the main body and addressing the grammatical issues.

I recommend rejection due to the insufficient presentation of results and lack of clarity in the main text, despite the strong theoretical contributions. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to improve clarity, provide more detailed reporting of results in the main body, and better articulate the practical implications of the improvements.
