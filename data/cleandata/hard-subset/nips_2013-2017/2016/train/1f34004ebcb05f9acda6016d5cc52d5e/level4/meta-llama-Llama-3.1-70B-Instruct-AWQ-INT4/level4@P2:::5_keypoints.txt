- Proposes a novel approach to learning Supervised PageRank models that incorporates node and edge features.  
- Formulates a non-convex loss-minimization problem for the model.  
- Develops two optimization methods: a gradient-based and a gradient-free approach.  
- Algorithms use an inexact oracle framework and come with theoretical convergence guarantees.  
- Paper is well-motivated and clearly explains limitations of existing Supervised PageRank methods.  
- Technical assumptions and the loss function are clearly defined.  
- Lemmas bounding the approximate gradient are useful and well-presented.  
- The general gradient-free method is well-explained and includes a valuable performance theorem.  
- Adaptation of the gradient-free method to the learning problem is solid with complexity bounds provided.  
- Experimental results on a web graph are promising but sparsely reported.  
- Key experimental details (parameters, graph size, iteration counts) are missing from the main text.  
- Writing is clear overall but contains grammatical errors and at least one incorrect citation.  
- Major concern: unclear practical impact and whether assumptions hold on real data.  
- Specific assumption critique: restricting phi search space to an R-ball around phi0 may be limiting.  
- Overreliance on the appendix for proofs and empirical analysis reduces accessibility; include key proofs and results in the main body.  
- Paper lacks discussion on practical consequences and comparative benefits over existing methods.  
- Suggestions: add more experimental detail, discuss assumption implications, include proof outlines and full results in the main text, and expand on practical applicability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, thorough analysis, well-defined assumptions and loss, useful gradient bounds, and a strong gradient-free contribution.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited perceived impact, questionable empirical validity of technical assumptions, restrictive search-space choice, heavy appendix dependence, and insufficient practical discussion.
