Summary
The paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for supervised PageRank: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN). Both methods optimize ranking-quality losses over parameters that influence a PageRank-like vector without requiring exact evaluations of the global objective. The approach builds on the framework of Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref: 17) for inexact oracles and establishes convergence-rate guarantees toward a target accuracy. The gradient-free variant guarantees monotonic decrease of the loss. The methods are designed to avoid explicit construction or inversion of large matrices, improving scalability. The paper provides hyperparameter settings, methodological details (Sections 2–5), and complete proofs in the supplementary material. Experiments indicate competitive or improved performance, though the evaluation has limitations (see below).

Strengths
- Methodological contribution: Clear formulation of supervised PageRank as a bilevel problem and development of two algorithms (GBN and GFN) with provable convergence guarantees under inexact objective information.
- Practicality and scalability: The approach avoids computing large matrices, which is crucial for PageRank-scale problems, and leverages iterative primitives instead.
- Theoretical guarantees: Both methods come with convergence-rate estimates given a target accuracy; GFN also guarantees descent of the loss.
- Clarity of algorithmic setup: Hyperparameters and implementation choices are described, and proofs are provided in the supplement, aiding reproducibility despite non-public datasets.
- Relevance: Supervised PageRank remains a meaningful setting for learning-to-rank with graph structure; the proposed framework could be useful beyond the specific instantiation studied here.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Notational overload and missing definitions: The paper uses a large number of symbols, and not all are introduced when first used. For example, N in Eq. (3.1) is defined only much later. This makes the paper difficult to follow on first read.
- Limited experimental evaluation of ranking quality: The experiments primarily compare against GBP on the loss function; comparisons on standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) are limited or insufficiently highlighted. Claims of superior ranking quality would be more convincing with comprehensive metric-based comparisons.
- Baselines: Since the approach generalizes ref: 17, a direct empirical comparison to that baseline (or its most relevant instantiation) is needed to quantify the benefits of the proposed variants. Additional learning-to-rank baselines would also help contextualize performance.
- Reproducibility: While descriptions are clear, the datasets are not publicly available. Without code or scripts, reproducing exact results may still be difficult. Providing code, pseudocode for inner/outer loops, and parameter search ranges would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical efficiency: The paper emphasizes avoiding large matrices, but more concrete runtime and memory results are needed. Per-iteration complexity, total wall-clock time, and convergence profiles (accuracy vs. iterations) would help assess practical scalability.

Questions for the Authors
- Oracle/inexactness model: The methods “do not require the exact value of the objective function.” Please clarify the oracle assumed for GBN and GFN (e.g., noisy function values, stochastic gradients, or unbiased gradient estimates). How is the inexactness controlled, and how does it affect the stated convergence rates?
- Accuracy parameter: How is the target accuracy chosen in practice? What is the trade-off between target accuracy, runtime, and ranking quality?
- Gradient-free method: What are the variance and sample complexity of the gradient-free estimator (if applicable)? How many function evaluations per iteration are required?
- Inner solver details: Which iterative solver is used to compute PageRank vectors and their sensitivities, and how is its tolerance coupled to the outer-loop accuracy to ensure overall convergence?
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How sensitive are results to step sizes, smoothing parameters, and regularization? Please include ablations.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add a notation table summarizing all symbols, with units/dimensions and the section where they first appear. Define N and other key symbols at first use.
- Expand the experimental section:
  - Report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, P@k) alongside loss values.
  - Include direct comparisons to ref: 17 (or its closest operational counterpart) and to additional baselines where feasible.
  - Provide runtime, memory usage, and convergence plots. Include scalability tests with graph size and feature dimension.
  - Detail data splits, feature construction, and hyperparameter tuning ranges; report multiple random seeds.
- Add concise pseudocode for GBN and GFN (outer loop, inner solver interface, stopping criteria) to ease implementation.
- If datasets cannot be released, consider releasing synthetic benchmarks and code to reproduce figures and tables.

Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated and technically solid contribution to supervised PageRank optimization, with provable convergence under inexact oracles and a practical design that avoids heavy linear algebra. The theoretical development is a clear strength. The main weaknesses lie in notation clarity and, more importantly, in the experimental evaluation, which should more thoroughly assess ranking quality and include stronger baselines, including a direct comparison to ref: 17.

I lean toward a weak accept, contingent on improving the empirical section (metrics, baselines, and efficiency reports) and clarifying the notation and oracle assumptions. Confidence: medium.
