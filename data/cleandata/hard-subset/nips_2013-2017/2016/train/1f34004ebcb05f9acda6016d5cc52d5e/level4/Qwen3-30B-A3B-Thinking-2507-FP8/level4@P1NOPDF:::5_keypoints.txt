- Proposes learning node and edge weights in a PageRank-like model by combining stationary-distribution approximation with gradient-free optimization.  
- Core framework largely restates prior work [21] under new notation, offering limited novelty in problem formulation.  
- Introduces technical assumptions and a loss function, framing the learning task as challenging.  
- Provides lemmas bounding approximate gradients and analyzes a gradient-free method with a biased oracle, but leaves key details unresolved.  
- Major concern: empirical validation and clarity are insufficient.  
- Experiments are reported on a single web graph (same as [21]) with detailed results and parameter choices relegated to the appendix.  
- Paper does not explain how φ₀ and the R-ball radius were chosen, nor whether these choices affect performance.  
- No experiments on diverse graph types, so generalizability to other graphs is unclear.  
- Lacks specific metrics, tables, or quantitative evidence demonstrating superiority over baselines and the practical significance of improvements.  
- Inconsistent problem labeling: claims "Supervised PageRank" while [21] describes semi-supervised PageRank, creating confusion about novelty.  
- Restricting the search to an R-ball around φ₀ is a questionable assumption that may severely limit practical applicability and is not adequately discussed.  
- Theoretical bounds are strong but their relevance depends on empirical validity of assumptions, which the paper fails to substantiate.  
- Writing and presentation issues: grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology, sloppy citations, and core contributions hidden in the appendix.  
- The gradient-free theoretical work appears technically sound, but empirical shortcomings and restricted assumptions weaken practical value.  
- Recommendations: clarify novelty relative to [21], move full experimental details into the main text (including graph diversity and parameter selection), and discuss limitations of the R-ball constraint.  
- Overall: technically plausible approach but falls short in demonstrating practical relevance; claims are not sufficiently supported.
