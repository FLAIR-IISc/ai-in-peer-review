The authors introduce a weighted PageRank framework built upon a feature model, addressing limitations of prior work concerning performance guarantees in parameter estimation. Their approach combines a Markov random walk stationary distribution approximation with a gradient-free optimization technique applicable to loss functions with estimation-based evaluation. The presented framework largely replicates existing work ([21]) under alternative notation. The paper details several technical assumptions, defines a loss function, and acknowledges the challenges inherent in solving the proposed problem, supported by lemmas providing approximate gradient bounds. A general gradient-free method, employing a biased oracle, is discussed with a corresponding performance theorem. Building on this, the authors develop a learning algorithm tailored to their problem, extending the gradient-free method and providing associated bounds. Experimental results on a web graph, previously used in related studies, are summarized, with comprehensive details deferred to the appendix. 

However, the reporting of experimental parameters is incomplete, and claims of improved performance lack sufficient quantitative evidence or discussion of the magnitude and significance of these improvements. The breadth of graph datasets used for evaluation is unclear. The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors and inconsistencies in citation (e.g., the problem is termed “Supervised PageRank” citing [21], which describes semi-supervised PageRank). 

I question the practical impact of the solved problem. While the theoretical bounds are strong, the empirical validity of the stated assumptions is uncertain; some appear quite restrictive. For instance, limiting the search space for φ to an R-ball around φ0 could significantly hinder the algorithm’s applicability, a limitation that warrants discussion. Crucially, the core contributions – proofs and detailed empirical results – are relegated to the appendix. The main body lacks proof sketches and substantive experimental data. Specific questions arise regarding the selection of φ0 and R in the experiments and whether the evaluation extended beyond a single graph. Furthermore, the practical implications of the observed improvements remain unclear. Despite the potential power of the proposed bounds, analysis, and algorithm, the paper, in its current form, fails to persuasively demonstrate its contributions.
