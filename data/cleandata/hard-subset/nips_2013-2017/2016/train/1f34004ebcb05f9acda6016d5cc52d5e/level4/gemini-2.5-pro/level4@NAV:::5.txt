This paper addresses the challenge of learning parameters for a feature-based PageRank model. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the absence of performance guarantees in a prior algorithm. They propose a novel method that combines an existing technique for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method suitable for problems where the loss function can only be estimated.

The technical contribution begins with the presentation of a framework, which appears to be a re-notation of the one introduced in [21]. The authors proceed to outline several technical assumptions and a loss function, explaining the difficulties inherent in the optimization problem. A series of lemmas are stated to bound the approximate gradient. The paper then introduces a general gradient-free method that utilizes a biased oracle, accompanied by a theorem on its performance. By introducing further assumptions, the authors construct a specific algorithm for their learning problem, adapting the general method and providing additional theoretical bounds.

The experimental evaluation is briefly outlined on a web graph used in related work. However, a detailed reporting of results and their corresponding analysis is deferred to the appendix. The authors state that their algorithm outperforms existing methods, but this claim is not supported by a discussion of the magnitude or practical implications of the improvement. Key experimental parameters are not reported, and it is unclear if the evaluation was conducted on a diverse set of graphs to test for generalizability.

The paper suffers from several weaknesses. The writing contains numerous grammatical errors, and citations are imprecise; for example, the problem is referred to as "Supervised PageRank" with a citation to [21], which appears to only discuss semi-supervised PageRank. A more significant concern is that the paper does not sufficiently establish the impact of the problem being solved. While the theoretical bounds are strong, their relevance is questionable, as it is unclear whether the underlying technical assumptions will hold in practice. Some assumptions seem particularly restrictive, such as the constraint of the search space for φ to an R-ball around a chosen φ₀, which may limit the algorithm's efficacy and applicability. This limitation is not discussed.

The most critical issue is that the substantive technical content, including proofs and detailed empirical results, is almost entirely located in the appendix. The main body of the paper lacks proof outlines and fails to present any tables or specific experimental outcomes. This makes it impossible to verify the paper's claims. To strengthen the submission, the authors should address the following: How were φ₀ and R chosen for the experiments? Was the algorithm tested on more than one graph? What are the practical consequences of the reported improvements? While the theoretical analysis and algorithm may be powerful, the paper in its current form fails to convey the significance of its contributions.
