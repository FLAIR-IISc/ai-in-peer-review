This paper presents two novel optimization methods – a gradient-based approach (GBN) and a gradient-free approach (GFN) – designed to address the supervised PageRank problem. A key strength of this work is the theoretical analysis provided; the gradient-based method offers a convergence rate guarantee to achieve a specified accuracy, and the gradient-free method demonstrably decreases the loss function with each iteration. Importantly, both methods avoid the computational burden of evaluating the objective function exactly and provide estimates for their convergence rates. Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform current state-of-the-art techniques in terms of ranking quality. The authors clearly document the hyperparameter settings used in their experiments. While the datasets are not publicly available, the paper provides a sufficiently detailed description to facilitate reproduction of the results.

The proposed methods build upon the two-level optimization framework developed by Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref:17). By circumventing the need for exact objective function calculations, these methods offer a significant advantage when dealing with large-scale problems where computing and inverting large matrices can be prohibitive. The paper thoroughly details the methodology from Section 2 through Section 5, and the mathematical proofs are included in the supplementary material. 

However, the paper would benefit significantly from the inclusion of a symbol table. The density of notation throughout the equations makes it challenging to follow the derivations, and readers may find themselves losing track of variable definitions. For example, the variable *N* appears in Line 139 and Equation 3.1 without explicit definition; its meaning as the number of steps is not clarified until Line 193. Improving the clarity of the notation would substantially enhance the paper’s readability.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation focuses primarily on comparing the loss function values to those of Gradient Boosting (GBP), rather than directly assessing and comparing ranking quality. A more direct comparison of ranking performance against competing methods would provide a stronger validation of the proposed approach. Given that this work generalizes the method in ref:17, including a direct comparison to that baseline would be particularly valuable. This would allow for a clear assessment of the performance gains achieved through the authors’ extensions and modifications.
