Summary
The paper formulates supervised PageRank as a constrained, generally nonconvex optimization problem that incorporates node and edge features. It proposes two algorithms to learn the model parameters: (i) a gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle (GFNM), and (ii) a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle (GBNM). The authors provide theoretical guarantees under oracle inexactness and report empirical results in the supplementary material.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practically relevant problem: learning query-dependent PageRank with rich features under constraints.
- Clearly motivated need for handling inexact oracles, given that each function or gradient evaluation involves solving a PageRank-like subproblem.
- Provides theoretical analysis for both zero- and first-order methods under inexact information.
- The two complementary algorithms cover use cases where derivatives are hard or impossible to obtain.

Weaknesses
- All empirical results are relegated to the supplement; the main paper is incomplete without summaries of datasets, baselines, metrics, and key findings.
- Assumption–reality gap: Theorem 1 assumes convexity while the objective in (2.4) is stated to be nonconvex; conditions ensuring convexity are unclear.
- Convergence claims are hard to interpret: it is not clear whether the provided bounds imply convergence to a stationary point in the nonconvex setting.
- Reported convergence of GFNM appears slow (per Figure 1 in the supplement), and there is no quantitative analysis of efficiency or scalability.
- Several clarity and notation issues (e.g., definition and tractability of the feasible set Φ; consistency of indices; cost of projections).

Detailed comments and questions
1) Experiments in the main text
- The main paper lacks an experimental section. Please summarize in the main text: datasets (size, graph statistics), feature sets (node/edge properties), baselines (e.g., classical PageRank, personalized PageRank, prior supervised PageRank methods, learning-to-rank models), evaluation metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, etc.), and key quantitative results (e.g., improvement percentages, statistical significance).
- Include at least one figure/table showing convergence (objective vs. iterations and wall-clock), final ranking quality, and ablations on oracle accuracy.
- Provide computational budgets: time per iteration, number of PageRank solves, total training time, and memory footprint.

2) Convexity vs. nonconvexity (Theorem 1 vs. equation (2.4))
- Theorem 1 assumes convexity, but (2.4) is described as nonconvex. Please specify precisely:
  a) Under what choices of loss, regularization, and feasible set Φ does the objective become convex?
  b) If convexity cannot be guaranteed, clarify whether Theorem 1 applies only to a restricted special case (state it explicitly), and present separate results for the general nonconvex case.
  c) Provide examples of Φ that are both practically meaningful and make the problem convex (e.g., linear parameterization with convex loss and convex constraints), and discuss the tractability of projecting onto Φ.

3) Convergence to stationarity (Theorem 3)
- Theorem 3 bounds M_K (x^K − x^{K+1}) in some norm. Please clarify:
  a) Does this imply that the sum over K of ||x^{K+1} − x^K||^2 is finite (i.e., square-summable differences), and under what step-size conditions?
  b) In the nonconvex setting, can you show that the expected gradient norm (or a suitable stationarity measure, e.g., projected gradient or gradient mapping) converges to zero? If so, provide the rate (e.g., O(1/√K)) and assumptions on oracle inexactness (bias, variance, Lipschitz constants).
  c) If only subsequence convergence holds, state this explicitly and specify additional conditions (e.g., Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz) that would ensure full-sequence convergence.

4) Efficiency and scalability
- Figure 1 suggests GFNM converges slowly. Please:
  a) Report wall-clock time vs. objective (and vs. validation metric), not only iteration counts.
  b) Compare GFNM and GBNM under matched computational budgets, since zero-order methods typically require more function evaluations per effective descent step.
  c) Analyze sensitivity to oracle accuracy: how do tighter inner solves (more PageRank iterations) affect outer-loop convergence and total time?
  d) Discuss the complexity of each outer iteration: number of calls to the PageRank subroutine, complexity per call, and any acceleration (e.g., warm starts, early stopping, residual thresholds).
  e) Provide results on larger graphs to demonstrate scalability, including sparsity exploitation and parallelization.

5) Oracle models and assumptions
- Clearly define the inexact oracle models for both methods: bias and variance bounds, dependence on smoothing radius (for zero-order), Lipschitz/smoothness constants, and how inner-solve tolerance translates to oracle errors.
- For GFNM, specify the dimension dependence in the complexity (often O(d/ε^2) or worse); for GBNM, state the iteration complexity to reach ε-stationarity with inexact gradients.
- Explain how hyperparameters (step sizes, smoothing radius, inner tolerance) are chosen and tuned.

6) Feasible set Φ and projections
- Describe Φ concretely (e.g., box constraints, simplex, norm balls) and the cost of projection. If projection is nontrivial, indicate whether you use proximal mappings or approximate projections and how this affects theory.

7) Clarity and presentation
- Include pseudocode for both algorithms with oracle definitions and stopping criteria.
- Ensure consistent notation (e.g., use x^k, not xk; define M_K, norms, and any matrices involved).
- State assumptions at the start of each theorem and map them back to the practical setting (what they mean for PageRank solves).

Minor suggestions
- Consider a hybrid strategy: initialize with GFNM when gradients are unavailable, then switch to GBNM once a reliable gradient estimator is accessible.
- Explore variance-reduction or momentum for GBNM, and improved smoothing schemes for GFNM.
- Provide qualitative examples of ranking improvements to illustrate practical impact.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a relevant learning-to-rank problem with thoughtful algorithmic and theoretical components. However, the absence of core empirical evidence in the main text and unresolved gaps between assumptions and the stated nonconvex objective make the submission feel incomplete. Clarifying the convexity conditions, strengthening the nonconvex convergence claims (in terms of stationarity), and moving key experimental results and efficiency analyses into the main paper would substantially improve the work. Pending these revisions, I am not able to make a strong recommendation for acceptance.
