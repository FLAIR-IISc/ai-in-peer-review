In this paper, the authors propose two optimization methods to solve the supervised PageRank problem. Unlike existing methods, this paper proposes a gradient-based method with a theoretically guaranteed convergence rate and can achieve a given accuracy. The proposed gradient-free method has a guaranteed loss function decrease value. These two methods do not require the exact value of the objective function and have an estimated convergence rate, and also outperform the state-of-the-art in terms of ranking quality. The hyperparameters used in both methods are provided. The datasets are not publicly available, but the description is clear, suggesting reproducibility should be feasible. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods, namely gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN), to solve the supervised PageRank problem. The lower and upper level optimization builds upon the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref:17). The proposed method does not require the exact value of the objective function and provides a proven estimate of the convergence rate given an accuracy, thus avoiding the computation with large matrices while maintaining good performance. From Section 2 to Section 5, the authors detail their methods and include proofs in the supplementary material. However, a symbol table would be beneficial, as the equations contain many symbols, making it easy to lose track of their meaning. For example, in Line 139 and Equation 3.1, the authors use $N$ without defining its meaning (number of iterations) until Line 193. Also, in the experimental section, the authors only compare their work with GBP regarding the loss function, and not the ranking quality. Moreover, since this work generalizes ref:17, a direct comparison with it would be valuable to assess performance improvements.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both proposed methods, addressing a key limitation of previous work (ref: 21).
*   **Practical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in ranking quality, which is a crucial outcome for the targeted application.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methods are based on a solid theoretical foundation, building upon established optimization techniques (e.g., Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work).
*   **Novelty:** The work addresses a challenging optimization problem and introduces new approaches to tackle it, specifically dealing with inexact oracles and convergence analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper contains a large number of symbols and equations, which can make it difficult to follow. A symbol table would significantly improve readability.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The comparison with the state-of-the-art focuses primarily on the loss function, and lacks a direct comparison of ranking quality metrics. A comparison with the method generalized (ref:17) is also missing.
*   **Data Availability:** The lack of publicly available datasets hinders reproducibility, though the authors provide a sufficient description.
