

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank parameters via non-convex loss minimization, proposing two-level optimization methods that provide convergence guarantees missing in prior work. The authors build upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends classical PageRank by incorporating node/edge features through linear combinations. While [21] employed a two-level gradient-based method without convergence proofs (due to inexact stationary distribution estimates), this work develops both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches using an inexact oracle framework. Crucially, the authors establish theoretical convergence rates for both methods, which is a significant advancement over the state of the art. The paper also provides complexity bounds and demonstrates empirical superiority on a real-world web ranking task, outperforming [21] in both loss minimization and ranking quality.

**Strengths:**  
The paper’s primary strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution. The authors successfully prove convergence rates for both optimization methods under inexact oracle conditions—a critical gap in [21] and related work. The inexact oracle approach (using the linearly convergent method from [17] for stationary distribution approximation) is well-motivated and leverages recent advances in optimization for large-scale problems. The complexity analysis (Theorems 2 and 4) explicitly connects lower-level approximation accuracy to overall computational cost, providing actionable insights for practitioners. Empirically, the methods consistently outperform [21] across all test sets (p < 0.005 via paired t-tests), with the gradient-free method (GFN) showing particularly strong results. The experimental design (using Yandex data, query-dependent graphs, and relevance labels) is robust and directly relevant to real-world ranking applications.

**Weaknesses:**  
The main weakness is insufficient detail on computational complexity comparisons. While the paper derives complexity bounds (Theorems 2–4), it does not explicitly compare the *actual runtime* of the proposed methods against [21] or other baselines. For instance, the draft review’s vague note about "reporting the time of the proposed optimization method" is valid—readers would benefit from a concrete runtime analysis (e.g., "GFN runs in X seconds vs. GBP’s Y seconds on dataset size Z") to assess practical scalability. Additionally, the paper does not discuss how the choice of the parameter *R* (defining the feasible set Φ) impacts convergence in practice, though this is acknowledged in the supplementary materials.

**Originality and Significance:**  
This work stands out for its novel integration of random gradient-free optimization with inexact oracle techniques for constrained non-convex problems—a first in the context of Supervised PageRank learning. The problem itself is significant: graph-based ranking is central to search engines, and the paper’s methods directly improve reliability and speed (as noted in the conclusion). Compared to recent NIPS work (e.g., [6, 12, 21]), this paper advances the state of the art by resolving the convergence gap in learning algorithms, not just proposing new ranking models. The theoretical framework (Sections 4.1–5.1) also contributes broadly to optimization literature.

**Clarity and Quality:**  
The paper is well-structured and technically sound, with clear definitions (e.g., inexact oracle conditions in Sections 3–5) and thorough proofs in the supplementary materials. The experimental section (Section 6) adequately supports the claims, though the computational complexity gap mentioned above slightly weakens the practical impact. The writing is precise, though minor phrasing improvements (e.g., "random gradient-free" instead of "random-gradient-free") would enhance readability.

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept.** The paper makes a strong, novel contribution with rigorous theory, clear empirical validation, and significant practical relevance. The theoretical guarantees resolve a key limitation in prior work, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate real-world utility. While the runtime analysis could be expanded, this does not undermine the core scientific contribution. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on high-quality, impactful machine learning research. The authors should address the computational complexity point in the final version, but this is a minor refinement rather than a flaw.
