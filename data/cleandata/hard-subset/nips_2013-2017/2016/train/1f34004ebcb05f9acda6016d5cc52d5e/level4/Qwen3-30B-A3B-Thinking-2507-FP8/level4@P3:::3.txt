

This paper proposes two novel optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank models, addressing the non-convex loss-minimization problem that arises when incorporating node and edge features into the ranking framework. The authors present a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN), both operating within a two-level optimization framework that leverages Nesterov's inexact oracle approach [17] to provide convergence rate guarantees previously unavailable in the field. The work builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21] and extends the theoretical foundations of optimization methods for Markov chain-based ranking problems.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretical convergence guarantees for both optimization methods, which is a major advancement over the state-of-the-art method [21] that lacked such guarantees. The authors prove convergence rates for both methods under appropriate assumptions, demonstrating that their algorithms can achieve a specified accuracy with bounded computational complexity. Second, they provide a thorough analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. Third, the experimental results demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the existing state-of-the-art algorithm [21] in terms of ranking quality on real-world web page ranking tasks.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported by detailed proofs (provided in supplementary materials). The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous approaches by addressing the critical limitation of [21] - the absence of convergence guarantees. The connection to Nesterov and Nemirovski's work [17] is appropriately established, and the authors correctly identify the limitations of standard optimization methods for this specific problem due to constraints on the parameter vector and the inability to compute exact loss function values.

The writing is generally clear, though the paper would benefit from a symbol table to help readers navigate the numerous mathematical notations. For instance, the meaning of the parameter N (number of iterations in the lower-level approximation) is not clearly defined until Line 193, creating unnecessary confusion when first encountering Equation 3.1. Similarly, the relationship between the error bounds (δ1, δ2) and the overall algorithm parameters could be made more explicit.

The significance of this work is substantial for the ranking community. The proposed methods provide a theoretically grounded approach to learning Supervised PageRank models that can be applied to large-scale web ranking problems. The ability to provide convergence guarantees while avoiding exact computation of the loss function (which is computationally prohibitive for large graphs) represents a meaningful advance in the field. The experimental results on a real-world web browsing dataset demonstrate practical improvements in ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art, supporting the authors' theoretical claims.

However, there are some weaknesses that should be addressed. While the paper compares the proposed methods to the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of loss function values, it would be more compelling to demonstrate improvements in standard ranking metrics (such as NDCG or MAP) that directly reflect ranking quality. The authors mention that their methods "outperform" existing approaches in ranking quality, but this claim isn't substantiated with appropriate metrics in the main results. The comparison with the baseline Nesterov method [17] is also insufficient; the paper should explicitly demonstrate how their two-level framework improves upon the direct application of Nesterov's approach to this specific problem.

The authors have made a valuable contribution by developing optimization methods with convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank problem. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The experimental results provide empirical support for the theoretical claims. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a significant technical contribution to optimization methods with clear applications in ranking problems.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Clear distinction from previous work (addressing the lack of convergence guarantees in [21])
- Thorough analysis of trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational complexity
- Practical application to a real-world ranking problem with demonstrated improvements

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient comparison with the baseline Nesterov method [17] in the experimental section
- Limited demonstration of ranking quality improvements using standard evaluation metrics
- Ambiguous notation throughout the paper without a symbol table
- Some claims about "better ranking quality" lack specific metric-based evidence

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution with clear theoretical and practical significance. The paper advances the state of the art in optimization for ranking problems and provides a valuable framework for future work. With minor revisions to clarify notation and strengthen the experimental comparison with standard ranking metrics, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper provides theoretically grounded optimization methods with convergence guarantees for a significant problem in ranking, with empirical validation showing clear improvements over the state-of-the-art. The work represents a meaningful advancement in both optimization theory and its application to ranking.

Con-acceptance arguments: The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive experimental comparison using standard ranking metrics and a clearer presentation of notation. The comparison with the baseline Nesterov method [17] is insufficient.
