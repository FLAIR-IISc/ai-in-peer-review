This paper introduces two novel two-level optimization methods—a gradient-based (GBN) and a gradient-free (GFN) approach—for solving the supervised PageRank problem. Drawing inspiration from the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17], the authors develop algorithms that offer significant advantages over existing methods. A key contribution is that these methods do not require the exact value of the objective function, thereby avoiding computationally expensive operations on large matrices. The proposed methods are supported by strong theoretical guarantees: the gradient-based method has a provable convergence rate to a specified accuracy, and the gradient-free method ensures a guaranteed decrease in the loss function per iteration.

The empirical results presented are compelling, demonstrating that the proposed methods outperform state-of-the-art baselines in terms of ranking quality. The authors have also provided the hyperparameters used in their experiments and a clear description of the datasets. Although the datasets are not publicly available, the detailed description should facilitate the reproduction of the results.

While the technical contributions are sound and well-supported by proofs in the supplementary material, the paper could be improved in several key areas:

**Clarity and Presentation:**
The main weakness of the paper lies in its presentation. The derivations in Sections 2-5 are dense with mathematical notation, which makes them difficult to follow. The readability would be significantly improved by including a symbol table at the beginning of the paper. This would help readers keep track of the numerous variables and parameters. For example, the symbol $N$ is introduced in Line 139 and Equation 3.1, but its definition (the number of steps) is not clarified until Line 193, nearly two pages later. A central table of notation would prevent such confusion and streamline the reading experience.

**Experimental Evaluation:**
The experimental evaluation, while promising, could be more comprehensive.
1.  The comparison with the GBP baseline is limited. The authors only report results with respect to the loss function value and do not provide a direct comparison of ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP). Since improved ranking is the ultimate goal of the task, this comparison is essential for a complete evaluation.
2.  The paper positions itself as a generalization of the framework in Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. Therefore, a direct empirical comparison against the method from [17] would be highly valuable. Such an experiment would clearly quantify the practical benefits and performance gains achieved by the proposed generalization.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and empirically promising approach to supervised PageRank. The core ideas are strong, and the proposed methods offer clear advantages. However, the paper's impact could be substantially enhanced by addressing the issues of notational clarity and by strengthening the experimental section with more comprehensive comparisons.
