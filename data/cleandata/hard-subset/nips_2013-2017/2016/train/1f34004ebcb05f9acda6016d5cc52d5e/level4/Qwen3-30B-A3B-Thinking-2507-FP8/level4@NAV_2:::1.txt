

The paper addresses a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning Supervised PageRank parameters, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating features of nodes and edges. The authors propose two-level optimization methods that operate with inexact oracle information, providing theoretical convergence guarantees that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks. The approach builds on prior work in Supervised PageRank [21, 22] and extends Nesterov & Spokoiny's gradient-free method [16] and Nesterov's gradient methods [7] to constrained non-convex problems with inexact oracle information. The main contribution lies in adapting these optimization frameworks to the specific context of Supervised PageRank, where the loss function and its derivatives cannot be computed exactly but can be approximated with known accuracy.

Strengths: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a significant technical contribution. The experimental results demonstrate that both proposed algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art [21] on a real ranking task, with the gradient-based method achieving better convergence rates. The paper is well-structured with clear mathematical formulations, and the proofs appear sound (though I did not verify all technical details). The authors carefully analyze the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is particularly valuable for practical implementation.

Weaknesses: The main novelty consists of adapting existing optimization techniques to this specific problem rather than introducing fundamentally new optimization methods. The connection to the seminal work by Nesterov & Spokoiny [16] on random gradient-free methods is not sufficiently emphasized, and the relationship to prior Supervised PageRank work [21, 22] could be more clearly articulated. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of how the proposed framework fits within the broader landscape of optimization methods for machine learning, particularly highlighting what makes this specific application different from general optimization problems. Additionally, the conclusion is somewhat brief and could better synthesize the main contributions and their significance.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors carefully address both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly regarding the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational complexity.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with logical organization. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, though the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results.

Originality: While the core optimization techniques are adaptations of existing methods, the specific application to Supervised PageRank with inexact oracle information represents a meaningful contribution to the field. However, the paper would better establish the novelty by more explicitly differentiating their approach from prior work on Supervised PageRank and optimization methods.

Significance: The proposed algorithms demonstrate improved performance on a real ranking task compared to state-of-the-art methods, suggesting practical relevance for web search applications. The theoretical guarantees provide a stronger foundation for using these methods in practice. The work advances the state of the art by providing convergence guarantees where none existed before for this specific problem.

Pro: Theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, clear experimental validation, well-structured technical content, practical significance for ranking tasks.

Con: Main optimization techniques are adaptations of prior work rather than fundamentally new contributions, insufficient differentiation from existing Supervised PageRank work, conclusion could be more comprehensive, and the generalization of the framework to broader optimization contexts is not sufficiently developed.

Overall, the paper makes a solid technical contribution to the application of optimization methods in graph-based ranking. While the core optimization techniques are not entirely novel, their application to this specific problem with theoretical guarantees is valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better articulate the novelty and place the work within the broader context of optimization and ranking algorithms.
