

The authors propose a framework for learning node and edge weights in a PageRank-like model, motivated by prior work on supervised PageRank. They note that existing parameter learning methods lack performance guarantees and aim to address this by combining a Markov random walk stationary distribution approximation with a gradient-free optimization approach adapted for loss estimation. However, the core framework is largely a restatement of [21] under new notation, with insufficient novelty in the problem formulation. The paper introduces technical assumptions, a loss function, and characterizes the problem as challenging. While they provide lemmas bounding approximate gradients and analyze a general gradient-free method using a biased oracle, key details remain unresolved.  

The most significant concerns involve empirical validation and clarity. The main paper outlines experimental results on a single web graph (previously used in [21]) but relegates detailed results, parameter choices, and analysis to the appendix. Crucially, the paper does not report:  
- How φ₀ and R (the radius of the R-ball constraint on the search space) were selected for experiments, nor whether this choice impacts performance.  
- Whether results were tested on diverse graph types beyond the single web graph, raising questions about generalizability.  
- Specific metrics or tables demonstrating the claimed superiority of the proposed algorithm over baselines, leaving the magnitude and practical significance of improvements unclear.  

The problem statement contains inconsistencies: the title and text refer to "Supervised PageRank," but the cited reference [21] describes *semi*-supervised PageRank, creating confusion about the problem’s novelty. The technical assumptions also warrant scrutiny. For instance, restricting the search space to an R-ball around a fixed φ₀ may severely limit the algorithm’s applicability in practice—a limitation that should be explicitly discussed rather than assumed. While the theoretical bounds are strong, their relevance hinges on whether these assumptions hold empirically, which the paper fails to substantiate.  

The writing contains multiple grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology (e.g., "constriction" instead of "restriction" for the search space). Citations are sloppy: the claim of "Supervised PageRank" is misaligned with [21]. More critically, the paper’s main body lacks substantive content; the core contributions—proof outlines, key empirical results, and algorithmic implementation details—are confined to the appendix. This severely undermines the paper’s ability to convince readers of its impact. Without explicit justification for φ₀/R choices, validation on multiple graphs, or concrete results in the main text, the claimed advantages remain unsubstantiated.  

The authors’ theoretical work on gradient-free optimization is technically sound, but the paper’s failure to address empirical limitations and its over-reliance on the appendix make it difficult to assess the method’s practical value. The significance of the problem itself is also questionable: while the bounds are rigorous, the assumptions appear restrictive for real-world graph data, and the lack of diverse experimental validation weakens the case for the algorithm’s broader utility.  

In summary, the paper presents a technically plausible approach but falls short in demonstrating its practical relevance. The core innovations are obscured by insufficient empirical reporting, unclear problem framing, and unexamined assumptions. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) clarify the problem’s novelty relative to [21], (2) provide full experimental details (including graph diversity, parameter selection, and quantitative results) in the main text, and (3) discuss the limitations of the R-ball constraint in practice. As written, the paper’s claims are not sufficiently supported.
