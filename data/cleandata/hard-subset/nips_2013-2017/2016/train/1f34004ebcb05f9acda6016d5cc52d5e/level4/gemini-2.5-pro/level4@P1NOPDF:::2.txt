This paper formulates Supervised PageRank models as a constrained, non-convex optimization problem that incorporates features of nodes and edges. To solve this problem, the authors propose two algorithms: a gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle.

My primary weakness of this paper is the complete omission of experimental results from the main text. All empirical evaluation has been relegated to the supplementary material. This makes the main paper feel incomplete and prevents the reader from assessing the practical performance and utility of the proposed algorithms in a self-contained manner. A strong paper must be able to stand on its own, and key results validating the proposed methods are essential for this.

Detailed comments are listed below:

1.  **Placement of Experimental Results:** As mentioned above, placing all experimental results in the supplement is a significant issue. The main text should include a summary of the key findings, datasets, and comparisons to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approaches.

2.  **Convexity Assumption:** There appears to be a contradiction regarding the convexity assumption. Theorem 1 assumes that the objective function is convex, yet the function defined in Equation (2.4) is generally non-convex. The authors should clarify under what conditions, if any, the set Φ can be chosen to ensure the objective function is convex, thereby satisfying the theorem's assumptions.

3.  **Convergence Guarantee:** Theorem 3 provides a bound related to the difference between successive iterates ($x_k$ and $x_{k+1}$). It is not clear from the text whether this bound is sufficient to guarantee that the sequence $\{x_k\}$ converges to a stationary point. The authors should explicitly state and prove the convergence properties of their algorithm in light of this result.

4.  **Algorithm Efficiency:** Based on Figure 1 in the supplementary material, the GFNM algorithm appears to converge quite slowly. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the algorithms' efficiency. The authors should consider reporting metrics such as wall-clock time or the number of function evaluations required to reach a desired accuracy and compare them against relevant baselines.
