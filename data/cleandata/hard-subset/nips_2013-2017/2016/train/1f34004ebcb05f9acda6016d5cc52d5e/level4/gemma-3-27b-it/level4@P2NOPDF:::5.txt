This paper introduces a framework for PageRank with node and edge weights derived from a feature model, addressing a limitation of previous approaches that lacked performance guarantees. The authors propose a method combining stationary distribution approximation via Markov random walks with a gradient-free optimization technique suited for problems with estimated loss functions. While the framework bears resemblance to prior work [21] – specifically, the approach described is effectively a re-implementation under altered notation and termed “Supervised PageRank” despite [21] focusing on semi-supervised learning – the paper aims to provide theoretical guarantees.

The authors carefully outline technical assumptions, define a loss function, and articulate the challenges inherent in solving the proposed learning problem. They present lemmas bounding approximate gradient estimates and a theorem characterizing the performance of a general gradient-free method utilizing a biased oracle. Building upon this foundation, they construct a learning algorithm tailored to their problem, adapting the gradient-free method and establishing associated bounds. 

However, the presentation suffers from significant deficiencies. The empirical evaluation, conducted on a web graph previously used in related work, is severely limited. Crucially, detailed results and analysis are relegated to the appendix, hindering a clear understanding of the algorithm’s performance. Specific parameter choices – namely, the selection of *φ₀* and *R* in the empirical section – are not explained. It remains unclear whether the algorithm was tested on a diverse set of graphs beyond the single example provided. The main body of the paper lacks tables or specific quantitative results, making it difficult to assess the claimed outperformance over other algorithms and, importantly, the *magnitude* of any such improvement. Without this detail, the practical implications of the authors’ contributions remain unclear.

Furthermore, the writing contains numerous grammatical errors and exhibits inconsistent citation practices. More fundamentally, I question the overall impact of the problem being addressed. While the theoretical bounds presented are strong, the paper provides insufficient justification for the practical relevance of the proposed solution. Several of the stated assumptions appear restrictive. For instance, constraining the search space for *φ* to an R-ball around *φ₀* could substantially limit the algorithm’s efficacy and generalizability, a limitation that warrants thorough discussion. 

My primary concern lies with the significant displacement of critical information to the appendix. The absence of proof sketches *and* comprehensive empirical results in the main body of the paper severely undermines its persuasive power. The presented analysis and algorithm may be theoretically sound, but the current manuscript fails to convincingly demonstrate their value. A more complete and accessible presentation of results, coupled with a clear discussion of the limitations and practical implications of the assumptions made, is required to significantly improve this work.
