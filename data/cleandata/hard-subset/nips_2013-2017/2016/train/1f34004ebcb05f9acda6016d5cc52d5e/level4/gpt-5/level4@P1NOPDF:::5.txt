The paper motivates PageRank and proposes a framework in which node and edge weights are derived from features. The authors note that a prior algorithm for learning the model parameters lacked performance guarantees. They aim to address this by combining an existing method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov chain with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method suited to settings where the loss can only be estimated. However, much of this framework appears to reproduce the setup of [21] under a different name and notation.

The paper presents several technical assumptions and a loss function, and explains why the optimization problem is challenging. It states lemmas that bound the approximate gradient, introduces a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle, and provides a theorem on its performance. Building on this, the authors make additional assumptions, develop an algorithm for the learning problem, adapt the gradient-free method to their setting, and derive further bounds.

While the theoretical development culminates in nontrivial bounds, the empirical and expository components of the paper are insufficient. The experimental section briefly describes results on a web graph used in prior work, but defers virtually all details and analysis to the appendix. Important parameters are not reported, and although the proposed algorithm is claimed to outperform baselines, there is no discussion of the magnitude, consistency, or practical significance of the improvement. It is also unclear whether experiments were conducted on more than one graph or whether the test suite is diverse enough to support general claims.

The writing suffers from numerous grammatical issues, and the citations are sloppy. For example, the problem is called “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], but [21] appears to focus on semi-supervised PageRank. Renaming a known framework without clearly delineating novelty, and with inconsistent citation, undermines the contribution.

I am not convinced of the impact of the problem as posed here. Although the bounds are strong, I have substantial doubts that the technical assumptions will hold in realistic data. Some assumptions also seem overly restrictive. In particular, constraining the parameter vector phi to lie within an R-ball around a chosen phi0 may significantly limit the practical efficacy and applicability of the algorithm. This design choice should be explicitly justified and its implications thoroughly discussed, ideally with empirical sensitivity analysis.

My main concern is that almost all substantive material is relegated to the appendix. The main paper neither provides proof outlines for key results nor presents complete empirical findings. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution and robustness of the methods. At a minimum, the main text should include:
- Clear statements of the core lemmas and theorems with proof sketches that highlight the key ideas and where the assumptions are used.
- Comprehensive reporting of experimental setup and results: datasets (including multiple, diverse graphs), parameter choices, metrics, baselines, and ablations.
- Tables or figures with specific numbers, confidence intervals where appropriate, and discussion of practical consequences.

Several concrete questions need answers in the main body:
- How were phi0 and R chosen for the experiments? Were they tuned on validation data, set a priori, or derived from domain knowledge? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Were experiments run on more than one graph, and if so, what are their characteristics (size, sparsity, degree distribution, topic diversity)?
- What are the exact performance numbers relative to each baseline, and what is the variability across runs?
- What practical benefits follow from the theoretical improvements (e.g., speedups, stability under noise, sample efficiency), and in what regimes do they matter?

In its current form, the paper’s theoretical work may be solid, but the presentation and empirical validation are too thin to be convincing. Strengthening the exposition, clarifying the relationship to [21] and prior art, justifying and testing the assumptions (especially the R-ball restriction around phi0), and providing thorough, transparent experiments on diverse datasets are necessary for the paper to meet the bar.
