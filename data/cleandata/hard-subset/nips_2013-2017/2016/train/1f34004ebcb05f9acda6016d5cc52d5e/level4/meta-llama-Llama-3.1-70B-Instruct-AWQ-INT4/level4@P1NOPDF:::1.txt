This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk conducted by weights of nodes and edges, which are in turn defined by random walks based on nodes' and edges' features. Notably, the optimization problem cannot be solved by existing optimization methods that require exact values of the objective function. To address this challenge, the proposed approach operates on two levels.

At the first level, a linearly convergent method is employed to estimate an approximation to the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk. This approach is validated through various experiments, and the authors demonstrate that the value of the loss function can be approximated with any given precision. Furthermore, they develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems using an inexact oracle and prove its convergence to the stationary point of the problem. The key contribution lies in adapting this approach to the case of constrained optimization problems, where the value of the function can be calculated with some known precision. By proving the convergence of this method, the authors effectively exploit it on the second level of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be correct, although I did not scrutinize all of them thoroughly. However, my primary concern is that the main optimization techniques presented here are adaptations of those proposed in "Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015, pp. 1–40". Additionally, the supervised PageRank algorithm has been previously proposed. On a minor note, I would have expected a conclusion, especially given that there is sufficient space to include one.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest presenting the optimization problem in a more general context, positioning the studied algorithm as a specific case. This would enable a clearer placement of the contribution within the state-of-the-art landscape, thereby highlighting its significance and novelty.
