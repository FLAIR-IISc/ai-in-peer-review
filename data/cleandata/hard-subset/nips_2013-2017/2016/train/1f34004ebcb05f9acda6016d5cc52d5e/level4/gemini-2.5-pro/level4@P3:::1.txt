### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking model where node and edge weights are parameterized by their features. A key challenge in this problem is that the loss function, which depends on the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk, cannot be calculated exactly. This "inexact oracle" setting has previously prevented theoretical convergence guarantees for existing methods.

The authors propose a two-level optimization framework to solve this problem. At the lower level, they employ an efficient iterative method [17] to compute an approximation of the stationary distribution and its derivatives. Crucially, this method allows them to bound the approximation error, providing a controllable inexact oracle. At the upper level, they develop and analyze two optimization algorithms designed to work with this oracle:
1.  A random gradient-free method, which adapts the framework of Nesterov and Spokoiny [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is known only up to a certain accuracy.
2.  A gradient-based method, which generalizes the work of Devolder et al. [25] for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle.

The main contribution of the paper is providing, for the first time, theoretical convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. The authors also provide a full complexity analysis for both proposed algorithms. These theoretical results are supported by experiments on a real-world web ranking task, where the proposed methods are shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art algorithm [21].

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Contribution (Significance/Quality):** The paper successfully bridges a significant theoretical gap. The previous state-of-the-art method for this problem lacked convergence guarantees due to the inexactness of the gradient calculations. By formalizing the problem within the inexact oracle framework and providing rigorous proofs of convergence rates, this work places the learning of Supervised PageRank on a much firmer theoretical footing.

2.  **Methodological Soundness (Quality):** The proposed two-level framework is elegant and well-motivated. The choice of a lower-level algorithm [17] that provides explicit accuracy bounds is key to enabling the convergence analysis of the upper-level methods. The development of both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches provides valuable flexibility. The proofs, from what I could verify, appear to be sound.

3.  **Clarity and Organization (Clarity):** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It starts by motivating the problem, systematically describes the model, and then presents the general optimization frameworks before applying them to the specific learning problem. This structure makes the contributions easy to follow.

4.  **Empirical Validation (Quality):** The experiments, while focused on one dataset, effectively demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods. Both the gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) algorithms are shown to achieve a better final loss value than the previous state-of-the-art (GBP), validating the theoretical contributions with practical performance gains.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality of Optimization Techniques (Originality):** A potential weakness is that the core optimization algorithms are presented as adaptations or extensions of existing frameworks ([16], [7], [25]). The novelty lies in applying and adapting these methods to the specific constrained, non-convex, inexact setting of Supervised PageRank, rather than in inventing fundamentally new optimization principles. The authors could strengthen the paper by more explicitly highlighting why this adaptation is non-trivial and what specific technical challenges were overcome.

2.  **Restrictive Assumption for Gradient-Free Method (Quality):** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. Given that the overall problem is non-convex, this is a strong local assumption. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when this assumption might hold and the implications if it is violated.

3.  **Minor Presentation Issues (Clarity):** The paper currently lacks a dedicated conclusion section. Adding one would help summarize the key takeaways and could provide an opportunity to discuss potential future directions, which would make the paper a more complete scientific contribution.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, a significant theoretical advance for this problem.
*   The proposed framework for handling the inexact oracle is rigorous and could potentially be applied to other similar problems.
*   The theoretical claims are well-supported by empirical results showing superior performance over the previous state-of-the-art.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-structured.

**Con:**
*   The underlying optimization methods are extensions of existing work, which might limit the perceived originality.
*   The convergence proof for the gradient-free method rests on a local convexity assumption that may be restrictive in practice.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution. My recommendation is leaning towards acceptance. To further improve the manuscript, I would suggest the following:

*   Consider presenting the optimization framework in a slightly more general context first, and then showing the Supervised PageRank problem as a specific, important application. This would help to better position the broader methodological contribution with respect to the state-of-the-art in optimization with inexact oracles.
*   Add a dedicated conclusion section to summarize the paper's findings and impact.
*   Briefly discuss the local convexity assumption for the gradient-free method in more detail, perhaps mentioning how sensitive the method is to its violation or in what practical scenarios it might be expected to hold.
