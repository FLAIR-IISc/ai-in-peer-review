

This paper addresses the learning of Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features through linear combinations with model parameters. The authors formulate the parameter learning as a non-convex loss-minimization problem and propose two-level optimization methods to solve it, with the key contribution being theoretical convergence guarantees that were previously missing in the state-of-the-art approach [21].

The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced by Zhukovskii et al. [21], which uses random walk models where transition probabilities depend on node and edge features. While [21] proposed a two-level gradient-based method for learning the parameters, it lacked convergence guarantees because the lower-level power method for estimating stationary distributions and their derivatives provided only approximate values. The authors address this gap by developing two new two-level methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free.

The key technical contributions are: (1) a linearly convergent method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution with any desired accuracy, which they prove has the lowest complexity bound among existing methods; (2) a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, with convergence rate analysis to stationary points; and (3) a gradient-free method adapted to constrained problems with function values calculated with known accuracy. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis of both methods, including complexity bounds for achieving a given accuracy. Their experimental results demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality on a real-world web page ranking task, while providing theoretical convergence guarantees.

The paper is well-structured and the mathematical arguments appear sound. The authors effectively connect their contributions to related work, particularly noting how their methods extend Nesterov and Spokoiny's random gradient-free minimization [25] to constrained problems with inexact oracle. The experiments are appropriately designed to validate the theoretical claims, with careful attention to computational complexity and empirical performance.

Strengths:
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Demonstrates both theoretical novelty (convergence rates for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle) and practical value (superior performance in experiments)
- The two-level framework elegantly separates the lower-level approximation of the stationary distribution from the upper-level optimization
- Comprehensive complexity analysis that accounts for the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational cost

Weaknesses:
- The connection to Nesterov and Spokoiny [25] is somewhat implicit; the authors should more explicitly highlight how their constrained optimization framework extends the unconstrained setting in [25]
- The conclusion section is brief and could better synthesize the broader implications of their approach for large-scale graph-based learning problems
- While the experiments show empirical superiority, the paper could better explain why the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art beyond the numerical results

The paper is well-written and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors have made a significant contribution by providing convergence guarantees for a practical learning problem that has previously lacked theoretical underpinnings. Their methods represent a meaningful advance over [21] by combining theoretical rigor with empirical performance.

The main concern is that the paper does not sufficiently position its contribution in the broader context of optimization methods. While the authors reference Nesterov and Spokoiny [25], they don't clearly articulate how their constrained optimization framework extends the unconstrained setting. A more detailed comparison with related optimization literature would strengthen the paper's contribution statement.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of graph-based learning by providing the first theoretically guaranteed optimization methods for Supervised PageRank. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates practical value through empirical results. The authors have made a clear advance over the state of the art by resolving the convergence question that was previously unaddressed in [21].

Pros for acceptance:
- First theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Rigorous analysis of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods
- Practical implementation with empirical validation
- Clear demonstration of outperforming state-of-the-art methods

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of how the constrained optimization framework extends unconstrained methods in the literature
- Conclusion section could be expanded to better contextualize the broader implications
- Minor issues with positioning the contribution relative to existing optimization literature

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality scientific contributions. It advances the state of the art in graph-based learning with both theoretical rigor and empirical validation.
