The authors motivate the use of PageRank and introduce a framework incorporating node and edge weights derived from a feature model. They identify a lack of performance guarantees in a prior algorithm for learning the parameters of this model, and propose a solution combining a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique suitable for loss functions with only estimated values. The presented framework largely reproduces existing work from [21] under a different nomenclature and notation. The paper details several technical assumptions, a defined loss function, and explains the challenges inherent in solving the problem. The authors state several lemmas bounding the approximate gradient, discuss a general gradient-free method utilizing a biased gradient-free oracle, and present a corresponding performance theorem. Further assumptions are introduced, leading to the construction of a learning algorithm adapted from the gradient-free methods, accompanied by additional bounds. Experimental results are outlined on a web graph previously used in related work, though detailed reporting and analysis are relegated to the appendix. Crucially, several parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform existing methods, the extent and implications of this improvement remain unclear. It is also unclear whether the evaluation encompassed a diverse set of graph structures. 

The writing contains numerous grammatical errors, and the citations are imprecise; for instance, the problem is referred to as “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], which in fact addresses semi-supervised PageRank. Furthermore, I am not convinced that the addressed problem represents a significant advancement. While the theoretical bounds presented are strong, I harbor doubts regarding the validity of the stated technical assumptions in empirical settings. Some assumptions appear particularly limiting; for example, restricting the search space of φ to an R-ball around a chosen φ₀ may substantially constrain the algorithm's efficacy and applicability – a limitation that should be thoroughly discussed. 

My primary concern is the deferral of substantial content to the appendix. The paper lacks both proof outlines and complete empirical results within the main body. Specifically, how were φ₀ and R chosen in the experimental setup? Were tests conducted on more than one graph? The absence of tables or specific results in the main text severely hinders evaluation. Finally, what are the practical consequences of the reported improvements? Although the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be technically powerful, the paper’s current presentation fails to convincingly demonstrate its value.
