

This paper formulates Supervised PageRank models as constrained non-convex optimization problems by leveraging node and edge properties, proposing two solution approaches: a gradient-free method using an inexact zeroth-order oracle and a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle. The problem is timely and well-motivated, with potential applications in network analysis. However, the paper suffers from critical flaws that significantly undermine its contribution and completeness.

The most severe issue is the absence of core experimental results in the main text. All results are relegated to the supplement, violating NIPS standards for substantive contributions. The main body lacks quantitative comparisons, convergence behavior, or performance metrics on benchmark datasets, making it impossible to assess the practical efficacy of the proposed methods. This omission renders the paper incomplete and detracts from its technical claims. The authors should integrate key results—such as accuracy, convergence rates, and scalability comparisons—into the main text, relegating only supplementary details (e.g., ablation studies) to the appendix.

Three technical concerns further weaken the paper’s rigor. First, Theorem 1 assumes convexity to establish convergence, yet the objective function in Equation (2.4) is explicitly non-convex. The authors must clarify how the set $\Phi$ is chosen to ensure convexity, as this gap invalidates the theorem’s premise. Without this, the theoretical foundation is unsound. Second, Theorem 3 provides a bound for $\|K(x_K - x_{K+1})\|$, but this does not guarantee convergence to a stationary point. The bound only addresses step-size behavior; the authors should explicitly state whether the sequence $\{x_k\}$ converges to a critical point (e.g., via $\lim_{k \to \infty} \|\nabla f(x_k)\| = 0$) or clarify the theorem’s scope. Third, Figure 1 (supplement) shows the gradient-free method (GFNM) converging slowly compared to baselines. The authors should report computational efficiency metrics (e.g., iterations to reach tolerance, wall-clock time) to contextualize this observation, as slow convergence undermines practical utility.

Minor points require attention. The term "zero-order oracle" is unconventional; "zeroth-order oracle" is standard in optimization literature. The paper also lacks a clear comparison to related work on constrained non-convex PageRank (e.g., [Citation] in *ICML 2015*), making it difficult to gauge novelty. Additionally, the optimization problem’s constraints are not fully justified—why are they necessary for Supervised PageRank? A brief discussion of constraint selection would strengthen the modeling.

The writing requires refinement. For example, "formulate the Supervised PageRank models as a constrained non-convex optimization problem" should be "formulates Supervised PageRank as a constrained non-convex optimization problem." Ambiguous phrases like "incorporating properties" should specify *which* properties (e.g., "node labels and edge weights"). Theorem 2’s proof sketch is insufficient; a concise argument for the bound’s tightness would bolster confidence.

In summary, while the problem formulation is promising, the paper’s failure to present key results in the main text, unresolved theoretical contradictions, and incomplete experimental analysis prevent a fair assessment of its contribution. The authors must address these issues before publication. Specifically: (1) move critical results to the main body; (2) resolve the convexity-nonconvexity inconsistency in Theorem 1; (3) clarify the convergence implications of Theorem 3; and (4) report efficiency metrics for the proposed algorithms. Without these revisions, the paper’s claims remain unverified and its impact unconvincing. The current version does not meet NIPS’s standards for technical depth or completeness.
