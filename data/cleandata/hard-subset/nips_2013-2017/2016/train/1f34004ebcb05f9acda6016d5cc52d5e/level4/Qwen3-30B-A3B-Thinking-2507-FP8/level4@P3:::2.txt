

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models as a constrained non-convex optimization problem by incorporating node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: a gradient-free method with inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method with inexact first-order oracle. The key theoretical contributions include providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which is a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art approach [21] that lacks such theoretical guarantees. The authors demonstrate that their methods outperform the existing approach in ranking quality on real-world web page ranking tasks.

The paper makes several valuable contributions to the field. First, it provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods applied to Supervised PageRank learning, which is a notable advancement over [21]. Second, the authors develop a framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, which extends the work of [16] and [7] to handle the specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank problem. Third, they establish clear complexity bounds for their algorithms, which is particularly valuable for practical implementation on large-scale graphs. The experimental results demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality, with the gradient-based method showing significant improvements on 50% of the test queries (p < 0.005 in paired t-tests).

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors carefully analyze the trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of optimization for large-scale graph problems. The problem formulation and experimental setup are well-motivated by real-world applications in web search ranking, where the ability to incorporate node and edge features beyond simple link structure is highly valuable.

However, there are several important weaknesses that need to be addressed before publication. First, the experimental results are relegated to the supplement, which makes the main text incomplete and reduces the paper's impact. The reviewers and readers should be able to assess the empirical performance without having to consult supplementary materials. The authors should move key results, including the main performance comparisons and convergence plots, to the main text.

Second, Theorem 1 assumes convexity of the objective function, while the authors explicitly state that the loss function (2.4) is non-convex. The paper does not provide sufficient justification for how the set Φ is chosen to guarantee local convexity in the vicinity of a minimum, which is critical for the theoretical guarantees. Without a clear explanation of this assumption's validity for the specific problem, the theoretical contribution is weakened.

Third, Theorem 3 provides a bound for ‖MK(xK - xK+1)‖2 rather than directly addressing convergence to a stationary point. It would be helpful to clarify whether this bound implies convergence to a stationary point for the non-convex problem, as this is a central theoretical contribution of the paper. The authors should provide a more explicit connection between their bound and the convergence properties required for optimization of non-convex problems.

Fourth, Figure 1 in the supplement shows that the gradient-free method (GFN) converges more slowly than the gradient-based method (GBN), which contradicts the authors' claim that "the main advantage of the second gradient-free algorithm is that it avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix." The paper should address this discrepancy and provide a more balanced assessment of the relative efficiencies of the two methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate citations to related work. The connection to previous NIPS work on graph-based ranking methods (e.g., [2, 8, 9, 12, 20, 21]) is well-established. The contributions are clearly distinguished from prior work, and the authors have made a careful effort to position their work within the broader literature.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach to learning Supervised PageRank models with important convergence guarantees. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, but the current presentation has significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The main concerns are the lack of key experimental results in the main text and the unclear justification for the convexity assumption in Theorem 1.

Strengths: Theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods; clear complexity analysis; strong experimental results demonstrating improvements over state-of-the-art; well-motivated problem formulation.

Weaknesses: Experimental results not in main text; insufficient justification for convexity assumption in Theorem 1; unclear connection between Theorem 3's bound and convergence to stationary point; slow convergence of gradient-free method contradicts authors' claims.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graph-based ranking algorithms. With the necessary revisions to address the concerns above, it would be a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. As currently written, it would benefit from a major revision to include key experimental results in the main text, clarify the convexity assumption, and provide a more complete analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed methods.
