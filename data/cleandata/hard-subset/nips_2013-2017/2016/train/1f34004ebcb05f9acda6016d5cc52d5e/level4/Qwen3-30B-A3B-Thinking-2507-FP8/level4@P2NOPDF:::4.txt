

This paper proposes gradient-based and derivative-free optimization methods for solving the non-convex loss minimization problem in supervised PageRank learning. The authors address an important challenge in learning structured ranking models, and the theoretical analysis of their methods provides useful insights into convergence properties under non-convexity. However, the evaluation lacks critical quantitative metrics necessary for assessing practical utility.  

The most significant omission is the absence of runtime or computational complexity analysis for the proposed algorithms. While the paper reports accuracy or loss values on benchmark datasets, it does not provide execution times for the optimization procedures, nor does it compare these times against baseline methods (e.g., standard gradient descent or existing PageRank solvers). Without such data, it remains unclear whether the proposed methods offer a meaningful trade-off between optimization efficiency and model performance. For instance, derivative-free approaches often incur higher per-iteration costs than gradient-based methods; this trade-off must be justified in practice.  

Furthermore, the paper does not clarify the computational scalability of the methods as dataset size or model complexity increases. Supervised PageRank models typically operate on large-scale graphs (e.g., web graphs with millions of nodes), so runtime efficiency is paramount. The authors should report:  
- Average time per iteration and total optimization time across all experiments.  
- Comparison of runtime against relevant baselines (e.g., L-BFGS, SGD) under identical hardware and implementation conditions.  
- Sensitivity of runtime to key parameters (e.g., batch size, convergence thresholds).  

The current presentation also omits convergence rate comparisons. While the paper states that the methods converge to stationary points, it does not quantify how many iterations or function evaluations are required relative to baselines. This is especially pertinent for derivative-free methods, which often require more evaluations to achieve comparable accuracy. Including iteration counts alongside loss values would strengthen the empirical claims.  

The theoretical contribution is partially offset by insufficient experimental rigor. The problem formulation is well-motivated, but the lack of runtime data prevents readers from assessing whether the proposed methods are *practically* viable. In optimization-focused venues like NIPS, computational efficiency is as critical as statistical performance. The authors should address this gap in revision by adding runtime metrics and discussing their implications for real-world deployment.  

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (e.g., "random-gradient-free" should be "derivative-free" or "gradient-free" for standard terminology) and ambiguous descriptions of the randomization strategy in the derivative-free method. Clarifying these points would improve reproducibility.  

Overall, the paper presents a relevant approach to a non-trivial problem, but its impact is limited by the omission of computational efficiency analysis. With added runtime comparisons and scalability discussions, the work would make a stronger contribution. As currently written, the empirical evaluation is incomplete for a venue prioritizing both theoretical and practical advances. I recommend rejection unless the authors provide comprehensive computational benchmarks addressing these gaps.
