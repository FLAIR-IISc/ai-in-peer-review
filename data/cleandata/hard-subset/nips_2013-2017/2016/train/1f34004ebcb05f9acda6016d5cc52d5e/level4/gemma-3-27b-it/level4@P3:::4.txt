This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for solving the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem. The core idea builds upon existing Supervised PageRank formulations [21] but focuses on providing theoretical convergence guarantees for the optimization process, which were lacking in prior work. The authors tackle this by employing an “inexact oracle” approach and analyzing the trade-off between lower-level accuracy (estimating stationary distributions) and overall computational complexity.

This work is related to a substantial body of research on PageRank and its extensions. The foundational PageRank algorithm [18] and its variations [8, 9, 11, 19] are well-established. More recent approaches focus on incorporating node and edge features into the ranking process [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21], which is the space this paper occupies. Specifically, the paper builds on the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], aiming to address the convergence issues present in the original method. The use of inexact oracle methods is also connected to broader research in optimization, particularly in settings where exact gradient calculation is expensive or impossible [7, 16].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** A key strength of this work is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a significant improvement over existing approaches like [21], where convergence was not formally proven.
*   **Inexact Oracle Approach:** The use of an inexact oracle is practical and relevant, particularly for large-scale graph problems where calculating exact stationary distributions can be computationally prohibitive. The analysis of the accuracy/complexity trade-off is also valuable.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper presents a detailed analysis of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, along with complexity estimations. It also explores the impact of different parameters (e.g., accuracy of the lower-level approximation).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach [21] on a real-world ranking task, providing empirical support for the theoretical findings.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the authors make an effort to explain the technical details and motivations behind their work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experimental results are positive, they are limited to a single dataset and task (web page ranking with Yandex data). It would be beneficial to evaluate the methods on other graph datasets and ranking problems to assess their generalizability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices (e.g., L, ε) isn’t fully explored. A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Comparison to other Gradient-Free Methods:** The paper primarily compares to the state-of-the-art *gradient-based* method [21].  A comparison with other established gradient-free optimization techniques, beyond the adaptation of [16], would be helpful to understand the relative advantages of the proposed approach.  
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The complexity analysis, while detailed, can be hard to grasp quickly. Clarifying the practical implications of the theoretical complexities (e.g., how the complexity scales with graph size and features) would be helpful.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies**: It would be helpful to include ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the proposed methods.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in ranking and recommendation systems.
*   The theoretical contributions (convergence guarantees, inexact oracle analysis) are novel and valuable.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The complexity analysis, while thorough, could be more readily interpretable.
*   The comparison with other gradient-free algorithms is incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The provision of convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank optimization is a notable achievement. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the positive results and thorough analysis justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions). The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include additional datasets and ranking tasks, and provide a more intuitive explanation of the complexity analysis. Adding a comparison to more gradient-free algorithms would also be valuable.
