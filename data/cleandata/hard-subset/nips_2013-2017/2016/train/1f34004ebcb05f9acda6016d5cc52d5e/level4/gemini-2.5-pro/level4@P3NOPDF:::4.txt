This paper addresses the problem of learning supervised PageRank models by formulating it as a non-convex loss minimization task. The authors propose two distinct optimization approaches to tackle this problem: a gradient-based method and a random-gradient-free method. The latter is presented as a valuable alternative for scenarios where computing the full gradient is computationally prohibitive.

**Strengths**

The problem of learning parameters for a supervised PageRank model is well-motivated and has significant practical importance in areas such as web search, recommendation systems, and node importance estimation in networks. The paper's main contribution is the development of novel optimization frameworks for this task.

The proposal of both a gradient-based and a gradient-free algorithm is a key strength. This dual approach offers flexibility, catering to different computational constraints and problem structures. The random-gradient-free method, in particular, is an interesting contribution, as it circumvents the potentially expensive computation of the exact gradient, which can be a bottleneck in large-scale settings.

The paper is generally well-written, and the problem formulation is clearly presented. The motivations for both optimization approaches are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation. While the authors report on the performance of their models in terms of standard ranking metrics, a critical piece of information is missing: the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed optimization methods.

Without runtime analysis or wall-clock time comparisons, it is difficult for the reader to assess the practical viability of these algorithms, especially in the large-scale scenarios they are intended for. The central claim for the gradient-free method is its utility when gradients are expensive, but this claim is not empirically substantiated.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should include a detailed analysis of the computational performance. Specifically, it would be highly beneficial to:
1.  Report the training time required for each method to converge on the presented datasets.
2.  Provide a comparison of the time per iteration for the gradient-based and gradient-free approaches.
3.  Include runtime comparisons against relevant baseline methods.

A comprehensive evaluation should not only demonstrate that the proposed methods achieve competitive or superior accuracy but also that they are computationally efficient. Such an analysis would provide a much clearer picture of the trade-offs involved and would significantly bolster the paper's contributions.
