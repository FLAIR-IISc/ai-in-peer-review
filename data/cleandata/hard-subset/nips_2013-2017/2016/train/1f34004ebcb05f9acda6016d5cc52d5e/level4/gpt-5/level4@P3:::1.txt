Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the learning of Supervised PageRank models where transition and restart probabilities depend on node and edge features via linear parametrizations. The authors formulate a two-level optimization framework for minimizing a nonconvex, pairwise loss over queries: at the lower level, they approximate query-specific stationary distributions and, when needed, their derivatives; at the upper level, they update parameters using either a random gradient-free method or an adaptive projected gradient method. The novelty lies in integrating inexact-oracle techniques into both zero-order and first-order methods, and in analyzing the trade-off between inner approximation accuracy and outer optimization complexity.

Relative to prior work: Supervised PageRank and related feature-weighted random-walk models have been studied (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec; Liu et al.; the “Supervised PageRank” line by Gusev/Serdyukov and co-authors). Previous learning approaches relied on power-method-based estimates of stationary distributions and their gradients, but lacked convergence guarantees due to oracle inexactness. On the optimization side, the paper builds on Nesterov–Spokoiny’s random gradient-free optimization (for convex functions), Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov’s inexact oracle framework, and gradient methods for composite optimization, as well as Ghadimi–Lan’s nonconvex stochastic methods. The authors adapt these to constrained problems with inexact oracles and nonconvex objectives, and they replace the power method with a linearly convergent scheme (Nesterov–Nemirovski) for approximating stationary distributions, claiming better complexity bounds.

Quality
- Theory: The paper defines precise inexact zero- and first-order oracles obtained via controlled-accuracy approximations of stationary distributions (and their derivatives), and provides convergence-rate results for (i) a gradient-free method under convexity within a ball and bounded oracle error, and (ii) an adaptive gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite objectives with inexact first-order oracles. The theorems relate inner tolerances (δ) and smoothing parameters (τ) to target accuracy (ε), and yield overall computational complexity bounds. The derivative computation extends [17] to obtain accuracy-controlled gradient estimates. While I did not verify all details, the statements are reasonable and the analysis appears careful.
- Algorithms/Complexity: The two-level design is clear, and the proofs tie inner iteration counts (N, N1, N2) to outer performance. The discussion of the trade-off between inner precision and outer cost is useful.
- Experiments: On Yandex user-browsing graphs (600 queries), the proposed gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods reduce the training loss more than the prior gradient-based baseline (GBP) and converge with fewer outer iterations, with some significance testing. However, the evaluation focuses on the surrogate loss; standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) and detailed runtime/wall-clock comparisons are not reported, and the baselines are limited to prior Supervised PageRank learning rather than broader learning-to-rank methods.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, though notation is heavy. The structure (model, oracle construction, zero-/first-order frameworks, application, experiments) is logical. Some typos and language issues remain. It would help to:
- Clarify assumptions ensuring positivity/normalization and the practical choice of Φ (ball around the all-ones vector) versus the theoretical requirement that f be convex over Φ for the gradient-free method.
- Explicitly define β1 and constants in the main text or give intuitive bounds.
- Provide algorithm boxes with all inputs/outputs and a brief discussion of parameter sensitivity.

Originality
The optimization tools draw from established frameworks (randomized zero-order optimization, inexact oracles, composite gradient methods), but the paper contributes:
- An adaptation of random gradient-free methods to constrained domains with bounded-value inexact oracles based on sphere randomization.
- An adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracles and unknown L, with a practical backtracking scheme.
- A concrete two-level application to Supervised PageRank with provable convergence rates, including new accuracy–complexity coupling for stationary distribution and gradient approximations.

These are incremental but meaningful extensions that address a real gap in earlier Supervised PageRank learning methods (lack of guarantees).

Significance
Providing convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for supervised random-walk learning is valuable, as such methods are widely used in ranking and graph mining. The theoretical framework is of interest beyond this application. Empirically, the improvements over the prior Supervised PageRank learner are promising, but broader experimental evidence (standard ranking metrics, stronger baselines, scalability and runtime) would strengthen the practical impact.

Strengths
- Clear two-level design with principled inexact-oracle control.
- Convergence-rate guarantees for both zero- and first-order methods in a setting where prior work lacked theory.
- Extension of stationary-distribution and derivative approximations with provable accuracy and complexity.
- Demonstrated improvements on a real graph-ranking task; use of significance tests.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The gradient-free method assumes convexity within Φ, but Φ is chosen heuristically; clarify when this holds in practice or relax the assumption.
- Experiments report only the training loss; add standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) on held-out queries and show correlation between loss reduction and ranking quality.
- Limited baselines: include non-PageRank learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) to contextualize performance.
- Report wall-clock time, memory, and scalability (|V|, |E|, |Q|) for all methods; show sensitivity to ε, L, α, and inner iteration counts.
- Improve presentation: fix typos, provide full references with years/venues, and better motivate the choice of feature sets and the ball radius R.

Questions for the authors
- How often does f exhibit convexity within the chosen Φ in practice? Did you observe cases where GFN fails due to nonconvexity?
- Can you report NDCG/MAP on Q2, and do gains in f translate to gains in those metrics?
- What are the wall-clock times for GFN/GBN/GBP on Q1j/Q2j, and how do they scale with graph size and feature counts?
- How sensitive are results to α and to the number of inner iterations (N, N1, N2)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Sound and relevant theory addressing an identified gap in Supervised PageRank learning.
- Useful adaptation of inexact-oracle optimization to constrained, two-level settings.
- Empirical improvements over prior Supervised PageRank training.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty is incremental; much is adapted from known frameworks.
- Empirical evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics, broader baselines, and runtime scaling analysis.
- Some assumptions (local convexity for GFN) are not fully justified empirically.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The theoretical contributions are solid and relevant to NIPS audiences working on optimization for graph-based learning, and the application is timely. Strengthening the experimental evaluation (metrics, baselines, runtime) would substantially improve the paper.
