This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, which enhance the classic PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in prior work [21]: the existing gradient-based learning algorithm lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. The central contribution of this work is to propose two new optimization methods—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that are designed for this non-convex loss-minimization problem and come with formal convergence rate guarantees. The core technical approach is to frame the optimization within an inexact oracle setting, acknowledging that the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov process (and its derivatives) can only be approximated. The authors adapt and analyze optimization methods under this framework and validate them empirically on a web page ranking task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its strong theoretical contribution. By providing the first convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models, the authors place the problem on a more solid theoretical footing. The analysis of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods within a constrained, non-convex setting with an inexact oracle is comprehensive and valuable. The development of complexity bounds for the entire two-level optimization process is a significant and non-trivial achievement.

2.  **Novel Methodological Combination:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated combination of techniques. It leverages an efficient method from [17] for the lower-level task of approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, and integrates this with sophisticated upper-level optimization frameworks adapted from [7, 16]. This combination directly addresses the challenge of optimizing a function whose value and gradient cannot be computed exactly.

3.  **Potential for Impact:** The proposed algorithms are shown empirically to outperform the existing state-of-the-art method. If these improvements hold more generally, the methods could increase the reliability and performance of learning-to-rank systems that are based on graph structures, which is an important application area.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's main weakness lies in its organization and heavy reliance on the supplementary material. Crucial details, including full proofs, detailed complexity analysis, and the complete experimental results, are relegated to the appendix. While space constraints are understandable, the main body of the paper lacks sufficient detail to be self-contained and fully convincing on its own. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including proof sketches, more detailed algorithm descriptions, or key empirical tables and figures in the main text. This would allow a reader to better appreciate the technical depth and evaluate the paper's claims without constant reference to the supplement.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section, while promising, could be more thorough.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The process for selecting key hyperparameters, such as the center `ϕ̂` and radius `R` for the feasible set `Φ`, is not described. This is a critical choice, as it defines the optimization space. It is unclear how sensitive the algorithm is to these choices and how they might be set in a practical application.
    *   **Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is conducted on a single dataset from a commercial search engine. While this is a realistic setting, the paper's claims of general applicability would be more robust if the methods were tested across a more diverse set of graphs with varying sizes and structural properties.
    *   **Practical Significance:** The results in Table 1 show an improvement in the loss function, but the paper does not discuss the practical significance of this improvement. For example, does a lower loss value translate to a meaningful improvement in standard ranking metrics like NDCG or MAP? Quantifying the impact on user-facing metrics would make the contribution more compelling.

3.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The paper relies on several key assumptions to establish its theoretical guarantees, and a discussion of their practicality would be welcome.
    *   For the gradient-free method, the analysis assumes that the function is convex within the optimization domain `Φ`. Given that the overall problem is non-convex, it would be helpful to discuss why this local convexity assumption is reasonable for this problem.
    *   The constraint of the parameter vector `ϕ` to a Euclidean ball seems potentially restrictive. A discussion on whether this limits the model's expressive power or performance in practice would clarify the applicability of the proposed methods.

4.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   The clarity of the writing could be improved with a thorough pass for grammar and phrasing.
    *   There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the problem framing. The paper refers to the model as "Supervised PageRank" and cites [21], which introduces a "Supervised Nested PageRank" model. Clarifying this relationship or using consistent terminology would avoid potential confusion.

In summary, this paper presents a strong theoretical contribution by developing and analyzing two optimization algorithms with convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. The core ideas are sound and the work addresses a clear gap in the literature. However, the paper is hindered by its over-reliance on the appendix and an experimental section that could provide more depth and practical insight. With improved presentation and a more comprehensive evaluation, this could be a very strong paper.
