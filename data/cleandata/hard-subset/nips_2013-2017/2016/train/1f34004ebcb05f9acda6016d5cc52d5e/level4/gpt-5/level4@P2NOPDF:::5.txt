The paper motivates PageRank and proposes a feature-based framework that assigns weights to nodes and edges. The authors note that prior learning methods for this model lacked performance guarantees. They propose to combine a known approximation of a Markov chain’s stationary distribution with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method designed for settings where the loss can only be estimated.

The framework largely reproduces ideas from [21] under different terminology and notation. The paper introduces several technical assumptions and a loss function, explains why the optimization is difficult, and states lemmas that bound an approximate gradient. It then discusses a general gradient-free scheme based on a biased gradient oracle and provides a performance theorem. With additional assumptions, the authors instantiate an algorithm for their learning problem and adapt the gradient-free analysis to obtain further bounds.

Experiments are briefly described on a web graph used in related work, but most details are deferred to the appendix. Important parameters are not reported. Although the paper claims the new method outperforms baselines, it does not quantify the gains or discuss their significance. It is also unclear whether the method was tested on multiple or diverse graphs.

Writing and citations need attention. The manuscript contains many grammatical errors. Citations are inconsistent: the problem is labeled “Supervised PageRank” with a citation to [21], yet [21] appears to address semi-supervised PageRank. This discrepancy should be clarified.

Technical strength and assumptions: While the theoretical bounds are strong, I am not convinced the assumptions hold in practice. In particular, restricting the parameter search to an R-ball around a chosen phi0 may substantially limit the algorithm’s effectiveness and applicability. This choice should be justified and its impact evaluated empirically.

Major concerns:
- Much of the substantive content (proofs and empirical results) is relegated to the appendix. The main text lacks proof sketches and concrete experimental results (tables/figures, metrics, numbers).
- The empirical setup omits crucial information: How were phi0 and R selected? Were hyperparameters tuned on validation data? Were multiple graphs or domains evaluated?
- Practical impact is not demonstrated: What improvements matter for real ranking tasks? What is the computational cost versus baselines? Are the gains robust across datasets and metrics?

Requested clarifications and additions:
- Clearly differentiate the contribution from [21]: specify what is genuinely new (assumptions, algorithms, analyses) versus rephrased or rebranded.
- Include proof outlines in the main paper, highlighting the effect of the biased oracle and the sources of bias/variance in the gradient estimates.
- Report complete experimental details in the main text: datasets, graph sizes, feature sets, parameter choices, tuning protocols, metrics, baselines, and significance testing.
- Quantify improvements and discuss their implications. Add experiments on additional graphs to demonstrate robustness. Provide sensitivity analyses for phi0 and R.

Overall assessment: The theoretical development appears solid, but the presentation, empirical validation, and positioning relative to prior work are insufficient. Without clearer novelty, stronger experiments, and improved exposition, I remain unconvinced of the problem’s impact and the practical relevance of the results.
