Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies learning a feature-parameterized PageRank model (“Supervised PageRank”) in which restart and transition probabilities depend on node and edge features. The learning objective is a nonconvex pairwise-ranking square loss over query-dependent browsing graphs. The authors propose two two-level optimization approaches: (i) a gradient-based method and (ii) a random gradient-free method. At the lower level, both rely on the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution (and, for the gradient-based approach, its derivatives), yielding an inexact oracle. At the upper level, the paper develops (a) a gradient-free algorithm with a biased spherical smoothing oracle and convergence guarantees for constrained problems with inexact zero-order evaluations, and (b) an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown smoothness constant. For both methods, the authors derive iteration and arithmetic complexity bounds, and discuss the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and total complexity. Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing dataset compare the proposed methods with a prior gradient-based approach that uses the power method; the authors report improved loss and convergence behavior.

This builds on PageRank/HITS and supervised random walks, prior supervised/semi-supervised ranking on graphs, inexact-oracle optimization (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), random gradient-free methods (Nesterov–Spokoiny), and iterative methods for stationary distributions and their derivatives (Nesterov–Nemirovski, Andrew).

Strengths:
- Addresses an important practical variant of PageRank with feature-based restart/transition and query-dependent graphs.
- Clear framing as a two-level problem with inexact oracles; convergence guarantees are provided for both a gradient-free and a gradient-based upper-level method.
- Uses a faster lower-level stationary distribution approximation than the power method and generalizes it to derivative computation.
- Provides explicit complexity bounds and discusses the accuracy–cost trade-off between lower and upper levels.
- Empirical results indicate improvement over a commonly used gradient-based baseline that relies on the power method.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Clarity and presentation: the paper contains numerous grammatical errors, incomplete references, and missing details (e.g., some citations lack venue/year; some titles/authors appear misattributed). Careful copyediting is needed.
- Terminology and attribution: please clarify the relationship to “Supervised PageRank” in [21] and to prior “semi-supervised ranking” work. The paper at times appears to reintroduce existing frameworks with new notation; make the novelty over [21] explicit (e.g., guarantees with inexact oracle, constrained setting, derivative-free option).
- Assumptions: the gradient-free analysis assumes convexity in a neighborhood; the gradient method assumes an inexact first-order oracle satisfying a specific inequality. It is unclear when these conditions hold for the stated nonconvex loss on real data. Please discuss sufficient conditions (e.g., on features/parameter ranges) and practical diagnostics.
- Constraints: restricting ϕ to an L2 ball intersected with the positive orthant may limit expressivity. The paper should justify this choice, report how ϕ̂ and R are selected in experiments, and assess sensitivity. Alternatives (e.g., simplex constraint, log-parameterization) could be discussed.
- Experimental reporting in the main text is too sparse. Key results (tables/figures, metrics, confidence intervals, ablations) are deferred to the appendix. The extent and practical implications of improvements are unclear.
- Evaluation breadth: experiments appear to use one dataset and subsets of queries sorted by graph size. It is not clear whether multiple graphs/datasets were used, how diverse the graphs are, or whether improvements translate to standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) beyond the training loss.

Quality:
- The theoretical components are interesting and potentially solid, but the main text lacks proof sketches or intuitive explanations; everything is deferred to the appendix. Including brief outlines would help readers assess correctness.
- The empirical section lacks sufficient detail to fully evaluate the claims (hyperparameters, stopping criteria, random seeds, sensitivity to τ, ε, L, α, R, N/N1/N2, and wall-clock/time-per-iteration comparisons).

Clarity:
- The paper would benefit from thorough editing. Define all notation once, avoid reusing symbols with new meanings, and standardize terms (restart vs. teleport; smoothing parameter τ; oracle accuracy δ).
- Ensure all references are complete and accurate; explain how your setting differs from supervised random walks and semi-supervised ranking on graphs.

Originality:
- The lower-level use of Nesterov–Nemirovski and the inexact-oracle upper-level analyses are combinations of known ideas, but the adaptation to this constrained, feature-rich PageRank learning problem, the biased spherical gradient-free oracle under inexact evaluations, and the adaptive gradient method for inexact first-order information appear to offer novel guarantees and a coherent framework.

Significance:
- If the assumptions are reasonable in practice and the reported gains hold across datasets and metrics, the contribution could be significant for learning-to-rank on graphs. However, the current empirical evidence in the main text is insufficient to establish practical impact.

Questions for the authors:
- How were ϕ̂ and R chosen? Please report sensitivity analyses to R and to the positivity constraint.
- What values were used for α and how sensitive are results to α?
- What are the precise step counts N, N1, N2 used in practice, and how do they relate to the prescribed δ? Please provide time/iteration trade-offs.
- Did you evaluate on multiple datasets or graph types? If not, can you at least vary query subsets to show robustness?
- Beyond the training loss, do your methods improve held-out ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP)? Please report these.
- For the gradient-free method, how were τ and L set, and how sensitive are results?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Move a concise proof sketch of the main theorems and a clear statement of assumptions into the main text.
- Add a results table in the main body with concrete numbers (mean ± std), statistical tests, and standard ranking metrics; include ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Provide implementation details sufficient for reproduction (hyperparameters, stopping criteria, seeds) and, if possible, release code.
- Clarify the relation to [21] and correct/complete references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides convergence-guaranteed algorithms for a practically relevant learning problem with inexact oracles.
- Demonstrates theoretically sound trade-offs and complexity bounds.
- Promising empirical results versus a known baseline.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient experimental detail and breadth in the main text to substantiate practical gains.
- Heavy reliance on the appendix for critical content; clarity and citation issues hinder readability.
- Assumptions underlying guarantees are not convincingly justified for the target application.

Overall, the theoretical framework is appealing and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger empirical evidence, clearer exposition, and more careful scholarship to meet NIPS standards.
