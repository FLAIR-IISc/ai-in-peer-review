This paper introduces a framework for feature-based PageRank where both node and edge weights are determined by a feature model. The authors correctly point out that a previous algorithm for learning the model's parameters lacked performance guarantees. To address this, they propose a new method that combines an approach for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method.

The paper first reformulates a framework from [21] under different notation and discusses several technical assumptions and a loss function, explaining the difficulty of the optimization problem. The authors then state lemmas that bound the approximate gradient. Following this, they describe a general gradient-free optimization method that uses a biased oracle and state a theorem on its performance. By introducing further assumptions, they construct a specific learning algorithm for their problem by adapting this gradient-free method, providing additional performance bounds. The authors outline experimental results on a web graph used in related work, but a detailed reporting and analysis of these results are relegated to the appendix.

While the theoretical analysis appears to be the paper's main strength, there are several major weaknesses.

**Clarity and Presentation:**
*   The manuscript's clarity is hindered by numerous grammatical errors.
*   The most significant issue is that the core contributions—both proof outlines and detailed empirical results—are entirely in the appendix. The main body of the paper is not self-contained and lacks the necessary evidence to support its claims.
*   The scholarship regarding related work is lacking in precision. For instance, the problem is referred to as "Supervised PageRank" with a citation to [21], but that work appears to only discuss semi-supervised PageRank.

**Technical Assumptions and Impact:**
*   The paper does not convincingly argue for the impact of the problem being solved.
*   While the theoretical bounds are a key contribution, their practical relevance is undermined by strong technical assumptions whose validity in empirical settings is not discussed. For example, the constriction of the search space for the parameter φ to an R-ball around a chosen φ₀ seems limiting. The potential impact of this restriction on the algorithm's efficacy and general applicability should be addressed.

**Experimental Evaluation:**
*   The main paper provides no tables or specific quantitative results, making it impossible to assess the empirical performance of the proposed algorithm.
*   The authors claim their algorithm outperforms others, but they do not discuss the magnitude or practical implications of this improvement.
*   The experimental setup is underspecified. Key parameters, such as how φ₀ and R were selected, are not reported.
*   The evaluation appears to be limited to a single graph, which raises questions about the generalizability of the results.

In summary, while the paper presents a potentially powerful algorithm with rigorous theoretical analysis, the current manuscript fails to convincingly demonstrate its contributions and impact due to significant issues in its presentation, experimental validation, and justification of its core assumptions.
