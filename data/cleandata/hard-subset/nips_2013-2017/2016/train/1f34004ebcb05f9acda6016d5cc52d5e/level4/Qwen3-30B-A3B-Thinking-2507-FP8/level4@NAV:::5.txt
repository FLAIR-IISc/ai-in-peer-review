

The authors motivate PageRank and introduce a framework incorporating node and edge weights derived from a feature model. They note that prior work on parameter learning for this model lacks performance guarantees and propose addressing it by combining a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique suitable for loss functions estimated via sampling. The framework essentially reproduces that of [21] under different terminology and notation. The paper presents technical assumptions, a loss function, and justification for the problem's difficulty, including lemmas bounding the approximate gradient. A general gradient-free method using a biased oracle is discussed, accompanied by a performance theorem. Additional assumptions lead to an adapted algorithm for the learning problem, with further bounds established. Experimental results on a web graph from prior work are briefly outlined, but detailed results and analysis are deferred to the appendix. Key parameters remain unreported, and the claim of superior performance lacks discussion of improvement magnitude or practical significance. It is unclear if diverse graph datasets were tested. The writing contains grammatical errors and inconsistent citations (e.g., "Supervised PageRank" cited to [21], which describes semi-supervised PageRank). The problem's impact is questionable. While the theoretical bounds are rigorous, the technical assumptions appear restrictive (e.g., constraining the search space for $\phi$ to an $R$-ball around a chosen $\phi_0$), and their empirical validity is doubtful without justification. The most significant concern is that substantive content—proof outlines, full empirical results, parameter selection rationale ($\phi_0$, $R$), and multi-graph testing—resides solely in the appendix. The main text provides no tables, specific results, or discussion of practical implications, failing to substantiate the claimed contributions despite the algorithm's apparent theoretical strength.
