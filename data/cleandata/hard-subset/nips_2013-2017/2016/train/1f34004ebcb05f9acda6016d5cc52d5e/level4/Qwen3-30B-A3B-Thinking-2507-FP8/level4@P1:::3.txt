

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors address a critical limitation in previous work [21], which proposed a gradient-based method without theoretical convergence guarantees. By introducing an inexact oracle framework, they develop a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN) that provide rigorous convergence rate guarantees for solving the non-convex loss-minimization problem.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretical convergence guarantees for both optimization methods, which is a major advancement over prior approaches. The authors prove that their methods can achieve a specified accuracy with well-defined complexity bounds, addressing a critical gap in the literature. Second, they provide a comprehensive analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy (used to estimate stationary distributions) and overall computational complexity. Third, their experimental results demonstrate that both methods outperform the state-of-the-art GBP method in ranking quality on a real-world web ranking task.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors clearly define the problem, present their theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs (provided in supplementary materials), and validate their approach through comprehensive experiments. The gradient-based method (GBN) offers convergence guarantees without requiring convexity assumptions and has fewer hyperparameters than the gradient-free alternative. The gradient-free method (GFN) avoids the need to compute derivatives for large matrices, which is a significant computational advantage.

Several aspects of the paper could be improved. First, the manuscript contains an excessive number of symbols without clear definitions, making it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. For example, the parameter N (used in equation 3.1 to denote the number of iterations) is not defined until line 193, creating unnecessary confusion. A symbol table would greatly enhance readability.

Second, while the paper compares the proposed methods to GBP in terms of the loss function, it does not adequately demonstrate improvements in standard ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP), which are more meaningful for ranking tasks. The authors should provide these metrics to better evaluate the practical impact of their approach.

Third, the paper claims to generalize the work in [17], but does not directly compare their methods to [17] in the experiments. A direct comparison would help readers understand the practical benefits of the authors' approach over the base method.

The paper addresses an important problem in ranking and graph analysis with a well-motivated approach. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the experimental results support the claimed advantages. The methods are clearly described, and the proofs are rigorous, making the paper suitable for publication at NIPS.

Strengths:
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods
- Demonstrates superior performance over state-of-the-art methods in ranking quality
- Analyzes the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational complexity
- Well-structured with rigorous mathematical proofs

Weaknesses:
- Excessive use of symbols without clear definitions
- Limited evaluation using standard ranking quality metrics
- Does not directly compare with the base method [17] in experiments

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graph-based ranking methods. The theoretical guarantees and practical performance improvements over existing methods are compelling. With minor improvements to the presentation and evaluation, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
