The authors motivate PageRank and then bring up a framework that has node and edge weights from a feature model. They point out that a previous algorithm for learning the parameters of the model had no performance guarantees. They plan to solve the methods by combining a previous method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free method used for optimization when the loss function can only be estimated.

The authors reproduce a framework under a different name and notation from [21]. They discuss a number of technical assumptions and a loss function and explain why the problem is hard to solve. Some lemmas bounding the approximate gradient are stated. A general gradient-free method is discussed, using a biased gradient-free oracle, and a theorem on its performance is stated.

The authors make some more assumptions and construct an algorithm for the learning problem and adapt the gradient-free methods to their problem, stating some more bounds. They outline some details of experimental results on a web graph used in related work, but detailed reporting of results and analysis are relegated to the appendix.

The strengths of the paper include the development of a gradient-free optimization method for general convex problems with inexact zero-order oracle and an adaptive gradient method for possibly nonconvex general composite optimization problems with inexact first-order oracle. The authors also provide convergence rate analysis for both methods.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper. The writing is littered with grammatical errors, and citations are sloppy. For example, the problem is called Supervised PageRank with a cite to [21], but only semi-supervised PageRank appears there.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the problem solved is impactful, and the technical assumptions stated may not hold in empirical data. Some assumptions seem limiting, e.g., the constriction of the search space of phi to an R-ball around some chosen phi0 may greatly restrict the efficacy/applicability of the algorithm.

The biggest issue with the paper is that all the meat appears in the appendix. The authors neither provide proof outlines nor full empirical results. The choice of phi0 and R in the empirical results section is not explained, and it is unclear whether tests were run on more than one graph. No tables or specific results are reported in the main body of the paper, and the practical consequences of the improvements are not discussed.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The authors develop a gradient-free optimization method for general convex problems with inexact zero-order oracle and an adaptive gradient method for possibly nonconvex general composite optimization problems with inexact first-order oracle.
* The authors provide convergence rate analysis for both methods.
* The paper proposes a new algorithm for the learning problem and adapts the gradient-free methods to their problem.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The writing is littered with grammatical errors, and citations are sloppy.
* The problem solved may not be impactful, and the technical assumptions stated may not hold in empirical data.
* The paper lacks detailed reporting of results and analysis, and the practical consequences of the improvements are not discussed.
* The choice of phi0 and R in the empirical results section is not explained, and it is unclear whether tests were run on more than one graph.

Recommendation:

The paper has some strong points, including the development of new algorithms and convergence rate analysis. However, the weaknesses in the paper, including grammatical errors, sloppy citations, and lack of detailed reporting of results and analysis, need to be addressed. The authors should revise the paper to address these issues and provide more details on the empirical results and analysis. Additionally, the authors should discuss the practical consequences of their improvements and explain the choice of phi0 and R in the empirical results section.
