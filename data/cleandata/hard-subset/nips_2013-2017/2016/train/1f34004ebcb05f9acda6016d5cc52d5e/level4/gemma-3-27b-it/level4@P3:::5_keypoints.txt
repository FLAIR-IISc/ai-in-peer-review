- Paper tackles learning Supervised PageRank with node/edge features and proposes gradient-based and gradient-free methods using inexact oracle approximations.
- Authors establish convergence rate guarantees for both proposed optimization methods.
- Empirical results claim improvements over a state-of-the-art approach on a web page ranking task.
- Builds on PageRank literature and identifies Supervised PageRank as the appropriate framework to extend with features.
- Uses an approximation of the stationary distribution (method from [17]) argued to be preferable to alternatives.
- Theoretical contributions (convergence guarantees and adaptation to constrained inexact evaluations) are a major strength.
- Novelty lies in combining inexact oracle approximations with gradient-based and gradient-free methods for Supervised PageRank.
- Potential practical impact on web search and recommendation ranking applications.
- Problem statement is well-defined and limitations of prior work are clearly highlighted.
- Paper suffers from poor writing quality, grammatical errors, and convoluted phrasing that hinder readability.
- Excessive reliance on supplementary materials for proofs and experimental details reduces the paper’s self-containedness.
- Empirical evaluation in the main text is limited; key experimental details and choices (e.g., ϕ̂ and R) are omitted or relegated to the appendix.
- Diversity of tested graphs and comprehensive quantitative comparisons are lacking, making practical significance unclear.
- The constraint of parameter space Φ to an R-ball around ϕ̂ is questionably restrictive and requires further justification.
- Contains citation inaccuracies (e.g., mislabeling [21] as "semi-supervised page rank" instead of "Supervised PageRank").
- The overall importance and broader impact of solving this specific problem are not strongly articulated.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically rigorous methods with promising empirical results and potential to spur further research.
- Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity and presentation, insufficient empirical validation, unaddressed assumption limitations, and citation errors.
- Overall assessment: technically sound and valuable contributions but hindered by clarity, incomplete experiments, and questionable assumptions; recommendation to reject with invitation to revise and resubmit.
- Recommended revisions: improve writing, move key material into the main text, expand and detail experiments (tables/statistics), justify assumptions, and correct citations.
