### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper tackles the non-convex optimization problem associated with learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank models. Supervised PageRank, as introduced by Zhukovskii et al. (2015), extends the classic PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features into the transition and restart probabilities, making the ranking model more expressive. The key challenge, which this paper addresses, is that the optimization method proposed in prior work lacked theoretical convergence guarantees due to the use of inexact gradient estimates derived from the power method.

The authors propose a two-level optimization framework to solve this problem. At the lower level, they employ an efficient, linearly convergent method by Nesterov & Nemirovski (2011) to approximate the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) with a controllable, pre-specified accuracy. This provides an "inexact oracle." At the upper level, they introduce two novel optimization algorithms that leverage this inexact oracle:
1.  A random gradient-free method, which extends existing work to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is calculated with a known error bound.
2.  A gradient-based method, which generalizes adaptive gradient approaches to constrained, non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle.

Crucially, the authors provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods. They analyze the computational complexity, considering the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the number of upper-level iterations. The methods are empirically validated on a web page ranking task using real-world data, demonstrating superior performance over the previous state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Guarantees (Quality & Originality):** The primary contribution of this paper is the rigorous theoretical analysis. By formalizing the learning problem within the framework of optimization with an inexact oracle, the authors provide the first convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank. This addresses a significant gap in the prior work and places the learning procedure on a solid theoretical foundation. The complexity analyses in Theorems 2 and 4, which link the upper-level convergence to the lower-level approximation effort, are comprehensive and valuable.

2.  **Novel Methodological Contributions (Originality & Significance):** The paper presents novel extensions to existing optimization frameworks. The adaptation of a random gradient-free method to constrained problems with a biased, inexact oracle (Section 4) and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle (Section 5) are valuable contributions in their own right, with potential applications beyond the specific PageRank problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation (Quality):** The proposed methods are benchmarked against the state-of-the-art on a real-world, large-scale dataset from a commercial search engine. The experimental results clearly show that both the gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods outperform the previous method (GBP) in terms of the final loss function value. The finding that the gradient-free method performs best is particularly interesting.

4.  **Well-Motivated Problem (Significance):** Learning to rank is a fundamental problem in information retrieval and web search. Supervised PageRank is an important model that combines the structural information of link graphs with rich feature data. Providing robust and theoretically-grounded algorithms to train such models is a significant and practical contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Practical Runtime Comparison (Clarity & Quality):** While the paper provides detailed theoretical complexity bounds in terms of arithmetic operations, it omits a comparison of practical, wall-clock training times. The theoretical bounds are complex and depend on several problem-dependent constants (L, R, etc.), making it difficult for a practitioner to gauge the real-world efficiency of the proposed methods versus the baseline. Reporting the actual time taken for each method to converge on the experimental datasets would significantly strengthen the paper and provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs. For instance, does the improved convergence guarantee and final loss value come at the cost of significantly longer training time?

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is technically dense, and the connection between the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) and their application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2) could be made more explicit. A brief paragraph in each application subsection reminding the reader how the specific components (e.g., the set Φ, the function f(ϕ)) map to the general notation (X, f(x)) would improve readability for a broader audience.

3.  **Convexity Assumption (Quality):** Theorem 2, which provides the convergence guarantee for the gradient-free method, relies on the assumption that the objective function f(ϕ) is convex on the feasible set Φ. The authors acknowledge that the function is generally non-convex and state this assumption is for a local analysis. This is a reasonable approach, but it should be highlighted more prominently as a limitation of the global guarantees for that specific method. The gradient-based method's analysis for non-convex functions is a strong point, but this contrast is not fully emphasized.

4.  **Limited Experimental Baseline (Quality):** The experiments focus exclusively on comparing against the previous optimization technique for the *same* Supervised PageRank model. While this is the most direct and necessary comparison, the paper would be even more impactful if it included comparisons against other standard learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) that also use the same node/edge features. This would help situate the performance of the Supervised PageRank model itself, in addition to the performance of the optimization algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, a significant theoretical advance over prior work.
*   Proposes novel and well-analyzed gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for constrained problems with inexact oracles.
*   Demonstrates clear empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on a large, real-world ranking task.
*   Addresses an important and practical problem at the intersection of graph analysis and learning to rank.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is missing a crucial component: a comparison of practical training times, which limits the assessment of the methods' real-world utility.
*   The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method's analysis is a notable limitation that could be stated more clearly.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical and empirical contributions to the problem of learning feature-based graph ranking models. The development of optimization methods with formal convergence guarantees is a crucial step forward for the reliability of such models. The weaknesses, particularly the lack of runtime analysis, are present but can be addressed in a final version. Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
