This paper formulates Supervised PageRank as a constrained nonconvex optimization problem by incorporating node and edge features. The authors propose two methods to solve it: (i) a gradient-free approach with an inexact zero-order oracle, and (ii) a gradient-based approach with an inexact first-order oracle. My main concern is that none of the experimental results appear in the main text, which makes the paper feel incomplete and limits the assessment of practical relevance.

Detailed comments:
1) All experimental results are relegated to the supplement. The main paper should include at least a concise empirical evaluation to substantiate the claims, illustrate typical performance, and justify design choices. Without this, it is difficult to assess the methods’ effectiveness and practical trade-offs.
2) Theorem 1 assumes convexity, yet the objective in (2.4) is nonconvex. How should the set Φ be chosen to ensure convexity of the objective (or the overall problem) so that Theorem 1 applies? If convexity can be guaranteed only for special cases or restricted feature families, please state these conditions explicitly. Otherwise, clarify how the assumptions of Theorem 1 are reconciled with the nonconvex formulation.
3) Theorem 3 provides a bound for MK(xK − xK+1). Does this bound imply that the sequence {xk} converges to a stationary point? Under what additional conditions (e.g., summability of errors, bounded MK, Lipschitz continuity, or properties of the inexact oracle) can you conclude stationarity or that the gradient mapping goes to zero? Please clarify whether convergence is guaranteed for the whole sequence or only for a subsequence, and specify the precise stationarity notion used.
4) According to Figure 1 in the supplement, the gradient-free method (GFNM) converges slowly. Please report the algorithms’ efficiency more comprehensively, including wall-clock times, number of oracle calls, iteration counts, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to oracle inaccuracies. A comparison on problem sizes representative of PageRank-scale graphs would help readers understand scalability and practical viability.

Overall, the paper tackles a relevant problem and presents methods designed to cope with inexact oracles, which is important in large-scale ranking scenarios. However, the absence of experimental results in the main text and unclear conditions bridging the nonconvex formulation with the convexity assumed in Theorem 1 leave significant gaps. Strengthening the main paper with core empirical evidence and clarifying the theoretical assumptions and convergence implications would substantially improve the work.
