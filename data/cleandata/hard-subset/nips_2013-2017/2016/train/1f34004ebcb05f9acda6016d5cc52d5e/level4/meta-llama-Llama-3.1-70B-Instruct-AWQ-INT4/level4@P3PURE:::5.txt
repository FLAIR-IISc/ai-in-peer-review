The authors motivate PageRank and introduce a framework that incorporates node and edge weights from a feature model. They highlight the limitations of a previous algorithm for learning the model parameters, which lacked performance guarantees. To address this, they propose a novel approach that combines a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization technique, suitable for scenarios where the loss function can only be estimated.

The authors reproduce a framework under a different name and notation from [21], and discuss several technical assumptions and a loss function, explaining the challenges of solving the problem. They present lemmas that bound the approximate gradient and describe a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle, accompanied by a theorem on its performance. By making additional assumptions, they construct an algorithm for the learning problem and adapt the gradient-free method to their specific problem, stating further bounds.

The authors provide a brief overview of their experimental results on a web graph used in related work, but relegate the detailed reporting of results and analysis to the appendix. However, some parameters are not reported, and while the novel algorithm is claimed to outperform other algorithms, there is no discussion on the extent or implications of this improvement. It is also unclear whether a diverse set of graphs were considered for testing.

The writing is marred by grammatical errors, and citations are sloppy. For instance, the problem is referred to as Supervised PageRank with a citation to [21], but only semi-supervised PageRank is discussed in that reference. I am not convinced that the problem solved has a significant impact. Although the bounds presented are strong, I have doubts about the validity of the technical assumptions in empirical data. Some assumptions, such as the restriction of the search space of φ to an R-ball around a chosen φ0, may greatly limit the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm, which warrants further discussion.

My primary concern with the paper is that the majority of the content, including proof outlines and full empirical results, is relegated to the appendix. The main body of the paper lacks essential details, such as tables or specific results. Furthermore, the choice of φ0 and R in the empirical results section is not explained, and it is unclear whether tests were conducted on more than one graph. The practical consequences of the improvements achieved by the algorithm are also not discussed. While the bounds, analysis, and algorithm may be powerful, the content presented in the paper fails to convince me of its significance.
