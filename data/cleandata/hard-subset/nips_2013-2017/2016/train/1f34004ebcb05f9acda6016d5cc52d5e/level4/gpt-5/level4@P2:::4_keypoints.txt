- Problem: Learns Supervised PageRank by minimizing a nonconvex loss where node/edge features parameterize restart and transition probabilities.
- Methods: Proposes two two-level algorithms — a gradient-based adaptive projected method with inexact first-order oracle and a randomized gradient-free method with inexact zero-order oracle, both using a linearly convergent inner scheme.
- Theoretical results: Proves convergence to stationary points and gives complexity bounds that account for inner accuracy and oracle inexactness.
- Empirical result: On a Yandex query-dependent browsing graph, both methods reduce training loss versus a prior power-iteration-based baseline; gradient-free often best, gradient-based uses fewer outer iterations.
- Novelty vs prior work: Replaces power iterations and explicitly models inexact oracles, building on inexact-oracle and gradient-free theory and adapting them to constrained, biased settings.
- Technical strengths: Clear algorithmic contribution with convergence-rate guarantees and explicit accuracy–iteration trade-offs.
- Technical novelty: Constrained, biased gradient-free oracle analysis and an adaptive gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with unknown Lipschitz constant.
- Practical relevance: Faster inner scheme for stationary distributions and derivative propagation; gradient-free variant avoids explicit derivatives.
- Empirical gains: Demonstrated reduction in training loss and faster outer convergence in some settings compared to prior method.
- Limited evaluation: Experiments use only 600 queries and ~1.7K labeled pairs from one engine and report only training loss.
- Missing metrics: No standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP) or held-out evaluation to assess end-task impact.
- Missing runtime/memory data: Wall-clock time, operation counts, memory usage, and parallelization details are not reported.
- Assumptions underexplained: Convexity-in-a-ball assumption, choice of Φ, and constants (L, β1, α) need justification or empirical estimation.
- Narrow baselines: Comparison restricted to one prior supervised PageRank learner; lacks modern learning-to-rank or alternative baselines.
- Reproducibility gaps: Hyperparameter selection, stopping criteria, sensitivity analyses, and code availability are not detailed.
- Theoretical quality: Theorems and derivative constructions appear sound and align with inexact-oracle literature.
- Clarity issues: Paper is dense; definitions of constants, assumptions for key theorems, and parameter choices need clearer exposition; minor typos and inconsistent refs.
- Originality: Novel adaptation of random gradient-free methods to constrained, biased inexact oracles and coupling of inner scheme accuracy to overall complexity.
- Significance: Potentially valuable for supervised PageRank and broader inexact-oracle optimization, but impact would increase with standard metrics and runtime results.
- Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical development, practical algorithms with empirical improvements, and careful accuracy–cost analysis.
- Arguments against acceptance: Incomplete empirical evaluation (metrics, runtime), limited baselines and data diversity, and under-motivated assumptions/constants.
- Key suggestions: Report wall-clock time and inner iteration counts, add NDCG/MAP on held-out queries, justify convexity/Φ and estimate constants, include sensitivity analyses and more baselines, and improve exposition and references.
