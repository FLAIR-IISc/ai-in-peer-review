- Proposes two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based GBN and gradient-free GFN) for Supervised PageRank that incorporate node and edge features.
- Builds on Nesterov & Nemirovski [17] to address the non-convex loss in Supervised PageRank and provides convergence rate guarantees lacking in prior work [21].
- Two-level structure: lower level approximates stationary distribution and derivatives with a linearly convergent method; upper level uses adaptive gradient methods to optimize parameters.
- Provides detailed theoretical analysis and proofs establishing convergence rates and controlled computational complexity without computing large matrices directly.
- Experiments on a real web ranking task show both methods outperform the state-of-the-art GBP algorithm, with GFN performing particularly well across test sets.
- Hyperparameters and experimental setup are clearly documented, but the dataset used is not publicly available.
- Strength: significant theoretical contribution by supplying convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
- Strength: the two-level approach effectively leverages existing methods for approximating stationary distributions.
- Strength: empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements in ranking quality over the state-of-the-art.
- Weakness: lacks a comprehensive symbol table; several symbols (e.g., N in Eq.3.1) are undefined until later, reducing readability.
- Weakness: experimental evaluation focuses more on the loss function than on direct ranking-quality metrics, the ultimate performance goal.
- Weakness: no direct comparison with the foundational optimization framework [17], which would contextualize the performance gains.
- Quality assessment: technically sound with rigorous analysis and attention to trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational complexity.
- Clarity assessment: well-structured but readability is impeded by excessive notation and delayed symbol definitions.
- Originality assessment: meaningful extension of [17] to Supervised PageRank and novel combination of gradient-free/gradient-based methods with inexact oracles.
- Significance assessment: practical implications for search engines and ranking systems; theoretical contributions have broader applicability.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, compelling empirical evidence, and practical relevance.
- Arguments against acceptance: presentation issues (notation) and insufficient experimental comparisons/metrics.
- Overall recommendation: strong technical paper recommended for acceptance, contingent on improved notation presentation and strengthened experimental comparisons.
