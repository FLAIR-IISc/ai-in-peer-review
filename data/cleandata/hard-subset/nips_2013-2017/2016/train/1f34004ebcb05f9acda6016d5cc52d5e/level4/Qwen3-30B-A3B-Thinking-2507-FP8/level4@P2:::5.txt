

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models with a non-convex loss-minimization framework, proposing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with theoretical convergence guarantees. The work builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. Previous work [21] used a two-level gradient-based method without convergence guarantees, as the lower-level power method for approximating the stationary distribution provided imprecise gradient estimates. The authors' key contribution is providing convergence rate guarantees for both their gradient-based and gradient-free approaches by leveraging inexact oracle methods.

The paper makes several important theoretical contributions. First, it develops a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, establishing convergence rates to stationary points. Second, it adapts random gradient-free methods to constrained optimization problems with function evaluations at known accuracy. Third, it provides detailed complexity bounds for both algorithms, analyzing the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity. The authors prove lemmas bounding the approximation error for both the loss function and its gradient, which are crucial for establishing their theoretical guarantees.

The paper demonstrates the practical relevance of their approach through experiments on a web ranking task using Yandex data. However, the main text lacks sufficient empirical detail to fully evaluate the practical impact. While the paper states that their algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art [21], the main body does not include specific results, tables, or statistical significance tests for the comparative performance. The experimental section is underdeveloped, with key details relegated to the appendix. For instance, the paper mentions "p-values of paired t-tests" but doesn't present these results, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the improvements. The paper also fails to report how the authors selected the initial parameter vector φ₀ and radius R used in the constrained optimization.

The technical assumptions require careful examination. The authors constrain the parameter space to an R-ball around a chosen φ₀ (‖φ-φ₀‖₂ ≤ R), which may significantly limit the algorithm's applicability in practice. The paper does not discuss how the choice of φ₀ and R affects performance or whether these choices could be made more robustly. This constraint appears restrictive compared to more flexible approaches in the literature, and the authors should address whether this limitation is fundamental to their approach or merely a technical convenience.

The writing contains several grammatical errors that undermine clarity, particularly in the mathematical descriptions. Some citations are inconsistent with the referenced work (e.g., citing [21] for "Supervised PageRank" when the cited paper describes "Semi-supervised PageRank"). The paper also fails to clearly distinguish its contributions from previous work, as the framework appears to be a rephrasing of [21] with different notation.

While the theoretical analysis is strong and the convergence guarantees are valuable, the paper's main weakness is the insufficient empirical validation in the main text. The authors claim their methods "outperform" the state-of-the-art, but without clear quantitative comparisons in the main body, it's difficult to assess the magnitude or practical significance of the improvements. The paper also does not discuss whether the improvements are consistent across different graph sizes or structures, as the experimental setup uses only one dataset (Yandex) with query-dependent graphs.

The paper's significance is questionable given the lack of clear practical impact. The authors claim their algorithms "can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine," but without specific performance metrics or comparisons to production systems, this claim remains unsubstantiated. For a NIPS paper, it's crucial to demonstrate how theoretical advances translate to practical benefits.

The paper contains several strengths worth noting: the detailed complexity analysis, the clear development of the inexact oracle framework, and the theoretical contribution of providing convergence guarantees where previous work lacked them. The gradient-free method's advantage of avoiding derivative calculations for large matrices is particularly noteworthy.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Move key experimental results (including tables, statistical tests, and parameter choices) from the appendix to the main text
2. Discuss the practical implications of their method with concrete performance metrics
3. Address the limitations of their R-ball constraint and how it might affect real-world applications
4. Clarify the relationship between their work and [21] to better position their contributions
5. Correct grammatical errors and improve writing clarity

While the theoretical contributions are sound, the paper fails to convince readers of its practical significance due to insufficient empirical validation in the main text. The authors' claims about performance improvements require stronger evidence to support their assertions. The paper would be significantly improved by providing a clearer demonstration of how their methods outperform existing approaches in meaningful ways for real-world ranking tasks.
