The authors begin by motivating PageRank and then present a framework incorporating node and edge weights derived from a feature model. They identify a lack of performance guarantees in a prior algorithm for learning the parameters of this model and propose a solution combining a Markov random walk stationary distribution approximation with a gradient-free optimization method suitable for loss functions accessible only through estimation. The framework largely reproduces existing work [21] under different notation, referred to as “Supervised PageRank” despite [21] focusing on semi-supervised PageRank – a citation error. The paper details several technical assumptions, defines a loss function, and explains the inherent challenges of the problem, supporting these claims with lemmas bounding the approximate gradient. A general gradient-free method is discussed, leveraging a biased gradient-free oracle, and accompanied by a theorem outlining its performance. Further assumptions are introduced, an algorithm is constructed for the learning problem, and the gradient-free methods are adapted to this specific case, with corresponding bounds provided. Experimental results on a web graph from related work are outlined, but detailed results and analysis are primarily contained in the appendix. Crucially, several parameter settings are unreported, and the claim of outperformance is made without quantifying the extent or practical significance of the improvement. The scope of empirical validation is also unclear; it is not evident whether the algorithm was tested on a diverse set of graphs. 

The writing contains numerous grammatical errors and exhibits sloppy citation practices. More fundamentally, I question the overall impact of the solved problem. While the theoretical bounds are strong, I harbor concerns regarding the applicability of the stated technical assumptions to real-world data. Some assumptions, such as restricting the search space of φ to an R-ball around a chosen φ₀, appear particularly limiting and could severely hinder the algorithm's efficacy; this limitation should be thoroughly discussed. 

My primary concern is the placement of substantive contributions in the appendix. The paper lacks both proof sketches and comprehensive empirical results within the main text. Specifically, the paper does not explain how φ₀ and R were selected in the experimental evaluation, nor does it confirm whether testing extended beyond a single graph. The absence of tables or specific results in the main body prevents a clear assessment of the algorithm’s performance. Furthermore, the practical implications of the reported improvements remain unclear. Despite the potential power of the bounds, analysis, and algorithm, the paper as presented fails to provide a convincing justification for its contributions.
