

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models through the lens of non-convex optimization. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods—gradient-based and gradient-free—that provide convergence guarantees for the loss-minimization problem introduced in previous work [21]. The Supervised PageRank framework extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features through linear combinations with model parameters, allowing for more nuanced ranking models. Previous approaches [21] lacked theoretical convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of their oracle computations, which the authors address by introducing precise error bounds for their methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods applied to Supervised PageRank learning. The authors develop a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle and prove its convergence rate, while also adapting random gradient-free methods [16] to constrained optimization settings with inexact function evaluations. Second, they analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level Markov chain approximation and the overall computational complexity. Third, they demonstrate through experiments that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality while providing theoretical guarantees.

Regarding the four review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors rigorously prove convergence rates and complexity bounds for both methods. The experimental results validate the theoretical claims, showing that both algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality across multiple datasets. However, the paper lacks detailed computational complexity analysis and runtime comparisons, which would strengthen the quality assessment.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem definition to theoretical development to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including data collection and evaluation metrics. The only minor clarity issue is that some technical terms (e.g., "inexact oracle") could benefit from brief intuitive explanations for readers less familiar with optimization theory.

Originality: This work makes a substantial contribution to the field by extending the Supervised PageRank framework with theoretically grounded optimization methods. While Supervised PageRank [21] and related work [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21] have been proposed previously, this paper is the first to provide convergence guarantees for optimization methods applied to this problem. The authors' approach of combining inexact oracle techniques with both gradient-based and gradient-free methods represents a novel contribution to optimization theory for graph-based learning problems.

Significance: The methods have clear practical implications for improving web ranking algorithms, as demonstrated by the experimental results. The theoretical guarantees address a significant gap in the literature, making the approach more reliable for real-world applications. The paper's techniques could also be applied to other graph-based learning problems, potentially broadening its impact beyond web ranking. However, the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how these methods could be integrated into existing search engine architectures and their potential impact on user experience.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contributions: First convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank optimization
2. Comprehensive analysis of trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall complexity
3. Experimental validation showing superior performance over state-of-the-art
4. Well-organized and technically sound presentation

Weaknesses:
1. Limited computational complexity analysis (runtime comparisons missing)
2. Minimal discussion of practical implementation challenges
3. Theoretical results could be better contextualized within broader optimization literature
4. Some technical details (e.g., parameter selection) could be clarified

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank optimization
- Demonstrates practical improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Makes a significant contribution to the intersection of graph-based learning and optimization
- Well-written with clear technical presentation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks detailed runtime analysis and computational complexity comparisons
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges
- Some theoretical contributions could be better contextualized within optimization literature

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing a gap in theoretical guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning while providing practical improvements. The authors successfully bridge the gap between theoretical optimization and practical graph-based learning applications. The main missing element is a more detailed computational analysis that would help practitioners understand the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical efficiency.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include a detailed computational complexity analysis and runtime comparisons with the state-of-the-art. The paper is technically sound, makes a significant contribution to the field, and demonstrates practical value through experimental results. The authors' approach of combining inexact oracle techniques with both gradient-based and gradient-free methods represents an important advancement in optimization theory for graph-based learning problems.
