Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles learning in Supervised PageRank, where node and edge features parameterize restart and transition probabilities. The authors formulate the task as minimizing a nonconvex loss over query-specific graphs and propose two two-level optimization schemes. At the lower level, they approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives using the linearly convergent method of Nesterov and Nemirovski rather than the power method; at the upper level, they optimize the model parameters using either (i) a gradient-free method adapted to constrained problems with an inexact zero-order oracle, or (ii) a gradient-based method for composite, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle. Both upper-level methods come with convergence-rate guarantees despite inexact oracle information, and the paper analyzes the trade-off between the lower-level accuracy and the overall computational complexity. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset compare the proposed methods (GFN and GBN) against the gradient-based baseline GBP from prior work on Supervised PageRank [21], reporting better loss values and faster convergence in many settings. The paper also notes that the Nesterov–Nemirovski method converges faster than the power method for computing the stationary distribution, aligning with earlier findings.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation for learning Supervised PageRank with node/edge features and constraints on parameters.
- Novel adaptation of random gradient-free optimization to constrained settings with inexact function values, and a general adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles.
- Theoretical convergence-rate guarantees for both upper-level methods, including complexity bounds that explicitly account for oracle accuracy.
- Careful analysis of the accuracy–complexity trade-off between lower- and upper-level computations.
- Empirical evaluation on a real-world, query-dependent user browsing graph, with reported improvements over the prior gradient-based baseline.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Clarity and notation: the paper is notation-heavy. A symbol table (covering all sets, parameters, norms, and iteration counters) would significantly improve readability. For example, N in Eq. (3.1) is used before its meaning (number of lower-level iterations) is explained later in the text; several other symbols are introduced far from their first use.
- Experimental metrics: results focus primarily on the surrogate loss f. Since the goal is ranking, please report standard ranking measures (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, Precision@k) in addition to loss, and clarify whether the observed loss reductions translate into statistically significant gains in ranking quality.
- Baselines: the experimental comparison is largely with GBP from [21]. It would strengthen the paper to include (i) an ablation comparing different lower-level solvers (power vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski) within the same upper-level optimizer, and (ii) a broader set of learning-to-rank baselines or recent supervised random walk methods when feasible.
- Reproducibility: while the dataset is not public, the feature definitions and hyperparameters are described. Consider releasing code and synthetic or anonymized data to facilitate replication. Also report wall-clock time, memory usage, and the sensitivity of GFN/GBN to ε, τ, L/L0, α, and R.
- Relation to [17]: the lower-level method is based on [17]; the paper includes a partial empirical comparison (stationary distribution convergence). It would be helpful to more explicitly quantify the end-to-end effect of replacing the power method with [17] on the overall learning objective and runtime.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical development appears sound. The inexact-oracle frameworks are carefully specified, and convergence rates are provided with explicit dependence on oracle accuracies. The empirical section supports the main claims on convergence and loss reduction, but ranking metrics and runtime analyses are limited.
- Clarity: Overall well organized, with detailed algorithms and lemmas, but heavy notation and delayed definitions hinder readability. A consolidated notation table and a pass to unify symbols and equation references would help.
- Originality: The work extends random gradient-free methods to constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracle, and develops an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite optimization with inexact first-order oracle. Applying these to Supervised PageRank with complexity guarantees is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution, though the lower-level component builds directly on [17].
- Significance: Given the prevalence of PageRank-like models and large-scale graphs, methods that guarantee convergence under inexact computations and avoid expensive derivative calculations can be impactful. The results are promising, but broader empirical validation (metrics and baselines) would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contributions for optimization with inexact oracles (both zero- and first-order).
- Clear integration of theory into a practical two-level learning framework for Supervised PageRank.
- Demonstrated empirical improvements over a relevant baseline on real data.
- Useful complexity analysis characterizing accuracy–cost trade-offs.

Cons against acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation focuses on loss; limited reporting on standard ranking metrics and runtime.
- Notational load and delayed definitions reduce accessibility.
- Baseline coverage is narrow; more comprehensive comparisons would increase confidence in generality.
- Dataset is not public; reproducibility would benefit from code or synthetic data release.

Overall assessment:
This is a technically strong paper with meaningful theoretical and algorithmic advances for learning Supervised PageRank under inexact computations. With improved clarity and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation (ranking metrics, runtime, and broader baselines), it would make a strong contribution. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the experimental and clarity issues above.
