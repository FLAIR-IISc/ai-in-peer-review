This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, framing it as a constrained non-convex optimization problem that accounts for node and edge features. The authors propose both a gradient-free and a gradient-based method to solve this problem, leveraging the concept of inexact oracles and providing convergence rate guarantees for both. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees where existing methods lacked them, and demonstrating improved ranking quality in experiments.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the established foundation of PageRank and Supervised PageRank [21]. The authors correctly identify the limitations of prior work, particularly the lack of convergence guarantees for the state-of-the-art gradient-based method. The proposed approaches are logically presented, with clear connections drawn to existing optimization techniques, such as those in [16, 7]. The introduction of inexact oracles is a sensible approach given the computational challenges of evaluating the loss function and its derivatives. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of the proposed algorithms.

However, a significant weakness is the placement of the experimental results exclusively in the supplementary materials. This omission severely hinders the assessment of the practical impact of the proposed methods and renders the main text incomplete. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, empirical validation is crucial for convincing the NIPS community of the work's significance. 

Further points regarding the technical aspects: The assumption of convexity in Theorem 1 seems at odds with the explicitly stated non-convexity of the problem (specifically the loss function in (2.4)). The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the set Φ is chosen to ensure local convexity, and a discussion of the implications if this assumption does not hold. Regarding Theorem 3, while providing a bound on the distance between successive iterates, it doesn't explicitly guarantee convergence to a stationary point; clarifying this would strengthen the analysis. Finally, the slow convergence observed in Figure 1 (supplementary material) raises concerns about the practical efficiency of the gradient-free method. Reporting computational complexity or wall-clock time alongside the convergence curves would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous convergence guarantees for both proposed methods, addressing a key limitation of existing approaches.
*   **Novelty:** The adaptation of gradient-free and gradient-based methods to the constrained non-convex Supervised PageRank problem is a novel contribution.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-defined and motivated, with a clear connection to relevant prior work.
*   **Inexact Oracle Approach:** The use of inexact oracles is a practical and sensible approach to handle the computational challenges of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Missing Experimental Results:** The exclusion of experimental results from the main text significantly weakens the paper's impact.
*   **Convexity Assumption:** The assumption of convexity in Theorem 1 requires further justification and discussion.
*   **Convergence Guarantee:** Theorem 3's bound doesn’t explicitly guarantee convergence to a stationary point.
*   **Slow Convergence:** The observed slow convergence of the gradient-free method (Figure 1 in the supplement) is a concern.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem with theoretical guarantees lacking in previous work.
*   The proposed methods are novel adaptations of existing techniques to a challenging optimization problem.
*   The theoretical analysis appears sound and provides valuable insights.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The omission of experimental results from the main text makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the work.
*   The assumptions regarding convexity require further justification.
*   The slow convergence of the gradient-free method raises concerns about its efficiency.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with potential practical value. However, the absence of experimental results in the main text is a major drawback. I recommend **weak reject** pending the inclusion of key experimental findings, a clarification of the convexity assumption, and a more detailed discussion of convergence properties.  If the experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, and the theoretical concerns are adequately addressed, a reconsideration for acceptance would be warranted.
