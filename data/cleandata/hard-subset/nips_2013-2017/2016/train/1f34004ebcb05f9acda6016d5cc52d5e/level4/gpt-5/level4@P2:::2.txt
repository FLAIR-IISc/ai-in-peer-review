Summary
The paper formulates learning Supervised PageRank as a constrained, generally non-convex optimization problem in which node and edge features determine restart and transition probabilities. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods that rely on inexact oracles:

- A gradient-free method that uses an inexact zero-order oracle and a randomized sphere-smoothing estimator to guide projected updates over a constrained parameter set.

- A gradient-based method that uses an inexact first-order oracle with an adaptive projected gradient routine for composite (possibly non-convex) objectives.

On the lower level, both methods approximate PageRank stationary distributions (and, for the gradient-based method, their derivatives) using a linearly convergent scheme in the spirit of Nesterov–Nemirovski, replacing the power method commonly used for PageRank. The upper level then performs the optimization with explicit control of the inner accuracy to obtain convergence rate guarantees for the overall procedures. The authors provide complexity bounds that expose the trade-off between inner accuracy and total runtime. Experiments (in the supplement) on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset compare the proposed methods to a previous gradient-based approach for Supervised PageRank.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on supervised random walks and supervised PageRank variants (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec; prior Supervised PageRank work) and on methods for computing PageRank and its derivatives (power methods, Nesterov–Nemirovski). On the optimization side, it adapts random gradient-free optimization (e.g., Nesterov & Spokoiny) and inexact-oracle frameworks (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Ghadimi & Lan) to constrained and non-convex settings with explicit oracle inaccuracies.

Strengths
- Theory: Clear, modular analysis of two-level algorithms with inexact lower-level computations, including convergence-rate guarantees and complexity accounting for both gradient-free and gradient-based variants.
- Practical modeling: Incorporates node and edge features into PageRank via supervised parameters, which is relevant for learning-to-rank on graphs.
- Methodological contribution: Extends random gradient-free methods to constrained problems with biased/noisy function values; develops an adaptive projected gradient scheme with inexact first-order oracle.
- Implementation detail: Addresses the inner–outer accuracy trade-off and shows how to set inner iteration counts to meet prescribed outer tolerances.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing results in the main text: Key experimental findings (loss curves, ranking quality, runtime/iteration counts) are relegated to the supplement, which makes the paper feel incomplete. Please move the main quantitative results (final loss, ranking metrics, wall-clock time, oracle calls/iteration counts) into the paper.
- Convexity assumption vs non-convex objective: Theorem 1 assumes convexity, while the objective defined via the squared hinge over pairwise differences in PageRank scores is generally non-convex in the parameters. The paper mentions choosing Φ to be a “small vicinity” where f is convex. How is Φ selected in practice to ensure convexity, and how sensitive are results to its radius? Can you provide conditions on features/α under which f is convex on Φ?
- Stationarity guarantee for the gradient-based method: Theorem 3 bounds MK‖xK − xK+1‖, which resembles a projected gradient mapping. Does this imply convergence to a first-order stationary point (e.g., vanishing projected gradient) for the non-convex case? Please clarify the precise stationarity notion and the steps linking the bound to stationarity in constrained settings.
- Empirical efficiency: Supplementary Figure 1 suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly. Please report wall-clock time, total arithmetic operations, inner iterations (N, N1, N2), and number of outer iterations to reach comparable loss for all methods. Ablations demonstrating the inner–outer accuracy trade-off would be valuable.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is notation-heavy, with important assumptions and constants pushed to the supplement. Provide a self-contained summary of key constants (e.g., β1) and practical parameter choices. Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted. Minor typos also occur (e.g., “concider,” “probablities,” “th Conference”).

Quality
The theoretical developments are promising and appear technically sound, contingent on the stated oracle bounds. However, the convexity assumption for the gradient-free theory and the stationarity implications for the gradient-based scheme need clearer connection to the non-convex objective at hand. Experimental evidence should be more prominent and include efficiency comparisons.

Clarity
The overall organization is reasonable, but the main text should include core empirical results and a concise, self-contained statement of assumptions and constants. Clarify the selection and role of Φ, the precise inexact oracle definitions used in practice, and how tolerances are set.

Originality
Adapting random gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with uniformly bounded inexact oracles, and developing an adaptive projected gradient method for composite non-convex problems with inexact first-order information, are meaningful contributions. Applying linearly convergent stationary-distribution computations to learning supervised PageRank within an inexact-oracle framework is a solid synthesis of ideas.

Significance
If the guarantees hold under practical settings and the empirical gains are robust, the methods could be impactful for large-scale graph-based ranking. Demonstrated improvements over the state of the art are encouraging, but stronger in-paper empirical validation and efficiency data are needed to establish practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contributions for inexact-oracle optimization in a relevant application.
- Clear articulation of inner–outer accuracy trade-offs and complexity bounds.
- Promising empirical improvements over prior supervised PageRank training.

Cons for acceptance
- Core experiments and efficiency analyses are not in the main text.
- Convexity assumption for Theorem 1 vs non-convex objective needs clearer justification.
- Stationarity/convergence guarantees for the gradient-based method in the non-convex constrained setting require clearer statements.
- Presentation and references need polishing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move key experimental tables/figures (final loss, ranking metrics, runtime, iteration counts) into the main paper; add ablations on inner accuracy (N, N1, N2) vs outer progress.
- Precisely state conditions under which f is convex on Φ and provide guidance (or diagnostics) for choosing Φ.
- Clarify the stationarity criterion implied by Theorem 3 and its relevance to constrained non-convex problems.
- Consolidate assumptions and constants in a readable table; fix typos and complete references.
