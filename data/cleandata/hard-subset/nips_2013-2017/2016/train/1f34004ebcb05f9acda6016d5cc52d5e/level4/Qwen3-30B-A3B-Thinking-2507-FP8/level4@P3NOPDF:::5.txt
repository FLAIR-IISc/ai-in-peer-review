

The paper proposes a framework for learning node and edge weights in a PageRank-like model, addressing a gap noted in prior work regarding performance guarantees for parameter estimation. While the technical contributions include adapting gradient-free optimization to a biased oracle setting and establishing theoretical bounds, significant concerns regarding presentation, empirical validation, and problem impact undermine the paper's contribution.

**Problem Framing and Technical Contributions.** The authors frame the problem as "Supervised PageRank," but this mislabels the approach—[21] only discusses semi-supervised PageRank, creating a citation inconsistency. More critically, the core framework reuses concepts from [21] under new notation without sufficient justification for the rebranding. The technical analysis proceeds by positing assumptions (e.g., constraining the parameter space φ to an R-ball around a fixed φ₀), stating a loss function, and deriving bounds on approximate gradients. While the theoretical bounds appear strong, the assumption that φ remains near φ₀ is problematic. The paper fails to discuss whether this restriction is realistic in practice—e.g., whether φ₀ can be reasonably chosen for diverse graphs, or whether the R-ball size significantly limits the algorithm's applicability. Without empirical validation of this assumption, the theoretical guarantees risk being inapplicable to real-world data.

**Empirical Validation and Presentation.** The most severe flaw is the paper’s reliance on the appendix for all substantive results. The main text outlines experiments on "a web graph used in related work" but provides no tables, figures, or specific metrics in the body. Key details—such as how φ₀ and R were selected, the number of graphs tested, or the extent of performance gains—are deferred to the appendix, leaving no evidence to assess the algorithm’s practical utility. The claim that "the novel algorithm outperforms others" lacks context: *by how much*? *On which metrics*? *For which graph types*? The absence of even a single comparative table in the main text renders the empirical contribution unverifiable. Furthermore, the paper does not report critical parameters (e.g., R, φ₀ values used in experiments), making replication impossible. The single graph tested appears insufficient to support general claims, and the lack of diversity in graph datasets raises questions about scalability.

**Writing and Scholarly Rigor.** The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology (e.g., "constriction" instead of "restriction" for the R-ball). Citations are poorly executed (e.g., mislabeling [21] as "Supervised PageRank"), undermining credibility. The theoretical section also lacks clarity: proof sketches are omitted, and the connection between assumptions and empirical feasibility is underdeveloped. The gradient-free method adaptation is described generically, but the paper does not explain *why* this approach is necessary over standard optimization methods given the problem structure.

**Impact and Conclusion.** The core problem—learning PageRank parameters with guarantees—is of moderate interest, but the paper fails to demonstrate its significance. The theoretical advances are overshadowed by the lack of empirical evidence supporting their real-world relevance. The algorithm’s performance claims rest entirely on unreported appendix results, and the restrictive assumptions (e.g., fixed φ₀) cast doubt on its practical value. Without clear evidence that the method outperforms baselines *on diverse datasets* or that the assumptions hold in practice, the contribution remains speculative. The reviewers’ key question—"What are the practical consequences of your improvements?"—goes unanswered in the main text.

In summary, while the theoretical bounds are technically sound, the paper’s failure to validate assumptions empirically, its overreliance on the appendix for results, and its misalignment with cited work significantly weaken its contribution. The authors should either: (1) move critical results (empirical details, parameter choices, graph diversity) to the main text with rigorous analysis, or (2) substantially revise the problem statement to justify the restrictive assumptions. As written, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method solves an impactful problem with practical significance. I recommend rejection pending substantial revision addressing these issues.
