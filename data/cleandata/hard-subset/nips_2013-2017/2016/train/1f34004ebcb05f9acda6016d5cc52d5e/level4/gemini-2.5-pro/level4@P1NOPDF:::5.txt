### Review

**Summary**

The authors introduce a supervised PageRank framework where node and edge weights are parameterized by a feature model. They correctly note that prior learning algorithms for this model lack theoretical performance guarantees. To address this, the paper proposes a new algorithm that combines a method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with a gradient-free optimization technique. The framework itself appears to be a re-formulation of the one presented in [21], albeit with different notation. The authors establish several technical assumptions, define a loss function, and present theoretical contributions in the form of lemmas bounding the approximate gradient and a theorem on the performance of their proposed biased gradient-free oracle. They then adapt this general method to their specific learning problem, providing further performance bounds. The experimental evaluation is outlined on a single web graph from a related work, but a detailed report and analysis of the results are deferred to the appendix.

**Strengths**

The main strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contributions. The development of an algorithm with performance guarantees for this problem is a valuable direction. The bounds and the analysis of the gradient-free optimization method appear to be rigorous and strong. If the underlying assumptions are sound, the proposed algorithm and its analysis could be a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical work may be powerful, the paper in its current form is not convincing due to several major weaknesses:

1.  **Paper Structure and Lack of Detail:** The most significant issue is that the main body of the paper is not self-contained. Nearly all of the essential content, including detailed proofs and the full empirical results, is relegated to the appendix. The paper provides neither proof outlines nor a substantive summary of the experimental findings. This makes it impossible for a reviewer to properly assess the work based on the main text alone. Critical details, such as tables, figures, or specific performance metrics, are entirely absent.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is cursory and lacks sufficient detail to be reproducible or convincing.
    *   The authors claim their algorithm outperforms others, but they do not quantify this improvement or discuss its practical implications. By how much is it better? What is the real-world consequence of this improvement?
    *   Key hyperparameters, such as the choice of phi_0 and the radius R for the search space, are not reported or justified.
    *   The evaluation appears to be limited to a single graph. It is unclear if the method's performance generalizes to a more diverse set of graphs with different properties.

3.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** While the theoretical bounds are a strength, I have doubts about whether the technical assumptions hold for real-world empirical data. Some assumptions seem particularly limiting and their impact is not discussed. For instance, the constraint that confines the parameter search space to an R-ball around a pre-selected vector phi_0 could greatly restrict the efficacy and applicability of the algorithm. This choice needs to be justified, and its potential limitations should be acknowledged and discussed.

4.  **Clarity and Scholarship:** The writing quality could be improved, as there are numerous grammatical errors. Furthermore, the scholarship appears sloppy at times. For example, the paper refers to the problem as "Supervised PageRank" and cites [21], but that reference appears to only discuss a semi-supervised variant of the problem.

5.  **Impact:** The paper fails to make a compelling case for the impact of the problem being solved. A stronger motivation for why this supervised PageRank formulation is important and what applications would benefit from it would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you please incorporate the key experimental results (e.g., a main table or figure) into the main body of the paper and discuss the magnitude and practical significance of your algorithm's performance improvement?
*   How were the hyperparameters phi_0 and R selected for the experiments? Could you discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to these choices?
*   Was the algorithm evaluated on more than one graph? If so, please include these results. If not, could you comment on the expected generalizability of your findings?
*   Could you provide more intuition on why your technical assumptions are reasonable for real-world graphs and discuss the potential limitations imposed by them, particularly the R-ball constraint on the parameter space?

**Conclusion**

The paper presents a potentially powerful algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees. However, the current manuscript fails to convince me of its contributions. The core evidence, both theoretical and empirical, is hidden in the appendix, and the experimental section is too vague to be meaningful. The practicality of the assumptions is also questionable and requires further discussion. Due to these significant issues, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
