

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models through novel optimization methods with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors consider a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning parameters that define node and edge weights in a graph-based ranking framework. Unlike previous work [21], which lacked convergence guarantees for its gradient-based optimization approach, this paper develops two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) that provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees while maintaining practical efficiency.

The main contribution is the development of a framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle information, specifically adapted to the Supervised PageRank setting. The authors demonstrate that the linearly convergent method [17] can approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk with any desired accuracy, enabling the construction of inexact oracle information for the optimization problem. They then develop two algorithms: a gradient-based method with convergence guarantees for constrained non-convex problems (extending the framework of [7]), and a gradient-free method adapted from [16] to handle constrained optimization with function value approximations. The paper rigorously analyzes the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, providing theoretical complexity bounds for both methods.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning, which was previously missing in the literature. While the core optimization techniques (particularly the gradient-free method) draw from Nesterov and Spokoiny [16], their adaptation to the specific constrained optimization problem with inexact oracle information for Supervised PageRank is novel. The authors properly contextualize their work by acknowledging prior Supervised PageRank formulations [21] and clearly differentiating their theoretical contributions.

The experimental section validates the theoretical claims, demonstrating that both proposed algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art [21] on a real-world web ranking task. The results are statistically significant (p < 0.005), and the paper provides a thorough analysis of how different parameters affect performance. The empirical evidence strongly supports the claim that the proposed methods achieve better ranking quality with theoretical guarantees.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous convergence analysis for both optimization methods
- Well-structured presentation of the two-level optimization framework
- Comprehensive experimental validation on a meaningful real-world ranking task
- Thorough analysis of trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational complexity
- Proper contextualization of contributions relative to prior work (including [21] and [16])

Weaknesses:
- The novelty of the optimization techniques themselves is somewhat limited as they adapt existing methods (Nesterov [16] and [7]), though the specific adaptation to the constrained Supervised PageRank problem is novel
- The paper could better articulate how the theoretical guarantees translate to practical advantages beyond the experimental results
- The significance discussion could more clearly explain the broader impact on the field beyond web ranking applications
- The conclusion section is brief and could better summarize the key contributions and their implications

The paper is technically sound, with well-written proofs that appear to be correct (though I have not verified all details). The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined.

Regarding originality, while the core optimization techniques are adaptations of prior work, the specific application to Supervised PageRank with theoretical convergence guarantees represents a meaningful contribution. The paper does not claim novelty of the optimization methods themselves but rather their application to this specific problem with convergence guarantees. This is appropriately contextualized in the introduction, where the authors clearly identify the missing theoretical guarantees in prior work [21].

For significance, the work has clear potential impact in web search and ranking applications, as demonstrated by the experiments. However, the paper could better articulate how the theoretical guarantees might translate to more reliable and efficient ranking systems in practice. The connection to broader machine learning applications beyond web ranking is not sufficiently explored.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
1. Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
2. Develops practical algorithms that outperform state-of-the-art in experiments
3. Rigorous theoretical analysis of trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational complexity
4. Well-written paper with clear presentation of the two-level optimization framework

Con-acceptance arguments:
1. The core optimization techniques are adaptations of prior work rather than fundamentally new methods
2. The significance discussion could better articulate broader implications beyond web ranking
3. The paper could more explicitly position its contribution relative to the NIPS community's interest in optimization methods

Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in the Supervised PageRank literature. The theoretical guarantees are valuable for practitioners who require reliable optimization methods, and the experimental results confirm the practical advantages. The paper meets NIPS standards for technical quality and clarity, with the primary limitation being the somewhat incremental nature of the optimization techniques themselves. However, the specific application to Supervised PageRank with convergence guarantees represents a significant contribution to the field.

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation, clear experimental validation, and practical relevance to a major application domain, I recommend acceptance. The contribution is sufficiently novel within the context of the Supervised PageRank problem, and the paper makes a clear scientific contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based learning.
