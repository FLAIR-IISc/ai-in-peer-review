This paper addresses the non-convex loss-minimization problem that arises when learning the parameters of a Supervised PageRank model. In this ranking model, a random walk is defined on a graph where the transition and restart probabilities are parameterized as linear functions of node and edge features. A significant challenge in this setting is that the objective function and its gradient cannot be calculated exactly.

To address this, the authors propose a two-level approach that operates with an "inexact oracle"—that is, it relies on approximations of the loss function and its gradient that are accurate to within a known bound. At the lower level, a linearly convergent iterative method is used to compute these approximations. At the upper level, two optimization algorithms are proposed to learn the model parameters: one gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex problems, and one random gradient-free method adapted for constrained problems. For both methods, the authors provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees. The proposed algorithms are experimentally validated on a web page ranking task, where they are shown to outperform the existing state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths**

*   The primary strength of this work is that it provides formal convergence guarantees for learning the Supervised PageRank model. This is a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art method, which lacked such a theoretical foundation.
*   The paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis, including the proofs of convergence, appears to be correct, although I did not verify them in full detail.
*   The adaptation of gradient-based and gradient-free optimization frameworks to handle problems with inexact oracles, particularly under constraints, is a valuable contribution with potential applications beyond the specific ranking problem studied here.

**Weaknesses**

*   My main concern is regarding the overall originality of the contribution. The core optimization techniques seem to be adaptations of previously proposed methods, especially the random gradient-free approach, which builds heavily on the work of Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015). Furthermore, the Supervised PageRank algorithm itself is not novel. While combining these elements and proving convergence in this specific setting is a non-trivial effort, the conceptual novelty could be more clearly articulated.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   To better contextualize the contribution with respect to the state-of-the-art, I would suggest presenting the optimization framework in a more general context first, and then framing the Supervised PageRank problem as a specific application. This would help to more clearly delineate the general optimization contributions from their application to a known model.
*   On a minor note, the paper currently lacks a conclusion section. Adding one to summarize the key results and perhaps suggest directions for future work would strengthen the paper, and there appears to be sufficient space to do so.
