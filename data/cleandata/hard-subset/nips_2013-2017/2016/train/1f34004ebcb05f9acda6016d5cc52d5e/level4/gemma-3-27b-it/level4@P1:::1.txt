This paper analyzes the convergence of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning the parameters of a general graph-based ranking model. This model is defined by a random walk weighted by nodes and edges, where these weights are themselves determined by features of the nodes and edges. Existing optimization methods struggle with this problem due to the inability to compute the objective function exactly. The proposed approach employs a two-level strategy. The lower level utilizes a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, demonstrating that the loss function can be approximated to any desired precision.  The authors also develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization problems using an inexact oracle, and prove its convergence to a stationary point. A key contribution lies in adapting this approach to constrained optimization problems where the function value is known with a specified accuracy, proving convergence and applying it to the upper level of the proposed algorithm. The paper is well-written and the proofs appear sound, though a complete verification was beyond the scope of this review. 

My primary concern is that the core optimization techniques presented build heavily on prior work, particularly “Yurii Nesterov and Vladimir Spokoiny, Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015.” Furthermore, the supervised PageRank algorithm itself has been previously introduced. While the adaptations and convergence guarantees are valuable, the novelty of the core approaches feels incremental. 

A minor point is the absence of a concluding section; given the length and detail of the paper, a conclusion summarizing the contributions and outlining potential future work would be beneficial. Finally, presenting the optimization problem within a more general framework—demonstrating how this work represents a specific case of a broader class of problems—would help to better situate the contribution relative to the state-of-the-art and highlight its potential applicability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for optimization methods applied to a challenging non-convex problem.
*   The authors rigorously analyze the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity.
*   Experimental results demonstrate improved performance compared to existing methods.
*   The work is well-written and the mathematical arguments appear sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core optimization techniques are largely adaptations of existing methods.
*   The supervised PageRank framework is not novel in itself.
*   The paper lacks a concluding section summarizing the contributions.
