Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank with node- and edge-level features. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN). At the lower level, they approximate stationary distributions and their derivatives using the Nesterov–Nemirovski approach [17], rather than the classical power method used by prior work. At the upper level, they apply either an adaptive gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle (GBN) or a random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle (GFN). Both methods come with convergence-rate guarantees and complexity bounds under inexact oracle assumptions, and neither requires exact values of the objective.

This extends prior work on PageRank, HITS, and supervised random walks, and addresses a key limitation of the state-of-the-art two-level gradient method for Supervised PageRank [21], which used power iterations and lacked convergence guarantees due to oracle inexactness. The paper builds on and generalizes ideas from Nesterov–Nemirovski [17] for computing stationary distributions and from random gradient-free and inexact-oracle optimization (e.g., [7,16]), adapting them to constrained problems with inexact evaluations. A practical benefit is avoiding explicit derivative computations for large matrices.

Strengths:
- Theory: Clear convergence-rate guarantees for both GBN and GFN under inexact oracles, including complexity bounds. The gradient-based method handles nonconvex composite problems in a constrained setting with unknown Lipschitz constants via an adaptive scheme; the gradient-free method provides a guaranteed decrease using a biased gradient-free oracle on the sphere.
- Algorithmic design: Sound two-level structure with a linearly convergent lower-level method [17] for stationary distribution approximation and upper-level optimization tailored to inexact oracles. The analysis quantifies the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and total complexity.
- Practical relevance: Avoids computing a derivative for each element of a large matrix; hyperparameters are specified; proofs are provided in the supplement.
- Empirics: On a real web-ranking task from Yandex, both methods outperform the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach (GBP) in terms of the loss function, and GBN requires fewer upper-level iterations than GBP for certain step sizes.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement:
- Clarity/notation: The paper is notation-heavy and difficult to follow in places. A symbol table would help significantly. As an example, N in Eq. (3.1) appears long before it is defined as the number of steps (e.g., the meaning is not made explicit until much later). Defining all symbols at first use and consolidating them in a table would reduce cognitive load.
- Experimental evaluation: The empirical comparison focuses on the loss function (Eq. 2.4) rather than ranking quality metrics. Since the problem is learning to rank, reporting standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) alongside the loss would better support the claim of improved ranking quality. The paper states improved ranking quality, but Table 1 and the plots focus on the loss.
- Baselines: Given that this work generalizes/refines [17] in the lower-level computation and uses it centrally, a direct empirical comparison with [17] (e.g., as a baseline for the lower-level approximation’s effect on the upper-level performance) would strengthen the evaluation.
- Reproducibility: The dataset is not public. While the description is clear and hyperparameters are given, public code or additional implementation details (e.g., how restarts and edge features are constructed per query, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to L and ε) would help others reproduce the results.

Quality:
- The paper appears technically sound. Theorems are stated clearly, and proofs are provided in the supplement. The methods are carefully formulated for inexact oracle settings, and the assumptions (e.g., convexity in a local neighborhood for the gradient-free method) are made explicit where needed. Empirical results support the theoretical claims on convergence and performance relative to GBP in terms of loss.

Clarity:
- Overall clear structure from model to numerical oracles to optimization and experiments. However, readability suffers from heavy notation and delayed definitions. A symbol table and earlier clarification of frequently used symbols (e.g., N, N1, N2, α, r, s, β1) would improve accessibility. Minor edits to streamline the presentation of the two-level scheme and to cross-reference where accuracy parameters translate into iteration counts would also help.

Originality:
- The combination of a lower-level linearly convergent method [17] with upper-level optimization under an inexact oracle is well-motivated and, to my knowledge, new in this supervised PageRank setting. The gradient-free method’s adaptation to constrained problems with inexact function values and the adaptive gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracles are meaningful contributions.

Significance:
- The approach is relevant for large-scale ranking, where exact computations and full derivatives are infeasible. The theoretical guarantees and the practical two-level design are likely to be useful beyond this specific task. That said, the empirical section would be stronger with ranking metrics and a direct comparison to [17].

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contributions with convergence guarantees under inexact oracles.
- Practical algorithms that avoid computing large matrix derivatives.
- Empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art method in terms of loss.
- Clear hyperparameter reporting and comprehensive supplementary proofs.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation focused on loss rather than ranking metrics.
- Clarity issues due to heavy notation and delayed definitions; a symbol table is missing.
- No direct experimental comparison to [17], despite its central role in the approach.
- Non-public dataset makes reproducibility harder (though the description is helpful).

Suggestions to authors:
- Add a symbol table and define N, N1, N2, and other recurrent quantities at first use.
- Report standard ranking measures (e.g., NDCG, MAP) in addition to loss, and clarify the link between loss reductions and ranking improvements.
- Include a direct empirical comparison to [17] (e.g., lower-level approximations via [17] vs. power method, and impact on upper-level performance).
- If possible, release code or provide more implementation details to facilitate reproduction.

Overall, this is a strong paper with solid theory and promising empirical results. Addressing the clarity and evaluation issues would further strengthen the contribution.
