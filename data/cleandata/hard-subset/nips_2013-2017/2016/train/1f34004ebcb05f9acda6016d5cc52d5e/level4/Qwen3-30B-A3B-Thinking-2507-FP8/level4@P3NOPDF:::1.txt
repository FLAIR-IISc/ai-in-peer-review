

This paper addresses the convergence analysis of a non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning parameters in a graph-based ranking model. The model is defined by a random walk on a graph whose transition weights depend on node and edge features. A key challenge is that the objective function cannot be evaluated exactly, rendering standard optimization methods inapplicable. The authors propose a two-level algorithm to circumvent this: (1) a linearly convergent method approximates the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov chain, enabling loss function evaluation within any desired precision; (2) a novel gradient-based method for constrained non-convex optimization, leveraging an inexact oracle, is developed and proven to converge to a stationary point. The approach is validated on the proposed ranking model, with theoretical guarantees for both levels of the algorithm.

The paper is well-structured and written with clarity. The core proofs appear sound, though the reviewer did not verify every detail. The technical contributions are significant for the specific context of graph-based ranking with inexact function evaluations. The adaptation of inexact oracle techniques to constrained non-convex optimization—particularly their application to the second-level optimization—represents a useful extension for problems where exact function values are unavailable.

However, the novelty of the core optimization framework requires careful contextualization. The primary algorithmic components (e.g., the linearly convergent stationary distribution estimator and the inexact oracle gradient method) are closely aligned with techniques from Nesterov and Spokoiny (2015), *Random Gradient-Free Minimization of Convex Functions*, which introduced similar inexact oracle approaches for convex problems. While the authors adapt these ideas to non-convex constrained settings and apply them to a graph-ranking problem, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from this prior work. A clearer comparison—explicitly stating how their constrained non-convex extension advances beyond Nesterov and Spokoiny’s convex framework—would strengthen the novelty claim. Additionally, the supervised PageRank model used as a case study has been previously proposed (e.g., in work by Zhou et al., 2007, or subsequent graph-based ranking literature), though the authors’ focus on *optimization convergence* for this model is new. The paper should clarify whether the model itself is novel or if the contribution lies solely in the optimization analysis.

The absence of a conclusion is a notable oversight. The paper ends with results and a brief discussion, but a dedicated conclusion would synthesize key insights, limitations, and future directions—particularly regarding the practical scalability of the two-level approach or broader applicability beyond graph ranking. Given the space constraints of the conference, a concise conclusion (150–200 words) is essential.

To better position the work, the authors should frame the optimization problem within a more general mathematical context. For instance, they could present the core problem as a specific instance of constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle, where the oracle’s precision depends on auxiliary approximations (like the stationary distribution estimation here). This would: (i) clarify that the algorithm is a specialized solution to a broader class of problems; (ii) highlight how their approach resolves a key gap (inexact objectives in constrained settings); and (iii) enable a more precise comparison with related work beyond Nesterov and Spokoiny. Currently, the paper’s focus on graph ranking risks obscuring the wider relevance of the optimization framework.

Minor grammatical and stylistic improvements are needed. For example:  
- “a random walk conducted by weights” → “a random walk defined by weights”  
- “the value of the loss function can be approximated with any given precision” → “the loss function can be evaluated within arbitrary precision”  
- “the supervised pagerank algorithm has been also proposed previously” → “the supervised PageRank model has been previously proposed”  

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous convergence analysis for a practically relevant problem. To elevate its impact, the authors must:  
1. Explicitly contrast their constrained non-convex framework with Nesterov and Spokoiny’s convex setting, emphasizing the technical challenges overcome.  
2. Replace the current case-study presentation with a general problem formulation that situates the graph-ranking model as an application.  
3. Add a concise conclusion summarizing contributions, limitations, and future work.  

Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as a straightforward application of existing optimization techniques rather than a meaningful advance. Given the high bar for NIPS, the current version would benefit from stronger positioning against the state-of-the-art. The core ideas are promising but require clearer articulation of their distinctiveness. With minor structural and contextual refinements, this work could make a valuable contribution to optimization for graph-based learning.
