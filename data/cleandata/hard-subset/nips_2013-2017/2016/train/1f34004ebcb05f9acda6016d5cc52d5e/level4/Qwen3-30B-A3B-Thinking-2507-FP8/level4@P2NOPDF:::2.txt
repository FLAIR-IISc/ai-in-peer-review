

This paper proposes a novel formulation of Supervised PageRank as a constrained non-convex optimization problem leveraging node and edge properties, introducing two solution approaches: a gradient-free method utilizing an inexact zeroth-order oracle and a gradient-based method employing an inexact first-order oracle. While the theoretical framework presents an interesting direction, the paper suffers from a critical weakness that undermines its core contribution: all experimental results are relegated to the supplementary material, rendering the main text incomplete and preventing evaluation of the proposed methods' empirical validity. This omission significantly hinders the assessment of the work's practical significance and comparative effectiveness.

The following points elaborate on key concerns:

1.  **Missing Experimental Results in Main Text:** The absence of any quantitative results, comparisons, or visualizations within the main body of the paper is unacceptable. The supplementary material should not substitute for the primary presentation of results. Without these, readers cannot judge the performance claims, scalability, or real-world applicability of the GFNM (gradient-free) and GFNM (gradient-based) algorithms. This omission makes the paper incomplete and violates standard publication practices for NIPS.

2.  **Convexity Assumption vs. Non-Convex Objective (Theorem 1):** Theorem 1 assumes convexity of the objective function to establish a bound. However, the objective function defined in Equation (2.4) is explicitly stated to be non-convex. This creates a fundamental logical inconsistency. The authors must clarify: *How is the set $\Phi$ chosen to guarantee the convexity of the objective function in Theorem 1, given that the underlying problem is formulated as non-convex?* Without resolving this contradiction, the theoretical foundation of Theorem 1 is unsound.

3.  **Convergence Guarantee of Theorem 3:** Theorem 3 provides a bound for $\|M_K (x_K - x_{K+1})\|$. This bound, however, does not directly establish convergence of the sequence $\{x_k\}$ to a stationary point. The authors need to explicitly state whether this bound implies convergence to a stationary point (e.g., by showing $\|x_K - x_{K+1}\| \to 0$ implies stationarity under their assumptions) or if additional conditions are required. The current presentation leaves this critical aspect ambiguous.

4.  **Algorithm Efficiency (Supplemental Figure 1):** As evident from Supplemental Figure 1, the gradient-free method (GFNM) exhibits slow convergence. The authors should report and discuss computational efficiency metrics (e.g., wall-clock time, iterations to reach a target accuracy) for both algorithms, especially given the practical importance of convergence speed. The lack of such discussion in the main text, coupled with the slow convergence observed, raises questions about the practical utility of the proposed methods.

In summary, while the theoretical formulation of Supervised PageRank as a constrained non-convex optimization problem is potentially interesting, the paper's critical flaw—omitting all experimental results from the main text—prevents a meaningful assessment of its contribution. Furthermore, the logical inconsistency regarding convexity in Theorem 1 and the lack of clarity on convergence guarantees in Theorem 3 require substantial revision. The authors should address these fundamental issues by: (1) moving all essential experimental results to the main text; (2) resolving the convexity contradiction in Theorem 1; (3) clarifying the convergence implications of Theorem 3; and (4) including efficiency analysis for both algorithms. Without these revisions, the paper's claims lack empirical support and theoretical rigor, making its current form unsuitable for publication.
