This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into the ranking process. The authors propose two two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a random gradient-free method (GFN). The core contribution lies in providing, for the first time, theoretical convergence rate guarantees for this problem. This is achieved by framing the optimization within an inexact oracle setting, where the stationary distribution and its derivatives are approximated at a lower level using an efficient method from Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. The upper-level optimizers are adaptations of existing methods [7, 16] to handle constrained problems and inexact information, for which the authors provide new convergence analyses.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution (Originality and Significance):** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical analysis. It is the first work to provide convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for learning Supervised PageRank models, addressing a significant gap in the previous state-of-the-art [21]. The development of a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle and an adaptive step-size is a valuable contribution that could be of interest beyond this specific application.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs provided in the supplementary material. The analysis is thorough, extending to the complexity of the entire two-level algorithm and investigating the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall computational cost. This represents a complete and technically sound piece of work.

*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a large-scale, real-world web ranking task demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods. Both GBN and GFN outperform the existing gradient-based baseline in terms of the final loss function value, with the gradient-free method (GFN) showing particularly strong performance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is technically dense and introduces a large amount of notation, which can make it challenging to follow. For instance, the parameter `N` is used in Equation 3.1, but its role as the number of iterations is not explicitly clarified until much later in the text. The inclusion of a symbol table at the beginning of the paper would significantly improve its readability and accessibility.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments focus on the final value of the loss function as the primary metric for comparison. While this is the objective that the algorithms directly optimize, the ultimate goal of the model is to improve ranking quality. The empirical claims would be substantially strengthened by reporting performance on standard information retrieval metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) for the test set. This would provide a more direct assessment of the practical impact of achieving a lower loss value.

*   **Reproducibility:** The experiments are conducted on a proprietary dataset from a commercial search engine. While the description of the data-generation process is clear, the unavailability of the dataset could hinder efforts by other researchers to reproduce the experimental results and directly compare future methods.
