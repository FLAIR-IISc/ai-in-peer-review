The authors address the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. They highlight a lack of convergence guarantees for a previous optimization method [21] and propose both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches leveraging inexact oracle estimations. Their algorithms build upon existing techniques for approximating the stationary distribution of Markov random walks [17] and adapt random gradient-free optimization methods [16] to the constrained optimization setting. The paper provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods and demonstrates improved ranking quality in experiments.

The work builds upon the foundation of PageRank and its extensions [18, 11, 8, 9, 19], acknowledging the limitations of structure-based approaches and the need to incorporate node and edge features as explored in previous research [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21, 22]. The authors correctly identify the two-level optimization structure inherent in Supervised PageRank [21] and address the convergence issues present in the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach.  The use of an inexact oracle framework, coupled with analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity, is a valuable contribution.

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and the provision of convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods – a significant improvement over existing work [21]. The adaptation of existing optimization techniques to the constrained non-convex problem and the detailed analysis of oracle accuracy are commendable. The experimental results, while summarized in the main body, demonstrate performance improvements on a real-world ranking task. 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The writing contains numerous grammatical errors which hinder clarity. The citations are occasionally imprecise; for example, the problem is referred to as Supervised PageRank with a citation to [21], while the original presentation of this framework appears in [21] under the name “Semi-supervised PageRank.” The assumptions underlying the theoretical results, such as the restriction of the parameter space to an R-ball around a chosen point, could limit the applicability and efficacy of the algorithm, and this limitation deserves further discussion. Crucially, a substantial amount of detail, including proof outlines and comprehensive experimental results, is relegated to the supplementary materials. The lack of detailed results within the main text makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the observed improvements. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation appears limited to a single dataset, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Important implementation details, such as the method used to select ϕ̂ and R in the experiments, are not clearly articulated.  The discussion of the experimental results lacks nuance; simply stating that the proposed methods “outperform” the state-of-the-art without quantifying the extent of the improvement or providing statistical significance measures is insufficient.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
*   Addresses a well-motivated problem with clear relevance to ranking and recommendation systems.
*   Adaptation of existing optimization techniques to a challenging non-convex setting.
*   Empirical validation demonstrating improved performance on a real-world dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous grammatical errors and imprecise citations detract from clarity and credibility.
*   Significant detail relegated to supplementary materials, hindering evaluation.
*   Limited experimental evaluation with only one dataset.
*   Lack of detailed analysis and quantification of experimental results.
*   Potentially restrictive assumptions regarding the parameter space.
*   The impact of the improvements is not clearly articulated.



Overall, while the paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution, the lack of clarity, limited experimental evaluation, and reliance on supplementary materials significantly weaken its impact. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to resubmit after addressing these concerns, particularly by including a more comprehensive and detailed presentation of the experimental results within the main text.
