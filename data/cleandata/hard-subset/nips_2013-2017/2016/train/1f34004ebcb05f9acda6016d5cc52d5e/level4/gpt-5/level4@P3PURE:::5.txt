Summary
The paper motivates PageRank and proposes a learning framework in which node and edge weights are derived from a feature-based model. The authors note that a prior algorithm for learning the model’s parameters lacked performance guarantees. They propose to address this by combining (i) a previous method for approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk with (ii) an adaptation of a gradient-free optimization method suitable when the loss function can only be estimated.

The work reproduces a framework from [21], albeit under a different name and with different notation. The paper lays out several technical assumptions, defines a loss function, and explains why the resulting optimization problem is challenging. It presents lemmas that bound an approximate gradient, discusses a general gradient-free method using a biased gradient-free oracle, and states a performance theorem. With additional assumptions, the authors construct an algorithm for the learning problem, adapt the gradient-free methods to their setting, and provide further bounds. Experiments are outlined on a web graph used in related work; however, most details and analyses are relegated to the appendix.

Strengths
- The theoretical analysis is ambitious, with nontrivial bounds for a biased, gradient-free procedure applied to an estimated loss.
- The combination of stationary-distribution approximation with gradient-free optimization is well motivated for settings where the loss is only indirectly observable.

Major concerns
- Novelty and positioning: The framework appears to reproduce results from [21] under a new name and notation. The paper refers to the problem as “Supervised PageRank” while citing [21], but [21] addresses semi-supervised PageRank. This raises concerns about the clarity and accuracy of citation and about incremental contribution versus prior art.
- Assumptions and practicality: While the bounds are strong, I am not convinced the stated technical assumptions will hold in empirical settings. In particular, restricting the parameter search to an R-ball around a chosen phi0 could significantly limit the algorithm’s efficacy and applicability; this restriction should be justified and its practical impact discussed.
- Insufficient empirical evidence: The empirical section is too thin to support the claims. It is unclear whether more than one graph was used for testing or whether a diverse set of graphs was considered. Some key parameters are not reported. The paper claims that the proposed algorithm outperforms others, but there is no discussion of the extent or implications of the improvement. No tables or specific quantitative results are provided in the main body.
- Presentation of core content: Much of the substantive material (proofs and experimental details) is deferred to the appendix. The main text does not provide proof outlines or summaries of empirical findings, making it hard to assess the contribution and its practical significance.
- Writing quality and citations: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors. Citations are sloppy, and the mismatch between “Supervised PageRank” in this paper and “semi-supervised PageRank” in [21] is confusing.

Questions for the authors
- How were phi0 and R chosen in the experiments? Please provide a rationale and sensitivity analysis.
- Did you evaluate on more than one graph, and if so, what was the diversity of graph types and sizes?
- What are the concrete, practical consequences of your theoretical improvements? Please quantify the improvements and explain their implications for real-world use.

Overall assessment
The bounds and algorithmic ideas may be powerful, but the current presentation, reliance on restrictive assumptions, unclear positioning relative to [21], and especially the lack of substantive empirical evidence in the main text prevent the paper from making a compelling case for impact. Without clear proof outlines, comprehensive reporting of experimental results, and a careful discussion of assumptions (including the restriction to an R-ball around phi0), the work does not yet convincingly demonstrate either novelty or practical value.
