Summary
The paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank models that incorporate node and edge features through restart and transition probabilities. The learning objective is a (non-convex) pairwise squared hinge-style loss over query-dependent graphs. The authors propose two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-based method that uses an inexact first-order oracle and an adaptive projected gradient scheme to seek a stationary point; (ii) a gradient-free (randomized zero-order) method based on spherical smoothing with an inexact function oracle. On the lower level, both methods approximate the stationary distribution of the PageRank-like Markov chain using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent scheme, and derive upper bounds on the error in the function value and gradient via a generalization of the power method for derivatives. They analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity, proving convergence rates for both upper-level methods. Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing graph (≈600 queries, ≈11.7K vertices, ≈7.5K edges, ≈1.7K labeled pairs) compare to the state-of-the-art two-level gradient method [21] (which uses power-method-based derivatives) and show lower loss values for the proposed methods.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank (e.g., Richardson & Domingos; BrowseRank; Supervised Nested PageRank) and supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec), and on optimization with inexact oracles (Devolder et al.), nonconvex first/zeroth-order methods (Ghadimi & Lan), and random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov & Spokoiny). It replaces the classical power method with the Nesterov–Nemirovski weighted-iterate scheme for faster approximation of stationary distributions and extends derivative computation for Markov chains (Andrew, 1998/2000). The main theoretical novelty is adapting gradient-free and projected gradient frameworks to constrained problems with inexact oracles and providing explicit complexity bounds coupled to the lower-level approximations.

Quality
- Theory: The oracle constructions (Lemmas 1–2) and the upper-level algorithms’ convergence analyses are generally sound and useful. The inexact first-order oracle condition (5.1) is appropriate, and the adaptive scheme that does not require a known Lipschitz constant is appealing. However:
  - The gradient-free convergence guarantee (Theorem 1) requires convexity of f on the feasible ball Φ and x* ∈ int Φ. In the application, f is non-convex, and Section 4.2 assumes local convexity on Φ without verification. The empirical setup fixes R=0.99 without evidence that f is convex on that ball, so the stated guarantee may not apply in practice.
  - Several constants (e.g., β1 in Lemma 2) are deferred to the supplement; practical computability and tightness of the resulting bounds are not clear.
  - Complexity claims compare favorably to power-method-based approaches, but no wall-clock or iteration complexity comparisons are reported in experiments.
- Experiments: The dataset is modest. The main reported metric is the training objective (and some test loss); standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) are absent, so claims about ranking quality improvement are not substantiated. The stopping rule for the baseline uses the test set, which is improper and can bias comparisons. Hyperparameter selection appears ad hoc (e.g., L=1e−4), and there is no ablation on the lower-level accuracy vs. overall performance (beyond a brief mention).

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but dense. Notation is heavy in Sections 3–5; several typographical and grammatical issues persist. Key algorithmic choices (how β1 is computed; how L is selected; projection operator details) should be made explicit in the main text. The experimental protocol (splits, stopping criteria, metrics) needs clearer justification and separation of train/validation/test.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of (i) inexact-oracle adaptive projected gradient for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems and (ii) gradient-free optimization with bounded oracle error is a useful extension of known frameworks. Applying these to Supervised PageRank with lower-level guarantees and deriving end-to-end complexity bounds appears novel relative to [21] and related methods.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners learning parameters of random-walk-based ranking models, and theoretically interesting in handling inexact lower-level computations. However, the limited empirical evaluation, lack of standard ranking metrics, and reliance on unverified local convexity limit practical impact.

Constructive suggestions
- Provide validation-based early stopping and hyperparameter tuning; do not use test data for stopping.
- Report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) on test queries to substantiate “ranking quality” improvements.
- Include wall-clock runtime and memory usage, and show how lower-level accuracy (N, N1, N2) trades off time vs. final loss/metrics. Compare directly to the power method for both π and gradient computations.
- Justify or estimate the Lipschitz constant L used in practice; discuss sensitivity. Similarly, explain how β1 is computed and its magnitude in your data.
- Either empirically verify convexity of f on the chosen Φ (e.g., via Hessian sampling or convexity diagnostics) or temper the convergence claims for the gradient-free method.
- Compare to additional baselines: supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec), and standard learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) computed on the same features, to contextualize the gains.
- Provide more thorough description or pseudocode for the derivative approximation (Equations 3.2–3.3) and its numerical stability.

Pros
- Solid theoretical development for inexact-oracle optimization in constrained settings; adaptive gradient scheme.
- End-to-end complexity bounds that incorporate lower-level approximation error.
- Lower-level method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) is well motivated and can be faster than power iteration.
- Empirical results suggest improvements over the prior two-level baseline on the provided dataset.

Cons
- Gradient-free method’s theoretical guarantee hinges on unverified convexity; application to a nonconvex objective is not fully justified.
- Experimental methodology has issues (test-set stopping), lacks standard ranking metrics and runtime comparisons, and uses a small dataset.
- Some constants and assumptions are opaque, making reproducibility and practical relevance of the bounds uncertain.
- Limited comparison to broader baselines in learning-to-rank and supervised random walks.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are interesting and relevant to learning with inexact lower-level computations, but the empirical evaluation and some assumptions (convexity for the zero-order method) weaken the case. Strengthening the experimental rigor and clarifying key constants and assumptions would significantly improve the paper.
