

This paper proposes two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank models, extending the classic PageRank algorithm to incorporate node and edge features. The main contribution is providing theoretically proven convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which is lacking in the state-of-the-art approach [21]. The authors prove convergence rates for their gradient-based method (Theorem 3) and gradient-free method (Theorem 1), and demonstrate that their methods outperform [21] on a real web ranking task.

The paper's strengths are its strong theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence proofs and practical validation through experiments on a large-scale web browsing graph from Yandex. The authors correctly identify the key challenge of the inexact oracle problem in Supervised PageRank optimization and provide a well-structured solution. The experimental setup is sound, with proper comparison to the state-of-the-art and statistical significance testing (p-values < 0.005). The paper correctly situates itself within the literature, referencing relevant work on PageRank variants [18, 11, 21] and optimization methods [16, 7].

However, the paper has minor weaknesses. It does not sufficiently discuss the sensitivity of their methods to hyperparameter choices (ε, L, R), which is crucial for practical application. While they mention that choosing L affects the gradient-free algorithm (Figure 2 in supplementary), they don't provide guidance on optimal parameter selection. Additionally, the paper lists avoiding derivative calculations for large matrices as a main advantage of the gradient-free method but doesn't adequately explain why this is possible.

The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations and logical flow from problem definition to theoretical results to experiments. It makes a significant contribution by providing the first theoretical guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning with convergence rates, addressing a gap in the literature. The practical relevance to search engine ranking is well-justified, with the authors correctly noting that their algorithms could improve search engine reliability and speed.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field with solid theoretical results and practical validation. The minor weaknesses regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and explanation of the gradient-free advantage do not detract from the main contribution. The work is novel, well-supported, and demonstrates clear significance for both theory and practice in graph-based ranking problems.
