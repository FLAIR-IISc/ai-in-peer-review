## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a ranking framework that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to solve the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem, focusing on providing theoretical convergence guarantees – a key contribution given the lack of such guarantees for existing approaches like [21]. The core idea revolves around utilizing an inexact oracle for estimating the stationary distribution, enabling convergence analysis for both proposed algorithms.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established PageRank literature ([18, 11]) and previous supervised extensions ([3, 4, 6, 21]). The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing methods, particularly the lack of convergence proofs for the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach [21]. The proposed algorithms are presented with sufficient detail, and the convergence analysis appears rigorous (though a detailed verification requires expertise in optimization theory). The experimental evaluation on a real-world web ranking dataset demonstrates improved performance compared to the baseline method [21], further supporting the efficacy of the proposed methods.  The inclusion of a discussion on the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity is a valuable addition.

A strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. Providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in this context is a significant advancement.  The use of the inexact oracle concept is well-justified and allows for a practical approach to solving the problem.  However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other ranking algorithms beyond Supervised PageRank. While demonstrating superiority over [21] is positive, positioning the results within the broader ranking literature would be beneficial. Furthermore, the experimental section, while showing improvement, lacks detailed statistical significance testing beyond the p-values mentioned in passing. More comprehensive ablation studies exploring the impact of key hyperparameters would also strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms, addressing a key limitation of previous work.
*   Demonstrates improved performance on a real-world dataset compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Clear presentation of the proposed methods and a rigorous convergence analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison with other ranking algorithms beyond Supervised PageRank.
*   Experimental evaluation could benefit from more detailed statistical analysis and ablation studies.

**Overall:**

This is a solid contribution with a strong theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. While some aspects of the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the novelty and significance of the theoretical contributions warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
