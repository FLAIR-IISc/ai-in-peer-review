This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into ranking. The authors identify a critical limitation in the prior state-of-the-art method [21], namely its lack of theoretical convergence guarantees due to its use of inexact power method estimations. The central contribution of this work is the development of two novel optimization algorithms, one gradient-based and one random gradient-free, which are both framed within the concept of an inexact oracle. Crucially, and unlike previous work, the authors provide a full theoretical analysis of their methods, proving convergence rate guarantees.

**Strengths:**

The paper’s main strength lies in its significant theoretical contribution. By providing the first provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank, it addresses a clear and important gap in the literature. The originality comes from the successful application and adaptation of general frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles [7, 16] to this specific, constrained, non-convex problem. The quality of the work is high; the theoretical analysis is rigorous, complete with complexity bounds for both proposed algorithms.

These theoretical claims are well-supported by a strong experimental section. The authors validate their methods on a large-scale, real-world web browsing dataset from a commercial search engine, which adds significant practical relevance. The experiments clearly demonstrate that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free algorithm, outperform the existing baseline [21] in terms of the final loss function value.

**Weaknesses:**

While the paper is technically sound, its clarity could be improved in a few areas. The connection between the general theoretical frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) and their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem could be made more explicit. For instance, a more detailed discussion on the practical justification for the local convexity assumption required for the gradient-free method's convergence theorem would strengthen the paper. A reader might wonder how one selects a region Φ where this assumption holds in practice. Furthermore, the experimental comparison is focused solely on the direct predecessor [21]. While this is the most relevant baseline, including other feature-based ranking methods could have provided broader context for the performance gains.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions. The combination of novel, provably convergent algorithms for an important problem, rigorous theoretical analysis, and strong empirical validation makes it a compelling submission. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. The paper advances the state of the art in learning-to-rank and provides valuable, reliable tools for practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
