This paper addresses the non-convex problem of learning the parameters of a Supervised PageRank model. This model extends the classic PageRank by allowing the restart and transition probabilities to be parameterized linear functions of node and edge features. The main contribution is the proposal of two new two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method and a random gradient-free method. Both algorithms operate in an "inexact oracle" setting, where the loss function and its gradient can only be approximated. Crucially, and in contrast to the prior state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], the authors provide a full theoretical analysis of their methods, including convergence rate guarantees. The theoretical contribution extends existing optimization frameworks: the gradient-free method is adapted for constrained problems with bounded error, and the gradient-based method is generalized for constrained non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle. The paper also provides a complexity analysis that considers the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (approximating the stationary distribution) and the overall computational cost. The proposed methods are evaluated on a real-world web page ranking task and are shown to outperform the existing method from [21].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. Providing convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank is a novel and important contribution that addresses a key limitation of previous work [21]. The analysis for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods in a non-convex, constrained setting with an inexact oracle is sound and valuable not only for this specific problem but also for the broader optimization community.

2.  **Novelty in Optimization Methods:** The paper makes solid contributions to optimization theory. It successfully extends the random gradient-free framework of [16] to handle constrained problems and bounded oracle error. Similarly, it generalizes the adaptive gradient method of [7] to a non-convex setting with an inexact first-order oracle. These are non-trivial extensions that enhance the applicability of these powerful optimization paradigms.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Strong Empirical Results:** The problem of learning to rank is of high practical importance. The proposed methods provide a principled and more reliable way to train feature-rich graph-based ranking models. The experiments, conducted on a large-scale, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms. The gradient-free method (GFN), in particular, shows a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art baseline in terms of the final loss value.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem is clearly motivated and formulated. The algorithms are described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated formally. The distinction between the upper-level optimization and the lower-level oracle is well-explained.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Convexity Assumption for the Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free algorithm (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the optimization domain `Φ`. This seems to contradict the initial statement that the problem is non-convex. The authors justify this by assuming that `Φ` is a small neighborhood around a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in general. It would strengthen the paper to either discuss the conditions under which this assumption is valid or, preferably, provide a convergence analysis for the non-convex case (e.g., to a stationary point), which would be more consistent with the analysis provided for the gradient-based method.

2.  **Scope of Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation could be strengthened in a couple of ways.
    *   First, while the loss function is the direct objective of the optimization, demonstrating the impact on standard information retrieval metrics like NDCG or MAP on the test set would be more compelling. An improvement in the loss function does not always translate directly to a tangible improvement in ranking quality as perceived by users, and showing this link would make the practical significance of the work clearer.
    *   Second, the comparison is made against a single, albeit important, baseline [21]. Including other relevant baselines could provide a more comprehensive picture of the methods' performance.

3.  **Clarity on Hyperparameter Choices:** The experiments are run with fixed hyperparameters (e.g., `L = 10^-4`). While the supplementary material is referenced for a discussion on the choice of `L`, a brief sensitivity analysis in the main paper would be beneficial. It would help readers understand how robust the algorithms are to the choice of these crucial parameters and provide better guidance for practitioners looking to apply these methods.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions to both machine learning on graphs and numerical optimization. The development of theoretically-grounded algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank is an important step forward. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to clarifying assumptions and extending the experimental validation. The paper's strengths, particularly its solid theoretical foundation and promising empirical results, are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
