- Paper proposes two two-level training algorithms for Supervised PageRank: an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle and a random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle.  
- Lower-level routine replaces the power method with a linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski approach to approximate stationary distributions and derivatives with controlled accuracy.  
- Theoretical contributions include formalizing an inexact oracle, proving convergence rates, and deriving end-to-end complexity bounds that couple upper- and lower-level accuracies.  
- Experiments on Yandex browsing graphs show improvements over a prior gradient method used in Supervised PageRank [21].  
- Theoretical development is careful and instantiated to the problem via explicit lemmas controlling approximation errors.  
- Some assumptions are strong (local convexity for zero-order) and key constants are deferred to the supplement, limiting practical clarity.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: main metric is training loss, with missing standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP), wall-clock timings, and sparse baselines beyond the GBP variant.  
- Notation is dense and many proofs/algorithmic details are relegated to the supplement; clearer discussion of convexity conditions and trade-offs would help.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of inexact-oracle gradient and gradient-free methods to constrained settings with biased, bounded-error evaluations for PageRank learning.  
- Significance: providing convergence guarantees for supervised PageRank is valuable to optimization and graph-ranking communities but broader impact needs stronger empirical validation.  
- Pros: solid and nontrivial theory for inexact oracles; practical lower-level improvement over power method; reported empirical gains over prior training.  
- Cons: limited experimental scope and metrics, strong/local convexity assumptions for zero-order method, and reproducibility concerns due to no code and many supplemental details.  
- Suggestions: report NDCG/MAP on held-out queries, include wall-clock and iteration budgets, compare to supervised random walks and other learning-to-rank baselines, ablate α and lower-level accuracy, clarify constants and convexity regions, and release code.
