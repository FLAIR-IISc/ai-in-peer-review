- Proposes two optimization methods (gradient-based GBN and gradient-free GFN) for Supervised PageRank using an inexact-oracle framework.  
- Main contributions: theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods and empirical improvement in ranking performance.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with clear lemmas and theorems, addressing the lack of convergence guarantees in prior work.  
- Empirical results show statistically significant improvements over the state-of-the-art (p < 0.005); GBN often needs fewer iterations and GFN avoids derivatives for large matrices.  
- Novelty claim (combining random gradient-free/gradient methods with inexact-oracle large-scale optimization) is insufficiently justified and the connection to prior work [16] is superficial.  
- Experiments lack key computational-efficiency metrics (time per iteration, total training time, memory usage) necessary for huge-scale claims.  
- Scalability is underexplored — experiments are limited to query subsets and do not demonstrate behavior on larger graphs.  
- Theoretical guarantees rely on a restrictive convexity assumption (Theorem 4) that’s not justified for the inherently non-convex loss.  
- Recommended improvements: add time/memory comparisons, better justify novelty vs. [16], discuss practical limits of convexity assumptions, and include larger-scale experiments.  
- Overall: solid theoretical contribution with convincing empirical gains; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address novelty and experimental detail.
