- Studies learning Supervised PageRank parameters where transition/restart probs are feature-parametrized and the loss aggregates pairwise preference violations over query graphs.
- Main challenge: loss and gradient require stationary distributions and their derivatives, computable only approximately.
- Proposes two two-level schemes: a gradient-free randomized directional differences method with inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based projected method with an inexact first-order oracle and adaptive step-size.
- Uses a lower-level linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method (and generalization) to approximate stationary distributions and derivatives with controllable accuracy via inner iterations.
- Connects to prior work on supervised random walks, efficient PageRank computation, and optimization with inexact oracles.
- Novelty: extends gradient-free optimization to constrained problems under uniform inexactness and introduces an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with unknown Lipschitz constant.
- Provides end-to-end complexity bounds that explicitly trade off lower-level accuracy with upper-level progress.
- Theoretical results: explicit inner-iteration schedules (Lemmas 1–2) and accuracy–complexity trade-offs for both algorithms (Theorems 1–4); analysis appears technically sound under assumptions.
- Limitation: gradient-free end-to-end guarantee requires local convexity of f on a feasible ball, a strong and unjustified assumption for the nonconvex objective.
- Limitation: gradient-based analysis guarantees stationarity rather than convergence to a local minimum; constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to supplement without practical discussion.
- Experiments use appropriate Yandex browsing graphs but evaluate only the surrogate training loss, not standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP).
- Experiments lack wall-clock time and memory comparisons despite claims of computational efficiency.
- Hyperparameter choices (τ, δ schedules) are set without systematic justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Baseline comparison is limited and uses power-100 for matrix powers; fairness and comparable oracle accuracies are unclear.
- Clarity: paper is generally well organized but notation is heavy; derivative computation intuition and constant roles should be clarified, and minor inconsistencies fixed.
- Pros: clear problem formulation, solid theoretical contributions for both settings, end-to-end complexity bounds, and empirical training-loss improvements over prior method.
- Cons: reliance on local convexity for guarantees, missing standard ranking/runtime metrics, limited baselines, and under-specified practical details and fairness across methods.
- Suggestions: report test ranking metrics and runtime/memory, empirically probe local convexity, compare additional baselines, clarify GBP stopping rule and inner-accuracy parity, and provide sensitivity guidance for τ, δ, and L.
- Overall: theoretically careful and practically relevant contribution to learning on graphs under inexact oracles that would be stronger with expanded empirical validation and clearer assumptions.
