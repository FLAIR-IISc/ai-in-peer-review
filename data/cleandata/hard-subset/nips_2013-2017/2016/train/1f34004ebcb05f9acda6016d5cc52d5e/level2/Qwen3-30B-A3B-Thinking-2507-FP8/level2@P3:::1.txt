

The paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. Previous work [21] proposed a two-level gradient-based method for this problem but lacked convergence guarantees. This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method, both of which provide theoretically guaranteed convergence rates. The authors analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and the overall computational complexity, providing complexity bounds for both methods. The paper demonstrates empirical superiority of both proposed methods over the state-of-the-art on a web page ranking task.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, it develops a general framework for gradient methods with inexact first-order oracles for non-convex optimization, extending previous work [7, 25]. The convergence rate analysis for this framework is thorough, with clear theorems establishing bounds on the number of arithmetic operations required to achieve a given accuracy. Second, it adapts random gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact zero-order oracles, providing theoretical guarantees that were previously missing for this problem. The authors carefully analyze the interplay between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation (using the linearly convergent method [17]) and the overall computational complexity, which is an important practical consideration.

The experimental evaluation is well-designed and demonstrates the practical advantages of the proposed methods. The authors compare their gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) methods against the state-of-the-art gradient-based method (GBP) on a real-world web page ranking task using data from Yandex. The results consistently show that GFN outperforms GBP on all test sets, while GBN significantly outperforms GBP on certain subsets. The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup, including the dataset characteristics (600 queries, ~11.7K vertices, ~7.5K edges) and the features used (26 features for nodes, 52 for edges). The ablation studies on hyperparameters (L, ε, step sizes) provide useful insights into the stability and sensitivity of the algorithms.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient mathematical detail to allow reproduction of the results. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon existing literature in optimization (e.g., [16, 25, 7]) while addressing specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank learning problem.

Some minor weaknesses are worth noting. The choice of the feasible set Φ = {ϕ ∈ Rm : ‖ϕ− em‖2 ≤ 0.99} appears somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from more justification, particularly given the importance of this choice for the convergence guarantees. The paper could also provide more detailed discussion about how the proposed methods compare to other ranking models beyond the state-of-the-art [21], which would strengthen the significance argument. While the paper demonstrates that both proposed methods outperform GBP, it does not explicitly discuss how the computational complexity of the proposed methods compares to GBP in practice, beyond the theoretical complexity bounds. The gradient-free method is described as avoiding "calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix," but the paper could better explain how this translates to practical computational advantages.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of optimization for ranking problems. The theoretical guarantees provided for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods address a significant limitation of previous work. The empirical results support the theoretical claims, demonstrating improved performance on a real-world ranking task. The paper is technically sound, with careful attention to the trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity. The presentation is clear and the mathematical arguments are well-developed.

The paper meets NIPS criteria as follows:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by empirical validation. The authors carefully consider both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Originality: The convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods represent a significant novelty over existing work, which lacked such guarantees. The adaptation of random gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact oracles is also novel.
- Significance: The results advance the state of the art in learning Supervised PageRank, which has practical implications for search engine ranking. The methods could be applied to other graph-based learning problems.

The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed discussion of the choice of Φ and how the proposed methods compare to other ranking approaches beyond the state-of-the-art. However, these are minor issues that do not detract from the overall contribution. The paper provides valuable theoretical and practical advances to the problem of learning graph-based ranking models.
