## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a ranking framework extending traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The core contribution lies in proposing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to solve the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem, and crucially, providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both – a feature lacking in prior work [21]. The authors also investigate the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity, and demonstrate improved ranking performance on a real-world web ranking task.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established PageRank literature [18, 11] and the advancements in supervised ranking [3, 4, 6, 21]. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods, specifically the lack of convergence proofs for gradient-based approaches [21]. The proposed methods, based on inexact oracle concepts [17], represent a significant step towards a more theoretically grounded solution. The inclusion of both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches is a strength, offering flexibility depending on the problem characteristics. The experimental validation on a real-world dataset demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms, outperforming the state-of-the-art [21].

However, the paper suffers from a degree of density and could benefit from improved clarity. The mathematical notation is heavy, and the connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation isn’t always immediately apparent. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more detailed discussion of the scalability of these methods to extremely large graphs would be valuable. The experimental section, while demonstrating improvement, lacks a thorough ablation study to isolate the impact of specific design choices.  Furthermore, the comparison to the state-of-the-art is limited to a single dataset and task; exploring other ranking scenarios would strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Provides convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, a significant advancement over existing work.
*   Theoretical Soundness: Rigorous mathematical analysis supports the proposed algorithms.
*   Empirical Validation: Demonstrates improved ranking performance on a real-world dataset.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity: The paper is dense and requires significant effort to follow the technical details.
*   Scalability: Limited discussion of scalability to very large graphs.
*   Experimental Scope: The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking. Despite the challenges in clarity and scope, the theoretical novelty and empirical results justify acceptance, with a strong recommendation for the authors to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
