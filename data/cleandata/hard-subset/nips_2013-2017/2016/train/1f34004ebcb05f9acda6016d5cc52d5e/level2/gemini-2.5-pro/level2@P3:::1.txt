This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, which involves minimizing a non-convex loss function. The key challenge in this setting is that the objective function and its gradient cannot be calculated exactly, a scenario the paper terms an "inexact oracle." The work contrasts itself with the prior state-of-the-art method [21], which employs a gradient-based approach using the power method but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to the uncontrolled error in its oracle.

The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of two novel two-level optimization methods—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. On the lower level, both methods utilize an efficient, linearly convergent algorithm [17] to approximate the PageRank stationary distribution, allowing the oracle's error to be controlled. On the upper level, the paper develops general optimization frameworks of broader interest:
1.  A random gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization, adapted from [16] to handle inexact function evaluations.
2.  An adaptive gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle, which generalizes prior work [7] and does not require knowledge of the Lipschitz constant.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis of these methods, establishing a clear trade-off between the computational effort at the lower level (oracle accuracy) and the overall complexity. The proposed algorithms are validated experimentally on a web page ranking task, where they are shown to outperform the baseline method [21] in terms of the final loss function value.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness and Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution. It provides the first provably convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. The development of general frameworks for constrained optimization with inexact zero-order and first-order oracles is a significant contribution in its own right and could be valuable for other large-scale problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are prohibitive. The convergence analysis is detailed and appears to be correct.

*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins by clearly motivating the problem and the shortcomings of existing work. It then introduces the general optimization frameworks before applying them to the specific Supervised PageRank problem, which makes the contributions easy to follow.

*   **Problem Significance:** Learning feature-based ranking models on graphs is an important problem with direct applications in web search and recommendation systems. By providing robust and theoretically-grounded optimization methods, this work addresses a practical need for more reliable training procedures.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, but its impact could be further enhanced with the following revisions.

*   **Evaluation Metrics:** The experiments exclusively report the value of the loss function (2.4). While this is appropriate for demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed optimization algorithms, it does not directly measure the quality of the final ranking. To better establish the practical significance of the work, it is essential to include standard information retrieval metrics such as Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) on the test set. This would connect the improved optimization performance to tangible improvements in the end task, strengthening the claim that these algorithms can enhance search engine performance.

*   **Convexity Assumption for the Gradient-Free Method:** Theorem 2, which provides the convergence rate for the gradient-free method, assumes that the objective function `f(phi)` is convex on the feasible set. However, the Supervised PageRank loss function is generally non-convex. The paper notes this by stating the assumption holds in a "small vicinity of a local minimum." It would be beneficial to expand on the practical implications of this assumption. For instance, does the algorithm still exhibit good empirical performance when this assumption is violated? A brief discussion on the robustness of the method to non-convexity in practice would be valuable.

*   **Experimental Details for Reproducibility:** The paper sets key hyperparameters like the Lipschitz constant estimate `L = 10^-4` and the accuracy `epsilon = 10^-6`. To improve reproducibility, it would be helpful to briefly describe how these values were chosen (e.g., via a validation set or prior knowledge). While the paper commendably tests sensitivity to the initial Lipschitz constant `L0`, adding details for the other parameters would be a useful addition.

*   **Comparison to Baseline:** The comparison to the state-of-the-art method [21] is direct and appropriate. The paper notes that the baseline method is stopped when the loss decreases by less than 10^-5, while the proposed methods use a target accuracy of `epsilon = 10^-6`. A brief clarification on whether these stopping criteria are comparable would be helpful to ensure a fair comparison of convergence speed.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with solid theoretical contributions that advance the state of the art in learning graph-based ranking models. The development of provably convergent optimization methods for this task is a notable achievement. The primary recommendation for revision is to augment the experimental evaluation with standard ranking metrics, which would substantially bolster the paper's claims of practical significance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first provably convergent methods for learning Supervised PageRank.
*   Introduces novel and general optimization frameworks for constrained problems with inexact oracles.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and complete.
*   Experimental results demonstrate superior performance over the existing state-of-the-art method on the chosen optimization objective.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG), making it difficult to assess the practical impact on the end task of web search.
*   The discussion around the local convexity assumption for the gradient-free method could be more detailed.
