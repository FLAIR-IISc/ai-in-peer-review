Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies learning the parameters of Supervised PageRank, where node and edge features parametrize restart and transition probabilities. The learning objective is a nonconvex loss aggregating squared hinge-like violations of pairwise preferences over query-dependent browsing graphs. The key challenge is that evaluating the loss and its gradient requires stationary distributions of Markov chains and their derivatives, which can only be computed approximately. The authors propose two two-level optimization schemes: a gradient-free method using randomized directional differences with an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based projected method with an inexact first-order oracle and adaptive step-size. Both are coupled with a lower-level linearly convergent method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate the stationary distribution and (via a generalization) its derivatives, with accuracy controlled by the number of inner iterations.

The work builds on and connects several strands: supervised random walks/learning on graphs (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011; Supervised PageRank [21]), efficient PageRank computation (e.g., power method vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski), and optimization with inexact oracles (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov for convex problems; Nesterov–Spokoiny and bandit gradient-free methods; Ghadimi–Lan for nonconvex). The novelty lies in adapting gradient-free optimization to constrained problems under uniform inexactness, and in developing an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, together with end-to-end complexity bounds that explicitly trade off lower-level accuracy with upper-level progress. Empirically, on Yandex browsing graphs, both proposed methods outperform the previous two-level gradient method used in [21] in terms of the training loss, with the gradient-free approach showing the strongest improvements on the reported splits.

Quality:
- Theory: The lower-level choice and its error controls are well motivated. Lemma 1 and 2 provide explicit inner-iteration schedules to meet prescribed accuracies for function and gradient approximations. The gradient-free Algorithm 1 analysis (Theorem 1) accommodates a constrained domain and biased finite-difference estimator under bounded inexactness, leading to clear accuracy–complexity trade-offs (Theorem 2 for the full two-level scheme). The gradient-based Algorithm 2 analysis (Theorem 3) provides a stationarity guarantee with adaptive L estimates; Theorem 4 propagates oracle inaccuracies to a total arithmetic complexity bound. Overall, the claims appear technically sound given the stated assumptions.
- Assumptions/limitations: The gradient-free end-to-end guarantee (Theorem 2) requires that f be convex on the feasible ball Φ around a local minimizer. This is a strong condition for the nonconvex supervised ranking objective; its practical validity is not justified empirically or theoretically. The gradient-based analysis guarantees a stationarity measure rather than descent to a local minimum, which is standard but should be emphasized. The constants (e.g., β1) and their magnitudes are deferred to the supplement; a discussion of their practical size would help interpret the bounds.
- Experiments: The Yandex dataset and browsing-graph setting are appropriate and relevant. However, the evaluation focuses on the surrogate training loss f; standard ranking metrics on held-out labels (e.g., NDCG, MAP) are not reported. Wall-clock time and memory comparisons are also missing, despite the emphasis on computational efficiency and the advantage claimed for gradient-free methods when derivatives of large matrices are expensive. Hyperparameter choices (e.g., τ for smoothing, δ schedules) are set but not systematically justified. The comparison to the baseline GBP uses power 100 for matrix powers; clarity on how this maps to comparable oracle accuracies would strengthen fairness.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with clear separation between lower- and upper-level methods and explicit statements of algorithms and theorems (many details in the supplement). Notation is heavy; a brief intuition on the derivative computation scheme and the role of constants would help readability. A few minor inconsistencies (e.g., the GBP stopping rule description) should be clarified.

Originality:
- Extends gradient-free methods to constrained domains with uniformly bounded inexactness in function values, with a sphere-based randomization and explicit bounds.
- Proposes an adaptive projected gradient method for potentially nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, and integrates it into a practical two-level learner.
- Generalizes the Nesterov–Nemirovski PageRank computation to derivative estimation with accuracy control.

Significance:
Providing convergence-rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank under realistic inexact oracles is valuable for theory and practice. The two-level complexity analysis that couples inner accuracy with outer convergence is useful beyond the specific application. If supported by stronger empirical comparisons (including ranking metrics and runtime), the methods could impact learning on large graphs where exact gradients are prohibitive.

Pros:
- Clear formulation of a practically important learning problem with inexact oracles.
- Solid theoretical contributions for both gradient-free and gradient-based settings, including constrained domains and nonconvexity.
- End-to-end complexity bounds with explicit dependence on graph and feature sizes.
- Empirical improvements over a prior method on a real dataset.

Cons:
- Reliance on local convexity for the gradient-free end-to-end guarantee; not validated.
- Experiments report only surrogate loss; no standard ranking metrics or runtime/memory benchmarks.
- Limited baselines; comparisons to other graph-based learning-to-rank or supervised random-walk methods would strengthen the case.
- Some practical details (e.g., τ, δ selection, constants) and fairness of oracle accuracy across methods are under-specified.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report test-time ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k) alongside loss, and include wall-clock time and memory. Provide learning curves for both metrics.
- Justify or probe the local convexity assumption empirically (e.g., local Hessian estimates, convexity diagnostics) or discuss robustness when it fails.
- Compare with additional baselines (e.g., supervised random walks, standard learning-to-rank models on the same features).
- Clarify the GBP stopping rule and ensure comparable inner accuracies across methods. Include an ablation on N, N1, N2 and their impact on speed/accuracy.
- Provide guidance on setting τ, δ, and L in practice; include sensitivity analyses.
- Improve clarity around constants like β1 and include a brief intuition for the derivative computation scheme in the main text.

Overall assessment:
A theoretically careful and practically relevant contribution to learning on graphs under inexact oracles. With strengthened empirical evaluation and clarification of assumptions, this would be a strong submission.
