

This paper proposes two optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank models, addressing a key limitation in prior work: the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees for the state-of-the-art approach [21]. The authors develop algorithms that operate within an "inexact oracle" framework, where lower-level approximations of the stationary distribution and its derivatives are used as inputs to upper-level optimization. The paper makes two main contributions: 1) theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both optimization methods, and 2) empirical evidence of improved ranking performance.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clear lemmas and theorems establishing convergence rates under reasonable assumptions. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior methods, noting that existing approaches [21] lack convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of the oracle. The paper also demonstrates practical significance through experiments on a real web ranking task, showing that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] with statistical significance (p < 0.005). The gradient-based method (GBN) shows particular promise in requiring fewer iterations than the state-of-the-art on some datasets, while the gradient-free method (GFN) avoids derivative calculations for large matrices, which is a notable advantage.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the claim that this is "the first time when the ideas of random gradient-free and gradient optimization methods are combined with some efficient method for huge-scale optimization using the concept of an inexact oracle" requires stronger justification. The paper cites [16] for gradient-free methods, but doesn't adequately compare their framework to [16] in the context of constrained optimization with inexact oracles. The connection to [16] appears superficial, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach fundamentally differs from or improves upon [16] for the specific problem of Supervised PageRank.

Second, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail. The authors report that GFN "significantly outperforms" the state-of-the-art, but don't provide concrete computational efficiency metrics (e.g., time per iteration, total training time, or memory usage). For a method claiming to be suitable for "huge-scale optimization," these metrics are essential to evaluate practical utility. The paper also doesn't discuss how the methods scale with graph size beyond the limited experiments on subsets of queries.

Third, the paper's theoretical guarantees come with restrictive assumptions that aren't adequately justified. The convergence analysis for the gradient-based method (Theorem 4) assumes that the problem is convex on the feasible set Φ, which is a strong assumption for a non-convex loss function. The authors acknowledge the non-convex nature of the problem but don't provide evidence that the local convexity assumption holds in practice for the ranking task.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) More detailed experimental results comparing computational efficiency (time, memory) across methods
2) A clearer justification of the novelty claims against [16] and similar works
3) Discussion of the practical limitations of the convexity assumption
4) Additional experiments demonstrating scalability to larger graphs

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of optimization for Supervised PageRank, and the empirical results are convincing. However, the theoretical claims need stronger contextualization against prior work, and the practical implications would be better demonstrated with more comprehensive experimental results. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, and the significance of providing convergence guarantees for this important ranking problem is clear. 

Overall, this paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with practical relevance, but the novelty claims require refinement and the experimental evaluation would benefit from greater depth. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about novelty and experimental detail.
