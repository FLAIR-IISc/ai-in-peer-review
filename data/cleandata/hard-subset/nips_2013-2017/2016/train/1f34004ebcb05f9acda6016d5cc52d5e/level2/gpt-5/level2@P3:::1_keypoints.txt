- Problem and approach: learns Supervised PageRank with linear feature-dependent restart/transition probabilities using a two-level scheme (inner stationary distribution + derivative approximation, outer inexact optimization) for a nonconvex constrained objective.

- Lower-level contribution: replaces the power method with a Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent PageRank solver and introduces a recursion to approximate derivatives.

- Upper-level contributions: (1) a constrained random gradient-free random-smoothing method with a one-sided sphere estimator and bounded function-value noise; (2) an adaptive projected inexact first-order method for nonconvex composite problems with unknown Lipschitz constants.

- Theoretical results: convergence and complexity guarantees under inexact-oracle models, with explicit accuracy–complexity trade-offs and end-to-end bounds that depend on graph parameters.

- Empirical findings: on a Yandex browsing graph (~600 queries) both proposed methods reduce training loss and outperform a baseline that used the power method; the lower-level solver is empirically faster than the power method.

- Strength: addresses a relevant learning-to-rank setting where gradients are inherently inexact and provides rigorous inexact-oracle optimization tools.

- Strength: concrete lower-level advances and explicit coupling of inner/outer accuracy and complexity.

- Strength: provides end-to-end complexity bounds and empirical improvements over a known baseline.

- Key theoretical weakness: the gradient-free method’s complexity assumes convexity on the feasible ball, an unverified condition making Theorem 2 conditional.

- Experimental weakness: standard ranking metrics on held-out test queries (NDCG@k, MAP, ERR) are not reported, so ranking-quality claims are not well supported.

- Experimental weakness: no wall-clock runtime reporting or detailed inner-iteration (N, N1, N2) breakdowns or sensitivity analyses for ε and δ schedules.

- Practicality weakness: practical constants (e.g., β1) and Lipschitz estimates seem conservative and are not quantified or shown to be informative for step sizes/iteration counts.

- Scalability concern: loss uses all pairwise constraints (r can be large) with no discussion of pair subsampling or efficient pairwise summation strategies.

- Baseline coverage weakness: lacks comparisons to Supervised Random Walks and strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART) adapted to the same features.

- Clarity issues: multiple typos, notation slips, incomplete references, and insufficient implementation detail (e.g., cost of dpi/dϕ and derivative accumulation).

- Quality assessment: theoretically thoughtful and relevant; lower-level methods appear sound, but practical applicability is limited by the convexity assumption and possibly loose constants.

- Clarity assessment: paper is structural clear but readability and reproducibility suffer; needs parameter-schedule schematics and completed references.

- Originality: novel constrained/inexact extensions to gradient-free and inexact first-order methods and a useful integration with a linearly convergent PageRank solver.

- Significance: advances optimization for inexact-oracle problems and supervised PageRank but requires stronger empirical validation and clarified assumptions for broader impact.

- Essential suggestions: report standard ranking metrics and per-query tests, provide wall-clock runtimes and inner-iteration breakdowns, justify or reframe the convexity assumption, quantify constants, and detail derivative computation costs.

- Optional suggestions: compare to more baselines, explore pair subsampling, study α sensitivity, release code/pseudocode, and polish language/refs.

- Reasons to accept: solid optimization contributions with proofs, practical two-level scheme, and empirical improvement over a baseline.

- Reasons to reject: missing standard metrics and runtime evaluations, gradient-free guarantees hinge on unverified convexity, and presentation requires significant polish.

- Overall: technically promising and interesting but needs stronger empirical evidence and clearer/verified assumptions to be fully convincing.
