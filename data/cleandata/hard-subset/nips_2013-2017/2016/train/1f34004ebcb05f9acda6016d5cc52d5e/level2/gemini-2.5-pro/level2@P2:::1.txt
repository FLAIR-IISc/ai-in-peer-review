This paper tackles the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into the PageRank computation. The learning task is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization problem. The authors identify a key weakness in the state-of-the-art method [21], which uses a gradient-based approach with power iteration to estimate the stationary distribution and its derivatives. Due to the inexactness of these estimations, the existing method lacks theoretical convergence guarantees.

The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. This is achieved by framing the problem within the concept of an inexact oracle. For the lower-level task of approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, the authors employ a more efficient method from [17] instead of the standard power method. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis for both proposed methods, including complexity bounds that account for the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall performance. Finally, the authors present experimental results on a real-world web ranking task, showing that their methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art approach in terms of the final loss function value.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical contribution. It provides the first convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. By leveraging and extending optimization frameworks for inexact oracles [7, 16], the authors place the problem on solid theoretical ground. The analysis for the gradient-based method in the non-convex setting is particularly strong.

2.  **Originality:** The application of random gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods with inexact oracles to learn a ranking model like Supervised PageRank is novel. The specific adaptations of these general frameworks to handle the constraints of the problem and the particular structure of the inexactness are valuable contributions. This work successfully bridges the gap between general non-convex optimization theory and a specific, practical machine learning problem.

3.  **Significance:** The results are important for researchers and practitioners in information retrieval and graph-based learning. Supervised PageRank is an expressive model, and having reliable, theoretically-backed optimization algorithms is crucial for its practical application. The proposed methods demonstrate better performance empirically, suggesting they can lead to more effective and robust ranking models. Furthermore, the general two-level optimization approach presented could be adapted to other problems where a model's output (and its derivatives) can only be approximated iteratively.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors first present the general optimization frameworks and then specialize them to the Supervised PageRank problem. This separation makes the core ideas easy to understand. The problem setup and the proposed algorithms are described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within a neighborhood of a local minimum. As the authors acknowledge, the overall problem is non-convex. While this is a common assumption for analysis, it limits the applicability of the theoretical guarantee. It would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed discussion on when this assumption might hold in practice for this specific loss function, or to explicitly state this as a limitation of the current analysis compared to the more general non-convex analysis provided for the gradient-based method.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments show promising results, they could be more comprehensive.
    *   **Evaluation Metrics:** The comparison is based solely on the value of the loss function. Since the ultimate goal is ranking, it would be much more compelling to report performance using standard ranking metrics like Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP). An improvement in the loss function does not automatically guarantee an improvement in ranking quality.
    *   **Computational Cost:** The paper provides theoretical complexity bounds but lacks an empirical comparison of wall-clock time. The proposed methods, particularly the adaptive gradient-based one, involve line searches or multiple function evaluations per iteration, which might be computationally more expensive than the baseline. A comparison of training times would provide crucial practical insight into the trade-offs between the methods.
    *   **Dataset:** The experiments are performed on a proprietary dataset. While valuable, evaluating the methods on a public benchmark dataset (e.g., from the LETOR collection) would improve the reproducibility of the results and allow for a broader comparison with other ranking algorithms.
    *   **Baseline:** The stopping criterion for the baseline method (GBP) seems somewhat heuristic. Ensuring that all methods are run for a comparable amount of time or until a more standardized convergence criterion is met would make the comparison fairer.

3.  **Clarity of Details:** Some constants and parameters central to the theoretical bounds (e.g., β1 in Lemma 2) are defined only in the supplementary materials. While understandable due to space constraints, this makes it slightly harder to fully grasp the dependencies of the complexity bounds from the main paper alone. A brief intuitive explanation of what these constants depend on (e.g., graph properties, feature dimensions) in the main text would be helpful.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of learning graph-based ranking models. It addresses a key gap in prior work by providing optimization methods with proven convergence guarantees. The empirical results are positive and support the theoretical claims. The weaknesses identified, primarily related to the scope of the experimental evaluation and a strong theoretical assumption for one of the methods, are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is a solid contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
