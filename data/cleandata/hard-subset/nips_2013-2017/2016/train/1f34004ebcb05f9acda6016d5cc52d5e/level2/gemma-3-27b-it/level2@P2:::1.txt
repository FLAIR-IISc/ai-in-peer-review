This paper investigates the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with convergence rate guarantees, a notable improvement over previous work. The approach utilizes inexact oracle information and aims to address the non-convex nature of the problem, alongside constraints on the parameter vector and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. The authors demonstrate empirically that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach on a web page ranking task.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, the proposed methods, and the experimental results. The motivation for addressing the convergence issues of existing methods is well-articulated. The contributions are significant, specifically providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods – something lacking in the prior state-of-the-art. The detailed analysis of the trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity is also valuable. The experimental validation on a real-world dataset further strengthens the claims.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors cite related work, the connection to the broader optimization literature, beyond the specific PageRank context, feels somewhat limited. For example, the discussion of the inexact oracle and its impact on convergence could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing results on optimization with noisy gradients, particularly in the context of stochastic gradient descent (SGD). References such as Nesterov & Polyak (2006) or Ghadimi & Lan (2012) could provide valuable context.  The sheer number of lemmas and theorems, while rigorous, can be overwhelming and detract from the overall flow of the paper. A more streamlined presentation, potentially consolidating some of the theoretical results, would improve readability.

The explanation of some of the notation and assumptions is dense and requires careful reading. Specifically, the details surrounding the constraints on the parameter vector Φ and the choice of the prox-function in the gradient-based method could be more clearly explained. The explanation of the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameter tuning process, is lacking. The choice of L=10^-4 seems arbitrary and a justification for this value would be beneficial. The impact of different step sizes in GBP is mentioned, but the final selected step size is not clearly stated. Furthermore, the gains in ranking quality aren't quantified in a particularly insightful way; more detailed metrics beyond just the loss function value would be welcome (e.g., NDCG, MAP). 

The paper also lacks a discussion of limitations. While the authors mention that the problem is non-convex, they don’t extensively discuss the potential for getting stuck in local optima. Similarly, a discussion of the scalability of the proposed methods to even larger graphs would be helpful. The assumption that the feature vectors are positive (and the justification for this choice) deserves more scrutiny.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, a significant advancement over prior work.
*   Demonstrates improved performance on a real-world ranking task.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in graph-based learning.
*   Detailed analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited connection to the broader optimization literature.
*   Overly dense theoretical presentation with numerous lemmas and theorems.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup and results analysis.
*   Lack of discussion of limitations and potential future work.
*   The paper lacks clarity and a concise argument at times, making it hard to fully grasp.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Supervised PageRank and optimization methods for graph-based learning. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on improving the clarity of the presentation, strengthening the connection to related work, and providing more detailed experimental analysis. The authors should also address the limitations of their approach and discuss potential avenues for future research.
