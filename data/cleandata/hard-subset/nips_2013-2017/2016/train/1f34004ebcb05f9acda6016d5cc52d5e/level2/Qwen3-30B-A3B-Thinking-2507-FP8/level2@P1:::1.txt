

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features to improve ranking quality. The authors propose two optimization methods: a gradient-based approach with convergence guarantees for non-convex problems and a gradient-free method for constrained optimization. The key contribution is providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which the state-of-the-art [21] lacks.

The paper builds on previous work in supervised ranking [21], [2], [12], and optimization methods [16], [7], [17]. The authors develop a framework using an "inexact oracle" to handle the computational challenges of estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives in the Markov random walk model. Their approach involves two levels: a lower-level method for approximating the stationary distribution with controlled accuracy, and an upper-level optimization algorithm that uses these approximations.

The paper's strengths are significant. First, the theoretical contributions are strong: the authors provide convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a major advancement over previous work that lacked such guarantees. The proofs for Lemmas 1-2 and Theorems 1-4 are well-structured, with supplementary materials providing sufficient detail. Second, the experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality, with the gradient-free method showing particular promise for large-scale problems. Third, the paper clearly articulates the trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and computational complexity, which is valuable for practitioners.

I have a few constructive suggestions for improvement. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the inexact oracle framework is necessary for this problem. While the authors reference previous work [5], a more detailed comparison of why standard optimization methods (e.g., [16], [7]) cannot be directly applied would strengthen the motivation. Additionally, the experimental section could provide more insight into how the algorithms scale with graph size, particularly since the authors mention studying dependency on graph sizes in Section 6.1. The current presentation in Table 1 and Figure 1 is clear but somewhat limited in scope.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with appropriate citations to related work including recent NIPS papers. The authors make a clear contribution to the field of optimization for large-scale ranking problems. The theoretical guarantees provided are meaningful for the problem at hand, and the experimental results support the claims made.

One minor concern is that the gradient-based method (GBN) requires careful tuning of the Lipschitz constant L, which might be challenging for practitioners. While the authors note that results are robust to L in the range 10^-4 to 10^-1, a more detailed discussion of this sensitivity would be helpful.

The significance of this work is substantial. The problem of learning ranking models with node and edge features is important for search engines and recommendation systems. The proposed methods not only outperform existing approaches but also come with theoretical guarantees that provide confidence in their performance. This is particularly valuable for applications where reliability is critical.

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field. The theoretical advances in optimization with inexact oracles are significant, and the practical results on a real ranking task demonstrate the value of these contributions. The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and clearly written. The authors have addressed a meaningful problem with a novel approach that combines insights from optimization theory and ranking algorithms.

The main strengths are the theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods (which previous work lacked), the clear experimental validation on a real-world task, and the well-organized presentation of the mathematical framework. The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of the sensitivity of the gradient-based method to the Lipschitz constant L and the somewhat limited exploration of scalability with graph size.

Given the quality of the theoretical contributions, the clear experimental validation, and the significance of the problem addressed, I recommend acceptance. The paper advances both the theory of optimization with inexact oracles and the practice of learning ranking models.
