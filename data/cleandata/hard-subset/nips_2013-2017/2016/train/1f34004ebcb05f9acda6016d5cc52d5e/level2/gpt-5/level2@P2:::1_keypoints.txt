- Paper studies learning Supervised PageRank parameters with node/edge features using a nonconvex pairwise squared hinge loss over query graphs.

- Proposes two two-level algorithms: (i) inexact first-order adaptive projected gradient, (ii) gradient-free randomized zero-order via spherical smoothing with inexact oracle.

- Lower-level approximates stationary distributions with the Nesterov–Nemirovski weighted-iterate scheme and generalizes power-method derivatives to bound errors.

- Provides trade-off analysis between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity and proves convergence rates for both upper-level methods.

- Experiments on a Yandex browsing graph (~600 queries, ~11.7K vertices) report lower loss than a prior two-level gradient baseline [21].

- Builds on prior work in Supervised PageRank, supervised random walks, inexact-oracle optimization, and zeroth/first-order nonconvex methods.

- Replaces the classical power method with Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration and extends derivative computation for Markov chains; novelty lies in constrained inexact-oracle adaptations and explicit end-to-end complexity bounds.

- Oracle constructions and convergence analyses are generally sound; the adaptive inexact first-order scheme that avoids needing a Lipschitz constant is appealing.

- Gradient-free theoretical guarantee requires convexity of f on the feasible ball and x* in the interior, which is unverified for the nonconvex application and empirically assumed without justification.

- Several important constants (e.g., β1) are deferred to the supplement, making practical computability and tightness of bounds unclear.

- Complexity comparisons favorably claim improvements over power-method approaches, but experiments do not report wall-clock or iteration complexity to support this.

- Experimental evaluation is limited: small dataset, primary metric is training/test loss, and standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP) are absent.

- Baseline stopping rule improperly uses the test set, risking biased comparisons; hyperparameter selection appears ad hoc and lacks ablation studies on lower-level accuracy.

- Paper is readable but dense with heavy notation, some typos, and missing explicit descriptions of key algorithmic choices (β1 computation, L selection, projection details) and experiment protocol.

- Originality is moderate: useful extension of known frameworks to constrained inexact-oracle settings applied to Supervised PageRank with end-to-end bounds.

- Significance is potentially practical and theoretically interesting, but limited by weak empirical validation and unverified assumptions.

- Suggested fixes: use validation-based stopping, report standard ranking metrics, include runtime/memory and lower-level accuracy trade-offs, compare to power method and additional baselines (supervised random walks, RankSVM, LambdaMART).

- Additional suggestions: justify or estimate Lipschitz constant L, explain β1 computation, empirically check convexity on the chosen feasible ball or temper claims, and provide clearer pseudocode/stability discussion for derivative approximation.

- Pros: solid theoretical development for inexact-oracle constrained optimization, adaptive gradient scheme, end-to-end complexity bounds, and motivation for using Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level solver.

- Cons: gradient-free guarantees hinge on unverified convexity, experimental methodology flaws (test-set stopping, no IR metrics, small dataset), opaque constants/assumptions, and limited baseline comparisons.

- Recommendation: borderline reject — interesting theoretical contributions but weakened by empirical shortcomings and unverified assumptions; needs stronger experiments and clearer exposition.
