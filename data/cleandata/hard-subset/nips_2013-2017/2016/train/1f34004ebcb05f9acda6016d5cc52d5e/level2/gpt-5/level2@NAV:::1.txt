This paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where node/edge features modulate restart and transition probabilities. The authors cast training as minimizing a nonconvex pairwise loss over parameters ϕ under constraints, and propose two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-based method using an adaptive projected gradient scheme with an inexact first-order oracle; and (ii) a random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle. Both rely on a lower-level routine to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives with controlled accuracy, replacing the power method by the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski approach. They derive convergence-rate guarantees and total complexity bounds that couple upper- and lower-level accuracies, and report experiments on Yandex browsing graphs showing improvements over the prior gradient method used in Supervised PageRank [21]. The work builds on and extends inexact-oracle optimization (e.g., Devolder et al.) and random gradient-free methods (Nesterov–Spokoiny), and is related to supervised random walks and BrowseRank.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful: the inexact oracle is formalized; convergence is proved for the gradient-free method under convexity and for an adaptive projected gradient method under a generalized oracle model, and these are instantiated to the supervised PageRank problem via explicit lemmas controlling approximation errors. However, some assumptions (local convexity for zero-order, unknown constants) are strong and practical constants are deferred to the supplement. Empirically, the evaluation is limited: the main metric reported is the training loss; standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) and wall-clock comparisons are missing; baselines beyond the specific GBP variant are sparse.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized but notation is dense, and key details (proofs, constants, algorithm variants) are relegated to the supplement. A clearer discussion of when convexity holds and a more intuitive explanation of the trade-offs would help.

Originality: Adapting inexact-oracle gradient and gradient-free methods to constrained settings with biased, bounded-error evaluations and tying them to PageRank learning with rigorous end-to-end complexity is novel relative to [21] and prior inexact-oracle work.

Significance: Providing convergence guarantees for supervised PageRank learning is valuable and likely of interest to both optimization and graph-ranking communities. Broader impact would be strengthened by a more comprehensive empirical study.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid and nontrivial theory for inexact oracles; end-to-end complexity accounting.
- Practical lower-level improvement over power method.
- Empirical gains over prior supervised PageRank training.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited experimental scope and metrics; unclear practical speedups.
- Strong/local convexity assumptions for the zero-order method; discussion of limitations is brief.
- Reproducibility: no code and many details in supplement.

Suggestions: Report NDCG/MAP on held-out queries; include wall-clock and iteration budgets; compare to supervised random walks and other learning-to-rank baselines; ablate α and lower-level accuracy; clarify constants and convexity regions; release code.
