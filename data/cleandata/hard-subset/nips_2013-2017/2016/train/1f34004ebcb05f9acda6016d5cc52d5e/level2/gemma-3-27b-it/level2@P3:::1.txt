This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models with a focus on providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods. The work builds upon existing methods, particularly [21], and proposes improvements to address the lack of convergence proofs in prior work. The core contribution lies in the theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees provided for both proposed methods, alongside experimental validation on a web page ranking task.

The paper is well-structured and clearly outlines the problem, related work, proposed methods, and experimental setup. The introduction effectively motivates the need for the research and positions it within the context of existing PageRank algorithms and supervised learning techniques. The mathematical formulation of the problem is concise and well-defined. The detailed description of the algorithms and the associated convergence analysis are significant strengths. The inclusion of theoretical complexity bounds is particularly valuable. The experimental section provides a clear comparison with the state-of-the-art method and demonstrates the performance improvements achieved by the proposed methods.

A key strength is the rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a significant contribution as the prior work [21] lacked such guarantees. The detailed breakdown of the computational complexity of the algorithms is also a valuable contribution. The authors carefully consider the trade-offs between accuracy and complexity, providing insights into the practical implementation of the methods. The connection between the number of iterations on the lower level and the overall performance is well-articulated. The exploration of both gradient and gradient-free approaches offers a valuable comparison and caters to different computational constraints.

However, the paper could be improved in several areas. The presentation of the experimental results, while clear, could be more comprehensive. While the authors state the p-values are less than 0.005, providing the actual p-values would enhance reproducibility and statistical rigor. More details on the hyperparameter tuning process and the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings would be beneficial. The explanation of the choice of the specific norm (‖ ⋅ ‖2) is relatively brief and could be expanded. Further elaboration on why the set Φ is chosen as described would also be helpful. The discussion of related work, while present, could be broadened to include more recent advances in optimization techniques applicable to non-convex problems. The notation, while mostly clear, is occasionally dense and could benefit from more frequent explanations of variable meanings. For example, clarifying the meaning of ‘a.o.’ in the complexity analysis would improve readability.

Arguments for acceptance:
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the convergence of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, addressing a key limitation of previous work.
* **Novelty:** The combination of inexact oracle techniques with convergence analysis for Supervised PageRank learning is novel.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in a real-world ranking task.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the algorithms and analysis.

Arguments against acceptance:
* **Limited Experimental Detail:** The experimental section could be more comprehensive, with more detailed reporting of results and hyperparameter tuning.
* **Dense Notation:** The notation can be dense and occasionally difficult to follow, potentially hindering understanding.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The convergence guarantees and performance improvements demonstrated in the experiments make this work a valuable addition to the field. Addressing the minor weaknesses related to experimental detail and notation would further strengthen the paper. The paper is of high quality and is recommended for acceptance.
