Summary
The paper tackles learning in Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities depend linearly on node/edge features. The learning objective is a pairwise square-hinge loss over query-dependent graphs, leading to a nonconvex constrained problem in the parameter vector. The work adopts a two-level architecture: a lower-level routine approximates the stationary distribution and its derivatives with respect to parameters; an upper-level optimizer updates parameters using inexact oracle information from the lower level.

On the lower level, the paper replaces the commonly used power method by a linearly convergent scheme (Nesterov–Nemirovski, 1991) to approximate the stationary distribution and proposes a related recursion to approximate derivatives. On the upper level, two algorithms are developed with convergence guarantees under inexact oracles:
- A gradient-free, random-smoothing method for constrained convex objectives with uniformly bounded function-value noise, tailored to projection onto an L2-ball and using a one-sided estimator on the sphere. Convergence and complexity bounds are given, together with an explicit accuracy trade-off between oracle precision and outer iterations.
- An adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex composite optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (in the Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov spirit), extended to constraints and unknown Lipschitz constants; convergence to a stationary point is proven and complexity derived.

Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset (≈600 queries) compare the proposed gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods with a prior gradient-based approach (GBP) that uses the power method and lacks convergence guarantees. The proposed methods reduce the training loss and, according to the text, improve ranking quality; statistical significance is reported. The lower-level method is empirically faster than the power method.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant learning-to-rank setting with graph-based supervision where gradients are inherently inexact due to iterative estimation of PageRank-like quantities.
- Clear algorithmic contributions for optimization with inexact oracles:
  • A constrained, convex variant of random gradient-free optimization with bounded function-value noise, and a practical one-sided estimator.
  • An adaptive inexact first-order method for constrained, potentially nonconvex problems, with a rigorous stationarity guarantee.
- Concrete lower-level advances: use of a linearly convergent PageRank solver and a derivative-approximation scheme; explicit accuracy–complexity trade-offs for coupling inner and outer loops.
- End-to-end complexity bounds that expose dependence on graph parameters (|Q|, p, s, r, α, m) and oracle accuracies.
- Empirical evidence that both upper-level algorithms outperform a state-of-the-art baseline and that the lower-level method improves convergence.

Weaknesses
- The key assumption for the gradient-free method’s complexity (convexity of f on the feasible ball Φ) is not justified. The problem is nonconvex, and the text only assumes the existence of a locally convex neighborhood around an optimizer without verifying or diagnosing convexity in practice. As a result, Theorem 2 is conditional, and it is unclear when the stated guarantees apply.
- The experimental evaluation emphasizes the training loss; standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP, ERR) on the test set are not reported in the main text. Claims about “ranking quality” are therefore hard to assess.
- Runtime and iteration budgets are not reported in wall-clock time, nor is there a breakdown of lower-level iteration counts (N, N1, N2) or sensitivity to ε and δ schedules. Since the primary claim blends lower- and upper-level advances, measuring wall-clock performance and sample efficiency would be valuable.
- Practical constants (e.g., β1 in Lemma 2) and Lipschitz estimates appear potentially conservative; their computed values and impact on step sizes/iteration counts are not provided, which limits the practical interpretability of the bounds.
- The loss uses all pairwise constraints among labeled items; r can be large in practice. No discussion of subsampling or efficient pairwise summation is given, which may affect scalability.
- Baseline coverage is narrow: no comparison to Supervised Random Walks (Backstrom & Leskovec, WSDM’11) or strong learning-to-rank methods commonly used on query–document data (e.g., LambdaMART) adapted to the same features.
- Clarity/presentation issues: multiple typos, notational slips, and a reference list with incomplete entries and missing years make the manuscript harder to follow. Some algorithmic details (e.g., computing dpi/dϕ and the cost of derivative accumulation) could be elaborated.

Quality
The theoretical developments are thoughtful and relevant. The inexact-oracle formulations are well-motivated and, if the assumptions hold, the proofs lead to meaningful convergence guarantees. The lower-level stationary distribution approximation and its derivative recursion appear sound and beneficial. The main concerns are (i) reliance on unverified convexity in the gradient-free analysis and (ii) the practical tightness of constants. Experimental methodology demonstrates advantages in the objective but lacks standard ranking metrics and wall-clock comparisons.

Clarity
Overall structure is clear, but readability suffers from typos, occasional imprecise statements, and sparse implementation detail. The coupling between δ, τ, M, and inner-loop N, N1, N2 is conceptually described; a concise algorithmic schematic with parameter schedules would help. References need to be completed.

Originality
Extends random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with uniformly bounded inexact function values and proposes an adaptive inexact first-order method for constrained nonconvex optimization. The integration with a linearly convergent PageRank solver and derivative recursion within a two-level learning framework is a useful combination. Relative to Ghadimi–Lan and Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov, the constrained/inexact aspects are tailored to this application and appear novel.

Significance
The work advances optimization methodology for a common class of learning problems with inexact oracles and demonstrates its utility in supervised PageRank. With stronger experimental evaluation and clarified assumptions, the impact could be meaningful for graph-based ranking and for broader applications with similarly structured inexact oracles.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential
- Report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP/ERR) on held-out queries alongside the loss; include per-query statistical testing.
- Provide wall-clock runtime and iteration breakdowns (outer iterations, total inner iterations N, N1, N2), as well as sensitivity to ε and δ; plot accuracy vs time.
- Clarify and justify the convexity assumption used for the gradient-free method. If convexity cannot be established, reframe the result as conditional and/or provide a nonconvex variant with stationarity guarantees.
- Quantify β1 and other constants in the bounds for the given dataset; discuss whether the bounds are practically informative.
- Detail the computation and cost of dpi/dϕ and Πk recursions; compare GBN vs GFN when m and p are large to validate the stated advantage of the gradient-free method.

Optional
- Compare against Supervised Random Walks and a strong learning-to-rank baseline on the same features.
- Consider pair subsampling strategies to control r and report their effect on performance and complexity.
- Explore learning α or conducting a sensitivity study over α.
- Release code or provide detailed pseudocode and parameter schedules to improve reproducibility.
- Revise for language, notation consistency, and complete bibliographic references.

Reasons to accept
- Solid and relevant optimization contributions for inexact oracles with proofs and complexity bounds.
- Practical two-level methods that address a real learning problem; empirical improvements over a known baseline.
- Clear articulation of the accuracy–complexity trade-offs in coupling inner and outer loops.

Reasons to reject
- Experimental evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics and runtime results, limiting the assessment of practical impact.
- Gradient-free guarantees hinge on an unverified convexity assumption, leaving a gap between theory and practice.
- Presentation and references need significant polishing.

Overall
A promising and technically interesting submission that would benefit from stronger empirical validation and clarification of assumptions. With the essential improvements, the contribution would be competitive and useful to the community.
