- Tackles learning parameters for Supervised PageRank with an inexact oracle due to non-computable exact objective and gradient.
- Main contribution: two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and random gradient-free) with formal convergence rate guarantees.
- Lower level: uses a linearly convergent algorithm [17] to approximate PageRank stationary distribution and control oracle error.
- Upper level: develops two general frameworks — (a) a random gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization with inexact function evaluations, and (b) an adaptive gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle that does not require the Lipschitz constant.
- Provides theoretical analysis showing the trade-off between lower-level oracle accuracy and overall complexity.
- Empirical validation on a web page ranking task shows improved final loss compared to baseline [21].
- Strength: first provably convergent algorithms for Supervised PageRank with rigorous, apparently correct convergence analysis.
- Strength: introduces general optimization frameworks for inexact zero- and first-order oracles applicable beyond this problem.
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation that motivates the problem and methods effectively.
- Strength: addresses a significant problem with practical relevance to web search and recommendation systems.
- Suggestion: include standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) to demonstrate practical ranking quality improvements, not just loss reduction.
- Suggestion: clarify practical implications and robustness of the convexity assumption for the gradient-free method given the non-convexity of the true loss.
- Suggestion: provide details on how hyperparameters (e.g., L = 1e-4, ε = 1e-6) were chosen to improve reproducibility.
- Suggestion: clarify whether stopping criteria for baseline [21] and proposed methods are comparable to ensure fair convergence-speed comparisons.
- Argument for acceptance: delivers first provably convergent methods for the task and novel general optimization frameworks with rigorous theory.
- Argument for acceptance: theoretical analysis is complete and experiments show superior optimization performance.
- Argument against acceptance: experimental evaluation omits standard ranking metrics, limiting assessment of practical impact.
- Argument against acceptance: discussion of the local convexity assumption for the gradient-free method is insufficient.
