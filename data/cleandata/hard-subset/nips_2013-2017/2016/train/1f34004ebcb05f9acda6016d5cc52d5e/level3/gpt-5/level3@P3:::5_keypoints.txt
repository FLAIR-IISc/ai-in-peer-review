- Paper studies feature-parameterized PageRank with linear feature-based transition/restart probabilities and a pairwise hinge-squared loss over judge-labeled pairs per query.
- Optimization is two-level: inner approximates per-query stationary distributions and derivatives, outer optimizes feature weights.
- Main technical idea: combine Nesterov–Nemirovski stationary-distribution approximation with inexact-oracle optimization (biased zeroth-order and inexact first-order methods) and provide convergence rates/trade-offs.
- Related to prior PageRank/BrowseRank and supervised random-walk/semi-supervised ranking literature; inner approximation is standard and some citations/attributions appear incorrect.
- Theory: formalizes inexact zero/first-order oracles, states assumptions, and proves convergence theorems with useful lemmas bounding inner-to-outer errors.
- Theory concerns: several crucial assumptions are strong or insufficiently justified (local convexity in a ball, known/tuned Lipschitz constants, positivity/scale constraints, restricting search to an R-ball).
- Presentation of proofs: main proofs relegated to appendix; main text lacks proof sketches and practical interpretation of bounds.
- Empirics: experimental section is thin and many key parameters (α, τ, N, N1, N2, δ, stopping criteria) are missing or only partially reported.
- Empirics: sensitivity analyses deferred to appendix; only loss values reported—no ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP), runtime/compute, or robust baselines/comparisons.
- Empirics: unclear dataset breadth—seems limited to one web-browsing graph and 600 queries from one engine.
- Clarity: writing is hard to follow, with grammatical errors, inconsistent notation, sloppy/incomplete citations, and repeated referrals to the appendix for core content.
- Clarity: important modeling choices (how ϕ0 and R were chosen, why α, how Φ intersects simplex) are not explained.
- Originality: combination of techniques is reasonable but largely incremental relative to existing inexact-oracle and Markov-chain approximation literature.
- Originality: learning framework appears to reproduce prior supervised/semi-supervised PageRank formulations; novelty over Supervised Random Walks is unclear.
- Significance: theoretical bounds are valuable, but practical impact is not demonstrated due to empirical shortcomings and strong/unvalidated assumptions.
- Pros for acceptance: clear formalization of inexact oracles and convergence guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods.
- Pros for acceptance: useful error bounds linking inner Markov-chain approximation accuracy to outer optimization progress.
- Pros for acceptance: potentially helpful for practitioners optimizing nonconvex objectives with approximate evaluations.
- Cons for acceptance: inadequate empirical evaluation—missing metrics, parameters, baselines, and effect-size discussion; much of results hidden in appendix.
- Cons for acceptance: questionable novelty and possible mis-citation/misnaming of related work.
- Cons for acceptance: strong and potentially unrealistic assumptions with limited justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Cons for acceptance: writing quality and referencing issues hinder reproducibility and understanding.
- Questions for authors: clarify relation to “Supervised PageRank” vs. Supervised Random Walks and what is genuinely new in model/loss beyond optimization details.
- Questions for authors: how were ϕ0 (or ϕ̂) and radius R chosen, why restrict search to an R-ball, and how sensitive are results to R?
- Questions for authors: what damping α was used and how were τ, δ, N, N1, N2 selected in practice?
- Questions for authors: can you report standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP), wall-clock times for inner/outer loops, and comparisons to power method and standard baselines (RankSVM/GBDT, Supervised Random Walks)?
- Questions for authors: do results generalize to other graphs/datasets?
- Suggestions: include proof sketches and full assumptions in main text; correct citations and unify notation; proofread thoroughly.
- Suggestions: provide complete experimental details, ranking metrics, computational costs, sensitivity studies (R, α, L, ε), and ablation studies.
- Suggestions: compare against stronger baselines including non-graph learning-to-rank and supervised random-walk methods, and discuss realism of assumptions and hyperparameter guidance.
- Recommendation: Reject in current form; theory is solid but lacks clarity, novelty justification, and empirical validation.
