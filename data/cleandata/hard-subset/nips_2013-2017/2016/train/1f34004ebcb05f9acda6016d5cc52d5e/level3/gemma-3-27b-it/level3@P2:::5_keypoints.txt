- Paper proposes gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for Supervised PageRank with theoretical convergence guarantees, addressing a key limitation of prior work.  
- Uses an inexact oracle framework to estimate stationary distributions and derivatives for a non-convex loss over Markov random walks.  
- Builds on established optimization literature and adapts techniques to supervised PageRank context.  
- Empirically shows improvements over prior GBP [21] on a web page ranking task (Yandex dataset).  
- Motivation to incorporate node and edge features into random-walk ranking is well-aligned with prior link-analysis and supervised ranking research.  
- Theoretical contributions are potentially valuable but presented densely with limited intuition.  
- Writing quality is poor with grammatical errors, hindering comprehension.  
- Citations are imprecise; notably mischaracterizes Zhukovskii et al. (2016) as supervised when it is semi-supervised.  
- Many crucial technical details and proofs are relegated to supplementary materials, making assessment difficult.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Yandex), raising generalizability concerns.  
- Key experimental parameters (step sizes, hyperparameter tuning) and magnitude of improvements are not clearly reported.  
- The parameter-space constraint (search within an R-ball around ϕ0) is unjustified and its implications are not discussed; choice of ϕ0 and R unexplained.  
- Technical assumptions (smoothness, convex approximations in a non-convex problem) may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Computational complexity analysis is hard to interpret without implementation and dataset details.  
- Strength: provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, addressing prior gaps.  
- Strength: leverages known optimization techniques in a novel application.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical results advance understanding; empirical results suggest improvements; topic is relevant to ML and IR audiences.  
- Arguments for rejection: poor presentation, limited empirical validation, unrealistic assumptions, and overreliance on supplementary material.  
- Overall assessment: reviewer recommends weak reject, urging substantial revisions including clearer exposition, more extensive experiments, and justification of assumptions.
