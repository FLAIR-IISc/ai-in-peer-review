

Review:

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods: one gradient-based and one gradient-free, to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem. The key contribution is providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, which is lacking in the state-of-the-art approach of [21]. The methods operate with an "inexact oracle" that approximates the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, and the authors prove convergence rates while controlling the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and the overall computational complexity.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by filling a gap in the existing literature. Previous work [21] used a two-level gradient-based method but provided no convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of the oracle. The authors develop a general framework for gradient methods with inexact oracle for non-convex problems and extend random gradient-free methods to handle constrained optimization with inexact zero-order oracle. They prove convergence rate theorems for both methods, which is a major contribution.

The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality on a real-world web page ranking task using Yandex data. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation (controlled by the number of iterations in the power method) and the overall computational complexity.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good balance between theoretical development and practical implementation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the theoretical results are clearly presented. The experimental setup is well-described, using a real-world dataset with 600 queries.

However, there are two critical weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, the paper does not report computational runtime (in seconds) for the proposed methods, which is essential for assessing their practical utility. The reviewer strongly recommends adding runtime measurements for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, as this would provide crucial information for practitioners evaluating these methods.

Second, while the authors claim their methods are the first to combine random gradient-free and gradient optimization with an inexact oracle, this claim could be better supported with a more thorough comparison to recent work on inexact oracle methods in the NIPS literature. The paper could better contextualize their approach relative to Nesterov's work on inexact oracles [25] and the gradient-free methods of Nesterov and Spokoiny [16].

The paper's significance is high, as it provides both theoretical guarantees and practical improvements for a problem with clear real-world applications in search engines and information retrieval. The theoretical results on convergence rates with inexact oracles could also be useful for other optimization problems in machine learning.

In terms of originality, the paper's main contribution is the theoretical analysis of gradient-based and gradient-free methods for non-convex optimization with an inexact oracle in the context of Supervised PageRank. While the general concept of inexact oracles has been explored in optimization literature, the specific application to this problem domain with the provided convergence guarantees is novel.

The paper meets quality criteria with technically sound theoretical claims and well-supported experimental results. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and weaknesses, including the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity.

Clarity is good, but the lack of runtime data limits the practical assessment of the proposed methods. A more detailed comparison to related work in the NIPS community would strengthen the paper.

Pro: Theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods; Strong experimental results showing outperformance of state-of-the-art; Careful analysis of trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational complexity.

Con: Lack of computational runtime measurements; Limited comparison to recent NIPS literature on inexact oracle methods; Insufficient explanation of when to prefer gradient-free over gradient-based methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and practical validation. The main weakness is the lack of computational runtime measurements, which is a critical omission for an optimization paper. With this information added, the paper would be highly suitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the condition that the authors include computational runtime measurements in the final version.
