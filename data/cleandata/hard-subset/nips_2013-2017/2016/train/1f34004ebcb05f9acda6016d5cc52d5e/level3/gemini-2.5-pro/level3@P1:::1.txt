This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a graph-based ranking algorithm where random walk probabilities are determined by features of nodes and edges. The learning problem is formulated as the minimization of a non-convex loss function. The core challenge, as the authors correctly identify, is that the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly. Instead, they must be approximated via an iterative process, which renders standard optimization methods with proven convergence guarantees inapplicable.

The authors propose a principled two-level optimization framework to solve this problem. On the lower level, they employ a linearly convergent iterative method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk. A key insight is that this method allows them to compute the loss function and its gradient up to any pre-specified accuracy, creating a controllable "inexact oracle". On the upper level, they use this oracle to drive two different optimization algorithms: (1) a gradient-free method adapted from [16] to handle constraints and the specific error model, and (2) a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex optimization that builds on the inexact oracle framework of [7, 25]. The main contribution of the paper is providing a full theoretical analysis of these algorithms, establishing convergence rates to a stationary point. This is in contrast to the prior state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], which lacked such guarantees. The theoretical results are supported by experiments on a web page ranking task, demonstrating that the proposed methods outperform the previous approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Quality:** The paper provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for the Supervised PageRank model. This is a significant contribution, as the previous method lacked theoretical guarantees, leaving its reliability in question. The work is technically sound, and the analysis that connects the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (controlled by the number of iterations N) to the overall convergence rate (ε) of the upper-level algorithm is rigorous and well-executed.

2.  **Broader Impact:** The framework for handling constrained optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (Section 5.1) is of general interest beyond the specific application. Many large-scale machine learning problems involve objective functions that depend on the output of an iterative subroutine (e.g., matrix factorizations, other graph algorithms). The methods and analysis presented here could provide a template for developing provably convergent algorithms in those settings as well.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the limitations of existing work, and detailing their proposed solution. The distinction between the lower-level oracle and the upper-level optimizer is articulated effectively.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while conducted on a single dataset, are well-designed and convincingly show that the new methods (particularly the gradient-free one) outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of the final loss value.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality:** The primary weakness lies in the originality of the optimization techniques themselves. The concepts of using inexact oracles in gradient-based and gradient-free optimization are well-established in the literature (e.g., [7, 16, 25]). The paper's contribution is adapting these frameworks to a specific constrained, non-convex setting and providing a complete complexity analysis for a particular application. While this is a valuable engineering and theoretical exercise, the conceptual advance in optimization theory is incremental rather than groundbreaking.

2.  **Framing of Contribution:** The paper's contribution to general optimization theory is somewhat understated by being framed almost exclusively within the Supervised PageRank application. To improve the paper and broaden its appeal, the authors could consider presenting the general algorithms and their convergence theorems (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) more prominently. For instance, a dedicated section on "Constrained Optimization with Inexact Oracles" preceding the application could help readers from the core optimization community better appreciate the general-purpose nature of the proposed methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   It provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for an important and practical graph-based learning problem.
*   The technical analysis is rigorous and complete, linking the complexity of the oracle to the overall optimization performance.
*   The proposed algorithms are shown to be empirically superior to the previous state-of-the-art method.
*   The work on adapting inexact oracle methods to constrained settings is a useful contribution to the optimization community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty in the core optimization ideas is limited, as the work is an adaptation and combination of existing frameworks.
*   The contribution is primarily focused on providing a theoretical underpinning for an existing model, rather than introducing a new model or a fundamentally new algorithm.

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution by placing the training of Supervised PageRank on a firm theoretical footing. While the core optimization ideas are not entirely new, their careful adaptation, analysis, and application to this problem are significant. The strengths of the paper, particularly its technical quality and the importance of solving the stated problem, outweigh the concerns about originality.
