### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a non-convex, feature-driven graph ranking model known as Supervised PageRank. In this model, the transition probabilities of a random walk are defined by learnable weights on node and edge features. The core challenge is that the loss function, which measures ranking quality, depends on the stationary distribution of this random walk, which cannot be computed exactly. This makes standard optimization techniques that require exact objective and gradient values inapplicable.

To overcome this, the authors propose a two-level optimization framework. On the lower level, they employ a linearly convergent iterative method to estimate the stationary distribution of the Markov chain. They formally show that this allows them to approximate the loss function value and its gradient to any prescribed precision, creating an "inexact oracle." On the upper level, they develop a gradient-based algorithm for constrained non-convex optimization that is designed to work with this inexact oracle. The paper's main theoretical result is proving that this upper-level algorithm converges to a stationary point. This work extends existing literature on optimization with inexact oracles, particularly to the constrained setting where function values are computable up to a known, controllable error.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically strong. It tackles a difficult and practical problem where the objective function is computationally intractable. The proposed two-level algorithm is well-motivated by this challenge. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, providing convergence guarantees for a non-convex problem where the previous state-of-the-art method lacked them. The adaptation of inexact-oracle methods to handle constrained optimization is a valuable contribution.

*   **Significance:** The work is significant for both the ranking and optimization communities. For ranking, it provides a principled and provably convergent algorithm for learning a powerful feature-based PageRank model. For optimization, the main contribution is the development and analysis of a gradient method for constrained non-convex problems where the oracle's precision can be controlled. This framework is potentially applicable to other large-scale learning problems where objectives can only be estimated (e.g., via sampling or iterative methods).

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the problem, motivate their approach, and lay out their theoretical results. The two-level structure is presented logically, making the complex interaction between the stationary distribution estimation and the parameter optimization easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Originality:** While the paper is technically sound, there are some concerns about its novelty. The core optimization ideas for handling inexact oracles bear a resemblance to prior work, such as Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), which explored gradient-free methods with inexact function values. Furthermore, the Supervised PageRank model itself has been established in previous work. The primary novelty lies in the synthesis of these ideas and their specific adaptation to a *constrained* non-convex gradient-based setting, which is a solid but perhaps incremental contribution rather than a fundamentally new approach. The authors should be more explicit in delineating their theoretical contributions from this prior art.

*   **Framing of Contribution:** The paper's focus is almost exclusively on the Supervised PageRank application. While this provides a concrete use case, it may undersell the generality and importance of the optimization framework itself. Presenting the optimization problem (constrained non-convex minimization with a controllable inexact first-order oracle) in a more general context first, and then showing Supervised PageRank as a key application, would better highlight the paper's contribution to the broader optimization literature and clarify its relationship to the state-of-the-art.

*   **Minor Issues:** The manuscript is missing a conclusion section, which leaves it feeling incomplete. Adding a conclusion to summarize the contributions and discuss future directions would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this is a solid paper that provides a provably convergent algorithm for an important learning problem. The main strengths are its technical rigor and the practical relevance of the problem. The primary weakness is a potential lack of groundbreaking novelty in the underlying optimization techniques, though the specific adaptation is valuable.
