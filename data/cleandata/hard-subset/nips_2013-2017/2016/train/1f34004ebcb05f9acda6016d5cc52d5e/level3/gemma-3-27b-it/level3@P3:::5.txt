This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models with feature-based node/edge weighting, aiming to improve upon existing methods lacking convergence guarantees. The authors propose combining stationary-distribution approximation with gradient-free and gradient-based optimization techniques, offering theoretical convergence analysis. While the theoretical contributions appear strong, the paper suffers from significant clarity and empirical validation issues, hindering a convincing demonstration of practical impact.

The motivation is sound. Existing PageRank methods often ignore valuable node and edge features, and previous supervised approaches, such as the one by Zhukovskii et al. [21], lacked theoretical convergence guarantees. The approach of approximating the stationary distribution and employing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization is a reasonable strategy. However, the paper immediately flags a potential issue: the cited work [21] is referred to as "Supervised PageRank" while actually presenting "semi-supervised PageRank," indicating a careless oversight.

The core technical contribution lies in providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods within this framework. The authors introduce lemmas bounding approximate gradients and a general biased gradient-free oracle, culminating in a performance theorem. This is further refined with tailored algorithms and additional assumptions leading to further theoretical bounds. The method leveraging the linearly convergent method [17] for stationary distribution approximation appears promising, addressing a key limitation of prior work.

However, the presentation is significantly hampered by poor writing quality. Numerous grammatical errors and sloppy citations impede understanding. The technical assumptions, while standard for this type of analysis, could be unrealistic in practice, particularly the constraint of searching for parameters within an R-ball around a specific point (ϕ0). The justification for choosing R and ϕ0 is entirely absent in the empirical section, raising concerns about the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. The restriction to this R-ball is also not discussed regarding its potential to limit the efficacy of the search.

The experimental section is particularly weak. Tests are conducted on a web graph used in related work, but detailed results and analysis are relegated to the appendix. Key experimental parameters, such as the values of α, R, and the specific features used, are not reported.  Crucially, the extent or significance of the claimed improvement over baselines is not discussed – simply stating that GFN outperforms GBP isn’t sufficient without quantitative comparison and statistical significance testing.  It’s unclear whether the experiments covered a diverse set of graphs or multiple datasets, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings. The reliance on the appendix for crucial details – proofs, experimental setup, and results – makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the validity of the claims. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, addressing a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Novel Combination:**  The combination of stationary-distribution approximation with gradient-free and gradient-based optimization is a potentially valuable approach.
*   **Addressing Prior Limitations:** The work explicitly addresses the lack of convergence guarantees in previous supervised PageRank methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Writing:** Poor writing quality, grammatical errors, and sloppy citations severely hinder understanding.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Insufficiently detailed and analyzed experiments. Key parameters are missing, and the significance of reported improvements is unclear.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  Unclear whether the experiments were conducted on a diverse set of graphs or datasets.
*   **Appendix-Heavy:**  Essential details (proofs, experimental setup, full results) are relegated to the appendix, making assessment difficult.
*   **Unjustified Assumptions:** Some technical assumptions may be unrealistic in real-world scenarios.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of key parameters (R, ϕ0) is unexplained, raising concerns about robustness.
*   **Citation Error:** Incorrectly labels [21] as "Supervised PageRank" instead of "Semi-supervised PageRank."

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contributions with convergence guarantees.
*   Addresses a known limitation of previous work in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor writing quality and lack of clarity.
*   Insufficient and poorly presented experimental validation.
*   Concerns about the practical applicability of the assumptions.
*   Overall, the paper reads more like a theoretical exploration than a fully validated scientific contribution.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound approach to learning Supervised PageRank, but its significant weaknesses in clarity, experimental validation, and presentation prevent a positive recommendation for acceptance. While the theoretical contributions are noteworthy, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate practical impact or empirical validation. A major revision addressing the issues outlined above, including a thorough experimental evaluation and improved writing, would be necessary for reconsideration.
