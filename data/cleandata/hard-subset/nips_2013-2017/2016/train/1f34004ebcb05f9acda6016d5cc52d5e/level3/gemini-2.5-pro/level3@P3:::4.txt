This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for a Supervised PageRank model. This model extends the classic PageRank by incorporating node and edge features, making it more expressive for ranking tasks. The central challenge is that the loss function and its gradient, which depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov process, cannot be computed exactly. The authors frame this as optimization with an "inexact oracle".

The main contributions are the proposal and analysis of two two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem:
1.  A random gradient-free method, adapted from prior work by Nesterov and others, to handle constrained optimization problems where only noisy function evaluations are available.
2.  A gradient-based method that generalizes existing frameworks for non-convex composite optimization to work with an inexact first-order oracle.

Crucially, unlike the previous state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], the authors provide full theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both of their proposed algorithms. They analyze the complexity of the entire two-level procedure, connecting the accuracy required by the upper-level optimization algorithm to the number of iterations needed for the lower-level oracle (which approximates the stationary distribution). Experiments on a large-scale web ranking dataset show that the proposed methods outperform the previous approach in terms of final ranking quality.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality & Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its theoretical rigor. It provides the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models, a significant step forward from the prior heuristic approach in [21]. By formally analyzing the problem within the framework of optimization with an inexact oracle, the authors provide a principled way to manage the trade-off between computational effort at the lower level (approximating PageRank) and the convergence of the upper-level learning algorithm. The derived complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) are a valuable contribution.
*   **Originality:** While the optimization techniques are extensions of existing frameworks ([7], [16], [25]), their adaptation to the constrained, non-convex Supervised PageRank problem is novel and non-trivial. The careful analysis of how to set the lower-level precision (N, N1, N2) to satisfy the upper-level algorithm's requirements is a key part of this contribution. This work successfully bridges the gap between theoretical optimization literature on inexact oracles and the practical problem of learning graph-based ranking models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly defines the Supervised PageRank model, explains why exact optimization is intractable, presents the general optimization frameworks, and then details their application to the specific learning problem. The separation of the general framework (Sections 4.1, 5.1) from the specific application (Sections 4.2, 5.2) aids understanding.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are conducted on a large, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine, which demonstrates the practical relevance of the work. The results convincingly show that both proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free one (GFN), achieve a better final loss value than the existing method (GBP).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Missing Runtime Comparison:** The experimental evaluation is incomplete as it lacks a comparison of the computational time (wall-clock time) of the different methods. The paper reports convergence in terms of upper-level iterations and final loss value, but this does not tell the whole story. An iteration of GFN, GBN, or GBP may have vastly different computational costs depending on the required precision of the inexact oracle. The provided theoretical complexity bounds are excellent, but an empirical validation of these complexities in terms of actual runtime is necessary for a fair and practical comparison. Without this, it is difficult to assess the true efficiency of the proposed algorithms versus the baseline.
*   **Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within the optimization domain Φ. However, the problem is stated to be non-convex in general. The authors justify this by assuming that Φ is a small neighborhood around a local minimum where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that is not empirically verified. The analysis for the gradient-based method does not require this, making its theoretical guarantees more general.
*   **Clarity of Experimental Parameters for Baseline:** The description of the baseline method (GBP) could be more detailed. The stopping criterion—"when the differences between the values of the loss function on the next step and the current step are less than -10^-5"—is unusual and seems to imply the algorithm only stops when making significant progress, which might not be a fair comparison. A more standard criterion, like the absolute change in loss or a fixed computation budget, would be more appropriate.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution by placing the problem of learning Supervised PageRank on a solid theoretical foundation. The development of optimization algorithms with proven convergence guarantees is a clear advance over prior work. The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical runtime analysis, which is a critical component for evaluating optimization algorithms. However, this does not invalidate the paper's core theoretical contributions. The convexity assumption for the gradient-free method is a limitation, but the paper also proposes a gradient-based method for the general non-convex case.

Overall, the strengths in theoretical soundness and significance outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors add a discussion of computational runtime to the final version to provide a more complete experimental picture.
