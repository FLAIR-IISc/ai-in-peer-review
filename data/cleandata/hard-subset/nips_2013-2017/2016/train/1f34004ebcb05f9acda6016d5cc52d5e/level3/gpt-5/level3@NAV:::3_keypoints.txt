- Proposes a two-level supervised PageRank learning scheme using inexact lower-level oracles and principled first- and zero-order upper-level updates.  
- Lower level approximates stationary distributions with the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method, avoiding costly matrix powers.  
- Extends the lower-level routine to approximate gradients for the upper-level optimization.  
- Upper level GBN (gradient-based) method: convergence-rate guarantees to a stationary point in the nonconvex, constrained, inexact-oracle setting.  
- Upper level GFN (gradient-free) method: guaranteed loss decrease and complexity bounds under oracle error.  
- Both GBN and GFN include explicit oracle-error propagation, step-size/accuracy schedules, and operation-count estimates without requiring exact objective values.  
- Theoretical development is careful and proofs are provided in the supplement; building on ref. 17 is computationally motivated.  
- Empirical evaluation on large query-dependent browsing graphs shows improved ranking quality over state of the art, but comparisons focus on loss rather than standard ranking metrics.  
- Missing direct empirical comparison to ref. 17 and lacking ablation studies on lower-level accuracy/runtime trade-offs weaken empirical claims.  
- Clarity issues: heavy notation and undefined symbols (e.g., N appear before definition); recommend a symbol table and defining quantities on first use.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of random gradient-free methods to constrained inexact-oracle optimization and extension of the NN lower-level method to derivative estimation.  
- Significance: methods are broadly applicable to large-scale, feature-rich graph ranking and provide valuable convergence guarantees under realistic inexact computations.  
- Reproducibility is hindered by private data; releasing code or a synthetic benchmark would increase impact.  
- Pros: solid theoretical guarantees, computationally efficient lower-level routine, clear hyperparameter reporting, and a potentially generalizable inexact-oracle framework.  
- Cons: experiments omit NDCG/MAP comparisons and a ref. 17 baseline, notation is heavy, and private data limits reproducibility.
