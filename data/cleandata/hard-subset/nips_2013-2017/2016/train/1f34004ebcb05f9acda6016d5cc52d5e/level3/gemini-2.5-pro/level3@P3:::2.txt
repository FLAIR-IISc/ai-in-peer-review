### Paper Review

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into the classic PageRank algorithm. The authors formulate this learning task as a constrained, non-convex optimization problem. The primary challenge is that the objective function and its gradient, which depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain, cannot be computed exactly. The main contribution of this work is the proposal of two optimization algorithms designed to handle this inexactness: a random gradient-free method based on an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle. A key strength of the paper is providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which the authors claim was lacking in prior state-of-the-art approaches for this problem. The methods are developed within a general framework for optimization with inexact oracles and then specialized for the Supervised PageRank problem.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical treatment of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. By formalizing the optimization using the concept of inexact oracles and providing convergence guarantees (Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4), the authors place the learning procedure on solid theoretical footing. This addresses a significant limitation of previous work [21], which lacked a formal proof of convergence.

2.  **Novel Application of Optimization Techniques:** The application and adaptation of modern optimization theory for inexact oracles [7, 16, 25] to the specific structure of the Supervised PageRank problem is novel and well-motivated. The development of a gradient-free method for the constrained case and an adaptive gradient method for the non-convex case represents a valuable contribution.

3.  **Significance of the Problem:** Learning to rank on graphs is a fundamental problem with wide-ranging applications, from web search to social network analysis. By proposing theoretically-grounded algorithms, this work has the potential to advance the state of the art and provide more reliable and efficient tools for practitioners.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**

The paper has several significant weaknesses, primarily concerning its completeness, the clarity of its theoretical assumptions, and the evaluation of its proposed methods.

1.  **Incomplete Main Paper:** The most critical issue is that the paper is not self-contained. The entire experimental section (Section 6), including all tables and figures, is placed in the supplementary material. The main paper only contains a brief description of the dataset and experimental setup, followed by a paragraph summarizing results that the reader cannot see or verify. A scientific paper must provide evidence to support its claims within the main text. Without the experimental results, it is impossible to assess the practical performance of the proposed GFN and GBN algorithms or validate the claim that they "outperform [the state-of-the-art] in the ranking quality". This is a major flaw that should be rectified by moving the essential results into the main paper, even if it requires shortening other sections.

2.  **Unjustified Convexity Assumption for Theorem 1:** Theorem 1, which provides the convergence guarantee for the gradient-free method, assumes that the objective function f(ϕ) is convex on the feasible set Φ. However, the loss function defined in equation (2.4) is generally non-convex due to the composition of the squared norm with the non-linear function πq(ϕ). The authors attempt to address this by stating, "we assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) (2.4) is convex". This is a very strong assumption without any justification. The paper provides no guidance on how to choose the set Φ to ensure this property holds, nor does it discuss when this assumption might be realistic. This makes the applicability of Theorem 1 unclear.

3.  **Ambiguous Convergence Criterion in Theorem 3:** The convergence result for the gradient-based method, Theorem 3, states that after M iterations, it holds that ||MK(xK - x{K+1})||² ≤ 4L(ψ(x0)−ψ∗)/(M+1) + ε²/2. While this is a valid stopping criterion and shows that the quantity on the left-hand side converges to zero, it is not immediately obvious whether this guarantees that the sequence of iterates {xk} converges to a stationary point of the optimization problem. A more direct connection to standard stationarity conditions for constrained non-convex optimization (e.g., showing that the norm of the projected gradient vanishes) would significantly strengthen the theoretical claims and improve clarity.

4.  **Lack of Efficiency Analysis:** The paper claims its algorithms improve reliability and speed, but provides insufficient evidence. The experimental results (in the supplement) focus on the final loss function values. However, Supplementary Figure 1 appears to show that the gradient-free method (GFN) converges quite slowly compared to the gradient-based methods. The paper would be much stronger if it included a thorough comparison of algorithmic efficiency, reporting metrics like wall-clock time, total number of oracle calls, or total arithmetic operations required to reach a certain solution quality. This is crucial for assessing the practical utility of the proposed methods.

**Questions for Authors:**
*   Could you provide any conditions on the graph structure or features under which the local convexity assumption for Theorem 1 holds?
*   Can you please clarify the relationship between the stopping criterion in Theorem 3 and convergence to a KKT point or other standard measure of stationarity?
*   Why were all experimental results relegated to the supplement? Given their importance for validating the paper's contributions, they should be a central part of the main text.
