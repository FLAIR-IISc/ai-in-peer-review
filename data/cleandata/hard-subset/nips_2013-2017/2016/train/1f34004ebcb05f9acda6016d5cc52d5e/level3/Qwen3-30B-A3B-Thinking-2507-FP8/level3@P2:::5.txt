

# Review for "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper proposes two-level optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend the traditional PageRank framework by incorporating node and edge features through linear combinations with model parameters. The authors address a non-convex loss-minimization problem that previous work [21] (which actually uses "Semi-supervised PageRank" as noted in the references) failed to provide convergence guarantees for. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis of gradient-based and gradient-free methods for this problem, with complexity bounds for the overall optimization procedure.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds on [21], which introduced Supervised PageRank as a framework for incorporating node and edge features into ranking models. Previous work [21] used a two-level approach with the power method for stationary distribution approximation, but lacked convergence guarantees due to the inexactness of the oracle. The authors extend this framework by incorporating inexact oracle concepts from [16, 7, 17] and provide theoretical convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This work relates to recent advances in optimization with inexact oracles [25, 26], but applies them to a specific learning problem in graph ranking.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical contributions are sound and well-justified. The authors provide rigorous convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods under inexact oracle conditions, which is a significant technical contribution.
- The complexity analysis for the two-level algorithms is thorough and provides useful bounds for practitioners.
- The paper correctly identifies the limitations of previous work [21] regarding convergence guarantees, which is a clear gap in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity and Writing Quality:** The paper suffers from poor writing quality with grammatical errors, inconsistent notation (e.g., using "Supervised PageRank" when reference [21] uses "Semi-supervised PageRank"), and unclear explanations of key concepts. The mathematical presentation is sometimes sloppy, making it difficult to follow the technical arguments.
  
- **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is severely lacking. The paper claims improvements over state-of-the-art methods but:
  - Defers detailed results to the appendix without providing key findings in the main text
  - Fails to report critical hyperparameters (e.g., how phi0 and R were chosen, the specific values of L for different methods)
  - Does not convincingly demonstrate the significance of the claimed improvements (e.g., the exact magnitude of performance gains is not quantified in the main text)
  - The dataset description is insufficient (no details on how the 600 queries were selected, no analysis of graph diversity)
  - The constraint of searching phi within an R-ball around phi0 (with R=0.99) is not justified and could severely limit the model's expressiveness in practice.

- **Significance:** The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical impact of the proposed methods. The claimed "improvements" over [21] are not properly contextualized, and the experimental results lack statistical significance testing for the claimed improvements (though the paper mentions p-values < 0.005, these are not reported in the main text).

- **Originality:** While the technical contributions to optimization with inexact oracles are valid, the application to Supervised PageRank appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing methods rather than a novel combination that advances the field. The paper essentially reproduces a framework from [21] under different notation, which undermines the originality claim.

**Quality Assessment:**
The technical content is sound from a theoretical perspective, with well-justified lemmas and theorems. However, the paper lacks proper empirical validation to support the practical value of the theoretical contributions. The experimental section is insufficient to demonstrate that the proposed methods provide meaningful improvements in real-world settings.

**Recommendation:**
This paper has a strong theoretical contribution but is significantly weakened by poor experimental validation and writing quality. The main technical content and empirical results are relegated to the appendix, which is inconsistent with NIPS standards for a complete paper. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of its theoretical advances.

The reviewers recommend **weak rejection** for the following reasons:
1. The experimental section does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claimed improvements over existing methods.
2. Critical details about the experimental setup and parameter choices are missing from the main text.
3. The writing quality and presentation are subpar, with grammatical errors and inconsistent notation.
4. The significance of the contribution is not sufficiently demonstrated in the context of practical ranking applications.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
- Provide complete experimental results in the main text (not just in the appendix)
- Report all key hyperparameters and justify their choices
- Clarify the relationship between their work and [21] (correcting the "Supervised PageRank" vs. "Semi-supervised PageRank" confusion)
- Better contextualize the practical significance of their theoretical results
- Improve the writing quality and mathematical presentation

While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the paper currently fails to meet NIPS standards for a complete scientific contribution due to the lack of proper empirical validation and presentation.
