This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models by tackling the associated non-convex loss-minimization problem. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in prior work [21], which uses a gradient-based method without theoretical convergence guarantees due to its reliance on an inexact oracle (the power method). The main contribution of this work is the proposal of two new optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that are designed to work with an inexact oracle while providing formal convergence rate guarantees. The methods build upon existing optimization frameworks [7, 16] but extend them to constrained problems and formally analyze the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall computational complexity.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Quality:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models. This closes an important gap in the literature and increases the reliability of such models. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with complexity bounds provided for both proposed methods.
*   **Originality:** The work presents a novel and well-motivated combination of techniques. It adapts general-purpose optimization methods for inexact oracles to a specific, practical problem in information retrieval. The generalization of these optimization frameworks to handle constraints and inexactness is a valuable contribution in its own right.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on real-world user browsing data from a commercial search engine. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free approach (GFN), outperform the state-of-the-art baseline in terms of final loss value.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity and Completeness of Evaluation:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the paper compares the methods based on loss function value and upper-level iteration counts, it omits a crucial metric for practitioners: computational runtime. The complexity analysis provides theoretical bounds in terms of arithmetic operations, but this does not always translate directly to wall-clock time, especially given the different lower-level computations required (e.g., function evaluations vs. gradient evaluations). To provide a more complete picture of the practical trade-offs between the methods, I strongly recommend that the authors report the actual runtimes (e.g., time to convergence) for GFN, GBN, and the baseline GBP in their experiments. This would significantly strengthen the paper by helping readers understand not just which method is most accurate, but also which is most efficient in practice.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The lack of runtime comparison is a notable omission but one that can be addressed to further improve the manuscript.
