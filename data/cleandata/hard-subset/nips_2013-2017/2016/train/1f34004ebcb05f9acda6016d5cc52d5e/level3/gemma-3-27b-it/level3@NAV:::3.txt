## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN). The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in previous approaches like the state-of-the-art method from [21]. The authors cleverly leverage the concept of an inexact oracle, avoiding the computational burden of exact objective function and derivative calculations. Both methods also estimate convergence rates, and avoid computing large matrices, contributing to practical scalability.

The paper builds upon Nesterov and Nemirovski’s work [17] in its two-level optimization framework, and clearly outlines the method details in Sections 2-5 with proofs relegated to the supplementary material. Experimental results demonstrate that both GBN and GFN outperform the existing method [21] in ranking quality. The authors also provide hyperparameter settings, enhancing reproducibility, and describe datasets sufficiently well to allow for replication. 

A significant strength is the theoretical rigor. Providing convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in this context is a valuable contribution. The avoidance of large matrix computations is also a practical benefit. The experimental validation, demonstrating improved ranking quality, further strengthens the work. The description of the experimental setup is clear, despite the datasets not being publicly available. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity due to extensive symbol usage without a symbol table. Many symbols are introduced before being defined, hindering readability. More critically, while the authors claim to generalize the work in [17], the experimental comparison is limited to loss values, not ranking quality. A direct comparison with [17] on ranking performance is essential to demonstrate the improvement over the foundational work they build upon. The discussion of the experimental results could also be more nuanced, including statistical significance testing beyond just stating p-values. Additionally, the connection to related work on large-scale ranking and optimization, especially beyond PageRank variants, could be strengthened.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees for convergence of both proposed methods.
*   Practical improvements in ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Well-defined methods and clear explanations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a symbol table significantly hinders readability.
*   Insufficient experimental comparison with the generalized work from [17] (only loss is compared).
*   Limited discussion of experimental results.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid contribution with significant theoretical and practical value. However, addressing the clarity issues and providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points above.
