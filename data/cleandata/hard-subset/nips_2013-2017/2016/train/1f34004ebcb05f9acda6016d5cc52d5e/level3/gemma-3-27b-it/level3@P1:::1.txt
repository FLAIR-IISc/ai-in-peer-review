This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, extending the standard PageRank algorithm to incorporate node and edge features. The authors tackle a non-convex loss minimization problem arising in this context, recognizing that existing optimization methods struggle due to the inability to compute exact loss function values and derivatives. Their main contribution lies in proposing and analyzing two two-level optimization methods – one gradient-based and one gradient-free – that leverage an inexact oracle approach. The lower level of each method focuses on approximating the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk, utilizing a linearly convergent method, while the upper level employs either a gradient descent or a gradient-free technique to tune the model parameters.  A key theoretical result is proving convergence rates for both methods, something lacking in prior work on Supervised PageRank [21]. The paper also explores the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level approximation and the overall computational complexity.  Experimental results on a web page ranking task demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach [21].

The work builds upon a well-established line of research in graph-based ranking algorithms, stemming from PageRank [18] and HITS [11], and their subsequent adaptations [8, 9, 19]. These traditional methods primarily focus on graph structure, while Supervised PageRank [21] and related approaches [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21] aim to integrate node and edge features into the ranking process. The authors correctly identify a gap in the existing literature: the lack of convergence guarantees for the state-of-the-art gradient-based method proposed in [21].  The two-level optimization framework is a reasonable approach for tackling this problem, separating the approximation of the stationary distribution from the parameter optimization.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The technical analysis appears rigorous, with clear convergence rate estimations provided for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. The use of an inexact oracle is a well-motivated strategy given the inherent difficulties in calculating exact values for the loss function and its derivatives. The experimental validation on a real-world dataset provides empirical support for the proposed methods, demonstrating their superior performance compared to the existing approach. The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easier to follow the complex technical details. The authors also provide a clear discussion of the computational complexity and the trade-offs involved.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the technical contributions are sound, the overall novelty is somewhat limited. The concepts of inexact-oracle optimization, particularly gradient-free methods, are well-established in the literature.  The work of Nesterov and Spokoiny [26] presents similar ideas for convex functions and could have been discussed in more detail. The supervised PageRank framework itself has been explored in prior work, meaning the core problem isn’t entirely new. While the authors address the convergence issues, the advancement is in applying and adapting existing techniques to this specific problem.  The theoretical contribution, while solid, feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. Furthermore, the paper lacks a concise concluding section summarizing the key findings and outlining potential future research directions. Finally, framing the optimization problem in a more general context – perhaps explicitly relating it to other constrained non-convex optimization problems – would help to better position the contributions and highlight their broader applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong technical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Empirically demonstrated improvement over the state-of-the-art.
*   Addresses a practical problem with real-world relevance.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – builds heavily on existing optimization techniques.
*   Lack of a conclusive closing section.
*   Could benefit from a broader contextualization of the optimization problem.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the novelty is not exceptional, the technical soundness, rigorous analysis, and empirical validation make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings. The convergence guarantees, in particular, are a valuable contribution to the Supervised PageRank literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the addition of a concluding section and a more explicit discussion of the relationship to existing work in inexact-oracle optimization.
