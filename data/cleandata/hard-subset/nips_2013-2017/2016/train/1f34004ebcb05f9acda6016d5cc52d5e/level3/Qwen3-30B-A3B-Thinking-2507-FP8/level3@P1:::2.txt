

The paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models with features of nodes and edges as a constrained non-convex optimization problem, proposing two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach with inexact first-order oracle and a gradient-free approach with inexact zero-order oracle. This builds on the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends standard PageRank to incorporate node and edge features through linear combinations with model parameters. Previous work [21] used a two-level gradient-based method without convergence guarantees, while this paper provides theoretical convergence rate analysis for both proposed methods.

The main strengths of the paper include: (1) providing the first convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank using gradient-based optimization; (2) developing novel gradient-free methods suitable for constrained optimization with inexact oracle; (3) establishing complexity bounds for both algorithms; and (4) demonstrating superior performance on a real-world web ranking task. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of previous approaches, particularly the lack of convergence guarantees due to the inexact nature of the oracle.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the experimental results are entirely relegated to the supplement, making the main paper incomplete and difficult to evaluate. This violates the standard practice of including key experimental results in the main text, especially for a machine learning conference like NIPS where empirical validation is crucial. The authors should move these results to the main paper.

Second, Theorem 1 assumes convexity of the objective function f(ϕ), but the loss function in (2.4) is explicitly non-convex. The paper claims to assume "there is a local minimum ϕ* and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ*, in which f(ϕ) is convex," but it fails to provide any justification for why such a convex neighborhood exists or how to choose Φ to guarantee this property. Without this critical detail, the convergence guarantees for the gradient-free method lack practical applicability.

Third, Theorem 3 provides a bound on ‖MK(xK - xK+1)‖2 but does not establish convergence to a stationary point of the non-convex problem. For non-convex optimization, a standard convergence criterion is that the norm of the gradient approaches zero, not that the difference between consecutive iterates approaches zero. The paper fails to clarify how this bound translates to convergence to a stationary point, which is essential for a non-convex optimization method.

Fourth, Supplementary Figure 1 shows the gradient-free method converging very slowly compared to the gradient-based method. The authors should report and compare the actual computational efficiency of their methods, including runtime and number of iterations required to reach a given accuracy, rather than just reporting final loss values. Without this information, it's unclear whether the gradient-free method offers any practical advantages over the gradient-based method.

The paper also has some minor weaknesses. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both f̃(ϕ, δ) and f̃(ϕ, δ1)), and the connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation could be clearer. The explanation of how the inexact oracle is used in practice is somewhat vague, particularly regarding the relationship between the required accuracy for the oracle and the overall convergence rate.

Regarding significance, the paper makes a technically interesting contribution to optimization theory for constrained non-convex problems with inexact oracles. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the experimental results being hidden in the supplement, the unclear convexity assumption, and the lack of computational efficiency comparisons.

The quality of the theoretical contributions is generally strong, with well-justified convergence proofs for the proposed methods. However, the lack of clarity around the convexity assumption for Theorem 1 and the convergence interpretation for Theorem 3 weakens the theoretical foundation.

The paper is generally well-written, with clear mathematical formulations, but the omission of key experimental results from the main text significantly impacts its clarity and completeness.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper provides novel convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, which is an important practical problem. The two-level optimization framework with inexact oracle is theoretically sound and represents a meaningful advancement over the state-of-the-art.

Arguments against acceptance: The main paper lacks critical experimental results, the convexity assumption for Theorem 1 is unsupported, the convergence interpretation for Theorem 3 is unclear, and the computational efficiency of the proposed methods is not properly evaluated.

In summary, the paper makes a technically interesting contribution to optimization theory for non-convex problems with inexact oracles, but the current presentation has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance. The authors should move key experimental results to the main paper, clarify the convexity assumption for Theorem 1, and provide a proper convergence interpretation for Theorem 3. Without these revisions, the paper's claims about convergence and practical utility cannot be properly evaluated.
