- Problem: learning parameters of Supervised PageRank involves a non-convex loss whose value and gradient depend on a Markov chain stationary distribution that must be approximated, yielding an inexact oracle.
- Gap: the prior state-of-the-art method [21] lacks theoretical convergence guarantees under oracle inexactness.
- Contribution: two two-level optimization algorithms with explicit handling of inexact oracles and formal convergence rate guarantees.
- Methods: an adaptive gradient-based method for constrained non-convex problems (extension of [7]).
- Methods: a random gradient-free method for constrained problems using only noisy function values (adapted from [16]).
- Lower-level solver: use of the Nesterov–Nemirovski method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and derivatives with explicit error bounds.
- Theory: full complexity analysis linking lower-level oracle accuracy to upper-level iteration counts, yielding provable convergence rates.
- Empirical: experiments on a large real-world web ranking dataset show the proposed methods obtain lower loss and better iteration-wise convergence than the baseline.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical treatment formalizing inexact oracles and accounting for the trade-off between oracle effort and optimization iterations.
- Originality: novel synthesis of recent optimization theory with Supervised PageRank, producing the first provably convergent algorithms for this problem.
- Significance: frameworks are broadly applicable to large-scale learning problems with costly exact evaluations, valuable to researchers and practitioners.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and well-structured, with consistent notation and clear motivation.
- Suggestion: paper is dense; adding more high-level intuition in Sections 4 and 5 would aid readability for a broader audience.
- Weakness: experimental evaluation omits wall-clock runtime comparisons, obscuring practical efficiency trade-offs between methods (e.g., derivative cost vs iterations).
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on adding runtime reporting to better assess practical performance.
