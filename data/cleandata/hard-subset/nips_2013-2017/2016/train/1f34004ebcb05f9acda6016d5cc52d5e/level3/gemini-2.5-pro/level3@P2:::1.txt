This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of a Supervised PageRank model. This task is formulated as a non-convex loss-minimization problem where the node and edge weights in the underlying random walk are parameterized by linear functions of their features. A key challenge in this setting is that the objective function, which depends on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain, cannot be computed exactly. Consequently, standard optimization algorithms are not directly applicable.

The authors propose a two-level optimization framework to solve this problem. On the lower level, they employ an efficient, linearly convergent iterative method from [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives. A key insight is that this method allows them to control the approximation error, effectively providing an "inexact oracle" for the objective function and its gradient with a known error bound. On the upper level, the authors develop two algorithms that can leverage this inexact oracle: a random gradient-free method for convex settings and an adaptive gradient-based method for the more general non-convex case. A significant contribution of this work is providing a full theoretical analysis of these methods, establishing convergence rates and computational complexity bounds, which were absent in the prior state-of-the-art method for this problem [21]. The proposed methods are experimentally validated on a web page ranking task, where they outperform the existing baseline.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality and Significance:** The paper provides a rigorous and theoretically sound approach to a challenging and practical problem. Learning feature-based ranking models on graphs is an important task, and the non-convex nature of the Supervised PageRank formulation makes optimization difficult. Providing the first convergence guarantees for this problem is a significant contribution. The framework of combining a controllable lower-level approximation with an upper-level optimization algorithm designed for inexact oracles is elegant and well-executed.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem, explaining the challenges posed by the inexact objective function, and detailing their proposed two-level solution. The separation of the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application makes the core algorithmic contributions clear.

3.  **Completeness:** The work is a complete research contribution, containing a well-defined problem, novel algorithmic solutions, thorough theoretical analysis with convergence proofs, and empirical validation on a real-world dataset. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods not only have theoretical guarantees but also achieve better performance in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality:** While the application and the complete framework are novel, the core optimization ideas build heavily on existing work. The random gradient-free method is presented as an extension of Nesterov & Spokoiny [16] to the constrained case, and the gradient-based method for the inexact oracle setting is a generalization of the framework in [7]. The Supervised PageRank model itself is also from prior work [21]. The main novelty lies in the careful adaptation of these methods to the constrained setting and the specific inexact oracle derived from the stationary distribution approximation. The paper could be strengthened by more clearly delineating its specific theoretical advancements over these foundational works.

2.  **Positioning of Contribution:** The contribution could be framed more sharply. The paper presents a solution to the Supervised PageRank problem, but the core technical contributions are in optimization theory (methods for constrained optimization with a particular class of inexact oracles). It would be beneficial to present the optimization problem in a more general context initially to better highlight how the proposed algorithms advance the state-of-the-art in optimization before narrowing the focus to the specific application.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   The proofs appear to be correct, although I did not check them in full detail. The theoretical analysis is the strongest part of the paper.
*   To address the originality concern, I would recommend that the authors explicitly discuss how their analysis for constrained optimization with a known error bound `δ` differs from or extends previous work on inexact oracles, such as that of Devolder et al. [25]. Highlighting the specific challenges introduced by the constraints or the nature of the error `δ` would make the contribution clearer.
*   The conclusion section is quite brief. It would be helpful to expand it to better summarize the key findings, reiterate the importance of theoretical guarantees in this learning problem, and perhaps suggest future research directions, such as extending the framework to other graph-based learning models with computationally intensive objectives.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a solid, well-supported contribution to the problem of learning graph-based ranking functions. The main strength is the rigorous theoretical analysis that provides convergence guarantees where none previously existed. The primary weakness is that the core optimization techniques are adaptations of existing methods, which slightly tempers the novelty. Nevertheless, the adaptation is non-trivial and the resulting framework is both theoretically sound and practically useful.
