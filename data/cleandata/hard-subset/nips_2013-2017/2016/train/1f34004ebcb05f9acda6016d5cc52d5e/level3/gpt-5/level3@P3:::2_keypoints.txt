- Paper formulates supervised PageRank learning as constrained non-convex optimization with feature-weighted restart/transition and a pairwise hinge-squared loss on query-specific graphs.

- Main algorithmic contribution: two-level inexact-oracle scheme — (i) gradient-free randomized-sphere smoothing with inexact PageRank evaluations, and (ii) adaptive projected gradient using an inexact first-order oracle.

- Provides iteration and arithmetic-operation complexity bounds coupling oracle accuracy (δ), smoothing parameter (τ), inner PageRank iteration counts (N, N1, N2), and outer iteration complexity, with inner–outer trade-off analysis.

- Builds on supervised random-walk/ranking literature and inexact/zero-/first-order optimization theory, using Nesterov–Nemirovski’s PageRank solver for inner computations.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of the learning problem and non-convex constrained objective.
- Two complementary solution strategies with provable complexity under inexact oracles.
- Adaptive inexact first-order method for non-convex composite problems is a useful, theoretically-backed tool.
- Valuable analysis of inner–outer accuracy/runtime trade-offs often omitted in prior work.
- Empirical evaluation uses real industrial (Yandex) query-dependent graphs.

Major weaknesses / required fixes:
- Key experimental results are mostly relegated to the supplement; main paper lacks essential quantitative tables, learning curves, runtime and efficiency metrics required for assessment.
- Theorem 1 assumes convexity while the target objective is non-convex; assuming local convexity on an a priori unknown ball Φ is circular and needs clarification or verifiable sufficient conditions.
- Theorem 3 bounds MK(xK − xK+1) but does not explicitly relate this to standard stationarity measures (e.g., ||∇f|| or prox-gradient); the mapping or a corollary should be provided or claims softened.
- Gradient-free method appears slow in supplemental plots; paper must report wall-clock time, inner PageRank iteration counts, total arithmetic ops, and fair stopping-criteria comparisons to justify practical value.

Other clarity/quality notes:
- Theoretical components are generally sound but need clarification regarding convexity assumptions and stationarity interpretation.
- Over-reliance on supplementary material (for experiments and proofs) impairs verification; notation is dense — include a symbol table and report practical choices for δ, τ, L0 and concrete N, N1, N2.

Originality and significance:
- Novel combination of inexact-oracle zero-/first-order methods with supervised PageRank and end-to-end complexity trade-offs.
- Potentially significant for large-scale graph-ranking learning if empirical/runtime evidence is presented transparently.

Questions for authors:
- How should Φ be chosen to ensure convexity for Theorem 1; are there verifiable sufficient conditions?
- Can you provide a corollary linking ||MK(xK − xK+1)|| to a standard stationarity measure?
- What are typical values of N, N1, N2 and sensitivity to ε and τ?
- Can you report wall-clock times and oracle-call counts and include convergence vs time plots for all methods?

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contributions, practical relevance, and well-engineered two-level approach.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing core experiments in main text, theorem–assumption mismatch on convexity, unclear stationarity guarantees, and unimpressive practical performance of gradient-free method as presented.

Recommendation:
- Borderline reject unless authors move key experiments and efficiency analysis into the main paper and clarify theoretical gaps (convexity scope for Theorem 1; stationarity implication of Theorem 3); with these fixes the paper could be strong.
