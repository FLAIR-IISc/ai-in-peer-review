- Problem: learning parameters for Supervised PageRank formulated as a non-convex loss minimization incorporating node and edge features.
- Prior work [21] approximates stationary distribution and derivatives via power methods without theoretical convergence guarantees.
- Main contribution: two two-level optimization algorithms—gradient-based (GBN) and random gradient-free (GFN)—with formal convergence rate guarantees.
- Uses the "inexact oracle" framework to control function and gradient evaluation accuracy.
- Lower-level oracle: employs a linearly convergent method from [17], more efficient than the standard power method.
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis linking lower-level approximation accuracy to overall convergence rate and computational complexity.
- Empirical results: on a large-scale web ranking dataset, GBN/GFN outperform the state-of-the-art baseline in final loss value.
- Strength: first provable convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, replacing heuristic optimization with a principled approach.
- Strength: solid technical development and well-supported proofs adapting inexact-oracle optimization to a non-convex constrained problem.
- Weakness: experimental evaluation omits wall-clock runtime comparisons, limiting assessment of practical performance.
- Weakness: analysis of the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption near a local minimum, which is strong for a non-convex problem and merits discussion.
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written and structured; two-level algorithm description and theorems are clearly presented.
- Originality: novel adaptation of gradient-based and gradient-free frameworks plus the inexact-oracle viewpoint to the Supervised PageRank problem; sensible use of [17] as lower-level solver.
- Significance: advances reliability and practicality of feature-based ranking models and offers a transferable framework for other large-scale problems.
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested additions of runtime experiments and a discussion on the local convexity assumption.
