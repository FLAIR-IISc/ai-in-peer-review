This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into the classic PageRank algorithm. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a random gradient-free method (GFN). A key contribution is that both methods are designed to work with an inexact oracle, meaning they do not require precise calculations of the loss function or its gradient. Unlike the state-of-the-art method for this problem [21], the proposed algorithms come with theoretical convergence rate guarantees and complexity analyses. The lower level of their framework, which approximates the stationary PageRank distribution and its derivatives, is based on the work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. The upper level uses either the proposed adaptive gradient method or the random gradient-free method to update the model parameters. The authors provide experimental results on a real-world web page ranking task, showing that their methods outperform the existing baseline in terms of the final loss function value.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical contribution. It addresses a key weakness in the prior work on Supervised PageRank [21], which lacked convergence guarantees. The authors provide a formal analysis of convergence rates for both a gradient-based and a gradient-free method in a constrained, non-convex setting with an inexact oracle. The generalization of the random gradient-free framework [16] to constrained problems and the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (generalizing [7, 25]) are valuable and novel contributions to the optimization literature.

2.  **Significance and Practical Relevance:** Supervised PageRank is an important model for ranking problems where side information (features) is available. Providing optimization algorithms that are not only effective but also theoretically well-founded is a significant step forward. The methods' ability to handle inexact calculations is particularly relevant for large-scale problems where computing the exact stationary distribution is computationally prohibitive. The promise of more reliable and faster training for search and recommendation engines makes this work highly significant for practitioners.

3.  **Completeness of Work:** The paper presents a complete piece of research. It clearly defines the problem, develops two novel algorithmic solutions, provides detailed theoretical analyses with proofs (in the supplementary material), analyzes the computational complexity, and validates the approaches with experiments on real-world data from a commercial search engine.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is mathematically dense and can be difficult to follow due to the large number of symbols and notations. For instance, `N` is used in Lemma 1 before it is formally introduced as a parameter of Method (3.1). The introduction of many variables (`r`, `p`, `n`, `s`, `m`, `R`, etc.) without a central reference point makes the theoretical sections challenging to parse. A table of notation in the appendix would significantly improve the paper's readability and accessibility.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation, while positive, has two main limitations:
    a. **Lack of Ranking Metrics:** The paper aims to improve a *ranking* algorithm, but the experiments only compare the final *loss function value*. While minimizing the loss is the objective of the optimization, it does not guarantee improved ranking quality. The authors should include standard ranking metrics like NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) or MAP (Mean Average Precision) on the test set to demonstrate that a lower loss value translates into a tangible improvement in ranking performance. This is a critical omission for a paper focused on a learning-to-rank problem.
    b. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The proposed methods (GBN, GFN) are compared to the state-of-the-art GBP method from [21]. However, the paper explicitly states that the lower-level oracle, based on [17], is superior to the power method used in GBP. Therefore, the observed performance gains may stem from the better oracle rather than the novel upper-level optimization algorithm. A more insightful comparison would be to evaluate an augmented baseline—the GBP algorithm using the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] as its oracle. This would isolate the performance contribution of the new upper-level optimization strategies.

3.  **Convexity Assumption:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the set `Φ` and that the optimum is in its interior. While the authors acknowledge that the problem is generally non-convex and this is a local assumption, the implications of this simplification should be discussed more thoroughly. It is unclear how one would verify this condition in practice or how the algorithm would perform if the condition is not met.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   Please add a table of notation to aid the reader.
*   In Section 6, the evaluation should be expanded to include standard information retrieval metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) to directly measure the impact on ranking quality.
*   To strengthen the experimental claims, please consider adding a baseline that combines the upper-level optimization from GBP [21] with the improved lower-level oracle from [17]. This would provide a clearer picture of the benefits derived specifically from your proposed GBN and GFN algorithms.
*   In Section 6.2, it is mentioned that GBN requires fewer iterations than GBP for certain step sizes. It would be helpful to also report the total wall-clock time to convergence for all methods, as this is often the most important metric for practitioners.

In summary, this paper presents strong theoretical results for an important practical problem. The introduction of optimization methods with convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank is a significant contribution. However, the paper's impact is currently limited by a dense presentation and an experimental evaluation that does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits for the end task of ranking. Addressing the weaknesses in the experimental section would substantially strengthen the paper.
