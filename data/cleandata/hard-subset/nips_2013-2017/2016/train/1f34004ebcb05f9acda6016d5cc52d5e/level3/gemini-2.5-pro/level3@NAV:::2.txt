This paper addresses the parameter learning problem in Supervised PageRank, a powerful extension of the classic PageRank algorithm that incorporates node and edge features. The authors formulate this task as a constrained non-convex optimization problem. The core contribution is the proposal of two new optimization methods: a random gradient-free method utilizing an inexact zero-order oracle, and a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle. Building on prior work in optimization with inexact information, the paper provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which it claims were lacking for previous approaches to this problem.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work is its theoretical rigor. Applying the inexact oracle framework to the Supervised PageRank problem is a novel and significant contribution. Providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods in this challenging non-convex, constrained setting advances the theoretical understanding of learning-to-rank on graphs. The analysis is technically sound and addresses a clear gap in the literature.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
Despite its theoretical promise, the paper suffers from several major issues that significantly weaken its contribution.

1.  **Quality/Clarity:** The most critical flaw is the decision to place all experimental results in the supplementary material. A research paper must be self-contained. Without empirical validation in the main text, it is impossible to assess the practical performance, significance, or even correctness of the proposed algorithms. The paper currently reads as an incomplete theoretical report.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The justification for Theorem 1 is questionable. The theorem assumes convexity of the objective function, but the loss function in (2.4) is non-convex. The authors suggest choosing the set Φ such that the function is convex within it, but provide no guidance on how this can be guaranteed or achieved in practice. This assumption seems overly strong and undermines the result's applicability.
3.  **Clarity of Theoretical Claims:** The convergence guarantee in Theorem 3 is not clearly articulated. The theorem bounds the quantity `||MK(xK - xK+1)||`, but it is not explained whether or why this ensures the convergence of the iterates `x_k` to a stationary point. This connection should be made explicit to substantiate the convergence claims.
4.  **Significance:** According to Supplementary Figure 1, the gradient-free method (GFNM) appears to converge very slowly. A proper evaluation of practical significance requires a thorough comparison of algorithmic efficiency (e.g., wall-clock time, number of oracle calls to reach a certain accuracy). This analysis is currently missing but is crucial for practitioners.

In summary, while the theoretical direction is interesting, the paper is not ready for publication. The lack of experiments in the main body makes it incomplete, and key theoretical assumptions and claims require substantial clarification and justification.
