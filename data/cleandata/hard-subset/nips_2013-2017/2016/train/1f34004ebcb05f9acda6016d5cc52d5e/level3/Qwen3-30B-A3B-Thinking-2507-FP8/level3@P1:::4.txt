

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank (SPR) models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features for ranking tasks. The authors propose two novel optimization methods: a gradient-based method and a random gradient-free method for solving the non-convex loss-minimization problem inherent in SPR learning. The work builds upon previous SPR formulations (Zhukovskii et al., 2015) and the two-level optimization framework introduced in that work, but significantly advances the field by providing theoretical convergence guarantees that were previously missing.

The main contributions are threefold: first, the authors develop a gradient method for general constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, establishing its convergence rate to stationary points; second, they adapt the random gradient-free method (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2011) to constrained optimization with inexact oracle, proving convergence; third, they conduct a thorough trade-off analysis between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity, providing complexity bounds for both methods.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality through well-constructed proofs for convergence rates and complexity bounds. The authors carefully analyze the trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy (controlled by iteration counts in the power method) and overall computational complexity, providing explicit complexity bounds for achieving a given optimization accuracy. The experiments on a real-world web ranking task (using Yandex data) show that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art method (Zhukovskii et al., 2015) in ranking quality, with the gradient-based method showing particularly strong performance.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with well-structured mathematical formulations and clear exposition of the two-level optimization framework. The problem formulation (Section 2) is precise, and the algorithm descriptions (Sections 4-5) are sufficiently detailed for reproduction. However, the motivation for choosing specific parameter values (e.g., R=0.99 in the constraint set) could be strengthened with more discussion of how these choices affect the optimization landscape.

The originality of the work is significant. While SPR has been previously proposed (Zhukovskii et al., 2015), this paper is the first to provide convergence guarantees for optimization methods in this setting. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2011; Nesterov, 2013) by addressing constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle, a critical gap in the SPR literature. The paper also makes a valuable theoretical contribution by extending gradient-free methods to constrained problems with inexact oracle.

The significance of this work is high, both theoretically and practically. Theoretical advances in optimization with inexact oracle have broad applicability beyond SPR, and the practical results demonstrate real-world impact on ranking quality. The authors convincingly argue that their methods could improve search engine reliability and speed. The experimental results on real-world data provide strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

The main weakness of the paper is the lack of reported computational runtime for the proposed methods. While the authors provide complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), they do not report actual computational times for the algorithms on their datasets. For optimization papers, especially those proposing new methods, runtime performance is critical for assessing practical utility. The reviewers should be able to compare not just the quality of the solution but also the time required to reach it. This omission makes it difficult to fully evaluate the practical advantages of the proposed methods.

Another minor weakness is the limited discussion of how the methods scale to extremely large graphs, despite the paper's focus on "huge-scale optimization." While the complexity bounds suggest scalability, specific runtime measurements on larger graph sizes would strengthen the practical significance.

Strengths: Theoretical rigor with convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, clear differentiation from previous work, strong empirical results on a real-world ranking task, thorough complexity analysis.

Weaknesses: Lack of reported computational runtime, limited discussion of scalability to extremely large graphs, some parameter choices lack justification.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The work provides the first convergence guarantees for SPR learning optimization, addresses a significant theoretical gap in the literature, demonstrates clear empirical advantages, and makes a meaningful contribution to both optimization theory and practical ranking applications.

Con-acceptance arguments: The absence of actual computational runtime measurements weakens the practical contribution, and the paper would be stronger with additional scalability analysis.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to optimization with inexact oracle and demonstrates practical value for a well-motivated application. The theoretical advances are substantial, and the empirical results support the claims. While the lack of computational runtime is a notable omission, the paper's theoretical contributions and strong empirical results outweigh this weakness. I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016.
