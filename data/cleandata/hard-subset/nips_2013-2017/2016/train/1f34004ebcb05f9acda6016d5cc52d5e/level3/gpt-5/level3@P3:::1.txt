Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning a graph-based ranking model (Supervised PageRank) where node and edge weights are defined by feature-driven random walks. The learning objective is a non-convex loss over pairwise ordering constraints across query-specific graphs. A key challenge is that evaluating the objective and its gradient requires stationary distributions of Markov chains whose parameters depend on the model; exact values are unavailable, and standard optimization methods that require exact function/gradient values (or unbiased estimates) do not apply.

To address this, the authors propose a two-level approach. At the lower level, they use a linearly convergent method for Markov chains (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to estimate stationary distributions and, via a generalized iteration, their parameter derivatives. They show the loss can be approximated to any prescribed precision with explicit complexity bounds. At the upper level, they develop: (i) a gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization with an inexact zero-order oracle, extending Nesterov–Spokoiny-style random gradient-free smoothing to constrained sets and uniformly bounded function-value errors; and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle, generalizing Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov’s (δ,L)-oracle framework to constrained, possibly non-convex problems and unknown smoothness. They prove convergence to a stationary point and provide end-to-end complexity that trades off inner and outer accuracies.

This work builds on Supervised PageRank and supervised random walks (e.g., Zhukovskii et al.; Backstrom & Leskovec), on power/Nesterov–Nemirovski methods for PageRank computation, on inexact-oracle optimization (Devolder et al.), on composite gradient methods (Nesterov), and on random gradient-free methods (Nesterov & Spokoiny; bandit optimization).

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important setting where only inexact function/gradient information is available due to Markov-chain computations; the two-level design is well motivated.
- Lower-level analysis: clear construction of a linearly convergent stationary-distribution estimator and an extension to derivative estimation; explicit accuracy–complexity relations; proof that the loss can be approximated to any target precision.
- Upper-level algorithms: convergence guarantees for (a) a constrained gradient-free method with bounded function-value errors, and (b) an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle; proofs appear technically solid.
- Useful trade-off analysis between inner accuracy (N, N1, N2) and outer complexity; end-to-end complexity bounds with explicit dependence on graph/query parameters.
- Empirically, both proposed methods outperform a prior gradient-based baseline on a real ranking task; the gradient-based method uses fewer outer iterations than the baseline.

Weaknesses and concerns about novelty
- The optimization ideas are close to known frameworks: the gradient-free method resembles Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015) with sphere smoothing and projection; the inexact first-order analysis is conceptually similar to Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov and to non-convex analyses (e.g., Ghadimi & Lan, 2013). The novelty lies mainly in adapting these to constraints and to the specific inexactness structure where function values are computable up to known precision via Markov-chain solvers.
- The gradient-free convergence in Section 4.2 assumes convexity of f on Φ (“small vicinity”); this limits applicability to the non-convex case emphasized in the title/abstract.
- Relation to prior supervised PageRank/supervised random walks could be clarified: how do the features, loss, and training protocol advance over prior work beyond providing convergence guarantees?
- Experimental evaluation focuses on the pairwise-square loss; standard ranking metrics (NDCG/ERR) and wall-clock runtime breakdowns (lower-level vs. upper-level) would strengthen the empirical case.
- Several constants (e.g., β1) and assumptions are deferred to the supplement; some complexity expressions are opaque, which hinders reproducibility.

Quality
- The paper is technically careful overall. The reduction from inexact function/gradient approximations (Lemmas 1–2) to an inexact oracle satisfying the key inequality is convincing. The adaptive line-search style scheme in the non-convex constrained setting is well designed. I did not check every derivation in detail, but the arguments I checked are consistent.
- The empirical study is on real data and compares to a reasonable baseline. More metrics, ablations (e.g., sensitivity to δ1/δ2; N vs. N1/N2), and runtime reporting would improve completeness.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; notation is heavy but mostly consistent. Providing intuition for the lower-level estimators and a small schematic of the two-level flow would help readability.
- Make the general optimization problems explicit up front, then instantiate with Supervised PageRank; this would better position the contributions relative to state of the art.
- Some definitions (e.g., β1), norm choices, and assumptions (local convexity, bounded diameters, positivity constraints on ϕ) should be stated prominently in the main text.
- The paper would benefit from a stronger concluding discussion distilling when to prefer gradient-free vs. gradient-based methods; if a conclusion was missing in earlier drafts, please add one.

Originality
- Moderate. The main technical novelty is adapting inexact-oracle gradient approaches to constrained problems with computable-up-to-precision function values, and integrating them with a linearly convergent Markov-chain estimator. The optimization components are evolutions of known techniques.

Significance
- Practical and methodological significance is reasonable: many learning problems over graphs face similar inexactness. The guarantees and complexity trade-offs are useful and could be adopted in related applications. The incremental nature of the optimization contributions tempers the impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can the gradient-free method be extended to provable convergence to stationary points under non-convexity (e.g., via smoothing/Moreau envelopes)?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of α and to the radius R of Φ? Any automatic tuning?
- Are the lower-level derivative approximations stable in ill-conditioned cases (e.g., nearly reducible chains)?
- Can you report NDCG/ERR and wall-clock times split between inner and outer loops?

Suggestions for improvement
- Present the optimization framework first in a general form; explicitly compare to Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), Devolder et al. (2014), Ghadimi & Lan (2013), and clarify the differences.
- Expose constants and assumptions (β1, Lipschitz bounds) in the main text; simplify/compress complexity expressions and discuss dominant terms in typical regimes.
- Add standard ranking metrics, ablations on inner accuracies, and runtime breakdowns; compare to alternative PageRank solvers (e.g., Monte Carlo) if relevant.
- Clarify the status of the conclusion; add a succinct section highlighting takeaways and limitations.

Pros
- Well-motivated two-level approach with end-to-end guarantees
- Solid lower-level estimators and upper-level inexact-oracle analyses
- Clear trade-off analysis; real-data experiments show gains

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established inexact-oracle and gradient-free frameworks
- Convexity assumption for gradient-free method; empirical evaluation could be richer
- Some technical details/assumptions pushed to the supplement

Overall: A solid and careful paper with moderate novelty and practical relevance. I lean toward borderline accept if the authors clarify novelty, strengthen experiments, and sharpen the presentation as suggested.
