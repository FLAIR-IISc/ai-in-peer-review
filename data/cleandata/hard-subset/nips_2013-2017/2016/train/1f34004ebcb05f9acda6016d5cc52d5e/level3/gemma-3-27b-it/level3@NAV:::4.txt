## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a significant task in ranking and information retrieval. The authors propose both gradient-based and random gradient-free methods for solving the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem. A key contribution is providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a notable improvement over existing approaches like [21], which lack such guarantees. The work builds on established concepts like PageRank and random walks, extending them to incorporate node and edge features, as seen in previous work at NIPS (e.g., [12, 20, 21]). The authors appropriately position their work within this existing literature.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical contributions. Providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in this context is a valuable achievement. The careful analysis of the trade-off between lower-level algorithm accuracy and overall computational complexity is also commendable. Furthermore, the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach in ranking quality, reinforcing the practical relevance of the work. The paper is generally well-written and the logical flow is clear, though dense at times due to the mathematical derivations.

However, a significant weakness is the lack of reporting runtime (computational time) for the proposed optimization methods. While the authors analyze computational complexity, knowing the actual runtime on the datasets used would be crucial for assessing the practical scalability of their approach. Comparing the runtime performance against the baseline method [21] would be particularly informative. Additionally, while the theoretical analysis is thorough, the discussion of the limitations of the methods – particularly regarding the convexity assumption – could be expanded.  The dependence of performance on hyperparameter settings, beyond the mentioned exploration of L, could also be discussed in more detail.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical results: Convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
*   Improved performance: Empirically outperforms the state-of-the-art method.
*   Sound methodology: Builds upon well-established techniques in ranking and optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Missing runtime analysis: Lack of reported computational time hinders practical assessment.
*   Limited discussion of limitations: Could benefit from a more thorough discussion of assumptions and potential drawbacks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of supervised ranking with a strong theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. However, the absence of runtime information is a significant omission.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors including a section detailing the computational time of their methods and a discussion of the practical implications of their complexity analysis. Addressing the points related to convexity and hyperparameter sensitivity would further strengthen the paper.
