This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models with node and edge features, formulating it as a non-convex loss minimization problem. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods, built upon the concept of an inexact oracle, and provide convergence rate guarantees for both. Experimental evaluation on a web page ranking task demonstrates the performance of the proposed methods compared to a state-of-the-art approach.

The work builds upon the existing Supervised PageRank framework [21], aiming to overcome the lack of convergence guarantees in previous gradient-based methods. The authors correctly identify the challenges associated with this problem, including the non-convexity of the loss function, constraints on the parameter vector, and the difficulty in calculating the exact loss function value. The proposed approach of utilizing inexact oracle information and providing convergence guarantees is a valuable contribution. The connection to existing optimization techniques, such as those in [1, 7, 16, 17, 23, 24], is well established.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** Providing convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant strength, especially in the context of non-convex optimization.
*   **Inexact Oracle:** The use of an inexact oracle is a pragmatic approach to dealing with the computational challenges of evaluating the loss function and its gradients.
*   **Two Approaches:** Offering both gradient-based and gradient-free methods provides flexibility and allows users to choose the most appropriate method for their specific needs. The authors highlight trade-offs between the two, such as the gradient-based method's lower input parameter count versus the gradient-free method avoiding derivative calculations.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem and the assumptions made, facilitating understanding and potential extension of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Results in Supplement:** The most significant weakness is that all experimental results are relegated to the supplementary material. This makes the paper incomplete and prevents a proper assessment of the empirical performance of the proposed methods within the main body of the paper. A concise presentation of key results is essential for a NIPS submission.
*   **Convexity Assumption:** Theorem 1, which underlies the gradient-free method, assumes convexity. However, the problem formulation (specifically loss function 2.4) is explicitly stated as non-convex. The paper lacks a clear justification for this assumption or a discussion of how the choice of the feasible set Φ (the constraint set for parameters ϕ) ensures convexity within that set. This requires clarification.
*   **Convergence Criterion:** Theorem 3 provides a bound on `MK(xK - x{K+1})`, but it is unclear if and how this bound guarantees convergence of `xk` to a stationary point. A more direct connection between the bound and convergence to a solution is needed.
*   **Algorithmic Efficiency:** Supplementary Figure 1 suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of algorithmic efficiency and a comparison of computational costs between the proposed methods and the baseline. Reporting and analyzing runtime performance is crucial.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a challenging optimization problem in the context of Supervised PageRank. The provision of convergence guarantees is a significant contribution. However, the critical weakness of placing all experimental results in the supplement, combined with the unclear convexity assumption and the limited discussion of algorithmic efficiency, prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical novelty in providing convergence guarantees for gradient-based and gradient-free methods in this context.
*   The inexact oracle approach addresses a practical challenge in the optimization of Supervised PageRank.
*   Potential for impact in areas reliant on ranking algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of experimental results in the main paper makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed methods.
*   The convexity assumption in Theorem 1 is questionable given the non-convex nature of the problem.
*   Insufficient analysis of algorithmic efficiency and runtime performance.
*   The connection between the theoretical bound in Theorem 3 and actual convergence is unclear.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize including a concise summary of the experimental results in the main paper, clarifying the convexity assumption, and providing a more detailed analysis of algorithmic efficiency. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and improve its chances of acceptance. The supplement should be referenced in the main text, but the core results should be present for a full evaluation by the committee.
