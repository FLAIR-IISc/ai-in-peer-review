This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, extending the standard PageRank algorithm to incorporate node and edge features through a non-convex loss minimization framework. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-free method leveraging an inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method utilizing an inexact first-order oracle. A core contribution is the provision of theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in prior work on this problem, specifically the state-of-the-art approach presented in [21]. The paper concludes with experimental validation on a web page ranking task, demonstrating improved performance compared to existing methods.

The work builds upon the established foundation of PageRank and its variants [18, 11, 8, 9, 19], which rely on random walk models. It differentiates itself by incorporating node and edge features, drawing from a line of research aiming to enrich ranking algorithms with such information [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21, 22]. The paper directly addresses the challenges associated with optimizing the Supervised PageRank model [21], where existing methods lack convergence guarantees due to the non-convexity of the loss function and the imprecise nature of the estimations involved.  The authors rightly point out that existing optimization methods ([16, 7]) are unsuitable due to parameter constraints and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. 

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical contributions. Providing convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods applied to this problem is a significant advancement. The detailed analysis and derivation of convergence rates, even in the presence of inexact oracles, demonstrate a rigorous mathematical treatment of the problem.  The exploration of the trade-off between lower-level algorithm accuracy and overall computational complexity is also valuable. Furthermore, the experimental results, while presented in supplementary material, suggest that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in ranking quality.

However, the paper has several weaknesses.  The most significant is the placement of all experimental results in the supplementary material. This makes the main paper feel incomplete and hinders a thorough evaluation of the proposed methods’ practical performance.  A clear presentation of results within the main body is crucial for a strong NIPS submission.

Regarding the theoretical analysis, Theorem 1, underpinning the gradient-free method, explicitly assumes convexity. The problem being addressed, however, is formulated as a non-convex optimization. The authors need to clearly justify how the choice of the feasible set Φ can guarantee the convexity required for Theorem 1 to hold, or acknowledge this limitation and discuss its implications.  Similarly, Theorem 3 provides a bound on  MK(xK - x{K+1}), but it's not immediately clear how this bound translates to convergence of the sequence x_k to a stationary point of the objective function. A more explicit connection between the bound and convergence properties would strengthen the theoretical argument.

The discussion of algorithmic efficiency is also lacking. Supplementary Figure 1 indicates that the gradient-free method converges slowly. A more detailed comparison of the computational cost and runtime of the proposed methods, along with the state-of-the-art, is necessary to fully assess their practicality. Reporting algorithmic efficiency in terms of iterations and runtime would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to solving a challenging optimization problem relevant to ranking and network analysis.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable convergence guarantees, a key contribution missing in prior work.
*   Experimental results (though in the supplement) demonstrate improved ranking performance compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   The exploration of the accuracy-complexity trade-off is insightful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The placement of all experimental results in the supplementary material significantly weakens the paper.
*   The assumption of convexity in Theorem 1 is questionable given the non-convex formulation of the problem.
*   The connection between the convergence bound in Theorem 3 and actual convergence to a stationary point is unclear.
*   The paper lacks a thorough analysis of algorithmic efficiency and runtime performance.



**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound contribution to the field of ranking algorithms and optimization.  However, the current presentation falls short of NIPS standards due to the incomplete presentation of experimental results and some ambiguities in the theoretical analysis. I recommend **rejection** with the strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the aforementioned weaknesses, particularly incorporating the experimental results into the main body of the paper and clarifying the convexity assumption and convergence arguments. The theoretical contributions are promising, and a more complete and well-presented paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
