## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach (GBN) and a gradient-free approach (GFN). The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in prior work like [21]. Both methods cleverly avoid computing large matrices, making them scalable, and empirical results demonstrate superior ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art. The paper builds upon the framework established by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17], extending it to this specific problem.

The strengths of this work are significant. The theoretical guarantees are a major plus, providing a solid foundation for the proposed methods. The avoidance of large matrix computations is crucial for scalability, a common bottleneck in PageRank-related algorithms. The empirical results, while presented on datasets not publicly available, are promising and suggest a practical benefit. The detailed method descriptions in Sections 2-5 and the comprehensive proofs in the supplementary material enhance reproducibility and allow for thorough scrutiny. The inclusion of hyperparameter settings is also commendable.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The sheer number of symbols used throughout makes the paper difficult to follow, and a symbol table is *essential*. The experiments only compare GBN with GBP on the loss function, not directly on ranking quality, which is the ultimate goal. Given that the work generalizes the approach in [17], a direct comparison with that reference is crucial to demonstrate the improvements achieved. While the datasets are described adequately, their lack of public availability hinders independent verification of the results. Furthermore, the discussion of the theoretical convergence rates could be more accessible to a broader audience; a more intuitive explanation would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contributions with convergence guarantees.
*   Practical improvements in ranking quality demonstrated empirically.
*   Scalable methods avoiding large matrix computations.
*   Well-documented methods and detailed proofs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a symbol table significantly hinders readability.
*   Insufficient experimental comparison with relevant baselines (specifically [17] and ranking metrics).
*   Datasets are not publicly available.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical results and empirical performance are promising. However, addressing the issues of readability (symbol table) and experimental completeness (comparison with [17] on ranking metrics) would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these points.
