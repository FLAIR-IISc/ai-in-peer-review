## Review of “Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods”

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a ranking framework that incorporates node and edge features. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to minimize the non-convex loss function associated with this learning problem. A key contribution is the provision of theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach [21]. The paper concludes with experimental validation on a web page ranking task, demonstrating performance improvements over existing methods.

The work builds upon the established foundation of PageRank [18] and its extensions [8, 9, 19], recognizing the limitations of structure-based ranking and the need to incorporate node and edge properties [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21]. Supervised PageRank [21] introduces a two-level optimization approach, which the authors aim to improve. The paper explicitly acknowledges previous difficulties in achieving convergence guarantees for gradient-based methods due to imprecise estimations during the lower-level power method calculations. The authors position their work as addressing this gap, alongside the limitations of existing optimization techniques [16, 7] when applied to this constrained, non-convex problem. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The provision of theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant strength. This distinguishes the work from prior art [21] and offers a stronger foundation for understanding the algorithms’ behavior.
* **Addressing a Limitation of Existing Work:** The paper directly tackles the lack of convergence proofs for the state-of-the-art method [21], a crucial issue for practical application and theoretical understanding.
* **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors present both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, offering a broader toolkit for tackling the problem and allowing for a trade-off analysis between their respective advantages.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation on a real-world web ranking task provides evidence of the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the baseline [21].
* **Detailed Analysis & Lemmas:** The paper provides thorough analysis, including lemmas and proofs to support their claims regarding approximation accuracy and complexity bounds. This adds significant rigor to the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the paper provides complexity analysis, a more direct comparison of *runtime* (computational time) between the proposed methods and the baseline [21] would be highly valuable. The current presentation of complexity in terms of arithmetic operations isn't immediately intuitive for practical users. The reviewer recommends reporting the runtime in seconds or minutes for the experiments.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions the impact of the Lipschitz constant L in the gradient-free method (and L0 in GBN), but a more detailed exploration of parameter sensitivity and guidelines for choosing appropriate values would be beneficial.  A figure showing performance variation with different L values, similar to Figure 2 in the supplementary materials, would be helpful in the main text.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The numerous equations and lemmas can be dense, and a more consistent and explanatory use of notation might improve readability. For instance, clarifying the meaning of certain variables used in lemmas would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The experimental comparison is primarily focused on the baseline [21]. Exploring performance against other relevant ranking algorithms (e.g., learning-to-rank methods) could provide a broader context for the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of supervised ranking.
* The convergence guarantees for both proposed methods are a significant advancement over existing work.
* The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements in ranking performance.
* The paper is well-written and thoroughly analyzed, with detailed proofs and lemmas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a direct runtime comparison hinders a complete assessment of the practical efficiency of the methods.
* The parameter sensitivity analysis could be more comprehensive.
* The experimental evaluation could be broadened to include more diverse ranking algorithms.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the area of supervised ranking. The theoretical rigor and empirical validation are commendable. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Rating:** Accept (Strongly Consider)

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer’s concerns regarding the inclusion of runtime comparisons and a more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity. Providing this additional information will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the research community.
