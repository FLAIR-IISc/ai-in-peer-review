This paper tackles learning Supervised PageRank with query-dependent node/edge features by framing it as a constrained, nonconvex loss minimization over the feature weights ϕ. The main contribution is two two-level algorithms that use inexact oracles: (i) a gradient-based method (GBN) built on an adaptive projected gradient framework for nonconvex composite objectives with inexact first-order oracle; and (ii) a gradient-free method (GFN) using random-direction finite differences on the Euclidean sphere with bounded, adversarial zero-order noise. The inner level uses the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski Markov chain solver [17] (and its extension for sensitivities) to approximate the stationary distribution πq(ϕ) and its Jacobian with provable accuracy-cost trade-offs. The outer level leverages these accuracy guarantees to prove convergence-rate bounds for both GBN (to a stationary point) and GFN (to ε-optimality under local convexity). In contrast to the earlier supervised PageRank optimization [21], which employed power methods and had no guarantees under inexact gradients, this work provides explicit complexity bounds and analyzes the interplay between inner accuracy and overall runtime. Experiments on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset show lower loss than the state-of-the-art gradient baseline (GBP) and the untuned model.

Relation to prior work:
- Supervised PageRank and supervised random walks [21, WSDM’11 Backstrom & Leskovec] motivate incorporating features into restart/transition probabilities. Prior learning methods relied on power iterations with approximate gradients and lacked convergence proofs.
- The inner solver builds on [17] to approximate stationary distributions and extends it to derivatives, replacing classical power methods [18] and enabling accuracy control via Lemmas 1–2.
- For optimization, the paper adapts random gradient-free methods [16] to constrained problems with biased inexact zero-order evaluations and extends inexact-first-order frameworks (e.g., [25]) to nonconvex constrained settings with an adaptive step-size scheme.

Quality:
- The theoretical development is careful: the inexact oracle inequalities are consistent with the Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov framework and are adapted to nonconvexity via stationarity guarantees (Theorem 3). The gradient-free analysis (Theorem 1) uses sphere-randomization and yields explicit bias-variance terms in τ and δ. Lemmas 1–2 cleanly connect inner iteration counts (N, N1, N2) to upper-level oracle tolerances and derive arithmetic operation counts.
- Assumptions are transparent: GBN targets stationary points for general nonconvex f, while GFN requires local convexity in the chosen ball Φ. The trade-off analysis between inner accuracy and total complexity is a strong point.
- Empirically, the methods regularly improve the loss vs. GBP and the untuned baseline, and the authors report some sensitivity analyses for L and ε. However, the evaluation uses only the surrogate loss (pairwise squared hinge over Aqπq)+; core ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP, pairwise accuracy) are not reported, making it hard to assess practical ranking gains. Wall-clock time and memory comparisons (especially inner solver costs vs. power method) are also missing in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is dense and symbol-heavy. A consolidated symbol table would significantly improve readability. Some notation appears before being defined (e.g., N) and several constants (e.g., β1) are introduced late or in the supplement. Norm choices switch between ℓ1/ℓ2 and matrix norms; a brief summary of norms and their roles would help. Minor typos are present.
- The two-level algorithmic flow is clear once the reader reaches Sections 3–5; an overview figure could help orient readers earlier.

Originality:
- Extending gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with uniformly bounded, possibly adversarial zero-order noise on the sphere is novel and practically relevant.
- The adaptive gradient approach with inexact first-order oracle for nonconvex constrained problems, with backtracking on the oracle “Lipschitz” parameter, is a useful generalization of prior convex results.
- Extending [17] to compute derivatives with accuracy guarantees and integrating it systematically into upper-level convergence proofs is a meaningful technical contribution.

Significance:
- For large-scale graph ranking, the ability to decouple inner approximation accuracy from outer convergence and to avoid full Jacobian computations (via GFN) is valuable. The methods could be adopted beyond supervised PageRank in any setting where objectives are only approximately evaluable via Markov chain solvers or simulators.
- That said, the empirical section would be more convincing with standard IR metrics, runtime/throughput comparisons, and broader baselines.

Specific suggestions and questions:
- Report NDCG/MAP and/or pairwise accuracy in addition to the loss. The current loss improvements may or may not translate into ranking quality.
- Provide wall-clock time, number of inner iterations, and memory for power method vs. [17] and for GBN vs. GFN vs. GBP, along with sensitivity to α and to the radius R of Φ.
- Clarify fairness of the GBP baseline (step-size schedule, stopping, and inner accuracy). Could GBP be strengthened by using [17] instead of the power method?
- Since the work builds on and generalizes [17], include a direct empirical comparison on inner-solver performance and its effect on outer convergence (some evidence is mentioned in the supplement; please surface it in the main text).
- Reproducibility: Although the dataset is proprietary, the description is fairly detailed. Consider releasing code and synthetic or public benchmarks to facilitate replication.
- Exposition: add a symbol table; define all variables upon first use; unify notation for norms; move key constants (e.g., β1) to the main text; add a high-level algorithm schematic.

Pros:
- Solid theoretical framework for inexact oracles with explicit convergence and complexity bounds.
- Two practical algorithms (gradient-based and gradient-free) that exploit inner solver accuracy-cost trade-offs.
- Clear advancement over [21] by providing guarantees and improved performance.
- Applicability beyond the specific ranking problem.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation focuses on the surrogate loss; lacks standard ranking metrics and runtime/memory comparisons.
- Limited baselines; fairness of GBP tuning and inner solver choices not fully established.
- Heavy notation and some clarity issues; key details deferred to the supplement.
- Local convexity assumption for GFN limits its stated guarantees.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are strong and relevant to NIPS, and the application to supervised PageRank is timely. Strengthening the empirical section with ranking metrics, runtime, and a clearer comparison to [17]/power methods would make the case more compelling.
