### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters for Supervised PageRank models, which enhance traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The learning problem is formulated as the minimization of a non-convex loss function. A key challenge is that the loss function and its gradient depend on the stationary distribution of a Markov process, which can only be approximated iteratively. Consequently, any optimization algorithm must operate with an "inexact oracle." The state-of-the-art method for this problem [21] lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to this inexactness.

The main contribution of this paper is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms that explicitly handle the inexact oracle and come with formal convergence rate guarantees. The first is an adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex constrained problems, extending the work of [7]. The second is a random gradient-free method adapted from [16] for constrained problems where only noisy function values are available. For the lower-level oracle (approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives), the authors employ the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17], which provides explicit error bounds. The paper provides a full theoretical analysis of the complexity of the proposed two-level algorithms and demonstrates their empirical superiority over the state-of-the-art method on a real-world web page ranking task.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The primary strength is the rigorous theoretical treatment of the optimization problem. By formalizing the concept of an inexact oracle and deriving convergence rates for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, the paper places the learning of Supervised PageRank on solid theoretical footing. This is a significant advance over the previous heuristic approach [21]. The theoretical analysis correctly accounts for the trade-off between the computational effort at the lower level (oracle accuracy) and the number of iterations at the upper level (optimization). The experimental results on a large-scale, real-world dataset are compelling and support the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed methods achieve a lower loss than the baseline.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While the paper compares the final loss function values and convergence in terms of iterations (Figure 1), it omits a crucial practical detail: the wall-clock computation time. The theoretical complexity analysis is valuable, but reporting the actual runtime of the GFN, GBN, and GBP methods would provide a much clearer picture of their practical efficiency. For instance, the gradient-free method avoids costly derivative calculations at the lower level, but may require more upper-level iterations. A direct comparison of runtime would help practitioners understand these trade-offs and choose the most suitable algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation is laid out very clearly in the introduction, highlighting the limitations of the existing method. The paper does a good job of separating the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2). The mathematical notation is consistent and the problem setup is formally defined.
*   **Suggestions for improvement:** While clear, the paper is quite dense. A bit more high-level intuition, particularly in Sections 4 and 5, explaining why these specific optimization frameworks are well-suited to the structure of the Supervised PageRank problem, could improve readability for a broader audience.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes several novel contributions.
*   **Strengths:** The main novelty lies in the synthesis of recent advances in optimization theory (methods for inexact oracles and non-convex problems) with a practical and important application in web search. While the core optimization algorithms build upon existing ideas [7, 16, 25], their adaptation to the constrained, non-convex setting of Supervised PageRank, complete with a full complexity analysis that links the upper and lower optimization levels, is a non-trivial and original contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide provably convergent algorithms for this problem.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Strengths:** For researchers in graph-based learning and ranking, this paper provides a robust and theoretically-grounded toolbox for learning models like Supervised PageRank. The improved reliability and performance demonstrated in the experiments are of clear practical value. Furthermore, the general frameworks for optimization with inexact first-order and zero-order oracles are of broad interest. They can be applied to other large-scale learning problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are computationally prohibitive, making the paper relevant to a wide audience at NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first provably convergent optimization algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a major gap in the existing literature.
2.  **Generalizable Frameworks:** The proposed optimization methods for constrained problems with inexact oracles are of broader interest and potential impact beyond the specific application.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The methods are shown to outperform the state-of-the-art on a large-scale, real-world web ranking task, demonstrating practical relevance.
4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents a complete piece of work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The lack of runtime (wall-clock time) comparisons makes it difficult to fully assess the practical efficiency and trade-offs of the proposed methods against the baseline.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution by bridging theory and practice. The theoretical results are solid, and the empirical validation is promising. The main weakness is the lack of runtime reporting in the experiments, which I strongly encourage the authors to add to the final version. Despite this, the strengths of the paper in providing the first provably convergent methods for this important problem are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
