### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a graph-based ranking model where the transition and restart probabilities are functions of node and edge features. The authors correctly point out that the state-of-the-art gradient-based method for learning the model's parameters [21] lacks theoretical convergence guarantees, as it relies on inexact gradient estimations from the power method.

The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. The core idea is to frame the problem using the concept of an "inexact oracle." On the lower level, a linearly convergent method [17] is used to approximate the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) up to a desired accuracy. On the upper level, this inexact information is fed into either a gradient-free or an adaptive gradient-based optimization algorithm, for which the authors provide a rigorous analysis of convergence and computational complexity. The methods are evaluated on a web page ranking task using a real-world dataset.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. By providing convergence guarantees for learning a non-convex Supervised PageRank model, it fills a notable gap in the existing literature. The complexity analysis, which connects the accuracy of the low-level approximation to the overall convergence rate, is novel and valuable.

2.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem of learning feature-rich ranking functions on graphs is important and has many practical applications, particularly in web search and recommendation systems. Addressing the theoretical shortcomings of existing learning algorithms is a solid contribution to this area.

3.  **Sound Methodological Framework:** The use of an "inexact oracle" is a fitting and elegant way to handle the challenge that the loss function and its gradients cannot be computed exactly. The paper develops this framework for both gradient-free and gradient-based settings, offering flexibility and exploring different trade-offs.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is difficult to follow. There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that hinder readability. More importantly, the paper relegates almost all of the crucial technical details—including full proofs, complete algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 2 and 4 are in the supplement), and detailed experimental results—to the supplementary materials. While this is common, the main paper lacks even high-level proof sketches or a sufficiently detailed experimental summary, making it feel incomplete and hard to evaluate as a standalone document. For instance, the paper uses inconsistent naming for the model from [21], calling it "Supervised PageRank" in the text but "Supervised Nested PageRank" in the reference list.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is a major weakness and does not adequately support the paper's claims of practical impact.
    *   **Missing Details:** Critical hyperparameters and experimental design choices are not reported. For example, the choice of the initial parameter vector `ϕ̂` and the radius `R` for the feasible set `Φ` is fundamental to the algorithm, yet there is no explanation of how these were selected. This makes the results difficult to interpret and impossible to reproduce.
    *   **Superficial Analysis:** The paper claims that its methods "significantly outperform" the baseline but provides very little analysis. Table 1 shows final loss values, but there is no discussion of the magnitude of the improvement in terms of actual ranking quality (e.g., using metrics like NDCG or MAP). Figure 1 is mentioned but not analyzed in depth.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset. It is unclear if the results would generalize to other graphs with different structures or feature sets.

3.  **Unrealistic or Undiscussed Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions whose practical implications are not discussed. The gradient-free method's analysis requires local convexity, which may not hold. More critically, the constraint that the solution `ϕ` must lie within a Euclidean ball of radius `R` around a pre-selected `ϕ̂` could severely limit the model's effectiveness. This is a strong constraint, and the paper fails to justify its use or analyze its potential impact on finding a good ranking model.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for:**
*   Provides the first formal convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The inexact oracle framework is well-suited for this problem and is analyzed rigorously for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
*   The problem being solved is of practical importance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against:**
*   The empirical evaluation is weak, lacks critical details for reproducibility, and is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed methods.
*   The paper's clarity is poor, and its reliance on the supplementary material makes the main text feel incomplete.
*   The practical applicability is questionable due to strong, undiscussed assumptions, particularly the constraint of the parameter search space to a small ball.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically strong piece of work but fails to connect its theoretical contributions to demonstrated practical impact. The weak empirical validation and poor presentation are significant drawbacks.
