

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models through non-convex loss-minimization, proposing both gradient-based and random gradient-free optimization methods. The authors build upon the Supervised PageRank framework [21], which extends traditional PageRank to incorporate node and edge features. Their key theoretical contribution is providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which is lacking in the state-of-the-art [21]. The authors establish their results using the concept of an inexact oracle, carefully analyzing the trade-offs between lower-level approximation accuracy and overall computational complexity.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions by rigorously proving convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in constrained non-convex optimization settings. The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task show that their methods outperform existing approaches in ranking quality while providing theoretical guarantees. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate connections to previous work on PageRank [18, 21] and optimization methods [16, 7].

However, the paper has a significant limitation: it fails to report the computational runtime of the proposed optimization methods. While the authors mention that the gradient-free method avoids calculating derivatives for large matrices, they provide no empirical evidence of computational time savings compared to existing methods. This omission is particularly problematic for a conference like NIPS, where practical efficiency is as important as theoretical guarantees. Without runtime data, it's impossible to assess the practical trade-offs between the theoretical advantages and computational costs of the proposed algorithms.

The paper's significance is high, as it provides a theoretically grounded approach to a practical ranking problem with potential applications in search engines. The theoretical contributions to optimization with inexact oracles represent a meaningful advance in the field. 

The main weakness preventing stronger recommendation is the lack of runtime information. For a paper claiming practical utility in search engines, computational efficiency is crucial. The authors should report runtime measurements in a revised version to fully support their claims about the practical value of their approach.
