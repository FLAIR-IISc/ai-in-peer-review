This paper studies the learning of Supervised PageRank models, where node and edge transition/restart probabilities are linear functions of features, and the loss is a pairwise squared hinge over query-dependent browsing graphs. The key issue is that both the stationary distribution and its parameter derivatives must be computed approximately, yielding only inexact access to the loss and gradient. The paper proposes two two-level learning algorithms: (i) a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle and (ii) a random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, both methods estimate the stationary distribution via a linearly convergent scheme (Nesterov–Nemirovski-type averaging) rather than the classical power method. On the upper level, the paper develops convergence guarantees: to a stationary point for the gradient-based method in a constrained, non-convex setting with inexact first-order oracle; and to an ε-accurate solution for the gradient-free method in a constrained convex neighborhood with inexact zero-order oracle. Theoretical complexity bounds quantify the trade-off between lower-level accuracy (number of Markov chain iterations) and overall optimization complexity. Empirically, on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (600 queries with ∼1.7K labeled pairs), both methods outperform the state-of-the-art gradient-based baseline that relies on power-method estimates.

Relation to prior work: The learning framework builds directly on Supervised PageRank and supervised random walks (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec) and on the “intelligent surfer” family of feature-augmented random walks. On the optimization side, the paper extends inexact-oracle frameworks (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Nesterov & Spokoiny’s gradient-free smoothing; Ghadimi & Lan’s nonconvex first/zeroth-order methods) to a constrained setting with explicitly controlled oracle inaccuracies derived from the lower-level PageRank computations. The lower-level estimator derives from Nesterov–Nemirovski’s accelerated averaging for Markov chains and is argued to have superior complexity to power iterations.

Strengths:
- Addresses a practically important setting where only inexact function/gradient information is available due to approximate stationary distributions and derivative computations.
- Provides explicit convergence rates and complexity bounds for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, with careful accounting of lower-level accuracy and its effect on upper-level convergence.
- Adapts gradient-free random-smoothing methods to constrained domains with inexact zero-order oracle, and develops an adaptive projected gradient scheme with inexact first-order oracle for nonconvex composite problems.
- The lower-level estimator is linearly convergent and admits target-accuracy control, enabling principled inexact-oracle construction.
- Solid empirical evidence on a real, query-dependent browsing graph shows improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline, and the experiments explore hyperparameter sensitivity to some extent.

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- Novelty overlap: Core ideas (random smoothing for gradient-free, inexact oracle analysis, line-search/backtracking for unknown L) are close to prior theory (Nesterov & Spokoiny; Devolder et al.; Ghadimi & Lan). The main novelty is the way these are adapted to constrained problems with explicitly controlled lower-level approximation. This is valuable but should be positioned more clearly relative to those works, with a sharper comparison of assumptions and guarantees.
- Convexity assumption for the gradient-free method: Section 4.2 relies on local convexity of f(·) in Φ, whereas the overall problem is nonconvex. Please articulate clearly when this assumption holds in practice, how Φ is chosen, and what guarantees remain if convexity fails locally.
- Practical sensitivity and constants: The complexity bounds include large constants/log factors (dependence on r, m, p, |Q|, s, and 1/α). Empirical guidance on choosing ε, δ, and τ, and the effect on wall-clock time, would help practitioners. The choice L = 1e-4 for GFN seems ad hoc; justify or provide sensitivity plots in the main paper.
- Competing baselines and metrics: The experimental comparison centers on a single “GBP” baseline and the training loss. It would strengthen claims to include standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k) and additional baselines (e.g., supervised random walks, classical learning-to-rank models) and to report wall-clock runtime and memory.
- Derivative computation: While the gradient-free method avoids per-entry derivatives, the gradient-based method still requires derivative propagation through PT(ϕ). A clearer discussion of its computational cost and memory footprint at web scale would be useful.
- Presentation details: The optimization problem could be introduced first in a general inexact-oracle constrained framework, then specialized to Supervised PageRank. This would more cleanly separate general contributions from application-specific details and help clarify novelty boundaries. The conclusion could better synthesize assumptions/limitations and future work.

Quality:
- The analysis is technically careful, with explicit oracle-accuracy control and upper-level convergence proofs. The derivations for the lower-level accuracy-to-complexity mapping are appropriate. While I did not verify every proof in the supplement, the main arguments appear sound.
- Experimental methodology is reasonable but could be broadened (additional baselines, metrics, and runtime comparisons).

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. Some sections (assumptions on convexity/Lipschitz constants, parameter choices for ε, δ, τ) would benefit from clearer upfront statements and practical guidance. Moving key intuition and parameter trade-offs from the supplement into the main text would improve readability.

Originality:
- The synthesis—tying inexact-oracle methods to feature-augmented PageRank learning with explicit, controllable lower-level errors—is a useful contribution, though many components are adaptations of known techniques. The constrained, inexact-oracle extensions and the two-level complexity analysis are the most original aspects.

Significance:
- The problem is important for graph-based ranking and more broadly for learning with Markov random walks under feature parametrization. Providing provable convergence under realistic inexactness is valuable, and the empirical gains suggest practical impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the local convexity assumption and provide diagnostics to check it in practice.
- Report NDCG/Precision@k and wall-clock runtimes; add stronger baselines.
- Expand discussion of sensitivity to L, ε, τ and to restart probability α.
- Summarize key constants and complexity terms in a compact table and provide practical parameter recipes.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical framework for inexact-oracle optimization in a relevant application.
- Clear convergence guarantees and complexity accounting across the two levels.
- Empirical improvements on real data.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to existing inexact-oracle and gradient-free frameworks.
- Experimental evaluation could be broader and more rigorous in terms of baselines and metrics.
- Some reliance on local convexity and parameter choices is under-discussed.
