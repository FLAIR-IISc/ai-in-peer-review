This paper addresses the non-convex loss-minimization problem associated with learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models. The main contribution is the proposal of two two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN). These methods are designed to work with an "inexact oracle," where the objective function and its gradient cannot be computed exactly but can be approximated to a desired accuracy. This is a crucial feature for the Supervised PageRank problem, where the stationary distribution of a Markov process must be estimated iteratively.

The authors build upon the Supervised PageRank framework of Zhukovskii et al. [21] but replace the power method used for estimating the stationary distribution with a more efficient, linearly convergent method by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. On the upper level, they develop a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex problems that adapts the framework of [7], and a random gradient-free method that extends the work of [16] to constrained problems. A key contribution is providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms, a property that was missing from the previous state-of-the-art method [21]. The methods are evaluated on a web page ranking task using a large-scale dataset from a commercial search engine, where they are reported to outperform the existing method in terms of the final loss function value.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution (Originality & Quality):** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. It provides the first convergence rate guarantees for the problem of learning Supervised PageRank. By framing the problem in terms of an inexact oracle and developing algorithms with provable convergence, the authors place the learning problem on a much firmer theoretical footing than previous work. The development of a general adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle is a valuable contribution in its own right and could be of interest to the wider optimization community at NIPS.

2.  **Sound and Well-Developed Methodology (Quality):** The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The two-level optimization approach is well-motivated. The authors clearly detail how to control the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives) and how this accuracy trades off with the overall complexity of the upper-level optimization algorithm. The proofs for the main theorems, while deferred to the supplement, provide the necessary support for the paper's claims.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Promising Results (Significance):** The paper addresses a practical and important problem in web search and information retrieval. The experimental results, conducted on a large, real-world dataset, demonstrate that both proposed methods (GFN and GBN) achieve lower loss values than the state-of-the-art GBP method [21]. The gradient-free method (GFN) in particular shows a substantial improvement. This suggests that the proposed algorithms are not only theoretically sound but also practically effective.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation (Quality & Significance):** The main weakness is that the experimental evaluation focuses exclusively on the value of the loss function (Eq. 2.4) rather than on direct measures of ranking quality. The ultimate goal of a ranking algorithm is to produce a better-ordered list of results. While a lower loss is often correlated with better ranking, it is not a substitute for it. The evaluation would be far more convincing if it included standard ranking metrics like Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Mean Average Precision (MAP) on the test set. Without these metrics, it is difficult to assess the true impact of the proposed methods on the end task of ranking.

2.  **Clarity and Readability (Clarity):** The paper is mathematically dense and can be difficult to follow due to the large number of symbols and variables. For instance, the parameter `N` is used in the bound in Lemma 1 before it is explicitly defined as the number of iterations for Method 3.1. The readability could be significantly improved by adding a table of notation in the main paper or appendix to serve as a reference for the reader.

3.  **Insufficient Ablation in Experiments (Quality):** The paper uses the method from [17] for its lower-level oracle, which is noted to be more efficient than the power method used by the baseline [21]. The experiments compare the full proposed algorithms (GBN/GFN) against the full baseline algorithm (GBP). To better isolate the contribution of the proposed *upper-level* optimization strategies, it would be valuable to include a comparison where the baseline method from [21] is also equipped with the more efficient oracle from [17]. This would clarify how much of the performance gain is attributable to the new optimization algorithms versus the improved lower-level oracle.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Pro:**
    *   The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank.
    *   The proposed optimization frameworks for inexact oracles are general and of potential interest beyond this specific application.
    *   The empirical results show a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art in terms of the optimization objective on a real-world task.

*   **Con:**
    *   The experimental validation is critically incomplete because it omits standard ranking quality metrics, making it hard to judge the practical significance of the results.
    *   The paper's clarity could be improved, which currently hinders a full appreciation of its technical depth.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical core. The lack of ranking metrics in the evaluation is a significant drawback, but the theoretical contributions are novel and important for this line of research. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors include a comparison of ranking quality in the final version to fully demonstrate the significance of their work.
