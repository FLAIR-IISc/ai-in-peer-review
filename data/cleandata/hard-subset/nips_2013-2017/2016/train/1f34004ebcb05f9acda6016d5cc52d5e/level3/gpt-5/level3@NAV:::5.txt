This paper studies learning feature-based node/edge weights for PageRank-style random walks under a nonconvex loss, arguing that prior learning approaches lacked performance guarantees. The authors approximate the stationary distribution via an iterative scheme (building on Nesterov–Nemirovski) and couple this with two upper-level optimizers: (i) an adaptive gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle and (ii) a biased, random gradient-free method that tolerates uniformly bounded noise in function values. They derive lemmas that bound the error of approximate gradients, prove convergence rates and complexity bounds for both oracles, and present experiments on a query-dependent browsing graph. Much of the modeling setup and loss follow earlier “Supervised PageRank”/semi-supervised rank learning for graphs (e.g., [21], Gao et al.), while the optimization draws on inexact-oracle methods (e.g., Devolder et al.; Nesterov–Spokoiny).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful, with clear oracle definitions, bias/variance trade-offs, and complexity accounting; the derived bounds are a strength. However, core proofs and most empirical details are moved to the appendix, and several technical assumptions (local convexity in a ball around ϕ0, positivity assumptions, bounded errors) seem strong and are not validated on real data. Empirically, key parameters (e.g., choice of ϕ0, radius R, α, τ, N/N1/N2, step rules) are missing; the scope of datasets is unclear; and the claimed gains over baselines lack sufficient reporting (metrics beyond the surrogate loss, magnitude of improvements, variance, and runtime).

Clarity: Writing quality is below the NIPS bar, with grammatical issues, inconsistent notation, and sloppy citations. The paper appears to rebrand an existing framework under different terminology, and there may be a mis-citation (the work referred to as “Supervised PageRank” corresponds to semi-supervised ranking in [21]). Proof sketches and critical empirical details should be in the main text.

Originality: The main novelty lies in adapting inexact-oracle gradient and gradient-free methods to this graph-learning setting and in analyzing the bias introduced by approximate stationary distributions. The modeling and loss largely reproduce prior supervised/semi-supervised PageRank frameworks.

Significance: Theoretical bounds are potentially useful, but practical impact is not convincingly demonstrated. The constraint of optimizing within an R-ball around ϕ0 is not discussed and could limit efficacy.

Pros:
- Solid convergence and complexity analysis for inexact oracles
- Clear articulation of biased gradient-free oracle and trade-offs
- Addresses an important class of graph ranking problems

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation insufficient and underreported
- Questionable/unstated assumptions for real data; R-ball constraint unexplained
- Writing/citation issues; relation to prior work unclear
- Main technical content relegated to the appendix

Recommendation: Reject, but encourage resubmission after (i) clarifying novelty vs. [21] and related supervised random walks, (ii) integrating proof sketches and thorough experiments (multiple datasets, metrics, ablations, sensitivity to R and ϕ0, runtime), and (iii) fixing writing and citation quality.
