

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank to account for node and edge features. The authors formulate this as a non-convex loss-minimization problem and propose two novel optimization methods: a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method. The key contribution is providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, which the previous state-of-the-art method [21] lacked due to inexact oracle information in the lower-level Markov chain calculations.

The work builds on the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features through linear combinations. Previous work on learning these parameters (e.g., [21]) used a two-level gradient-based method but provided no convergence guarantees. The authors extend the framework of Nesterov's work on inexact oracles [25] to constrained non-convex optimization, developing a general gradient method with inexact oracle for non-convex problems and adapting the random gradient-free method from [16] for constrained optimization.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions: they prove convergence rates for both methods and analyze the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity. The experimental results on a web page ranking task show that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality, with the gradient-free method showing particular advantages in avoiding derivative calculations for large matrices.

Strengths:
- Theoretical soundness: The paper provides rigorous convergence rate guarantees for both optimization methods, which is a significant advancement over prior work.
- Theoretical novelty: The authors successfully adapt gradient-free and gradient-based optimization methods to the constrained non-convex setting with inexact oracle, filling an important gap in the literature.
- Practical relevance: The experiments demonstrate clear improvements in ranking quality over the state-of-the-art, with the gradient-free method showing particular advantages in avoiding derivative calculations for large matrices.
- Comprehensive analysis: The authors provide a thorough trade-off analysis between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity.

Weaknesses:
- The paper lacks runtime analysis (computational time) for the proposed methods. While the authors provide arithmetic operation counts, they do not report actual computational times, which would be valuable for practitioners to assess practical utility.
- The experimental section could be strengthened by comparing with more baselines or testing on additional datasets.
- The description of the implementation details for the lower-level Markov chain calculations could be more complete.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The convergence proofs are rigorous, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims. However, the absence of runtime measurements weakens the practical evaluation.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent, though the supplementary materials would benefit from more detailed proofs for the convergence theorems.

Originality: This work represents a significant advancement in the application of optimization methods to the Supervised PageRank learning problem. The combination of gradient-based and gradient-free methods with the inexact oracle concept for this specific problem is novel, and the paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work (including [21]).

Significance: The results have clear practical implications for web ranking systems, as the authors note. The ability to learn Supervised PageRank with convergence guarantees while improving ranking quality is significant for both theoretical and applied machine learning. The methods could potentially be applied to other graph-based ranking problems beyond the specific web ranking task studied.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank with both gradient-based and gradient-free methods.
- Demonstrates clear empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art.
- The theoretical framework is general enough to potentially apply to other constrained non-convex optimization problems with inexact oracles.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks runtime measurements for the proposed methods, making it difficult to assess practical efficiency.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset and task.
- The connection to previous NIPS work could be strengthened with more explicit comparisons to recent NIPS publications on similar optimization problems.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances both the theory and practice of learning Supervised PageRank. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the empirical improvements are significant. The primary weakness is the lack of runtime analysis, which is important for practical adoption. However, this is a minor issue given the paper's strong theoretical contributions and empirical results.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include runtime measurements for the proposed methods, as this would greatly strengthen the practical evaluation. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based learning problems.
