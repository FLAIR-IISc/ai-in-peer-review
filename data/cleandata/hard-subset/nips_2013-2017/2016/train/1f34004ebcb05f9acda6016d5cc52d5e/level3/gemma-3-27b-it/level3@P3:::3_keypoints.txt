- Proposes two two-level optimization methods (gradient-based GBN and gradient-free GFN) for learning Supervised PageRank with theoretical convergence guarantees.  
- Uses an inexact oracle to avoid exact objective evaluation and large matrix computations while providing convergence rate estimates.  
- Builds on Nesterov & Nemirovski’s two-level optimization framework, estimating loss and derivatives at the lower level and tuning parameters at the upper level.  
- Empirical results show improved ranking quality over prior state-of-the-art [21].  
- Detailed methodological descriptions and experimental setup (dataset, hyperparameters) enhance reproducibility.  
- Main strength: provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees missing in prior Supervised PageRank work.  
- Main strength: inexact oracle design enables scalability and a trade-off analysis between oracle accuracy and complexity.  
- Main strength: empirical performance demonstrates practical advantage over [21].  
- Main strength: clear methodology and experimental detail support reproducibility.  
- Main strength: appropriately builds on established optimization and ranking techniques.  
- Weakness: no symbol table—many symbols (e.g., N) introduced without definition, reducing clarity.  
- Weakness: limited experimental comparisons—focuses on loss vs. [21] and omits direct comparison to Nesterov-Nemirovski [17] despite claiming generalization.  
- Weakness: dataset is not publicly available, hindering independent verification and impact.  
- Weakness: weak justification of convexity assumption for Φ; lacks discussion or experiments under non-convexity.  
- Weakness: complexity analysis is parameter-heavy and lacks intuitive interpretation of practical costs.  
- Originality: novel combination of two-level optimization with inexact oracles and theoretical analysis for Supervised PageRank.  
- Significance: potential for substantial impact on web search/ranking due to improved quality and theoretical guarantees; framework may apply to other large-scale ML problems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical guarantees, superior empirical performance, practical scalability via inexact oracles, and clear experimental presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: readability issues from missing symbol table, incomplete baseline comparison (missing [17]), dataset unavailability, and weak convexity justification.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution with strong theory and empirics but needs clarity and broader comparisons.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—add a comprehensive symbol table, include direct comparison to [17], and release the dataset or a representative subset.
