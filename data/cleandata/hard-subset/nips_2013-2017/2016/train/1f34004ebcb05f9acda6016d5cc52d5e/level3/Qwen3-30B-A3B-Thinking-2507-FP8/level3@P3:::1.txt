

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, where node and edge weights are defined by feature-driven random walks. The authors propose two-level optimization algorithms to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem, where the loss function cannot be evaluated exactly due to the need to compute stationary distributions of Markov random walks. The work builds on prior supervised PageRank approaches [21] but extends them by providing theoretical convergence guarantees, which were previously missing.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it develops a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, showing that this approximation can achieve any prescribed precision. Second, it introduces gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for constrained non-convex problems with inexact oracles, proving convergence to stationary points. The authors demonstrate that both algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art [21] on a real ranking task, with the gradient-based method having fewer hyperparameters and the gradient-free method avoiding derivative calculations for large matrices.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with correct theoretical analysis. The proofs appear rigorous, though not all were verified in detail. The authors provide clear complexity bounds for their algorithms and demonstrate their effectiveness through experiments. The main technical contribution—adapting inexact-oracle methods to constrained non-convex optimization—is well-executed. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing superior performance on real ranking data.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear organization. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the two-level algorithm framework is well-explained. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed conclusion section summarizing the key contributions and their implications. Additionally, the optimization problem could be presented in a more general context to better clarify the relationship to state-of-the-art methods.

**Originality:** The core optimization ideas closely resemble those in Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), which is noted as a limitation by the authors. While the application to Supervised PageRank is novel, the main technical contribution is not significantly different from prior work on inexact oracles. The supervised PageRank algorithm itself also has prior work [21, 22], and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its specific contributions from these earlier approaches. This limits the novelty of the paper.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important problem in graph-based ranking where exact optimization methods are inapplicable. The proposed algorithms provide theoretical guarantees that were previously missing, which is significant for practitioners. The experimental results demonstrate practical improvements over the state-of-the-art, suggesting potential real-world impact in search engine ranking. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the limited novelty of the optimization techniques.

**Strengths:**
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank
- Well-executed theoretical analysis with clear complexity bounds
- Demonstrates practical superiority over existing methods in experiments
- Good balance between theoretical and empirical results

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited novelty of optimization techniques (resembles Nesterov & Spokoiny 2015)
- Insufficient distinction from prior work on supervised PageRank
- Missing conclusion section
- Could better contextualize the optimization problem relative to state-of-the-art

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to the practical application of inexact-oracle methods in constrained non-convex optimization, particularly for the Supervised PageRank problem. While the novelty of the core optimization techniques is limited, the paper's clear presentation, sound analysis, and empirical validation make it a solid contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the following suggestions: 1) Add a more detailed conclusion section, 2) Better contextualize the optimization problem relative to recent advances, and 3) More clearly distinguish the paper's specific contributions from prior work on supervised PageRank.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a well-motivated application of optimization techniques to a significant problem in graph-based learning, with both theoretical and empirical components that meet the conference's quality standards. The work advances the state-of-the-art by providing convergence guarantees where none existed previously for this specific problem.
