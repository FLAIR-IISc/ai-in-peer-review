### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that incorporates node and edge features into the classic PageRank algorithm. The authors frame this as a non-convex loss-minimization problem. The primary contribution is the proposal of two novel two-level optimization algorithms: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN).

These methods are designed to handle the challenge that the objective function (and its gradient) cannot be computed exactly, as it depends on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain. The two-level structure tackles this by: (1) using an efficient iterative method from Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] on the lower level to compute approximations of the stationary distribution and its derivatives to a controllable accuracy, and (2) using these approximations within an upper-level optimization algorithm to update the model parameters.

This work builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework of [21] but provides a crucial improvement: theoretical convergence rate guarantees. Unlike the prior art, which used a power method with no convergence proof for the overall learning process, the proposed GBN and GFN methods are analyzed within the framework of optimization with an "inexact oracle." The paper provides convergence rate estimates for both methods, demonstrating that a desired accuracy can be reached with a bounded number of computations. Experiments on a large-scale web ranking task suggest that these new methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art method in terms of the final loss function value.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor (Quality):** The paper's main strength is its solid theoretical foundation. It provides the first convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. By formalizing the problem in the context of an inexact oracle, the authors derive explicit convergence rates for both the gradient-based method (GBN) on non-convex problems and the gradient-free method (GFN) on locally convex problems. This is a significant advance over the heuristic nature of the previous state-of-the-art method [21].

2.  **Novel Methodological Contributions (Originality):** The paper presents novel adaptations of existing optimization frameworks. Specifically, it extends random gradient-free methods [16] to handle constrained optimization problems with a biased, inexact oracle. It also generalizes adaptive gradient methods [7] for non-convex constrained problems where the first-order oracle is inexact. The synthesis of these advanced optimization techniques with the lower-level solver from [17] to create a complete, provably convergent system for a practical machine learning problem is a noteworthy contribution.

3.  **Practical Significance:** The work addresses an important and practical problem in web search and information retrieval. The proposed algorithms are shown to be effective on a real-world dataset from a commercial search engine. The ability to provably converge to a stationary point while avoiding the computation and storage of large matrices makes these methods practical for large-scale applications. The reported empirical improvements over the baseline suggest a tangible impact on ranking quality.

4.  **Completeness:** The paper is a complete piece of work. It clearly describes the model, develops two distinct optimization algorithms, provides full theoretical analyses with proofs in the supplementary material, and validates the approaches with empirical experiments. The inclusion of hyper-parameter details aids in reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is technically dense and suffers from a lack of notational clarity, making it difficult to follow. Many symbols are used, sometimes before they are formally defined (e.g., `N` in Section 3). The exposition would be significantly improved by including a symbol table at the beginning of the methods section or in an appendix. This would greatly help the reader keep track of the numerous parameters related to graph sizes, features, and optimization settings (`p, n, s, r, m, R, N, M, ε, δ`, etc.).

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, the evaluation could be more comprehensive.
    *   **Ranking Metrics:** The comparison with the baseline GBP method is conducted solely on the basis of the final loss function value. While a lower loss is desirable, it does not directly translate to better ranking performance. The claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art in "ranking quality" should be substantiated with standard information retrieval metrics like Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or Expected Reciprocal Rank (ERR) on the test set. This would provide a more direct and convincing measure of real-world performance.
    *   **Baseline Comparisons:** The paper's optimization framework is presented as a generalization of ideas from Nesterov and others. While [17] is used for the lower level, a more direct comparison or discussion against the general optimization algorithms being extended (e.g., [7]) would be illuminating. For instance, how does the adaptive GBN method compare in practice to a non-adaptive version with a fixed step-size, which might be a more direct application of prior work? This would help to better situate the contribution within the broader optimization literature.

3.  **Convexity Assumption for GFN:** The theoretical analysis for the GFN method (Theorem 1 and 2) assumes that the objective function is convex over the optimization domain `Φ`. However, the overall loss function (2.4) is stated to be non-convex. The authors briefly mention this by assuming `Φ` is a small vicinity of a local minimum where `f(ϕ)` is convex. This critical assumption should be stated more prominently and perhaps justified, even if empirically, to clarify the scope of the GFN guarantee.

### **Final Assessment**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution by introducing two provably convergent optimization methods for the important problem of learning Supervised PageRank. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses a key limitation of prior work. The methods are practical and show promising results on a real-world task. The main weaknesses lie in the clarity of the presentation and the scope of the experimental evaluation. These are, however, addressable issues. The paper is technically sound, original, and significant, making it a solid candidate for acceptance. I would strongly encourage the authors to incorporate the suggestions above, particularly adding a symbol table and reporting standard ranking metrics, to further strengthen the paper.
