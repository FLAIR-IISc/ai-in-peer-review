This paper investigates learning Supervised PageRank models, extending the work of Zhukovskii et al. (2016) [21] by proposing gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with theoretical convergence guarantees – a key limitation of the prior work. The authors address a non-convex loss minimization problem, leveraging inexact oracle information to estimate the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk and its derivatives. They present theoretical results regarding convergence rates for both proposed methods and demonstrate improvements over the state-of-the-art (GBP [21]) on a web page ranking task.

The core idea of adapting random walk-based ranking algorithms to incorporate node and edge features is well-motivated and aligns with established research in link analysis (Page et al., 1998; Kleinberg, 1998) and supervised ranking (Backstrom & Leskovec, 2011; Liu et al., 2010).  The identified lack of theoretical guarantees for the existing gradient-based method (GBP) is a genuine issue, and the authors’ attempt to address this is commendable. The use of an inexact oracle framework is sensible given the computational challenges of calculating exact gradients for large-scale graph ranking problems.  The paper builds upon established optimization techniques (Nesterov & Polyak, 2006; Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2016) and adapts them to the specific constraints of the supervised PageRank setting.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The writing is often unclear and contains grammatical errors, hindering comprehension. Citations are occasionally imprecise – the paper repeatedly refers to “Supervised PageRank” citing Zhukovskii et al. (2016), which actually presents *semi*-supervised PageRank. This suggests a lack of thorough engagement with the cited work.  The theoretical contributions, while potentially interesting, are buried in a dense presentation with limited intuition. A significant portion of the technical details, including crucial proofs and experimental settings, is relegated to the supplementary materials, making it difficult to fully assess the validity and significance of the claims.

A primary concern is the limited empirical evaluation. The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (Yandex web browsing graphs), raising questions about the generalizability of the results. Key experimental parameters (step sizes, hyperparameter tuning procedures) are not adequately reported, and the magnitude of the performance improvements over the baseline is not clearly articulated.  The statement that the proposed methods “significantly outperform” the state-of-the-art lacks quantifiable support in the main text. The constraint imposed on the parameter space (searching within an R-ball around ϕ0) is not justified and could potentially limit the algorithm’s effectiveness; its implications are not discussed.  Additionally, the choice of ϕ0 and R is unexplained.

The technical assumptions, particularly concerning the smoothness of the loss function and the applicability of the convex approximations, may be unrealistic in practice.  The authors acknowledge that the loss function is generally non-convex, yet rely on convexity arguments within a limited neighborhood of a potential solution. The computational complexity analysis, while present, is difficult to interpret without a more detailed understanding of the implementation and the characteristics of the dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a well-motivated problem in graph ranking with potential practical relevance.
*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both proposed optimization methods, addressing a key limitation of prior work.
*   Leverages established optimization techniques (gradient-based and gradient-free) in a novel context.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Poor writing quality and imprecise citations.
*   Limited and inadequately reported empirical evaluation.
*   Unclear justification for key assumptions and parameter choices.
*   Significant technical details relegated to supplementary materials.
*   Potential limitations of the imposed constraint on the parameter space.
*   Lack of diverse datasets for testing.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results are a valuable contribution to the understanding of optimization methods for supervised PageRank.
*   The empirical results, although limited, suggest that the proposed methods can outperform existing approaches.
*   The problem is relevant to a broad audience in machine learning and information retrieval.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The paper's clarity and presentation are significantly hampered by poor writing and lack of detail.
*   The limited empirical evaluation casts doubt on the practical significance of the proposed methods.
*   The technical assumptions may be unrealistic in real-world applications.
*   The dependence on supplementary materials makes a thorough review difficult.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the theoretical contributions are potentially valuable, the significant weaknesses in presentation, experimental validation, and justification of assumptions outweigh the strengths. The paper needs substantial revisions to address these concerns before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. A more detailed and well-documented empirical evaluation, a clearer exposition of the technical details, and a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach are essential.
