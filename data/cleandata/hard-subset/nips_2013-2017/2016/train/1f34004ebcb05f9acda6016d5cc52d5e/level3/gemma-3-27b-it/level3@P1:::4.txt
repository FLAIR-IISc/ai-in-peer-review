## Review of Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, extending the standard PageRank approach to incorporate node and edge features. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to tackle the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem. A key contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in the existing state-of-the-art gradient-based approach [21]. The paper concludes with empirical evaluation on a web page ranking task, demonstrating improved performance compared to the baseline.

The work builds upon the foundation of PageRank and its variants [18, 11, 8, 9, 19] which are established methods for assessing node importance in graphs.  It acknowledges the limitations of these approaches in capturing richer graph properties and positions itself within the growing body of work that integrates node and edge features into the ranking process [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21]. Specifically, the paper addresses the optimization challenges inherent in Supervised PageRank [21], a framework that learns weights for node and edge features.  The authors correctly identify the difficulties in applying existing optimization techniques [16, 7] due to constraints and the inability to compute the exact loss function value.  They appropriately situate their contributions within the context of inexact oracle optimization methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The provision of convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant strength. This is particularly valuable given the non-convex nature of the problem and the lack of such guarantees in prior work [21].
* **Novelty:** The adaptation of existing optimization techniques (gradient descent and a randomized approach from [16]) to the constrained, inexact oracle setting of Supervised PageRank represents a novel contribution.  The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity is also insightful.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation on a real-world web ranking task demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the state-of-the-art baseline [21].  The comparison with the standard Supervised PageRank also highlights the benefit of the proposed optimization strategies.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem, the model, and the optimization challenges. The mathematical notation is generally well-handled.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The included lemmas and theorems and the explicit detailing of the complexity of the algorithms demonstrate a rigorous approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Readability:** While technically sound, the paper is dense and can be challenging to follow, particularly in Sections 3, 4, and 5.  The numerous equations and lemmas contribute to this density. More intuitive explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors analyze the complexity of the algorithms, a direct comparison of runtime (computational time) with the state-of-the-art method [21] is missing. Reporting runtime on the experimental setup would significantly strengthen the practical implications of the results. This is especially important given the theoretical complexity analysis.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning parameters (e.g. L in GFN), but a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithms to these parameters, and a justification for the chosen values, would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate improved ranking quality, they are limited to a single dataset.  Evaluating the algorithms on a broader range of datasets and graph types would increase confidence in their generalizability.
* **Connection to Recent Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, the field of optimization for large-scale machine learning is rapidly evolving. A more thorough discussion of connections to recent advances in distributed optimization, or adaptive learning rates, could further contextualize the contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging optimization problem.
* The theoretical guarantees are a significant contribution to the field.
* The empirical results demonstrate practical improvements over the state-of-the-art.
* The work addresses a relevant problem with potential applications in web search and related areas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and readability.
* The lack of runtime comparison limits the practical impact of the results.
* The experiments are limited in scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking and optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are promising. While the paper suffers from some density and could benefit from additional analysis and clarity, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the clarity issues, include a runtime comparison, and consider a more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity.  Expanding the experimental evaluation would also be beneficial.
