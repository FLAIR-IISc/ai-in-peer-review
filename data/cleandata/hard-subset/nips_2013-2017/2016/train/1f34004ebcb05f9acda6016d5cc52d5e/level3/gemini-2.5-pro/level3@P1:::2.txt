This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a model that extends the classic PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features. The authors frame this learning problem as a constrained, non-convex loss minimization task. A key difficulty in this setting is that the loss function and its gradient, which depend on the stationary distribution of a Markov chain, cannot be computed exactly.

The main contribution of the paper is the proposal and analysis of two optimization algorithms that operate with such an "inexact oracle". The first is a random gradient-free method designed for convex problems, and the second is an adaptive gradient-based method for non-convex problems. For both methods, the authors provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees, a significant improvement over the prior state-of-the-art method for Supervised PageRank [21], which lacked such guarantees. The analysis relies on carefully bounding the error of the inexact oracle by controlling the number of iterations of an underlying power-method-like algorithm [17] used to approximate the stationary distribution. The proposed methods are experimentally compared against the previous gradient-based approach on a web page ranking task.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a relevant and practical problem. Learning feature weights for graph-based ranking models like PageRank is an important way to improve their performance. The primary contribution—providing the first theoretically convergent algorithms for the Supervised PageRank learning problem—is a notable advance over existing heuristics [21]. The formal treatment under the inexact oracle framework is sound and well-motivated.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical development is the paper's core strength. The authors successfully connect the accuracy of the low-level stationary distribution estimators (Lemmas 1 and 2) to the convergence of the high-level optimization algorithms (Theorems 1-4). Deriving complexity bounds for the entire two-level optimization process is a complete and valuable piece of analysis. The proposed adaptive gradient method for non-convex optimization with an inexact first-order oracle (Section 5) is a general contribution that may be of independent interest.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and structured. The problem setup, the concept of the inexact oracle, and the proposed algorithms are clearly explained. The introduction provides good context and properly situates the work with respect to prior literature on PageRank, supervised ranking, and optimization with inexact information [7, 16, 25].

### Weaknesses

1.  **Experimental Evaluation and Self-Containment:** A major weakness is that the experimental section in the main paper is quite brief, and crucial results appear to be relegated to the supplementary material. A paper on optimization methods should provide a clear picture of their practical performance, including not just the final objective value (Table 1) but also their computational efficiency (e.g., wall-clock time, total arithmetic operations). The user mentions that supplementary figures (e.g., Figure 1 in the supplement) show the gradient-free method converges slowly. This type of analysis is essential for a complete evaluation and should be in the main paper. Without it, it is difficult for a reader to fully assess the practical trade-offs between the proposed methods and the baseline. The paper is not self-contained in its current form.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice for the Gradient-Free Method:** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1) assumes that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set `Φ`. However, the loss function defined in (2.4) is generally non-convex. The authors acknowledge this and state in Section 4.2 that they "assume that there is a local minimum ϕ∗, and Φ is a small vicinity of ϕ∗, in which f(ϕ) is convex". This is a very strong assumption with little practical justification. The paper provides no guidance on how one would identify or construct such a set `Φ` in practice, which significantly limits the applicability of the theoretical guarantees. The authors should discuss the implications of applying this algorithm when the local convexity condition is not met.

3.  **Clarity of the Gradient-Based Method's Convergence Criterion:** The convergence result for the adaptive gradient-based method (Theorem 3) shows that the quantity `||MK(xK - xK+1)||^2` can be made arbitrarily small. While this term is related to the size of a gradient step, the paper does not explicitly connect this criterion to a standard first-order stationarity condition for constrained non-convex optimization (e.g., the norm of the projected gradient or gradient mapping being close to zero). A more thorough explanation of why this stopping condition implies convergence to an approximate stationary point would strengthen the theoretical claims.

### Detailed Comments for Authors

*   To improve the paper's self-containment and the strength of the experimental validation, I strongly recommend moving the key results from the supplementary material into the main text. This should include convergence plots (loss vs. iterations/time) for all methods and a direct comparison of their computational efficiency.
*   Please address the convexity assumption in Section 4.2 more directly. Can you provide any theoretical or empirical evidence that the loss function is likely to be convex near a local minimum for this type of problem? If not, could you reformulate the theorem for non-convex functions (e.g., convergence to a stationary point) or, at a minimum, have a more detailed discussion of this limitation?
*   In Section 5, please clarify the relationship between the convergence criterion `||MK(xK - xK+1)||^2 ≤ ε` and conventional definitions of an ε-approximate stationary point for constrained optimization. This would make the theoretical guarantee more interpretable.

In summary, this paper presents a strong theoretical contribution by developing the first provably convergent algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank. However, its claims are weakened by a significant gap between the theoretical assumptions and practical application for the gradient-free method, and by an incomplete experimental evaluation in the main text. Addressing these issues would substantially improve the quality and impact of the work.
