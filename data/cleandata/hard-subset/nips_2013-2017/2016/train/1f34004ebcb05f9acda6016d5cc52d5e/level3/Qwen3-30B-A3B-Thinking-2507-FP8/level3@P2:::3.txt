

# Review for "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (GBN and GFN) for solving the non-convex loss-minimization problem associated with learning these models. The lower level of their methods uses the linearly convergent method from Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, while the upper level employs gradient-based or gradient-free optimization with inexact oracle information. This builds upon and extends the work of [21] and [17], providing theoretical guarantees that were previously lacking for the state-of-the-art method.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors provide convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods, which is a significant advancement over the state-of-the-art [21] that lacked such guarantees. The experimental evaluation on a real-world web ranking task shows consistent improvements in loss function values, with statistical significance (p<0.005) on multiple test sets. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with proofs provided in supplementary materials. However, the paper could better address the limitations of the non-convex optimization approach - while the authors note the problem is non-convex, they don't fully discuss how this affects practical performance or what guarantees hold for local minima.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured but suffers from excessive symbol usage without a symbol table, making it difficult to follow (e.g., N is used before definition). The two-level optimization framework is conceptually sound but the notation becomes cumbersome. The experimental section clearly describes the dataset and evaluation metrics, though the comparison with state-of-the-art is primarily on loss function values rather than direct ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG), which would better demonstrate practical significance. A clearer symbol table would substantially improve readability.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. First, it provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models, which previously lacked such guarantees. Second, it adapts random gradient-free methods [16] to constrained optimization with inexact oracles. Third, it develops a gradient-based method for general constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle. The work builds upon [17] but extends it to the constrained optimization setting for Supervised PageRank, which is a significant contribution. The authors properly cite related work, though they could more explicitly clarify how their two-level framework differs from the lower-level method in [17].

**Significance:** The results are significant for both theoretical and practical applications. The proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in ranking quality (as measured by the loss function) while providing theoretical guarantees. The gradient-free method's advantage of avoiding derivative calculations for large matrices is particularly valuable for large-scale applications like web ranking. The work has clear potential to improve search engine ranking algorithms. However, the paper would be strengthened by demonstrating how the improved loss function translates to actual ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG) rather than just loss values.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank optimization
- Proposes two novel optimization methods with rigorous theoretical analysis
- Demonstrates practical improvements over state-of-the-art on real-world ranking task
- Addresses an important problem with clear practical applications
- Builds upon and extends prior work in a meaningful way

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Lack of symbol table significantly hinders readability
- Limited demonstration of how improved loss translates to actual ranking quality metrics
- Does not clearly distinguish how the two-level framework differs from [17] in the main text
- The non-convexity of the problem is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed in terms of practical implications

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes meaningful theoretical and practical contributions to the field of graph-based ranking algorithms. The theoretical guarantees are particularly valuable as they address a critical gap in the current state of the art. The empirical results are convincing, though the paper could better demonstrate the practical significance of the improved loss function. The clarity issues can be addressed with minor revisions (adding a symbol table, clarifying the relationship to [17]). The paper is well-suited for NIPS and represents a significant contribution to the optimization and ranking communities.

I recommend acceptance. The theoretical contributions are substantial, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper addresses an important problem in graph-based ranking. The clarity issues are fixable with minor revisions, and the significance of the work is clear for both theoretical and practical applications.
