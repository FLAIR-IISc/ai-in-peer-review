This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing both gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) optimization methods. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both algorithms, addressing a limitation of prior work [21] which lacked such guarantees. The authors carefully consider the challenges of inexact oracle computations and develop methods to analyze and control the accuracy trade-offs. Empirical results on a web page ranking task demonstrate that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21].

The paper builds upon the established foundation of PageRank and Supervised PageRank [21], recognizing the need to incorporate node and edge features beyond graph structure. The two-level optimization approach, leveraging inexact oracle information, is a natural extension of previous work [6, 21].  The authors correctly identify the difficulties in applying existing optimization methods [16, 7] due to constraints and the inability to compute the exact loss function.  Their use of method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution is well-justified, citing its favorable complexity bound.  The theoretical analysis provided, demonstrating convergence rates for both GBN and GFN, is a significant achievement.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength of this work is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a crucial contribution, particularly in the context of non-convex optimization problems.
* **Inexact Oracle Handling:** The paper explicitly addresses the challenges of inexact oracle computations, which is a realistic consideration for large-scale graph problems. The analysis of accuracy trade-offs is valuable.
* **Novelty:** While building on existing techniques, the combination of the two-level optimization framework with the specific inexact oracle handling and convergence analysis appears novel.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem of Supervised PageRank learning is clearly defined, and the connection to existing literature is well-established.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over the state-of-the-art.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Notation:** The paper is dense and employs a large number of symbols, making it difficult to follow at times. A symbol table would be immensely helpful. For example, the variable 'N' is used with different meanings in different contexts before being clearly defined.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on the loss function. A direct comparison of ranking quality (e.g., NDCG, MAP) with the state-of-the-art method [21] and, importantly, with the original method [17] (since this work generalizes it) is missing. Showing how the ranking *itself* improves is critical.
* **Dataset Accessibility:** While the dataset is described, its lack of public availability hinders reproducibility.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, but the process is not detailed. It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the methods to hyperparameter choices.
* **Convexity Assumption:** The claim that f(ϕ) is convex "on Φ" needs further clarification. The authors state this is an assumption to simplify analysis, but the practical implications should be discussed, especially if the function is significantly non-convex elsewhere.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contributions – convergence guarantees for both methods – are significant and address a gap in the literature.
* The practical improvements demonstrated in the experiments are promising.
* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in graph-based learning.
* The technical details are generally sound, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant optimization techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a direct ranking quality comparison limits the impact of the experimental results.
* The density of notation and lack of a symbol table make the paper challenging to read and understand.
* The non-public dataset hinders reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of graph-based learning and optimization. The theoretical results are particularly noteworthy. However, the limited experimental scope and clarity issues detract from its overall impact.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Add a symbol table.**
2. **Include a direct comparison of ranking quality (NDCG, MAP) with [21] and [17].**
3. **Provide more details on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity.**
4. **Clarify the convexity assumption and its implications.**
