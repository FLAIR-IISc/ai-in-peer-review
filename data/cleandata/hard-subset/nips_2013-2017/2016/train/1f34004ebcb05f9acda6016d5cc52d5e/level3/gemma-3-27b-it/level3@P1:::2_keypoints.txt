- Addresses learning Supervised PageRank by incorporating node and edge features into a non-convex loss minimization framework.  
- Proposes two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle.  
- Core contribution: provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which prior work (e.g., [21]) lacks.  
- Experimental validation on a web page ranking task shows improved performance over existing methods, but all results are placed in the supplementary material.  
- Builds on PageRank/random-walk literature and previous efforts to enrich ranking with node/edge features, distinguishing itself by optimization-focused contributions.  
- Correctly notes that existing optimization methods are unsuitable due to parameter constraints and inability to compute the exact loss.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis deriving convergence rates even with inexact oracles and exploring the accuracy–complexity trade-off.  
- Weakness: main paper lacks experimental results (all in supplement), making practical evaluation and the submission weaker.  
- Theorem 1 assumes convexity despite the problem’s non-convex formulation; authors must justify how the feasible set Φ ensures this or acknowledge the limitation.  
- Theorem 3 provides a bound on MK(xK − xK+1) but does not clearly show how this implies convergence of x_k to a stationary point.  
- Algorithmic efficiency is under-discussed; supplementary figure suggests slow convergence for the gradient-free method and runtime/iteration comparisons are missing.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel solution to a challenging problem, valuable convergence guarantees, better empirical ranking performance, and insightful accuracy–complexity analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: experiments relegated to supplement, questionable convexity assumption, unclear link from bounds to stationarity, and insufficient runtime/efficiency analysis.  
- Overall: technically promising but presentation and theoretical ambiguities warrant rejection; recommend resubmission after integrating experiments into the main paper and clarifying convexity and convergence arguments.
