- Paper proposes two two-level methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for learning Supervised PageRank using inexact oracles to handle stationary distribution and derivative estimation.
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches for this problem and detailed complexity bounds.
- Develops a general theoretical framework for optimization with inexact oracles that could apply beyond Supervised PageRank.
- Experimental results (in supplement) claim both methods outperform prior work [21], with the gradient-free method particularly strong.
- Major presentation issue: key experimental results are relegated to the supplement, making the main paper incomplete.
- Theorem 1 assumes convexity, but the stated objective is non-convex; the paper fails to explain how to choose feasible set Φ to ensure local convexity.
- Theorem 3 bounds ‖MK(ϕK - ϕK+1)‖2, but the paper does not clarify whether this implies convergence to a stationary point for the non-convex problem.
- Algorithmic efficiency concerns: gradient-free method appears to converge slowly (supplementary Fig.1) and lacks comparisons of iterations or wall-clock time versus prior work.
- Clarity lacking on why gradient-free is preferable at large scale; no clear computational complexity comparison to justify avoiding derivatives.
- Recommendation: not ready for acceptance without major revisions addressing experiments placement, convexity assumption, convergence interpretation, and efficiency comparisons.
- Requested revisions: move key experiments to main paper; either justify Φ for local convexity or extend theory to non-convex case; clarify link between Theorem 3 bound and stationarity; add computational-efficiency comparisons.
- Pros for acceptance: novel theoretical contribution, first convergence guarantees for gradient-free method, promising empirical results.
- Cons for acceptance: unresolved convexity assumption, incomplete presentation (experiments in supplement), unclear convergence claims for non-convex problem, insufficient efficiency comparisons.
