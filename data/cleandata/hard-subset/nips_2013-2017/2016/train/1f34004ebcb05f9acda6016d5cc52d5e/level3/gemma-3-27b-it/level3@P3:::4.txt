This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a crucial task in ranking problems with applications in web search and social networks. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to tackle the non-convex loss minimization inherent in this problem. A key contribution is providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which is a notable improvement over existing approaches like the one in [21]. The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods and justifying the need for new approaches.

The work builds upon the foundations of PageRank and its supervised extensions [18, 21], recognizing the need to incorporate node and edge features beyond the graph structure. The authors correctly identify the challenges in optimizing the Supervised PageRank model, stemming from the non-convex loss function, parameter constraints, and the difficulty in computing exact gradients. The proposed methods leverage the concept of an inexact oracle, a common strategy in optimization when exact computations are prohibitive. The related work section adequately cites relevant publications in PageRank, optimization methods, and the use of inexact oracles [1, 6, 7, 16, 17, 23, 24].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The provision of convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a significant strength. This is a substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art method [21], which lacks such guarantees.
* **Inexact Oracle Approach:** Utilizing an inexact oracle allows the authors to address the computational challenges of estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives, a key bottleneck in Supervised PageRank learning.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper includes a detailed analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level algorithm and the overall computational complexity, providing valuable insights for practical implementation.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed methods compared to the state-of-the-art approach [21] on a real-world web page ranking task. The inclusion of p-values strengthens the statistical significance of the results.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Runtime/Computational Time:** The reviewer’s recommendation to report the runtime (computational time) of the proposed optimization methods is crucial and currently missing. While the analysis of computational complexity is present, providing actual runtime measurements on the experiments would significantly strengthen the practical relevance of the work. It would allow for a direct comparison of the efficiency of the gradient-based and gradient-free approaches, and a clearer understanding of their scalability.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters (e.g., L0 for the gradient-based method) and reports limited sensitivity analysis. A more thorough exploration of hyperparameter settings and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments on a real-world dataset are valuable, the scope is somewhat limited. Expanding the experiments to include different datasets and graph structures could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the methods' robustness and generalizability.
* **Complexity of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, while thorough, is quite dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with optimization theory to fully grasp. Providing more intuition and explanations of the key concepts would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents novel optimization methods with strong theoretical guarantees for a relevant and challenging problem.
* The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over the state-of-the-art.
* The analysis of computational complexity is detailed and provides valuable insights.
* The paper is well-written and contributes to the understanding of Supervised PageRank learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of runtime measurements limits the practical assessment of the methods.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and the scope of experiments could be further investigated.
* The theoretical analysis may be inaccessible to a broader audience.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and experimental results are compelling. Addressing the weakness regarding runtime reporting would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to include runtime measurements and a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity. The contribution is significant, the work is technically sound, and the paper is well-written and clear.
