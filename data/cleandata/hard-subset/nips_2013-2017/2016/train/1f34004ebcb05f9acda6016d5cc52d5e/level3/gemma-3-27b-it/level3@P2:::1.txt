This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a graph-based ranking approach that incorporates node and edge features into random walk probabilities. The authors focus on the non-convex optimization challenges inherent in this learning process, proposing gradient-based and gradient-free methods, both employing an “inexact oracle” approach. This means they approximate the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk, rather than computing it exactly, and utilize these approximations within an iterative optimization scheme. The core contribution lies in establishing convergence guarantees for these methods, something lacking in prior work on Supervised PageRank [21], and demonstrating superior empirical performance.

The paper builds upon the established foundation of PageRank [18] and related random walk ranking algorithms [11, 8, 9, 19].  It correctly identifies the limitations of these traditional methods in leveraging richer node and edge information, referencing several works that attempt to address this shortcoming [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21]. The context of Supervised PageRank [21] is well-defined, and the paper clearly articulates the need for new optimization techniques due to the non-convexity of the loss function and the impracticality of exact computation. The approach of utilizing an inexact oracle is sensible and positioned effectively as a way to overcome these limitations.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically sound, with a rigorous mathematical treatment of the optimization problem and the proposed algorithms. The convergence proofs for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods are a significant accomplishment, and the analysis of trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity is valuable.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem of learning Supervised PageRank with feature-driven random walks is clearly defined and motivated. The authors articulate the challenges and the rationale behind their approach effectively.
* **Novelty (Incremental):** While the core ideas aren’t entirely novel (see weaknesses), the adaptation of inexact-oracle optimization techniques to the specific context of Supervised PageRank with constrained parameters, coupled with the convergence guarantees, constitutes a worthwhile contribution.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in terms of ranking quality on a real-world web page ranking task. The comparison against a standard Supervised PageRank implementation further highlights the benefits of the proposed algorithms.
* **Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methods and theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:**  The gradient-free approach draws heavily from Nesterov and Spokoiny [16], and the gradient method builds upon existing techniques for inexact optimization. While the adaptation and convergence analysis are valuable, the fundamental algorithmic ideas are not entirely new.  The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of these related works and a clearer articulation of the unique aspects of their contribution.
* **Contextualization:** The optimization problem, while well-defined within the context of Supervised PageRank, could be presented in a more general framework. This would help to position the work more effectively relative to the broader literature on constrained optimization with inexact oracles.  Specifically, elaborating on *why* existing methods (e.g., those cited in the introduction – [16, 7]) aren’t directly applicable beyond the specific constraints of Supervised PageRank would strengthen the argument for the novelty of their approach.
* **Missing Conclusion:** The paper ends abruptly after the experimental results. A conclusion summarizing the main findings, discussing the limitations of the work, and outlining potential future research directions would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the experiments show improved results, a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the algorithms to hyperparameter settings (e.g., the step size in the gradient-based method) would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach to a challenging optimization problem in the context of graph-based ranking.
* The convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithms are a significant contribution, addressing a key limitation of prior work.
* The empirical results demonstrate that the algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is somewhat limited, as the core algorithmic ideas are based on existing techniques.
* The paper lacks a strong contextualization of its contribution within the broader literature on optimization.
* The missing conclusion leaves the paper feeling incomplete.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**.  The technical soundness and empirical results are strong, but the limited novelty and lack of broader contextualization are concerns. Addressing the weaknesses – by adding a robust conclusion, more thorough discussion of related work, and a stronger articulation of the unique contributions – would significantly strengthen the paper. The theoretical contributions (convergence proofs) are valuable enough to warrant acceptance, but the authors should be encouraged to refine the presentation and emphasize the specific contributions more clearly. I suggest the authors are asked to address the novelty concerns in a revision before final acceptance.
