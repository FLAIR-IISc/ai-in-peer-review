

# Review for "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features through linear combinations with model parameters. The authors formulate this as a constrained non-convex optimization problem and propose two novel two-level methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free approach. Both methods leverage the concept of an inexact oracle to handle the computational challenges of estimating the stationary distribution and its derivatives for the Markov random walk. The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods and demonstrates their superiority over the state-of-the-art approach [21] on a web page ranking task.

The work builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21] and extends the optimization techniques from [16, 7, 17] to handle the specific challenges of the problem. While previous work on Supervised PageRank [21] proposed a two-level gradient-based method without convergence guarantees, this paper provides the first theoretical convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods for this problem.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a clear contribution to the optimization of Supervised PageRank models by providing the first theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches to this specific problem.

2. The authors develop a comprehensive theoretical framework for optimization with inexact oracles, which is applicable beyond just Supervised PageRank and could be useful for other problems with similar computational challenges.

3. The paper provides detailed complexity bounds for both algorithms, which is valuable for understanding their computational trade-offs.

4. The experimental results, though relegated to the supplement, indicate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art [21] on a real-world ranking task, with the gradient-free method showing particularly strong performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of self-contained presentation**: The experimental results are entirely relegated to the supplement, making the main paper incomplete and difficult to evaluate without consulting supplementary materials. This is a significant issue for a conference paper, as the main text should contain all essential results. The authors should move the key experimental results to the main paper.

2. **Convexity assumption in Theorem 1**: Theorem 1 assumes convexity of the objective function for the gradient-free method, but the objective function (2.4) is explicitly non-convex as stated in the paper. The paper does not adequately address how to choose the feasible set Φ to guarantee local convexity around a minimum, which is crucial for the theoretical guarantees to hold in practice. This is a significant gap in the theoretical analysis.

3. **Convergence interpretation for Theorem 3**: Theorem 3 bounds the quantity ‖MK(ϕK - ϕK+1)‖2, but it is unclear whether this condition ensures convergence to a stationary point of the non-convex problem. The paper should clarify the relationship between this bound and convergence to a stationary point.

4. **Algorithmic efficiency**: Supplementary Figure 1 shows that the gradient-free method (GFN) converges relatively slowly. The paper should include a comparison of the computational efficiency (e.g., number of iterations, wall-clock time) between the proposed methods and the state-of-the-art [21] to demonstrate practical advantages.

5. **Clarity of the gradient-free method**: The paper does not clearly explain why the gradient-free approach is preferable to the gradient-based approach for large-scale problems, as the main advantage cited ("avoids calculating the derivative for each element of a large matrix") is somewhat vague without a clear computational complexity comparison.

## Recommendation
The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to the Supervised PageRank framework with promising empirical results. However, the major weaknesses related to the lack of self-contained presentation, the unresolved convexity assumption, and the unclear convergence interpretation for the non-convex problem prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

The paper has significant potential, but requires substantial revisions before it can be accepted. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Move key experimental results to the main paper
2. Address the convexity assumption in Theorem 1 by either:
   a) Providing a more detailed explanation of how to choose Φ to guarantee local convexity, or
   b) Modifying the theoretical results to apply to non-convex problems
3. Clarify the relationship between the bound in Theorem 3 and convergence to a stationary point
4. Include a comparison of computational efficiency between the proposed methods and the state-of-the-art

Without these revisions, the paper's theoretical claims remain questionable, and the practical significance is difficult to assess. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these issues, particularly the experimental presentation and the convexity assumption.

## Pro and Con Acceptance
**Pros for acceptance**: Novel theoretical contribution to optimization of Supervised PageRank, first convergence guarantees for gradient-free method, promising empirical results.

**Cons for acceptance**: Critical theoretical gaps (convexity assumption), lack of self-contained paper (experiments in supplement), unclear convergence interpretation for non-convex problem, insufficient comparison of computational efficiency.
