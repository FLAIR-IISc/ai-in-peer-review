

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a supervised PageRank model, where node and edge weights are modeled as linear combinations of features. The authors propose two optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for this non-convex learning problem, claiming theoretical convergence guarantees that were absent in prior work.

The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], though it mislabels this as "Supervised PageRank" while [21] actually presents a semi-supervised variant. The authors formulate the learning problem as minimizing a square loss function over a constrained parameter space, where the loss function requires approximating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk. They develop theoretical guarantees for their methods by combining a stationary-distribution approximation technique with gradient-based and gradient-free optimization frameworks.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution in providing convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, which was previously missing in the literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of prior work [21] that used gradient-based methods without convergence guarantees. Their theoretical analysis, including Lemmas 1 and 2 bounding approximation errors and Theorems 1-4 establishing convergence rates, demonstrates careful mathematical development. The paper's theoretical framework for constrained optimization with inexact oracles is also of general interest beyond the specific application.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its failure to convincingly demonstrate practical impact. Despite claiming "outperformance" in experiments, the paper does not provide sufficient experimental details in the main text. Key parameters (such as how φ₀ and radius R were chosen) are not explained, and the extent of improvement over baselines is not quantified in a meaningful way. The experimental section is particularly weak, deferring all detailed results to the appendix while making strong claims about performance improvements in the main text.

The paper also contains significant issues with clarity and presentation. The writing contains grammatical errors and sloppy citations (e.g., mislabeling [21] as "Supervised PageRank" instead of "semi-supervised"). The constraint of searching within an R-ball around φ₀ is not justified, and the paper does not discuss whether this limitation would severely restrict the model's practical efficacy. Some technical assumptions may be unrealistic for real-world applications, undermining the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

The experimental evaluation is superficial. The paper tests only on a single web graph dataset (from Yandex), and it's unclear whether the results generalize to diverse graph structures or datasets. The paper does not report key parameters like the exact choice of R or how φ₀ was selected, making reproduction difficult. The claim that "GFN significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art algorithms on all test sets" lacks statistical significance testing details, and the experimental results are not sufficiently contextualized to understand their practical importance.

**Overall assessment:**
The paper makes a theoretically strong contribution to the optimization of supervised PageRank models, providing convergence rate guarantees that were previously missing. However, it fails to demonstrate practical impact or provide sufficient empirical validation to convince readers of its real-world utility. The experimental section is inadequate for a NIPS paper, with key details relegated to the appendix and insufficient discussion of the practical significance of the results.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound from a theoretical perspective, but the empirical validation is weak and insufficient to support the claims of practical superiority. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, but the paper lacks convincing evidence of practical impact.

**Clarity:** The writing is poor with grammatical errors and unclear explanations. The paper would benefit from significant editing and clarification of key technical choices.

**Originality:** The core ideas are not highly novel—they adapt existing optimization techniques to a specific problem. The paper's main contribution is the theoretical analysis rather than a new algorithmic approach.

**Significance:** The significance is limited by the lack of convincing empirical validation. While the theoretical framework is interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed methods would be useful in practice for search engines or other applications.

**Recommendation:** The paper should be rejected. While it makes a theoretically interesting contribution to optimization under inexact oracles, the lack of sufficient experimental validation and the poor presentation undermine its overall contribution. The authors should either provide substantially more detailed experimental results in the main paper or substantially revise their theoretical claims to better reflect the limitations of their approach. The paper's failure to convincingly demonstrate practical impact, combined with its unclear experimental results and poor writing, prevents it from meeting NIPS standards for publication.
