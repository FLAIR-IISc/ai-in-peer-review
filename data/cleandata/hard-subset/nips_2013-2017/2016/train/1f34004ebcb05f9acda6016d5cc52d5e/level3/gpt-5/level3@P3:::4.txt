Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the supervised PageRank learning problem where node/edge features parametrically determine restart and transition probabilities. The authors frame learning as minimizing a non-convex pairwise square-hinge loss over parameters ϕ subject to an ℓ2-ball constraint. The core methodological contribution is a two-level approach: (i) a lower-level routine approximates the query-specific stationary distributions πq(ϕ) and their derivatives using the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski (NN) method, rather than the classical power method; (ii) an upper-level optimizer is either an adaptive projected gradient method for non-convex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle, or a random gradient-free (zeroth-order) method adapted to constrained problems and biased/inexact function evaluations. The authors provide convergence rate guarantees and complexity bounds for both variants via an inexact oracle framework. Empirically, on a Yandex user-browsing graph dataset (600 queries, query-dependent graphs), their methods outperform the previously used two-level gradient approach that relied on power-method estimates and lacked convergence guarantees.

The work relates to supervised random walks and supervised PageRank (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec; prior Supervised PageRank training), PageRank computation methods (power vs NN), random zeroth-order optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny), non-convex first/zeroth-order methods (Ghadimi & Lan), and convex optimization with inexact oracles (Devolder et al.). The extension to constrained settings and biased inexact zero-order oracles is a noteworthy refinement over prior analyses.

Quality:
- Theory: The inexact oracle analyses are careful and technically sound. For the gradient-based method, the paper generalizes composite gradient methods to non-convex objectives with unknown Lipschitz constants under an inexact first-order oracle, providing a practical backtracking rule and a stationarity guarantee. For the zeroth-order method, the authors control the bias introduced by the inexact function values and provide finite-time bounds under convexity on Φ. Complexity bounds tie together lower-level accuracies (N, N1, N2) with upper-level convergence.
- Assumptions: The gradient-free analysis requires convexity of f on Φ; this partially tempers the “non-convex” headline for that method. The gradient-based method handles non-convexity via a stationarity measure but still relies on oracle smoothness. Both rely on accurate control of the lower-level approximation error via NN iterations, which is well motivated.
- Experiments: The Yandex experiments show lower loss values than the prior gradient-based baseline and the untuned model. However, (a) the evaluation focuses on the training objective; standard learning-to-rank metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP/ERR@k) on held-out data would make the case stronger; (b) the empirical comparison lacks wall-clock runtime, iteration counts at both levels, or memory usage. Given the theoretical emphasis on complexity, reporting computational time is essential.
- Reproducibility: Many details are given, but key constants (e.g., β1) and the full algorithms are deferred to the supplement; a consolidated pseudocode in the main text and a table of symbols would aid reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized but dense. Some notational overload (π, π0, P across queries), minor typos, and the reliance on supplementary materials impede readability. Suggested improvements:
- Move the definitions of the two-level algorithms (both variants) into the main text with concise pseudocode and an algorithmic flow diagram clarifying how lower-level accuracies map to upper-level stopping.
- Provide intuition for the NN method vs power method (why linear convergence translates to smaller N at a target error).
- Clarify β1 and the dependence of bounds on α, sparsity s, and feature dimensionality (m1, m2).
- Fix the GBP stopping rule description (“difference less than −1e−5” seems odd; likely absolute difference threshold).

Originality:
- Extends random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with biased, inexact function values—useful in practice and not directly covered by prior work.
- Provides an adaptive projected gradient method for non-convex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle, with backtracking and explicit complexity bounds.
- Integrates these advances with a practically important lower-level PageRank/derivative estimator (NN), yielding end-to-end guarantees for supervised PageRank training.
These combinations appear novel and relevant.

Significance:
The results matter for both optimization under inexact oracles and for learning on large graphs. The guarantees address a known gap in prior supervised PageRank training. Practitioners could benefit from a principled alternative to power-method-based training. That said, broader impact would be clearer with (i) standard ranking metrics, (ii) scalability evidence on larger graphs, and (iii) runtime comparisons.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean two-level design with provable rates.
- Both gradient-based and gradient-free options; the latter avoids expensive derivative computations.
- Solid complexity accounting that captures lower-level accuracy trade-offs.
- Empirical improvements over the prior training approach on real data.

Cons:
- Gradient-free theory assumes convexity on Φ, limiting its non-convex applicability.
- Missing wall-clock runtimes, hardware specs, and per-iteration costs; complexity claims are not validated empirically.
- Evaluation emphasizes the loss; lacks standard ranking metrics and broader baselines (e.g., Supervised Random Walks/BrowseRank).
- Some clarity issues and heavy reliance on the supplement for key constants and algorithms.

Questions and requests for the authors:
- Please report runtimes: wall-clock time, number of upper-level iterations, and average NN iterations (N, N1/N2) per step for GBN/GFN/GBP, together with hardware and implementation details. Plots of loss vs time and NDCG vs time would be ideal.
- Provide NDCG@k/MAP on the test sets, with statistical significance, and clarify whether improvements in f translate into better ranking quality.
- Include sensitivity analyses for ε, L0, α, and the radius R. How robust are the methods to these hyperparameters?
- Justify the GBP configuration (power = 100) and step sizes; can stronger GBP settings close the gap?
- Clarify how β1 is computed and its magnitude in practice; discuss memory usage of Π̃ and the cost of derivative computations.

Recommendation:
This is a strong paper with meaningful theoretical contributions and a practically relevant application. With clearer empirical reporting—especially runtimes and ranking metrics—it would make a compelling NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding comprehensive runtime comparisons and standard ranking evaluations.
