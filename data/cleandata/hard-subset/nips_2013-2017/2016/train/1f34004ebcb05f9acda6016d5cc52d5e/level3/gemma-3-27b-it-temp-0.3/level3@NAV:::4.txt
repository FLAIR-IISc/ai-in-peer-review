## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a ranking framework extending traditional PageRank to incorporate node and edge features. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to tackle the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem. A key contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in previous work like [21]. The paper builds upon existing literature in random walk models for ranking (e.g., PageRank [18], HITS [11]) and supervised ranking approaches [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21]. 

The strengths of this work are significant. The theoretical convergence analysis is a valuable addition, addressing a critical gap in the existing literature. The exploration of both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches provides a useful comparison and caters to different scenarios where gradient computation might be expensive. The experimental evaluation on a real-world web ranking task demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming the state-of-the-art [21]. The detailed analysis of computational complexity and the trade-offs between accuracy and computation time are also commendable. The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical formulations and algorithmic details.

However, the paper suffers from a notable weakness: the lack of runtime reporting. While the authors discuss computational complexity, providing actual runtime measurements (in seconds or minutes) for each method on the experimental dataset would significantly strengthen the evaluation. This would allow for a more practical assessment of the methods' scalability and efficiency. The extensive supplementary material, while thorough, could be better integrated into the main text for improved readability. The numerous references to supplementary materials throughout the main text disrupt the flow of the argument. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge the non-convexity of the problem, a more detailed discussion of the impact of this non-convexity on the convergence behavior and the potential for getting stuck in local optima would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, a novel contribution.
*   Demonstrates superior performance compared to the state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task.
*   Offers a comprehensive analysis of computational complexity and trade-offs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks reporting of actual runtime measurements, hindering practical assessment.
*   Heavy reliance on supplementary material disrupts readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the missing runtime information, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with the request that the authors include runtime measurements in a revised version. Addressing this point would significantly enhance the paper's practical impact and strengthen its overall evaluation.
