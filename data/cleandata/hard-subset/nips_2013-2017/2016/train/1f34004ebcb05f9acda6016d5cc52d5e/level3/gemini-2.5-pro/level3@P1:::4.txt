This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank, a framework that incorporates node and edge features into the PageRank algorithm. The learning problem is formulated as a non-convex loss minimization task. The authors correctly point out that the state-of-the-art method for this problem [21] relies on a gradient-based approach where both the stationary distribution and its derivatives are approximated using power methods. A key limitation of this prior work is the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees for the overall learning algorithm due to the inexactness of these approximations.

The main contribution of this paper is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based (GBN) and one random gradient-free (GFN)—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. This is achieved by framing the problem within the concept of an "inexact oracle," where the accuracy of the function and gradient evaluations can be controlled. On the lower level, the authors employ a linearly convergent method from [17] to approximate the stationary distribution, which is more efficient than the standard power method. For both of their proposed upper-level algorithms, they provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence rate and the overall computational complexity, linking the required accuracy of the lower-level oracle to the desired accuracy of the final solution. Experiments on a large-scale web ranking dataset demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art baseline in terms of final loss function value.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The primary strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution. Providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank is a significant step forward, moving the approach from a heuristic optimization procedure to a principled one. The theoretical development, particularly the adaptation of optimization frameworks for inexact oracles to this specific non-convex, constrained problem, is solid and well-executed. The claims are well-supported by the provided proofs (in the supplementary material) and the experimental results, which show a clear improvement over the existing method.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. A notable omission is the reporting of wall-clock computational time. While the paper provides a thorough analysis of complexity in terms of arithmetic operations, this does not always translate directly to practical runtime due to factors like memory access patterns, parallelization, and implementation details. To make a compelling case for the practical utility of the proposed methods, it is crucial to compare the actual time taken for GBN, GFN, and the baseline GBP to reach convergence. I strongly recommend the authors add runtime results to their experimental section.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is strong, and the authors do a good job of explaining the limitations of prior work and how their methods address them. The two-level structure of the algorithms (upper-level parameter update and lower-level oracle approximation) is explained effectively. The theoretical results in Theorems 1-4 are stated clearly.

A minor point for improvement is the discussion around the assumptions. For instance, the analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex in the neighborhood of a local minimum. This is a common tool for local convergence analysis but is a strong assumption for a generally non-convex problem. A brief discussion on why this assumption might be reasonable in this context or its limitations would be beneficial for the reader.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and valuable combination of ideas. While the high-level optimization algorithms are extensions of existing frameworks for gradient-based [7] and gradient-free [16] optimization, their adaptation to the Supervised PageRank problem is non-trivial. The key original contribution lies in formulating the PageRank learning problem in terms of an inexact oracle and analyzing the trade-off between the lower-level approximation accuracy and the upper-level optimization complexity. This provides a complete, end-to-end analysis that was missing from prior work. The use of the method from [17] as the lower-level oracle is also a sensible and well-justified choice that contributes to the overall system's efficiency.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners in graph-based machine learning, information retrieval, and web search. By providing provably convergent algorithms, this work increases the reliability of learning feature-based ranking models like Supervised PageRank. The demonstration of improved ranking quality on a real-world dataset from a commercial search engine underscores the practical impact of this research. The general framework of combining efficient iterative methods with optimization schemes that tolerate inexactness is a powerful paradigm that could be applied to other large-scale learning problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are intractable.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for the important problem of learning Supervised PageRank.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and connects the accuracy of the underlying oracle to the overall convergence rate of the learning algorithm.
*   The proposed methods are shown to empirically outperform the state-of-the-art method on a large-scale, real-world ranking task.
*   The paper is well-written and combines ideas from different fields in a novel and impactful way.

**Arguments for Rejection / Major Revision:**
*   The experimental section lacks a comparison of wall-clock runtimes, which is a critical metric for evaluating the practical efficiency of optimization algorithms. This should be addressed.
*   The local convexity assumption made for the gradient-free method's analysis could be discussed in more detail regarding its implications and practical validity.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on learning graph-based ranking models. The theoretical guarantees for a problem that previously relied on heuristic optimization are a significant advance. The empirical results further validate the utility of the proposed methods. The main weakness is the omission of runtime comparisons in the experiments, but this is a point that can be addressed in a final version. I believe the paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation: Accept**
