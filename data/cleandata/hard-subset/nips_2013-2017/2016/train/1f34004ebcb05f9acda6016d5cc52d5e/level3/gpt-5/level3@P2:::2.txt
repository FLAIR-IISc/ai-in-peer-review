Summary
The paper studies learning a Supervised PageRank (SPR) model where node and edge weights are linear in features and parameterized by ϕ, with rankings given by the stationary distribution of a query-dependent Markov chain. The objective is a pairwise squared-hinge loss over assessor-labeled pairs. The key technical challenge is that evaluating the objective and its gradient requires computing stationary distributions and their derivatives, which must be approximated.

Contributions:
- Lower-level estimators: The authors adopt the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate stationary distributions, and propose an extension to approximate derivatives with controllable accuracy. They provide error bounds and arithmetic operation complexity for these approximations (Lemmas 1–2), arguing better theoretical complexity than the standard power method used by prior work.
- Upper-level optimization: Two algorithms using inexact oracles:
  1) A random gradient-free method for constrained optimization with uniformly bounded zero-order error; they analyze convergence for convex objectives and derive total complexity when combined with the lower level (Theorem 1, Theorem 2).
  2) An adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, with a stopping rule based on the gradient mapping (Theorem 3) and a total complexity bound when combined with the lower level (Theorem 4).
- Empirics: On a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (~600 queries), they report that both methods outperform a prior SPR learner [21] that uses power iteration–based gradients without convergence guarantees. They also claim fewer upper-level iterations for their gradient-based method.

Relation to prior work: This builds on supervised random walks/SPR (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec 2011; Zhukovskii et al.), PageRank and HITS, and lower-level PageRank solvers. On optimization, it extends inexact-oracle frameworks (e.g., Nesterov & Spokoiny for zero-order, Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov for inexact oracles, Ghadimi–Lan for nonconvex) to constrained settings and coupled upper/lower-level complexity.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical and well-motivated problem (learning-to-rank on graphs) with a principled two-level treatment of inexact oracles.
- Provides convergence-rate guarantees missing from prior SPR training methods; the derivative approximation scheme and its error control are useful.
- Complexity accounting explicitly ties lower-level accuracy to upper-level progress; the bounds include graph parameters (|Q|, p, s, α).
- Empirical results suggest improvements over the state-of-the-art baseline, and the use of a real industrial dataset increases relevance.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Theorem 1 (gradient-free) requires convexity, but the objective in (2.4) is nonconvex in ϕ. Section 4.2 assumes there exists a small ball Φ where f is convex, but no conditions are provided to verify or enforce this (e.g., on features, α, or ϕ). Without a constructive way to choose Φ, the guarantee is difficult to apply. If convexity cannot be ensured, consider using known nonconvex zeroth-order guarantees to stationary points (e.g., Ghadimi & Lan, 2013) and adjust complexity claims accordingly.
- Theorem 3 bounds ||MK(xK − xK+1)||, but the manuscript does not explicitly connect this to a standard stationarity measure. With Euclidean prox, MK(xK − xK+1) is the projected gradient mapping; a bound on its norm implies approximate first-order stationarity for constrained problems. Please state this explicitly and provide the inequality relating ||MK(xK − xK+1)|| to dist(0, ∂ψ(xK+1) + N_X(xK+1)), clarifying the precise stationarity notion achieved.
- Lipschitz constant L for f: the gradient-free method assumes L is known; experiments set L heuristically. It would strengthen the paper to bound L in terms of α and feature magnitudes, or default to an adaptive scheme analogous to Section 5 (backtracking for zero-order).
- Claims of “lowest proved complexity bound” relative to [5] should be made precise: specify which class of methods and which complexity measure, with a direct theorem-to-theorem comparison and assumptions aligned.
- Empirical evaluation is underreported in the main paper. Many details, tables, and figures are deferred to the supplement. Key results (full tables/plots, statistical tests, and wall-clock costs) should be in the main text to support claims.

Clarity
- Notation is dense; several constants (e.g., β1) are only defined in the supplement. Provide at least high-level bounds or interpretations in the main text.
- The description of Φ and positivity constraints is confusing: the text alternates between a Euclidean ball and positivity constraints to ensure denominators are nonzero. Since probabilities are scale-invariant, a simplex constraint would be simpler; otherwise, clearly state conditions guaranteeing ⟨ϕ1, Vi⟩ and ⟨ϕ2, Eij⟩ remain positive over Φ.
- References are inconsistently formatted and in several cases incomplete (years/venues missing). Ensure that key related works (e.g., Flaxman et al. for bandit gradient-free, Duchi et al. for random direction methods) are properly cited if relevant.

Originality
- The combination of inexact-oracle optimization (both zero- and first-order) with lower-level PageRank approximation and derivative estimation in a constrained setting is a meaningful synthesis. The extension of random gradient-free methods to bounded-error oracles over constrained domains is a useful variant. The derivative approximation scheme extending [17] is also novel in this context.

Significance
- The theoretical framework can influence future work on learning with Markov processes where exact oracles are unavailable. However, the empirical section should be strengthened to convincingly demonstrate practical impact:
  - Include standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) alongside the training loss.
  - Report wall-clock time, numbers of lower-level iterations (N, N1, N2), total function/gradient evaluations, and per-iteration costs for GBN, GFN, and GBP.
  - Supplementary Figure 1 suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly; quantify this relative to GBN/GBP and discuss scenarios where GFN is preferable (e.g., very high-dimensional derivatives).
  - Compare against additional baselines (e.g., untuned PR, RankSVM/LambdaMART on the same features) to contextualize gains.

Pro acceptance
- Addresses an important problem at the intersection of learning-to-rank and optimization with inexact oracles.
- Provides new convergence analyses and complexity accounting for practical two-level training.
- Empirical gains over a relevant baseline.

Con acceptance
- Reliance on an unverified convexity assumption for the gradient-free guarantee limits applicability.
- Stationarity guarantee in Theorem 3 not clearly tied to standard optimality criteria in the main text.
- Experimental section in the main paper is thin; efficiency results largely deferred to the supplement; limited runtime analysis.
- Clarity/notation issues and incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move core experimental tables/figures (including runtime and standard IR metrics) into the main paper; add ablations on τ, δ, and the Nesterov–Nemirovski vs power method trade-offs.
- Either (a) provide verifiable sufficient conditions ensuring local convexity on Φ, or (b) revise the gradient-free analysis to target nonconvex stationarity and adjust complexity bounds accordingly.
- Explicitly interpret MK(xK − xK+1) as the gradient mapping; state the stationarity guarantee and its relation to ∥∇f(ϕ)∥ and KKT conditions with the projection onto Φ.
- Clarify and justify the constraint set Φ and how positivity of probabilities is maintained; consider a simplex constraint to exploit scale invariance.
- Improve reference formatting and definitions of constants in the main text.

Overall: promising theory with practical relevance, but the current presentation (particularly experimental evidence and clarity around assumptions/guarantees) needs strengthening.
