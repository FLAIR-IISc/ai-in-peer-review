Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles supervised PageRank, where node and edge features parameterize restart and transition probabilities, and learns the parameters by minimizing a nonconvex pairwise hinge-squared loss over queries. The authors propose two two-level optimization schemes that explicitly embrace inexact oracles:

- A gradient-based method (GBN): The lower level computes an approximate stationary distribution and its derivatives using the linearly convergent method of Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref. 17) instead of the classical power method. The upper level is an adaptive projected gradient scheme with an inexact first-order oracle, providing convergence-rate guarantees to a stationary point under mild assumptions and without requiring exact objective values.

- A gradient-free method (GFN): The lower level computes approximate loss values via the same linearly convergent Markov chain solver. The upper level is a randomized, constrained, zero-order method with inexact oracle, adapted from Nesterov/Spokoiny-style gradient-free optimization to bounded-error function evaluations, providing expected decrease and convergence-rate bounds.

Both approaches avoid forming or exponentiating large matrices, operate with sparse graph operations, and provide explicit complexity trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and overall convergence. Empirically, on a proprietary Yandex browsing-graph dataset, the methods outperform the state-of-the-art supervised PageRank learner (GBP) on the specified loss; hyperparameters and stopping rules are given. The work builds directly on Nesterov and Nemirovski (ref. 17) for the lower level and extends inexact-oracle theory (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Nesterov–Spokoiny) to the constrained and nonconvex setting at the upper level. All proofs are in the supplementary material; method details are in Sections 2–5.

Quality
Strengths
- Solid theory: Clear convergence-rate analyses for both GBN (to a stationary point with inexact first-order oracle) and GFN (expected suboptimality bounds with inexact zero-order oracle), along with explicit complexity bounds that couple upper- and lower-level accuracies.
- Algorithmic care: Lower-level uses the linearly convergent scheme (ref. 17), shown to be more efficient than the power method and capable of approximating loss/gradients to any target accuracy; the upper-level GBN includes an adaptive backtracking-like procedure without needing the exact Lipschitz constant.
- Practicality: Both methods avoid forming large matrices and, for GFN, avoid computing derivatives of large transition matrices altogether.
- Empirical promise: On the Yandex dataset, both methods improve the loss over GBP; hyperparameters (L, L0, ε, R) and stopping criteria are specified.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope: Results report only the training objective (pairwise hinge-squared loss). For a ranking problem, standard metrics (NDCG, MAP, P@k) are essential; the abstract claims ranking-quality gains but the main results table shows only the loss. Please include ranking metrics on held-out queries.
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to GBP. Given that the approach generalizes/refines ref. 17 at the lower level, a direct empirical comparison to ref. 17’s learning setup (if applicable) or at least to the power-method-based learner under matched compute would be informative. More broadly, comparisons to standard learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM/ListNet on the same features) would calibrate the practical impact.
- Assumptions: The GFN analysis assumes local convexity of f on the feasible ball Φ, which may not be verifiable in practice for this nonconvex objective. Please discuss how robust GFN is when this fails and whether the algorithmic “best-so-far” safeguard is used.
- Clarity: The notation is heavy and some symbols (e.g., N, r, p, s, n) appear before definition. A symbol table would greatly aid readability. A few constants (e.g., β1) are defined only in the supplement; summarize their dependence and scale in the main text.
- Metrics vs. guarantees: The claim that GFN guarantees decrease in the loss is accurate in the “best-so-far/expected” sense; per-iteration monotonic decrease is not guaranteed. Please clarify the form of the guarantee in the text.

Clarity
- Organization is generally good and sections 2–5 are detailed, with all proofs in the supplement. However, the paper is hard to follow due to many symbols and delayed definitions. Please add:
  - A symbol/notation table on the first page of Section 2.
  - Early definitions of N, r, p, s, n; keep consistent norms (1- vs 2-norm) and state where each is used.
  - Brief pseudocode in the main text for the lower-level derivative approximation (Eqs. 3.2–3.3).
  - A concise summary of how δ, N, N1, N2, τ, and step sizes are chosen from the target ε (now scattered across lemmas/theorems).

Originality
- The paper makes nontrivial extensions of inexact-oracle optimization to constrained and nonconvex settings and couples them with a linearly convergent Markov chain solver for supervised PageRank. The gradient-free adaptation to bounded-error oracle and constraints is, to my knowledge, novel in this application. The two-level analysis with explicit accuracy/complexity trade-offs is useful.

Significance
- Supervised PageRank at web scale remains practically important. Avoiding exact matrix derivatives while retaining convergence guarantees is appealing. If ranking metrics corroborate the reported loss improvements and the runtime benefits over power-method approaches are demonstrated, this could be impactful to both ML optimization and IR practitioners.

Detailed comments and requests
- Experiments:
  - Report NDCG@k, MAP, and P@k on Q2 and subsets; include statistical tests.
  - Provide wall-clock time and lower-level iteration counts for GBN/GFN vs GBP (and vs the power method and ref. 17), highlighting the benefit of linear convergence and sparse ops.
  - Sensitivity: show performance vs ε and L (you partially do), and vs α and graph sparsity s.
  - Fairness: ensure identical stopping criteria and computational budgets across methods; clarify the choice of “raise to power 100” in GBP and whether tuning could help it.
- Methodology:
  - Clarify the nature of guarantees for GFN (expected suboptimality, best-so-far monotonicity).
  - Summarize β1 and constants driving N1, N2 in the main text (order-wise dependence on m, p, s, r, α).
  - Discuss memory complexity and sparsity assumptions explicitly.
- Reproducibility:
  - The dataset is not public, but the description is detailed. Please consider releasing synthetic graphs or a small anonymized subset, and release code for the two-level solvers with default hyperparameters.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and careful theory for inexact-oracle, two-level optimization (gradient-based and gradient-free).
- Practical algorithms that avoid large matrix operations and handle huge graphs.
- Clear hyperparameterization and convergence-rate estimates; methods likely to generalize beyond PageRank.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation focuses on loss, not ranking metrics; limited baselines.
- Some assumptions (local convexity for GFN) are not validated.
- Clarity/notation issues hinder accessibility.

Overall
A solid optimization contribution applied to an important ranking problem, with promising empirical results. If the authors strengthen the experimental section with ranking metrics and broader baselines, clarify guarantees/assumptions, and add a symbol table, this would be a strong NIPS paper.
