

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors formulate this as a constrained non-convex optimization problem and propose two solution approaches: a gradient-free method with inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method with inexact first-order oracle. The work builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced by Zhukovskii et al. (2016) and extends the theoretical foundation for optimization methods in this context.

Previous work on Supervised PageRank (Zhukovskii et al., 2016) provided a gradient-based optimization method but lacked convergence guarantees. The authors here fill this gap by developing two methods with provable convergence rates. They also improve upon the numerical calculation of the stationary distribution using a more efficient method from Nesterov and Nemirovski (2011) compared to the power method used in prior work.

## Strengths
The paper makes several significant contributions:
1. Theoretical: The authors provide convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods in the context of constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracle information, which is novel for this problem setting.
2. Technical: The paper develops efficient methods for approximating the stationary distribution and its derivatives with controlled error bounds (Lemmas 1 and 2), which is crucial for practical implementation.
3. Practical: The experiments demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art (Zhukovskii et al., 2016) on a real-world ranking task, with the gradient-free method showing particularly strong results.

## Weaknesses
Several critical weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution:

1. **Experimental Results Placement**: All experimental results are relegated to the supplement, making the main paper incomplete and non-self-contained. The NIPS reviewing guidelines emphasize that "a superbly written paper provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce its results," which this paper fails to do.

2. **Theorem 1's Convexity Assumption**: Theorem 1 assumes convexity to prove convergence, but the problem formulation (Equation 2.4) is explicitly stated to be non-convex. The authors do not clarify how to choose the feasible set Φ to guarantee objective convexity in practice, which is a critical gap in the theoretical analysis.

3. **Theorem 3's Convergence Interpretation**: Theorem 3 bounds MK(xK - x{K+1}) but does not establish whether this ensures convergence of x_k to a stationary point. For non-convex problems, this is a fundamental question that needs addressing.

4. **Algorithmic Efficiency**: Supplementary Figure 1 shows the gradient-free method (GFNM) converging slowly. The authors should report and compare the algorithmic efficiency (e.g., time complexity, number of iterations) of their methods against the state-of-the-art, which is currently missing from the main text.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions with well-motivated methods. However, the lack of clarity regarding convexity assumptions and convergence guarantees for non-convex problems weakens the quality assessment. The experimental results in the supplement are insufficient to support the claims of outperformance without direct comparison in the main text.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized, but the placement of all experiments in the supplement severely hinders clarity. The theoretical contributions are clearly presented, but the lack of concrete details about how to choose Φ to ensure convexity (as required by Theorem 1) creates confusion.

**Originality**: The paper introduces novel theoretical frameworks for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, which is a significant contribution to the optimization literature. The application to Supervised PageRank is a good fit, though the problem itself is not entirely new.

**Significance**: The results could be significant for large-scale ranking applications, but the lack of experimental comparisons in the main text and unclear convergence guarantees diminish the perceived significance. The potential impact on search engine ranking is promising but not fully demonstrated.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro**:
- Novel theoretical contributions to constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles
- Theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods
- Practical demonstration of outperformance over state-of-the-art methods

**Con**:
- All experimental results are in the supplement, violating NIPS's self-contained requirement
- Theorem 1's convexity assumption contradicts the non-convex problem formulation
- Theorem 3's convergence interpretation is unclear
- No direct comparison of algorithmic efficiency in the main text

## Recommendation
This paper presents interesting theoretical contributions to constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, but the current version has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being accepted in its current form. The most critical issue is the placement of all experimental results in the supplement, which violates NIPS's requirement for self-contained papers. Additionally, the theoretical analysis contains important gaps regarding convexity assumptions and convergence guarantees.

I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should address these concerns by: (1) moving all experimental results to the main paper, (2) clarifying how to choose Φ to guarantee convexity for Theorem 1, (3) providing a clear interpretation of Theorem 3's bound in terms of convergence to stationary points, and (4) reporting and comparing algorithmic efficiency in the main text. With these revisions, the paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS.
