This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, extending traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors address the non-convex nature of the optimization problem by proposing two two-level methods: a gradient-based approach (GBN) and a gradient-free approach (GFN). A significant contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in the prior state-of-the-art (ref. 21).  The core idea revolves around utilizing an inexact oracle, allowing for efficient approximation of the loss function and its derivatives, which is crucial for large-scale graph problems. The work builds upon existing iterative methods for estimating stationary distributions (ref. 17) and extends them to the context of constrained optimization with inexact information.

This paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem in network analysis and ranking.  Supervised PageRank, as presented, offers a flexible framework for incorporating diverse information into ranking algorithms, going beyond purely structural graph properties. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on PageRank (refs. 18, 11), supervised ranking (ref. 21), and optimization techniques (refs. 16, 7). The use of an inexact oracle is a practical and justifiable approach, especially for large graphs where exact computations are prohibitive. The theoretical analysis, although detailed and relegated to the supplementary material, is a strong point, as it provides confidence in the methods' convergence properties.

The strengths of the paper are several. First, the theoretical guarantees of convergence for both GBN and GFN are a notable achievement, distinguishing this work from previous approaches. Second, the authors clearly articulate the trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity, providing practical insights for implementation. Third, the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world web ranking task. The detailed description of the experimental setup, including the dataset (although not publicly available) and hyperparameter settings, facilitates reproducibility. The acknowledgement of the computational cost and the effort to analyze it is commendable.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the convergence analysis is a strength, the presentation of the mathematical derivations (even referencing the supplement) can be dense and difficult to follow. A symbol table would significantly improve readability, as many symbols are introduced without clear explanation. The experimental evaluation, while showing improvement in loss function values, lacks a direct comparison of ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) with the method in ref. 21. A more comprehensive ranking evaluation would strengthen the claims of improved performance.  Additionally, while the authors state their work generalizes ref. 17, a more direct experimental comparison demonstrating this generalization would be beneficial. The paper could also benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods, such as sensitivity to hyperparameter settings or potential issues with scalability to extremely large graphs. It’s not immediately clear what assumptions are required on the graph structure or feature distributions for the convergence guarantees to hold.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to solving a non-convex optimization problem with theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrated improvement over the state-of-the-art in a real-world ranking task.
*   Clear and detailed presentation of the methods and experimental setup.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with a well-motivated approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Dense mathematical notation and lack of a symbol table hinder readability.
*   Experimental evaluation primarily focuses on loss function values, lacking a direct comparison of ranking metrics.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and potential scalability issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of readability and experimental evaluation, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity of the notation (adding a symbol table) and including a more comprehensive evaluation of ranking quality. A comparison to the original method in ref. 17 to demonstrate generalization would also be valuable.
