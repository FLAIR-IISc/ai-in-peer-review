This paper proposes Supervised PageRank with gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods, formulating the problem as a constrained non-convex optimization. The authors address the challenges of solving this problem with node and edge features by introducing two approaches: a gradient-free method employing an inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle. Both methods are accompanied by theoretical convergence guarantees.

The work builds upon prior research in PageRank variants [18, 11, 8, 9, 19] and Supervised PageRank [21], aiming to incorporate richer information into the ranking process. It acknowledges limitations of existing optimization techniques [16, 7] for this specific problem and proposes solutions tailored to handle constraints and oracle inexactness. The authors position their work as extending the framework of gradient-free [1, 16] and gradient methods [7, 25] to handle these challenges.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of Supervised PageRank as a constrained non-convex optimization is a valuable contribution, providing a solid mathematical framework for incorporating node/edge features.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** Providing convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods is a significant achievement, especially considering the non-convexity of the problem. This distinguishes the work from prior approaches [21] lacking such guarantees.
* **Inexact Oracle Approach:** The use of inexact oracles is pragmatic and addresses the inherent difficulties in calculating exact gradients and function values for large-scale graph problems.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper includes detailed theoretical analysis, developing and proving theorems regarding convergence and complexity bounds for both proposed algorithms.
* **Potential Significance:** Successful application of these methods could improve the effectiveness of ranking algorithms in various domains, including web search and social networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incomplete Presentation:** The most significant weakness is the placement of all experimental results in the supplementary materials. This makes the main paper incomplete and hinders a clear assessment of the practical performance and effectiveness of the proposed methods. A concise summary and key findings from the experiments *must* be included in the main text.
* **Convexity Assumption:** Theorem 1 assumes convexity, but Equation (2.4) is explicitly stated as a non-convex loss function. The authors need to clarify how the choice of Φ guarantees objective convexity within the region of interest or acknowledge the limitation of the theorem's applicability. This is a critical point affecting the validity of the theoretical claims.
* **Convergence Criterion:** Theorem 3 provides a bound on MK(xK - xK+1), but it’s unclear whether this bound is sufficient to ensure convergence of xK to a stationary point. Further explanation of the implications of this bound is needed. Does this imply convergence to a local minimum, a stationary point, or something else?
* **Algorithmic Efficiency:** Supplementary Figure 1 indicates that the gradient-free method (GFNM) converges slowly. The authors should report and compare the algorithmic efficiency (e.g., runtime, number of iterations) of GFNM, GBN, and GBP in the main paper, providing a more complete performance comparison.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions sensitivity to hyperparameters like L, but doesn’t thoroughly discuss their impact or provide guidance on choosing appropriate values.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed methods are well-motivated. However, the incomplete presentation of experimental results severely limits the ability to fully evaluate the practical impact of the work. Addressing the convexity issue, clarifying the convergence criterion, and providing a more thorough analysis of algorithmic efficiency are also crucial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to supervised ranking.
* Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
* Potential to improve ranking performance in real-world applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Incomplete presentation due to placement of all experimental results in the supplement.
* Ambiguity regarding convexity assumptions and convergence criterion.
* Lack of detailed discussion on algorithmic efficiency.



**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.** I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors *must* include a concise summary of the experimental results in the main paper. Clarification of the convexity assumption and convergence criterion is essential. Addressing the algorithmic efficiency concerns would further strengthen the paper. If these issues are adequately addressed, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
