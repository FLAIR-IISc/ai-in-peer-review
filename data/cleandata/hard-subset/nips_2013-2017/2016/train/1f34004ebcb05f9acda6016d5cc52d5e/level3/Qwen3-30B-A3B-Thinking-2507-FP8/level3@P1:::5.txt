

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank parameters through non-convex optimization, proposing gradient-based and gradient-free methods with theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors build upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. While prior work [21] solves this problem using a two-level gradient-based method without convergence guarantees, the authors claim their methods provide theoretically proven convergence rates.

The paper's main strengths lie in its technical contributions. The authors develop a rigorous convergence analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods under an inexact oracle setting, which is a significant theoretical contribution. Their Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 provide precise bounds on the accuracy of the stationary distribution approximation, and Theorems 1-4 establish meaningful convergence rates for their proposed algorithms. The authors correctly identify that existing methods cannot be applied due to the non-convexity, constraints, and inability to calculate exact loss values, which is a valid technical challenge.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. First, there is a critical citation error: the authors refer to the method in [21] as "Supervised PageRank," while the cited work actually uses "semi-supervised PageRank" (see [21] title and abstract), which undermines the paper's scholarly foundation. Second, the experimental section is severely lacking in detail. The authors claim their methods "outperform" state-of-the-art approaches but provide no concrete evidence in the main text, deferring all results to supplementary materials. Key parameters like R (the radius around ϕ̂) and ϕ̂ itself are not explained, and the selection of these critical hyperparameters appears arbitrary. The paper also fails to demonstrate the diversity of their experimental setup, with only one dataset used and no discussion of scalability or generalization across different graph structures.

The writing quality is poor throughout, with grammatical errors and inconsistent notation (e.g., using both "Supervised PageRank" and "Semi-supervised PageRank" interchangeably). The constraint Φ = {ϕ ∈ R^m : ||ϕ - ϕ̂||_2 ≤ R} is problematic, as it restricts the search space to a small ball around ϕ̂, which may severely limit the practical efficacy of the method. The paper makes no attempt to justify why this constraint is appropriate for real-world ranking problems, and the choice of ϕ̂ = e_m (vector of all ones) is not motivated.

The significance of the paper is questionable. While the theoretical contributions are sound, the practical impact is not convincingly demonstrated. The authors claim their methods "can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine," but provide no evidence of this in the main text. The experimental results (which are only in the appendix) show marginal improvements on a single dataset with no statistical significance testing reported in the main text, and the claimed "significant outperformance" lacks supporting evidence. The paper also fails to compare against more recent alternatives to the baseline [21].

The originality is somewhat limited, as the authors essentially adapt existing gradient-free [16] and gradient-based [7] methods to a constrained non-convex setting, without introducing fundamentally new algorithmic concepts. The core contribution appears to be the application of these existing frameworks to the Supervised PageRank problem with appropriate convergence analysis, rather than novel optimization techniques.

The clarity of the paper is poor. Key technical details are relegated to the appendix, and the main text lacks proof sketches or intuitive explanations of the theoretical results. The experimental methodology is inadequately described, and the results section is underdeveloped.

Strengths: Strong theoretical analysis, well-motivated problem statement, clear identification of limitations in prior work.

Weaknesses: Critical citation error, poor experimental validation, lack of detail in key methodological choices, questionable practical significance, poor writing quality.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Theoretical contribution is non-trivial and fills a gap in the literature regarding convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning. The paper correctly identifies limitations in previous work and provides a rigorous solution.

Con-acceptance arguments: The paper fails to demonstrate practical impact through sufficient empirical evidence. The experimental section is inadequate for a NIPS paper. The theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental, building on existing frameworks rather than introducing new concepts. The citation error undermines credibility.

Overall, while the paper presents technically sound theoretical results, the lack of convincing empirical validation and poor presentation significantly diminish the paper's contribution to the field. The theoretical advances are valuable but insufficiently supported by practical evidence to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to address the experimental shortcomings, clarify the methodology, and provide a more convincing demonstration of the practical value of the proposed algorithms.
