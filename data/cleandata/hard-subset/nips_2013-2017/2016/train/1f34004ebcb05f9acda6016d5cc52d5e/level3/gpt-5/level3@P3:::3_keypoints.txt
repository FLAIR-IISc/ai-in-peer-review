- Paper addresses supervised PageRank with feature-parameterized restart/transition probabilities and minimizes a nonconvex pairwise hinge-squared loss over queries.
- Proposes two two-level inexact-oracle optimization schemes: GBN (gradient-based) and GFN (gradient-free).
- GBN: lower level uses Nesterov–Nemirovski linear solver to approximate stationary distribution and derivatives; upper level is an adaptive projected gradient with inexact first-order oracle and convergence-to-stationary-point guarantees.
- GFN: lower level approximates loss via same linear solver; upper level is a randomized constrained zero-order method adapted to bounded-error evaluations with expected-decrease and convergence-rate bounds.
- Both methods avoid forming/exponentiating large matrices, operate with sparse graph operations, and give explicit trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and overall convergence.
- Empirical results on a proprietary Yandex browsing-graph dataset show both methods improve the training loss over GBP; hyperparameters and stopping rules are provided.
- Builds on Nesterov & Nemirovski for the lower-level solver and extends inexact-oracle theory (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Nesterov–Spokoiny) to constrained, nonconvex upper-level settings.
- Strength: Strong theoretical analysis with clear convergence-rate bounds and explicit complexity coupling between levels.
- Strength: Careful algorithm design — linear-convergent lower-level solver is more efficient than power method; GBN uses adaptive backtracking-like step selection.
- Strength: Practicality — avoids large matrix derivatives (GFN avoids derivatives altogether) and works with sparse operations.
- Strength: Promising empirical performance on reported training loss and well-specified hyperparameters.
- Weakness: Evaluation reports only training objective; lacks standard ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP, P@k) on held-out queries despite claims of ranking-quality gains.
- Weakness: Limited baselines — comparison only to GBP; missing comparisons to ref.17’s learning setup, power-method-based learner, and common learning-to-rank methods (e.g., LambdaMART, RankSVM).
- Weakness: GFN analysis assumes local convexity on the feasible ball, which may be unverifiable for this nonconvex objective; robustness when this fails is not discussed.
- Weakness: Clarity issues — heavy notation with some symbols introduced before definition; certain constants (e.g., β1) only in supplement.
- Weakness: Ambiguous guarantee wording — GFN’s decrease guarantee is in expectation/best-so-far, not per-iteration monotonic decrease, and needs clearer statement.
- Clarity requests: add a symbol/notation table, early definitions of key symbols, brief pseudocode for lower-level derivative approximation, and a concise summary of parameter/step-size choices tied to target ε.
- Originality: Nontrivial extension of inexact-oracle optimization to constrained and nonconvex problems and a novel gradient-free adaptation for bounded-error oracles in this application.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for large-scale supervised PageRank if ranking metrics and runtime benefits over power-method approaches are demonstrated.
- Experimental requests: report NDCG@k, MAP, P@k with statistical tests; provide wall-clock times and lower-level iteration counts vs baselines; show sensitivity to ε, L, α, and graph sparsity; ensure fair stopping criteria and budgets.
- Methodology requests: clarify GFN guarantees, summarize the dependence/scale of constants (β1, N1, N2) in main text, and discuss memory complexity and sparsity assumptions.
- Reproducibility request: release synthetic or anonymized data subsets and code for the two-level solvers with default hyperparameters.
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous inexact-oracle theory, practical large-graph algorithms, and explicit hyperparameterization and convergence estimates.
- Cons for acceptance: empirical focus on loss without ranking metrics, limited baselines, unverifiable assumptions (local convexity), and readability/notation issues.
- Overall: Solid optimization contribution with promising empirical signs; acceptance would be strengthened by added ranking metrics, broader baselines, clarified assumptions, and improved exposition.
