This paper studies learning Supervised PageRank models with node- and edge-level features by framing training as a non-convex loss minimization and proposing two two-level solvers: (i) an adaptive gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a random gradient-free method with an inexact zero-order oracle. The lower level computes approximations to query-dependent stationary distributions and, for the gradient method, their derivatives. Unlike prior work that relied on power iterations (e.g., [21]) without convergence guarantees for the overall learning procedure, the authors adopt the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method for Markov chains [14] and generalize it to derivative computations, enabling controllable oracle accuracy. The upper-level algorithms provide convergence rate guarantees (to stationarity in the non-convex case), and the authors analyze the trade-off between the lower-level accuracy and overall complexity. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph ranking task show improvements over the state-of-the-art gradient method based on power iterations.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on supervised random-walk/PageRank approaches [3, 10, 12, 21] that incorporate features into restart and transition probabilities, and on inexact-oracle optimization, extending Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov’s framework to constrained, possibly nonconvex settings with unknown smoothness constants. The random gradient-free method follows the smoothing/one-point feedback ideas of Nesterov & Spokoiny [13] but adapts them to constrained domains with bounded oracle error. For nonconvex first/zeroth-order methods, the work relates to Ghadimi & Lan [7]; the novelty is in handling deterministic inexact oracles from controlled Markov-chain approximations and deriving end-to-end complexity bounds for the two-level scheme.

Strengths
- Solid theoretical contribution: precise inexact-oracle definitions, adaptive projected gradient with backtracking on the oracle “L,” convergence-to-stationarity guarantees in the nonconvex composite setting, and complexity bounds that include the cost of lower-level approximations.
- Lower-level advance: adopting the linearly convergent scheme for PageRank and generalizing it to derivative computation, with explicit error controls and complexity (Lemmas 1–2).
- Practical relevance: avoiding per-entry differentiation of large transition matrices (via gradient-free) and enabling controlled accuracy in both gradient-free and gradient-based solvers.
- Empirical evidence: consistent improvements in the loss over the state-of-the-art training procedure; analysis of sensitivity to algorithmic hyperparameters (ε, L0).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Runtime/reporting: The paper reports function values and iteration counts but not wall-clock time or throughput. Please report runtime (CPU/GPU specs, parallelism), and break down time spent in lower vs. upper levels for GBN/GFN/GBP across Q1/Q2 splits. End-to-end latency matters for search settings.
- Evaluation metrics: Beyond the square-loss proxy, standard information retrieval metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) on labeled pairs would strengthen the practical impact. The paper mentions p-values but not ranking metrics.
- Assumptions and clarity: GFN relies on f being convex on the ball Φ despite the broader problem being nonconvex; this local convexity assumption should be justified empirically (e.g., convexity diagnostics, sensitivity to R) or relaxed. The choice and estimation of L (set to 1e−4) and β1 should be clarified; provide guidance or adaptive estimation strategies.
- Baselines: Compare against additional learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., RankSVM/LambdaMART on the same features) and alternative PageRank learning schemes (e.g., BrowseRank variants) to situate gains in a broader context.
- Lower-level ablations: Include plots showing the trade-off between N, N1, N2, oracle accuracy (δ1, δ2), and end-to-end convergence/runtime; compare power vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski quantitatively in time, not only in iterations.
- Constraints and projection: Φ is an ℓ2-ball centered at 1 with radius 0.99 to stay in the positive orthant; please state whether projections are guaranteed to preserve positivity and how violations are handled (e.g., projection onto the intersection of the ball and Rm++). Also clarify α handling (fixed vs. learned) and sensitivity.
- Reproducibility: Provide implementation details (sparsity handling, memory footprint, stopping criteria for inner loops), random seeds, and code if possible.

Quality
- The technical development is careful and well-grounded; the inexact-oracle inequalities and backtracking are appropriate. Proofs are deferred but appear standard extensions with nontrivial bookkeeping. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though runtime and ranking metrics are missing.

Clarity
- Generally clear but dense. Suggestions: define all constants when introduced (β1), unify notation for norms and dimensions, and add a short “practical recipe” summarizing how to set ε, δ1, δ2, N, N1, N2 from a target accuracy and budget.

Originality
- Moderately high. While the core tools (random smoothing, inexact oracles) are known, the constrained/inexact extensions, derivative computation scheme, and full two-level complexity analysis for Supervised PageRank training appear novel.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for large-scale graph ranking and other problems requiring stationary distributions with features. The empirical scale is modest; stronger runtime and metric-based evidence would better demonstrate practical significance.

Pros
- Theoretical guarantees for both gradient and gradient-free training with inexact lower-level computations.
- Clear two-level design with controllable accuracy and complexity.
- Empirical improvements over prior supervised PageRank training.

Cons
- No wall-clock runtime or scalability evaluation.
- Reliance on local convexity for the gradient-free method; limited empirical validation of this assumption.
- Limited evaluation metrics and baselines.

Questions for authors
- What are the wall-clock times per method on Q1j/Q2j subsets? How do costs split between f and ∇f oracles?
- How sensitive are results to α and to the choice of Φ (center and radius)?
- Can the methods learn α jointly with ϕ, and would the guarantees extend?
- Do projections ever leave the positive orthant? If so, how is feasibility enforced?

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding runtime results and at least one standard ranking metric. Confidence: 0.7 (familiar with optimization and ranking, not with the specific Yandex dataset).
