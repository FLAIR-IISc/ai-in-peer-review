- Proposes two two-level optimization algorithms (gradient-based and random gradient-free) for Supervised PageRank with formal convergence rate guarantees.  
- Frames the problem using an "inexact oracle" and uses a linearly convergent lower-level method to approximate stationary distribution and derivatives.  
- Provides rigorous analysis connecting low-level approximation accuracy to overall convergence and computational complexity.  
- Addresses an important, well-motivated problem (learning feature-rich graph ranking functions) with practical relevance.  
- Methodological framework is sound and flexible, covering both gradient-free and adaptive gradient-based settings.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: first formal convergence guarantees for learning non-convex Supervised PageRank models.  
- Paper presentation is poor: grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and heavy reliance on supplementary material for key proofs and algorithm descriptions.  
- Inconsistent naming (e.g., "Supervised PageRank" vs "Supervised Nested PageRank") reduces clarity.  
- Empirical validation is insufficient: missing critical hyperparameters and experimental design choices (e.g., initial ϕ̂ and radius R).  
- Experimental analysis is superficial: reports loss values but lacks standard ranking metrics (NDCG/MAP) and in-depth discussion of results.  
- Experiments are limited to a single dataset, raising questions about generalization to other graphs.  
- Theoretical assumptions are under-discussed and may be unrealistic (e.g., required local convexity for gradient-free method).  
- Strong constraint that solution must lie within a Euclidean ball around a pre-selected ϕ̂ is unexplained and may limit practical effectiveness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel formal guarantees, rigorous inexact oracle framework, and practical importance of the problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak and non-reproducible empirical validation, poor clarity, and questionable practical applicability due to strong assumptions.  
- Overall: theoretically strong work that fails to convincingly demonstrate practical impact due to weak experiments and presentation issues.
