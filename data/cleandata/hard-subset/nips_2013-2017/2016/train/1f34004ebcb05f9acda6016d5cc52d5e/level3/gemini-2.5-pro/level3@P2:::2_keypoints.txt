- Problem: Learning parameters of a Supervised PageRank model where node/edge features define transition/restart probabilities, yielding a non-convex constrained loss dependent on a Markov chain stationary distribution (inexact objective/gradient).

- Methods: Two two-level optimization algorithms using an inexact oracle — a random gradient-free method adapted for constraints and known oracle error, and a gradient-based non-convex composite method generalized to an inexact first-order oracle with adaptive step-size.

- Main theoretical contribution: Provable convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods, a property missing from the cited state-of-the-art.

- Experimental claim: Algorithms are validated on a real-world web page ranking task, but experimental results are not included in the main text.

- Strength — theoretical soundness/novelty: Rigorous bridge between optimization theory for inexact oracles and practical learning-to-rank, providing novel guarantees.

- Strength — problem formulation: Practical, principled formulation of Supervised PageRank and insightful analysis of the trade-off between lower-level oracle accuracy and upper-level complexity.

- Strength — method motivation: Complementary choice of gradient-free (avoids expensive derivatives) and gradient-based (sample-efficient) methods with thorough analysis.

- Weakness — not self-contained: Critical experimental results (Table 1, Figure 1) are omitted from the main paper, making empirical claims unverifiable.

- Weakness — gradient-free convexity assumption: Convergence analysis assumes f(ϕ) is convex on Φ, relying on an unverifiable “small vicinity” around a local minimum and an unclear choice of R.

- Weakness — gradient-based convergence criterion: Theorem 3 bounds ‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖^2, but the paper does not clearly relate this to standard stationarity measures like ‖∇ψ(x)‖ being small.

- Weakness — practical efficiency evaluation: Paper lacks comparisons of convergence speed (wall-clock time or oracle calls); supplement hints gradient-free may converge slowly.

- Argument for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution with convergence guarantees for an important, practical ranking problem.

- Argument for acceptance: Novel connection between optimization with inexact oracles and real-world ranking, addressing gaps in prior work.

- Argument for acceptance: Well-motivated algorithms that address shortcomings of existing methods.

- Argument against acceptance: Main-text omission of experimental results prevents assessment of empirical performance.

- Argument against acceptance: Unjustified local convexity undermines practical applicability of the gradient-free guarantee.

- Argument against acceptance: Insufficient clarity on how the gradient-based stopping criterion ensures standard stationarity.
