Summary
The paper tackles learning in Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities depend on node and edge features via linear parametrization. The learning objective is a non-convex pairwise square loss over query-dependent graphs. The key technical contribution is to cast the lower-level computation (stationary distribution and its derivatives) as an inexact oracle, and to develop two upper-level solvers with provable convergence: (i) an adaptive gradient-based method for constrained, possibly non-convex composite optimization with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a randomized gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, the paper advocates the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate stationary distributions (and generalizes it to derivatives), arguing it has better complexity guarantees than classical power iteration. The paper provides convergence-rate bounds and complexity estimates for both two-level algorithms, including a trade-off analysis between oracle accuracy and overall complexity. Experiments on a real Yandex browsing-graph ranking task suggest both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art gradient-based approach for Supervised PageRank [21], with the gradient-free method achieving the largest empirical gains.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Supervised PageRank [21] and a line of feature-augmented random-walk ranking models [3,4,6,10,12,20]. For lower-level computation, it replaces the power method used in PageRank/HITS variants [8,9,18] and in [21] with the Nesterov–Nemirovski method [17], and generalizes it to derivative estimation following ideas from iterative eigenderivative computation [23,24]. For upper-level optimization, it extends inexact-oracle frameworks: zero-order methods [1,13,16] to constrained, inexact settings; and first-order composite methods [7,25] to non-convex constrained problems with an unknown oracle “Lipschitz constant,” using an adaptive line-search style strategy. Compared to stochastic non-convex optimization [7], the novel piece is handling the inexactness induced by approximate Markov chain solves under constraints.

Strengths
- Clear identification of the inexact-oracle nature of the problem and end-to-end complexity accounting across both levels.
- Theoretical convergence guarantees for both a gradient-free and a gradient-based solver in settings that include constraints and inexact lower-level computations—an improvement over [21], which lacked convergence guarantees due to inexact gradients.
- Thoughtful trade-off analysis between lower-level accuracy (iterations of [17] and its derivative generalization) and overall complexity.
- Empirical evaluation on a real-world, query-dependent browsing graph from a major search engine; the proposed methods outperform the prior gradient-based state of the art.
- Practical benefit: the gradient-free method avoids expensive derivative computations on large matrices, and the gradient-based method reduces tuning burden via adaptivity.

Weaknesses
- Convexity assumption for the gradient-free upper level: Section 4 applies the method under local convexity in Φ around a local minimum. However, the overall loss is non-convex. The paper should clarify how convexity is ensured or detected in practice, and discuss robustness when f is not convex on Φ.
- Limited baselines and metrics: Comparisons are primarily against the specific gradient-based method of [21] and the untuned model. Modern learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART) or even simple pairwise RankSVM are not included, and evaluation relies on optimization loss rather than standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP) on held-out labels.
- Runtime reporting is missing: the paper reports iteration counts and some complexity derivations, but not wall-clock times for the lower-level solvers and overall training. This is important given the claim of improved efficiency and the cost of Markov chain computations across many queries.
- Some technical components are deferred to the supplement (constants like β1, proofs, derivative method complexity). The main text could benefit from more intuition and concrete parameter guidance.
- Notation density and readability: Section 3 packs many definitions and symbols; the exposition could be streamlined.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The inexact oracle analyses appear careful, with proofs and complexity bounds stated. The adaptive gradient method for a non-convex composite problem with an inexact oracle is well-motivated; the stationarity criterion ‖MK(ϕK−ϕK+1)‖2 ≤ ε is standard. The gradient-free analysis under inexact zero-order oracle and constraints is a meaningful extension.
- Experimental support: Results indicate consistent improvements, and the lower-level method converges faster than power iteration. However, missing runtime and broader baselines limit the empirical evidence for practical significance.

Clarity
- Generally clear problem setup and relation to prior work. Some sections (3, 5) are heavy with notation; more intuition and a small worked example of the two-level accuracy tuning would help. The assumptions (e.g., feasibility set Φ intersecting positive orthant, scale invariance) are reasonable and well stated.

Originality
- The combination of constrained, inexact-oracle zero- and first-order methods with a principled lower-level stationary-distribution solver is novel in this application. Extending random gradient-free methods to constrained, inexact settings and the adaptive inexact first-order method for non-convex problems are original and non-trivial.

Significance
- The setting—learning feature-augmented PageRank on query-dependent graphs—is practically important. Theoretical guarantees where previous work had none is valuable. Wider adoption would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons, runtime analysis, and evaluation with standard ranking metrics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report end-to-end wall-clock time and memory usage for all methods, including breakdowns: lower-level (stationary distribution and derivatives) and upper-level optimization, across Q1/Q2/Q3 subsets.
- Evaluate with standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) on held-out labeled pairs in addition to training loss.
- Add baselines beyond [21], such as RankSVM/LambdaMART or other supervised random-walk variants.
- Clarify the convexity assumption in Section 4: when is Φ convex enough for the theory to apply? Provide diagnostics used in practice.
- Provide more guidance on setting ε, δ, τ, and lower-level iterations N, N1, N2; include sensitivity to these hyperparameters.
- Improve exposition by collecting key constants (like β1) in the main text and adding intuition for the derivative computation method.
- Discuss robustness to feature scaling and the effect of the scale invariance noted in Section 3.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical advances for inexact-oracle optimization under constraints with non-convex objectives.
- End-to-end complexity accounting bridging lower- and upper-level computations.
- Empirical improvements on a real, large-scale dataset.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing runtime results and limited empirical comparisons/metrics.
- Dependence on a local convexity assumption (for the gradient-free method) without practical verification.
- Exposition could be clearer in key algorithmic components and hyperparameter choices.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical contributions and practical relevance are strong; the paper would be substantially strengthened by reporting runtime, broader baselines, and standard ranking metrics. Confidence: medium.
