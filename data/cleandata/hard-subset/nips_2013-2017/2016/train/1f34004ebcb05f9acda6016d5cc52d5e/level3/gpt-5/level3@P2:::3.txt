Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning in Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The optimization objective is a pairwise square loss over query-dependent graphs. The authors propose two two-level algorithms:

- A gradient-based method (GBN): a projected, adaptive gradient scheme for possibly nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle. The lower level uses the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution and a related iterative scheme to approximate its derivatives, yielding controlled inaccuracies that fit an inexact oracle model. The authors prove convergence to a stationary point in terms of a proximal-gradient norm bound and derive complexity bounds that account for the accuracy/complexity trade-offs of the lower-level computations.

- A gradient-free method (GFN): a random gradient-free projected method over a Euclidean ball with a biased finite-difference estimator on the sphere and an inexact zero-order oracle. They extend prior gradient-free results (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny; Agarwal et al.; Matyas) by handling constraints and bounded oracle noise, and provide complexity bounds.

The work builds directly on: Supervised PageRank (and related supervised random walks), PageRank/HITS and efficient stationary distribution computation (Nesterov–Nemirovski vs. power method), inexact oracle methods for smooth optimization (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), nonconvex first-/zeroth-order methods (Ghadimi–Lan), and random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny). The main novelty is integrating the linearly convergent stationary-distribution solver with upper-level optimization under an inexact oracle framework for both gradient-based and gradient-free settings, providing end-to-end rates and explicit complexity trade-offs.

Quality
- Theory: The lemmas on approximating f and ∇f with controllable accuracy (via N, N1, N2) are plausible and address gaps in prior work that used imprecise power methods. The inexact-oracle convergence results for the projected gradient method with unknown “Lipschitz constant” and the constrained gradient-free method appear technically sound; proofs are in the supplement. However, the gradient-free analysis in Section 4.2 relies on an assumption of local convexity of f on Φ, which is strong given the overall nonconvexity; this limitation should be made explicit in the abstract and conclusion. For GBN, the guarantee is on the prox-gradient norm (stationarity), not objective suboptimality; this should be clearly stated when claiming “convergence rate guarantees.”
- Empirics: The experiments use a real Yandex user-browsing dataset and report loss values; the methods reportedly outperform a state-of-the-art gradient-based (GBP) approach. However, ranking quality metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) are not reported for the comparisons, despite the task being ranking. The claim that both methods “outperform in ranking quality” should be supported by standard retrieval metrics and statistical tests on Q2. Wall-clock times and iteration counts at both levels would clarify practical efficiency. Baseline tuning details for GBP are limited and the stopping rule “difference < −1e−5” reads oddly (suggesting increases); please clarify.

Clarity
- The paper is notation-heavy; a symbol table would significantly improve readability. Some symbols (e.g., N) are used before being defined, and N, N1, N2 overloading is confusing.
- Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted (authors/years missing). Algorithm numbering and references to supplementary algorithms could be streamlined.
- Minor typos/grammar occur throughout.

Originality
- Extending random gradient-free methods to constrained problems with bounded, deterministic inexactness and adapting a projected adaptive gradient scheme with inexact first-order oracle to nonconvex constrained settings are novel in the context of supervised PageRank learning. The explicit end-to-end complexity that couples lower-level Markov chain computation with upper-level optimization is also a useful contribution. The lower-level method itself follows Nesterov–Nemirovski.

Significance
- Learning feature-augmented PageRank with provable convergence and controlled accuracy is practically relevant. If supported by ranking metrics and timing comparisons, the empirical advances could be impactful for graph-based ranking systems. The theoretical framework may be reusable in other inexact-oracle learning problems on graphs.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and integration of lower-level approximation with upper-level optimization.
- Convergence-rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches under inexact oracles.
- Complexity bounds exposing accuracy/compute trade-offs.
- Real-world dataset; hyperparameters reported.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation focuses on loss rather than standard ranking metrics; limited baseline tuning details and no runtime comparisons.
- Reliance on local convexity for gradient-free method in the application section is a strong, unstated limitation.
- Clarity issues: heavy notation, symbols used before definition, missing symbol table; incomplete references.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Report NDCG/MAP (at multiple cutoffs) and p-values on Q2 for GBN/GFN vs. GBP and the untuned baseline; include wall-clock times and number of lower-level iterations (N, N1, N2) used.
- Clarify the GBP setup: step sizes tried, stopping rule (sign), number of power iterations and whether they are tuned for equal compute.
- Provide a direct empirical comparison to the power method at the lower level and to alternative upper-level optimizers (e.g., Ghadimi–Lan SGD variants or standard projected gradient with exact/approximate gradients), to substantiate advantages beyond the specific GBP baseline.
- Discuss how L, β1, and δ are estimated in practice; include a sensitivity analysis. Explain the practical implications of the local convexity assumption in Section 4.2 and whether GFN is used outside that regime.
- Add a symbol table and unify notation for N/N1/N2. Ensure all references are complete (authors, venue, year).
- Consider releasing a synthetic or public dataset and code to improve reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
Promising and well-motivated theoretical contributions with a plausible path to practice. However, the empirical evaluation should include standard ranking metrics and runtime comparisons, and the clarity/notation issues need attention. I lean toward a weak accept if the authors can strengthen the experimental section and clarify assumptions; otherwise, borderline.
