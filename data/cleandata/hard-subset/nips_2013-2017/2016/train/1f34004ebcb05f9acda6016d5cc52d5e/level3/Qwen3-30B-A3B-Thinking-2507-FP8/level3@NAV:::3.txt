

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods (GBN and GFN) for supervised PageRank learning, addressing a non-convex loss-minimization problem. Building on Nesterov and Nemirovski's framework (ref. 17), the authors develop gradient-based and gradient-free approaches that operate with inexact oracle information, avoiding exact objective function evaluations. GBN provides theoretical convergence rate guarantees for non-convex problems, while GFN ensures a guaranteed decrease in loss function values. Both methods avoid computing large matrices while achieving superior empirical performance in ranking quality compared to state-of-the-art.

The paper's strengths include strong theoretical contributions with convergence rate analysis for both methods, clear method descriptions in Sections 2-5, and comprehensive empirical validation on real-world ranking tasks. The authors carefully address the trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity, providing meaningful complexity bounds. The work significantly extends Nesterov and Nemirovski's framework to a novel application domain with practical implications for large-scale ranking systems.

However, the paper has notable clarity issues. The dense notation (e.g., N used before definition) makes the paper difficult to follow, and a symbol table would greatly improve readability. Additionally, the experimental comparison with GBP only evaluates loss function values, not ranking quality, and fails to directly compare with ref. 17 as requested. While the datasets are described clearly enough for reproduction, they are not publicly available.

The paper excels in Quality with rigorous theoretical analysis and solid empirical results. Originality is strong, as the authors extend inexact oracle optimization to supervised PageRank with novel theoretical guarantees. Significance is high, as the methods have practical applications for improving search engine ranking. Clarity is the main weakness, though fixable with minor revisions.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution that advances optimization theory for large-scale ranking problems. The theoretical guarantees and empirical performance make it a valuable addition to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and completing the experimental comparison with ref. 17.
