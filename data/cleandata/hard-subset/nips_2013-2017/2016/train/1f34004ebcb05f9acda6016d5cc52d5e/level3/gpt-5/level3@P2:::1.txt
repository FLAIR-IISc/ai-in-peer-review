Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies learning a feature-driven random-walk–based ranking model (Supervised PageRank) where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features with learnable parameters. The objective is a pairwise square loss over query-dependent graphs. Because exact objective values and gradients are unavailable (stationary distributions and their derivatives are only approximable), the authors adopt a two-level approach: a lower level computes approximations to the stationary distribution (and its parameter derivatives), and an upper level optimizes the parameters with inexact oracle information.

On the lower level, they use the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution and extend it to approximate derivatives, with explicit accuracy–iteration trade-offs. They prove that the loss and its gradient can be approximated to any prescribed accuracy (Lemmas 1–2) and quantify arithmetic complexity.

On the upper level, they propose:
- A random gradient-free method for constrained problems with a bounded-error zero-order oracle, using a sphere-randomized finite-difference estimator. They prove convergence for convex objectives (Theorem 1) and apply it under a local convexity assumption of f within the feasible ball (Theorem 2).
- An adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly non-convex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle (Definition via inequality (5.1)), with a backtracking-like mechanism to adapt to an unknown “Lipschitz” constant of the inexact oracle (Algorithm 2, Theorem 3). They instantiate the inexact oracle for Supervised PageRank using their lower-level approximations and derive overall complexity (Theorem 4).

Empirically, on a Yandex user-browsing, query-dependent graph dataset (≈600 queries; ~11.7K nodes, ~7.5K edges overall; 26 node and 52 edge features), their gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) upper-level methods outperform the prior gradient-based baseline (GBP) that uses the power method and unguaranteed inexact gradients. They report statistically significant reductions in the training loss across subsets, and fewer upper-level iterations than GBP for some settings.

The work builds on and extends inexact oracle methods (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov, 2014) and random gradient-free optimization (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny, 2015) by addressing constrained problems with bounded oracle error and non-convexity. It also relates to non-convex zeroth/first-order methods (Ghadimi–Lan, 2013) and PageRank computation methods (Nesterov–Nemirovski). Relative to prior Supervised PageRank work, the main advance is to provide convergence-rate guarantees and explicit complexity bounds end-to-end.

Quality:
- Theory: The lower-level approximation bounds and the inexact-oracle constructions are carefully stated. The convergence analyses (Theorems 1–4) appear sound and connect the oracle accuracies to overall complexity. I did not check every derivation in the supplement, but the arguments are consistent with the cited frameworks.
- Experiments: The empirical section shows consistent improvements in the training loss and some runtime proxies (iterations). However, the evaluation would be stronger with standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/ERR on held-out judgments), wall-clock timings and memory, and a more thorough study of the lower–upper-level accuracy trade-off beyond the supplement. Reproducibility is limited by the proprietary dataset and lack of code.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with crisp problem statements and algorithms. The notation is heavy (e.g., multiple Ns, β1, norms) and could benefit from a consolidated notation table. The conclusions are brief; expanding the discussion of practical implications and limitations would help. It would also help to present the optimization frameworks in a more general context early, then specialize to Supervised PageRank to emphasize generality vs. problem-specific aspects.

Originality:
- The key ingredients (inexact oracle framework, random gradient-free smoothing, adaptive projected gradient) are known, but the paper provides non-trivial adaptations: constrained settings with bounded, known oracle error; extension to non-convex via stationarity; and a derivative-approximation scheme tied to the random-walk structure. The combination and the end-to-end complexity analysis for the Supervised PageRank learning problem are valuable, though the conceptual novelty relative to Nesterov–Spokoiny (random gradient-free) and Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov (inexact oracles) is moderate. Please clarify the precise differences from these works and why existing analyses do not directly apply.

Significance:
- Convergence guarantees and explicit complexity for learning feature-augmented random-walk models are important for large-scale ranking. The empirical gains over the prior baseline are encouraging. The practical impact would be clearer with standard ranking metrics, runtime/throughput on larger graphs, and comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., Ghadimi–Lan-style nonconvex first-order with approximate gradients; power-method vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski in wall-clock).

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Report NDCG/ERR@k on Q2, alongside loss, to demonstrate improvements translate to ranking quality.
- Provide wall-clock time and per-iteration cost comparisons (GFN vs. GBN vs. GBP), including dependence on m, s, |Q|.
- Expand the analysis of the trade-off between lower-level accuracies (N, N1, N2) and total runtime; include ablations in the main text.
- Discuss sensitivity to α and to the choice of the feasible radius R; justify positivity constraints and the scale-invariance handling.
- Clarify the role and magnitude of β1 and other constants in Lemma 2; are these practical to estimate in real systems?
- Situate the gradient-free estimator choice (sphere vs. Gaussian) and constrained domain handling explicitly relative to Nesterov–Spokoiny; explain why prior results are insufficient.
- Consider comparing to simple stochastic finite-difference baselines and to projected gradient with power-method gradients but with your adaptive backtracking.
- Improve the related work section with more precise attributions (years/venues) and connections to recent NIPS papers on inexact oracles and nonconvex optimization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical development of inexact-oracle methods in a constrained/nonconvex setting.
- End-to-end complexity bounds for a practically relevant ranking problem.
- Empirical improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline, with statistical significance.
- Clear two-level design and analysis of accuracy–cost trade-offs.

Cons against acceptance:
- Conceptual novelty is moderate given strong reliance on established frameworks.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: no standard ranking metrics, limited runtime analysis, proprietary data with no code.
- Some assumptions (local convexity for GFN, oracle constants) and constants lack practical illustration.
- Heavy notation and brevity in conclusions/positioning; contributions relative to Nesterov–Spokoiny and Devolder et al. could be made crisper.

Overall: A careful and useful contribution that strengthens the theoretical foundations of learning Supervised PageRank with inexact computations, with promising but somewhat limited experiments. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors strengthen the empirical evaluation and clarify the novelty and practical relevance of their theoretical advances.
