This paper studies convergence guarantees for learning a supervised PageRank-style ranking model in which node and edge weights depend on features via a parameterized Markov random walk. Because the loss and its gradient can only be computed approximately (through stationary distributions and their derivatives), existing methods that require exact objective values are not directly applicable. The authors therefore propose a two-level scheme: (i) a linearly convergent estimator of the stationary distribution (following Nesterov–Nemirovski) that enables approximating the loss and gradient to any prescribed precision; and (ii) upper-level optimization methods that use this inexact oracle—both a random gradient-free method and an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, potentially non-convex problems, with convergence to a stationary point. The main technical contribution is adapting inexact-oracle gradient approaches to constrained settings where function values are only available up to a known accuracy, and quantifying the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity. Empirically, on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset, the proposed methods outperform a prior supervised PageRank baseline.

Quality: The paper is technically careful; the oracle constructions, error propagation, and complexity bounds are well motivated, and the convergence analyses for both zero- and first-order methods appear sound. The reliance on a local convexity assumption to deploy the gradient-free algorithm (and the inexact composite framework) is clearly stated. That said, the experimental section could be stronger: baselines are limited (e.g., no comparison to alternative graph-based learning-to-rank methods), stopping/tuning protocols may risk test-set peeking, and the practical impact of constants in the complexity bounds is not fully explored.

Clarity: The paper is well written overall and the proofs seem correct (though not all were checked line-by-line). A conclusion section is missing. Presenting the optimization framework in a more general form upfront would better position the contribution relative to inexact-oracle literature (e.g., Devolder et al., 2014; Nesterov, 2013; Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2015).

Originality: The high-level ideas build on known inexact-oracle and random gradient-free methods, and on supervised PageRank. The novelty lies in adapting these to constrained non-convex problems with computable-precision function values and integrating them into a practical two-level learner. This is incremental but nontrivial.

Significance: The results are relevant to large-scale graph ranking where exact objectives are unavailable. The theoretical guarantees fill a gap and may be useful beyond this application.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid and carefully executed theory for inexact oracles under constraints
- Clear two-level design with tunable accuracy and complexity trade-offs
- Empirical gains over a supervised PageRank baseline

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to prior inexact-oracle and gradient-free work
- Experimental evaluation is narrow; tuning protocol and baselines could be stronger
- Missing conclusion; clarity could improve by framing the general optimization problem earlier
