## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a graph-based ranking approach incorporating node and edge features. The core challenge lies in the non-convexity of the optimization problem and the difficulty of obtaining exact objective function values, motivating a two-level optimization scheme. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, built upon the concept of an inexact oracle, and provide convergence guarantees for both. The first level estimates the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, while the second level employs a gradient method for constrained non-convex optimization.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical contributions. Adapting inexact-oracle gradient approaches to constrained problems with computable function values up to a known precision is a valuable addition to the optimization toolbox. The convergence analysis for both methods is well-developed and appears correct, although a full verification of all proofs was not possible within the scope of this review. The paper is also well-written and clearly presents the methodology and theoretical results. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods on a web page ranking task, outperforming the state-of-the-art.

However, the novelty of the work is somewhat limited. The core optimization ideas bear a strong resemblance to Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), and the supervised PageRank framework itself has been explored in prior work (e.g., Zhukovskii et al., 2016). While the authors successfully adapt and extend existing techniques, the paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new algorithmic approach. Furthermore, the presentation of the optimization problem feels somewhat specific to the PageRank context. Framing it in a more general constrained optimization setting would better highlight the broader applicability and contribution of their inexact oracle approach. 

Minor issues include the lack of a concluding section summarizing the key findings and potential future directions. While the paper is thorough, a concise conclusion would improve its overall impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees for both proposed methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the methodology.
*   Empirical results demonstrate improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Addresses a practical challenge in learning graph-based ranking models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; builds heavily on existing optimization techniques.
*   Problem formulation is specific, obscuring broader applicability.
*   Missing conclusion section.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed contribution to the field of optimization for graph-based ranking. While the novelty is moderate, the theoretical results and empirical validation are strong. I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the missing conclusion and consider presenting the optimization problem in a more general context to better emphasize the contribution of their inexact oracle approach.
