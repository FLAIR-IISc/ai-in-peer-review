This paper tackles supervised PageRank learning via a two-level optimization scheme that uses inexact oracles on the lower level and principled first- or zero-order updates on the upper level. The lower level approximates stationary distributions using the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method (ref. 17), avoiding costly matrix powers; the authors also extend it to approximate gradients. On the upper level, they propose (i) a gradient-based method (GBN) with convergence-rate guarantees to a stationary point in the nonconvex, constrained, inexact-oracle setting, and (ii) a gradient-free method (GFN) with guaranteed loss decrease and complexity bounds under oracle error. Both methods provide explicit convergence-rate estimates without requiring exact objective values. Empirically, the approaches are evaluated on large query-dependent browsing graphs from a commercial search engine and reportedly improve ranking quality over the state of the art.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful: both GBN and GFN are analyzed with explicit oracle-error propagation, step-size/accuracy schedules, and operation counts. Building the two-level scheme on ref. 17 is appropriate and computationally motivated (no large matrix operations). Proofs are in the supplement; the main text states the theorems and dependencies clearly. However, experiments compare with GBP primarily on the loss, not standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP), and there is no direct empirical comparison to ref. 17 despite the work generalizing it; both would strengthen the claims.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized (Sections 2–5 detail the model and algorithms; proofs deferred). That said, the heavy notation makes it hard to follow; symbols such as N appear before being defined. Please add a symbol table and ensure all quantities are defined on first use.

Originality: Adapting random gradient-free methods to constrained optimization with inexact oracles, and extending the NN lower-level method to derivative estimation in this learning setting, are novel. The integration into a practical two-level scheme for supervised PageRank is a meaningful contribution beyond prior PageRank learning and inexact-oracle optimization work.

Significance: The methods are broadly applicable to large-scale graph ranking with feature-rich nodes/edges. Convergence guarantees under realistic inexact computations are valuable. Reproducibility is hindered by nonpublic data, though the dataset description and provided hyperparameters help; releasing code or a synthetic benchmark would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theory with convergence-rate and complexity guarantees for both GBN and GFN
- Computationally efficient lower-level routine avoiding large matrices
- Clear articulation of hyperparameters; practical relevance to ranking
- Potentially generalizable inexact-oracle framework

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental comparison focuses on loss; include NDCG/MAP vs GBP
- Missing direct empirical baseline to ref. 17 and ablations on lower-level accuracy/runtime trade-offs
- Heavy notation; needs a symbol table
- Private data limits reproducibility; code/synthetic data would help
