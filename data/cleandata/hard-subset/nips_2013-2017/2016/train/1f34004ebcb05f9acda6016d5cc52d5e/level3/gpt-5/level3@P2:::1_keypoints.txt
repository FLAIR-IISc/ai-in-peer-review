- Problem: Learns feature-driven Supervised PageRank where restart/transition probabilities are linear in features and parameters, optimizing a pairwise square loss over query-dependent graphs.
- Challenge: Exact stationary distributions and their derivatives are unavailable, so objective values and gradients must be approximated.
- Two-level approach: Lower level approximates stationary distributions and derivatives; upper level optimizes parameters with an inexact oracle.
- Lower-level method: Uses Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method and extends it to derivative approximation with explicit accuracy–iteration trade-offs (Lemmas 1–2).
- Lower-level guarantees: Proves loss and gradient can be approximated to any prescribed accuracy and quantifies arithmetic complexity.
- Upper-level methods: Proposes a random gradient-free constrained method with bounded-error zero-order oracle and sphere-randomized finite-difference estimator, with convergence for convex/local-convex cases (Theorems 1–2).
- Upper-level methods (cont.): Proposes an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly non-convex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and backtracking-like adaptation (Algorithm 2, Theorem 3).
- Oracle instantiation: Instantiates inexact oracle for Supervised PageRank using lower-level approximations and derives end-to-end complexity (Theorem 4).
- Empirical results: On a proprietary Yandex query-dependent graph dataset, GFN and GBN outperform prior gradient-based baseline (GBP) in training loss and some iteration/runtime proxies with statistical significance.
- Experimental limitations: Lacks standard ranking metrics (NDCG/ERR), wall-clock/memory measurements, ablation of lower–upper accuracy trade-offs; dataset and code are proprietary limiting reproducibility.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on inexact oracle and random gradient-free frameworks (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Nesterov–Spokoiny) but adapts them to constrained, bounded-error, and non-convex settings for this problem.
- Originality assessment: Adaptations are non-trivial (constrained bounded-error, non-convex stationarity, derivative scheme tied to random-walks), but conceptual novelty relative to prior frameworks is moderate.
- Theoretical quality: Lower-level bounds and inexact-oracle constructions are carefully stated; convergence analyses (Theorems 1–4) appear sound and consistent with cited frameworks.
- Experimental quality: Shows consistent training loss improvements and iteration gains, but lacks stronger practical evaluation and runtime comparisons.
- Clarity: Paper is well written and organized but uses heavy notation; would benefit from a notation table and expanded discussion of practical implications and limitations.
- Requests for clarification: Authors should clarify precise differences from Nesterov–Spokoiny and Devolder et al. and why existing analyses don’t directly apply.
- Specific suggested experiments: Report NDCG/ERR@k, wall-clock times and per-iteration costs, and ablations exploring lower-level accuracy (N, N1, N2) vs. total runtime.
- Additional analysis requests: Discuss sensitivity to α and feasible radius R, justify positivity constraints and scale handling, and clarify practical estimation of constants like β1.
- Methodological comparisons: Suggest comparing sphere vs Gaussian estimators, stochastic finite-difference baselines, and projected gradient with power-method gradients and adaptive backtracking.
- Related work: Improve related-work citations and positioning, including recent results on inexact oracles and nonconvex optimization.
- Pros: Solid theoretical development for inexact-oracle methods in constrained/nonconvex settings, end-to-end complexity bounds for Supervised PageRank, and empirical improvements over a baseline.
- Cons: Moderate conceptual novelty, limited experimental evaluation and reproducibility, some assumptions and constants lack practical illustration, heavy notation and brief positioning.
- Overall recommendation: Careful and useful contribution that strengthens theoretical foundations with promising experiments; leans toward acceptance if empirical evaluation and clarity on novelty/practicality are strengthened.
