This paper studies learning a Supervised PageRank model where node and edge features parameterize restart and transition probabilities. The learning objective (Eq. 2.4) is non-convex and constrained. The authors propose two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-free method that uses a random gradient estimator with an inexact zero-order oracle, and (ii) a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex optimization endowed with an inexact first-order oracle. Both approaches hinge on lower-level computations that approximate the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) of a query-dependent Markov chain using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method, rather than the power method typically used in Supervised PageRank [21]. The paper provides finite-time convergence guarantees for both upper-level optimizers under oracle inexactness, analyzes the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity, and reports empirical results on a Yandex browsing graph dataset indicating better loss values than the prior gradient method in [21].

Relation to prior work: The formulation extends Supervised PageRank [21], BrowseRank [10], and related random walk models [18, 11]. On the optimization side, it builds on random gradient-free methods (e.g., Nesterov–Spokoiny) and inexact oracle frameworks (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov), and nonconvex first/zeroth-order methods (e.g., Ghadimi–Lan). The contribution is to adapt these to a constrained setting with deterministic inexactness in function and gradient approximations arising from Markov chain computations, and to carry out a complexity analysis that accounts for lower-level iteration counts. This fills a gap in [21], which lacked convergence guarantees because power-method-based gradient estimates are inexact.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and connection to practical ranking tasks; the model accommodates rich node/edge features.
- Methodological novelty: constrained, inexact-oracle variants of gradient-free and gradient-based methods with provable convergence rates; explicit coupling of oracle accuracy with lower-level iteration budgets (N, N1, N2).
- Sound lower-level choice: leveraging the Nesterov–Nemirovski method yields linear convergence and allows tight control of approximation error; the analysis accounts for sparsity and graph degree via p, s.
- Theoretical contributions include Lemma 1–2 (error/complexity for function and gradient approximations), Theorem 1 (gradient-free) and Theorem 3 (adaptive projected gradient with inexact oracle), and overall complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4).
- Empirical evidence suggests improved surrogate loss and some efficiency advantages versus the state-of-the-art gradient baseline.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Main experimental results are largely deferred to the supplement. The paper’s main text should be self-contained: include the primary comparisons (loss curves, final loss, and standard IR metrics such as NDCG@k/MAP) with wall-clock times and iteration counts. Without this, the practical impact is difficult to assess.
- Convexity assumption in Theorem 1 vs. non-convex objective: Section 4.2 assumes that within a small ball Φ around a local minimum, f is convex. This is crucial for the gradient-free convergence guarantee but currently unsubstantiated. Please provide sufficient conditions (in terms of features, α, and Aq) under which f is convex on Φ, or diagnostics that verify local convexity in practice. Alternatively, discuss how to use nonconvex zeroth-order guarantees (e.g., convergence to stationary points) and what changes in rates/parameters would result.
- Stationarity guarantee in Theorem 3: The bound ‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖2 ≤ … is a step-size-weighted iterate difference. In projected gradient frameworks, a standard surrogate for first-order stationarity is the norm of the projected gradient mapping or dist(0, ∂ψ(x) + N_X(x)). Please clarify the exact stationarity notion implied by your bound and provide an explicit inequality relating ‖MK(xk − xk+1)‖ to a projected gradient norm or a measure of violation of first-order necessary optimality (with constants). This is especially important since you claim convergence to a stationary point in the nonconvex setting.
- Algorithmic efficiency and practical convergence speed: Supplementary Figure 1 suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly compared to alternatives. The main text should report:
  - Wall-clock time per upper-level iteration and total time to reach a target loss.
  - Lower-level iteration counts (N, N1, N2) chosen adaptively over iterations, and their impact on time and accuracy.
  - Sensitivity to the chosen L and ε (you currently fix L=L0=1e-4 and ε=1e-6); include ablations showing robustness and any tuning overhead.
  - Comparisons with [21] under matched accuracy tolerances for the lower-level computations (e.g., same stopping criteria rather than fixed powers).
- Evaluation metrics: Optimizing the pairwise hinge-like square loss is reasonable, but ranking quality should be reported using standard IR metrics on held-out data (NDCG@k, MAP). Relying only on the surrogate loss makes it hard to judge practical gains.
- The claim “lowest proved complexity” for the lower-level method relative to [5] is strong; please make the comparison precise, including assumptions and the role of sparsity, and quantify constants/log-factors.
- Clarity and presentation: Many key constants (e.g., β1) and proofs are only in the supplement. Consider bringing at least the definitions and key lemmas’ proof sketches into the main text, and streamline notation. Minor typos and grammar issues should be corrected.

Quality
- Technical development is generally sound and well-grounded in prior theory; however, the convexity assumption in Section 4 and the stationarity interpretation in Section 5 need clarification to fully support the claims.
- Experimental section needs to be strengthened with comprehensive, in-text results and efficiency analyses.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but mostly well-organized. Moving essential experimental figures/tables into the main paper and clarifying the points above would substantially improve readability.

Originality
- The adaptation of gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods to constrained inexact-oracle settings, with explicit coupling to Markov chain approximation routines, is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution.

Significance
- Learning feature-augmented PageRank with guarantees is valuable for ranking and graph learning. With stronger empirical evidence and clarified assumptions, the work could be impactful and reusable beyond web ranking.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical advances addressing a known gap in Supervised PageRank learning.
- Practical algorithmic framework that can generalize to other Markov-chain-based models.
- Empirical indications of improved performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumptions (local convexity; stationarity measure) are insufficiently justified.
- Main empirical results and efficiency evidence are relegated to the supplement; limited evaluation metrics.
- Some ambiguity in practical parameter selection and comparative fairness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Bring core experimental results (loss and ranking metrics, runtime, iteration budgets) into the main text; add larger-scale or more diverse datasets if possible.
- Justify or relax the convexity assumption in Section 4; if not possible, explicitly scope the guarantee and provide empirical checks.
- Provide a direct projected-gradient-mapping or subgradient residual bound to formalize stationarity claims for Algorithm 2.
- Include ablations on δ1, δ2, N, N1, N2 to illuminate the accuracy–cost trade-off, and discuss guidance for practitioners.
