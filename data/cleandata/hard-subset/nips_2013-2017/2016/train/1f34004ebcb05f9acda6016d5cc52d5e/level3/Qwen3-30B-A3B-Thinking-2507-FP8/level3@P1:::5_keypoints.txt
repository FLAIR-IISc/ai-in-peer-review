- Main contribution: proposes gradient-based and gradient-free methods with theoretical convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank parameters in a non-convex setting.  
- Theoretical strength: rigorous convergence analysis under an inexact oracle, with Lemmas 1–2 bounding stationary distribution approximation and Theorems 1–4 giving convergence rates.  
- Correct problem identification: existing methods fail due to non-convexity, constraints, and inability to compute exact loss values.  
- Critical citation error: incorrectly refers to [21] as "Supervised PageRank" when [21] actually uses "semi-supervised PageRank," undermining scholarly accuracy.  
- Experimental weaknesses: results are deferred to the appendix, main text lacks concrete evidence that the method outperforms baselines.  
- Hyperparameter ambiguity: key parameters (R and ϕ̂) and their selection are unexplained and appear arbitrary.  
- Limited evaluation: experiments use only one dataset, with no discussion of scalability, generalization, or diverse graph structures.  
- Marginal empirical gains: reported improvements are small, lack statistical significance reporting, and do not substantiate claims of significant outperformance.  
- Constraint concern: restricting Φ to a small ball around ϕ̂ may severely limit practical effectiveness and lacks justification.  
- Poor writing and clarity: grammatical errors, inconsistent notation (Supervised vs Semi-supervised PageRank), and key technical details pushed to the appendix.  
- Originality limited: mainly adapts existing gradient-free and gradient-based methods to a constrained non-convex setting rather than introducing fundamentally new algorithms.  
- Practical significance questionable: theoretical results are sound but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate real-world impact (e.g., search engine speed/reliability).  
- Missing comparisons: does not compare against more recent alternatives beyond the baseline [21].  
- Recommendation summary: valuable theoretical contribution but insufficient empirical validation and presentation issues mean the paper needs substantial revision before acceptance.
