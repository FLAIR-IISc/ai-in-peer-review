This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a non-convex, feature-driven Supervised PageRank model. The core challenge is that the objective function, which depends on the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk, cannot be computed exactly. This renders standard optimization techniques inapplicable. The authors propose a two-level algorithm to overcome this. The lower level uses a linearly convergent method to estimate the stationary distribution, which allows the loss function to be approximated to any prescribed precision. This provides an "inexact oracle" for the upper level, where the authors develop a gradient-based method for constrained non-convex optimization. This work relates to the broader literature on optimization with inexact oracles, such as Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), and builds upon previous formulations of the Supervised PageRank algorithm.

The paper’s primary strength is its technical quality. The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis providing convergence guarantees for learning the Supervised PageRank model, a guarantee that was absent in prior work. The authors successfully adapt inexact-oracle gradient methods to handle constrained optimization problems where function and gradient values are computable only up to a known precision. This is a significant result, as it provides a provably correct method for a practical problem. The paper is also very clearly written, with proofs that appear to be sound, making the complex material accessible.

The main weakness concerns the paper's originality. While the adaptation to the constrained case is novel, the fundamental optimization ideas are closely related to existing frameworks for inexact-oracle methods. The Supervised PageRank model itself also builds on established algorithms. The authors could strengthen the paper by more explicitly contextualizing their specific contribution—the handling of constraints—within the broader landscape of inexact optimization to better highlight its novelty.

Minor issues include the absence of a dedicated conclusion section to summarize the work. I would also recommend that the authors present the optimization problem in a more general context initially, before specializing to the PageRank case. This would help clarify the core optimization contribution for readers less familiar with graph-based ranking, thereby increasing the paper's potential impact.
