This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, where node and edge weights are defined by features. The authors correctly point out that the state-of-the-art learning algorithm for this model [21] is based on gradient descent using imprecise gradient estimates from the power method, and lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. The main contribution of this work is to propose two new optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. The proposed methods rely on a two-level optimization scheme. At the lower level, they use an efficient iterative method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk, providing an "inexact oracle" for the loss function and its gradient. At the upper level, they employ either a novel adaptive projected gradient method for non-convex problems or a random gradient-free method adapted for constrained optimization to update the model parameters.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical rigor. The authors provide a detailed analysis of their proposed algorithms, deriving explicit convergence rates and complexity bounds (Theorems 1-4). This is a significant improvement over prior work [21], which offered no such guarantees. The formulation of the optimization problem under an inexact oracle is sound and connects well with modern optimization literature [7, 25].

However, the overall quality of the paper as a complete scientific contribution is diminished by several factors:
1.  **Incomplete Main Body:** The paper relegates all proofs and detailed experimental results to the supplementary materials. Without at least proof sketches or a more thorough analysis of the empirical results in the main text, the paper is difficult to evaluate and feels incomplete.
2.  **Weak Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is cursory. While it claims the proposed methods outperform the baseline, it fails to provide the necessary details to substantiate this claim. Key hyperparameters (e.g., the Lipschitz constant L, initial L0, accuracy ε) are stated without justification for their selection. More importantly, the critical choice of the search space Φ as a ball of radius R around a point `ϕ̂` is not explained or motivated. This constraint could severely limit the model's practical performance, and its impact is not discussed.
3.  **Unrealistic Assumptions:** The analysis of the gradient-free method relies on an assumption that the objective function is convex within the optimization domain Φ. While stated as a local assumption around a minimum, this is a strong condition for a problem that is generally non-convex and may not hold in practice.

**Clarity:**
The clarity of the paper is a major weakness. The writing suffers from frequent grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, which impede readability. The citation practice is sloppy; for instance, the model from [21] is referred to as "Supervised PageRank" throughout, while the title of [21] is "Supervised Nested PageRank." The organization of the paper, with its heavy reliance on the appendix, also detracts from its clarity and self-containedness. A reader should not have to consult supplementary materials to understand the core evidence backing the paper's claims.

**Originality:**
The problem formulation itself is not novel, closely following the framework of [21]. The core optimization algorithms are also adaptations of existing techniques: the gradient-free method is an extension of [16], and the inexact gradient method builds upon ideas from [7, 25]. The originality lies in the specific combination and adaptation of these methods to the Supervised PageRank problem, and most notably, in providing the first formal convergence analysis for this setting. This theoretical contribution is new and valuable.

**Significance:**
The results have potential significance. Providing a theoretically sound method for learning feature-based PageRank models is an important step forward. Researchers working on graph-based learning and ranking could build upon these theoretical frameworks. Practitioners could benefit from more reliable and guaranteed optimization methods for training such models.

However, the practical significance is not convincingly demonstrated. The experiments are limited to a single dataset from a commercial search engine. It is unclear whether the results generalize to other types of graphs or datasets. The evaluation is done only in terms of the loss function value, without reporting standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP), which makes it difficult to assess the real-world impact on ranking quality. The reported improvement in loss, while statistically significant, may not translate to a meaningful improvement for end-users.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning this class of Supervised PageRank models, addressing a clear gap in the existing literature.
2.  The development and analysis of optimization algorithms for constrained, non-convex problems with an inexact oracle are solid and could be of interest to the broader NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The empirical validation is weak and lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility or for assessing the practical impact of the proposed methods.
2.  The paper is poorly written and organized. It fails to function as a self-contained contribution, with essential details on both theory and experiments relegated to the appendix.
3.  Key modeling choices, such as the constrained parameter space, are not justified, raising questions about the practical applicability and limitations of the proposed approach.

In summary, the paper presents a strong theoretical contribution but fails to support it with convincing empirical evidence or a clear, self-contained presentation. The weaknesses in experimental validation and clarity are significant enough to undermine the paper's overall quality.
