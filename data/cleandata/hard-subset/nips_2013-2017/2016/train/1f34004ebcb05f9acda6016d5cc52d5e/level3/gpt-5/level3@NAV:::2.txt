This paper formulates learning Supervised PageRank as a constrained, feature-aware optimization problem and proposes two upper-level solvers built around inexact oracles: (i) a gradient-free method using a biased zero-order oracle, and (ii) an adaptive gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle. Both leverage a fast lower-level routine to approximate stationary distributions and sensitivities, yielding complexity bounds and convergence-rate guarantees. The work connects to supervised random walks for ranking (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec), PageRank computation accelerations (Nesterov–Nemirovski), zero-order convex optimization (Nesterov–Spokoiny), and inexact-oracle first-order methods for nonconvex problems (Ghadimi & Lan), positioning itself as a principled extension to constrained/inexact settings.

Quality: Theoretical development is thoughtful and potentially useful, but several assumptions and conclusions are unclear. Theorem 1 is stated for convex f, while the learning objective (2.4) is non-convex; Section 4.2 assumes local convexity on Φ but does not provide criteria on features or a principled procedure to choose Φ that ensures convexity. Theorem 3 bounds MK(xK − xK+1), yet the manuscript does not demonstrate that this bound implies convergence to a stationary point (e.g., via gradient mapping or projected gradient norm) under the stated inexactness and adaptive Lk. Empirically, all results are relegated to the supplement, leaving the main paper incomplete; moreover, Supplementary Figure 1 suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly, but no wall-clock/runtime or oracle-call comparisons are reported.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but some assumptions are implicit. It would help to (a) clearly state everywhere which parts require convexity vs. merely smoothness; (b) tie the MK(xK − xK+1) criterion to standard stationarity measures for constrained nonconvex problems; and (c) provide a concrete recipe for selecting Φ that guarantees well-defined probabilities and, when needed, convexity.

Originality: The extension of inexact-oracle zero- and first-order methods to constrained settings and their integration with Supervised PageRank is novel and nontrivial, though it builds on established paradigms.

Significance: Potentially impactful for large-scale ranking, but weakened by limited in-text empirical evidence and unclear convergence-to-stationarity claims.

Detailed comments:
- Major: Move key experimental results (tables/figures, datasets, metrics, and ablations) into the main text to make the paper self-contained; include time-to-accuracy, oracle calls, and power-iteration counts; compare GFNM/GBNM to the state of the art with matched budgets.
- Clarify how Φ can be chosen to ensure convexity near the solution or provide a nonconvex zeroth-order analysis without convexity.
- For Theorem 3, relate MK(xK − xK+1) to a gradient-mapping norm and state whether every limit point is a stationary point under your oracle errors and adaptivity.
- Discuss the observed slow convergence of GFNM and report efficiency comparisons.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical development for constrained inexact-oracle optimization.
- Clear integration with a practical ranking model incorporating node/edge features.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete main paper without in-text experiments.
- Unclear convexity assumptions and stationarity guarantees.
- Limited empirical analysis of efficiency; gradient-free method appears slow.

Overall: Promising theory and application, but I lean toward rejection unless the above issues—especially the experimental presentation and stationarity claims—are addressed.
