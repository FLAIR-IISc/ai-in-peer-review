This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods – a gradient-based (GBN) and a gradient-free (GFN) approach – for learning Supervised PageRank models. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both methods, addressing a limitation of previous work [21]. Both methods utilize an inexact oracle, avoiding the need for exact objective function values and providing convergence rate estimates while circumventing large matrix computations. Empirical results demonstrate performance superior to the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality.

The work builds on Nesterov and Nemirovski’s framework [17] for two-level optimization. The lower level estimates the loss function and its derivatives, while the upper level tunes parameters using these estimations. Detailed method descriptions are provided in Sections 2-5, with proofs relegated to the supplementary material.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The most significant strength is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both GBN and GFN, a crucial addition missing from prior work on Supervised PageRank optimization [21]. This elevates the work from a purely empirical study to a more rigorous theoretical foundation.
* **Inexact Oracle:** Utilizing an inexact oracle is a smart design choice, enabling scalability and avoiding computationally expensive exact calculations. The paper analyzes the trade-off between oracle accuracy and algorithm complexity.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results show a clear improvement in ranking quality compared to the existing state-of-the-art method [21]. This practical validation strengthens the value of the proposed approaches.
* **Well-Defined Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the experimental setup, including detailed descriptions of the dataset and hyperparameter settings. This enhances reproducibility.
* **Building on Prior Work:** The work appropriately builds upon established optimization techniques (Nesterov & Nemirovski [17]) and relevant ranking algorithms (PageRank, Supervised PageRank [21]).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Symbol Table Absence:** The reviewer’s comment regarding the lack of a symbol table is highly valid. The paper employs a large number of symbols (e.g., ‘N’ is introduced without prior definition), making it challenging to follow the derivations and understand the method. A comprehensive symbol table is essential for clarity.
* **Limited Comparison:** The experimental comparison with the gradient-based approach [21] focuses solely on the loss function, not ranking quality. The reviewer correctly points out the need for a direct comparison with [17], considering that the current work generalizes it.  This is a significant omission, as the claim of generalization warrants a more direct benchmark.
* **Dataset Accessibility:** While the dataset is described sufficiently to attempt reproduction, its unavailability hinders independent verification of the results. Publicly releasing the dataset would substantially increase the impact of the work.
* **Convexity Assumption:** The justification for assuming convexity on Φ (in the application of the gradient-based method in Section 5.2) feels somewhat weak. A more thorough discussion of the implications of non-convexity, or an investigation of performance under non-convexity, would be beneficial.
* **Complexity Analysis:** While complexity analyses are provided, they are often expressed in terms of numerous parameters, making it difficult to grasp the practical implications of the theoretical bounds. A more intuitive understanding of the computational cost would be valuable.



**Originality:**

The work demonstrates originality through its combination of two-level optimization techniques with inexact oracles and its theoretical analysis of convergence rates for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods in the context of Supervised PageRank. While building on existing optimization principles, the application and adaptation to this specific ranking problem, coupled with the rigorous analysis, contribute a novel approach.

**Significance:**

The results have the potential to significantly impact web search and ranking applications. The improved ranking quality and theoretical guarantees offer a more reliable and efficient solution for learning Supervised PageRank models. The general framework for inexact oracle optimization may also be applicable to other large-scale machine learning problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for a challenging optimization problem.
* Demonstrates superior empirical performance compared to the state-of-the-art.
* Offers a practical solution for large-scale ranking problems through the use of inexact oracles.
* Clearly presented methodology with detailed experimental setup.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lack of a symbol table significantly hinders readability.
* Incomplete experimental comparison, missing direct benchmark against [17].
* Dataset unavailability limits reproducibility.
* Justification for convexity assumption needs strengthening.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of supervised ranking. The theoretical analysis and empirical results are strong, and the proposed methods have the potential for significant impact. However, the lack of a symbol table and the limited comparison to relevant baselines detract from the overall quality.

**Recommendation:**

Accept, with minor revisions. The authors should **absolutely** include a comprehensive symbol table. They should also add a direct comparison to the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17], and consider releasing the dataset (or a representative subset) to facilitate independent verification. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
