This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a Supervised PageRank model, where node and edge features are used to define the transition and restart probabilities of a random walk. The learning problem is formulated as a non-convex, constrained loss-minimization task. The core challenge is that the objective function (and its gradient) cannot be computed exactly, as it depends on the stationary distribution of a Markov process.

The authors propose two two-level optimization algorithms to solve this problem. Both methods rely on the concept of an "inexact oracle," where the stationary distribution is approximated iteratively. The first algorithm is a random gradient-free method, adapted from prior work to handle constraints and a known bound on the oracle's error. The second is a gradient-based method for non-convex composite optimization, which is also generalized to work with an inexact first-order oracle and features an adaptive step-size rule. A key contribution of the paper is providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods, a property that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks. The paper claims to validate these algorithms experimentally on a real-world web page ranking task.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its rigorous theoretical treatment of the Supervised PageRank learning problem. It successfully bridges the gap between optimization theory for methods with inexact oracles and the practical application of learning to rank. Providing convergence guarantees for gradient-free and gradient-based methods in this specific non-convex, inexact setting is a solid and valuable contribution.

2.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper formulates a practical and important problem in a principled way. Supervised PageRank is a powerful model, and developing robust and theoretically-grounded optimization methods for it is a significant research direction. The analysis of the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle and the overall complexity of the upper-level algorithm is insightful.

3.  **Well-Motivated Methods:** The choice of both a gradient-free and a gradient-based method is well-motivated. The gradient-free approach avoids the potentially complex and expensive computation of derivatives, while the gradient-based method is often more sample-efficient. Analyzing both provides a comprehensive view of the optimization landscape for this problem.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity and Self-Containment:** The most significant weakness is that the paper is not self-contained. The entire experimental section (Section 6) refers to results (Table 1, Figure 1) that are not present in the main text but are presumably in the supplementary material. A conference paper must be readable and its claims verifiable on its own. Without these results, the claims of outperforming the state-of-the-art are unsubstantiated within the paper itself. I strongly urge the authors to move the most critical experimental results into the main paper, perhaps by shortening the descriptions of the general optimization frameworks which are based on prior work.

2.  **Theoretical Assumptions:** There are some gaps in the connection between the general theory and the specific application:
    *   **Convexity Assumption (Theorem 1 & 2):** The analysis for the gradient-free method assumes that the objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex on the feasible set Φ. However, the loss function (2.4) is stated to be non-convex in general. The paper suggests choosing Φ as a "small vicinity" of a local minimum `ϕ*` where the function is convex. This is a strong assumption that is difficult to verify in practice. How would a user know how to choose `R` to guarantee convexity? The practical utility of the convergence guarantee for the gradient-free method is questionable without further justification or guidance on this point.
    *   **Convergence Criterion (Theorem 3):** For the gradient-based method, Theorem 3 provides a convergence rate for the quantity `‖MK(xK − xK+1)‖^2`. While this is a valid stopping criterion for adaptive gradient methods, it is not immediately obvious to the reader how this guarantees convergence to a stationary point where `‖∇ψ(x)‖` is small. The authors should briefly clarify the relationship between their criterion and more standard stationarity conditions for non-convex optimization.

3.  **Evaluation of Practical Efficiency:** The key points mention that a figure in the supplement suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly. The experimental evaluation in the main paper should not only focus on the final loss value but also on the practical efficiency of the algorithms. A comparison of convergence speed in terms of wall-clock time or the number of oracle calls is crucial for a paper on optimization methods. This would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between the proposed methods and the baseline.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  The paper provides a strong theoretical contribution by developing and analyzing optimization methods with convergence guarantees for the important problem of learning Supervised PageRank.
    2.  The connection between optimization with inexact oracles and a real-world ranking problem is novel and significant.
    3.  The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and address the shortcomings of the existing state-of-the-art method.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  The paper is critically incomplete as it lacks any experimental results in the main text, making its empirical claims impossible to evaluate.
    2.  The practical applicability of the theoretical guarantees for the gradient-free method is weakened by an unjustified local convexity assumption.
    3.  The discussion on the convergence criterion for the gradient-based method could be clearer.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution. However, the major issue of not including experimental results in the main text prevents a full assessment of its practical significance and must be addressed. If the authors can revise the paper to be self-contained and clarify the noted theoretical points, it would represent a strong contribution to the field.
