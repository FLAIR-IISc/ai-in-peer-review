This paper studies learning feature-parameterized PageRank on query-dependent graphs (“Supervised PageRank”) via two-level optimization: an inner routine that approximates the stationary distribution and its parametric derivatives, and an outer routine that updates parameters using inexact oracle information. The authors adopt the linearly convergent method of Nesterov–Nemirovski to approximate stationary distributions and extend it to compute sensitivities with respect to node/edge feature weights. On top of these oracles, they develop (i) a random gradient-free algorithm that operates with a biased, inexact zero-order oracle under constraints, and (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method for composite, possibly nonconvex objectives with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. They provide convergence-rate bounds and complexity estimates for both outer methods and for the full two-level procedures by explicitly linking desired accuracy to the number of inner iterations. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset compare the proposed methods against a prior gradient-based baseline that uses power iterations for both stationary distributions and derivatives; the new methods are claimed to have better theoretical guarantees and to improve the training loss and ranking quality.

Relation to prior work: The model follows a body of work that enriches random-walk ranking with node/edge features and user behavior (e.g., BrowseRank, supervised random walks), and builds specifically on [21]’s two-level gradient approach for supervised learning of PageRank-type models but without convergence guarantees due to inexact inner computations. The inner solver is based on the Neumann-series-style averaging method of [17] rather than the standard power method [18], and the outer-level optimization contributes inexact-oracle variants of Nesterov/Spokoiny-type gradient-free optimization and adaptive proximal gradient methods (cf. [7, 13, 22, 25]). The paper’s novelty is in adapting these ideas to constrained, inexact settings, establishing convergence rates for the composite two-level schemes, and demonstrating them for supervised PageRank.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a gap: prior supervised PageRank training lacked convergence guarantees because inner oracles were inexact; this work ties inner accuracy to outer progress and provides end-to-end complexity bounds.
- Technical contributions: (a) an inexact, biased zero-order oracle scheme with a convergence theorem for constrained problems; (b) an adaptive projected gradient method with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant; (c) derivative-approximation recursions for Markov chain sensitivities with explicit error control; and (d) trade-off analysis between inner accuracy and overall complexity.
- The inner method choice is sensible; the Nesterov–Nemirovski averaging is known to converge linearly and is practically important when α is moderate.
- Empirical comparison on a real industrial dataset, with some attention to graph-size effects.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The writing has grammatical/citation issues, and much of the core content (proofs, constants, experiments) is relegated to the supplement; the main text lacks proof sketches and sufficient empirical details to assess claims.
- Related work and naming: There is confusion around “Supervised PageRank” vs other closely related variants (e.g., supervised random walks, supervised nested PageRank). Please reconcile and cite precisely which prior method [21] is meant, and how the present approach differs technically and empirically.
- Assumptions and scope: The gradient-free analysis in Section 4.2 assumes local convexity of f on Φ, while the problem is generally nonconvex; this restricts the applicability of the theoretical guarantee. The gradient-based method targets stationarity but uses an inexact oracle satisfying (5.1); in practice, the bounded, known δ assumption and Lipschitz smoothness may fail or be hard to certify on real data.
- Constraints: Restricting ϕ to a Euclidean ball around ϕ̂ = 1 with R = 0.99 enforces positivity but may unduly limit expressiveness; no empirical sensitivity to R/ϕ̂ is provided.
- Experiments: Key details are missing in the main text—metrics beyond training loss (e.g., NDCG/MAP), absolute improvements, variance across queries, wall-clock runtime and inner iteration counts, ablations over N, N1, N2, and robustness to hyperparameters (L, ε). The stopping criterion for GBP (“differences less than −1e−5”) seems incorrect or misstated. Only one dataset domain is used.
- Practical impact: While theory is strengthened, the empirical section does not convincingly establish gains in ranking quality or efficiency over strong baselines.

Quality
- Theory appears carefully developed and consistent with the cited frameworks; however, the main text should include at least brief proof sketches, explicit constants (e.g., β1), and clearer assumptions. The empirical methodology needs more rigor (metrics, significance, runtime).

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from extensive editing for grammar, consistent notation, precise citations, and more informative figures/tables in the main text. Explain choices like ϕ0 and R, and justify the convexity assumption in the region used by the gradient-free method.

Originality
- The contributions are primarily adaptations and extensions of known inexact-oracle and random gradient-free frameworks to the constrained, biased-oracle setting, plus a derivative-approximation scheme for PageRank. This is incremental but nontrivial and useful for this class of problems.

Significance
- If empirically substantiated, providing convergent, efficient training for supervised PageRank could impact learning-to-rank on large graphs. As presented, the practical significance is undermined by limited and underreported experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- End-to-end convergence guarantees for a practically relevant two-level learning procedure.
- Solid and useful extensions of inexact-oracle optimization methods.
- Sensible inner solver with demonstrable benefits over power iteration.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation and reporting (metrics, runtime, ablations).
- Unclear relation and improvements over the exact prior supervised PageRank baseline; citation/naming confusion.
- Strong assumptions (local convexity on Φ, known uniform oracle error) without practical verification.
- Presentation and writing quality need substantial improvement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the relation to [21] and adjacent works; use consistent terminology and correct citations.
- Add main-text proof sketches, the explicit constants in lemmas, and a discussion of when (5.1) holds for your oracles.
- Report standard ranking metrics (NDCG@k, MAP), absolute numbers, confidence intervals, and statistical tests; include wall-clock time and inner iteration counts; provide ablations over N, N1, N2, R, and ϕ̂.
- Correct/clarify the GBP stopping rule and step-size choices; include stronger baselines if possible.
- Discuss the impact of constraining Φ and explore less restrictive positivity mechanisms (e.g., simplex/log-parameterization).

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical contributions are sound and relevant, but the paper, as written, does not provide sufficient empirical evidence or clarity to justify acceptance. A revised version with improved exposition and substantially stronger experimental validation could be competitive.
