

The paper proposes two optimization algorithms for learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features through feature-driven random walks. The authors address a non-convex loss minimization problem that arises in this context, where standard optimization methods fail due to constraints and the inability to compute exact loss values. They develop a two-level framework: the lower level uses a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk (with any desired accuracy), and the upper level applies gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with theoretical convergence guarantees.

The paper builds on previous work on Supervised PageRank (e.g., [21]) which lacked convergence guarantees, and extends Nesterov & Spokoiny's work on random gradient-free methods (2015) to constrained optimization problems with inexact oracles. The authors provide convergence rate analysis for both methods, demonstrating they outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution: The paper provides the first convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models. The authors develop a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, a valuable contribution to optimization theory.

2. Technical soundness: The proofs of the main theorems appear correct. The authors carefully analyze the trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and computational complexity, providing clear complexity bounds that are well-motivated.

3. Empirical validation: Experiments on a real-world web ranking dataset demonstrate that both proposed algorithms significantly outperform the state-of-the-art [21] in ranking quality, with statistically significant results (p < 0.005).

4. Practical relevance: The gradient-free method avoids computing derivatives for large matrices, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. The theoretical guarantees are directly applicable to improving search engine reliability and speed.

Weaknesses:
1. Novelty: The core optimization ideas are quite similar to Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), and the paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how their contribution differs. The main novelty is adapting these ideas to constrained optimization with inexact oracles, but this connection isn't emphasized enough.

2. Related work: The paper doesn't adequately discuss how their two-level framework relates to other inexact oracle approaches (e.g., [7, 25]). A more thorough comparison would strengthen the contribution claim.

3. Presentation: The paper lacks a conclusion section. The optimization problem could be presented in a more general context to better clarify the contribution relative to state-of-the-art.

4. Scope: While the application to web ranking is compelling, the paper could better articulate how this framework might be applied to other graph-based learning problems beyond the specific application.

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The key technical contributions are clearly explained, though the novelty claim could be strengthened with better contextualization against existing literature. The experiments are well-designed and support the theoretical claims.

Pro: Theoretical guarantees for non-convex learning problem, clear complexity bounds, strong empirical results, valuable contribution to inexact oracle optimization framework.

Con: Limited novelty compared to Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), insufficient differentiation from prior work, minor presentation issues.

Overall, the paper makes a solid technical contribution to optimization theory with practical applications in graph-based learning. The theoretical results are sound, the experiments are convincing, and the methods have clear potential for real-world applications. The main weakness is the insufficient differentiation from prior work on inexact oracle optimization, which weakens the novelty claim.

The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how their constrained optimization framework with inexact oracles differs from Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015), and a clearer articulation of the problem's significance in the broader context of graph-based learning. Despite this limitation, the technical contribution is substantial, the empirical validation is strong, and the work has clear practical relevance.

Given the quality of the technical contribution, the empirical results, and the practical relevance, I recommend acceptance. The paper advances both optimization theory and its application to a significant problem in information retrieval, with clear theoretical guarantees that address a gap in the literature.
