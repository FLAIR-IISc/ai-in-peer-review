This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of a Supervised PageRank model, where node and edge weights in a graph are parameterized by feature vectors. The authors correctly point out that the state-of-the-art learning algorithm for this model [21] lacks theoretical performance guarantees. The core of the problem is a non-convex loss minimization task where the loss function and its gradient depend on the stationary distribution of a large Markov chain, which cannot be computed exactly.

The main contribution is the development of two optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that can handle this "inexact oracle" setting. The authors build on general frameworks for optimization with noisy function estimates, adapting them to the constrained, non-convex nature of the Supervised PageRank problem. For both proposed algorithms, the paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates and overall complexity bounds. This represents a significant theoretical advance over prior work.

### Strengths

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper's primary strength is its theoretical rigor. It provides the first known convergence guarantees for learning a Supervised PageRank model, formally addressing the issue of inexact gradient and function value computations that was overlooked in previous work [21]. This is a valuable contribution to the literature on graph-based learning.
*   **General Optimization Frameworks:** The paper presents general algorithmic frameworks for constrained optimization with inexact oracles (Sections 4.1 and 5.1). The analysis of a gradient-free method for constrained problems with a biased oracle and an adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems with an inexact first-order oracle are of independent interest and could be applied to other large-scale machine learning problems where exact computations are intractable.
*   **Addresses a Difficult Problem:** The work directly confronts the challenging combination of non-convexity, parameter constraints, and inexact oracle information. The proposed methods and their analysis provide a principled approach to tackling such problems.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity and Paper Organization:** The paper is difficult to evaluate in its current form because the majority of its technical and empirical substance is relegated to the supplementary materials. The main text lacks proof sketches for the key theorems and does not contain the full experimental results or analysis. A reader should be able to understand the core contributions and assess the evidence without relying entirely on an appendix. The writing quality is also subpar, with grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that impede clarity (e.g., "our contribution... consists in adapting the approach... to the case... when the value of the function is calculated with some known accuracy").
*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is cursory and unconvincing.
    *   **Reproducibility:** Key hyperparameters and experimental choices are not justified. For instance, the paper does not explain how the initial parameter vector `ϕ̂` and the search radius `R` were selected. These choices are critical to the algorithm's performance and their omission hurts reproducibility.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The experiments appear to be conducted on a single dataset from a commercial search engine. Without testing on a diverse set of public graph datasets, it is difficult to assess the generalizability of the results.
    *   **Lack of Analysis:** The paper states that its methods "significantly outperform" the baseline but provides no analysis to support this claim beyond p-values. Table 1 presents final loss values, but there is no discussion on what this difference means for the actual ranking quality (e.g., using metrics like NDCG) or a qualitative analysis of the learned models.
*   **Practicality of Assumptions and Constraints:** The practical applicability of the proposed methods is undermined by several strong assumptions.
    *   The constraint of searching for the parameter vector `ϕ` within a Euclidean ball `‖ϕ - ϕ̂‖ ≤ R` is a major limitation. The paper provides no discussion on the sensitivity to `ϕ̂` and `R`, which could severely restrict the model from finding a good solution if the initial guess is poor.
    *   For the theoretical analysis of the gradient-free method, the authors assume that the non-convex objective function `f(ϕ)` is convex within the chosen search ball `Φ`. This is a very strong condition that may not hold in practice, and its justification is not provided.
*   **Originality of the Model:** The paper spends considerable space describing the Supervised PageRank model, which is taken directly from prior work [21]. While the proposed optimization methods are novel, the framing could be improved by more clearly positioning the work as a new, theoretically-grounded optimization approach for an existing model, rather than re-introducing the model itself at length.

In summary, the paper presents a strong theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for learning a feature-based PageRank model. However, it is severely weakened by a poorly organized manuscript that hides key details in the appendix, an insufficient and irreproducible experimental evaluation, and strong, un-discussed assumptions that question its practical impact. While the theoretical ideas are sound, the paper as a whole does not feel complete or convincingly validated.
