This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, extending previous work by proposing gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods with provable convergence guarantees – a key deficiency of prior approaches like the one presented in [21] (although the authors incorrectly refer to it as “Supervised PageRank” throughout; it's actually “Semi-Supervised PageRank”). The core idea is to estimate the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk using an iterative method and then leverage this approximation within a two-level optimization framework. The authors aim to address the challenges of non-convexity, constraints on the parameter vector, and the difficulty of obtaining exact loss function values.

The work builds upon the foundation laid by PageRank and its variants [18, 11, 8, 9, 19], acknowledging their limitations in capturing node and edge features. Existing approaches that incorporate features through modified transition probabilities [3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 21] are discussed, leading to the formulation of the supervised learning problem proposed in [21].  The authors correctly identify the lack of convergence guarantees for the existing gradient-based method [21] due to the inexactness of the power method used for stationary distribution estimation.  They then present their contributions: gradient-based and gradient-free methods with theoretical convergence rates, and an analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's primary strength lies in its attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for convergence.  Unlike much work in this area, the authors do not simply present an algorithm but attempt to analyze its properties and establish conditions for convergence, utilizing concepts like inexact oracles and Lipschitz continuity.
* **Addressing a Known Gap:** The authors explicitly address a known shortcoming of the previous state-of-the-art method – the lack of convergence proof – and attempt to fill this gap.
* **Exploration of Two Optimization Approaches:** Investigating both gradient-based and gradient-free methods allows for a broader exploration of the solution space and potentially identifies scenarios where each approach is more suitable.
* **Analysis of Complexity:** The authors attempt to analyze the computational complexity of their algorithms, providing insights into their scalability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from poor writing quality with numerous grammatical errors and occasionally unclear explanations.  The notation is dense, and the flow of ideas could be significantly improved. Redundant statements, like repeatedly calling [21] as “Supervised PageRank,” dilute the core message.
* **Lack of Empirical Detail:** The experimental section is remarkably thin. While the authors claim their methods outperform the state-of-the-art, the results are presented very briefly and lack detailed statistical analysis. Crucial experimental parameters (step sizes, training/test splits beyond percentages, hyperparameter tuning specifics) are not reported. A full comparison with baselines and an ablation study would be essential.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are performed on a single dataset, and it’s unclear whether the results generalize to other types of graphs or ranking tasks.  The description of the dataset (Yandex data) is brief and lacks specifics regarding its scale and characteristics beyond the number of nodes and edges.
* **Unrealistic Assumptions:**  The assumption of searching for parameters within a ball of radius *R* around a specific point ϕ̂ might be overly restrictive and could limit the algorithm's ability to find optimal solutions. This constraint, and the rationale for choosing *R*, needs stronger justification.
* **Deferred Proofs and Details:** The reliance on an extensive supplementary material for proofs and detailed explanations hinders the readability and accessibility of the paper. Critical technical details should be summarized or sketched within the main text.
* **Citation Errors:** The mischaracterization of [21] as “Supervised PageRank” undermines credibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contributions, while potentially challenging to fully verify, represent a significant step towards a more rigorous understanding of learning in Supervised PageRank models.
* The exploration of both gradient-based and gradient-free methods offers a valuable comparison and potentially widens the applicability of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed experimental results and analysis makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical findings.
* The poor writing quality and presentation hinder understanding and detract from the paper's overall impact.
* The potentially unrealistic assumptions and limited scope of the experiments raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.
* The absence of sufficient details in the main paper forces readers to delve into the supplementary material, which is not ideal for a program committee review.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an ambitious attempt to address a challenging problem with a strong theoretical foundation. However, the lack of thorough empirical validation, coupled with the writing quality and limited scope of experiments, significantly weakens its contribution. While the theoretical work is interesting, the paper, as presented, doesn’t convincingly demonstrate practical impact or offer a compelling advance over existing methods.  I recommend **rejection** but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above and resubmit to a future conference. A significantly expanded experimental section and improved clarity of presentation are essential for future success.
