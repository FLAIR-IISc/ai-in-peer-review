This paper addresses learning Supervised PageRank via two-level optimization under inexact-oracle information. On the lower level, the authors approximate stationary distributions and their sensitivities using the linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski scheme rather than the standard power method. On the upper level, they propose (i) an adaptive gradient method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle, extending inexact-oracle frameworks (e.g., Devolder–Nesterov–Glineur) to constrained settings and unknown Lipschitz constants, and (ii) a random gradient-free method tailored to constrained problems with bounded zero-order noise, adapting sphere-smoothing ideas (cf. Matyas; Nesterov–Spokoiny) and bandit-style estimators (cf. Agarwal–Dekel–Xiao). They analyze convergence rates and total complexity, including the inner–outer accuracy trade-off. Empirically, on a Yandex browsing graph dataset, both methods outperform a prior two-level gradient approach [21] in final loss, with the gradient-free approach most robust.

Quality: Theoretical contributions are carefully stated with convergence guarantees for both upper-level schemes under inexact lower-level computations. The assumptions (C1,1 smoothness; local convexity for the gradient-free method) are reasonable given the squared hinge-like loss. However, empirical evaluation reports only loss; standard IR metrics (e.g., NDCG, MAP) and ablations are missing. Critically, wall-clock runtime and memory usage are not reported.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written but notation is heavy. Providing a concise algorithmic summary of the two-level pipelines, a table of symbols, and clearer exposition of constants (e.g., β1) would aid readability. The GBP stopping rule description is slightly confusing.

Originality: Extending gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods to constrained, inexact-oracle settings and coupling them with the Nesterov–Nemirovski lower-level solver is a novel and well-motivated combination relative to PageRank learning literature and prior optimization work.

Significance: Supervised PageRank remains relevant; furnishing provable methods that outperform a strong baseline is useful. That said, experiments are modest in scale; demonstrating scalability to larger graphs and reporting runtime would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical advances with explicit convergence and complexity bounds for both methods.
- Clear integration of inner accuracy with outer convergence.
- Empirical improvements over [21] on a real dataset.

Cons for acceptance:
- No runtime/wall-clock or memory comparisons; report per-iteration and end-to-end times for all methods, and the realized inner iteration counts (N, N1, N2).
- Limited evaluation (lack of NDCG/MAP, sensitivity to α, larger graphs, and ablations of inner solver).
- Some presentation/notation issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding comprehensive runtime results and richer evaluation metrics.
