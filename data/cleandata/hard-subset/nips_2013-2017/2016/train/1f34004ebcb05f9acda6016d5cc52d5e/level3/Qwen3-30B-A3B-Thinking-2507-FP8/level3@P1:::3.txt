

This paper proposes two two-level optimization methods (GBN and GFN) for learning Supervised PageRank models, addressing a non-convex loss-minimization problem that accounts for features of nodes and edges. The authors build on Nesterov and Nemirovski's work [17] for approximating the stationary distribution of Markov random walks with any desired accuracy, which they use as the lower-level component of their two-level framework. The upper-level component applies gradient-based and gradient-free optimization with inexact oracles. Unlike previous work [21], which lacks convergence guarantees, the authors provide theoretical convergence rate analysis for both methods, which is their primary contribution.

The paper makes several strong technical contributions. First, it develops a gradient-based method (GBN) with theoretical convergence guarantees to stationary points without requiring convexity, which is a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art. Second, it proposes a gradient-free method (GFN) that avoids calculating derivatives for large matrices, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. Third, the paper provides detailed complexity analysis for both methods, showing how the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity can be managed. The theoretical results are well-supported by lemmas and theorems, with complete proofs provided in the supplementary material.

The empirical results demonstrate that both proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art gradient-based method [21] on a real-world web page ranking task. The authors report superior loss function values across multiple test sets, with statistically significant improvements (p<0.005) for GBN on most datasets. The paper also provides a clear description of the Yandex dataset, which consists of 600 queries with 1.7K labeled query-document pairs, allowing for reasonable reproducibility despite the non-public dataset. The hyperparameter choices are well-justified and consistent across experiments.

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, though it would benefit from a symbol table to help readers navigate the many mathematical symbols used throughout the paper. The relationship to prior work is appropriately contextualized, particularly with respect to Nesterov and Nemirovski's foundational contributions [17, 23, 24]. The authors correctly acknowledge that their work generalizes [17] while providing convergence guarantees that previous supervised PageRank optimization methods lacked.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the non-convex nature of the problem and the need for a small vicinity of the local minimum to apply the gradient-based method.

For clarity, the paper is generally well-organized, but the dense mathematical notation could be improved with a symbol table. The writing is precise and appropriate for the NIPS audience, with sufficient detail to reproduce the results.

The originality of the work is strong, as it successfully combines inexact oracle optimization methods with supervised PageRank learning. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by providing convergence guarantees that were previously missing. The authors properly reference related work, including recent NIPS papers on similar topics (e.g., [21] and [17]).

The significance of the paper is substantial. The proposed methods not only outperform existing approaches but also provide theoretical guarantees that are essential for practical deployment. The ability to learn supervised PageRank models with convergence guarantees directly benefits real-world applications like search engine ranking. The work represents a meaningful advance in the state-of-the-art for learning graph-based ranking models.

Strengths:
- Theoretical convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods
- Detailed complexity analysis with clear trade-off analysis
- Superior empirical performance on real-world ranking task
- Well-motivated approach that builds on strong theoretical foundations
- Clear description of the dataset and experimental setup

Weaknesses:
- Lack of a symbol table makes the paper difficult to follow
- Experiments only compare loss function values, not ranking quality metrics
- Does not explicitly connect how the methods generalize [17] in the main text
- Dataset is not publicly available, though described sufficiently for reproduction

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of graph-based learning and optimization. The theoretical advances are significant, the empirical results are compelling, and the work has clear practical implications. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the substantial contribution, and the paper should be accepted.
