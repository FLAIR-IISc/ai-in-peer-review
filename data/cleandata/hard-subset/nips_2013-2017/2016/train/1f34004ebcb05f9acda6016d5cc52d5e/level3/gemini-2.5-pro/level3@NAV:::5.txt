This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for a feature-based Supervised PageRank model. The authors correctly identify that the state-of-the-art learning algorithm for this model [21] lacks theoretical performance guarantees. To address this gap, the paper proposes two optimization methods—one gradient-based and one gradient-free—built upon an inexact oracle framework. The core idea is to combine an approximation for the stationary distribution of the Markov walk with optimization methods designed for noisy loss estimates, for which the authors provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contributions. By framing the learning problem within an inexact oracle setting, the authors provide the first, to my knowledge, convergence rate guarantees for this type of Supervised PageRank optimization. The paper presents a rigorous analysis, including lemmas that bound the approximate gradient and a performance theorem for a general biased gradient-free oracle. This theoretical work is sound and addresses a clear and important limitation of prior methods.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the strong theoretical foundation, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its presentation, empirical validation, and claims of originality.
1.  **Originality and Framing:** The underlying Supervised PageRank model is a direct reproduction of the framework from Zhukovskii et al. [21], albeit with different notation. The paper also incorrectly refers to this as "Supervised PageRank" from [21], while that paper's title is "Supervised Nested PageRank," which could cause confusion. The novelty is therefore limited to the optimization algorithm, not the model.
2.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is structured such that it is not a self-contained contribution. Nearly all essential technical content, including proofs and detailed experimental results, has been relegated to an appendix. The main text lacks proof sketches or sufficient empirical analysis, making it difficult to fully assess the work's claims. The quality of writing is also poor, with grammatical errors and inconsistent citations that hinder readability.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is cursory and unconvincing. It omits crucial details needed for reproducibility, such as the justification for selecting the initial parameters (ϕ0) and the search radius (R). The authors claim their methods outperform baselines but fail to discuss the magnitude or statistical significance of these improvements in the main text. Furthermore, the experiments appear to be limited to a single dataset, which is insufficient to demonstrate generalizability.
4.  **Practical Significance:** The practical utility of the proposed method is undermined by potentially unrealistic technical assumptions and an unaddressed major limitation: constraining the parameter search to a small ball around a predefined ϕ0 could severely restrict the model's performance in real-world scenarios.

In summary, while the paper provides valuable theoretical bounds for a known problem, it fails to present a complete, convincing case for its practical impact due to a critically underdeveloped experimental section and major clarity issues.
