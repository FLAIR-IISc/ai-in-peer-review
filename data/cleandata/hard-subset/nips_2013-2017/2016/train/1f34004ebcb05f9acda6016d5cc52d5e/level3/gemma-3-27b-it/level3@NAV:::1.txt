## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a graph-based ranking approach that incorporates node and edge features. The core challenge lies in the non-convexity of the optimization problem and the difficulty of obtaining exact objective function values. The authors propose two-level optimization algorithms – one gradient-based and one gradient-free – relying on inexact oracle estimations of the stationary distribution of the underlying Markov random walk. They provide convergence guarantees for both methods, a significant contribution given the lack of existing theoretical results for this problem.

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors convincingly demonstrate how to approximate the loss function to desired precision and subsequently develop convergence proofs for both optimization algorithms within this inexact oracle framework. Adapting inexact-oracle gradient approaches to constrained non-convex problems is a notable technical achievement. The work is well-written and the mathematical derivations appear sound, although a complete verification of all proofs was not feasible within the review timeframe. The experimental validation on a real-world web ranking dataset provides further support for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

However, the novelty of the approach is somewhat limited. The use of inexact oracles and two-level optimization schemes, while effective here, draws heavily from prior work, particularly Nesterov & Spokoiny (2015) on random gradient-free optimization. Additionally, the concept of supervised PageRank itself, while extended here with feature integration, is not entirely new, and related work in this area exists (as acknowledged in the paper).  The authors could strengthen the contribution by more clearly positioning their work within the broader landscape of supervised ranking algorithms and highlighting the specific advantages of their approach in terms of convergence guarantees and practical performance. 

Several minor issues detract from the overall presentation. Most notably, the paper lacks a concluding section summarizing the key findings and outlining potential avenues for future research. Furthermore, presenting the optimization problem in a more general context, beyond the specific application of Supervised PageRank, could help clarify the broader applicability and significance of the proposed techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundations with rigorous convergence proofs.
*   Addresses a challenging optimization problem with practical relevance.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Empirical results demonstrate improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; builds heavily on existing techniques.
*   Lack of a concluding section.
*   Potential to broaden the context and highlight broader applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of graph-based ranking and optimization. While the novelty is incremental, the rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation are commendable. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the missing conclusion and clarifying the broader contributions of the work within the context of existing optimization literature.
