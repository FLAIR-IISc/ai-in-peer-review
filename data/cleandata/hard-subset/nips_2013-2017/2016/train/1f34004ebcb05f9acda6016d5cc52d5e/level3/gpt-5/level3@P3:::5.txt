Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning a feature-parameterized PageRank model (“Supervised PageRank”), where node and edge transition/restart probabilities are linear in features. The loss is a pairwise hinge-squared loss over judge-labeled page pairs per query. The optimization is two-level: the inner level approximates per-query stationary distributions and their derivatives; the outer level optimizes the feature weights.

The authors’ main technical idea is to combine (i) a linearly convergent approximation for stationary distributions of Markov chains (Nesterov–Nemirovski aggregation scheme) to obtain controllable-accuracy estimates of f and ∇f, with (ii) robust optimization methods under inexact oracles: a random gradient-free (zeroth-order) method with a biased oracle and a projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle. They prove convergence rates for both, including bounds that trade off inner/outer accuracies. They then apply these to learn parameters on a web-browsing graph dataset.

This direction is related to prior work on PageRank variants (PageRank, HITS, BrowseRank), supervised/semi-supervised graph ranking with features (e.g., Supervised Random Walks; semi-supervised ranking with rich metadata), and to optimization under inexact oracles/bandit/zeroth-order methods (Flaxman et al., Agarwal–Dekel–Xiao, Nesterov–Spokoiny, Ghadimi–Lan, Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov). The inner stationary-distribution approximation is standard (Nesterov–Nemirovski) and has been compared to power method in prior literature. The paper appears to reproduce an existing feature-based PageRank framework under different notation, and the citation calling it “Supervised PageRank [21]” seems off (the cited item looks like a semi-supervised method); please clarify and correct.

Quality
- Theory: The paper formalizes inexact zero/first-order oracles for this problem, states assumptions, and proves convergence rate theorems. The lemmas bounding errors in f and ∇f from finite inner iterations are useful. The biased gradient-free oracle and adaptive projected gradient method are sound extensions of known templates. However:
  - Many crucial assumptions are strong or insufficiently justified for real data: local convexity of a globally nonconvex objective within a pre-specified ball Φ; Lipschitz constants known/tuned; positivity and scale-invariance constraints; and searching only within an R-ball around ϕ̂.
  - The main proofs and derivations are relegated to the appendix; the main text lacks proof sketches explaining key ideas and conditions for the bounds to be tight/useful in practice.
- Empirics: The experimental section is thin. Key parameters (α, τ, N, N1, N2, δ choices, stopping criteria) are either absent or only partially reported; sensitivity analyses are deferred to the appendix; and only loss values are reported—no ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP) or run-time/compute comparisons. Claims of “significant” improvements are not substantiated with detailed tables, effect sizes, or robust baselines. It is unclear whether multiple graphs/datasets were tested, and the dataset description suggests only one setting (600 queries from one engine).

Clarity
- The writing is hard to follow, with grammatical errors, inconsistent notation (e.g., ϕ0 vs ϕ̂, radius R), and several sloppy or incomplete citations. Important modeling choices (how ϕ0 and R were chosen; why α; how Φ intersects the simplex) are not explained. The paper repeatedly references the appendix for core content; the main text should include at least proof sketches and full experimental results.

Originality
- The combination—stationary distribution approximation with inexact-oracle gradient-free/gradient methods—is a reasonable synthesis but largely incremental relative to known zero-order/first-order inexact-oracle frameworks and standard Markov-chain approximation techniques. The “general biased gradient-free oracle” and constrained setting are useful, but similar ideas have appeared in bandit/zeroth-order literature. The learning framework itself appears to reproduce prior supervised/semi-supervised PageRank formulations; please clarify novelty over Supervised Random Walks and prior Supervised/Semi-supervised PageRank works.

Significance
- Theoretical bounds under inexact oracles for this application are valuable. However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate practical impact. The R-ball constraint, unvalidated assumptions (local convexity), and missing empirical depth limit the utility. Without clear ranking improvements, speedups, or broader datasets, it is hard to see near-term adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear formalization of inexact oracle setups for this class of problems; convergence guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods.
- Useful error bounds linking inner Markov-chain approximation accuracy to outer optimization progress.
- Potentially valuable to practitioners who must optimize nonconvex objectives with only approximate function/gradient evaluations.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is inadequate: missing metrics, parameters, baselines, and discussion of effect sizes; results largely in the appendix.
- Questionable novelty beyond adapting known techniques; reproduces prior frameworks under new notation; possible mis-citation/misnaming of related work.
- Strong and potentially unrealistic assumptions (local convexity in a fixed ball; positivity constraints; known L), with limited justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Writing quality and referencing issues hinder reproducibility and understanding.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the relation to “Supervised PageRank” and “Semi-supervised PageRank”/Supervised Random Walks; what is genuinely new in the model or loss beyond optimization details?
- How were ϕ0 (or ϕ̂) and radius R chosen? What is the sensitivity of results to R, and why is search restricted to an R-ball?
- What damping α was used? How were τ, δ, N, N1, N2 selected in practice?
- Can you report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) and wall-clock time/compute for inner/outer loops, including comparisons to power method and to simple baselines (e.g., RankSVM/GBDT on features, Supervised Random Walks)?
- Do results generalize to other graphs/datasets?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include proof sketches for main theorems and clear statements of all assumptions in the main text.
- Correct citations and nomenclature; unify notation; proofread thoroughly.
- Provide complete experimental details, ranking metrics, computational cost, sensitivity studies (R, α, L, ε), and ablations (inner accuracy vs outer progress).
- Compare against stronger baselines, including non-graph learning-to-rank and supervised random-walk methods.
- Discuss practical realism of assumptions and provide guidance on choosing hyperparameters.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The theoretical development is solid, but the paper does not meet NIPS standards for clarity, novelty, and empirical validation. With corrected citations, improved writing, expanded experiments with standard ranking metrics and multiple datasets, and a clearer novelty statement, this work could be competitive.
