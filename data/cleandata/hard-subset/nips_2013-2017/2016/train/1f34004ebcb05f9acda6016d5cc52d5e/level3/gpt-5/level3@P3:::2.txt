Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper formulates supervised PageRank learning as a constrained non-convex optimization over node/edge feature weights. The loss is a pairwise hinge-squared surrogate on query-specific graphs, with PageRank determined by feature-weighted restart and transition probabilities. The key algorithmic contribution is a two-level scheme with inexact oracles: (i) a gradient-free method that uses a biased zero-order oracle formed via randomized sphere smoothing and an inexact objective evaluation obtained by a fast PageRank solver (Nesterov–Nemirovski), and (ii) a gradient-based method using an inexact first-order oracle (approximate function value and gradient) with an adaptive projected gradient algorithm. The paper gives iteration and arithmetic-operation complexity bounds that couple oracle accuracies (δ), smoothing parameter (τ), and inner PageRank iteration counts (N, N1, N2) to outer iteration complexity. It also analyzes the trade-offs between inner accuracy and total runtime. Experiments (Yandex browsing graphs; query-dependent features) compare the proposed methods to a prior two-level gradient method used for supervised PageRank.

This work connects to supervised random walks/ranking on graphs (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec, 2011; Liu et al., BrowseRank; supervised/nested PageRank variants) and to optimization with inexact oracles and zero-/first-order methods (Nesterov & Spokoiny; Ghadimi & Lan; Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov). On the PageRank computation side, it builds on Nesterov–Nemirovski’s faster stationary-distribution estimation compared to the power method.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of learning supervised PageRank with feature-weighted restart/transition and a non-convex, constrained objective.
- Two solution strategies with provable complexity under inexact oracles, bridging practical PageRank estimation and outer-level optimization.
- The adaptive first-order method for non-convex composite problems with an inexact oracle is a useful general tool; proofs and complexity bounds are provided.
- Inner–outer trade-off analysis is valuable and often omitted in prior work on learning PageRank-like models.
- Practical problem with real industrial data and query-dependent graphs.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:
Major complaint: All (or nearly all) experimental results are in the supplement, leaving the main paper incomplete. For NIPS, the main claims should be assessable without the supplement. Please move the key quantitative results (tables with test-set loss/metrics; learning curves; sensitivity to ε, τ, N, N1, N2; wall-clock and oracle-call counts) into the main text, and keep only proofs and extended ablations in the appendix.

Detailed comment 1 (self-contained experiments): The main text should include at least one table with final performance on all test splits, plus plots of outer-iteration convergence, and a runtime/efficiency table. Without these, it is hard to judge significance.

Detailed comment 2 (Theorem 1 vs non-convexity): Theorem 1 assumes convex f, whereas the target objective (2.4) is explicitly non-convex. The paper subsequently “assumes” convexity on a small ball Φ around a local minimum. This creates a circularity: how should Φ be chosen a priori to ensure convexity? Are there verifiable sufficient conditions on features and Aq that imply local convexity? If Theorem 1 is used only as a local guarantee, the algorithmic procedure for verifying/maintaining this convexity (or adapting τ, δ, or shrinking Φ) should be stated. Otherwise, clarify that the zero-order method has no convergence guarantees for general non-convex f under the presented analysis.

Detailed comment 3 (Theorem 3 and stationarity): Theorem 3 bounds MK(xK − xK+1). Please clarify how this translates into a standard non-convex convergence criterion (e.g., E[||∇f(xK)||] ≤ ε or prox-gradient norm ≤ ε). Under (5.1), can you relate ||MK(xk − xk+1)|| to a stationarity measure for ψ(x)? If so, state the exact mapping and assumptions; if not, the claim “convergence to a stationary point” should be softened to “convergence to a point with small projected (prox) step.” A corollary bounding a canonical stationarity measure would strengthen the result.

Detailed comment 4 (GFN convergence speed and efficiency reporting): Supplementary Figure 1 suggests the gradient-free method converges slowly. Please quantify algorithmic efficiency: report wall-clock time, number of inner PageRank iterations (N, N1, N2) and total arithmetic operations, as well as outer iterations, for all methods on each dataset. Include stopping criteria equivalence (e.g., target loss gap or stationarity measure) to ensure fair comparison. If GFN’s advantage is derivative-avoidance for huge matrices, it is important to show when its time-to-accuracy is competitive.

Quality:
- The theoretical components are generally sound, but the convexity assumption in Theorem 1 versus the non-convex objective needs clarification, and Theorem 3’s implication for stationarity should be made explicit. Experimental claims are not adequately substantiated in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written overall, with clear problem setup and algorithms. However, the reliance on the supplement for both proofs and all key empirical results impairs readability and verification. Notation is dense; a short table of symbols would help. Please clarify how δ, τ, and L0 are chosen in practice, and provide concrete values of N, N1, N2 used.

Originality:
- Combining inexact-oracle zero-/first-order methods with supervised PageRank learning and providing end-to-end complexity trade-offs is novel relative to prior supervised random walk work. The adaptive inexact first-order framework is a solid contribution.

Significance:
- Methods addressing large-scale learning of graph-ranking models with provable guarantees are of broad interest. With stronger and more transparent empirical evidence (including runtime), this could influence practice. As is, significance is limited by incomplete presentation of results in the main paper.

Questions for the authors:
- How do you pick Φ to ensure convexity for Theorem 1? Any verifiable sufficient conditions?
- Can you provide a corollary linking ||MK(xK − xK+1)|| to a standard stationarity measure?
- What are typical values of N, N1, N2, and how sensitive are results to ε and τ?
- Can you report wall-clock and oracle-call counts for all methods, and include convergence vs time plots?

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contributions on inexact-oracle optimization and their application to supervised PageRank.
- Practical relevance and clear two-level engineering of the solution.
- Potentially strong empirical performance.

Cons for acceptance:
- Main paper lacks core experimental results; efficiency evidence is insufficient.
- Theorem–assumption mismatch (convexity) and unclear stationarity guarantee.
- Gradient-free method appears slow; practical conditions where it wins are not compellingly demonstrated.

Recommendation: Borderline reject unless the authors move key experiments and efficiency analysis into the main text and clarify the theoretical gaps (convexity scope for Theorem 1; stationarity implication of Theorem 3). With these fixes, this could be a strong contribution.
