- Tackles learning in Supervised PageRank where restart/transition probs are linear in node/edge features and the objective is a non-convex pairwise square loss over query graphs.
- Casts stationary distribution and its derivatives as an inexact oracle and develops end-to-end complexity accounting across lower- and upper-level computations.
- Proposes two upper-level solvers with provable convergence: (i) an adaptive gradient-based method for constrained, possibly non-convex composite optimization with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a randomized gradient-free method for constrained convex optimization with an inexact zero-order oracle.
- Advocates using the Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method (generalized to derivatives) for lower-level stationary distribution solves, arguing better complexity than power iteration.
- Provides convergence-rate bounds, complexity estimates, and a trade-off analysis between oracle accuracy and overall complexity for both algorithms.
- Empirical evaluation on a real Yandex query-dependent browsing graph shows both methods outperform prior gradient-based work, with the gradient-free method giving the largest gains.
- Builds on prior supervised PageRank and feature-augmented random-walk models, replacing power iteration with Nesterov–Nemirovski and extending eigenderivative ideas.
- Extends inexact-oracle zero- and first-order frameworks to constrained, inexact settings and handles unknown oracle Lipschitz constants via adaptive line-search style strategy.
- Strength: clear identification of the inexact-oracle structure and thorough end-to-end complexity analysis.
- Strength: theoretical convergence guarantees for both solvers in constrained, inexact settings—improving on prior work that lacked such guarantees.
- Strength: thoughtful trade-off and iteration-accuracy analysis for lower-level methods and their impact on overall complexity.
- Strength: practical benefit from gradient-free method avoiding expensive derivative computations and an adaptive gradient method reducing tuning needs.
- Weakness: gradient-free method requires a local convexity assumption around a local minimum, which is unclear given the overall non-convex loss.
- Weakness: limited empirical baselines (only prior gradient-based method and untuned model) and reliance on optimization loss rather than standard IR metrics like NDCG/MAP.
- Weakness: missing wall-clock runtime and memory reporting for lower-level and overall training despite complexity claims.
- Weakness: some technical constants and proofs deferred to the supplement; main text could give more intuition and concrete parameter guidance.
- Weakness: notation-dense sections (notably Sect. 3) hurt readability and could be streamlined.
- Quality: analyses appear technically sound with meaningful extensions for inexact oracles and non-convex composite optimization.
- Experimental quality: shows consistent improvements and faster convergence of lower-level method, but lacks runtime and broader baselines to confirm practical significance.
- Clarity: problem setup and prior work relations are clear, but heavy notation and lack of worked examples limit accessibility.
- Originality: novel combination of constrained inexact-oracle zero/first-order methods with principled lower-level solvers and extensions to derivative estimation.
- Significance: practically important setting and valuable theoretical guarantees where prior work had none, but broader empirical validation needed for wider adoption.
- Suggestions: report end-to-end wall-clock time and memory, evaluate standard IR metrics on held-out data, add stronger baselines (RankSVM/LambdaMART), and clarify convexity assumptions and hyperparameter guidance.
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theoretical advances, end-to-end complexity accounting, and empirical improvements on a large real dataset.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing runtime and broader empirical comparisons, reliance on unverified local convexity for gradient-free method, and exposition/hyperparameter clarity issues.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept—theoretical and practical relevance are strong but paper needs stronger runtime and empirical evaluation to be fully convincing.
