This paper addresses the non-convex loss-minimization problem for learning Supervised PageRank models. The authors propose two two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based method (GBN) and a gradient-free method (GFN). This work builds directly on the Supervised PageRank framework [21], which lacked formal convergence guarantees, and adapts the concept of inexact oracles for non-convex optimization, drawing inspiration from the foundational work of Nesterov and Nemirovski [17]. The core idea is to use an efficient lower-level algorithm to approximate the stationary distribution and its derivatives to a required accuracy, which then informs an upper-level optimization step.

The paper's primary strength lies in its rigorous theoretical contribution. By developing methods that explicitly handle inexact function and gradient calculations, the authors provide the first convergence rate guarantees for this problem. GBN is shown to converge to a stationary point with a given accuracy, while GFN guarantees a decrease in the loss function. This theoretical soundness is a significant advance over the previous state-of-the-art heuristic approach. The methods are also practically appealing, as they avoid the computation of large matrices. The experimental results, demonstrating superior performance in terms of loss minimization compared to the method in [21], support the practical value of the proposed algorithms.

However, the paper has weaknesses in clarity and the completeness of its experimental evaluation. The manuscript is dense with notation, making it challenging to follow; for instance, symbols like 'N' are used before being formally defined. The inclusion of a symbol table would greatly enhance readability. On the experimental side, the comparison against the baseline method (GBP) is limited to the loss function. Since the end goal is ranking, a direct comparison using standard ranking quality metrics (e.g., nDCG) is necessary to fully substantiate the claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art. Furthermore, given that the work is a generalization of the method in [17], a direct empirical comparison or a more detailed discussion of why the original method is not a suitable baseline would strengthen the paper's positioning.

In summary:
**Pro:** Strong theoretical contribution with convergence guarantees for an important problem; addresses a key limitation in prior work; promising empirical results on the loss function.
**Con:** Poor clarity due to dense notation; experimental evaluation is incomplete, lacking ranking quality comparisons and a direct comparison to the foundational method [17].

While the theoretical contribution is significant, the paper would be much stronger if the authors address the issues of clarity and expand their experimental validation.
