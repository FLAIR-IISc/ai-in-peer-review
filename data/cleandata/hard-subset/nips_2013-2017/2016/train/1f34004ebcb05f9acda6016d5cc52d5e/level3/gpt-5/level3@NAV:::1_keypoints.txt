- Paper studies convergence guarantees for supervised PageRank-style ranking where node/edge weights depend on features via a parameterized Markov random walk and loss/gradients are approximated.  
- Proposes a two-level scheme: (i) a linearly convergent estimator of the stationary distribution to approximate loss/gradient to arbitrary precision.  
- Upper-level uses inexact-oracle optimization: a random gradient-free method and an adaptive projected gradient method for constrained, possibly non-convex problems, with convergence to stationary points.  
- Main technical contribution: adapts inexact-oracle gradient approaches to constrained settings with only computable-precision function values and quantifies the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall complexity.  
- Empirically, methods outperform a prior supervised PageRank baseline on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset.  
- Quality: theory, oracle constructions, error propagation, and complexity analyses are technically careful and appear sound.  
- Methodological caveat: relies on a local convexity assumption to deploy the gradient-free algorithm and inexact composite framework.  
- Experimental weaknesses: limited baselines (no comparisons to other graph-based learning-to-rank methods), potential test-set peeking in stopping/tuning protocols, and unclear practical impact of constants in complexity bounds.  
- Clarity: paper is well written and proofs seem correct, but a conclusion is missing.  
- Suggestion: framing the optimization problem more generally up front would better position the contribution relative to inexact-oracle literature (e.g., Devolder et al., Nesterov, Ghadimi & Lan, Nesterov & Spokoiny).  
- Originality: builds on known inexact-oracle and gradient-free methods; novelty is incremental but nontrivial in adapting to constrained non-convex problems with computable-precision values.  
- Significance: relevant to large-scale graph ranking where exact objectives are unavailable; theoretical guarantees fill a practical gap.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theory for inexact oracles under constraints, clear two-level design with tunable trade-offs, and empirical gains over a supervised PageRank baseline.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited novelty relative to prior work, narrow experimental evaluation and tuning issues, and missing conclusion/earlier problem framing.
