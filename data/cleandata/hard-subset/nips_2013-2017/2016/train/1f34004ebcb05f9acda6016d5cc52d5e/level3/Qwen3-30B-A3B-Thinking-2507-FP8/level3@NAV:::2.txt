

This paper proposes a novel framework for learning Supervised PageRank models by formulating it as a constrained non-convex optimization problem incorporating node/edge features. The authors develop two solution approaches: a gradient-free method using an inexact zero-order oracle and a gradient-based method with an inexact first-order oracle. The key theoretical contribution is providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, addressing a gap in prior work [21] which lacked such theoretical foundations.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions by developing a general framework for optimization with inexact oracles and applying it to the specific Supervised PageRank problem. The analysis of the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. The approach builds upon recent work on inexact oracle methods [7, 16, 25] while extending it to constrained non-convex settings.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that substantially impact its evaluation. The most critical issue is that all experimental results are relegated to the supplement, making the main paper incomplete and impossible to evaluate without accessing supplementary materials. This violates standard conference expectations for self-contained papers. Theorem 1 assumes convexity for the objective function, yet Equation (2.4) explicitly describes a non-convex problem, creating a theoretical inconsistency without clarification on how to choose Φ to guarantee convexity in practice. Theorem 3 bounds MK(xK - x{K+1}) but fails to establish that this implies convergence to a stationary point, which is a fundamental requirement for optimization algorithms. Supplementary Figure 1 shows the gradient-free method converges slowly, yet the authors do not provide comparative efficiency analysis or discuss this limitation in the main text.

While the theoretical framework is sound and the problem is well-motivated, these issues significantly undermine the paper's quality and completeness. The lack of experimental results in the main text prevents proper assessment of the claimed performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The theoretical inconsistencies also raise questions about the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

Pro: Strong theoretical contributions, novel problem formulation, valuable analysis of approximation-complexity trade-offs. Con: Critical missing experimental results in main paper, theoretical inconsistencies, insufficient convergence analysis, and lack of algorithmic efficiency comparison. Given these issues, the paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.
