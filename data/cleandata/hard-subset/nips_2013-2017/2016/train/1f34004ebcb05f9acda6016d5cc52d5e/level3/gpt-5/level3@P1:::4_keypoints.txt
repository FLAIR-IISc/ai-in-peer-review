- Paper formulates supervised PageRank learning as a nonconvex loss minimization and proposes two two-level solvers: an adaptive inexact first-order method and a random inexact zero-order method.  
- Lower-level uses a linearly convergent Nesterov–Nemirovski method for Markov chains and generalizes it to compute derivatives with controllable oracle accuracy.  
- Upper-level algorithms come with convergence-to-stationarity guarantees and complexity bounds that include lower-level costs.  
- Empirical results on a Yandex browsing-graph ranking task show improvements over a state-of-the-art power-iteration-based gradient method.  
- Relates to prior supervised PageRank/random-walk work and extends inexact-oracle and smoothing/one-point feedback frameworks to constrained, possibly nonconvex settings.  
- Novelty lies in handling deterministic inexact oracles from Markov-chain approximations and providing end-to-end two-level complexity bounds.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: precise inexact-oracle definitions, adaptive projected gradient with backtracking, and nonconvex composite convergence guarantees.  
- Lower-level advance: adoption and generalization of a linearly convergent PageRank solver to derivative computation with explicit error/control (Lemmas 1–2).  
- Practical relevance: avoids per-entry differentiation of large transition matrices via gradient-free option and enables controllable oracle accuracy for both solvers.  
- Empirical evidence: consistent loss improvements and sensitivity analysis w.r.t. algorithmic hyperparameters (ε, L0).  
- Missing runtime reporting: no wall-clock times, hardware specs, parallelism details, or breakdown of time between lower and upper levels.  
- Evaluation metrics limited: uses square-loss proxy but lacks standard IR metrics (NDCG@k, MAP) on labeled pairs to assess ranking quality.  
- GFN assumes local convexity of f on the ball Φ despite overall nonconvexity; this assumption needs empirical justification or relaxation.  
- Choice and estimation of smoothness parameter L (set to 1e−4) and β1 lack clarification and adaptive estimation guidance.  
- Baselines insufficient: should compare to standard learning-to-rank models (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART) and other PageRank learning variants.  
- Need lower-level ablations: show trade-offs between iteration counts (N, N1, N2), oracle errors (δ1, δ2), and end-to-end runtime; compare power iteration vs. Nesterov–Nemirovski in time.  
- Projection/feasibility concerns: clarify whether projections preserve positivity or project onto intersection of ball and Rm++, and detail handling of α (fixed vs. learned).  
- Reproducibility issues: provide implementation details (sparsity handling, memory, stopping criteria), random seeds, and ideally code.  
- Technical quality: careful and well-grounded development; proofs are plausible though deferred; experimental methodology reasonable but missing runtimes and ranking metrics.  
- Clarity: generally clear but dense; suggest defining constants on first use, unifying notation, and adding a practical recipe for setting hyperparameters.  
- Originality: moderately high—core tools are known but constrained/inexact extensions and derivative computation scheme with full two-level analysis are novel.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for large-scale graph ranking, but empirical scale and missing runtime/metric evidence limit demonstrated practical significance.  
- Pros summary: theoretical guarantees for both methods, clear two-level design with controllable accuracy, and empirical improvements over prior training.  
- Cons summary: no wall-clock scalability evaluation, reliance on local convexity for GFN with limited validation, and limited evaluation metrics and baselines.  
- Key author questions: report wall-clock times and cost splits for Q1/Q2 subsets; sensitivity to α and Φ; possibility of learning α jointly and extending guarantees; and whether projections ever violate positivity and how that’s enforced.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on adding runtime results and at least one standard ranking metric.
