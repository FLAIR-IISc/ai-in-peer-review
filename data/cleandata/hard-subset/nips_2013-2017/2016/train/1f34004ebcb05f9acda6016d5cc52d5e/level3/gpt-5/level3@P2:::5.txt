Summary
The paper studies learning a feature-parameterized random-walk ranking model (“Supervised PageRank”), where restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node/edge features. The learning objective is a pairwise square-loss over query-specific graphs, with parameters constrained to a Euclidean ball to ensure positivity. The core challenge is that evaluating the objective and its gradient requires stationary distributions and their derivatives for many queries; these can only be computed approximately.

The paper proposes two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-free method that uses a biased, inexact zero-order oracle and projects onto the constraint set; (ii) an adaptive first-order method for constrained, possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle. On the lower level, both rely on a linearly convergent iterative method (Nesterov–Nemirovski) to approximate stationary distributions and, via a generalization, gradients w.r.t. parameters. The authors derive error bounds for approximate function values and gradients (lemmata) and prove convergence-rate guarantees for both upper-level methods under oracle inexactness. Experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset compare the proposed methods to a prior gradient-based baseline; reported results suggest improvements in ranking loss.

Relation to prior work
The model and learning setting are closely related to Supervised/Personalized PageRank, BrowseRank, and supervised random walks (e.g., Richardson & Domingos; Backstrom & Leskovec; Dai & Davison; Liu et al.), where features modulate restart/transition probabilities. On the optimization side, the paper builds on zero-order random gradient-free methods and inexact-oracle first-order methods (Nesterov; Nesterov & Spokoiny; Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Ghadimi & Lan). The novelty claimed is adapting these frameworks to constrained problems with biased inexact oracles, and coupling them with a specific lower-level PageRank approximation with explicit complexity bounds. The use of the Nesterov–Nemirovski iterative scheme to approximate stationary distributions (and its derivatives) is known; the paper’s contribution is to quantify its role within a full learning algorithm and propagate its errors to end-to-end guarantees.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant problem: feature-rich random-walk ranking with rigorous convergence guarantees under realistic inexactness.
- Clear separation of lower-level approximation and upper-level optimization, with explicit bounds linking target accuracy to the number of lower-level iterations.
- Extends random gradient-free optimization to constrained problems with biased inexact function values; and provides an adaptive projected gradient method for nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracles.
- Complexity bounds for end-to-end learning are stated; the lower-level method has favorable linear convergence compared to power iteration.
- Experiments on a real, query-dependent browsing graph; both proposed methods appear to outperform a prior baseline in loss.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: numerous grammatical issues, sloppy and sometimes inconsistent citations (e.g., “Supervised PageRank” vs semi-/nested PageRank; reference list entries incomplete), and heavy reliance on the supplement for key proofs and empirical details.
- Theoretical assumptions may be strong or unverified in practice. The gradient-free convergence guarantee assumes convexity on the feasible set; the paper argues local convexity near a minimizer but does not justify it. First-order guarantees require an inexact first-order oracle of a specific form; conditions under which this is satisfied for the nonconvex loss are not clearly demonstrated.
- The parameter constraint Φ = ball around ϕ̂ with small radius to ensure positivity could severely restrict expressivity. There is no ablation on the effect of R or discussion of practical constraint handling (e.g., reparameterization via softplus/simplex).
- Experimental validation is underreported. Key parameters (τ, δ, N, N1, N2), wall-clock times, and resource usage are missing. Only one dataset family is used; it is unclear whether results generalize beyond the particular Yandex browsing graphs. Comparative baselines are limited (a single prior gradient method) and not aligned with broader literature (e.g., supervised random walks, strong learning-to-rank baselines).
- Important methodological choices (initial ϕ0, selection of R, stopping criteria, step-size tuning) are not sufficiently justified. Claims of statistical significance lack full reporting (e.g., metrics beyond loss, variance, and effect sizes).
- Main technical content (proof sketches, algorithmic details) and empirical evidence are mostly in the appendix, leaving the main text thin.

Quality
- The lower-level approximation lemmas and the propagation of oracle errors to upper-level convergence are technically sound in spirit, but details are deferred. Conditions for the inexact first-order oracle and the constants (e.g., β1, L) require clearer derivation and discussion.
- Empirical support is insufficient to substantiate practical impact; runtime comparisons to power iteration and baseline methods are not convincingly demonstrated in the main text.

Clarity
- The manuscript needs substantial editing for language, notation hygiene, and citation accuracy. Many definitions are scattered; some assumptions are introduced late. Provide proof sketches and key intuitions in the main text; move only technical details to the supplement.

Originality
- Methodologically, the work largely adapts and combines known tools (inexact oracles, zero-order methods, adaptive gradient methods, Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration) to this application. The constrained, biased inexact-oracle treatment and end-to-end complexity accounting are incremental but useful. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- If convincingly demonstrated, the guarantees could be valuable for large-scale ranking systems where only approximate stationary distributions and gradients are available. As presented, limited empirical breadth reduces the immediate impact.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the choice of radius R and center ϕ̂? What happens if Φ is larger or positivity is enforced via reparameterization?
2) Can you justify or empirically verify convexity (or local convexity) of f on Φ?
3) Please report wall-clock times and total arithmetic operations for the lower and upper levels, versus power-method baselines and supervised random walks.
4) How were τ, δ, N, N1, N2 chosen in practice? Provide a sensitivity analysis.
5) Can you compare against additional baselines (e.g., Backstrom–Leskovec supervised random walks, state-of-the-art learning-to-rank) and report ranking metrics (NDCG, MAP)?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical development for learning with inexact oracles in a relevant application.
- Clear upper–lower level decomposition with complexity bounds.
- Promising preliminary empirical improvements.

Cons for acceptance
- Poor writing, incomplete citations, and heavy reliance on supplement.
- Unclear/strong assumptions and lack of justification for key constraints.
- Insufficient experimental detail, breadth, and runtime analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Substantially revise writing; correct and complete all references; add proof sketches and intuition.
- Provide ablations on R, initialization, and oracle accuracy; add runtime results.
- Broaden empirical evaluation across datasets and baselines; report standard ranking metrics.
- Clarify and justify assumptions (convexity, oracle model) and discuss practical enforcement of positivity without constraining to a small ball.

Overall recommendation
Reject at this time. The paper contains interesting and potentially useful theoretical contributions, but the presentation quality and empirical validation are not yet at NIPS standards. With a clearer exposition, stronger experimental section, and better integration with related work, this could become a solid submission. Confidence: medium.
