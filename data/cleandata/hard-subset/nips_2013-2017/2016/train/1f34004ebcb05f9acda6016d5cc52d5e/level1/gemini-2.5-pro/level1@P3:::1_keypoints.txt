- Problem: learning Supervised PageRank parameters where the ranking loss depends on an intractable stationary distribution, framed as optimization with an inexact oracle.  
- Gap in prior work: existing gradient-descent approach [21] has no theoretical convergence guarantees.  
- Main contribution: two-level algorithms with formal convergence rate guarantees for this learning problem.  
- Low-level oracle: replace power method with Nesterov–Nemirovski linearly convergent method, extended to approximate derivatives with cost bounds (Lemmas 1–2).  
- High-level methods: (1) random gradient-free algorithm adapted for constrained inexact zero-order oracles with convergence under a local convexity assumption; (2) gradient-based adaptive-step method for non-convex constrained optimization with convergence to a stationary point.  
- Theoretical analysis: rigorously links required low-level oracle precision to high-level convergence and gives overall complexity bounds.  
- Empirical results: on proprietary web-ranking data both proposed methods beat prior GBP in final loss, with GFN particularly strong.  
- Strength: first theoretically grounded algorithms with convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning.  
- Strength: solid methodological adaptation of modern optimization (inexact oracles, NN oracle, gradient-free methods) including non-convex analysis.  
- Strength: clear organization separating general frameworks from application-specific details.  
- Strength: empirical validation shows practical effectiveness on real-world data.  
- Weakness: gradient-free theory requires a strong local convexity assumption that may not hold in practice and is under-discussed.  
- Weakness: experiments use a proprietary dataset, limiting reproducibility for the community.  
- Weakness: experimental comparison reports final loss only and omits wall-clock/runtime or FLOP comparisons despite differing per-iteration costs.  
- Weakness: the connection from oracle error bounds to the optimization framework (Inequality 5.1) could be made more explicit with derivations or constants.  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a major theoretical gap, applies rigorous optimization theory to a practical problem, and shows empirical superiority.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on strong local convexity, non-reproducible experiments, and incomplete runtime analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept — strengths and contributions outweigh weaknesses; likely of interest to optimization and ML researchers.
