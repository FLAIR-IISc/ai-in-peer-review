Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node/edge features. The loss is a pairwise squared hinge over query-dependent graphs. The key difficulty is that both the stationary distributions and their derivatives are computed only approximately. The authors propose two two-level algorithms: (i) a gradient-based method for constrained, possibly nonconvex problems with an inexact first-order oracle, and (ii) a random gradient-free method for constrained convex problems with an inexact zero-order oracle. On the lower level, both use the Nesterov–Nemirovski method to approximate stationary distributions and (for the gradient method) a generalization to approximate derivatives, yielding tunable accuracy and complexity bounds. On the upper level, they analyze convergence rates under oracle error and provide total complexity bounds that trade off lower-level accuracy and upper-level progress. Experiments on a Yandex browsing graph benchmark compare against the prior gradient-based approach for Supervised PageRank [21], which used power iterations without convergence guarantees.

This work builds on and extends: (a) zero/first-order optimization with inexact oracles (e.g., Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov; Ghadimi–Lan; Nesterov–Spokoiny; bandit smoothing methods), (b) efficient PageRank computation (Nesterov–Nemirovski) versus classical power iteration, and (c) supervised random walks/PageRank variants for ranking (Backstrom–Leskovec; BrowseRank; Supervised PageRank). The novelty lies in handling constrained optimization with inexact oracles and integrating lower-level approximation accuracy into provable upper-level convergence for this task.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper gives clear oracle-error models and establishes convergence for both methods. The gradient-free method uses spherical smoothing with bounded additive oracle error; the bound exhibits the expected bias/variance trade-off in τ and δ. The gradient-based method adapts a proximal projected scheme with line search and an (δ,L)-style inexact oracle to a nonconvex setting, guaranteeing a stationarity measure ‖M_k(x_k−x_{k+1})‖^2 ≤ ε. The lower-level analysis provides δ-accurate approximations to f and ∇f by choosing N, N1, N2 in the NN method; arithmetic operation counts are explicit. Overall, the constructions are technically sound, though many proofs are deferred.
- Assumptions: For the gradient-free method, convexity on Φ and x* ∈ int Φ are required, while the overall problem is nonconvex; the authors assume local convexity around a minimizer without verifying it. For the gradient method, the guarantee is to a stationary point under an inexact oracle; this is appropriate for nonconvex settings.
- Empirics: The experimental section demonstrates improved loss relative to the prior gradient-based Supervised PageRank and shows faster convergence of NN vs. power. However, empirical evaluation is limited: only 600 queries (~1.7k labeled pairs), and results are primarily in terms of training objective f, not standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP). Runtime and wall-clock comparisons, and a detailed accounting of lower-level iterations versus accuracy, are missing. Baselines beyond [21] (e.g., RankSVM/LambdaMART or other graph-based learning-to-rank methods) are absent.

Clarity:
The paper is dense but mostly readable. The two-level structure, oracle definitions, and theorems are laid out. Some notational choices and constants (e.g., β1) are opaque without the supplement; better intuition and a short derivation sketch would help. There are typos and formatting issues. References are inconsistently formatted and sometimes incomplete. Including pseudocode for derivative approximation and summarizing complexity in a compact table would improve readability.

Originality:
- Methodologically, extending random gradient-free methods to constrained, inexact zero-order oracles with spherical smoothing, and adapting an inexact first-order oracle framework to nonconvex constrained problems with adaptive L, are meaningful but incremental extensions of known techniques (Flaxman/Kalai/McMahan-style smoothing; Devolder et al.; Ghadimi–Lan; Nesterov–Spokoiny).
- The integration with NN for stationary distribution and derivative approximation, plus end-to-end complexity that couples lower- and upper-level accuracies, is well-motivated and less explored in the ranking literature.
- For the application, learning Supervised PageRank is not new; the contribution is providing convergence guarantees and improved practice over [21].

Significance:
Theoretical significance is moderate: the inexact-oracle constrained extensions are of interest to optimization researchers and may transfer to other problems with expensive inner solves. Practical significance would be stronger with more comprehensive experiments: standard ranking metrics, larger datasets, and comparisons to common learning-to-rank baselines. As is, the empirical gains over [21] are promising but limited in scope.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a real gap: previous method lacked convergence guarantees due to inexact inner solves.
- Sound oracle-error modeling and convergence analyses for both gradient-free and gradient-based methods, with explicit complexity that reflects lower-level accuracy choices.
- Use of NN for faster stationary distribution computation, and a derivative approximation scheme with tunable accuracy.
- Empirical improvements over the prior Supervised PageRank training and evidence that NN outperforms power iteration.

Weaknesses:
- Gradient-free guarantee relies on convexity in Φ; nonconvexity is acknowledged but not addressed theoretically for that method.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small dataset, lack of standard IR metrics and broader baselines, no runtime/efficiency breakdowns.
- Some proofs, constants, and implementation details are relegated to the supplement, making it hard to assess practical parameter choices (e.g., β1 magnitude).
- Reference list is incomplete and poorly formatted; key related work in bandit convex optimization (e.g., Flaxman et al., 2005) appears missing or not properly cited.

Suggestions:
- Report NDCG/MAP on test sets, add strong LTR baselines (e.g., LambdaMART), and include wall-clock/runtime and iteration counts for lower/upper levels.
- Provide ablations showing the effect of δ, τ, and N on convergence and accuracy; compare power vs. NN across a range of graphs.
- Clarify the derivative-approximation algorithm and constants; summarize complexity with practical parameter ranges.
- Clean up references and add missing citations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a real methodological gap with principled algorithms and proofs.
- Integrates lower-level approximation control with upper-level convergence in a way that is likely reusable.
- Shows empirically better loss and improved inner solver.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical validation is too narrow to convince for a NIPS audience focused on learning-to-rank; lacks standard metrics and competitive baselines.
- The novelty over existing inexact-oracle frameworks is incremental.
- Gradient-free method’s guarantees hinge on an unverified convexity assumption.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are solid and relevant, but the empirical evidence and clarity fall short for NIPS. Strengthening experiments and clarifying the practical impact would significantly improve the paper. Reviewer confidence: moderate.
