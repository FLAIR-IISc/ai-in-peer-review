### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model. This model extends the classic PageRank by incorporating node and edge features into the restart and transition probabilities of the underlying random walk, making it more expressive for ranking tasks. The authors identify a key limitation in the state-of-the-art learning algorithm for this model [21]: its reliance on the power method to estimate the stationary distribution and its derivatives provides an "inexact oracle" without controllable accuracy, and consequently, the overall optimization procedure lacks convergence guarantees.

The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms that overcome this limitation by providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees.
1.  A **gradient-based method** is developed for general constrained non-convex problems. On the lower level, it uses the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] and a novel generalization thereof to compute the stationary distribution and its derivatives up to a specified accuracy δ. On the upper level, it employs an adaptive projected gradient algorithm that is robust to this inexactness.
2.  A **random gradient-free method** is also proposed, adapting the framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained problems and inexact function evaluations. The lower level again uses a provably accurate method to estimate the loss function value, serving as an inexact zero-order oracle.

For both methods, the authors provide a rigorous analysis of the total computational complexity required to reach a desired accuracy, explicitly considering the trade-off between the precision of the lower-level oracle and the number of upper-level iterations. Experiments on a real-world web page ranking task show that the proposed methods, particularly the gradient-free one, outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of the final loss function value.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The primary strength is its theoretical rigor. By formalizing the lower-level computation as an "inexact oracle" with controllable accuracy, the authors are able to provide convergence guarantees for a problem where none previously existed. The derivation of complexity bounds for the entire two-level procedure is a significant theoretical contribution.

There are, however, a few points that could be strengthened:
-   The analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) assumes that the objective function is convex in the optimization domain Φ. The paper acknowledges that the function is generally non-convex and justifies this by assuming Φ is a small vicinity of a local minimum where convexity holds. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice, and its implications for the algorithm's performance and guarantees should be discussed more explicitly.
-   The experimental evaluation focuses exclusively on the value of the loss function. While this is the direct objective of the optimization, it would be more compelling to also report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG). This would demonstrate that the superior performance in loss reduction translates into a tangible improvement in ranking quality, which is the ultimate goal of the model.

### Clarity and Organization

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation is established upfront, and the structure logically progresses from the model description to the methods for oracle computation, the two optimization frameworks, and finally the experimental results. The separation of the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to the Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2) aids in understanding the broader applicability of the proposed methods.

The presentation is quite dense, which is expected given the technical nature of the topic. A reader not deeply versed in convex optimization and inexact oracle models might find it challenging. The choice to relegate proofs to supplementary material is standard, but it makes a full assessment of the claims from the main text difficult. The experimental section is clear, though a more detailed justification for the hyperparameter choices (e.g., the Lipschitz constant L) would be beneficial.

### Originality and Novelty

The originality of this work lies not in the invention of fundamentally new components, but in the novel and rigorous combination and extension of existing ones to solve an important problem. The Supervised PageRank model [21] and the core optimization ideas [7, 16, 17] are from prior work. However, the paper's contribution is highly original in the following aspects:
-   It is the first to provide a theoretically sound optimization framework with convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank.
-   The adaptation of a random gradient-free method to the constrained setting with a controllable inexact oracle is a novel extension.
-   Similarly, the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex composite problems that explicitly handles an inexact first-order oracle with unknown Lipschitz constant is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature.
-   The use of the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] as the foundation for a provably accurate oracle for both function values and derivatives in this context is a clever and effective choice.

### Significance and Impact

The paper's contributions are significant for both researchers and practitioners. For researchers in optimization and machine learning, the paper provides two general frameworks for optimization with inexact oracles that could be applied to other large-scale problems where exact computations are intractable (e.g., models involving large matrix inversions or intractable integrals).

For practitioners, particularly in areas like web search and recommendation systems, this work provides more reliable and theoretically-backed algorithms for tuning feature-based graph ranking models. The experimental results, demonstrating superior performance over the previous method on a real-world commercial search engine dataset, underscore the practical relevance of the work. By providing algorithms that are guaranteed to converge, this research enhances the reliability of the learning process for a powerful class of ranking models.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper successfully provides the first convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a critical gap in the existing literature.
2.  **Generalizable Methods:** The proposed optimization frameworks for inexact oracles are of broad interest and applicable beyond the specific PageRank problem.
3.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The complexity analysis, which connects the accuracy of the low-level oracle to the overall convergence, is thorough and insightful.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The methods are shown to outperform the state-of-the-art on a large-scale, real-world task.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Questionable Convexity Assumption:** The analysis of the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption that may be too strong for a generally non-convex problem.
2.  **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:** The lack of standard ranking metrics in the evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the improved loss function values.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significant theoretical contributions and the successful application to an important problem, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a rigorous and valuable advancement in the field.
