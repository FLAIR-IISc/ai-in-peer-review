- Paper studies learning parameters of Supervised PageRank where node/edge transition/restart probs are linear in features and loss uses averaged squared pairwise hinge losses over query-dependent graphs.  
- Main challenge: loss and gradient require stationary distributions and their parameter derivatives, which must be approximated.  
- Contribution: two two-level optimization schemes with inexact oracles — a gradient-free method (GFN) adapted to inexact zero-order oracles with expected suboptimality bounds.  
- Contribution: an adaptive gradient-based method (GBN) for constrained nonconvex composite problems with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, converging to a stationary-point measure.  
- Lower-level solver: uses Nesterov–Nemirovski linear-convergence PageRank method and extends it to compute parameter derivatives with explicit accuracy–cost trade-offs (Lemmas 1–2).  
- Provides end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) that quantify how lower-level accuracy impacts total cost.  
- Empirical evaluation on a proprietary Yandex browsing graph (~600 queries, ~11.7K pages, ~1.7K labels) comparing GFN and GBN to a prior GBP method.  
- Results claim lower test loss and faster convergence of the lower-level stationary distribution than the classical power method.  
- Strength: clear formalization of inexact zero- and first-order oracles with careful derivation of accuracy–cost bounds for stationary distributions and gradients.  
- Strength: theoretical guarantees — expected suboptimality under convexity for GFN and stationarity convergence for GBN, with upper-level complexities accounting for lower-level iterations.  
- Strength: practical use of a linearly convergent PageRank solver extended to derivative computation, with concrete error bounds.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation — single proprietary dataset, no standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP), and few baselines (no LambdaMART or supervised random walks).  
- Weakness: practical efficiency not fully quantified — no wall-clock runtimes, memory usage, and complexities have large constants tied to graph parameters, limiting scalability assessment.  
- Weakness: some theoretical assumptions are restrictive (e.g., local convexity for gradient-free) and constants (e.g., β1) are buried in the supplement.  
- Weakness: clarity issues — typos, notation inconsistencies, odd stopping-criterion description, and incomplete/inconsistent references.  
- Quality: theoretical developments are sound and assumptions are stated; inexact-oracle constructions are well motivated, but experimental methodology is limited.  
- Originality: extends gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods to constrained inexact-oracle settings for Supervised PageRank and novel lower-level derivative computation.  
- Significance: useful theoretical framework for inexact oracles with promising empirical signs, but broader impact needs more datasets, metrics, and baselines.  
- Suggestions: report ranking metrics and wall-clock times, compare to stronger baselines (LambdaMART, supervised random walks), ablate trade-offs, clarify stopping rules, expose constants/pseudo-code, and discuss memory/implementation at scale.  
- Acceptance arguments: principled inexact-oracle treatment with convergence guarantees, end-to-end complexity accounting, and empirical improvements over prior optimization approach.  
- Rejection arguments: narrow empirical validation, missing standard metrics and baselines, restrictive assumptions, unclear practical speedups, and presentation issues.
