Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning parameters of Supervised PageRank models where restart and transition probabilities are linear in node and edge features. The training objective is a nonconvex pairwise squared hinge loss over query-dependent graphs. The authors propose two two-level algorithms: a gradient-free method and an adaptive projected gradient method, both operating with inexact oracles. On the lower level, they approximate the stationary distributions and their derivatives using a linearly convergent iterative method due to Nesterov–Nemirovski (rather than the standard power method), and on the upper level they optimize parameters with convergence guarantees despite inexact function/gradient evaluations. They provide complexity bounds, analyze the trade-off between inner accuracy and outer convergence, and report experiments on a Yandex browsing-graph ranking task. Relative to Supervised PageRank and related “supervised random walks” (e.g., Backstrom & Leskovec, WSDM’11) and prior use of the power method for derivatives [Andrew 1974/75], the new contributions are: (i) using a faster, linearly convergent lower-level routine with explicit error control; (ii) a constrained, gradient-free algorithm on the sphere with inexact function values; (iii) an adaptive projected gradient method for composite, possibly nonconvex problems with inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz parameter. The optimization side builds on and extends Nesterov–Spokoiny (zeroth-order), Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov (inexact first-order oracle), and Ghadimi–Lan (nonconvex first-order).

Quality:
- Theory: The lemmas precisely quantify how many inner iterations suffice to approximate f(ϕ) and ∇f(ϕ) within prescribed tolerances via the Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration and its derivative counterpart, with explicit dependence on α, r, s, m, and a norm bound β1. The gradient-free method analyzes a biased two-point oracle on the unit sphere with uniformly bounded function error, yielding an expected suboptimality bound with the standard τ2, δ/τ, and δ2/τ2 trade-offs. The adaptive gradient method ensures convergence to a stationary point (via gradient mapping magnitude) under an inexact oracle and backtracking on an unknown L. The two-level complexities are derived by composing inner accuracies with outer rates. These appear technically sound, though many proofs are deferred to the supplement and some constants (e.g., β1) are opaque in the main text.
- Empirics: The experimental setup uses real Yandex browsing graphs for 600 queries and compares to the state-of-the-art two-level gradient method that relies on power iterations for both the stationary vector and its derivatives. The proposed methods achieve lower training loss across subsets, and convergence of the lower-level method is empirically faster than the power method. However, the evaluation focuses almost exclusively on the training loss f and paired t-tests; there is no reporting of standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) on held-out data, nor wall-clock time or memory comparisons, which weakens the practical validation. Hyperparameter choices (α, R, ε, L) are briefly discussed, but sensitivity and runtime scaling are limited.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable but would benefit from editing. There are multiple typos and grammatical issues, occasional inconsistencies in notation (switches between ℓ1 and ℓ2 norms), and cross-references to supplementary algorithms rather than self-contained descriptions. Definitions of constants (e.g., β1) and their practical magnitudes are deferred. The high-level two-level design is clear, but the presentation could better separate the generic optimization results from the PageRank-specific instantiation.

Originality:
- The constrained, gradient-free method with inexact zero-order oracle over the unit sphere and explicit bounds is a meaningful extension of Nesterov–Spokoiny and bandit convex optimization results to a setting with unknown, uniformly bounded function error and constraints.
- The adaptive projected gradient method extends inexact oracle analysis to nonconvex composite problems with backtracking and guarantees stated via a normed gradient mapping; this is close in spirit to Ghadimi–Lan but adapted to inexact oracles and constraints.
- Applying the Nesterov–Nemirovski iteration to Supervised PageRank learning and deriving tight inner-outer error trade-offs is a useful methodological contribution. Overall, the optimization advances are incremental but nontrivial, and the combination yields a principled alternative to prior heuristics in this domain.

Significance:
On the optimization side, the results may interest researchers working with inexact oracles and large-scale Markov-chain-based models. For ranking, the study is more narrowly scoped: comparisons are only against the PageRank-specific baseline from [21], not against mainstream learning-to-rank methods (e.g., RankSVM, LambdaMART, supervised random walks with alternative training, or graph-based semi-supervised ranking). The dataset is modest and evaluation lacks standard IR metrics and runtime measurements, limiting the practical impact claims (e.g., “increase the reliability and speed of a search engine”).

Pros:
- Provides convergence-rate guarantees for both gradient-free and gradient-based two-level procedures in a setting where previous methods lacked such guarantees.
- Sound analysis of inner approximation error and its coupling to outer convergence; explicit complexity bounds.
- Practical lower-level improvement by using a linearly convergent PageRank solver with guaranteed accuracy control.
- Empirical improvements over the prior Supervised PageRank learning approach on real data.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation is limited: no NDCG/MAP, no ablation on α or R, no wall-clock/runtime or memory comparison, and no comparison to standard learning-to-rank baselines.
- Some theoretical assumptions are restrictive or under-emphasized (e.g., local convexity needed to apply the gradient-free guarantee; unknown constants like β1).
- Clarity and presentation issues (typos, notation switches, reliance on supplement for key constants/proofs).
- The gradient-free method’s practicality vs. gradient-based (cost per iteration, dependence on m) is not quantified experimentally.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) on held-out queries and include statistical significance tests; connect the pairwise loss reductions to improvements in these metrics.
- Provide wall-clock times and memory consumption for all methods, including breakdown of inner- vs. outer-level costs and sensitivity to α and graph size.
- Compare against additional baselines (e.g., RankSVM/LambdaMART on the same features; supervised random walks trained via alternative schemes).
- Clarify and, if possible, bound constants like β1 in the main text; unify norm choices and explain when ℓ1 vs. ℓ2 is used.
- Discuss robustness to the choice of R and the positivity constraint on ϕ; motivate α selection.
- Cite additional relevant derivative-free literature with noisy function values (e.g., Spall’s SPSA, bandit convex optimization) and clarify how your bounds differ.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. The optimization contributions are solid and relevant to NIPS, and the two-level guarantees for Supervised PageRank are novel and technically interesting. However, the empirical section needs stronger, standard ranking evaluation and runtime comparisons to solidify significance. Strengthening the experiments and polishing the presentation would make a compelling case.
