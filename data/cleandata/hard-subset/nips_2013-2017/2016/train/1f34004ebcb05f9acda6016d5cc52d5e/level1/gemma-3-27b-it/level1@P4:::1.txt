This paper proposes gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for learning Supervised PageRank models, aiming to address the lack of convergence guarantees in existing approaches. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods, achieved through the use of an "inexact oracle" concept and careful analysis of the lower-level approximation of the stationary distribution.  The authors also present experimental results demonstrating improved ranking quality compared to a state-of-the-art gradient-based method.

The work builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features.  Existing optimization techniques for this problem rely on estimating the stationary distribution of a Markov random walk, often using power methods. The authors highlight that the imprecision inherent in these methods prevents proving convergence.  Their approach utilizes a linearly convergent method [17] to approximate the stationary distribution, enabling convergence analysis for both gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms. Related work in optimization, such as [16, 7], is acknowledged as being unsuitable due to constraints and the inability to calculate the exact loss function. The paper references several other relevant works concerning PageRank variations [8, 9, 11, 18, 19], incorporating features into PageRank [2, 3, 4, 6, 10, 12, 20, 22], and optimization methods [23, 24, 25].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, which is a significant improvement over existing approaches.
* **Inexact Oracle Framework:**  The use of an inexact oracle is well-motivated and allows for a practical analysis of the algorithms given the inherent imprecision of the lower-level computations.
* **Detailed Analysis:**  The paper includes a detailed analysis of the trade-off between accuracy of the lower-level algorithm and the overall computational complexity.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world ranking task demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods. The comparison with the state-of-the-art method [21] supports the claim of improved ranking quality.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough overview of related work in PageRank, optimization, and random walks.
* **Multiple Algorithms:** Providing both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches expands the applicability of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and requires significant effort to follow. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while rigorous, contribute to the complexity. While thorough, the constant references to supplementary materials disrupts the flow of the main paper.
* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on specific assumptions, such as the convexity of f(ϕ) on Φ, which may not always hold in practice. This limitation is acknowledged, but its impact on real-world performance could be more thoroughly discussed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithms may be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., L, ε, R). The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to tune these parameters in practice.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental results are promising, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset and task. Extending the evaluation to other datasets and ranking scenarios would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Incremental Improvement:** While the theoretical guarantees and improved performance are valuable, the overall contribution is more of an incremental improvement over existing methods rather than a groundbreaking innovation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Supervised PageRank algorithms by providing theoretical convergence guarantees.
* The proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach in experimental evaluation.
* The rigorous analysis and detailed complexity analysis contribute to the understanding of the optimization landscape of Supervised PageRank.
* The work is well-motivated and builds upon a strong foundation of related research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper's complexity may limit its accessibility to a wider audience.
* The reliance on specific assumptions and limited scope of experiments raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.
* The contribution, while valuable, is more incremental than transformative.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field of Supervised PageRank optimization. The theoretical guarantees and experimental results demonstrate the value of the proposed methods. While the paper's complexity and limited scope are drawbacks, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and the generalizability of the results. Specifically, the authors should consider streamlining the presentation, providing more guidance on hyperparameter tuning, and expanding the experimental evaluation.
