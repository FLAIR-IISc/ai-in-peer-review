- Proposes gradient-based and gradient-free methods for learning Supervised PageRank (a non-convex problem) with theoretical convergence guarantees missing in prior work.  
- Uses a linearly convergent method to approximate the stationary distribution and embeds this in an inexact oracle framework.  
- Provides convergence rate guarantees for both proposed algorithms and analyzes the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity.  
- Delivers a comprehensive theoretical analysis including convergence estimates and complexity bounds.  
- Empirical results on a real-world web ranking task show improved ranking quality over the prior state-of-the-art.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, though technically dense.  
- Theoretical results are complex and may be hard to digest for readers unfamiliar with the optimization techniques.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset/task, reducing evidence of generality.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (step size, accuracy parameters) is not thoroughly studied.  
- No comparison to more recent optimization methods (e.g., adaptive optimizers like Adam).  
- Limited discussion of practical scalability and implementation challenges for very large graphs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: provides theoretical guarantees, practical improved algorithms, and advances understanding of inexact optimization methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: complexity hampers accessibility, experiments are narrow, and practical/parameter issues are underexplored.  
- Overall assessment: strong, valuable contribution and likely worthy of acceptance; reviewer confidence 8/10.
