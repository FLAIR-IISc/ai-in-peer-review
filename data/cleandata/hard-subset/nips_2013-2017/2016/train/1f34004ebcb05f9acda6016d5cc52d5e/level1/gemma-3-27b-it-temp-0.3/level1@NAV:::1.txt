## Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a ranking framework extending traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The core contribution lies in proposing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods to solve the resulting non-convex loss minimization problem, and importantly, providing theoretical convergence guarantees for both – a feature lacking in prior work [21]. The authors leverage the concept of an inexact oracle and demonstrate improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art [21] on a web page ranking task.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established foundation of PageRank and its supervised extensions [2, 12, 21]. The authors correctly identify the lack of convergence guarantees for existing gradient-based methods and address this limitation. The proposed methods, based on inexact oracles, are a reasonable approach to tackle the computational challenges of this problem. The detailed analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and complexity is a valuable addition. The experimental evaluation on a real-world dataset provides evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is a significant strength, the presentation is dense and can be difficult to follow. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while rigorous, could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implications for parameter tuning is not always clear. Furthermore, the experimental section, while demonstrating improvement, lacks a thorough ablation study. It would be beneficial to analyze the sensitivity of the algorithms to different hyperparameters and dataset characteristics. The comparison to other ranking algorithms beyond the state-of-the-art [21] would also strengthen the results. The writing style, while technically sound, could be improved for clarity and conciseness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** Provides convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods, addressing a key limitation of previous work.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addresses a relevant problem in ranking with potential applications in web search and other domains.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art on a real-world dataset.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Presentation:** Dense and difficult to follow, hindering accessibility.
*   **Experimental Analysis:** Lacks a comprehensive ablation study and comparison to a wider range of ranking algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The connection between theoretical results and practical implications could be clearer.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in presentation and experimental analysis, the theoretical contributions of this paper are significant. The provision of convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods is a valuable addition to the field. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation. Specifically, the authors should focus on simplifying the presentation of the theoretical results and conducting a more thorough ablation study to better understand the behavior of the proposed algorithms.
