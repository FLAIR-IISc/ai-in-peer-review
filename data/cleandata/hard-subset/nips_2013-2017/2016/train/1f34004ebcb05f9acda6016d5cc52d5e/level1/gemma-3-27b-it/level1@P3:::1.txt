This paper tackles the problem of learning Supervised PageRank, a crucial task in ranking applications. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for this non-convex problem, offering theoretical convergence guarantees—a significant contribution given the lack of such guarantees for existing approaches like [21]. The work is well-motivated, acknowledging the limitations of standard PageRank algorithms and the need to incorporate node and edge features. The experimental validation on a real-world web ranking dataset provides further support for the proposed methods.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea is to learn the parameters defining the transition and restart probabilities in a PageRank model, leveraging node and edge features. Previous work, particularly [21], formulates this as a non-convex optimization problem. However, [21]'s gradient-based method lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors address this gap by proposing two two-level algorithms. The lower level estimates the stationary distribution (and its derivatives) of the Markov random walk using an iterative method from [17], which is shown to be more efficient than methods cited in [5]. The upper level employs either a gradient descent (GBN) or a gradient-free (GFN) method, utilizing the inexact oracle information from the lower level. This builds upon existing optimization techniques like those in [6, 7, 16, 25], adapting them to the constrained non-convex setting and the inexactness inherent in the lower-level estimation.  The paper differentiates itself by providing convergence rate guarantees for *both* methods, a substantial improvement over prior work.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The most significant strength is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods. This is a critical advancement, particularly for the gradient-free approach where such guarantees are less common.
* **Inexact Oracle Handling:**  The explicit consideration and theoretical treatment of the inexact oracle from the lower-level estimation is well-handled.  This is a realistic scenario in practice and the analysis is thorough.
* **Comprehensive Optimization Framework:** The authors propose a well-defined framework, building upon existing optimization techniques and adapting them to the specific characteristics of the Supervised PageRank problem. The exploration of both gradient-based and gradient-free approaches offers valuable insights.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on a real-world dataset demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art approach [21], bolstering the practical relevance of the work. The comparison with untuned Supervised PageRank provides a useful baseline.
* **Detailed Analysis & Complexity Bounds:** The paper provides thorough analysis and estimates the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms. This is important for understanding their scalability and applicability to large-scale datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methods and theoretical results.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Dataset Size & Diversity:** While the experiments demonstrate improvement, the dataset size (≈ 11.7K vertices and ≈ 7.5K edges per query) appears relatively small.  It would be beneficial to evaluate the methods on larger and more diverse graph datasets to assess their scalability and robustness.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions sensitivity of the gradient-based method to the choice of the Lipschitz constant L, but a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be valuable. The justification for choosing L=10^-4 seems somewhat arbitrary.
* **Comparison with Alternatives:** The comparison is primarily limited to [21]. It would be helpful to compare the proposed methods with other relevant ranking algorithms, such as Learning to Rank (LTR) approaches (e.g., RankBoost, LambdaMART) which might offer different trade-offs.
* **Assumption of Convexity:** While the paper acknowledges the non-convexity of the overall problem, it relies on the assumption of local convexity within a small neighborhood of the optimal solution for applying certain theoretical results. The validity of this assumption and its potential impact on the performance should be discussed further.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** An ablation study to isolate the contribution of different components (e.g., the choice of the lower-level iterative method, the specific gradient-free/gradient-based update rule) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Supplementary Materials Reliance:** Many detailed explanations and proofs are relegated to the supplementary materials. While understandable, this makes it harder for the reader to quickly grasp the core concepts and results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a well-motivated approach.
* The theoretical contributions (convergence guarantees for both methods) are substantial and novel.
* The experimental results demonstrate practical improvement over the state-of-the-art.
* The work is technically sound and well-written.
* The handling of the inexact oracle is a key strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The relatively small dataset size raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
* The assumption of local convexity requires further justification.
*  Comparison with a broader range of ranking algorithms would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of supervised ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and experimental validation are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding dataset size and comparison with alternative approaches, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section (e.g., a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and a brief mention of comparisons with LTR methods in the related work section).  The authors should also consider integrating key results from the supplementary material into the main body of the paper for improved clarity.
