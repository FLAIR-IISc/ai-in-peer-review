### Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a powerful graph-based ranking framework where transition and restart probabilities are functions of node and edge features. The problem is framed as a non-convex loss minimization task. The authors identify a key weakness in the state-of-the-art training method [21], which relies on a two-level gradient descent approach: its lower level uses the power method to estimate the stationary distribution and its derivatives, yielding imprecise values that prevent any theoretical guarantee of convergence for the overall algorithm.

The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization methods—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. To achieve this, the authors replace the power method at the lower level with a more sophisticated, linearly convergent method [17]. This allows them to construct an "inexact oracle" that can approximate the loss function and its gradient to any specified accuracy. At the upper level, they develop optimization frameworks capable of handling this inexactness. The gradient-free method extends the work of [16] to constrained problems, while the gradient-based method generalizes the framework of [7] to constrained non-convex problems with an adaptive step-size rule. The authors provide a full theoretical analysis of the convergence and complexity for both proposed algorithms. Finally, they conduct experiments on a large-scale, real-world web ranking dataset, demonstrating that their methods, particularly the gradient-free approach, empirically outperform the previous state-of-the-art method.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound and well-supported by detailed analysis (with full proofs in the supplementary material). The authors rigorously formalize the problem of optimization with an inexact oracle, deriving convergence rates and overall complexity bounds for both the gradient-based and gradient-free settings. This is a complete piece of work that connects theoretical optimization with a practical machine learning application.

Claims are well-supported. The central claim—that this work provides the first convergence guarantees for training Supervised PageRank—is substantiated by the theoretical analysis. The empirical claim of superior performance is validated through experiments on a relevant, large-scale dataset. The authors are also transparent about the trade-offs between their two proposed methods, noting that the gradient-based method has fewer parameters while the gradient-free one avoids the potentially expensive computation of matrix derivatives.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, though the material is dense. The structure logically progresses from the model description to the challenges of its optimization, then presents the general theoretical frameworks for the proposed methods (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) before applying them to the specific Supervised PageRank problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2). This separation is effective, as it highlights the contributions to general optimization theory alongside the specific application. The notation is consistent, and the key algorithms are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand their mechanics.

One minor point for improvement would be to soften the claim in the introduction that the problem "can not be solved by existing optimization methods such as [16] and [7]". It would be more precise to state that these methods cannot be *directly applied* without the extensions for constraints and inexact oracles that this paper provides.

**Originality**

The paper's originality lies in the novel synthesis and extension of existing techniques to solve a known but challenging problem. While the Supervised PageRank model itself is from prior work [21], and the optimization methods are extensions of established frameworks ([7], [16], [17]), this paper is the first to successfully combine them. The formulation of the problem in terms of an inexact oracle and the subsequent development of algorithms with provable guarantees for this setting is a significant and novel contribution. The paper clearly articulates how its approach differs from and improves upon previous work, particularly by overcoming the lack of convergence guarantees in [21]. The related work is adequately cited.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant for several reasons.
1.  **Practical Impact:** Supervised PageRank is a powerful and flexible model for ranking on graphs. By providing a reliable and theoretically grounded training procedure, this work makes the model more practical and robust for real-world applications, such as web search, as demonstrated by the experiments.
2.  **Theoretical Impact:** The paper provides a strong bridge between the theory of first-order and zeroth-order optimization and its application. The general frameworks for optimization with an inexact oracle (especially the adaptive gradient method for non-convex problems) are of independent interest and could be applied to other large-scale machine learning problems where exact function or gradient evaluations are intractable.
3.  **Advancement of the State of the Art:** The work demonstrably advances the state of the art for learning Supervised PageRank by providing algorithms that are not only theoretically sound but also empirically superior.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Pros:**
*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning the Supervised PageRank model, a significant open issue.
*   Presents a novel and elegant application of advanced optimization theory (inexact oracles, random gradient-free methods, adaptive gradient methods) to a practical machine learning problem.
*   The proposed algorithms are shown to empirically outperform the state-of-the-art method on a large, real-world dataset from a commercial search engine.
*   The paper is technically solid, well-written, and the contributions are clearly articulated.

**Cons:**
*   The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (GFN) relies on a local convexity assumption for a problem that is globally non-convex. While the gradient-based method (GBN) analysis correctly handles the non-convex case, this discrepancy limits the theoretical strength of the GFN guarantee. A discussion on the practical implications of this assumption would strengthen the paper.
*   The complexity bounds in Theorems 2 and 4 are quite involved. A brief discussion on their practical meaning, particularly the scaling with respect to the feature dimension `m`, would be beneficial for the reader.

**Conclusion**

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a substantial contribution by providing the first provably convergent algorithms for training Supervised PageRank, a problem of both theoretical and practical importance. The blend of rigorous optimization theory with strong empirical results on a real-world task makes this an excellent paper for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
