

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features to better capture graph properties. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (gradient-based and gradient-free) for solving the non-convex loss-minimization problem inherent in Supervised PageRank learning. Unlike the state-of-the-art method [21], which lacks convergence guarantees, their approach provides theoretically sound convergence rates through the use of inexact oracle information. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions in graph-based ranking [12, 21] and optimization theory [16, 7], but makes a significant contribution by providing convergence guarantees for this specific problem.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors properly formalize the problem as a constrained non-convex optimization problem with an inexact oracle, and provide well-justified convergence rate guarantees for both proposed methods (Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4). The complexity analysis (Theorems 2 and 4) is thorough and provides practical insights for implementation. The experimental section validates the theoretical claims with clear results on a real-world web page ranking task, showing consistent improvement over the state-of-the-art [21]. The paper acknowledges the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for convexity in a local neighborhood) and provides appropriate experimental validation.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical development to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. However, some of the more technical sections (particularly Sections 4.1 and 5.1) could benefit from more intuitive explanations to make the methods accessible to a broader audience. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented in a straightforward manner (Tables 1, Figure 1). The supplementary materials contain necessary proofs, which is appropriate for a NIPS submission.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by providing the first convergence rate guarantees for optimization methods applied to Supervised PageRank learning. While Supervised PageRank was introduced in [21], the previous method lacked theoretical guarantees, and this paper addresses that gap. The authors' adaptation of random gradient-free methods [16] and gradient-based methods [7] to the inexact oracle setting for this specific problem is novel. The paper properly situates its work within the NIPS literature, citing relevant prior contributions on graph-based ranking [12, 21] and optimization [16, 7], and clearly articulates how their work differs from these prior contributions.

## Significance
The problem of learning effective ranking algorithms is highly significant for search engines and recommendation systems. The authors demonstrate that their methods outperform the state-of-the-art on a real-world web page ranking task, which has practical implications for improving search engine quality. The theoretical guarantees make their methods more reliable than previous approaches, which is particularly important for industrial applications. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in two ways: it provides the first convergence guarantees for this problem and demonstrates clear empirical improvements. The authors also provide a useful framework for applying inexact oracle methods to similar problems.

## Strengths
- Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for optimization methods in Supervised PageRank learning
- Thorough complexity analysis with practical implementation insights
- Clear experimental validation showing consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art
- Properly addresses the inexact oracle setting that is critical for this problem
- Well-structured and technically sound presentation

## Weaknesses
- Some technical sections (e.g., Section 4.1) could be made more accessible to readers without deep optimization background
- The choice of R=0.99 (the radius of the feasible set) lacks sufficient justification
- The paper could have included more ablation studies to better understand the impact of different parameters
- The comparison to other general-purpose optimization methods (beyond [21]) could be more extensive

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
1. The theoretical contributions are substantial and address a clear gap in the literature (lack of convergence guarantees)
2. The experimental results provide strong evidence of practical superiority over the state-of-the-art
3. The paper makes a clear contribution to the optimization literature by applying inexact oracle methods to this specific problem
4. The complexity analysis provides valuable practical guidance for implementation
5. The work has clear practical significance for web search and ranking applications

**Con:**
1. Some technical sections are difficult to follow without deep optimization knowledge
2. The choice of some hyperparameters (like R=0.99) lacks sufficient justification
3. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the trade-offs between convergence speed and accuracy
4. The comparison to other optimization methods beyond [21] is limited

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to both the optimization and graph-based ranking literature. The theoretical guarantees fill an important gap in the Supervised PageRank literature, and the experimental results validate the practical utility of the approach. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with clear significance for real-world applications. While some sections could be made more accessible, the technical depth is appropriate for a NIPS audience.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way, provides unique theoretical insights, and has clear potential for practical impact in web search and ranking applications. The authors have made a valuable contribution that will be of interest to the NIPS community.
