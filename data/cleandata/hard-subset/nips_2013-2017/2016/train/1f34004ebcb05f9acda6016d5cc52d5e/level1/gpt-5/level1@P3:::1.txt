Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning a supervised variant of PageRank in which restart and transition probabilities are linear functions of node and edge features. The learning objective is a (generally nonconvex) pairwise square loss over labeled preference pairs per query. The computational challenge is twofold: (i) computing, for each query graph, the stationary distribution π(ϕ) of a feature-parameterized Markov chain and (ii) using only inexact function/gradient information derived from approximate π(ϕ) to optimize ϕ under constraints.

On the lower level, the authors advocate the Nesterov–Nemirovski geometric-averaging scheme to approximate π(ϕ) (Eq. 3.1), and extend it to compute sensitivities dπ/dϕ via a companion recursion (Eqs. 3.2–3.3). They provide error and operation-count bounds (Lemmas 1–2).

On the upper level, they develop two optimization frameworks with inexact oracles: (a) a random gradient-free method based on spherical smoothing and a two-point estimator that tolerates uniformly bounded function value errors, for constrained (Euclidean ball) domains; (b) an adaptive projected gradient method for possibly nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact first-order oracle satisfying a Devolder-type inequality (Eq. 5.1), with backtracking on the unknown Lipschitz constant and a stationarity guarantee (Theorem 3). They then combine lower- and upper-level analyses to give end-to-end complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4), including trade-offs between lower-level accuracy and total cost.

Empirically, on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (≈600 queries, ≈11.7K pages, ≈7.5K edges total, ≈1.7K labeled pairs), both proposed methods outperform the previous supervised PageRank learner [21], which used power iterations and approximate gradients without convergence guarantees.

The work connects to: PageRank/HITS and supervised random walks; inexact oracle optimization (Devolder et al.); gradient-free methods (Nesterov & Spokoiny; Agarwal et al.); and nonconvex first-order methods (Ghadimi & Lan). The combination of inexact-oracle optimization with fast PageRank/sensitivity computation in a two-level architecture appears novel.

Quality
Strengths:
- Lower-level analysis is careful: Lemma 1 gives an explicit N dependent on α and target error; Lemma 2 provides a tractable way to approximate ∇f using a derivative recursion and controls ∞-norm error. Using a linearly convergent PageRank scheme is well motivated.
- Upper-level algorithms are well grounded: the gradient-free estimator and projection handle constraints and deterministic error; the adaptive gradient method provides a clear stationarity measure (scaled gradient mapping) with an inexact oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant.
- End-to-end complexity bounds make the trade-offs explicit in graph parameters (|Q|, p, s, r, m, α).

Weaknesses:
- Gradient-free guarantees require convexity; Section 4.2 assumes local convexity around a minimizer without a practical mechanism to verify or enforce this. In the general nonconvex case, the provided guarantee does not apply.
- The first-order inexact oracle bound (Eq. 5.1) is induced by separate approximations to f and ∇f with different norms and tolerances; while standard, the dependence on β1 and other hidden constants (Supplementary) obscures practical step-size/accuracy selection and the true cost in large graphs.
- The derivative scheme (Eqs. 3.2–3.3) is plausible, but a discussion contrasting it with solving the linear system (I − (1−α)P⊤)π′ = RHS (e.g., using iterative solvers or automatic differentiation) is missing. Empirical comparisons of derivative-computation time/accuracy would strengthen the case.
- The empirical evaluation is limited: datasets are small; results are shown mainly in terms of the training loss f, not standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k, MAP) on held-out data; and baselines are narrow. There is no comparison to mainstream learning-to-rank methods (RankSVM, ListNet, LambdaMART) using the same features, nor to other personalized PageRank learners beyond [21]. Wall-clock times, iteration counts for the lower- and upper-level methods, and memory footprints are not reported.
- Some stopping/parameter choices seem ad hoc (e.g., GBP “raises to power 100,” stopping when loss change < −1e−5). Sensitivity analyses to α, τ, ε, δ, and the ball radius R are minimal.

Clarity
The paper is mathematically dense but largely precise. However:
- Several typographical and referencing issues remain (incomplete citations, minor typos).
- Important constants (β1) and their dependence on graph parameters should be made explicit in the main text.
- Section 4.2’s “local convexity” assumption needs to be clearly labeled as a limitation; readers may otherwise assume global guarantees.
- Practical guidance is thin: a recipe for choosing τ, ε, δ, N, N1, N2 as functions of problem size would improve reproducibility. Pseudocode for lower-level derivative computation would help.

Originality
- The extension of random gradient-free methods to constrained domains with uniformly bounded function-value error and the adaptive inexact-prox gradient method for nonconvex problems are reasonable incremental advances over known frameworks (Nesterov–Spokoiny; Devolder; Ghadimi–Lan). The two-level coupling and derivative recursion tailored to supervised PageRank provide a useful synthesis for this application.
- Providing convergence rates for both zero- and first-order methods under inexact oracles, with explicit lower-level complexity, is a solid contribution.

Significance
- From an optimization perspective, the frameworks are broadly applicable to problems with expensive inner solves and biased function/gradient estimates.
- For learning PageRank parameters, the guarantees are welcome; however, the current experimental scale and lack of standard IR metrics/baselines limit immediate impact on practice. Demonstrations on larger web graphs and stronger baselines would elevate significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear two-level methodology with provable convergence under realistic inexactness.
- Novel derivative approximation scheme and explicit complexity bounds.
- Empirical improvements over the prior supervised PageRank learner.

Cons for acceptance
- Gradient-free guarantees hinge on unverified local convexity; nonconvex global setting remains untreated.
- Limited and nonstandard experimental evaluation; missing comparisons to strong LTR baselines and standard metrics.
- Practical constants/parameter choices insufficiently elucidated; reproducibility concerns.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report NDCG/MAP on held-out queries; include RankSVM/LambdaMART and personalized PageRank baselines.
- Provide wall-clock time, number of inner/outer iterations, and sensitivity plots for τ, ε, δ; discuss α and R.
- Compare derivative computation strategies (recursion vs linear solves vs AD).
- Make β1 and related constants explicit in the main text; give a practical parameter-selection guide.
- Clarify the scope of guarantees (convex vs nonconvex), and whether the method can be made robust to nonconvexity (e.g., stationarity guarantees for the gradient-free method).

Overall evaluation
A thoughtful and technically sound paper with meaningful theoretical contributions to inexact-oracle optimization for supervised PageRank. The empirical section is not yet commensurate with the theory; with stronger experiments and clearer practical guidance, this would be a strong NIPS contribution. As is, it is borderline, leaning positive on technical merit.
