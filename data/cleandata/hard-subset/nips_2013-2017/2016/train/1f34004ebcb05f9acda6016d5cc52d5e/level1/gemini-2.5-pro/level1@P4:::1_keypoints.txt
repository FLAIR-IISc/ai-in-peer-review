- Addresses learning parameters for Supervised PageRank by incorporating node/edge features into restart and transition probabilities.  
- Identifies limitation of prior method [21]: power method yields an inexact oracle with uncontrolled accuracy and no convergence guarantees.  
- Proposes two two-level optimization algorithms that provide theoretical convergence rate guarantees.  
- Gradient-based method: uses Nesterov-Nemirovski (and its generalization) to compute stationary distribution and derivatives to accuracy δ and an adaptive projected gradient to handle inexactness.  
- Random gradient-free method: adapts Nesterov’s zero-order framework to constrained problems with inexact function evaluations, using a provably accurate lower-level oracle for loss estimates.  
- Provides rigorous total computational complexity analysis linking lower-level oracle precision to upper-level iteration counts.  
- Experiments on a real-world web ranking task show the proposed methods, especially the gradient-free one, reduce loss more than prior state-of-the-art.  
- Technically sound with strong theoretical rigor; formalizes the inexact oracle and derives complexity bounds for the whole two-level procedure.  
- Concern: gradient-free analysis assumes local convexity of the objective in Φ, a strong assumption that may not hold and needs more discussion.  
- Concern: experimental evaluation reports only loss values and omits standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG) to demonstrate practical ranking improvements.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, but presentation is dense and deferred proofs make full assessment harder.  
- Hyperparameter choices (e.g., Lipschitz constant L) are insufficiently justified in experiments.  
- Originality comes from novel combination/extension of existing methods: first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank and constrained inexact zero-/first-order adaptations.  
- Significance: frameworks are broadly applicable to other large-scale inexact-oracle problems and practically relevant for search/recommendation model tuning.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, generalizable methods, rigorous analysis, and empirical validation on large-scale data.  
- Arguments for rejection: reliance on a questionable local convexity assumption and incomplete experimental evaluation on ranking quality metrics.
