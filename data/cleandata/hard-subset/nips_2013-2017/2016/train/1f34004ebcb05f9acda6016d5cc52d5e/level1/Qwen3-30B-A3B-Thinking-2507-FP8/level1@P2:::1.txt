

# Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors build upon the work of [21], which proposed a two-level gradient-based method for learning these models but lacked theoretical convergence guarantees. The paper's main contribution is the development of two novel two-level optimization methods with theoretical convergence rate guarantees: a gradient-based method and a gradient-free method. The authors establish convergence rates for both methods and demonstrate their superiority over the state-of-the-art on a real-world ranking task.

The paper's theoretical contributions are significant. The authors develop a general framework for constrained non-convex optimization with inexact oracles, extending previous work on gradient-based methods [7] and random gradient-free methods [16]. They prove convergence rate guarantees for both methods (Theorems 1, 3, 4) and provide complexity bounds (Theorems 2, 4) that account for the trade-off between lower-level accuracy and overall computational complexity. This is a substantial theoretical advance over [21], which lacked such guarantees. The paper also carefully distinguishes its contributions from related work, such as [5], [6], and [10], by highlighting the specific improvements in convergence guarantees and algorithmic design.

The experimental section demonstrates the practical value of the proposed methods. The authors apply their algorithms to a web page ranking task using data from Yandex, comparing their gradient-based (GBN) and gradient-free (GFN) methods with the state-of-the-art gradient method (GBP) [21]. The results (Table 1, Figure 1) show that GFN significantly outperforms GBP on all test sets, while GBN outperforms GBP on the largest test set (Q12). The p-values of paired t-tests for these differences are all less than 0.005, providing strong statistical evidence of the improvements. The authors also conduct a thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity, showing that the methods are robust to reasonable choices of hyperparameters.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through its careful theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors are appropriately cautious about the limitations of their approach, noting that the gradient-free method avoids calculating derivatives for large matrices (a significant practical advantage) but requires more iterations than the gradient-based method. The paper is technically sound, with claims well-supported by both theoretical analysis (in the main paper and supplementary materials) and experimental results.

The clarity of the paper is generally good. The problem is well-defined, the mathematical notation is consistent, and the organization is logical. The paper effectively connects the proposed methods to previous work in optimization and ranking, making it accessible to NIPS readers. However, some readers may find the proofs in the supplementary materials challenging to follow without additional explanation. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the inexact oracle approach specifically addresses the challenges of the Supervised PageRank problem.

The originality of the work is strong. While Supervised PageRank itself [21] is not entirely novel, the theoretical framework for optimization with inexact oracles and the specific application to Supervised PageRank represent a clear advance. The paper distinguishes itself from previous work on PageRank optimization (e.g., [18], [23], [24]) by providing convergence guarantees and addressing the specific challenges of the non-convex, constrained optimization problem.

The significance of the work is substantial. The proposed methods not only provide theoretical guarantees missing from prior work but also demonstrate clear practical advantages on a real-world ranking task. The ability to learn Supervised PageRank models with convergence guarantees has important implications for search engines and other ranking applications. The general framework for optimization with inexact oracles could potentially be applied to other machine learning problems beyond ranking.

Strengths:
- First convergence rate guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank
- Novel two-level optimization framework with theoretical guarantees
- Practical improvements demonstrated on a real-world ranking task
- Careful analysis of trade-offs between accuracy and computational complexity
- Clear distinction from related work

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on supplementary materials for complete proofs may reduce accessibility
- Experimental section could include more comprehensive parameter sensitivity analysis
- Limited discussion of practical runtime comparisons between methods

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank, which was missing from prior work. The proposed methods outperform the state-of-the-art in experiments, and the theoretical framework is general enough to be applicable to other optimization problems. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in machine learning.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper's reliance on supplementary materials for proofs may make it less accessible to some readers. While the experimental results are strong, a more comprehensive analysis of computational efficiency would further strengthen the practical contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field, advancing both the theory and practice of learning ranking models. The combination of theoretical guarantees and empirical validation demonstrates significant progress on a problem of practical importance. I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016.
