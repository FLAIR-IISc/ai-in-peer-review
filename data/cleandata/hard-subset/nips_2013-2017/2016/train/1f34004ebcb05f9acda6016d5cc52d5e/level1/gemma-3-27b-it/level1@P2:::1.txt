This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, proposing both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods. The core contribution lies in providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, a feature lacking in previous work (specifically, [21]). The authors also present experimental results demonstrating the superiority of their methods over the state-of-the-art.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing PageRank algorithms and the benefits of supervised approaches. The connection to prior work, particularly [21], is clearly established. The problem setting is standard and well-defined, and the proposed approach of leveraging inexact oracle information is a sensible one, given the computational challenges of calculating exact gradients and values in this context.  The extensive theoretical analysis, including convergence rate proofs for both methods, is a significant strength. The authors clearly state the assumptions required for their theorems and provide lemmas supporting their claims. The experimental setup is reasonably well-described, employing a real-world dataset and comparing against a relevant baseline.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the sheer length and density of the mathematical derivations make it difficult to follow the core ideas. While rigor is appreciated, streamlining the presentation and providing more intuition behind the mathematical steps would improve readability.  The notation is also quite heavy and requires significant effort from the reader.  Second, the experimental section feels somewhat limited. The results show that the proposed methods outperform the baseline, but the magnitude of the improvement is not always dramatic. More comprehensive experiments, including varying dataset sizes, different parameter settings, and comparisons with other relevant ranking algorithms, would strengthen the claims.  The ablation studies exploring the impact of different parameters (e.g., the accuracy parameter N in the lower-level methods) are insufficient; a more thorough investigation is needed. Third, while the authors mention the non-convexity of the problem, the convergence guarantees appear to be based on local convergence around a stationary point. A discussion of the sensitivity to initialization and potential issues with escaping local optima would be valuable. Finally, the connection between the theoretical convergence rates and the observed empirical performance isn't explicitly discussed.  It would be helpful to relate the theoretical bounds to the observed speed of convergence in the experiments.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the proofs seem well-constructed. However, the density of the mathematical presentation makes it difficult to fully assess the validity of all the claims without significant effort. The experiments provide support for the theoretical results but could be more comprehensive.

**Clarity:** The paper is relatively difficult to read. The notation is heavy, and the mathematical derivations are dense. The organization is logical, but the prose could be more concise and accessible. More intuitive explanations of the core ideas would greatly improve clarity.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel combination of existing optimization techniques (gradient and gradient-free methods) applied to the specific problem of Supervised PageRank with theoretical guarantees.  The use of inexact oracle information and the convergence analysis are significant contributions.  The authors have thoroughly referenced related work.

**Significance:** The results are potentially important, particularly for large-scale ranking problems where calculating exact gradients is computationally prohibitive.  The proposed methods offer a practical and theoretically grounded approach to learning Supervised PageRank models. The convergence guarantees are a valuable addition to the field.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for gradient-based and gradient-free methods for learning Supervised PageRank, addressing a gap in the existing literature.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art on a real-world dataset.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in ranking and information retrieval.
*   Presents a rigorous mathematical analysis of the proposed methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and difficult to read, with heavy notation and lengthy derivations.
*   The experimental section is somewhat limited and lacks comprehensive ablation studies.
*   The discussion of non-convexity and sensitivity to initialization is lacking.
*   The connection between theoretical convergence rates and empirical performance is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, but improvements in clarity and experimental validation are needed to fully realize its potential. Specifically, the authors should focus on streamlining the mathematical presentation, providing more intuitive explanations, and conducting more comprehensive experiments.  Addressing the points regarding non-convexity and the relationship between theory and practice would also strengthen the paper.
