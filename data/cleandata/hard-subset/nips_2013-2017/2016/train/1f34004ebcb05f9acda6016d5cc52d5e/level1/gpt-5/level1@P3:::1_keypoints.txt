- Problem: Learns supervised PageRank where restart and transition probs are linear in features; optimizes nonconvex pairwise squared loss over query-specific graphs.
- Computational challenge: must compute feature-dependent stationary distributions π(ϕ) and optimize parameters using inexact function/gradient info from approximate π(ϕ).
- Lower-level method: uses Nesterov–Nemirovski geometric-averaging scheme to approximate π(ϕ) (Eq.3.1).
- Sensitivity computation: extends the scheme with a companion recursion to compute dπ/dϕ (Eqs.3.2–3.3) and gives error/operation bounds (Lemmas 1–2).
- Upper-level methods: (a) random gradient-free spherical smoothing with two-point estimator for constrained (Euclidean ball) domains tolerating bounded function errors.
- Upper-level methods: (b) adaptive projected gradient for nonconvex composite objectives with an inexact Devolder-type first-order oracle and backtracking on unknown Lipschitz constant (Theorem 3).
- End-to-end theory: combines levels to provide complexity bounds and trade-offs between inner accuracy and total cost (Theorems 2 and 4).
- Empirics: on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset (~600 queries, ~11.7K pages) both methods outperform prior supervised PageRank [21] by training loss.
- Relation to prior work: connects supervised random walks/PageRank, inexact-oracle optimization, gradient-free methods, and nonconvex first-order methods; two-level coupling is novel.
- Strength: careful lower-level analysis with explicit iteration counts and ∞-norm control; motivates linear convergence PageRank scheme.
- Strength: well-grounded upper-level algorithms; gradient-free estimator handles constraints and deterministic errors; adaptive gradient gives stationarity guarantee.
- Strength: explicit end-to-end complexity makes graph-parameter trade-offs clear (|Q|, p, s, r, m, α).
- Weakness: gradient-free guarantees assume local convexity near a minimizer without a practical check, limiting applicability to general nonconvex problems.
- Weakness: first-order inexact oracle bound depends on hidden constants (β1, etc.), obscuring practical step-size/accuracy selection and real costs.
- Weakness: derivative recursion is plausible but lacks comparison to linear-system solves or automatic differentiation; no empirical timing/accuracy comparisons.
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation — small datasets, training-loss-focused results, missing standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP) and strong LTR baselines (RankSVM, LambdaMART).
- Weakness: missing wall-clock times, inner/outer iteration counts, memory usage; some stopping/parameter choices appear ad hoc.
- Clarity issue: typographical and referencing errors remain; important constants like β1 not explicit in main text.
- Clarity issue: local convexity assumption in Section 4.2 needs explicit labeling as a limitation to avoid misinterpretation.
- Clarity issue: practical guidance lacking — need recipes for selecting τ, ε, δ, N, N1, N2 and pseudocode for derivative computation.
- Originality: incremental extensions of known optimization methods, but novel synthesis for supervised PageRank and explicit lower-level complexity is useful.
- Significance: frameworks broadly applicable to problems with expensive inner solves; guarantees valuable for learning PageRank, but limited experiments reduce practical impact.
- Pros for acceptance: provable two-level methodology, novel derivative approximation, explicit complexity bounds, empirical improvement over prior method.
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on unverified local convexity, limited experiments and baselines, insufficient practical parameter guidance hurting reproducibility.
- Suggestions: report held-out NDCG/MAP and include RankSVM/LambdaMART baselines; provide wall-clock times, iteration/memory stats, sensitivity analyses, and compare derivative computation strategies.
- Overall evaluation: technically solid and thoughtful with meaningful theoretical contributions; empirical work needs strengthening — borderline, leaning positive.
