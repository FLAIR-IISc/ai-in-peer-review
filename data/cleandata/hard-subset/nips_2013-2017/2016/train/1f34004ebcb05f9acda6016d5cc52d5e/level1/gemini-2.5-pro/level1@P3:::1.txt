### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the parameters of the Supervised PageRank model, first introduced by Zhukovskii et al. [21]. The learning problem is formulated as a non-convex optimization problem where the objective function—a ranking loss—depends on the stationary distribution of a parameter-dependent Markov chain. A key difficulty is that this stationary distribution, and therefore the loss and its gradient, cannot be computed exactly. The authors frame this as optimization with an "inexact oracle."

The authors identify a major limitation in the existing state-of-the-art method [21], which uses a gradient descent approach with oracle values estimated via the power method: it lacks any theoretical convergence guarantees. The main contribution of this work is to propose two new two-level optimization algorithms for this problem that come with formal convergence rate guarantees.

On the lower level (the oracle), both proposed methods replace the standard power method with a more efficient, linearly convergent method by Nesterov and Nemirovski [17] for approximating the stationary distribution. The authors extend this method to also approximate the derivatives and provide rigorous bounds (Lemmas 1 and 2) on the computational cost required to achieve a desired oracle accuracy.

On the upper level, two distinct optimization algorithms are proposed:
1.  A random gradient-free method, which adapts the framework of Nesterov and Spokoiny [16] to handle the constrained optimization problem and the inexact zero-order oracle. A convergence rate is proven under a local convexity assumption.
2.  A gradient-based method for non-convex constrained optimization, which extends the work on inexact oracles [7, 25]. A key feature is an adaptive procedure for selecting the step size, removing the need to know the gradient's Lipschitz constant. A convergence guarantee to a stationary point is provided.

Finally, the paper presents experimental results on a real-world web page ranking task, demonstrating that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of the final loss value.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is that it provides the first theoretically-grounded algorithms with convergence guarantees for the Supervised PageRank learning problem. This is a significant step forward from the previous, more heuristic gradient descent approach [21] and places the problem on a firm optimization footing. The analysis rigorously connects the required precision of the low-level oracle to the convergence of the high-level optimization algorithm, resulting in overall complexity bounds.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper successfully applies and adapts modern, powerful optimization frameworks to a challenging, practical problem. The use of the Nesterov-Nemirovski method [17] for the oracle is well-justified. The development of the gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods for the specific setting of constrained optimization with a controllable inexact oracle is a solid technical contribution. The non-convex analysis for the gradient-based method is particularly relevant, given the nature of the loss function.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The separation of the general optimization frameworks (Sections 4.1 and 5.1) from their specific application to the learning problem (Sections 4.2 and 5.2) makes the paper's contributions clear and easy to follow for readers with a background in optimization.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, though conducted on a proprietary dataset, provide convincing evidence that the proposed algorithms are not only theoretically sound but also practically effective. Both GFN and GBN achieve a lower loss than the baseline GBP method, with GFN showing particularly strong performance. This suggests that the new methods are superior in finding better local optima.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Strong Convexity Assumption:** The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 1 and 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex on the feasible set Φ. The authors acknowledge the function is generally non-convex and state they assume Φ is a small vicinity of a local minimum where convexity holds. This is a very strong local assumption that may not hold in practice. While such assumptions are common in theoretical analyses, its practical implications should be discussed more thoroughly, especially since the method seems to work well empirically, suggesting it might be robust to violations of this assumption.

2.  **Reproducibility and Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are performed on a proprietary dataset from Yandex, which makes the results impossible to reproduce for the wider community. While the use of real-world industrial data is valuable, supplementing it with results on a public benchmark dataset (e.g., from LETOR) would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and verifiability. Furthermore, the comparison in Table 1 is based on the final loss value. A comparison of wall-clock time or total floating-point operations to reach a certain loss threshold would be much more informative about the practical efficiency of the methods, as their per-iteration costs differ.

3.  **Clarity on Technical Details:** The connection between the bounds derived for the inexact oracle (Lemmas 1 and 2) and the requirements of the general optimization frameworks could be elaborated. For example, in Section 5.2, the paper claims that the bounds on function and gradient error lead to the condition in Inequality 5.1. While this is a standard result from [25], a brief derivation or a more explicit statement of the resulting constants would improve the paper's self-containedness and clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant limitation (lack of convergence guarantees) in prior work on an important practical problem.
*   Provides a novel and rigorous application of modern optimization theory (inexact oracles, gradient-free methods) to machine learning.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between oracle accuracy and overall complexity.
*   The proposed methods are shown to be empirically superior to the state-of-the-art on a real-world task.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis for the gradient-free method relies on a strong local convexity assumption.
*   Experimental results are not reproducible due to the use of a proprietary dataset.
*   The experimental comparison is incomplete as it lacks an analysis of computational runtime.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between advanced optimization theory and the practical problem of learning to rank. The theoretical guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank are a genuine advance. While there are weaknesses, particularly concerning the convexity assumption and the reproducibility of experiments, the strengths outweigh them. The paper is well-executed, the results are important, and it is likely to be of interest to both optimization and machine learning researchers at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
