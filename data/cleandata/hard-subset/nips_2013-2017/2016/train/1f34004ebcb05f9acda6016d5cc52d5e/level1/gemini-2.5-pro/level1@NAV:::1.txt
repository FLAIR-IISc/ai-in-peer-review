This paper addresses the non-convex problem of learning parameters for Supervised PageRank models, which incorporate node and edge features into the ranking process. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in the state-of-the-art method by Zhukovskii et al. (2015), which, despite its empirical effectiveness, lacks theoretical convergence guarantees due to its use of an inexact gradient derived from the power method. The central contribution of this work is to provide two new two-level optimization algorithms—one gradient-based and one random gradient-free—that come with formal convergence rate guarantees. This is achieved by framing the learning problem within the established optimization framework of an inexact oracle, adapting general methods from Nesterov, Ghadimi, and Lan to this specific constrained, non-convex setting. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art on a real-world web page ranking task.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality & Significance:** The paper is technically strong and addresses a significant theoretical gap in a widely used class of models. Providing the first convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank is a valuable contribution that adds rigor to the field of graph-based learning. The claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-4) and empirical results on a commercial search engine's dataset, demonstrating a clear advance over prior work.
*   **Originality:** While the underlying optimization techniques are based on existing literature, their application and adaptation to solve this specific problem are novel and non-trivial. The paper successfully bridges the gap between general theory on optimization with inexact oracles and the specific application of learning ranking models on graphs.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the problem, the limitations of previous work, and its proposed solutions. The distinction between the lower-level oracle approximation and the upper-level parameter update is explained effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The paper is quite dense, and a full appreciation of the theoretical results requires familiarity with the cited optimization literature. The analysis for the gradient-free method assumes convexity in the neighborhood of a local minimum, which is a notable simplifying assumption for a generally non-convex problem.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution is the application of existing advanced optimization theory rather than the development of fundamentally new optimization algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  It solves an open theoretical problem by providing the first convergence proofs for learning Supervised PageRank.
2.  The proposed methods are shown to be empirically superior to the existing state-of-the-art.
3.  The work is a strong example of applying rigorous optimization theory to a practical machine learning problem.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The novelty is in the application and adaptation of known methods, not in inventing new ones.

Overall, the paper's strengths, particularly its significant theoretical contribution and strong empirical validation, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It represents a solid and important advance for graph-based ranking models.
