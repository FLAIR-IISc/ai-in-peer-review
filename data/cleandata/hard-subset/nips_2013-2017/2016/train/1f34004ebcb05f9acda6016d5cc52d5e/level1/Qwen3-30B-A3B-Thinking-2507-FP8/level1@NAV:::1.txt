

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank to incorporate node and edge features. The authors propose two-level optimization methods: a gradient-based approach and a gradient-free method, both providing theoretical convergence guarantees that were missing in the state-of-the-art [21]. Their work builds on the inexact oracle framework for optimization, extending it to the constrained non-convex problem of learning Supervised PageRank parameters. The key innovation lies in providing convergence rate guarantees for both methods, which previous work lacked due to the inexact nature of computing stationary distributions in the Markov random walk.

The paper excels in quality with rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rate proofs and complexity bounds for both algorithms. The experimental results on a real ranking task demonstrate clear empirical advantages over the state-of-the-art, with statistically significant improvements in loss function values. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though the mathematical notation could be slightly more accessible to readers outside optimization theory. The originality is strong, as it's the first to provide convergence guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank using these optimization approaches, differentiating from previous work [21] that lacked theoretical guarantees. The significance is moderate, as the improvements are demonstrated on a specific ranking task, though the framework could potentially generalize to other graph-based learning problems.

Strengths: Theoretical rigor with convergence guarantees, clear empirical validation, and well-motivated problem formulation. Weaknesses: Limited discussion of broader applicability beyond the specific ranking task and the dense mathematical notation that may hinder accessibility.

Pro acceptance: Strong theoretical contributions with convergence guarantees, clear empirical improvements, and well-executed experiments. Con acceptance: The significance could be more compellingly argued, and the paper would benefit from more discussion of practical implementation challenges. Overall, this is a solid contribution to optimization for graph-based learning problems with clear theoretical and empirical merits.
