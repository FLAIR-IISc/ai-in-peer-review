This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, a non-convex optimization problem. The authors propose both gradient-based and gradient-free methods to tackle this problem, focusing on providing theoretical convergence guarantees, which are missing in previous work [21]. They also present experimental results demonstrating improved ranking quality compared to the state-of-the-art.

The paper builds upon the Supervised PageRank framework introduced in [21], which extends the traditional PageRank algorithm by incorporating node and edge features. The core challenge lies in efficiently and accurately estimating the stationary distribution of the Markov random walk, which is crucial for computing the loss function and its gradients. Existing methods struggle with convergence guarantees due to the inexactness of these estimations. The authors address this by utilizing a linearly convergent method [17] for approximating the stationary distribution and then develop gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms leveraging this approximation within an inexact oracle framework. This is a sensible approach, as it directly tackles the core bottleneck of the problem. Related work in online convex optimization with bandit feedback [O, Agarwal, Dekel and L. Xiao, 2012] and stochastic gradient methods [Ghadimi, Lan, 2016] provide context for the optimization techniques employed.  The connections to power method iterations [Haveliwala, 2002; Andrew, 1996; Andrew, 1998] and previous supervised ranking methods [Backstrom & Leskovec, 2011; Dai et al., 2010; Liu et al., 2010; Zhukovskii et al., 2012, 2013, 2014] are well established.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength of this work is the provision of convergence rate guarantees for both the gradient-based and gradient-free methods, a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art [21]. This is crucial for understanding the reliability and performance of the algorithms.
*   **Inexact Oracle Framework:** The use of an inexact oracle is well-motivated and allows for practical implementation, as exact computation is often intractable. The analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity is valuable.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, including convergence rate estimates and complexity bounds.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to the existing state-of-the-art.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, although dense in technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Theoretical Results:**  While the theoretical analysis is a strength, it is also quite complex and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific optimization techniques used.  More intuitive explanations or summaries of the key results could be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset and task (web page ranking).  It would be valuable to see the performance of the algorithms on other types of graph ranking problems or datasets.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the algorithms to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., step size, accuracy parameters) is not thoroughly investigated. More detailed analysis of parameter tuning would strengthen the empirical results.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Modern Optimizers:** While compared to [21], the paper doesn’t compare to more recent, potentially relevant optimization methods. Newer adaptive optimizers like Adam might provide a useful baseline.
*   **Limited discussion of practical challenges:** The paper focuses heavily on the theoretical aspects, and doesn't sufficiently discuss practical considerations such as scalability for very large graphs or efficient implementation strategies.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical guarantees for a challenging non-convex optimization problem.
*   Offers practical algorithms with improved performance compared to the state-of-the-art.
*   Contributes to the theoretical understanding of inexact optimization methods.
*   The work is well-motivated and addresses a relevant problem in graph ranking.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is complex and may limit accessibility.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity and practical challenges.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graph ranking and optimization. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. While the complexity of the analysis and the limited scope of the experiments are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Confidence:** 8/10.  The work feels solid and the contributions are significant, but the complexity of the theory warrants a slightly lower confidence score.
