Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning parameters of Supervised PageRank, a random-walk-based ranking model where node and edge transition/restart probabilities are linear in features. The learning objective is a (generally nonconvex) average of squared pairwise hinge losses over queries, where query-dependent graphs drive the stationary distributions. The key challenge is that both the loss and its gradient require stationary distributions and their derivatives with respect to parameters, which must be approximated.

The paper contributes two two-level optimization schemes with inexact oracles:
- A gradient-free method: builds on random gradient-free optimization with smoothing and constrained projection, adapted to the case where only inexact function values are available with a known uniform error bound. The method uses a sphere sampling estimator and provides an expected suboptimality bound. This extends Nesterov-Spokoiny (2015) and Ghadimi-Lan (2013) to constrained problems with inexact zero-order oracles.
- A gradient-based method: proposes an adaptive projected gradient framework for possibly nonconvex composite problems with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant, providing convergence to a stationary point measure. This generalizes inexact-oracle analyses of Devolder et al. (2014) and proximal-gradient methods to constrained, nonconvex settings with backtracking.

At the lower level, the paper uses the Nesterov–Nemirovski linear-convergence method to approximate personalized PageRank (stationary distribution) and extends it to compute derivatives with respect to parameters. It derives explicit accuracy–cost trade-offs (Lemmas 1–2), enabling control of the upper-level oracle accuracy, and provides overall complexity bounds (Theorems 2 and 4) that quantify the impact of lower-level accuracy on total cost.

Experimentally, on a Yandex user browsing graph dataset (~600 queries, ~11.7K pages total, ~1.7K labels), the proposed gradient-free (GFN) and gradient-based (GBN) methods are compared to a prior gradient-based approach (GBP) from earlier Supervised PageRank work [21], which relied on power iterations and lacked convergence guarantees due to approximation errors. The proposed methods reportedly achieve lower loss on test queries and demonstrate faster convergence of the lower-level stationary distribution than the classical power method.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of inexact zero- and first-order oracles for this application and careful derivation of accuracy–cost bounds for stationary distribution and gradient approximations.
- Theoretical guarantees: expected suboptimality for the gradient-free method under convexity on a constrained set; convergence to a stationary point measure for the adaptive gradient method under nonconvexity with inexact first-order oracle. Upper-level complexities explicitly account for lower-level iteration counts.
- Use of a linearly convergent PageRank solver at the lower level and an extension to derivative computation, with concrete error bounds (Lemmas 1–2).
- Empirical results indicate improved objective values versus the prior Supervised PageRank optimization method and practical benefits of the lower-level solver.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation: one proprietary dataset, no standard ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG/MAP), and no comparison to strong learning-to-rank baselines (e.g., LambdaMART) or to supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec, 2011) on ranking tasks. The main comparison is to a single prior optimization scheme (GBP).
- Practical efficiency not fully quantified: no wall-clock runtimes or memory usage; complexities have large constants and depend on graph parameters (|Q|, p, s, r, m), making practical scalability hard to assess.
- Assumptions for guarantees are restrictive in places. The gradient-free method requires convexity on the feasible ball Φ around a local minimum; no procedure is given to verify convexity in practice. The first-order inexact-oracle inequality relies on constants (e.g., β1) defined in the supplement; it would help to expose them in the main text.
- Some clarity issues: typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., Rm++1; scale-invariance discussion; stopping criterion for GBP described as “less than −10−5” seems odd). Reference list is incomplete/inconsistent.

Quality:
- The theoretical developments are largely sound, with appropriate assumptions stated. The inexact-oracle constructions (Inequality 5.1) are well motivated by Lemmas 1–2. The adaptive gradient method’s backtracking and stationarity criterion are standard and correctly adapted. The convexity requirement for the gradient-free analysis is clear.
- Experimental methodology demonstrates proof of concept but does not thoroughly establish practical superiority across metrics and baselines.

Clarity:
- The structure is generally clear. However, some proofs and constants are deferred to the supplement; more intuition on β1 and the derivative-approximation method would aid readability. Minor typos and reference inconsistencies should be corrected. Clearly specify whether α is learned or fixed.

Originality:
- Extends random gradient-free and adaptive gradient methods to constrained, inexact-oracle settings and applies them to Supervised PageRank with explicit end-to-end complexity bounds. Prior Supervised PageRank work lacked convergence guarantees under inexact lower-level computations; addressing this is novel and useful. The lower-level derivative computation extension is also a practical contribution.

Significance:
- The theoretical framework for inexact oracles in this application is valuable and likely to be of interest beyond this specific model. The empirical evaluation suggests improvements over the prior optimization approach, but broader impact would be better supported with standard metrics, more datasets, and stronger baselines.

Questions and suggestions:
- Report ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@k) and wall-clock time per method; include ablations on N1/N2 and τ/δ/ε trade-offs.
- Compare to additional baselines (LambdaMART, supervised random walks adapted to ranking) and to learning α as a parameter.
- Clarify the stopping rule for GBP and ensure fair tuning. Provide public pseudo-code and constants (β1) in the main text or appendix with numerical values for your dataset.
- Discuss memory and implementation details for derivative computations at scale.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, principled treatment of inexact oracles with convergence guarantees adapted to a practical learning problem.
- Clear theoretical contributions with end-to-end complexity accounting.
- Empirical evidence of improved objective values and a better lower-level solver.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope, missing standard ranking metrics and broader baselines; unclear practical speedups.
- Some assumptions (local convexity for gradient-free) may not hold broadly; constants and practical parameter selection not fully elucidated.
- Presentation could be polished (notation, references).

Overall, the paper provides meaningful theoretical advances for learning Supervised PageRank under inexact computations and promising empirical results, but would benefit from stronger and more comprehensive experimental validation.
