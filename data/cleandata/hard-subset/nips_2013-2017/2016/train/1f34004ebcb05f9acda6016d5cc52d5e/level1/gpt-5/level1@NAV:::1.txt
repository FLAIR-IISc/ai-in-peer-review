This paper tackles learning Supervised PageRank by formulating parameter estimation as a constrained, generally nonconvex loss minimization where node/edge features influence restart and transition probabilities. The key technical move is a two-level scheme: a lower-level routine approximates the query-dependent stationary distributions and their parameter derivatives using the Nesterov–Nemirovski averaging method, and an upper-level optimizer uses either (i) a random gradient-free algorithm with an inexact zero-order oracle or (ii) an adaptive projected gradient method with an inexact first-order oracle and unknown Lipschitz constant. The authors derive convergence-rate guarantees and complexity bounds in terms of the inner accuracy and show how to balance inner/outer accuracy to achieve a target tolerance. Empirically, on a Yandex browsing-graph dataset, both methods improve the loss over the prior gradient baseline for Supervised PageRank [Zhukovskii et al., 2013/2015] and the lower-level routine appears faster than the power method. The work connects to PageRank/HITS; supervised random walks (Backstrom & Leskovec); inexact-oracle optimization (Devolder–Glineur–Nesterov); random gradient-free methods (Nesterov & Spokoiny); and nonconvex first-order methods (Ghadimi & Lan), and extends these in handling constraints and inexact oracles within this application.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and, to my knowledge, new in its specific inexact-oracle formulations for the constrained setting and their application to supervised PageRank. The inner/outer accuracy-complexity trade-off is clearly articulated. However, the gradient-free guarantee assumes convexity on Φ, while the task is nonconvex; the paper posits local convexity but does not verify it empirically or theoretically. The experiments are promising but limited (see below), and some methodological choices raise concerns about leakage.

Clarity: Generally well organized but dense; several key constants (e.g., β1) and proofs are relegated to the supplement, making it hard to assess tightness. The stopping criteria and data splits need clearer exposition.

Originality: Moderate-to-strong. The constrained inexact-oracle gradient-free and adaptive first-order frameworks and their deployment to supervised PageRank appear novel beyond incremental adaptations of [16,25].

Significance: Moderate. The guarantees for an important family of graph-learning problems are valuable, and the empirical gains suggest practical impact, but broader evaluation is needed to convince the NeurIPS audience.

Pros for acceptance:
- New convergence guarantees for both gradient-based and gradient-free methods with inexact oracles in a constrained setting.
- Useful analysis of inner/outer accuracy trade-offs; lower-level routine with better complexity than power.
- Real-data evaluation indicating improvements over the prior supervised PageRank learner.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical scope is narrow: no standard IR metrics (NDCG/MAP), no comparison to strong LTR baselines (e.g., RankSVM/LambdaMART) or to other graph-ranking learners.
- Potential evaluation leakage: stopping/tuning appears to reference test sets; unclear validation split.
- Convexity assumption for gradient-free method on Φ is unverified; discussion of practical parameter choices (L, ε, α) is limited.
- Reproducibility is limited (proprietary data; unclear code availability); sensitivity analyses (N1/N2, α, graph size) are brief.

Suggestions: Add a proper validation split and avoid test-driven stopping; report NDCG/MAP; compare to standard LTR and graph-based baselines; include public datasets where possible; justify or probe the local convexity assumption; and clarify constants and implementation details.
