

# Peer Review: "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Supervised PageRank models, which extend traditional PageRank by incorporating node and edge features. The authors propose two novel two-level optimization methods (one gradient-based and one gradient-free) to solve the non-convex loss-minimization problem, providing convergence rate guarantees that the state-of-the-art method [21] lacks. The work builds upon Nesterov's inexact oracle framework [25] and extends it to handle constrained optimization problems with the specific challenges of the Supervised PageRank framework.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed proofs for key lemmas and theorems (in supplementary materials) establishing convergence rates for both proposed methods. The complexity bounds for the algorithms are well-justified and provide valuable insights into the trade-off between lower-level approximation accuracy and computational complexity. The experimental results on a real-world web ranking task demonstrate clear superiority over the state-of-the-art method [21] in terms of ranking quality. However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail about the dataset characteristics and comparison with other relevant optimization methods (e.g., stochastic gradient descent variants), which would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results and experimental validation. The two-level optimization framework is well-explained. The mathematical notation is consistent, though the paper is dense with technical details that might challenge readers without a strong optimization background. The authors could improve readability by providing more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical results (e.g., Theorems 1, 3, and 4) before presenting the formal proofs.

**Originality:** This work makes several significant contributions to the field. First, it provides the first convergence guarantees for optimization methods applied to the Supervised PageRank problem, addressing a key limitation of the previous work [21]. Second, it extends the gradient-free optimization framework [16] to constrained optimization problems with inexact oracles, which is novel in the context of the Supervised PageRank problem. Third, it develops a general framework for gradient methods with inexact first-order oracles for non-convex problems, which is a meaningful contribution to optimization theory. The authors effectively distinguish their work from previous NIPS papers (e.g., [21], [5], [25]) by clearly articulating the theoretical gaps they address.

**Significance:** The results have both theoretical and practical significance. Theoretically, the work advances the understanding of optimization methods for constrained non-convex problems with inexact oracles. Practically, the proposed algorithms outperform the state-of-the-art in a real-world web ranking task, which could directly improve search engine performance. The claim that "these algorithms can increase the reliability and speed of a search engine" is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical computational advantages (beyond just ranking quality) of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- Provides the first convergence rate guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning
- Develops novel extensions of gradient-free and gradient-based optimization frameworks for constrained problems with inexact oracles
- Comprehensive complexity analysis for both algorithms
- Demonstrates clear empirical superiority over the state-of-the-art method [21]
- Well-structured theoretical development with rigorous proofs

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental comparison with other relevant optimization methods
- Insufficient detail about the dataset characteristics and comparison with standard benchmark datasets
- Theoretical results are presented without sufficient intuitive explanation
- The practical computational advantages beyond ranking quality are not fully articulated

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a clear limitation of previous work in the Supervised PageRank literature. The empirical results demonstrate practical significance, and the theoretical framework has broader applicability beyond the specific ranking problem considered. While the experimental section could be strengthened with more comparative analysis and dataset details, the theoretical contributions are substantial and well-justified. The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical advances with practical implications, and the work represents a meaningful step forward in the optimization of graph-based ranking models.

The paper's main contribution is providing convergence guarantees for Supervised PageRank learning, which has been a notable gap in the literature. This theoretical advancement, combined with the demonstrated empirical superiority of the proposed methods, makes this a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The authors successfully bridge the gap between optimization theory and practical application in web ranking, which is exactly the type of work that NIPS values.
