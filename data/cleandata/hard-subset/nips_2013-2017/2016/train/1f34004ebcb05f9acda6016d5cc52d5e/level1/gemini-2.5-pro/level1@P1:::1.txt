### Review of "Learning Supervised PageRank with Gradient-Based and Gradient-Free Optimization Methods"

#### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper tackles the non-convex optimization problem of learning parameters for the Supervised PageRank model, a powerful framework for ranking nodes in a graph by incorporating node and edge features. The core challenge is that the loss function, which depends on the stationary distribution of a Markov process, and its gradient cannot be computed exactly. The authors frame this as a problem of optimization with an "inexact oracle."

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  **A Gradient-Free Method (GFN):** The authors adapt the random gradient-free optimization framework of Nesterov [16] to handle constrained optimization problems where the function value is computed with a known, bounded error (an inexact zero-order oracle). They provide a convergence rate analysis for this method under a local convexity assumption.
2.  **A Gradient-Based Method (GBN):** They propose an adaptive projected gradient method for non-convex constrained optimization, extending the work of Ghadimi and Lan [7]. This method works with an inexact first-order oracle (approximations of both the function value and its gradient) and does not require knowing the Lipschitz constant of the gradient beforehand. A convergence analysis is provided for this method.

For both approaches, the paper carefully analyzes the trade-off between the accuracy of the lower-level oracle (which computes the stationary distribution and its derivatives) and the overall complexity of the upper-level optimization algorithm. This analysis provides, for the first time, theoretical convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for learning Supervised PageRank models. This is a significant advance over the state-of-the-art method by Zhukovskii et al. [21], which used a gradient-based approach with a power method-based oracle but lacked any proof of convergence. Experiments on a large-scale web ranking task show that the proposed methods outperform the previous state-of-the-art in terms of final loss function value.

#### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Rigor and Soundness (Quality/Originality):** The primary strength of this paper is its strong theoretical foundation. It provides the first convergence guarantees for the problem of learning Supervised PageRank. The authors skillfully bridge the gap between abstract optimization theory for inexact oracles and a concrete, important application. The derivation of complexity bounds that explicitly account for the cost of the lower-level oracle is a complete and valuable piece of work.

2.  **Novel Methodological Contributions (Originality/Significance):** The paper presents novel extensions to existing optimization frameworks. The adaptation of random gradient-free methods to the constrained setting with a deterministic error bound is a useful contribution. Similarly, the development of an adaptive gradient method for non-convex constrained problems with an inexact first-order oracle is a solid, general-purpose contribution that could be applied to other large-scale learning problems where exact gradients are intractable.

3.  **Demonstrable Improvement over State-of-the-Art (Significance):** The experimental results, though on a proprietary dataset, clearly show that both proposed methods (GFN and GBN) achieve better final loss values than the previous method (GBP). This provides empirical validation for the superiority of the new, theoretically-grounded approaches. The finding that the gradient-free method performs best is particularly interesting and practical, as it avoids the complex and potentially costly computation of derivatives.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It first introduces the Supervised PageRank model, then details the methods for numerically approximating the function and its gradient (the oracles), followed by the general frameworks for the two optimization methods, and finally their specific application and experimental validation. This logical flow makes the paper easy to follow despite the technical density of the material.

#### Weaknesses

1.  **Reproducibility (Quality):** The experiments are conducted on a proprietary dataset from the Yandex search engine. While this demonstrates real-world applicability, it makes the results impossible to reproduce and hinders direct comparison by other researchers. The paper's impact would be substantially greater if the methods were also evaluated on a standard, publicly available web graph or ranking dataset (e.g., LETOR).

2.  **Convexity Assumption for Gradient-Free Method (Quality):** The convergence analysis for the gradient-free method (Theorem 2) relies on the assumption that the objective function is convex within the chosen optimization domain Φ. The authors acknowledge that the problem is globally non-convex and state that this assumption holds if Φ is a small vicinity of a local minimum. This is a significant restriction. The paper would be stronger if it included more discussion on the practical implications of this assumption or provided empirical evidence that such convex regions are typically found.

3.  **Missing Practical Details in Experiments (Clarity/Quality):** The experimental comparison focuses on the final loss values and upper-level iteration counts. A more comprehensive comparison would include wall-clock timings. Claims about improving the "speed of a search engine" (Conclusion) are strong and should be supported by concrete timing data. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical trade-offs between the proposed methods and the baseline, especially since the proposed lower-level oracles might be more computationally intensive per iteration than the simple power method used in the baseline.

#### Arguments for Acceptance (Pro)

*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantees for learning a significant ranking model (Supervised PageRank).
*   Develops and analyzes novel extensions of gradient-based and gradient-free optimization methods for settings with inexact oracles, a contribution of broader interest.
*   Demonstrates superior performance over the existing state-of-the-art method on a large-scale, real-world task.
*   The work is technically solid, well-motivated, and clearly presented.

#### Arguments for Rejection (Con)

*   The experimental results are not reproducible due to the use of a proprietary dataset.
*   The theoretical guarantee for the gradient-free method relies on a local convexity assumption, which may be limiting in practice for a non-convex problem.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks wall-clock time comparisons, making it difficult to fully assess the practical efficiency of the methods.

#### Conclusion and Recommendation

Overall, this is a very strong paper that makes significant contributions to both optimization theory and its application in machine learning. The theoretical guarantees for learning Supervised PageRank models fill an important gap in the literature. The extensions of optimization methods for inexact oracles are valuable in their own right. Despite the weaknesses regarding reproducibility and the local convexity assumption, the strengths of the paper—particularly its theoretical depth and novelty—are substantial. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. I recommend acceptance. Minor revisions to address the discussion around the convexity assumption and to potentially include timing information would further improve the manuscript.
