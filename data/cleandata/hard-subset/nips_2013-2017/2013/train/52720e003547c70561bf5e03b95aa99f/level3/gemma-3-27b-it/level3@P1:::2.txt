This paper presents an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for solving the CLIME estimator for sparse precision matrix estimation, with a focus on scalability to large-scale distributed computations. The authors establish convergence rates and demonstrate the algorithm's performance on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, showing nearly linear scalability with the number of cores and the ability to handle problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters.

The work builds on the existing literature surrounding sparse precision matrix estimation, citing key papers like [3, 4, 19, 25, 28] that establish theoretical foundations for estimators and convergence rates.  The authors correctly identify the computational challenges associated with scaling these methods to ultra-high dimensions, where the number of parameters grows quadratically with the number of variables.  Recent advancements in scaling machine learning algorithms, as highlighted in references [2, 6, 7, 9, 20, 22, 23, 26, 29], motivate the need for efficient distributed solutions.  The selection of CLIME is well-justified, as its linear program formulation offers inherent computational advantages, particularly its column-wise separability.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical soundness and practical contributions. The derivation of the CLIME-ADMM algorithm appears standard, building upon established ADMM techniques as evidenced by the citations [2, 12, 27].  The authors effectively leverage the structure of the CLIME estimator—specifically, the separable nature of the linear program—to achieve parallelism.  The identification of matrix-matrix multiplication as the primary computational bottleneck is astute, and the strategy of solving in column blocks to facilitate efficient parallel computation is a key innovation.  The convergence analysis providing O(1/T) rates is reassuring, and the thorough evaluation on both shared- and distributed-memory architectures demonstrably showcases the algorithm's scalability. The proposed block cyclic distribution scheme to avoid load imbalance is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the convergence analysis is present, the practical implications of the convergence rate could be further discussed.  The experimental section, while demonstrating scalability, lacks sufficient detail regarding the accuracy of the estimated precision matrices. Simply showing runtime is insufficient; it's essential to compare the quality of the solutions obtained by CLIME-ADMM and the baseline methods. For example, providing metrics like the false positive rate and true positive rate in recovering the underlying graph structure, as commonly done in graphical model estimation, would significantly strengthen the evaluation. Further, a more thorough comparison of parameter tuning requirements for each method is warranted.  While the authors acknowledge Tiger’s tuning-free nature, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of CLIME and DC-QUIC to parameter choices would be beneficial. The discussion of perturbation of the sample covariance (Section 3.1) feels somewhat disconnected from the main body of the paper and could be more tightly integrated. While the theoretical basis is mentioned, the practical benefit and implementation details are vague.  The statement that CLIME-ADMM "achieves a linear speedup" in some experiments needs qualification – a plot showing both the observed speedup and the ideal linear speedup would be informative.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem—scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to extremely large datasets.
*   The proposed algorithm and distributed framework are novel and well-motivated.
*   The convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant scalability and outperform existing methods in a large-scale setting.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail regarding the *quality* of the estimated solutions.
*   The discussion of certain aspects, like the covariance perturbation, feels underdeveloped.
*   The claim of “linear speedup” needs more precise justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of large-scale machine learning. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more detailed accuracy metrics, the authors have presented a technically sound and practically useful algorithm for a challenging problem. The scalability results are impressive, and the framework has the potential to be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with high-dimensional data.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above regarding the experimental evaluation and clarification of the speedup claims.
