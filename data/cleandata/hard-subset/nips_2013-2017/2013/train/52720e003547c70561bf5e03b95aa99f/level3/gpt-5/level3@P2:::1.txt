Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact ADMM scheme that linearizes the quadratic penalty and adds a proximal term, yielding simple per-iteration updates consisting of elementwise soft-thresholding/box-projection and matrix multiplications. They establish O(1/T) convergence for both the objective gap and optimality residuals. To scale to ultra-high dimensions, they (a) operate on column-blocks of the precision matrix rather than single columns to exploit matrix–matrix multiplications, (b) leverage low rank of the sample covariance (C = A A′, with n ≪ p) to reduce matmul cost from O(p2k) to O(npk), and (c) use block-cyclic data distribution within a distributed-memory framework (OpenMPI + ScaLAPACK) for load balance and efficient use of memory hierarchies. Experiments on synthetic and real data compare against CLIME/flare, TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate near-linear speedups and a run with p = 10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.

The work builds on: CLIME and its guarantees (Cai et al.), ADMM and inexact/linearized ADMM (Boyd et al.; He & Yuan), scalable matrix multiplication and cache-aware blocking (Lam et al.; GotoBLAS), and distributed ADMM/GPBS (Parikh & Boyd). It complements scalable inverse covariance methods such as QUIC/DC-QUIC and covariance thresholding into components.

Strengths
- Clear system goal with a simple, implementable algorithm: closed-form proximal steps and BLAS-3 matmuls.
- Convergence guarantees (O(1/T)) for both objective and KKT residuals under standard assumptions on η.
- Sensible exploitation of problem structure: column-blocking for matmul efficiency, low-rank C = A A′, and potential sparsity in iterates.
- Practical distributed framework with block-cyclic distribution and parallel I/O; use of commodity libraries (OpenMP/MPI/ScaLAPACK).
- Strong empirical scaling, including million-dimensional precision estimation; comparisons to relevant baselines (TIGER, flare ADMM, DC-QUIC).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/notation: Several symbols (e.g., ρ, η) are introduced late or ambiguously; notation is scattered. Suggest a notation/variables table and explicit dimensions for all variables in Algorithm 1. Fix minor typos (“tuning,” “summarized”), and define E when first used in box(·).
- Distributed details missing: The “distributed algorithm” section reads more like an implementation sketch. Please quantify per-iteration communication (messages and words) for W = A′X and U = AW, the synchronization points, and overlap of communication/computation. A breakdown of time per iteration (compute vs communication) and weak/strong scaling would substantiate the claims.
- Load balance claims: Block-cyclic distribution is plausible, but evidence is anecdotal. Include experiments comparing large-block vs block-cyclic layouts, reporting load imbalance metrics (e.g., max/avg time per process), cache miss rates, and resulting speedups.
- Choice of column-block size k: Currently ad hoc. Provide guidance/heuristics or an auto-tuning strategy based on process grid, block sizes (pb, nb, kb), cache sizes, memory per node, and sparsity. Even a simple model predicting arithmetic intensity vs cache size would help.
- Evaluation metrics: On synthetic data, matching sparsity is not necessarily matching statistical quality. Consider reporting held-out negative log-likelihood or estimation error norms vs time, and residual/objective traces, to compare convergence behavior across methods.
- Perturbation/sparsification of C: Theorem 3 assumes independent sub-exponential perturbations; this is non-constructive for a practical truncation rule. Provide the concrete truncation threshold implied by your bound, and an experiment showing that truncation to achieve sparsity preserves downstream graph recovery and estimation error.
- Positioning: The algorithmic core (linearized ADMM with O(1/T) rate) is close to known results; the novelty is largely in the systems design (column-blocking, low-rank matmuls, block-cyclic distribution). Please make this explicit and better contrast with prior ADMM-based solvers for CLIME and GPBS-style distributed splitting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The inexact ADMM updates and convergence statements are reasonable and consistent with prior analyses. Proofs are summarized; full details are deferred—fine for NIPS if included in the supplement. Claims about load balancing and distribution would benefit from quantitative communication analysis and ablation studies.
- Empirics: Scaling results are compelling, but could be strengthened with accuracy vs time, communication breakdowns, and more systematic studies of k and block sizes.

Clarity
- Generally readable, with actionable improvements: consolidate notation; define all variables on first use; correct typos; clarify Algorithm 1 (dimensions, definitions of box/soft, E).

Originality
- Moderate. Linearized ADMM and CLIME are established; the contributions are in (i) column-block formulation/use of matmul, (ii) practical distributed framework with block-cyclic layout, and (iii) applying low-rank products to CLIME at scale. The perturbation result is interesting but not central nor fully developed empirically.

Significance
- High practical significance: enabling CLIME at million-scale is valuable for practitioners and may spur further work on distributed constrained optimization. The methodological novelty is incremental, but the systems contribution and demonstrated scale are impactful.

Specific questions for the authors
- How do you set ρ and η in practice to satisfy η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 without being overly conservative? Sensitivity?
- What is the per-iteration communication cost (in words) for W = A′X and U = AW under your block-cyclic mapping? Any overlap with compute?
- How is X distributed when k is not a multiple of kb, and how do you handle load balance at the tail blocks?
- Do you exploit symmetry of Ω to reduce computation/storage?
- Can you report weak scaling (increasing p and cores proportionally) and a compute/communication time breakdown?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely scalability problem with a practical, well-engineered solution.
- Solid convergence guarantees; sensible exploitation of low-rank structure.
- Strong empirical scaling to p = 10^6 with open-source-friendly tooling.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited novelty in optimization; distributed aspects under-specified.
- Insufficient analysis/evidence for load balance and communication claims.
- Evaluation emphasizes runtime; needs stronger accuracy vs time comparisons and guidance on k/parameter selection.

Overall recommendation: 7 (accept if space allows; strong systems contribution with clear practical value; minor revisions would further strengthen)
