## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper tackles the important problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high dimensions, a core task in various fields like bioinformatics and finance. The authors propose CLIME-ADMM, a scalable variant of the CLIME estimator, coupled with a distributed framework designed for datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The key novelty lies in estimating the precision matrix by column blocks rather than column-by-column, and demonstrating empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapak. This work builds upon existing research in sparse precision estimation (e.g., [28, 3, 19]) and distributed optimization techniques ([2, 26, 6, 9]).

The strengths of this paper are significant. The proposed approach addresses a critical bottleneck in sparse precision estimation – scalability to ultra-high dimensional data. The authors demonstrate promising results, achieving linear scalability with the number of cores in their experiments. The theoretical convergence analysis for CLIME-ADMM is a valuable contribution, and the exploration of leveraging sparse and low-rank structures within the algorithm is insightful. The empirical evaluation, including comparisons to established methods like DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare, provides a reasonable assessment of the algorithm’s performance.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The notation is indeed scattered across the initial sections, making it difficult to follow the derivations. A dedicated notation table is crucial. The justification for the block-cyclic data distribution and the selection of the column block size *k* are unclear. While the authors claim load balance and scalability, they provide no empirical evidence to support these claims regarding the distribution scheme itself. The description of the distributed framework feels incomplete; it’s more accurately characterized as a parallel implementation, as the paper lacks details on message passing or communication mechanisms. Minor writing issues, as noted, detract from the clarity. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses a significant scalability challenge in a relevant area of machine learning. The proposed method shows promising empirical results and includes theoretical analysis. The novelty of column-block estimation and the distributed implementation are valuable contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of clarity in the notation and justification for key design choices (block size, data distribution) weakens the paper. The limited description of the distributed framework and the minor writing errors further detract from its quality. 

**Overall:** While the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, the weaknesses regarding clarity and justification need to be addressed. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, specifically focusing on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing empirical support for the data distribution scheme, and elaborating on the communication aspects of the distributed framework.
