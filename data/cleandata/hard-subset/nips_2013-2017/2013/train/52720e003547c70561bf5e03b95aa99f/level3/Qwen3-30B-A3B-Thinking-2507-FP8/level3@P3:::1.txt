

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

**Summary**
This paper proposes a scalable algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors introduce a variant of the ADMM algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) that solves the precision matrix problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column, and develop a distributed framework that scales to millions of dimensions. Key innovations include an inexact ADMM algorithm with O(1/T) convergence rates, column-block processing to leverage matrix-matrix operations, and block cyclic data distribution for load balancing. The method is evaluated on shared-memory (OpenMP) and distributed-memory (OpenMPI, ScaLAPACK) architectures, demonstrating scalability to 1 million dimensions (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.

The work builds on prior statistical estimation methods like CLIME [3], DC-QUIC [13], and Tiger [19], which have strong statistical guarantees but do not scale well to ultra-high dimensions. The paper addresses this critical gap for "ultra-large-scale" problems.

**Strengths**
1. The paper makes a significant contribution to scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions, demonstrated by the ability to estimate a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.

2. The theoretical convergence rates (Theorems 1 and 2) provide strong mathematical grounding, establishing O(1/T) convergence for both objective function and optimality residuals.

3. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, showing both speed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, Flare) and scalability analysis across different architectures.

4. The column-block approach for processing (rather than column-by-column) is a novel and important insight that leverages matrix-matrix operations to improve computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses**
1. **Notation issues**: The paper's notation is scattered across Sections 1-3 without a dedicated notation table. Symbols like ρ and η are undefined until used in the algorithms, making it difficult for readers to follow the mathematical development. A notation table would significantly improve clarity.

2. **Block-cyclic distribution justification**: The paper claims block-cyclic distribution "guarantees load balance and scalability" but provides no theoretical justification or empirical evidence to support this claim. The authors should provide either a theoretical analysis or empirical results demonstrating that block-cyclic distribution achieves better load balancing than other schemes.

3. **Ad hoc block size selection**: The column block size k is chosen as 100 for experiments without theoretical guidance or intuition for this choice. The paper should discuss how to optimally select k based on problem characteristics (e.g., cache size, sparsity pattern) or provide a sensitivity analysis.

4. **Distributed vs. parallel distinction**: The paper claims to present a "distributed framework" but lacks detailed discussion of message passing or communication mechanisms between nodes. The algorithm appears to be better characterized as a parallel implementation rather than a distributed algorithm, which is a significant oversight for a paper claiming to present a distributed framework.

5. **Minor writing issues**: The paper contains typos ("tunning" instead of "tuning," "parameter tunning free" instead of "tuning," "summerized" instead of "summarized") that should be corrected.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance**
*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with solid theoretical results and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The claims are well-supported, though the lack of theoretical justification for key design choices is a weakness.

*Clarity*: The paper would benefit significantly from a dedicated notation table and clearer explanation of the block-cyclic distribution. The writing is generally clear but the scattered notation and lack of justification for key choices reduce clarity.

*Originality*: The column-block approach for precision matrix estimation is novel and represents a meaningful extension of existing CLIME solvers. The empirical analysis using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK provides valuable insights into scaling precision matrix estimation.

*Significance*: The significance is high, as the paper addresses a critical problem in modern statistics and machine learning: scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions. The demonstrated ability to handle 1 million dimensions represents a substantial advance.

**Recommendation**
This paper makes a valuable contribution to scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions. However, the lack of theoretical justification for the block-cyclic distribution, the ad hoc choice of block size k, and the unclear distinction between distributed and parallel computation are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance**
*Pro*: Significant contribution to scaling precision matrix estimation (1M dimensions, 1T parameters); strong theoretical results; comprehensive empirical evaluation.

*Con*: Scattered notation; lack of theoretical justification for block-cyclic distribution; ad hoc choice of block size k; unclear distinction between distributed and parallel computation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notation, theoretical justification, and clarity issues. The paper's significance and technical contribution are strong, and these revisions are straightforward to implement.
