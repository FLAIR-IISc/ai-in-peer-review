- Proposes a scalable distributed framework for sparse precision estimation using CLIME with inexact ADMM on column blocks and block-cyclic data distribution, targeting ultra-high dimensions (millions+).  
- Introduces an inexact ADMM algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) that operates on column blocks rather than individual columns.  
- Claims near-linear scaling with number of cores and the ability to handle up to one trillion parameters (1M dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores).  
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the inexact ADMM (Theorems 1 and 2).  
- Empirical evaluation shows significant scalability and performance improvements over DC-QUIC, Tiger, and FLARE on synthetic and real data.  
- Builds on prior CLIME and distributed optimization literature and positions the work as a more general, memory-hierarchy-aware framework.  
- Novelty: column block decomposition and convergence analysis are meaningful contributions; block-cyclic distribution application is less novel.  
- Theoretical analysis is sound but does not justify the ad hoc choice of column block size k or fully explain why block processing is more efficient than column-wise processing.  
- Empirical evidence for load balancing benefits of block-cyclic distribution is limited and lacks comparative measurements.  
- The paper gives insufficient detail about communication patterns, synchronization, and distributed scalability limitations.  
- Notation is scattered and confusing; a dedicated notation table and fixes for minor typos are recommended.  
- Algorithm 1 is clear, but the explanation of how block-cyclic distribution achieves load balance is inadequate.  
- Strengths: strong scalability demonstration, theoretical guarantees, thorough empirical comparisons, and clear parallelization strategy.  
- Weaknesses: ad-hoc block-size selection, unclear load-balancing claims, limited communication analysis, scattered notation, and some unsupported "distributed" claims.  
- Recommendation: accept, with minor revisions to clarify notation, justify block-size selection, and substantiate load-balancing/communication claims.  
- Pro arguments: solves an important scaling problem, is theoretically grounded, and demonstrates impressive empirical results; con arguments: incomplete theoretical justification for block approach and insufficient detail on distributed properties.
