This paper presents a scalable optimization framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors introduce an inexact ADMM algorithm that operates on column-blocks (k columns at a time) rather than per-column, enabling heavy use of matrix–matrix operations and distributed linear algebra. They provide O(1/T) convergence rates for the objective gap and constraint residuals. The system exploits both low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = A A^T with n ≪ p) and sparsity in iterates, and is implemented efficiently on shared- and distributed-memory architectures with block-cyclic data distribution. Experiments against CLIME solvers (flare/ADMM), TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and real datasets show strong runtime gains and nearly linear scaling with cores; the system solves a million-dimensional problem in about 11 hours on 400 cores. Relative to prior work, the paper situates itself among scalable ADMM frameworks and large-scale graphical model estimation (e.g., QUIC/DC-QUIC for graphical lasso, distributed ADMM and parameter servers). Its novelty lies in the block-wise inexact ADMM for CLIME and a careful distributed design that converts CLIME’s separability into efficient high-throughput matrix multiplications with load-balanced data layout.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The inexact ADMM derivation for CLIME with a linearized subproblem and proximal term is standard but well executed. The convergence guarantees (O(1/T) for objective and residuals) are consistent with known results for inexact/linearized ADMM under appropriate step-size conditions (η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2). The argument that low-rank products reduce cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k) is correct and crucial in the n ≪ p regime.
- Correctness and care: The algorithmic steps are simple and verifiable; the bottlenecks are clearly isolated to matrix multiplications, and the HPC distribution strategy (block cyclic) is appropriate. The “perturbed covariance” argument for enabling sparsity in C is plausible; a theorem is stated for sub-Gaussian data with sub-exponential perturbations, though more detail on constants and practical thresholding rules would strengthen this section.
- Empirics: Comparisons cover synthetic graphs and two real datasets; runtime scaling analyses on shared and distributed settings are thorough. Statistical accuracy is briefly compared (precision–recall); TIGER achieves higher recall as expected, while CLIME-based methods are competitive.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with useful algorithm pseudocode, computational cost accounting, and system diagrams. The separation between elementwise operations and matrix multiplications clarifies where parallelism is harvested. Suggestions: explicitly summarize stopping criteria, sensitivity to ρ and η, and selection of block size k (currently discussed qualitatively via cache and process-grid considerations).

Originality
- Using inexact, linearized ADMM for CLIME and proving O(1/T) rates is incremental relative to general ADMM literature, but tailoring it to column-block updates and exploiting low-rank/sample covariance structure for ultra-high dimensions is new in this context. The systems contribution—block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O, and end-to-end scaling to p = 10^6—is the most distinctive element. The “coarsened covariance” idea for accelerating multiplications via safe thresholding is interesting, albeit not fully developed empirically.

Significance
- Practically significant: estimating precision matrices at million-scale with trillions of parameters is beyond the reach of most existing solvers; demonstrating this with wall-clock times and near-linear speedups is impactful for large-scale graphical modeling.
- Scientific significance: While the optimization theory is standard-rate, the integration of algorithmic and systems insights for CLIME materially expands the scale at which such estimators can be used and will likely be adopted by practitioners.

Strengths
- Clear decomposition into elementwise ops and matrix multiplications; simple, robust updates.
- Careful exploitation of low-rank structure (C = A A^T) and iterate sparsity.
- Block-cyclic distribution and parallel I/O for load balance and cache efficiency.
- Strong empirical scalability, including distributed results to 400 cores and million-dimensional problems.
- Solid positioning relative to CLIME/TIGER/QUIC literature and distributed optimization frameworks.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theoretical novelty is limited; O(1/T) rates for (inexact) ADMM are known. Emphasize what is new in the analysis specific to CLIME/column-block structure, if any.
- Comparisons mix different estimators (CLIME vs graphical lasso vs TIGER). While runtime comparisons are still useful, more systematic statistical accuracy comparisons (e.g., support recovery at matched FDR/TPR, or spectral/Frobenius error vs n) would strengthen claims.
- The “perturbed covariance” sparsification is not validated empirically. A small study showing accuracy/runtime trade-offs vs threshold level would be valuable.
- CLIME does not enforce positive definiteness; discuss post-processing (e.g., symmetrization, projection) and its cost if needed in applications.
- More detail on communication costs per iteration and synchronization (especially for two-step products A^T X, then A W) would help readers reason about scaling beyond 400 cores.
- Hyperparameters: guidance on choosing ρ, η, and block size k; current discussion is heuristic.

Questions for the authors
- What stopping criteria were used (primal/dual residual thresholds, max iterations), and how sensitive are runtimes to these tolerances?
- How are ρ and η selected in practice? Any adaptive schemes tested?
- How is symmetry/PD of the final precision estimate handled?
- Can you report per-iteration communication time and total data moved as a function of q and k?
- For the covariance sparsification, what thresholds did you try and how did they affect accuracy?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely scalability gap with a simple, well-engineered approach.
- Demonstrates unprecedented problem sizes with careful systems design and strong empirical scaling.
- Clear exposition and useful insights (block sizing, distribution, low-rank products) that generalize to other constrained estimators.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond adapting known ADMM techniques.
- Empirical comparisons do not fully separate algorithmic speedups from differences in statistical estimators; limited accuracy analysis at scale.
- Some practical components (covariance sparsification, parameter selection) are underexplored.

Recommendation
- Leaning accept, contingent on release of reproducible code and scripts. Given the primarily systems-oriented contribution and the clear practical impact, making the implementation available would maximize the paper’s value to the community.
