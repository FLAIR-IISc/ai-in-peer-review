Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses large-scale sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation via CLIME in the ultra-high-dimensional regime (millions of variables, trillions of parameters). CLIME decomposes column-wise but prior solvers do not scale due to memory and single-machine limitations. The authors propose: (i) an inexact/linearized ADMM variant tailored to CLIME that operates on column blocks rather than single columns, enabling Level-3 BLAS matrix–matrix operations; (ii) convergence rate guarantees for both the objective suboptimality and primal/dual residuals; (iii) practical exploitation of structure—sparsity in iterates and sample covariance, and low-rank structure (using C = A A^T with n ≪ p to reduce multiplies from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k)); and (iv) shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations using block cyclic data distribution for load balance and cache efficiency. Experiments on synthetic and real data compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, showing substantial runtime gains and near-linear scaling up to hundreds of cores, including a p = 10^6 run in ~11 hours on 400 cores.

The work builds on and connects to prior NIPS and related work on sparse inverse covariance estimation (e.g., graphical lasso and QUIC; Hsieh et al., NIPS), ADMM for large-scale optimization (Boyd et al.), distributed ML systems (parameter servers, GraphLab), and cache-aware BLAS/ScaLAPACK. The statistical justification for covariance thresholding aligns with known CLIME deviation bounds.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important systems bottleneck: scalable precision estimation with convincing engineering and careful parallel/distributed design (column-blocking, block-cyclic distribution).
- Clear, simple algorithmic steps (soft-thresholding, box projection, matrix multiplies) that map well to modern architectures; excellent use of BLAS-3 and ScaLAPACK.
- Convergence analysis for the proposed inexact ADMM updates (O(1/T) rates for objective and residuals) under transparent conditions on η, ρ, and λmax(C).
- Useful structural insights: iterate sparsity and statistically-justified covariance thresholding; low-rank multiplications when n ≪ p.
- Extensive empirical evaluation with detailed scalability studies across block size k and core counts; strong performance compared to TIGER, CLIME (flare), and DC-QUIC.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Theoretical novelty is modest: O(1/T) rates for (linearized/inexact) ADMM are by now standard; the contribution is largely in tailoring and engineering for CLIME. Please clarify what is new beyond adapting known proofs.
- Dependence on low-rank multiplications is central to the largest wins; less is said about regimes with larger n (e.g., n comparable to p), where O(p^2 k) products dominate. Can the method remain competitive then?
- Symmetry/positive-definiteness: CLIME solutions typically require a final symmetrization step and can be indefinite. How do you symmetrize across column blocks in the distributed setting? Any impact on downstream use?
- Stopping criteria and tolerances are not fully specified. Please report the exact primal/dual stopping rules, tolerances, and how they were matched across baselines for fair runtime comparisons.
- Parameter selection: guidance for choosing ρ, η (beyond η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2), and the column-block size k is empirical. Can you provide a heuristic or auto-tuning procedure that considers cache sizes, communication, and sparsity?
- Theorem 2 notation: the seminorm term with I − C^2 is unclear dimensionally in the current text. Please restate precisely and verify the assumptions ensuring positive semidefiniteness of the weighting matrix.
- Baselines: DC-QUIC benefits from covariance thresholding into blocks; to ensure fairness, did you also exploit block structure in your matrix multiplies when available? For CLIME baselines, were there optimized LP/Lasso solvers beyond flare?
- Reproducibility and adoption: the impact will be much higher if the distributed implementation is released. A code release (or at least a research prototype) should accompany publication.

Quality:
- Technical soundness appears solid; the algorithm is carefully designed to map to parallel primitives. Proof sketches and conditions are reasonable; some details (Theorem 2 clarity) should be tightened. Experiments convincingly support scalability claims; accuracy comparisons suggest competitive estimation quality, with TIGER achieving higher recall as expected.

Clarity:
- Generally very well written and organized. Suggestions: clarify Theorem 2 notation; explicitly list per-iteration compute, memory, and communication costs; provide a short paragraph on the final symmetrization step and PD post-processing; summarize stopping criteria and parameter choices; and give a short guideline for selecting k.

Originality:
- The main novelty lies in the column-block inexact ADMM design for CLIME and the systems contribution (load-balanced block-cyclic distribution with BLAS-3 kernels) at unprecedented scale. The convergence rates are standard but their application here is useful. The sub-exponential perturbation result to justify covariance truncation is a nice touch.

Significance:
- High practical significance for large-scale Gaussian graphical modeling and related applications; likely to be used and built upon by practitioners needing ultra-large inverse covariance estimation. Advances the state of the art in scale and engineering.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely scalability problem with a well-engineered solution.
- Strong empirical scaling to p = 10^6; near-linear speedups; clear ablations on k and core counts.
- Clean algorithmic design that uses only widely available primitives.
- Convergence guarantees and statistical justification for covariance truncation.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond adapting known ADMM rate analyses.
- Performance gains depend heavily on n ≪ p; less clear in other regimes.
- Some details on stopping, symmetry/PD handling, and Theorem 2 clarity need tightening.
- Reproducibility hinges on code availability.

Recommendation:
- Weak accept to accept, contingent on releasing the implementation (or a usable prototype) to the community to maximize impact and reproducibility.

Confidence: High in the assessment of systems/scalability and comparison landscape; moderate in the fine details of the convergence proof pending clarified notation in Theorem 2.
