This paper proposes an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multiplier (ADMM) algorithm for Constrained L1-minimized Estimation (CLIME) with a convergence rate of O(1/T) and implements it in both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems. The algorithm is evaluated against DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

The paper is technically sound, and the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard. The CLIME estimator's structure allows for componentwise subproblem solves, making ADMM highly parallelizable. The main cost is matrix-matrix multiplication, which is well-known to parallelize effectively.

The paper is well-organized and clear, making it easy to follow. The evaluation of ADMM in shared- and distributed-memory environments is interesting and original. Solving large-scale sparse precision estimation is an important problem, and a simple, robust distributed algorithm is useful.

However, the scalability gains largely stem from CLIME's simple structure, which allows for easy parallelization. The numerical experiment setup needs more detail, such as the reported accuracy achieved by each solver.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposal of an inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME with a convergence rate of O(1/T)
* The implementation of the algorithm in both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems
* The evaluation of the algorithm against state-of-the-art methods
* The clear and well-organized writing style

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The lack of detail in the numerical experiment setup
* The reliance on CLIME's simple structure for scalability gains

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm for CLIME with a convergence rate of O(1/T)
* The algorithm is implemented in both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems, making it applicable to a wide range of problems
* The evaluation of the algorithm against state-of-the-art methods provides a clear comparison of its performance

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of detail in the numerical experiment setup may make it difficult to reproduce the results
* The reliance on CLIME's simple structure for scalability gains may limit the algorithm's applicability to more complex problems.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions to address the lack of detail in the numerical experiment setup.
