## Review of “Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation”

This paper addresses the important problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high dimensions, a core task in various fields. The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm for the CLIME estimator, coupled with a distributed framework designed to scale to datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The core idea of leveraging CLIME’s structure for parallelization, particularly through column-block operations and matrix-matrix multiplications, is well-motivated and technically sound. The derivation of the inexact ADMM is standard and correctly references relevant work. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings. The evaluation of ADMM in both shared- and distributed-memory environments is a valuable and original contribution.

A key strength lies in the scalability demonstrated, achieving linear speedup with increasing core count, a significant advantage over existing methods. The focus on exploiting the inherent structure of CLIME – its decomposability and suitability for parallel matrix operations – is a smart approach. The theoretical convergence analysis, while standard, provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm. The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structures to further optimize performance is insightful, although the implementation details remain somewhat high-level.

However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail. While comparisons are made to other methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, CLIME-Flare), the reported accuracy (e.g., precision/recall, F1-score) achieved by each solver is missing. Simply stating that CLIME-ADMM is faster isn’t enough; demonstrating comparable or superior accuracy is crucial. The impact of different block sizes on performance, particularly in the distributed memory setting, could be explored more thoroughly. The discussion of perturbations to the sample covariance matrix, while theoretically interesting, lacks concrete experimental validation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates significant scalability advantages.
*   Original evaluation in shared- and distributed-memory environments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient detail in the experimental evaluation regarding accuracy metrics.
*   Limited exploration of the impact of key parameters (e.g., block size).
*   Theoretical discussion of sparsity exploitation lacks experimental validation.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with the potential to be highly impactful. Addressing the weaknesses in the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its value to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on providing more comprehensive experimental results and a more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity.
