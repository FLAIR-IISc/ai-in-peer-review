This paper tackles sparse precision matrix estimation at ultra-large scales via an inexact ADMM scheme tailored to CLIME. The authors (i) reformulate CLIME’s columnwise LPs into column-block problems solved by a linearized/proximal ADMM with closed-form updates, (ii) prove O(1/T) convergence rates for objective suboptimality and optimality residuals under η ≥ ρ λmax(C)2, (iii) exploit structure in the covariance operator (sparse/low-rank) to reduce the cost of the dominant matrix multiplications, and (iv) develop shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations using block cyclic data distribution. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC; show near-linear scaling up to hundreds of cores; and demonstrate a million-dimensional run completed in ~11 hours on 400 cores. Relative to prior work on sparse inverse covariance estimation (e.g., GLasso/QUIC, DC-QUIC) and recent distributed ADMM frameworks, the novelty lies in a simple but effective column-block ADMM for CLIME coupled with HPC-aware data layout and low-rank multiplies.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The ADMM derivation is standard and careful. The linearized subproblem yields elementwise soft-thresholding and box projection; the O(1/T) rates are consistent with known results for inexact/linearized ADMM (e.g., He & Yuan; Wang & Banerjee), with clear assumptions on η,ρ and λmax(C). The block-cyclic distribution and low-rank multiplies (C=AA′) are appropriate and well-justified.
- Empirics: Scalability studies are compelling (strong scaling and speedups with k>1). However, the numerical evaluation would benefit from more detail on accuracy and fairness: for synthetic data, report estimation error (‖Ω̂−Ω0‖F, operator norm, matrix L1) in addition to precision–recall; for real data, report objective values or stability metrics across methods. Provide parameter selection procedures (λ, ρ, η), stopping criteria, and per-iteration residuals versus wall-clock. Clarify memory footprints and I/O when p=10^6 (storing Ω̂ at trillion scale is nontrivial; is it streamed/written in blocks?).

Clarity
- The paper is well-organized and readable. Algorithm 1 is clear; the low-rank and sparsity accelerations are well-motivated. A few points need clarification:
  - Symmetrization/PD post-processing for CLIME (standard practice) and its effect on the reported solutions.
  - Guidance for choosing the column-block size k given cache sizes, process grid, and sparsity; an autotuning heuristic would be useful.
  - Details of the convergence theorems (assumptions, constants) and how they translate into practical stopping rules.

Originality
- The ADMM variant and O(1/T) rates per se are not novel, but the realization that CLIME’s structure enables purely elementwise ops plus matrix-matrix multiplies, and the consequent column-block design with block-cyclic distribution, is a useful algorithm–systems contribution. The “perturbed covariance/thresholding” angle is a nice justification for computational sparsification, though conceptually close to prior covariance thresholding (e.g., Mazumder & Hastie) and standard deviation bounds in CLIME theory.

Significance
- Estimating sparse precision matrices at million-scale is important. A robust, easy-to-implement distributed solver that relies on well-optimized BLAS3 operations is valuable for practitioners. The demonstrated scaling and portability (OpenMP/OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK) make this a practical contribution. While some speedups stem from CLIME’s simplicity and from standard HPC techniques, consolidating them into an end-to-end scalable solution with convergence guarantees advances the state of practice.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Comparisons and metrics:
  - Report time–accuracy tradeoffs: plot wall-clock versus estimation error and versus constraint violation for synthetic data.
  - Ensure fairness by harmonizing stopping tolerances across methods; in addition to matching sparsity, match support recovery/estimation error when comparing runtimes.
  - Include QUIC (non-DC) where feasible and discuss differences between solving GLasso vs CLIME (distinct objectives).
  - For real data, provide qualitative stability/edge overlap analyses under subsampling/bootstrapping.
- Parameters and implementation:
  - Describe how ρ and η are selected; consider adaptive schemes (e.g., residual balancing) and report sensitivity.
  - Provide a rule-of-thumb for k as a function of cache and n; include a brief autotuning experiment.
  - Detail memory usage and output handling for trillion-parameter cases; indicate whether full Ω̂ is materialized, compressed, or stored sparsely.
  - Break down time per step (matmul vs elementwise vs communication) and report communication volumes to quantify the benefit of block-cyclic distribution.
- Theory:
  - Clarify whether the rates are ergodic or non-ergodic and how the bound (11) is used in practice.
  - For the perturbation theorem, connect explicitly to thresholding Cij at O(√(log p/n)), and discuss empirical impact on accuracy and speed.

Pros
- Clear, scalable algorithm with simple primitives (soft/box and matmul).
- Strong scaling on both shared- and distributed-memory; thoughtful data distribution.
- Solid convergence analysis compatible with the inexact updates used.
- Addresses an important problem at unprecedented scale.

Cons
- Limited accuracy reporting and fairness controls in empirical comparisons.
- Novelty in optimization theory is modest; several systems techniques are standard.
- Practical details (parameter selection, symmetrization/PD handling, memory/I/O at trillion scale) need elaboration.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a practically impactful, well-engineered distributed solver for CLIME with convincing scalability and sufficient theoretical backing. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying implementation details would further improve the contribution.
