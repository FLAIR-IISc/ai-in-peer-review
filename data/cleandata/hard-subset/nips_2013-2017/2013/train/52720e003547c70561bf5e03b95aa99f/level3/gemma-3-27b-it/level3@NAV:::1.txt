## Review: Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

This paper tackles the challenging problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high-dimensional settings, focusing on scalability to datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The authors propose a variant of the CLIME estimator, termed CLIME-ADMM, coupled with a distributed framework that leverages column-block operations and block-cyclic data distribution. This is a relevant contribution given the increasing prevalence of high-dimensional data in various fields and the limitations of existing methods. Prior work in this area, such as the use of ADMM for CLIME [18] and parallelized methods like those in [6, 23], are well-cited and contextualized. The focus on column-block estimation and the empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapak are highlighted as novel aspects.

The strengths of the paper lie in its ambition and potential for impact. Addressing scalability in precision matrix estimation is crucial, and the proposed approach appears promising. The theoretical convergence analysis, while standard, provides a foundation for the algorithm's reliability. The experiments demonstrate substantial scalability, particularly in a distributed memory environment, showcasing the ability to handle extremely large datasets. The insight of leveraging low-rank structure (Section 3) to further enhance performance is a valuable addition.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The notation is indeed scattered across the initial sections, and a notation table is *essential* for clarity. The rationale behind the block-cyclic data distribution's guarantee of load balance and scalability is asserted, but lacks a clear explanation or supporting evidence. While empirical results are presented, they don't specifically validate the load balancing claim. Furthermore, the selection of the column block size *k* is ad hoc; a more principled approach, potentially guided by theoretical considerations or cache behavior, is needed. Critically, while the authors refer to a "distributed" framework, the communication mechanisms are not described; the current implementation appears to be primarily a *parallel* implementation rather than a true distributed algorithm. Finally, minor writing issues (e.g., "tunning" instead of "tuning") detract from the paper’s polish.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Demonstrates strong scalability in a distributed environment.
*   Presents a novel approach to CLIME estimation using column-block operations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in notation and explanation of load balancing.
*   Ad hoc selection of block size *k*.
*   Unclear distinction between parallel and distributed implementation.
*   Minor writing/typo issues.

**Overall:** I recommend *rejection* at this stage. While the work has merit, the identified weaknesses need to be addressed to ensure clarity, rigor, and a more compelling presentation of the algorithm's contributions. Addressing the clarity concerns around distributed vs parallel computing is particularly critical. I believe with revisions addressing these points, the paper could be a valuable addition to the conference.
