This paper presents an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for solving the CLIME estimator for sparse precision matrix estimation, along with a scalable distributed framework for computations. The authors demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to handle problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, scaling to hundreds of cores. The key innovation lies in solving CLIME in column-blocks and leveraging efficient matrix-matrix multiplications, optimized for cache utilization.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of recent advances in both the statistical theory of precision matrix estimation and computationally efficient optimization methods. The authors correctly situate their contribution within this context, referencing key papers such as [3, 4, 13, 14, 18, 19, 21, 25, 28]. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods in handling ultra-large-scale problems and proposes a solution specifically tailored to address this challenge.  The theoretical analysis establishing convergence rates (O(1/T)) is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The ADMM derivation appears standard and well-supported by existing literature (e.g., [2, 12, 27]). The convergence analysis is a positive aspect, demonstrating a theoretical understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
* **Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its demonstrated scalability.  The ability to handle problems with a million dimensions and a trillion parameters on 400 cores is impressive and addresses a significant bottleneck in the field. The use of column-block decomposition and optimized matrix multiplication clearly contributes to this scalability.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are generally easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with the CLIME estimator or ADMM.
* **Originality:** While CLIME itself is not novel, the specific application of inexact ADMM in a column-block formulation, coupled with the efficient distributed computation framework leveraging block cyclic data distribution, represents a significant contribution.  The focus on practical considerations like cache optimization is a particularly valuable aspect.
* **Significance:**  Sparse precision matrix estimation is a crucial problem in many areas, including bioinformatics, finance, and signal processing.  A scalable and robust algorithm like the one presented here is likely to be useful to a wide range of researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated performance on large-scale datasets is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Detail:** The experimental section lacks some detail. While the authors compare their method to several baselines (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC), the reported results focus primarily on runtime.  It would be beneficial to include metrics related to the *accuracy* of the estimated precision matrices (e.g., precision and recall of the estimated graph structure, or a measure of the error in the estimated covariance matrix). Providing the parameters used for each method during the comparisons would improve reproducibility. 
* **Incremental Gain:** The authors acknowledge that the scalability gains largely stem from the inherent structure of CLIME. While this is true, it would be helpful to further analyze the performance improvements attributable *specifically* to the algorithmic innovations (inexact ADMM, column-block decomposition, block cyclic distribution).  Perhaps ablation studies could isolate the impact of each component.
* **Limited Comparison Depth:** The comparisons to existing methods are somewhat superficial. A deeper investigation into the strengths and weaknesses of each method, beyond just runtime, would be valuable. For instance, it's mentioned that Tiger achieves better recall, but the trade-offs associated with this performance aren't fully explored.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical need for scalable sparse precision matrix estimation.
* Provides a technically sound and well-analyzed algorithm.
* Demonstrates impressive scalability on large-scale datasets.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.
* Offers a practical contribution with potential for wide impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Experimental evaluation lacks detailed accuracy metrics.
* Incremental gains from the proposed innovations could be better quantified.
* Comparisons to existing methods could be more in-depth.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors successfully address a challenging problem and present a practical, scalable solution. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the demonstrated performance and theoretical analysis are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding experimental detail and a more thorough analysis of the algorithmic contributions. Specifically, I recommend including metrics beyond runtime to evaluate the accuracy of the estimated precision matrices.
