

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scalable sparse precision matrix estimation in ultra-high dimensions, proposing a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm that scales to millions of variables and trillions of parameters. The work builds on the CLIME estimator [3], which has strong statistical guarantees but has been limited to moderate-scale problems due to memory constraints. The authors present an inexact ADMM variant that solves the problem column-blockwise rather than column-by-column, leveraging block-cyclic distribution for load balancing and efficient memory usage. The theoretical contribution includes O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions, while the experimental results demonstrate near-linear scaling with up to 400 cores, solving a 1 million-dimensional problem in 11 hours.

The paper's primary strength lies in its significant scalability breakthrough for precision matrix estimation, a problem with quadratic parameter growth (p² for p dimensions). The column-block approach is novel in this context, as prior methods like CLIME [3], Tiger [19], and DC-QUIC [13] either solve column-by-column or assume block-diagonal structures. The distributed framework using block-cyclic distribution is well-motivated to minimize cache misses and improve memory hierarchy utilization, though the empirical justification for this choice is limited. The authors' implementation using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK for distributed memory and OpenMP for shared memory demonstrates practical feasibility, with compelling results showing CLIME-ADMM outperforming competitors on both synthetic and real datasets (Leukemia, Climate) for high-dimensional problems.

The work makes a meaningful contribution to the growing literature on scalable machine learning [2, 6, 22, 23]. While related to distributed ADMM frameworks [9, 23], the authors' focus on precision matrix estimation's specific structure (low-rank sample covariance, sparse iterates) and column-block processing distinguishes their approach. The theoretical analysis of convergence rates is solid and builds on established results [12, 26], though the paper could better clarify how their inexact ADMM differs from existing variants.

Several weaknesses merit attention. The notation is inconsistent and scattered throughout the paper, with key parameters like ρ and η appearing without definition (e.g., in Algorithm 1). A dedicated notation table would significantly improve clarity. The block-cyclic distribution is presented as a key innovation but lacks empirical evidence demonstrating its superiority over simpler distributions; the authors should provide concrete comparisons. The column block size k is chosen ad hoc without theoretical guidance or empirical exploration of its impact on performance, which is a notable gap given how k affects both computation and communication. The paper also overstates the "distributed" nature of the framework—message passing mechanisms are not described, and the algorithm appears more like a parallel implementation than a true distributed algorithm.

Minor issues include typos ("tunning" instead of "tuning," "summerized" instead of "summarized") and some repetitive explanations. The significance of the work is well-established, with clear applications in genomics and climate science where million-dimensional problems arise. The results on real datasets (Table 1) convincingly demonstrate CLIME-ADMM's superiority over competitors for high-dimensional problems, though the comparison with Tiger on small datasets could be more nuanced.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution to scalable precision matrix estimation with strong experimental results. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the scalability claims are well-supported by empirical evidence. However, the lack of notation clarity, insufficient justification for key design choices (block-cyclic distribution, block size), and some technical oversights in the distributed framework presentation limit its impact. The work would benefit from a more thorough exploration of the theoretical and practical implications of block size selection, and a clearer distinction between parallel and distributed aspects of the implementation.

Pro: Significant scalability breakthrough for ultra-high-dimensional precision matrix estimation; strong empirical results demonstrating linear speedup with cores; novel column-block approach to CLIME; well-motivated theoretical convergence analysis.

Con: Inconsistent notation and lack of notation table; insufficient justification for block-cyclic distribution; ad hoc choice of column block size k without theoretical guidance; overstatement of distributed nature of the framework; minor writing issues.
