

This paper proposes an inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME (Constrained L1 Minimization for Estimating sparse precision matrices), establishing O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions. The authors develop a distributed framework that scales to millions of dimensions using column-block processing, leveraging matrix-matrix multiplications that parallelize effectively. The work builds on prior ADMM-based approaches for precision matrix estimation [18, 23, 26] but extends them to ultra-large-scale settings through column-block processing and block cyclic data distribution.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with sound theoretical analysis and well-organized presentation. The derivation of inexact ADMM for CLIME follows standard approaches (citing [27, 12]), and the convergence proofs are properly established. The distributed framework is novel in its evaluation across both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, with empirical results showing near-linear scalability on up to 400 cores for million-dimensional problems.

Key strengths include the clear presentation of the algorithm, the practical implementation details for parallelization, and the compelling demonstration of scalability on large problems (estimating a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores). The paper makes a significant contribution to the growing literature on distributed optimization for large-scale statistical problems [2, 6, 9, 22].

However, the numerical experiments would benefit from more detail on the accuracy achieved by each solver (beyond just runtime comparisons), which would strengthen the evaluation. The scalability gains largely stem from CLIME's inherent structure rather than novel algorithmic innovations, which somewhat limits the originality claim.

Overall, this is a well-executed, technically sound contribution to large-scale distributed optimization for precision matrix estimation. While the core algorithmic innovation is modest, the practical implementation and scalability results are valuable for practitioners. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating effective scaling to previously unattainable problem sizes.

Pro: Strong experimental validation, clear presentation, practical contribution to large-scale distributed optimization
Con: Limited algorithmic novelty, insufficient accuracy metrics in experiments
