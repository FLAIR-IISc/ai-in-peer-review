This paper proposes a scalable variant of the Constrained L1-minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) algorithm, dubbed CLIME-ADMM, and a distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors present an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for CLIME, which they claim can scale to millions of dimensions and utilize hundreds of cores. They also develop a large-scale distributed framework for the computations, which can be applied to both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed algorithm and framework. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of the objective function and the residuals of optimality conditions, as well as experimental results comparing their algorithm with existing methods.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to scale to ultra-high-dimensional problems, which is a significant challenge in precision matrix estimation. The authors demonstrate that their algorithm can estimate a precision matrix with one million dimensions and one trillion parameters in 11 hours using 400 cores.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The notation is scattered across sections 1-3, and a notation table would be helpful in clarifying the meaning of symbols such as ρ and η. Additionally, the block-cyclic data distribution method is interesting, but it is unclear why it guarantees load balance or scalability, and no empirical evidence is provided to support this claim.

Furthermore, the column block size k is chosen ad hoc, and it would be beneficial to provide intuition or theoretical guidance for selecting k. The algorithm may be better characterized as a parallel implementation rather than a distributed algorithm, as message passing/communication mechanisms are not described.

In terms of novelty, the paper highlights the estimation of the precision matrix by column blocks (instead of column-by-column) and the empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapak.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of precision matrix estimation, and its scalability and efficiency make it a valuable tool for large-scale data analysis.
