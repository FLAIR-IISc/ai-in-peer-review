

This paper addresses the critical challenge of sparse precision matrix estimation at massive scale (millions of variables, trillions of parameters), building upon the CLIME estimator's theoretical advantages. The authors propose a novel block-wise CLIME-ADMM algorithm that solves the problem column-block-wise rather than column-by-column, leveraging sparsity and low-rank structure to dramatically reduce computational complexity. The theoretical contribution establishes O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective value and optimality residuals, while the implementation demonstrates remarkable scalability across shared- and distributed-memory architectures.

The paper excels in multiple dimensions. The problem is well-motivated and addresses a significant gap in ultra-high-dimensional statistics, where existing methods fail due to memory constraints and poor parallelization. The algorithmic innovation is substantial, with the block-wise approach enabling efficient matrix-matrix multiplications that outperform repeated matrix-vector operations. The implementation details are thorough, with careful consideration of load balancing through block cyclic distribution. Experimental results are compelling, showing CLIME-ADMM estimates a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, with near-linear speedup across architectures. The presentation is clear, with well-structured literature connections that distinguish this work from prior distributed optimization approaches.

The main concern is the lack of source code availability, which significantly impacts reproducibility and community adoption. While the paper's technical contributions are strong, the recommendation to make the tool publicly available is essential for this work to have maximum impact. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with rigorous theory and comprehensive experiments (Quality), clearly written with excellent organization (Clarity), presents a novel approach to a well-known problem (Originality), and addresses an important scalability challenge with demonstrable impact (Significance).

The paper makes a significant contribution to large-scale statistical learning and would be valuable to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors making their implementation publicly available.
