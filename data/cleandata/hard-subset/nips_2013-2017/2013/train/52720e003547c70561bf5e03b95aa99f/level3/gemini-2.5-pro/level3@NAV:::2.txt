This paper proposes a scalable framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The core of the contribution is an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm with a proven O(1/T) convergence rate. The authors develop and thoroughly evaluate implementations for both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems, demonstrating scalability up to a million dimensions. This work builds upon the established CLIME estimator and standard ADMM frameworks, positioning itself as a practical solution for ultra-large-scale problems, compared against methods like DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically sound, well-organized, and clearly presented. Its most significant contribution is successfully tackling a challenging and important problem: sparse precision matrix estimation at a massive scale. The engineering effort is commendable, and the results showing near-linear scalability on hundreds of cores are impressive. A key strength is the detailed and insightful evaluation of the algorithm on both shared- and distributed-memory architectures. This analysis of performance trade-offs, including the use of block cyclic data distribution to ensure load balancing, is a valuable and original contribution that will be useful for practitioners. The simplicity of the final algorithm, relying primarily on element-wise operations and matrix multiplications, makes it robust and broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary limitation of this work is its algorithmic novelty. The derivation of an inexact ADMM for the CLIME objective is a straightforward application of well-known optimization techniques. The remarkable scalability demonstrated is less a feature of a novel algorithm and more an exploitation of pre-existing properties: the inherent column-wise separability of the CLIME problem and the well-established efficiency of parallel matrix-matrix multiplication. While a valuable engineering contribution, it does not represent a fundamental advance in optimization methodology. Additionally, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. The paper focuses heavily on runtime but provides insufficient detail on the quality of the resulting estimates. For the speed comparisons to be fully convincing, the authors should report the accuracy achieved by each solver (e.g., final objective value or error against a ground truth), ensuring that the proposed method is not just faster but also converges to a solution of comparable quality. More details about the experimental setup would also aid reproducibility.
