This paper addresses the important problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm tailored for CLIME, coupled with a distributed computing framework capable of scaling to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The work is technically sound and leverages the inherent structure of CLIME – specifically its componentwise subproblem solves and potential for parallel matrix-matrix multiplications – to achieve significant scalability.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in sparse precision estimation (e.g., [28, 3, 19]) and distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 26, 6, 23]). It correctly identifies the limitations of existing methods in handling ultra-large datasets and positions itself as a contribution towards scaling machine learning algorithms to "Big Data," a trend highlighted in recent NIPS proceedings (e.g., [29, 22]). The use of inexact ADMM, referencing established literature [27, 12], is a standard and appropriate technique. The core innovation lies in the efficient parallelization achieved through column-block ADMM and a carefully designed block-cyclic data distribution scheme, capitalizing on the structure of CLIME and effective matrix multiplication implementations [15, 5, 11].

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME appears correct and well-justified, referencing relevant theoretical work. Theorems 1 and 2 provide convergence guarantees.
* **Scalability:** The primary strength is the demonstrated scalability of the algorithm to extremely large datasets (millions of dimensions) using a distributed memory architecture. The reported ability to estimate a one million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores is significant.
* **Originality:** While ADMM has been used for CLIME previously [18], the combination of inexact ADMM with column-block solves and the block-cyclic data distribution strategy for distributed computation is a novel and effective approach. The evaluation in both shared- and distributed-memory environments is also interesting and original.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. The algorithmic details are presented clearly, and the notation is consistent.
* **Leveraging CLIME Structure:** The authors effectively exploit the inherent structure of CLIME to enable efficient parallelization, highlighting the importance of algorithm-problem alignment.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Detail:** The numerical experiments lack sufficient detail. While comparisons are made against other methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC), the paper does not consistently report the *accuracy* achieved by each solver. For example, what sparsity level was recovered, and how was this evaluated?  Simply stating that CLIME-ADMM is faster is insufficient; demonstrating comparable or superior accuracy is crucial. The tuning parameters for the comparison algorithms should also be specified.
* **Limited Comparison Scope:** The comparison is primarily focused on runtime. A more thorough comparison of solution quality (e.g., precision/recall of graph structure recovery) across different methods and parameter settings would strengthen the results.
* **Perturbation Discussion:** While Theorem 3 regarding perturbing the sample covariance matrix is interesting, its practical implications are not fully explored.  How does this perturbation affect the accuracy of the estimation? What guidelines are there for choosing the perturbation level?
* **Block Size Selection:** The discussion of block size selection (pb x nb x kb) could be more detailed. What are the practical considerations for choosing these parameters in different scenarios?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach to scalable sparse precision estimation.
* Demonstrates significant scalability through experiments on large datasets.
* Well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of detailed reporting on solution accuracy in the experiments.
* Limited scope of comparison with other methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale sparse precision estimation. The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm and distributed framework offer a practical and scalable solution to a challenging problem. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the demonstrated scalability and technical soundness of the work warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses related to experimental detail by providing more comprehensive results on solution accuracy and clarifying the impact of the perturbation strategy.
