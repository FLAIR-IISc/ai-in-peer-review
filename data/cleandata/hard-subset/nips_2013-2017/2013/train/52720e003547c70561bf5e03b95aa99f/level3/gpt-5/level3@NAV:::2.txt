This paper tackles large-scale sparse precision estimation via the CLIME estimator by proposing an inexact ADMM scheme with O(1/T) convergence and a carefully engineered shared-memory and distributed-memory implementation based on column-block updates and block-cyclic data distribution. The method reduces each ADMM iteration to elementwise operations and matrix-matrix multiplications; the latter dominate cost but are known to parallelize and cache-optimize well. The authors position the work against CLIME (Cai et al.), QUIC/DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al., NIPS), and distributed ADMM frameworks (Boyd et al.; GPBS), emphasizing that CLIME’s separability enables independent column-block solves and highly parallel GEMMs. Empirically, they compare to DC-QUIC, TIGER, and CLIME-Flare, and demonstrate near-linear scaling on hundreds of cores, including a 10^6-dimensional run.

Quality: The algorithmic derivation is standard but correct—the inexact ADMM linearization/proximalization is textbook and the O(1/T) rate is consistent with known results. The mapping of CLIME to highly parallelizable kernels is sound, and the shared vs. distributed implementations are thoughtfully designed. However, the experimental section lacks critical details for scientific validation: stopping criteria, target accuracy/KKT residuals for each solver, statistical accuracy at matched regularization/graph sparsity, λ selection, and communication/memory footprints. Much of the observed scalability derives from CLIME’s structure and the use of GEMMs rather than algorithmic advances per se.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and readable; algorithmic steps and systems design choices (column-blocking, block-cyclic distribution) are clearly explained.

Originality: While the inexact ADMM for CLIME is not novel, the end-to-end evaluation of ADMM for CLIME across shared and distributed settings with block-cyclic distribution and column-blocking is original and of practical interest.

Significance: Solving ultra-large sparse precision problems is important. A simple, robust distributed solver for CLIME is likely to be used by practitioners. The main incremental value is engineering and scalability rather than new optimization theory.

Suggestions: Report accuracy per method (precision/recall at matched FPR; KKT residuals), exact stopping tolerances, λ-selection protocol, and wall-clock vs. accuracy trade-offs. Include strong/weak scaling, communication volumes, and memory usage. Clarify handling of symmetry/positive definiteness post-CLIME and the effect of covariance “perturbations” on statistical performance. Consider comparisons at matched accuracy to QUIC/Graphical Lasso baselines.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong systems contribution with clear scalability on both architectures
- Technically sound, simple algorithm with reproducible building blocks
- Important problem; practical impact likely

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited novelty in optimization
- Insufficient experimental detail on accuracy and fairness of comparisons
- Scalability benefits largely from CLIME structure and GEMM, not new methods

Overall: Weak accept.
