This paper proposes a scalable variant of CLIME for sparse precision estimation that combines a linearized (inexact) ADMM scheme with a column-block formulation and a distributed implementation using block-cyclic data distribution. The optimization reformulates CLIME with a split variable, uses soft-thresholding and box projections as elementwise updates, and replaces the heavy X-update by a proximal-linearized step, yielding two matrix multiplications per iteration. The authors establish O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality and KKT residuals under η ≥ ρ·λmax(C)^2, and show how (i) iterate sparsity and (ii) low-sample low-rank structure (C = AA^T, p ≫ n) reduce cost. The distributed framework (OpenMP/OpenMPI + ScaLAPACK) solves multiple columns jointly (blocks of size k), claims better cache/memory hierarchy use, and employs block-cyclic distribution. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate near-linear speedups and a 1M-dimensional run on 400 cores in ~11 hours.

Relation to prior work: QUIC/DC-QUIC (NIPS’11/’13) target the log-det (graphical lasso) objective and exploit block structure; prior CLIME solvers have used interior-point or ADMM column-by-column. The linearized ADMM and O(1/T) guarantees follow known analyses (e.g., He & Yuan), here specialized to CLIME. The block-cyclic distribution connects to classical dense linear algebra (ScaLAPACK); the authors position their approach relative to parameter servers, GraphLab/GPBS, and distributed ADMM.

Quality
- Theory: The non-ergodic O(1/T) rate and residual bounds are reasonable for linearized ADMM; assumptions and constants (η ≥ ρ·λmax(C)^2) are explicit. The perturbation result (thresholding C while preserving deviation bounds) is interesting but not integrated into practice or experiments; more guidance on constants would improve usefulness.
- Algorithmic soundness: The column-block formulation is faithful to CLIME’s column separability and enables BLAS-3 kernels. Practical issues remain under-specified: (i) choice and adaptation of ρ and η when λmax(C) is unknown; (ii) stopping criteria/tolerances; (iii) symmetry/post-processing of CLIME solutions (usual symmetrization step is not discussed).
- Empirics: Strong scaling results and very large-scale runs are convincing. Accuracy comparisons are limited (precision–recall on synthetics only); no convergence/optimality traces, constraint residuals, or estimation errors are reported. The block-cyclic claim would benefit from ablations against alternative layouts.

Clarity
- The presentation would benefit from a consolidated notation table. Symbols such as ρ and η appear before being defined; some norms and operators are defined multiple times across sections.
- The distributed algorithm description reads more like a high-level architecture; message-passing schedules, collectives used (e.g., SUMMA-style GEMM), and communication/computation breakdowns are not described.
- Minor typos: “tunning/tunning-free” → “tuning/tuning-free”; “summerized” → “summarized”; “achetectures” → “architectures”; “reguarlized” → “regularized”.

Originality
- Methodologically, the linearized ADMM formulation and its convergence for CLIME are incremental relative to known results; the block-columnization is a pragmatic but natural extension. The block-cyclic distributed realization at this scale, and the practical exploitation of low-rank structure without forming C, are the most original aspects. The perturbation theorem is a nice observation, albeit not operationalized.

Significance
- The system demonstrates that CLIME can be pushed to truly massive scales with near-linear speedups, which is valuable for practitioners needing sparse inverse covariance estimation beyond the reach of existing solvers. The approach (elementwise ops + GEMM, block-cyclic distribution) is reusable for other constrained problems amenable to linearized ADMM. The application-level impact would be stronger with real large-scale case studies and statistical accuracy assessments.

Specific comments and questions
- k selection: Provide an empirical or theoretical guideline (e.g., choose k so that X, W fit in L2 cache or so that GEMM reaches peak; autotune via microbenchmarks). How sensitive are speedups to k across sparsity patterns?
- Communication: Quantify communication volume/latency per iteration, overlap with computation, and strong/weak scaling efficiency. Compare block-cyclic vs large-block vs random distributions.
- Parameters and stopping: How are ρ and η chosen in practice without λmax(C)? Are they adapted during iterations? Report stopping thresholds and typical iteration counts.
- Accuracy: Include KKT residuals vs time, constraint violation ‖CX − Z‖∞, and estimation errors (Frobenius/L1/spectral) on synthetics; on real data, report stability across λ and symmetrization details.
- Symmetry/PD: Clarify post-processing (symmetrization, optional PD projection) used for CLIME outputs, especially when solving in blocks.
- Code/data: Consider releasing code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Demonstrates impressive scalability (to p = 10^6) with clear engineering insights and near-linear speedups.
- Simple, robust algorithm using only elementwise ops and matrix multiplies; leverages low-rank structure to avoid forming C.
- Convergence guarantees for the proposed linearized ADMM variant tailored to CLIME.
- Broadly applicable distributed framework (OpenMP/MPI + ScaLAPACK) likely useful beyond this problem.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited clarity on distributed communication and load balancing beyond high-level block-cyclic claims; no ablation evidence.
- Choice of column block size k and ADMM parameters lacks guidance; no auto-tuning.
- Empirical evaluation under-reports accuracy and optimality metrics; perturbation/sparsification theorem not validated experimentally.
- Writing/notation issues that impede readability.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the distributed communication design, adding guidance and sensitivity analyses for k and parameters, and expanding the accuracy/convergence reporting. The scalability contribution is strong and of interest to the NIPS community.
