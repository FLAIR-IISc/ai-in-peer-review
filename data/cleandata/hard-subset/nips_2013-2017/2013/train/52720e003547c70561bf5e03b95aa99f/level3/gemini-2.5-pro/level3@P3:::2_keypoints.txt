- Problem and goal: scalable, distributed algorithm for CLIME to estimate high-dimensional sparse precision matrices.  
- Main contribution: an inexact ADMM that solves CLIME in column-blocks, converting core work to matrix-matrix multiplications for parallelism.  
- Theoretical result: proves an O(1/T) convergence rate for the proposed algorithm.  
- Empirical scale: extensive experiments on shared- and distributed-memory systems showing scalability to 10^6 dimensions and ~10^12 parameters.  
- Framing: emphasizes constrained optimization and classical HPC parallelism rather than MapReduce-style frameworks.  
- Strength — significance: addresses a fundamental high-dimensional statistics problem and enables solving problems at unprecedented scale.  
- Strength — quality and clarity: technically sound, well-organized, clearly written with correct derivation and clear parallelization strategy.  
- Strength — practical originality: novelty lies in engineering and large-scale evaluation, especially exploiting column-block decomposition and matrix-matrix efficiency.  
- Strength — practitioner value: useful empirical comparison of shared- vs distributed-memory performance with practical lessons.  
- Weakness — algorithmic novelty: algorithm is largely a standard application of inexact ADMM; novelty is mainly systems/engineering.  
- Weakness — experimental detail: comparisons lack crucial solution-quality metrics (final objective and max constraint violation), so runtime speedups may be misleading.  
- Pros for acceptance: tackles an important problem at scale, presents a practical scalable method, thorough experiments, and valuable parallelization study.  
- Cons against acceptance: modest core methodological novelty and incomplete experimental reporting on accuracy.  
- Recommendation: accept with revision requested to report solution-quality metrics for fair runtime comparisons.
