This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multiplier (ADMM) algorithm for the Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) estimator, which has several desirable theoretical properties. The algorithm is designed to scale to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, using hundreds of cores. The authors establish rates of convergence for both the objective and optimality conditions, and evaluate their algorithm on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

The paper is technically sound, and the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard, following the approach of [26]. The algorithm's structure allows for componentwise subproblem solves, making it highly parallelizable. The main cost is matrix-matrix multiplication, which is well-known to parallelize effectively.

The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their algorithm, comparing it to state-of-the-art methods such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare. The experimental results show that their algorithm is substantially more scalable than existing methods and scales almost linearly with the number of cores.

The paper is well-organized and clear, making it easy to follow. The evaluation of ADMM in shared- and distributed-memory environments is interesting and original. Solving large-scale sparse precision estimation is an important problem, and a simple, robust distributed algorithm is useful.

However, the numerical experiment setup could be improved with more detail, such as reporting the accuracy achieved by each solver. Additionally, the scalability gains largely stem from CLIME's simple structure, which may not be representative of more complex problems.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, specifically the use of inexact ADMM for CLIME. The authors adequately reference related work and clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to solve large-scale sparse precision estimation problems efficiently. The results have important implications for various applications, including machine learning and data analysis. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to solving these problems.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field. The strengths of the paper include its technical soundness, originality, and significance. The weaknesses are minor and include the need for more detail in the numerical experiment setup.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and efficient algorithm for solving large-scale sparse precision estimation problems.
* The algorithm is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of its performance.
* The paper is well-organized and clear, making it easy to follow.
* The results have important implications for various applications, including machine learning and data analysis.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The numerical experiment setup could be improved with more detail.
* The scalability gains largely stem from CLIME's simple structure, which may not be representative of more complex problems.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the need for more detail in the numerical experiment setup.
