## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper tackles the critical problem of estimating sparse precision matrices at a massive scale – a challenge increasingly relevant with the rise of high-dimensional data. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, recognizing its column-wise solvability, and propose a novel block-wise solver coupled with an inexact ADMM approach. This is a strong contribution, as existing methods struggle with the computational demands of millions of variables and trillions of parameters. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly presented. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the existing literature on precision matrix estimation and scalable optimization, referencing key papers in both areas (e.g., [3, 13, 21, 26]).  The theoretical results establishing convergence rates are a significant strength, lending rigor to the proposed algorithm. Furthermore, the implementation details, emphasizing scalability through shared and distributed memory systems, are compelling. The experimental results, both on synthetic and real datasets, demonstrate substantial performance gains compared to state-of-the-art methods, with detailed scalability analysis regarding block sizes and core counts. The insights into leveraging sparsity and low-rank structure to reduce computation are also valuable.

A notable strength lies in the authors’ consideration of practical implementation details, such as block cyclic data distribution to address load imbalance in the distributed setting.  The theoretical analysis of perturbation strategies to induce sparsity in the sample covariance matrix is also a clever addition. 

However, the paper’s overall value is significantly contingent on the release of the source code.  The complexity of the implementation and the challenges in reproducing results for such a large-scale problem necessitate open access to the tool for verification and broader adoption. Without it, the reported performance gains remain difficult to independently assess. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial problem in high-dimensional statistics with a practical, scalable solution.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with established convergence rates.
*   Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Clear, well-written presentation with good literature connections.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of publicly available source code significantly limits reproducibility and impact.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors making the source code publicly available. This would elevate the paper from a promising research contribution to a truly impactful tool for the community. The quality of the work is high, and the potential benefits are considerable, but accessibility is paramount for a project of this scale and complexity.
