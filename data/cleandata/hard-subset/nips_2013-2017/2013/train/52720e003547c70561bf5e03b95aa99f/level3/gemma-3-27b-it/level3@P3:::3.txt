This paper addresses the crucial problem of sparse precision matrix estimation at massive scales, tackling datasets with millions of variables and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, leveraging its inherent column-wise solvability, and propose a block-wise alternative to improve computational efficiency. Their key algorithmic contribution lies in a novel block-wise solver coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers (ADMM). Rigorous theoretical analysis establishes convergence rates, and the work demonstrates an impressive scalable parallel implementation for both shared- and distributed-memory systems. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real data validate the strong performance and scalability of the proposed approach, showcasing near-linear speedup with increasing core counts. The paper is exceptionally well-written, presenting a clear statement of novelty, solid connections to relevant literature, and a balanced approach to technical details and theoretical exposition.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in sparse precision matrix estimation, referencing key papers like Cai et al. (2013), Yuan (2013), and Ravikumar et al. (2011) which established the theoretical foundations and initial algorithmic approaches. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms (Dean et al., 2012; Low et al., 2012), acknowledging efforts such as parameter servers and graph projection block splitting.  They effectively distinguish their block-wise approach from existing methods, highlighting its advantages in exploiting sparsity and low-rank structure. The paper acknowledges the related work on ADMM-based distributed optimization (Parikh & Boyd, 2013), and appropriately highlights how their framework differs by addressing global constraints instead of local ones.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The problem is both important and challenging, representing a significant bottleneck in the analysis of high-dimensional data. The proposed solution is well-motivated and technically sound, with a clear and detailed algorithmic description. The theoretical results, particularly the established convergence rates, provide a strong foundation for the method's validity. The emphasis on scalability and the detailed analysis of speedups across block sizes and core counts are crucial for demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.  The experiments are comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real datasets, and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of CLIME-ADMM compared to existing algorithms like Tiger, DC-QUIC, and standard CLIME implementations. The writing quality is exceptional, making the complex technical details accessible and easy to understand. The paper thoroughly acknowledges related work and clearly articulates its contributions.

However, the paper’s practical impact hinges critically on the availability of the implemented code. While the experimental results are compelling, reproducing them and applying the method to new datasets would be significantly hampered without access to the software. The authors themselves acknowledge this limitation in their conclusion, stating that the paper’s value depends on releasing the source code.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant Scalability:** The paper presents a demonstrably scalable algorithm capable of handling datasets far beyond the reach of existing methods.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Rigorous convergence analysis supports the validity and reliability of the proposed approach.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and understandable.
* **Novel Contribution:** The block-wise ADMM solver and the distributed framework represent a significant advancement in the field.
* **Importance of the Problem:** Sparse precision matrix estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of Code Release:**  The absence of publicly available code severely limits the reproducibility and practical impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a high-quality scientific contribution to the field. The technical soundness, theoretical rigor, and experimental validation are all excellent. However, the lack of publicly available code is a significant concern.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors making the source code available to the community.  Without this commitment, the paper, while technically excellent, would have limited practical impact and would not fully realize its potential contribution to the field.  The program committee should strongly encourage the authors to release the code as a condition of publication.
