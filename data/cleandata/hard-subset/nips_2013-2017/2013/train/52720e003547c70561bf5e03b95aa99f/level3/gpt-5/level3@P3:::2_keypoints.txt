- Proposes an inexact (linearized) ADMM algorithm for CLIME with shared- and distributed-memory implementations exploiting column-blocks and block-cyclic distribution.  
- Proves O(1/T) ergodic convergence for objective suboptimality and constraint residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2.  
- Core computation reduces to matrix–matrix multiplies (CX, CY) plus elementwise soft-thresholding and boxing.  
- Exploits sparsity/truncation of covariance and low-rank factorization C = A A^T to lower per-iteration cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k) when n ≪ p.  
- Empirically compares against DC-QUIC, TIGER, and CLIME-Flare and demonstrates strong shared- and distributed-memory scaling, including p = 10^6 on 400 cores.  
- Theory is standard for linearized/inexact ADMM and the O(1/T) rate is consistent with prior work.  
- Step-size condition is reasonable but practitioners need guidance on estimating λ_max(C) at very large p.  
- Column-block decomposition and low-rank matmul implementation are correct and well motivated.  
- Mapping CLIME to BLAS-3 matmuls and elementwise ops is clean and highly parallelizable.  
- Use of block-cyclic distribution is appropriate for load balancing and cache efficiency.  
- Scaling experiments are convincing on throughput and weak/strong scaling.  
- Experimental evaluation lacks crucial reproducibility and scientific-comparison details (stopping criteria, primal/dual residuals, and time-to-accuracy curves).  
- Accuracy comparisons are missing: report estimation errors (Frobenius, operator norms) and edge-level metrics at matched accuracy/sparsity.  
- Real-data experiments report only time-to-sparsity without accuracy or stability proxies.  
- Paper is well written and organized; pseudocode and complexity discussion are clear.  
- Reproducibility needs: exact stopping rules, tolerances, λ selection, hardware/I/O details for the large run, and block-size selection guidance.  
- Novelty is mainly in engineering and scale (column-block ADMM, low-rank realization, distributed pipeline), not algorithmic theory.  
- Significance is high: practical, scalable system for very large sparse precision estimation is valuable to practitioners.  
- Suggest reporting per-solver convergence tolerances, objective values, and time-to-target-residual curves.  
- Suggest clarifying λ selection method, symmetrization/postprocessing, practical recipes for η and ρ, and whether continuation/over-relaxation was used.  
- Recommend adding a computation vs communication model to explain scaling deviations and sensitivity analyses for block sizes and low-rank truncation.  
- Ask for empirical quantification of truncation/low-rank effects on runtime and accuracy, and memory/I/O footprints for the 10^6 run.  
- Pros: sound convergence guarantees, clean BLAS-3 mapping, excellent parallel scalability, clear exposition, and strong practical impact.  
- Cons: modest algorithmic novelty, experiments emphasize speed over matched-accuracy comparisons, fairness across different objectives is unclear, and limited hyperparameter/communication guidance.  
- Recommendation: lean accept, conditional on strengthening experimental accuracy/convergence details and clarifying parameter choices.
