- Proposes an inexact ADMM algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) for sparse precision matrix estimation, targeting scalable distributed computation with proven convergence and large-scale experiments.  
- Demonstrates nearly linear scalability across cores and the ability to handle problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters.  
- Builds on prior sparse precision estimation and scaling ML literature; justifies CLIME choice via its LP formulation and column-wise separability.  
- Algorithm derivation is technically sound and leverages CLIME’s separable structure to enable parallelism.  
- Identifies matrix-matrix multiplication as the main computational bottleneck and introduces column-block solving to enable efficient parallel computation.  
- Proposes a block cyclic distribution scheme to mitigate load imbalance in distributed settings.  
- Provides convergence analysis with O(1/T) rates.  
- Experimental evaluation convincingly shows runtime scalability on shared- and distributed-memory architectures.  
- Fails to sufficiently discuss practical implications of the convergence rate.  
- Experimental section lacks accuracy/solution-quality metrics (e.g., true/false positive rates) comparing CLIME-ADMM to baselines.  
- Insufficient comparison and discussion of parameter tuning requirements and sensitivity across methods (CLIME, DC-QUIC, Tiger).  
- The covariance perturbation discussion (Section 3.1) is underdeveloped and loosely integrated with implementation details.  
- The “linear speedup” claim is inadequately substantiated; needs plots comparing observed vs ideal speedup.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important scaling problem, offers a novel distributed framework with theoretical guarantees, strong scalability, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks detailed experimental accuracy analysis, has underdeveloped methodological discussion, and makes an unqualified speedup claim.  
- Overall assessment: solid, practically useful contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen experiments and clarify speedup/perturbation points.
