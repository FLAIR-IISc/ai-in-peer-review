This paper presents a scalable algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, and a distributed framework for estimating sparse precision matrices, a crucial task in high-dimensional statistics. The authors address a significant challenge – applying the statistically desirable CLIME estimator to problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters – by leveraging an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) and parallelizing computations across hundreds of cores. The work is positioned within the growing area of scaling machine learning algorithms to “Big Data” and provides empirical results demonstrating substantial scalability improvements over existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated, outlining the increasing need for scalable precision matrix estimation methods due to the quadratic growth of parameters with dimensionality. The CLIME estimator is a sensible choice, given its theoretical guarantees and inherent computational advantages. The core idea of solving the problem in column blocks, coupled with block-cyclic data distribution, is a novel approach to exploit matrix multiplication efficiency and achieve load balance. The authors correctly identify existing limitations in current solvers, namely their inability to handle datasets exceeding single-machine memory. 

The paper's strengths lie in its practical focus and demonstration of scalability. The experimental results, particularly the ability to estimate a one-million-dimension precision matrix in 11 hours using 400 cores, are impressive. Comparisons with established methods like CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC highlight the performance gains achieved by CLIME-ADMM. The authors thoughtfully analyze the impact of column block size (k) on performance in both shared-memory and distributed-memory environments, providing valuable insights. The theoretical convergence rates established for the ADMM algorithm further bolster the contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The notation is inconsistent and lacks a consolidated table, making it difficult to follow the equations and algorithm description. Symbols like ρ and η are introduced without clear definition, requiring the reader to search for them later in the text. The justification for the block-cyclic data distribution is somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors claim it guarantees load balance and scalability, the explanation lacks depth, and no empirical evidence specifically supports this claim. A more detailed analysis of communication overhead associated with this distribution scheme would be beneficial. 

The selection of the column block size (k) appears ad hoc. The paper states that choosing k depends on factors like the process grid size, cache size, and sparsity pattern, but provides no systematic guidance or theoretical justification for selecting an optimal k. A more principled approach to choosing k, perhaps based on a cost model or empirical analysis across various problem instances, would strengthen the contribution. Furthermore, while the authors demonstrate scalability, the characterization of the framework as a "distributed algorithm" is debatable. The description lacks details on message passing and communication mechanisms crucial for true distributed systems. It appears more accurately described as a parallel implementation leveraging distributed memory.

Minor writing issues, such as "tunning" instead of "tuning" and "parameter tunning free" instead of "tuning-free," detract from the paper's polish. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically important problem – scalable sparse precision matrix estimation.
*   Presents a novel approach using column-block ADMM and block-cyclic data distribution.
*   Demonstrates significant scalability improvements through empirical evaluation.
*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the ADMM algorithm.
*   Positions itself within a relevant research area (“Big Data” machine learning).

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Lack of clarity in notation and explanation of key concepts (block-cyclic distribution, k selection).
*   Limited theoretical justification for the choice of column block size.
*   Potentially misleading characterization of the framework as a "distributed algorithm".
*   Minor writing and grammatical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and scalable machine learning. The empirical results are compelling, and the proposed approach offers a practical solution to a challenging problem. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Provide a clear and concise notation table. 2) Elaborate on the rationale behind the block-cyclic data distribution and provide supporting empirical evidence. 3) Offer guidance on selecting the column block size (k), either through theoretical analysis or empirical investigation. 4) Clarify whether the framework is accurately characterized as a "distributed algorithm" and provide more details on communication mechanisms if applicable. 5) Correct the minor writing errors. Addressing these points would significantly improve the clarity, rigor, and impact of the paper.
