## Review: Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

This paper addresses the important problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high dimensions, specifically focusing on scaling the CLIME estimator to datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME, coupled with a distributed framework leveraging column-block computations and efficient matrix multiplication techniques. 

The work builds upon existing research in sparse precision estimation (e.g., [28, 3, 19]) and distributed optimization ([2, 26, 6, 9]). It leverages the inherent structure of CLIME – its decomposability into independent linear programs – to enable efficient parallelization. The use of inexact ADMM, as referenced [27, 12], and the focus on column-block operations coupled with parallel matrix multiplications, are key contributions. This approach is particularly appealing as matrix-matrix multiplications are well-suited for parallel architectures.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The technical approach appears sound, and the convergence analysis, while standard, is clearly presented. The exploration of both shared-memory and distributed-memory environments is original and insightful, demonstrating the algorithm’s versatility. The problem itself is highly relevant, and a scalable, robust algorithm for sparse precision estimation is a valuable addition to the field. The paper is also well-organized and clearly written.

However, there are weaknesses. While the paper claims substantial scalability gains, the numerical experiments lack sufficient detail. Reporting the *accuracy* achieved by each solver (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC, CLIME-Flare) alongside the runtime is crucial for a fair comparison. Simply showing faster runtime doesn’t necessarily indicate a better solution. The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structure feels somewhat disjointed; while the ideas are promising, a clearer connection to the practical implementation and performance gains would be beneficial. Additionally, the impact of the block size 'k' on performance isn't fully elucidated; while explored, the optimal choice for different scenarios could be discussed further.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and builds on established literature.
*   Addresses an important, challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Original evaluation of ADMM in shared- and distributed-memory environments.
*   Potentially valuable tool for practitioners dealing with very large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup regarding solution accuracy.
*   Discussion of sparse/low-rank structure could be more integrated with the implementation.
*   Limited analysis of optimal block size 'k' for varying problem characteristics.



**Overall:** I believe this paper is a strong contribution and leans towards acceptance. Addressing the concerns regarding experimental detail would significantly strengthen the work and bolster its impact. The scalability demonstrated is impressive, and the approach is likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners alike.
