Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation at unprecedented scale, targeting p in the millions (p^2 parameters in the trillions). It adopts the CLIME estimator (Cai et al., JASA 2011), which decomposes column-wise and avoids positive-definiteness constraints. The main algorithmic idea is to replace solving one column at a time with a block-wise scheme coupled with an inexact ADMM (a linearized/proximal update for X), yielding closed-form elementwise updates and matrix-matrix multiplications as the dominant kernels. The authors establish O(1/T) rates for both objective suboptimality and constraint residuals. They exploit two structures: (i) iterate and sample-covariance sparsity, with justification that careful truncation preserves CLIME’s deviation bounds; and (ii) low rank (n ≪ p), using C = A A^T to reduce matrix multiplies to O(npk). Implementation-wise, they provide scalable shared- and distributed-memory realizations with block-cyclic data distribution and parallel I/O, and present extensive runtime and scalability comparisons versus CLIME solvers, TIGER (Liu & Wang), and DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al., NIPS 2013). Results show near-linear speedups and the ability to estimate a 10^6 × 10^6 precision matrix in ∼11 hours on 400 cores. The work connects well to distributed optimization via ADMM (Boyd et al.), to parallel matrix multiplication and cache-aware blocking, and to large-scale ML frameworks beyond MapReduce.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The inexact ADMM derivation is standard and careful; the linearization produces closed-form soft-threshold updates. The convergence analysis proving O(1/T) for the objective gap (via ergodic averaging) and constraint residuals is appropriate and aligns with recent ADMM theory for linearized/proximal variants. The structural result justifying sparse perturbations of C to maintain deviation bounds is useful; assumptions (sub-Gaussian data and sub-exponential perturbations) are clearly stated.
- Empirics: Experiments include both synthetic and real data, with runtime and precision/recall on synthetic, and controlled sparsity matches on real data. The scalability study is unusually thorough: block-size effects, shared vs distributed cores, cache effects, process grids, and block-cyclic distribution are all examined. The 1M-dimensional run is compelling.
- Caveats: (1) Statistical accuracy evaluation for real datasets is limited to matching sparsity; additional checks (e.g., held-out likelihood, stability across λ) would strengthen conclusions. (2) CLIME’s lack of PD constraints is acknowledged; practical symmetrization/PD post-processing is not reported. (3) The low-rank path (O(npk)) is central; memory requirements for storing A (p×n) at p=10^6 and n=200, and I/O/fault tolerance, could be discussed more. (4) The O(1/T) rates are standard; a brief comparison to existing inexact ADMM rates (e.g., He & Yuan) would contextualize novelty.

Clarity
The paper is well organized. The problem setup, algorithm (with clear pseudo-code), and system design are easy to follow. The novelty is stated crisply. The shared- vs distributed-memory sections and block-cyclic distribution are well explained. Suggestions:
- Summarize how symmetry/PD are enforced in the final estimator (standard CLIME symmetrization).
- Provide guidance for choosing η and ρ, and how sensitive convergence is to these choices.
- Explicitly state the computational/memory footprint for typical (p, n, k) and the impact of data layout on communication.

Originality
The contribution is a novel and effective combination:
- A block-wise solver for CLIME that turns the dominant work into matrix-matrix multiplies (leveraging BLAS-3 and cache-friendly blocking), rather than repeated mat-vecs across columns.
- A tailored inexact ADMM with closed-form updates and provable rates.
- A scalable shared/distributed implementation with block-cyclic layout and parallel I/O.
Relative to prior NIPS work on covariance selection (e.g., QUIC/DC-QUIC) and distributed ADMM, this is distinct in targeting CLIME at truly massive scales and in detailing system-level design choices that matter at that regime.

Significance
The ability to estimate sparse precision matrices with p up to 10^6 is a substantial advance, opening possibilities in climate, genomics, and large sensor networks. The engineering and algorithmic insights (block-wise CLIME, block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplies) are likely to be reused in related constrained estimators. The reported speedups and strong scaling are convincing.

Pros
- Addresses an important and challenging scaling problem (millions of variables).
- Clean algorithmic design with inexact ADMM; O(1/T) guarantees for objective and residuals.
- Exploits both sparsity and low rank; clear justification for covariance truncation.
- Strong and comprehensive systems work: shared/distributed implementations, block-cyclic layout, parallel I/O.
- Extensive experiments with clear wins over strong baselines; detailed scalability analysis.
- Well written; good positioning within the literature.

Cons
- Limited statistical validation on real data beyond sparsity matching; no likelihood/stability metrics.
- Practical details about symmetrization/PD enforcement for CLIME outputs are missing.
- Heavy reliance on low-rank (n ≪ p); memory/I-O constraints and fault tolerance not deeply discussed.
- Convergence rates are standard (not novel), though appropriately tailored.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Report symmetrization/PD post-processing, and its cost and effect on runtime/accuracy.
- Add held-out likelihood or stability selection curves on real datasets.
- Provide guidance for choosing k (block size) automatically from cache/process grid parameters.
- Discuss out-of-core operation and resilience to node failures in the distributed setting.
- Clarify λ selection strategy and sensitivity.

Recommendation
Strong accept, contingent on releasing the source code and scripts used for the experiments. Given the paper’s impact hinges on practical scalability, making the tool publicly available will greatly increase its value to the community and enable reproducibility.
