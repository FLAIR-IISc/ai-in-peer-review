This paper proposes a scalable variant of the CLIME estimator (CLIME-ADMM) for sparse precision matrix estimation, coupled with a distributed/parallel framework designed for large-scale problems. The authors address a critical need in high-dimensional statistics – efficient computation of precision matrices when *n* << *p*. The core idea is to leverage column-block operations within an ADMM framework, combined with block-cyclic data distribution to improve scalability. While the paper demonstrates promising results, particularly on a 1 million dimension dataset, several areas require attention.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The estimation of sparse precision matrices has seen significant interest (Cai et al., 2013; Yuan, 2010). Methods like graphical lasso (Ravikumar et al., 2011) and CLIME (Cai et al., 2013) offer statistical guarantees, but often struggle with scalability. Existing parallel/distributed approaches (Dean et al., 2012; Fu et al., 2013; Parikh & Boyd, 2014) often rely on gradient descent or graph-based splitting, which may be less suitable for constrained optimization problems like CLIME. This work builds upon ADMM (Boyd et al., 2011) – a popular technique for distributed optimization – but adapts it specifically for CLIME and introduces the novel column-block approach. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms, noting the challenges of MapReduce and the need for more tailored approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The primary strength is the demonstrated scalability. The reported results on a 1 million dimension problem with 400 cores are significant and suggest the method can handle datasets beyond the reach of many existing techniques.
*   **Novelty:** The column-block approach to solving CLIME is a valuable contribution.  Decomposing the problem this way allows for more efficient matrix operations and potentially better utilization of hardware resources. The empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapack is also a positive aspect.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide convergence rate analysis for the CLIME-ADMM algorithm, demonstrating O(1/T) convergence.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of sparse and low-rank structure exploitation offers practical strategies to further improve performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clear notation. Symbols like ρ and η are introduced without definition, making it difficult to follow the algorithm and analysis. A notation table is *strongly* recommended.
*   **Block Size Selection:** The choice of column block size *k* is ad hoc.  There's a need for more intuition or theoretical guidance on how to select an optimal *k*.  While the experiments explore different values, the rationale behind these choices is unclear.
*   **Distributed vs. Parallel:** The characterization of the approach as a *distributed* algorithm is questionable. The paper lacks a description of message-passing or communication mechanisms between nodes.  It appears to be more accurately described as a parallel implementation leveraging distributed memory, but lacking the full complexity of a true distributed algorithm.
*   **Load Balancing Justification:** The claim that block-cyclic data distribution guarantees load balance and scalability is not sufficiently justified. While intuitive, a more rigorous explanation or supporting empirical evidence would strengthen the argument.
*   **Minor Errors:** Several minor writing/typo errors are present ("tunning", "parameter tunning free", "summerized") and should be corrected.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, and the convergence analysis provides a degree of theoretical rigor. However, the lack of clarity in notation and justification for key design choices weakens the overall quality. It feels like a complete piece of work in terms of implementation and experimentation but lacks sufficient detail to be fully reproducible without significant effort.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is a major concern. The scattered notation and lack of detailed explanation regarding the distributed framework hinder understanding. The paper could benefit from more illustrative diagrams and a clearer explanation of the underlying data distribution strategy.

**Originality:**

The combination of CLIME, ADMM, column-block operations, and a distributed framework represents a novel contribution. The focus on scalability for ultra-large-scale problems is also a valuable aspect. However, the novelty is somewhat diminished by the lack of a detailed comparison with other parallel/distributed sparse estimation methods.

**Significance:**

The results are potentially significant, particularly for applications dealing with very high-dimensional data. If the scalability claims hold up under further scrutiny, this work could provide a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners in fields such as genomics, finance, and sensor networks. However, the practical impact depends on the ease of implementation and the availability of appropriate hardware resources.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated scalability to a very large problem size.
*   Novel column-block approach to CLIME estimation.
*   Theoretical convergence analysis.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in notation and explanation.
*   Insufficient justification for block size selection and load balancing.
*   Questionable characterization as a “distributed” algorithm.
*   Minor writing/typo errors.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. The paper addresses an important problem and presents a promising solution. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above—particularly clarifying the notation, justifying the key design choices, and providing a more accurate description of the distributed framework—is crucial for improving the paper’s quality and impact.  I believe that with these revisions, this paper could make a significant contribution to the field.
