Summary
This paper tackles large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator by designing an inexact ADMM algorithm and a scalable shared-memory and distributed-memory implementation. The key idea is to reformulate the column-wise CLIME LPs into an ADMM-friendly constrained problem with a linearized (inexact) X-update that admits a closed-form soft-thresholding step, while the Z-update is a simple box projection. The computational bottleneck reduces to matrix-matrix multiplications CX and CY, which can be implemented efficiently and in parallel. The authors further propose solving CLIME in column blocks (k columns at a time), enabling BLAS3-style operations, cache-friendly blocking, and block-cyclic distribution across a process grid. They establish O(1/T) convergence rates for the objective suboptimality and constraint residuals under η ≥ ρλmax(C)^2. The paper also discusses leveraging iterate sparsity and the low-rank structure C = AA′ (with n ≪ p) to reduce computation. Experiments on both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems show near-linear scaling in cores and improved speed with larger column blocks; they report solving a p = 10^6 problem in ~11 hours on 400 cores. Comparisons to CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC show strong scalability advantages.

Relation to prior work: CLIME (Cai et al., JASA), QUIC and DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al., NIPS), and ADMM (Boyd et al., FnT) are directly relevant. The distributed framework relates to ADMM-based distributed optimization and prior systems work (e.g., block splitting, ScaLAPACK, block-cyclic layouts). The theoretical rates align with known inexact ADMM results; the contribution is tailoring these to CLIME with a practical, scalable implementation.

Quality
- Theory: The inexact ADMM scheme and O(1/T) rates for objective and primal residuals are standard and appear correctly adapted to (3), with clear conditions on η and initialization. The linearization and proximal terms are reasonable. It would help to state whether the rate is ergodic or non-ergodic and clarify constants and norm choices in Theorem 2 (the expression ‖·‖^2_{η/ρ I−C^2} deserves more explicit definition/positivity conditions).
- Algorithmic soundness: The column-block formulation is correct, and the updates are implementable with elementwise ops and matrix multiplications. Positivity/definiteness of the final precision estimate: CLIME does not enforce PD, and the standard symmetrization/post-processing step is not discussed—this is important for downstream use.
- Systems: The shared vs distributed designs and block-cyclic distribution are appropriate. Communication patterns and data movement costs (e.g., broadcasts/reductions for A′X and AY across the process grid) should be quantitatively characterized.
- Experiments: Strong scaling results. However, the numerical evaluation lacks details on accuracy and stopping:
  - Report per-dataset stopping criteria, primal/dual residual tolerances, and achieved KKT residuals.
  - For real data, matching sparsity is not a quality metric; include out-of-sample Gaussian likelihood, stability selection, or surrogate risk to assess estimation quality.
  - For synthetic data, include estimation error vs iteration and vs wall-clock, not only precision/recall across sparsity sweeps.
  - The covariance perturbation idea (Theorem 3) is interesting; include empirical results showing its effect on run time and estimation error, and guidance on setting thresholds.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. Suggestions:
- Algorithm 1: line 7 uses box(U+Y, λ) but later box(U+Y, E, λ) is defined; please make consistent and define E there. Similarly clarify the definition of Vt, V̂t, and the step 10 update Vt+1 = (ρ/η)(2V̂t+1 − V̂t).
- Explicitly state whether X is symmetrized at the end and how columns from different blocks are combined.
- Provide a concise per-iteration complexity/communication table (compute, memory, messages).
- Minor typos/grammar throughout; a careful proofread would help.

Originality
Algorithmically, the components (CLIME separability, inexact/linearized ADMM, BLAS3/block-cyclic distribution) are known; the novelty is in the integration: a column-block ADMM tailored to CLIME with a scalable implementation on both shared and distributed memory, plus convergence guarantees specialized to this setting and practical guidance on blocking. The covariance perturbation angle is a nice touch, but the probabilistic statement is close in spirit to standard thresholding results; practical instantiation would increase novelty.

Significance
Estimating sparse precision matrices at million-scale dimensions is important for several application areas. A simple, robust method that runs efficiently on hundreds of cores is valuable. While the theoretical advances are incremental, the systems contribution and demonstrated scalability could make this a useful reference and tool. Broader impact would be strengthened by more rigorous accuracy evaluations and clearer guidance on hyperparameter selection and stopping.

Pros
- Clear, simple algorithm with closed-form updates and O(1/T) guarantees.
- Thoughtful systems design (column blocks, BLAS3, block-cyclic data distribution).
- Strong scalability results on both architectures; million-dimensional demo.
- Practical exploitation of low-rank structure when n ≪ p.

Cons
- Limited statistical/accuracy evaluation and stopping/accuracy reporting.
- Modest algorithmic novelty; much of the gain comes from CLIME’s separability and matrix-matrix multiplies.
- Missing discussion of PD/symmetrization of the final precision estimate.
- Communication/memory footprint not quantified; covariance perturbation not empirically validated.

Questions for the authors
1) What stopping criteria (primal/dual residual thresholds) and max iterations were used? Please report achieved residuals and objective gaps.
2) How is λ chosen in experiments? Sensitivity to λ and η/ρ? Any rule-of-thumb for η ≥ ρλmax(C)^2 in practice?
3) Do you symmetrize the final estimate (as in CLIME) and ensure PD when needed? How is this done at scale across blocks?
4) Can you report communication volume per iteration and compute/comm breakdown on the distributed runs?
5) Can you include experiments demonstrating the covariance perturbation trick (runtime/accuracy trade-offs)?

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper is technically sound, well written, and makes a useful systems contribution with demonstrated scalability, though with limited algorithmic novelty and insufficient accuracy reporting.

Confidence: 3/5
