- Proposes a scalable, block-wise inexact ADMM for CLIME that updates k columns at a time to leverage matrix–matrix operations and distributed linear algebra.
- Proves O(1/T) convergence rates for objective gap and constraint residuals under standard step-size conditions (η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2).
- Exploits low-rank sample covariance structure (C = A A^T with n ≪ p) to reduce cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k).
- Leverages iterate sparsity and a “coarsened covariance” sparsification idea to accelerate multiplications (not fully empirically validated).
- Implements block-cyclic data distribution, parallel I/O, and load-balanced layout for shared- and distributed-memory architectures.
- Isolates bottlenecks to matrix multiplications, enabling high throughput and near-linear core scaling.
- Empirically outperforms existing CLIME solvers (flare/ADMM), TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and real datasets in runtime and scalability.
- Demonstrates solving p ≈ 10^6 problems in ~11 hours on 400 cores, showing practical significance for ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation.
- The inexact/linearized ADMM derivation is standard but well executed and verifiable.
- Convergence guarantees align with known inexact/linearized ADMM results; technical arguments are sound.
- The perturbed covariance argument for enabling sparsity is plausible and a stated theorem covers sub-Gaussian data with sub-exponential perturbations.
- More detail on constants and practical thresholding rules for sparsification would strengthen theoretical/practical claims.
- Empirical comparisons include runtime scaling and brief statistical accuracy (precision–recall); TIGER shows higher recall while CLIME-based methods are competitive.
- Statistical accuracy comparisons could be more systematic (e.g., matched FDR/TPR, spectral/Frobenius error vs n).
- Clarity is good: clear pseudocode, cost accounting, and system diagrams; separation of elementwise ops vs matrix multiplications is useful.
- Paper should explicitly summarize stopping criteria, sensitivity to ρ and η, and selection of block size k (currently heuristic/cache-based).
- Originality: adapting inexact ADMM to column-block CLIME is incremental, but applying it with low-rank exploitation and a careful distributed design at million-scale is novel.
- Systems contribution (block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O, end-to-end scaling) is the most distinctive element.
- “Coarsened covariance” idea is interesting but underdeveloped empirically.
- Practical concerns: CLIME does not enforce positive definiteness—post-processing/symmetrization costs should be discussed.
- Communication costs per iteration and synchronization details (especially for A^T X then A W) need more exposition for scaling beyond 400 cores.
- Hyperparameter guidance (ρ, η, block size k) and any adaptive schemes are missing and should be provided.
- Questions for authors include stopping criteria, selection/adaptation of ρ and η, symmetry/PD handling, per-iteration communication stats, and sparsification thresholds tried.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills an important scalability gap, strong empirical scaling to unprecedented sizes, clear engineering and reusable insights.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theoretical novelty, mixed estimator comparisons complicate accuracy claims, and some practical components are underexplored.
- Recommendation: leaning accept, contingent on releasing reproducible code and scripts.
