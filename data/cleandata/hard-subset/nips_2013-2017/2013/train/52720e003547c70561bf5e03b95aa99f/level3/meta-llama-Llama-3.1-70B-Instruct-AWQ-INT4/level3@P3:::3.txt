This paper tackles the problem of covariance selection at a massive scale, specifically focusing on sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose a block-wise alternative to the traditional column-wise solvable CLIME estimator and develop a new block-wise solver coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers. The paper presents a comprehensive study of the algorithm's convergence rates, scalability, and performance on both synthetic and real data.

The paper's main contributions are threefold. Firstly, the authors establish convergence rates for both the objective value and the distance to optimality, providing a theoretical foundation for their approach. Secondly, they develop a scalable parallel implementation for both shared- and distributed-memory systems, which enables the algorithm to scale to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. Finally, the authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating the algorithm's strong performance and scalability on both synthetic and real data.

The paper is well-written, with clear statements of novelty and solid connections to previous work in the field. The authors provide a balanced technical and theoretical exposition, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The presentation is clear, and the notation is well-defined, making it easy to follow the mathematical derivations.

One of the paper's main strengths is its ability to leverage sparsity and low-rank structure to reduce computation. The authors propose a few possible directions to further exploit these structures, including sparse and low-rank multiplications. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of these approaches, showing significant speedups over existing methods.

The scalability analysis is also a strong point of the paper. The authors conduct a detailed study of speedups across block sizes and the number of cores, providing valuable insights into the algorithm's performance. The results show that the algorithm scales almost linearly with the number of cores, making it an attractive solution for large-scale problems.

The paper's main concern is the lack of source code availability. The authors' claim that the paper's value depends on releasing the source code, and publication should be contingent on making the tool available to the community. While this is a valid concern, it is not a criticism of the paper's technical content.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, this paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and a complete piece of work. The writing is clear, and the organization is well-structured. The paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, and the authors adequately reference related work. The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.

Overall, this paper is a strong contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors' proposed algorithm and implementation demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, and the paper's thorough evaluation and analysis provide valuable insights into the algorithm's performance.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper tackles a challenging problem in sparse precision matrix estimation and proposes a novel solution.
* The authors establish convergence rates for both the objective value and the distance to optimality.
* The paper presents a scalable parallel implementation for both shared- and distributed-memory systems.
* The experimental evaluation demonstrates the algorithm's strong performance and scalability on both synthetic and real data.
* The paper is well-written, with clear statements of novelty and solid connections to previous work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of source code availability is a concern, as it may limit the paper's impact and reproducibility.
* The paper's focus on CLIME may be seen as limited, as other estimators may be more widely used in practice.
* The experimental evaluation, while thorough, may not be comprehensive enough to cover all possible scenarios.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, contingent on the authors making the source code available to the community.
* Encourage the authors to provide additional experimental results or comparisons with other estimators to further demonstrate the algorithm's performance and robustness.
