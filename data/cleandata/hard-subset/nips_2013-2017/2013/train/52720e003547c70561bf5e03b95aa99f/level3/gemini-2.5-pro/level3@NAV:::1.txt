This paper addresses the important problem of sparse precision matrix estimation in very high dimensions. The authors propose CLIME-ADMM, a scalable variant of the Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) estimator, and a distributed/parallel framework to implement it. The core ideas are to solve the CLIME problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column and to leverage a block-cyclic data distribution scheme for load balancing in parallel computation. This work builds upon existing literature on CLIME estimators and large-scale optimization using ADMM, positioning itself as a systems-focused contribution for scaling up a statistically well-founded method. The empirical results, demonstrating scalability to a million dimensions and comparing favorably against state-of-the-art methods like DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare, are a key part of the contribution.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength lies in its significance and quality of engineering. It tackles a challenging and relevant problem, and the proposed framework demonstrates impressive scalability, a significant advancement over existing methods. The novelty of processing the precision matrix in column blocks, which allows for efficient matrix-matrix multiplications, is a clever approach to improve computational performance on modern hardware. The empirical analysis is thorough, evaluating the method on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures using standard frameworks like OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK, and provides a compelling case for the algorithm's practical utility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from several clarity and quality issues that should be addressed.

1.  **Clarity of Notation and Algorithm:** The notation is scattered across Sections 1–3, making the paper difficult to follow. Important symbols like the ADMM parameters ρ and η are used in Algorithm 1 but are never defined or discussed in the text. I strongly recommend adding a centralized notation table and providing guidance on setting these parameters.
2.  **Unsupported Claims:** The paper claims that its use of a block-cyclic data distribution guarantees load balance and scalability. However, this is a strong claim that is not substantiated with either theoretical justification or empirical evidence (e.g., a comparison to a simpler data distribution scheme). The authors should provide a clearer argument for this design choice.
3.  **Ad Hoc Parameter Selection:** The column block size `k` is a critical parameter, but its selection appears ad hoc. The paper would be strengthened by providing intuition, theoretical guidance, or an ablation study on how to choose an optimal `k`.
4.  **Framing of Contribution:** The work is characterized as a "distributed algorithm," but the paper lacks a description of the message passing and communication mechanisms, which are fundamental to distributed computing. It may be more accurately described as a scalable parallel implementation. Clarifying this aspect would improve the paper's positioning.
5.  **Minor Issues:** There are several typos ("tunning" -> "tuning", "summerized" -> "summarized") that should be corrected.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution that pushes the state-of-the-art in large-scale precision matrix estimation. The weaknesses primarily relate to the clarity of the presentation and the justification of certain design choices rather than a fundamental flaw in the approach. Addressing the points above would significantly improve the paper's quality and impact.
