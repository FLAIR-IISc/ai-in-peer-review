## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of estimating sparse precision matrices at a massive scale – on the order of millions of variables and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, leveraging its inherent column-wise solvability, and propose a novel block-wise alternative to improve computational efficiency. Their primary algorithmic contribution is a new block-wise solver coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers (ADMM). A significant strength of the work lies in the theoretical analysis, providing convergence rate guarantees for both the objective value and distance to optimality. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate a keen understanding of structure exploitation, utilizing sparsity and low-rank properties to reduce computational burden. 

The implementation details are impressive, with a scalable parallel implementation designed for both shared- and distributed-memory systems. The experimental results are compelling, showcasing strong performance on both synthetic and real datasets, and a detailed scalability analysis reveals near-linear speedups with increasing core counts and block sizes. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear statements of novelty, solid connections to relevant literature, and a balanced presentation of technical and theoretical aspects. The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms. 

However, the paper’s ultimate value hinges on the availability of the developed tool. While the experimental results are convincing, the reproducibility of these results and the broader impact of this work are contingent upon releasing the source code. Without it, the community cannot readily benefit from this significant engineering effort and validate the claims made. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial problem in high-dimensional statistics with a practical and scalable solution.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with proven convergence rates.
*   Excellent experimental validation demonstrating significant performance gains.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of publicly available source code severely limits reproducibility and impact.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance *contingent upon* the authors making their source code publicly available. This is a significant contribution, but its full potential will not be realized without enabling the broader research community to build upon this work. If the code cannot be released, I would recommend rejection, as the reproducibility concerns outweigh the technical merits.
