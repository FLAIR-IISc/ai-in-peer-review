This paper addresses the critical problem of sparse precision matrix estimation in extremely high-dimensional settings, a challenge that frequently arises in modern data analysis. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, known for its strong theoretical properties, and present a novel and highly scalable algorithm for its computation. Their contribution lies in an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) variant, coupled with a block-wise solution strategy that facilitates efficient parallelization.  This work builds upon previous efforts in sparse covariance estimation, such as the work of Cai et al. [3], Hsieh et al. [13], and Yuan [28], but significantly extends the scalability to previously intractable problem sizes.  Recent advances in distributed optimization, highlighted in references like Dean et al. [6] and Parikh & Boyd [23], provide context, but the authors appropriately point out that existing frameworks are not directly applicable to their constrained optimization problem. 

The core strength of this work is its innovative approach to parallel computation. By solving CLIME in column blocks, rather than column-by-column, the authors unlock opportunities for more efficient matrix multiplications, leveraging existing libraries like ScaLAPACK [15]. The inexact ADMM algorithm further contributes to scalability.  Crucially, they provide theoretical guarantees—establishing convergence rates for both the objective function and the optimality conditions—which lends substantial credibility to their approach. The detailed discussion of data distribution strategies (block cyclic) and the careful consideration of cache efficiency demonstrate a deep understanding of the practical challenges in high-performance computing.  The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating that their implementation significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods, achieving nearly linear scaling with the number of cores and successfully estimating a precision matrix with one million dimensions and one trillion parameters in a reasonable timeframe (11 hours on 400 cores).

However, there are some areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the presentation could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the convergence rates.  The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structure (Section 3) feels somewhat fragmented. The potential gains from exploiting sample covariance sparsity, as detailed in Theorem 3, are intriguing but warrant further elaboration on practical considerations for applying the perturbation strategies.  Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of existing parallelization frameworks, a more detailed comparison with, for example, parameter server approaches for constrained optimization would be beneficial. 

Regarding the qualitative evaluation criteria:
**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The work is complete, presenting a fully developed algorithm with convergence guarantees and extensive evaluation. The authors are careful to address both strengths and limitations, showcasing a balanced assessment.
**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is clearly presented. However, some sections, such as the discussion of convergence rates, could benefit from more accessible explanations.
**Originality:** The combination of block-wise CLIME solving, inexact ADMM, and a scalable distributed implementation is novel.  The authors adequately reference related work and clearly delineate their contributions.
**Significance:** The results are highly significant. The ability to estimate precision matrices at this scale has the potential to impact a wide range of applications, from bioinformatics to financial modeling. The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art and provides a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Solves a very important and challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with strong scalability.
* Achieves impressive experimental results, demonstrating significant speedups compared to existing methods.
* Provides a detailed and well-engineered implementation with careful consideration of practical optimization techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* The theoretical explanation of the convergence rates could be more intuitive.
* The discussion of exploiting sparse/low-rank structure feels somewhat incomplete.
* Release of source code is critical for reproducibility and impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation. The innovative algorithm, coupled with the detailed implementation and compelling experimental results, make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. However, the full impact of this work hinges on the availability of the source code. **I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors making their implementation publicly available to the research community.** Without access to the code, reproducing the results and building upon this work will be difficult.
