Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an inexact ADMM algorithm for computing the CLIME estimator for sparse precision matrix estimation, together with shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations that exploit column-block decompositions and block-cyclic data distribution. The algorithm attains O(1/T) ergodic convergence rates for objective suboptimality and constraint residuals under a standard linearized ADMM scheme with a proximal term and a step-size condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. The core computational work reduces to matrix–matrix multiplications (e.g., CX and CY), with elementwise soft-thresholding and boxing operations elsewhere. The authors further leverage structure: (i) potential sparsity in iterates and in a truncated sample covariance, and (ii) low-rank factorizations C = A A^T when n ≪ p to lower per-iteration cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k). The paper evaluates performance against DC-QUIC (graphical lasso), TIGER, and CLIME-Flare, and presents scaling results on both a shared-memory box and a distributed cluster, including a run with p = 10^6 on 400 cores.

This work builds on a substantial literature: CLIME for ℓ1-constrained inverse covariance estimation and its statistical guarantees; ADMM and linearized/inexact ADMM convergence (e.g., He & Yuan, Wang & Banerjee, Boyd et al.); and HPC building blocks (BLAS-3, ScaLAPACK, block-cyclic data distribution). Compared to prior solvers that tackle CLIME column-by-column or via interior point methods, the contribution here is a column-block ADMM variant and distributed implementation designed for ultra-high dimensions.

Quality
- Theory: The inexact ADMM derivation is standard for linearly constrained problems (linearization, proximal term), and the O(1/T) convergence rate for both objective and feasibility is consistent with known results. The step-size requirement η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 is reasonable, though some guidance on estimating λ_max(C) in very large p (e.g., a few power iterations distributed) would help practitioners. The decomposition across column-blocks is correct, and the use of A(A'X) for low-rank multiplication is sound.
- Algorithms/engineering: The mapping of CLIME to a sequence of matmul and elementwise ops is clean and well-suited to parallelization. The use of block-cyclic distribution is appropriate and standard for load balancing and cache efficiency. The shared vs distributed measurements and discussion of cache effects are informative.
- Experiments: The scaling results are convincing, and the comparison set (DC-QUIC, TIGER, CLIME-Flare) is appropriate. However, the setup lacks crucial detail for scientific assessment: (i) stopping criteria and achieved primal/dual residuals for each solver; (ii) time-to-accuracy comparisons (e.g., to fixed feasibility and objective gaps), not only time-to-sparsity; (iii) for synthetic data, estimation error metrics (‖Ω̂−Ω0‖F, spectral norm, edge F1) at matched accuracy; (iv) for real data, no accuracy proxies or stability metrics are reported—only time to reach target sparsities. Because the methods solve different objectives (CLIME vs graphical lasso vs TIGER), reporting accuracy achieved by each solver is essential to ensure fair comparisons.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and clearly written. The algorithmic steps, complexity, and distributed framework are described crisply. Pseudocode is helpful. Reproducibility would benefit from:
- Exact stopping rules, tolerances, and how λ was chosen across methods.
- Hardware and I/O details for the 10^6-dimensional run (data layout, wall-clock breakdown across compute/communication/I/O).
- Guidance on selecting block sizes (pb, nb, kb) and column-block size k, and sensitivity analyses.

Originality
The ADMM variant itself is a standard linearized ADMM instantiation for CLIME, with appropriate citations; the novelty lies primarily in the engineering: column-block formulation, low-rank matmul realization, and a carefully implemented shared/distributed pipeline with block-cyclic distribution. Evaluating ADMM for CLIME in both shared- and distributed-memory environments at this scale is interesting and, to my knowledge, original within the NIPS literature of that period.

Significance
Efficient large-scale sparse precision estimation is important across science and industry. A simple, robust, distributed algorithm that scales to millions of variables is valuable. The scalability gains here largely stem from CLIME’s separable structure and the use of highly optimized matrix–matrix multiplications, which are known to parallelize well; nevertheless, packaging these insights into a working system with empirical validation is impactful. The practical message that CLIME’s structure enables straightforward strong/weak scaling is useful to practitioners.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Comparisons and accuracy: Please report, for all solvers, convergence tolerances, primal/dual residuals, and objective values; also provide time-to-target-residual curves. On synthetic data, report estimation errors (Frobenius, operator, and matrix ℓ1 norms) and edge-level F1 at matched false positive rates. On real data, consider stability selection or subsampling stability to provide accuracy proxies.
- Parameter selection: Clarify how λ is chosen across methods and datasets (theoretical scaling vs cross-validation vs fixed to match sparsity). Since TIGER is tuning-insensitive, discuss fairness of matching sparsity vs using its default.
- Symmetrization/PD: CLIME requires symmetrizing Ω̂; specify the symmetrization rule used and whether positive definiteness is enforced or post-processed.
- Step-size and initialization: Provide a practical recipe for η and ρ, and how λ_max(C) is estimated at scale. State whether continuation or over-relaxation was tried.
- Communication analysis: Add a back-of-the-envelope model separating computation and communication to explain deviations from ideal scaling, especially for small k and large core counts.
- Sparse/low-rank options: The truncation of small C_ij is a nice idea; quantify its empirical effect on accuracy and runtime. Likewise, report when low-rank acceleration ceases to help as n grows.
- Memory footprint: For the 10^6 run, report per-node memory usage, I/O time, and whether A is streamed or resident.

Pros
- Technically sound formulation with standard, correct convergence guarantees.
- Clean mapping of CLIME to elementwise ops and matmul; highly parallelizable.
- Effective shared- and distributed-memory implementations with near-linear scaling.
- Addresses an important, large-scale problem; practical impact is high.
- Clear exposition and solid engineering choices (block-cyclic distribution, BLAS-3).

Cons
- Algorithmic novelty is modest; the inexact ADMM derivation is standard.
- Experimental evaluation emphasizes speed over accuracy; missing time-to-accuracy and achieved optimality metrics.
- Cross-method comparisons span different objectives; fairness needs more care.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity, and on communication/computation breakdowns.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The contribution is primarily systems/engineering rather than algorithmic novelty, but the scale, clarity, and practicality make it a valuable addition. Strengthening the experimental section with accuracy and convergence details, and clarifying parameter choices, would substantially improve the paper.
