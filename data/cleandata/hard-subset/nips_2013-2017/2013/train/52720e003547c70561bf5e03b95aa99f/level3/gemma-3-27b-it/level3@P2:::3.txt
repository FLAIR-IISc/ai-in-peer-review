This paper addresses the important problem of sparse precision matrix estimation at an unprecedented scale – millions of variables and trillions of parameters – a challenge that remains largely unmet by existing methods. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, leveraging its inherent column-wise separability, and propose a novel block-wise alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) solver coupled with careful considerations for exploiting sparse and low-rank structures. The work is accompanied by strong theoretical convergence guarantees and a detailed scalability analysis on both shared- and distributed-memory architectures, showcasing significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art techniques.

The central idea of solving CLIME in column blocks, rather than individually, is a clever one. While the column-wise decomposition is known, the authors convincingly argue and demonstrate that exploiting matrix-matrix multiplications within block processing yields substantial efficiency gains, especially when combined with careful block cyclic data distribution to optimize memory access and load balancing. The inexact ADMM variant further enhances practicality by reducing computational cost per iteration. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions, provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach. 

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation, contributions, and experimental results. The authors effectively relate their work to the existing literature in both statistical estimation and parallel/distributed optimization. The discussion of related work is thorough, and the citations are relevant and up-to-date. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results convincingly demonstrate the scalability and performance advantages of CLIME-ADMM.  The analysis of speedup with varying block sizes and core counts is particularly insightful. The consideration of low-rank structure as a further optimization is also a valuable addition.

However, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. While the convergence analysis is valuable, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the inexactness in the ADMM algorithm would be beneficial. Specifically, how sensitive is performance to the choice of parameters controlling the inexactness, and how does this impact the overall accuracy of the estimated precision matrix?  Additionally, the empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, largely focuses on synthetic data. While real-world datasets are included, further investigation on a wider range of applications would bolster the claims of broad applicability.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The primary strength is the demonstrated scalability to extremely high-dimensional problems, significantly exceeding the capabilities of existing methods.
* **Novelty:** The block-wise ADMM solver and the integration of block cyclic data distribution are novel contributions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis provides a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental results are thorough and well-presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Implications of Inexactness:** Limited discussion on the impact of inexact ADMM on solution accuracy.
* **Real-World Data:** Could benefit from more extensive evaluation on diverse real-world datasets.
* **Code Availability:** The authors acknowledge the difficulty of reproducing results without access to the code.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical need for scalable sparse precision matrix estimation.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithmic approach.
* Demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods.
* Is clearly written and well-motivated.
* Advances the state-of-the-art in large-scale statistical estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The practical implications of the inexactness in the ADMM algorithm are not fully explored.
* The evaluation could be strengthened with more diverse real-world datasets.
* Lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility and adoption.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistical estimation. The proposed method effectively addresses the challenges of scaling CLIME to massive datasets, and the experimental results are compelling.  However, the lack of publicly available code is a significant concern.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors releasing the source code to the research community. Without code availability, the reproducibility of the results and the potential impact of this work are substantially limited. I believe the contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance *if* this condition is met. Otherwise, I would recommend a rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit with code.
