

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper presents a scalable distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, with a focus on handling ultra-high-dimensional problems (up to millions of dimensions). The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) that operates on column blocks rather than individual columns, along with a distributed computing framework that leverages block cyclic data distribution. The paper demonstrates that their approach scales almost linearly with the number of cores and can handle problems with up to one trillion parameters.

The work builds on previous advances in precision matrix estimation, particularly the CLIME estimator [3, 4, 19], and extends the computational scalability of these methods to unprecedented dimensions. The paper relates well to prior NIPS work on distributed optimization, such as [2, 6, 9, 23], while making specific contributions in handling the unique structure of precision matrix estimation problems. The authors acknowledge related work on distributed ADMM for graphical models [9] and graph projection block splitting [23], positioning their work as a more general framework with improved memory hierarchy utilization.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with theoretical convergence guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2), which is a strength. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates significant scalability improvements over state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, and FLARE). However, the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of the theoretical basis for the column block size k, which is currently chosen ad hoc. The theoretical analysis of the inexact ADMM is sound, but the paper doesn't fully justify why the column block approach provides the claimed efficiency gains over column-by-column processing. The empirical evidence for the load balancing properties of the block cyclic distribution is limited, with no specific measurements showing how the proposed distribution improves load balance over alternative schemes.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from scattered notation that makes it difficult to follow at times. The paper would benefit significantly from a dedicated notation table that defines all symbols (particularly ρ, η, and their relationship to the problem parameters). Some minor typos were noted ("tunning" instead of "tuning," "parameter tunning free" instead of "tuning," "summerized" instead of "summarized"). The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear, but the explanation of how the block cyclic distribution achieves load balance is insufficiently detailed. The paper's claim that it is a "distributed" algorithm is questionable without detailed explanation of communication patterns and synchronization mechanisms.

**Originality:** The column block approach to CLIME is a novel contribution that distinguishes this work from previous implementations. While column-wise decomposition was previously recognized [3, 18], the paper's extension to column blocks and the specific implementation details for large-scale problems represent a meaningful advance. The paper's theoretical contributions (convergence rates for the inexact ADMM) are also novel. However, the block cyclic distribution technique is not new to distributed computing [15, 16, 5], and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain how their specific application to this problem differs from prior uses of the technique.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in high-dimensional statistics: scaling precision matrix estimation to truly massive problems. The demonstrated ability to solve problems with one million dimensions (one trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores represents a substantial advance over existing methods. This work has clear potential impact for applications in genomics, climate science, and other fields where high-dimensional data is common. The framework could also be adapted to other large-scale constrained optimization problems, as noted in the conclusion.

**Strengths:**
- Demonstrates significant scalability (up to 1 million dimensions on 400 cores)
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the inexact ADMM
- Well-executed empirical evaluation comparing against multiple state-of-the-art methods
- Clear description of the computational structure and parallelization strategy
- Addresses a meaningful practical challenge in high-dimensional statistics

**Weaknesses:**
- Column block size k is chosen ad hoc without theoretical guidance or empirical analysis
- Insufficient explanation of why block cyclic distribution provides load balance
- Limited discussion of communication patterns and scalability limitations in distributed setting
- Scattered notation makes the paper harder to follow
- Some claims about distributed nature of the algorithm are not fully supported by the technical description

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating a practically scalable solution for ultra-high-dimensional precision matrix estimation. While there are some weaknesses in the presentation and theoretical justification (particularly regarding the block size selection and load balancing claims), the core technical contribution is strong and the empirical results are compelling. The paper addresses a genuine gap in the literature regarding the practical scalability of precision matrix estimation algorithms. With minor revisions to improve the notation and clarify the load balancing claims, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper solves a real and important problem (scaling precision matrix estimation to massive dimensions), provides a theoretically grounded approach, demonstrates impressive scalability in experiments, and makes a clear contribution to the practical implementation of statistical methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The theoretical justification for the column block approach is not fully developed, and the paper's claims about distributed computing properties could be better substantiated with more detailed explanation of communication patterns and scalability analysis.
