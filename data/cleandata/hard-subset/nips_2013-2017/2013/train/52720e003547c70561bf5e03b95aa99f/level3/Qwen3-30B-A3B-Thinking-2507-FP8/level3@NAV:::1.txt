

This paper proposes a scalable CLIME-ADMM variant (CLIME-ADMM) and a distributed/parallel framework for sparse precision matrix estimation, targeting ultra-high-dimensional problems with millions of variables. The authors present an inexact ADMM algorithm that processes the precision matrix in column blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging matrix-matrix operations to improve computational efficiency. The framework uses block-cyclic data distribution to achieve load balancing and demonstrates scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, with experiments showing linear scaling on hundreds of cores.

The paper's main strengths include its clear theoretical contribution with O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions, and its significant empirical demonstration of scalability on massive problems (1 million dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores). The column-block approach represents a meaningful advancement over previous column-by-column implementations, and the use of OpenMPI/Scalapak for distributed computation provides valuable practical insights. The work appropriately situates itself within the growing literature on distributed optimization, contrasting with prior approaches like GPBS and parameter servers.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The notation is scattered throughout Sections 1-3 without a dedicated notation table, leaving symbols like ρ and η undefined until later sections. The block-cyclic distribution's load balancing claims lack empirical justification—no evidence is provided to demonstrate how this distribution actually achieves load balance or scalability. The column block size k is chosen ad hoc without theoretical guidance or intuition, making it difficult to determine optimal parameters for new applications. Additionally, the algorithm is better characterized as a parallel implementation rather than a distributed algorithm, as the paper lacks detailed description of message passing or communication mechanisms between processes.

Minor typos ("tunning" instead of "tuning") and inconsistent terminology should be corrected. While the novelty of column-block processing is well-justified, the paper would benefit from more concrete guidance on parameter selection and empirical validation of its load balancing claims. Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a significant contribution to large-scale precision matrix estimation with strong practical implications. The work addresses an important problem with a scalable solution, though the technical presentation requires refinement to fully realize its potential.
