

## Review for "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper addresses the important problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation, which is critical for high-dimensional statistical analysis. The authors propose an efficient distributed algorithm for the CLIME estimator, which solves the linear program min ||Ω̂||₁ s.t. ||CΩ̂ - I||∞ ≤ λ. The key contribution is a column-block ADMM approach that leverages the inherent column-wise separability of CLIME while using efficient matrix-matrix multiplications to achieve high scalability. The paper establishes O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions, and develops a distributed framework using block cyclic distribution for load balancing.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with solid theoretical contributions. The inexact ADMM derivation for CLIME is well-motivated and follows established methods (with appropriate references to [27, 12]), though the derivation itself is somewhat standard. The convergence analysis is rigorous and extends prior work on ADMM convergence rates. The experimental evaluation demonstrates clear scalability improvements over state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, CLIME-Flare) on both synthetic and real datasets, with impressive results on million-dimensional problems. However, the numerical experiments would be stronger with more detail on the accuracy achieved by each method (e.g., spectral norm error, Frobenius norm error), not just runtime comparisons. The authors acknowledge the importance of accuracy but focus primarily on computational efficiency.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear, the algorithmic details are presented methodically, and the theoretical results are properly contextualized. The figures illustrating the distributed framework (Figure 1) are particularly helpful for understanding the implementation. The notation is consistent and well-defined. The presentation of the column-block approach and its advantages over column-by-column methods is especially clear. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including implementation details for shared-memory (OpenMP) and distributed-memory (OpenMPI, ScaLAPACK) architectures.

**Originality:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on scalable optimization methods for large-scale statistical problems. While the core ADMM approach for CLIME is not entirely novel, the column-block implementation with block cyclic distribution for load balancing represents a significant practical advance. The paper properly contextualizes its work against related approaches (e.g., [23] on graph projection block splitting) and clearly explains how their framework differs (more general blocking scheme, better cache utilization). The main novelty lies in the practical implementation details for scaling CLIME to extreme dimensions, rather than in the algorithmic core itself. The paper is sufficiently distinct from recent NIPS papers on distributed optimization (e.g., [6, 9, 22, 23]) as it specifically targets the unique challenges of precision matrix estimation.

**Significance:** The problem of large-scale sparse precision estimation is of significant importance in high-dimensional statistics, machine learning, and data analysis. The authors demonstrate that their approach can solve problems with one million dimensions (one trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, which represents a substantial advance over existing methods. This has clear practical implications for applications in genomics, climate science, and other domains with high-dimensional data. The framework is also generalizable to other constrained optimization problems, as noted by the authors. While the scalability gains largely stem from CLIME's simple structure (which is well-known to be amenable to parallelization), the authors provide a robust, practical implementation that makes this approach accessible to practitioners.

**Strengths:** 
- Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Excellent experimental evaluation demonstrating scalability
- Clear and well-organized presentation
- Practical implementation details for both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems
- Addressing a critical problem in high-dimensional statistics

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited detail on accuracy achieved by each method (only runtime comparisons provided)
- The main algorithmic novelty is somewhat incremental, with scalability largely benefiting from CLIME's inherent structure
- The comparison with DC-QUIC could be more thorough (DC-QUIC's block-diagonal assumption is mentioned but not fully explored)

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant practical contribution to the field of large-scale statistical computation. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, the practical implementation and scalability results are impressive and address an important problem. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The authors have successfully demonstrated that their framework scales almost linearly with the number of cores, which is a substantial advance over existing methods. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a significant problem with a clear, practical solution that advances the state of the art in scalable statistical computation.
