This paper tackles the challenging problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors present an inexact ADMM algorithm tailored for CLIME and a distributed framework capable of scaling to millions of dimensions. The core idea of solving the problem in column blocks and leveraging matrix-matrix multiplications is a significant contribution, particularly in the context of existing methods struggling with ultra-high dimensionality.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results. The connection to related work is adequate, citing key papers in sparse precision matrix estimation and distributed optimization. The theoretical analysis establishing convergence rates for the ADMM algorithm is valuable. The experimental validation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures convincingly demonstrates the scalability of the proposed approach. The comparison to existing methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) is thorough, highlighting the advantages of CLIME-ADMM for very large problem instances. The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structure, while not fully implemented, points toward potential future improvements.

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. First, the theoretical analysis, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The conditions for convergence (specifically η ≥ ρλ2max(C)) are stated without detailed justification or discussion of its practicality in real-world scenarios. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of the tuning parameters (λ, ρ, η) and their selection would be beneficial.

Second, the discussion of leveraging low-rank structure feels a bit tacked on. While theoretically sound, the claim that using the low rank of the sample covariance matrix significantly accelerates computation requires more concrete empirical evidence. The authors mention using low-rank multiplication in their experiments, but the resulting performance gains are not explicitly quantified or compared to the results obtained without exploiting low-rank structure.

Third, the choice of block size 'k' is largely empirical. A more systematic study exploring the optimal block size as a function of problem dimensions, memory constraints, and core count would be helpful. The rationale behind choosing k=100 for the shared-memory experiments and k=500 for the distributed-memory experiments isn't fully clear.

Fourth, the code availability is not mentioned.  Releasing the code would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.  Given the complexity of the distributed framework, code availability is particularly crucial.

Finally, while the paper acknowledges the limitations of ADMM on small problems, it doesn’t thoroughly explore hybrid approaches.  A combination of ADMM for the large-scale portion and potentially other methods for smaller subproblems could potentially improve performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Scalability:** The proposed framework demonstrably scales to problem sizes that are intractable for existing methods. The reported performance on a million-dimensional problem (solved in 11 hours on 400 cores) is impressive.
*   **Novel Approach:** The combination of inexact ADMM with column-block decomposition and optimized matrix multiplications is a novel and effective strategy for tackling this problem.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The convergence analysis, although brief, provides a solid theoretical grounding for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Sparse precision matrix estimation is a crucial task in various applications, and the ability to handle ultra-high dimensional data has significant practical implications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Depth:** The convergence analysis could be more rigorous and provide more practical guidance on parameter selection.
*   **Empirical Exploration of Enhancements:**  The exploitation of low-rank structure needs more thorough empirical validation. The optimal block size selection requires more systematic investigation.
*   **Lack of Code Availability:** This hinders reproducibility and limits the ability of others to build upon the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The scalability of the proposed framework is a significant achievement. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the concerns regarding the theoretical analysis, the empirical exploration of enhancements (low-rank structure and block size), and the inclusion of code availability.  Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
