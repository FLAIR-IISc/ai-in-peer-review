- Paper proposes a column-block linearized (inexact) ADMM for scalable CLIME, exploiting low-rank C = AA^T and sparsity in iterates to reduce per-iteration cost and use BLAS3-friendly kernels.  
- Authors implement shared- and distributed-memory versions with block-cyclic data distribution and show O(1/T) ergodic convergence under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2; experiments include p up to 10^6.

- Connects to graphical lasso, QUIC/DC-QUIC, prior CLIME solvers, linearized ADMM literature, and classic HPC blocking/ScaLAPACK techniques.

- Algorithmic soundness: linearized ADMM updates are standard and justified; proximal condition ensures convexity and metric nonnegativity.  
- Theory: O(1/T) ergodic/objective and residual bounds match existing analyses, but novelty in rates is limited.  
- Systems: distributed design and block-cyclic partitioning are appropriate; empirical scaling and block-size observations are convincing.

- Clarity issues: symmetrization/positive-definiteness handling of Ω̂ is not specified.  
- Clarity issues: practical guidance for choosing ρ and η (and estimating λ_max(C)) is missing.  
- Clarity issues: memory/IO for p = 10^6 (per-node memory, sparse output format) is not quantified.  
- Clarity issues: Theorem 3 needs precise assumptions about independence, constants, and λ adjustment.

- Originality: main novelty is engineering (column-block ADMM + HPC blocking); theoretical and low-rank ideas are standard.

- Significance: enabling CLIME at p in the millions is impactful for ultrahigh-dimensional, low-sample problems.  
- Limitation: approach relies strongly on n ≪ p; costs and memory may become prohibitive as n grows.

- Strength: maps CLIME to closed-form, BLAS3-heavy updates enabling efficient implementation.  
- Strength: solid distributed design with attention to load balance and cache effects.  
- Strength: convincing empirical scaling to hundreds of cores and p = 10^6.  
- Strength: practical exploitation of low-rank structure yields tangible speedups.

- Weakness: limited statistical validation of covariance perturbation/truncation and lack of end-to-end error bounds for CLIME after perturbation.  
- Weakness: no discussion of symmetrization/PD enforcement, which affects usability of Ω̂.  
- Weakness: comparisons emphasize runtime and precision/recall but lack comprehensive accuracy metrics (norm errors, matched FDR).  
- Weakness: unclear performance and memory behavior as n increases (e.g., n = 5k–20k).  
- Weakness: communication complexity analysis is qualitative; bytes-moved and compute/communication overlap not detailed.  
- Weakness: parameter choices (ρ, η, k, block sizes) appear tuned; auto-tuning heuristics are absent.

- Question: How is Ω̂ symmetrized/regularized to ensure PD, and what is the impact on runtime/accuracy?  
- Question: How are ρ and η set in practice; how sensitive is performance to these choices?  
- Question: What is the sparsity of the final Ω̂ for p = 10^6 and how is it stored/written?  
- Question: How does performance degrade as n increases (examples for n in thousands to tens of thousands)?  
- Question: Can you provide statistical error plots comparing truncated-C vs untruncated approaches?

- Arguments for acceptance: fills an important scalability gap with a practical, well-engineered distributed solution and strong empirical scaling.  
- Arguments for acceptance: methodology is sound and likely reusable for other constrained estimators.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical contributions are incremental and statistical guarantees for perturbation are underdeveloped.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing practical details (symmetrization/PD, parameter selection, output handling) limit usability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: evaluation of statistical accuracy is limited and applicability is narrowed by requirement n ≪ p.

- Overall: solid systems-and-algorithms contribution with practical value in the ultrahigh-dimensional, low-sample regime; would benefit from stronger statistical treatment of perturbation and clearer PD/symmetrization and parameter-memory details.
