- Contributions: inexact ADMM for CLIME replacing column LPs with elementwise updates and matrix multiplies.
- Contributions: column-block formulation exploiting matrix–matrix multiplies for cache and parallelism.
- Contributions: distributed block-cyclic framework for load-balanced scaling to hundreds of cores.
- Contributions: O(1/T) convergence rates for objective gap and feasibility residuals.
- Exploitation: reduces multiply cost using low-rank sample covariance C = A A′ from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k).
- Exploitation: discusses leveraging iterate sparsity and safe truncation of small entries in C.
- Experiments: strong scalability and runtime wins versus CLIME solvers and DC-QUIC, including a 1M-dimension 400-core run.
- Relation to prior work: builds on CLIME (Cai et al.) and scalable precision estimation (QUIC/DC-QUIC) and distributed ADMM literature.
- Difference from DC-QUIC: targets CLIME’s LP (no PD constraint) enabling matrix-op-heavy, highly parallel implementation.
- Technical soundness: linearized ADMM updates and linearization condition are reasonable and align with known analyses.
- Notational issues: inconsistent dual scaling/augmented Lagrangian definitions and mixed symbols (Y vs Ŷ, V̂).
- Theorem 2: problematic notation in weighting term (‖XT − XT−1‖2η/ρ I − C^2); metric and positivity need clarification.
- Algorithm text/alg mismatch: V̂t = CYt vs. Ŷt symbol inconsistency should be unified.
- Post-processing: paper should state whether/how symmetrization/PD projection is applied at scale for CLIME outputs.
- Experimental gaps: missing details on stopping criteria, tolerance effects, and communication vs computation breakdown.
- 1M experiment details missing: report n, per-node memory footprint, and how full p^2 solutions are stored or streamed.
- Comparisons: DC-QUIC solves a different objective; consider time-to-reach matched recovery accuracy (AUC/F1) or CLIME-only baselines.
- Theorem 3: plausible but should tie truncation thresholds to deviation bounds, λ selection, and give deterministic corollary with constants.
- Clarity: overall clear and well-organized but needs notational fixes, typos, and explicit per-iteration cost summaries and a memory/layout schematic.
- Originality: algorithmic pieces are standard, but their combination with low-rank/sparsity exploitation and a high-performance distributed design is a nontrivial, useful contribution.
- Significance: high — enables million-scale sparse precision estimation and provides broadly useful systems/implementation lessons.
- Pros: strong systems engineering, simple efficient core (elementwise + GEMM), practical low-rank insights, and convergence guarantees.
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty, comparisons include different-objective baselines, notational inconsistencies, and unclear PD/symmetry handling and λ selection.
- Suggestions: fix notation and Theorem 2, specify stopping/tolerance choices, report communication/computation breakdown, detail 1M run memory/I/O, add symmetrization/PD cost or justification, and add deterministic truncation corollary with constants.
- Recommendation: weak accept (poster) — solid systems/engineering paper with impressive empirical scale; clarify points and strengthen comparisons to improve impact.
