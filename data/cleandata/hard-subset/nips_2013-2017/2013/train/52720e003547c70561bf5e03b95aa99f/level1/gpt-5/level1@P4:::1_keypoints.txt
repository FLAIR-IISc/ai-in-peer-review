- Proposes an inexact (linearized) ADMM tailored to CLIME that updates column blocks jointly, enabling matrix–matrix operations instead of column-wise updates.  
- Per-iteration costs reduce to elementwise ops plus two matmuls, allowing efficient use of BLAS-3 and low-rank acceleration when C = A A′.  
- Establishes O(1/T) ergodic suboptimality and primal feasibility rates under step-size condition η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2 (Theorems 1–2).  
- Provides a perturbation/thresholding result (Theorem 3) showing sparsified sample covariances retain ∞-norm deviation bounds (straightforward concentration argument).  
- Exploits iterate-induced sparsity, sparsified covariance, and low-rank structure to reduce matmul cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k) when p ≫ n.  
- Develops scalable shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations with block-cyclic data distribution for load balance and cache efficiency.  
- Demonstrates strong scaling to 400 cores and solves p ≈ 10^6 (~10^12 parameters) in ~11 hours — an impressive large-scale empirical showcase.  
- Empirical comparisons vs CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC show favorable scalability and competitive accuracy, though baselines appear single-threaded.  
- The column-block strategy and block-cyclic distribution are the primary original contributions; the linearized ADMM rate and CLIME splitting are not novel.  
- Theory is correctly derived and plausible; Theorem 2’s matrix-weighted norm aligns with step-size conditions, but theoretical novelty is limited.  
- Algorithmic design decisions (block updates, low-rank matmuls) are sound and practically impactful.  
- Empirics convincingly show scalability, but statistical accuracy reporting is limited (mostly precision/recall) and baseline parallelization/low-rank tricks may be unfairly missing.  
- Clarity issues: inconsistent norm notation, ambiguous pseudocode scaling (ρ/η vs ρη), omission of symmetrization/PD post-processing, and unspecified stopping criteria.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of solution quality vs runtime, under-specified tuning (ρ, η, λ) and tolerances, modest theoretical novelty (Theorem 3 incremental), and missing communication/memory profiling.  
- Suggestions: report stopping/KKT criteria and sensitivity to ρ/η, include symmetrization/PD steps and costs, add parallel/optimized baselines, show accuracy/runtime trade-offs, and quantify communication/memory overheads.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a practical, implementable method, strong empirical scaling, and appropriate convergence guarantees.  
- Arguments against acceptance: primary novelty is systems/engineering rather than theory, potentially unfair empirical comparisons, and some clarity/practical guidance gaps.
