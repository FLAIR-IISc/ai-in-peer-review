

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of sparse precision matrix estimation in ultra-high dimensional settings (p = 10^6 dimensions, p^2 = 10^12 parameters), which has been a significant bottleneck for existing methods. The authors propose a distributed framework based on an inexact ADMM variant of the CLIME estimator, which solves the problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column. This approach leverages both the separable structure of CLIME and the potential sparsity/low-rank structure of the covariance matrix to enable efficient parallel computation. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) an inexact ADMM algorithm with O(1/T) convergence rates, (2) a column-block approach that enables efficient parallelization through matrix-matrix operations, and (3) a scalable distributed framework using block cyclic distribution for load balancing.

The work builds upon prior NIPS contributions on sparse precision estimation (e.g., [3, 19, 25, 28]) and distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 6, 9, 23]), while making significant advances in scalability. Previous methods like CLIME [3], Tiger [19], and DC-QUIC [13] have strong statistical guarantees but fail to scale beyond a few thousand variables due to memory constraints and lack of efficient parallelization.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides strong theoretical foundations with convergence rate analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors carefully analyze both the computational complexity and statistical properties of their approach, and they demonstrate significant improvements in scalability (11 hours for 1 million dimensions on 400 cores). The algorithm is technically sound, with appropriate attention to the details of parallelization and distributed computing.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with detailed pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and thorough explanations of the key innovations. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented in a logical manner. The relationship to previous work is clearly articulated, and the paper adequately addresses how it differs from related approaches like GPBS [23].

**Originality:** The column-block approach to solving CLIME is novel and addresses a key bottleneck in previous methods. The authors effectively leverage the problem's structure (sparsity and low-rank) to design an efficient parallel algorithm that scales to previously intractable problem sizes. This represents a meaningful advancement over prior work that either solved column-by-column (incurring high communication costs) or relied on restrictive assumptions about data structure.

**Significance:** The problem of ultra-high dimensional precision matrix estimation is of significant practical importance in genomics, climate science, and other fields with massive datasets. The proposed framework solves problems that are currently impossible with existing methods, demonstrating substantial speedups (e.g., 400x faster than Tiger on large datasets). The distributed framework with block cyclic distribution could also benefit other large-scale constrained optimization problems.

## Weaknesses

The paper would benefit from greater detail on several aspects. The theoretical convergence analysis is presented in the supplement, and the main text would be strengthened by including a brief explanation of the key proof techniques. The paper mentions that "k depends on the size of process grid, block size in matrix multiplication, cache size and probably the sparsity pattern of matrices" but doesn't provide guidance on how to select optimal block sizes in practice, which would be valuable for practitioners.

The experimental section could be strengthened by comparing with more recent distributed methods (e.g., [2, 6, 9]) beyond the three methods already considered. While the low-rank assumption (C = AA^T) is reasonable for n << p, the paper could better address how the method behaves when this assumption is violated or when the covariance matrix has a more complex structure.

## Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solves a significant practical problem that has been intractable with existing methods
- Makes clear theoretical and practical advances over prior work
- Provides comprehensive experimental validation showing substantial scalability
- The column-block approach is a clever and effective solution to the parallelization bottleneck
- The distributed framework with block cyclic distribution is well-designed and well-explained

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Theoretical analysis is somewhat brief in the main text
- Some details about optimal block size selection are missing
- Limited comparison with the most recent distributed optimization methods
- The low-rank assumption might limit applicability in some scenarios

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale statistical learning. The proposed framework effectively addresses a critical scalability bottleneck in precision matrix estimation, demonstrating the ability to solve problems that are orders of magnitude larger than what is possible with existing methods. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the approach is clearly explained. While the paper could be strengthened by including more details about optimal block size selection and expanding the comparison with recent distributed methods, these are minor concerns that do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS: it is of high quality with strong theoretical and empirical results, clearly written with a well-organized structure, makes an original contribution that advances the state-of-the-art, and addresses a problem of significant practical importance. I recommend acceptance.
