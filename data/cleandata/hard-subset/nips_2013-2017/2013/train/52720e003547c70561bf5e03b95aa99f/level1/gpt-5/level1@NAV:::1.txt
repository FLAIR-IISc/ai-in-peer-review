This paper tackles large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via CLIME, proposing an inexact ADMM solver that operates on column blocks and maps naturally to shared- and distributed-memory systems with block-cyclic data distribution. The algorithm linearizes the CX coupling to obtain a closed-form soft-thresholding X-update, uses a box projection for Z, and achieves O(1/T) rates for both objective suboptimality and feasibility residuals under η ≥ ρλmax(C)2, aligning with non-ergodic ADMM results (e.g., He & Yuan) while tailoring the proof to CLIME’s structure. For scalability, the authors exploit (i) low-rank C = AAT to reduce multiplies to O(npk), (ii) iterate sparsity, and (iii) block-cyclic distribution to balance load and cache use. Experiments against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC (NIPS’11/’13) show markedly better strong scaling, including p = 10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) on 400 cores in ~11 hours. The work connects to prior scalable ML systems (GraphLab, parameter server), distributed ADMM (Boyd et al.; Parikh & Boyd GPBS), and fast graphical model solvers (GLasso/Friedman; QUIC/DC-QUIC; Mazumder & Hastie).

Quality: The algorithmic derivations are sound and consistent with inexact ADMM theory; the block updates and complexity accounting are clear. Theorem 3 on covariance perturbations is interesting, though it is not used in experiments and raises questions about maintaining PSD and low-rank factorizations for C + Δ. Empirics convincingly demonstrate scaling; accuracy comparisons are limited beyond precision–recall on synthetic data.

Clarity: Generally well organized, but there are typos/incomplete references, missing details on stopping criteria, parameter selection (ρ, η, λ), communication costs, and memory footprint at extreme scale. The role of perturbation is underexplained.

Originality: While CLIME, ADMM, and block-cyclic distribution are established, their integration into a column-block ADMM with closed-form inexact updates and demonstrated million-dimensional scaling is a useful and nontrivial systems contribution.

Significance: High practical impact for large-scale GGM estimation; likely to be used or extended by practitioners in statistics and HPC-ML. Broader applicability to constrained convex programs is plausible.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong systems contribution with impressive scaling on both shared/distributed settings.
- Solid convergence analysis for the tailored inexact ADMM.
- Careful engineering (column blocks, low-rank multiplies, block-cyclic distribution).

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited accuracy/estimation-quality evaluation at scale; TIGER often yields better statistical performance.
- Perturbation theorem is intriguing but not validated empirically; PSD and factorization issues unaddressed.
- Incomplete references and missing implementation details (stopping criteria, comms analysis, code availability).

Suggestions: Add communication/runtime breakdowns; report memory use; provide ablations for block size k and η, ρ sensitivity; evaluate estimation error on larger real/synthetic settings; clarify applicability/limitations of covariance perturbation; and complete/correct references.
