This paper addresses the problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors present an inexact ADMM algorithm and a distributed framework to handle problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The core idea is to solve CLIME in column blocks, leveraging elementwise operations and parallel matrix multiplications. The paper provides convergence rate analysis and empirical validation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, demonstrating almost linear scalability with the number of cores.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in high-dimensional statistics – the computational burden of precision matrix estimation. The choice of CLIME is justified by its theoretical properties and inherent computational advantages. The proposed inexact ADMM variant and the column-block approach are reasonable and potentially effective strategies for scaling up the computation. The theoretical analysis of convergence rates is a positive aspect, although the reliance on prior work [26, 12] is evident. The empirical results are comprehensive, showcasing scalability on different architectures and a comparison to existing methods. The detailed discussion of load balancing and the block cyclic distribution scheme in the distributed-memory framework is valuable. The paper is generally well-written and organized.

However, several areas could be strengthened. While the theoretical convergence analysis is present, the practical implications of the parameters (e.g., η, λ, ρ) and their tuning are not thoroughly discussed. How sensitive is the performance to these parameters, and what guidelines can be provided for their selection in practical applications? The comparisons to existing methods are limited; for instance, DC-QUIC's performance drastically changes with sparsity, but this is only briefly noted. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between different methods under varying conditions would be beneficial. The presentation of the experimental results could also be improved. The figures are sometimes difficult to interpret, and clearer labeling/scaling would aid understanding. Furthermore, while the paper mentions leveraging sparsity in the sample covariance matrix (Theorem 3), this is not fully integrated into the experimental evaluation. Did exploiting this sparsity in practice lead to further performance improvements?

Originality: The combination of an inexact ADMM tailored to CLIME with a distributed column-block solving strategy is a novel contribution. While ADMM and distributed computation are established techniques, their application to this specific problem and the careful consideration of block sizes and data distribution represent a significant advancement. The reliance on existing convergence results and the basic ADMM framework doesn't negate the originality of the overall approach.

Significance: The ability to estimate precision matrices with millions of dimensions has significant implications for various fields, including genomics, finance, and image processing. The near-linear scalability achieved by the proposed method makes it a practical solution for these large-scale problems. The work has the potential to impact both researchers and practitioners dealing with high-dimensional data.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Addresses an important and challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated approach for scaling up CLIME estimation.
* Provides theoretical convergence guarantees.
* Demonstrates substantial scalability through comprehensive experiments.
* The distributed framework and block cyclic data distribution are well-designed.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and tuning.
* Incomplete comparison to existing methods under varying conditions.
* Presentation of experimental results could be improved for clarity.
* Limited integration of sparsity exploitation into the experimental validation.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work is likely to be valuable to the NIPS community. While some improvements could be made, the paper is currently suitable for acceptance.
