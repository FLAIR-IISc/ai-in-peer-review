## Review of Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high dimensions, specifically focusing on scalability to datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The authors present a distributed implementation of the CLIME estimator using an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm. They propose optimizations including column-block processing and a block cyclic data distribution strategy to improve performance on both shared and distributed-memory architectures. 

The work builds upon existing research in sparse precision matrix estimation (e.g., [28, 3, 19]) and distributed optimization frameworks (e.g., [6, 23]).  The choice of CLIME is well-motivated by its theoretical properties and inherent structure conducive to parallelization. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms to “Big Data” [29, 22, 6, 7, 20, 2, 23, 9].  The proposed use of column-block ADMM, combined with careful consideration of data distribution, represents a significant step beyond simply applying existing distributed ADMM frameworks to this specific problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The primary strength of this paper is its demonstrated scalability. The experimental results convincingly show near-linear scaling with the number of cores, reaching impressive performance on a million-dimensional dataset.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** Providing convergence rates for the proposed ADMM algorithm adds significant theoretical weight to the work. 
* **Practical Optimizations:** The column-block approach and block cyclic data distribution are insightful optimizations tailored to the specific problem and hardware constraints. 
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to existing algorithms, they are somewhat limited. A more comprehensive comparison against other scalable precision matrix estimation methods (if available) would strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different parameter settings (e.g., block size, regularization parameter) on performance is not thoroughly explored. A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Overly Detailed Notation:** The notation section is quite extensive and could be streamlined without losing crucial information.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant problem with practical relevance.
* The proposed algorithm and framework demonstrably scale to previously inaccessible problem sizes.
* The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the work.
* The experimental results are compelling and validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to existing methods is not exhaustive.
* A more in-depth analysis of parameter sensitivity is needed.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The demonstrated scalability of the proposed algorithm is particularly impressive and addresses a critical bottleneck in sparse precision matrix estimation. While some aspects could be further refined, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding comparison and parameter sensitivity.
