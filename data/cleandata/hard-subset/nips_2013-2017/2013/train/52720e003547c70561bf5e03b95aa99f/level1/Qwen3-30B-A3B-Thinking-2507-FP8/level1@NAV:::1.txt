

This paper presents a distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, targeting ultra-high-dimensional settings (millions of dimensions). The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column-blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging block cyclic distribution for load balancing and exploiting sparse/low-rank structures. They establish O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions, and demonstrate near-linear scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, achieving a 1-million-dimensional precision matrix estimation in 11 hours on 400 cores.

The work builds on prior NIPS contributions like Hsieh et al. (2011) on distributed optimization for graphical models and Boyd et al. (2011) on ADMM, but makes significant advances in scalability. The column-block approach represents a clear innovation over previous CLIME solvers that process columns individually, and the block cyclic distribution strategy addresses load imbalance more effectively than prior distributed frameworks like GPBS (Parikh & Boyd, 2014).

Strengths include strong theoretical guarantees with convergence rates, excellent experimental results showing superior scalability compared to state-of-the-art methods (Tiger, DC-QUIC), and a well-structured presentation of the algorithm. The paper clearly demonstrates how to handle the trillion-parameter challenge through clever algorithmic and architectural choices.

Weaknesses include limited comparison with very recent distributed optimization methods (e.g., Hogwild! variants), and the experimental section could better contextualize the practical significance of the 11-hour runtime on 400 cores. The theoretical analysis is sound but somewhat brief, with proofs relegated to an appendix.

Pro: Novel column-block approach with strong scalability claims, well-motivated use of sparse/low-rank structure, excellent empirical results on large-scale problems.

Con: Limited discussion of practical deployment challenges beyond the experimental setup, and the theoretical contribution is incremental rather than transformative.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable statistical learning, particularly for precision matrix estimation at massive scale. It addresses an important practical challenge with a technically sound approach that advances the state of the art in distributed optimization. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and addresses a significant problem with meaningful impact. I recommend acceptance.
