- Addresses large-scale sparse precision estimation using CLIME with an inexact ADMM that updates column blocks via soft-thresholding/box projections and matrix multiplications.  
- Proves O(1/T) convergence rates for objective gap and feasibility residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2.  
- Exploits low-rank structure C = A A^T (p ≫ n) and block-cyclic distribution to reduce matrix-multiplication cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k).  
- Implements shared- and distributed-memory versions and demonstrates near-linear scaling, including p = 10^6 solved in 11 hours on 400 cores.  
- Builds on prior work: CLIME statistical theory, ADMM optimization, block-cyclic parallel matrix multiplication, and scalable precision solvers (QUIC/DC-QUIC).  
- Novelty is primarily systems/engineering: a distributed CLIME solver using column-block ADMM enabling million-dimensional runs.  
- Theory quality: O(1/T) rate is sound and consistent with literature; condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 is standard.  
- Theorem 3 (perturbed covariance) is plausible but stated at a high level; constants and interactions with λ are not explicit.  
- Algorithmic correctness: ADMM splitting and linearization are standard; column-block reformulation is equivalent to column-wise CLIME.  
- Missing discussion on symmetrization/positive-definiteness: how symmetry/PD are enforced or restored across independently solved blocks is not addressed.  
- Empirics: careful implementations and informative scaling experiments; baselines include TIGER and DC-QUIC.  
- Evaluation emphasizes runtime and scaling over statistical accuracy; limited analysis of estimation error norms or support recovery at scale.  
- Million-dimensional run reports runtime but omits accuracy metrics, leaving effects of perturbations/engineering choices unclear.  
- Clarity: generally well written with clear algorithms and systems description.  
- Missing details: practical choices for λ, ρ, η; stopping criteria and their impact; precise statement/proof sketch for Theorem 3; minor typos/notation inconsistencies.  
- Originality: constituent algorithmic and systems ideas are known individually; contribution is in their combination and practical scale-up.  
- Significance: scaling precision estimation to millions of variables is important and the systems design is likely to be adopted.  
- Main limitations: limited statistical evaluation and under-specified treatment of covariance perturbation and post-processing.  
- Pros: simple parallelizable ADMM with O(1/T) rates; column-block design leverages matrix–matrix kernels; effective distributed design and low-rank exploitation; convincing scalability.  
- Cons: weak statistical evaluation; unclear symmetrization/PD handling; Theorem 3 under-specified; parameter selection/stopping criteria not fully described; theoretical novelty incremental.  
- Suggested improvements: add symmetrization/PD post-processing section and costs; report quantitative accuracy metrics and effect of truncating C; provide communication/computation breakdown and performance model; compare to strong multithreaded baselines and report memory/node; clarify parameter heuristics.  
- Recommendation: Leaning toward acceptance as a strong systems/optimization contribution despite limited theoretical novelty and shallower statistical evaluation.
