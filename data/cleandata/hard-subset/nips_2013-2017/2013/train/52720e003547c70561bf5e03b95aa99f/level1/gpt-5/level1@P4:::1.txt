Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator [3]. It proposes an inexact (linearized) ADMM algorithm tailored to CLIME that updates column blocks of the precision matrix jointly, rather than one column at a time. The algorithm’s per-iteration work reduces to elementwise operations and two matrix multiplications, enabling efficient use of BLAS-3 kernels. The authors establish O(1/T) rates for both suboptimality and primal feasibility (Theorems 1–2), under a standard step-size condition η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2. They also show how to exploit structure: (i) iterate-induced sparsity to accelerate matmuls, (ii) sparsified/thresholded sample covariance matrices while retaining deviation bounds (Theorem 3), and (iii) the inherent low rank of C = A A′ when p ≫ n, reducing matmul cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k). They develop scalable shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations with block-cyclic data distribution, and demonstrate strong scaling up to 400 cores, solving a problem with p = 10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) in ~11 hours. Empirical comparisons on synthetic and real data against CLIME (flare), TIGER [19], and DC-QUIC [13] show favorable scalability and competitive accuracy.

Relative to prior optimization for sparse precision estimation (graphical lasso [8,24], QUIC/DC-QUIC [14,13], interior-point solvers for CLIME [3,28], and ADMM frameworks [2,23,9]), the contribution is: a linearized ADMM specialized to the CLIME splitting, a column-block strategy enabling matrix–matrix operations and block-cyclic distribution for load balance, and a practical distributed implementation at unprecedented scale. The O(1/T) rate for linearized ADMM is consistent with existing analyses [12,26]; the paper adapts these to the CLIME setting. The perturbation/thresholding argument (Theorem 3) aligns with standard deviation bounds used in high-dimensional covariance estimation.

Quality:
- Theory: The linearized ADMM updates are correctly derived, and the rate statements (ergodic suboptimality and feasibility) are standard and plausible under η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2. The inclusion of matrix-weighted norm in Theorem 2 matches the step-size condition that ensures positive semidefiniteness. The perturbation result (Theorem 3) formalizes that bounded, sub-exponential perturbations preserve ∞-norm deviation bounds; while useful, it is a straightforward application of concentration and triangle inequality.
- Algorithmic design: The column-block formulation and the use of low-rank multiplications are sound and have clear performance implications. The block-cyclic distribution is a well-justified choice for load balancing and cache efficiency (consistent with ScaLAPACK practice [15]).
- Empirics: The scalability results are convincing, with careful analysis of both “parallel speedup” and “block-size speedup” in shared and distributed settings. However, some comparisons may not be entirely apples-to-apples: baselines appear single-threaded and may not exploit the same low-rank trick; there is limited reporting of statistical accuracy beyond precision/recall curves on synthetic data. Details on stopping criteria, tolerances, and parameter choices (ρ, η, λ) are sparse.

Clarity:
The paper is overall well written and organized, with a clear presentation of the algorithm and the distributed framework. A few clarity issues:
- Notation for norms: early definitions emphasize elementwise norms, but Theorem 2 uses a matrix-weighted 2-norm; defining ⟨X, M X⟩-type norms explicitly would help.
- Some pseudocode lines (e.g., V-update scaling ρ/η vs ρη) would benefit from consistent notation and units.
- Handling of symmetry/positive-definiteness: CLIME solutions are typically symmetrized post hoc; this is not discussed.
- Stopping criteria and convergence diagnostics are not specified.

Originality:
The linearized ADMM with O(1/T) rate is not novel per se [12,26], and CLIME’s column-wise separability is known [3]. The originality lies in the column-block strategy to leverage matrix–matrix multiplications, the concrete distributed implementation with block-cyclic layout, and demonstrating orders-of-magnitude scaling. The perturbation theorem is incremental.

Significance:
The ability to scale sparse precision estimation to p ≈ 10^6 with near-linear speedups is practically significant. The framework’s design (elementwise ops + matmuls, block-cyclic distribution) is likely reusable for other constrained estimators with similar structures. From a statistical-methods standpoint, the work does not advance estimation theory, but materially advances computational scalability for a widely used estimator.

Strengths:
- Clear, efficient algorithm with simple primitives amenable to HPC.
- Solid and appropriate convergence guarantees for the proposed linearized ADMM.
- Thoughtful exploitation of low-rank structure and block-cyclic distribution.
- Strong empirical scalability on both shared and distributed architectures; impressive large-scale demonstration.
- Competitive or better runtimes than state-of-the-art methods; reasonable accuracy.

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of solution quality at scale (e.g., estimation error vs runtime) and of fairness in comparisons; baselines are not parallelized or low-rank-accelerated.
- Tuning and stopping details (ρ, η, λ selection; tolerances) are under-specified; sensitivity analyses are absent.
- No discussion of symmetrization/PD-ness of the final precision estimate, common in CLIME implementations.
- Theorem 3 is a modest and expected observation; novelty is primarily systems/algorithmic engineering rather than theory.
- Communication/computation trade-offs and memory/I/O footprints are not quantitatively profiled.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report stopping criteria, KKT residual thresholds, and sensitivity to ρ and η; provide guidelines for practitioners.
- Include symmetrization and PD post-processing details and assess its cost.
- Provide parallel/optimized baselines where feasible, or clearly separate single-thread vs multi-thread comparisons.
- Add accuracy/runtime trade-off plots at scale (e.g., held-out likelihood, support recovery vs compute).
- Quantify communication overhead and memory usage per node; analyze block-size sensitivity more systematically.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a clean, implementable method.
- Demonstrates compelling large-scale results with near-linear scaling.
- Provides convergence guarantees appropriate to the method.
- Likely to be useful to practitioners and to spur further scalable implementations.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited novelty in theory; much of the contribution is engineering.
- Empirical comparisons may not be fully fair; limited evaluation of statistical accuracy at scale.
- Some clarity gaps (notation, post-processing for PD) and missing practical guidance (tuning/stopping).

Overall, this is a strong systems-oriented contribution to scalable precision matrix estimation with adequate theoretical underpinnings and convincing scaling results.
