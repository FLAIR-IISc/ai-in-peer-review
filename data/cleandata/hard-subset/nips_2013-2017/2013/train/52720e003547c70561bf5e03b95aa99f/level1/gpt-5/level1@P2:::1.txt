Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. It proposes an inexact ADMM algorithm that operates on column blocks rather than columns, yielding updates composed of elementwise soft-thresholding/box projections and matrix multiplications. The authors prove O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective gap and feasibility residuals under a standard condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. They further develop shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations using block cyclic data distribution and exploit both dynamic iterate sparsity and the inherent low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = A A^T with p ≫ n) to reduce matrix-multiplication cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k). Empirically, they compare against CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate near-linear scaling with cores, culminating in an example with p = 10^6 (10^12 parameters) solved in 11 hours on 400 cores.

This work builds on: statistical properties of CLIME (Cai et al.), scalable optimization via ADMM (Boyd et al.), parallel matrix multiplication and block-cyclic distribution (ScaLAPACK, Goto & Van de Geijn), and prior scalable precision estimation (QUIC/DC-QUIC; Hsieh et al.). The novelty lies in a distributed CLIME solver with column-block ADMM and a concrete systems design enabling million-dimensional runs.

Quality:
- Theory: The O(1/T) rate for inexact ADMM is sound and consistent with the literature (e.g., He & Yuan). The condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 is standard; proofs are deferred to the supplement. The “perturbed covariance” result (Theorem 3) is plausible but stated at a high level; constants and their interaction with the CLIME tuning λ are not made explicit, making it hard to assess the practical impact of thresholding C on estimation error/support recovery.
- Algorithmic correctness: The ADMM splitting and linearization are standard. The column-block reformulation is equivalent to column-wise CLIME. However, CLIME solutions typically require post-symmetrization; the paper does not discuss how symmetry/PD are enforced or restored across independently solved blocks, nor the effect on accuracy.
- Empirics: The implementations appear careful and the scaling results (shared vs. distributed, block sizes, core counts) are informative. The baselines include TIGER and DC-QUIC; timings and precision/recall on synthetic data are reported. However, the evaluation emphasizes runtime and scaling over statistical accuracy. Matching sparsity across methods is reasonable, but there is limited analysis of estimation error (e.g., spectral/Frobenius norms) or support recovery at scale. The million-dimensional run reports time but not accuracy, which is understandable but leaves unanswered how the proposed perturbations/engineering choices affect the estimator.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written. The algorithm is clearly stated with helpful notation, and the systems sections explain block-cyclic distribution and load balancing. Some details are missing or could be clarified:
- How λ, ρ, and η are chosen in practice; stopping criteria and their effect on runtime/accuracy.
- Symmetrization/PD post-processing for CLIME and how it is implemented in the distributed setting.
- More precise statement and proof sketch for Theorem 3, including constants and guidance on threshold levels relative to n and p.
- Minor typos/formatting in Algorithm 1 (step numbers/comments) and notation consistency.

Originality:
The constituent ideas (inexact ADMM linearization, column-blocking to enable matrix–matrix ops, low-rank multiplications using A A^T, block-cyclic distribution) are known individually. The contribution is in combining them to produce a practically scalable distributed solver for CLIME with convergence guarantees and demonstrating million-dimensional runs. This is a useful engineering advance with some algorithmic refinements (column-block ADMM, rate guarantees specialized to this setting), though the theoretical novelty per se is modest.

Significance:
Scaling precision estimation to millions of variables is important for large-scale graphical modeling. A distributed, open approach that relies on well-optimized matrix kernels and robust data distribution is likely to be adopted and extended. The empirical scalability and the clear systems design are valuable. The main limitations are on the statistical side: the work would be stronger with more thorough accuracy evaluation and a tighter treatment of covariance perturbation and its effect on CLIME’s guarantees.

Pros:
- Clear, simple ADMM variant with O(1/T) rates and easily parallelizable updates.
- Column-block approach that leverages matrix–matrix multiplication and achieves good cache and kernel utilization.
- Thoughtful distributed design (block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O) with convincing scalability results, including p = 10^6.
- Practical exploitation of low-rank structure (p ≫ n) to reduce cost.
- Empirical comparison against relevant baselines and analysis of speedups on different architectures.

Cons:
- Limited statistical evaluation; little discussion of accuracy–speed trade-offs at scale.
- Symmetrization/PD handling for CLIME is not discussed; potential mismatch between independently solved blocks.
- Theorem 3 on covariance perturbation is under-specified; guidance for practitioners is missing.
- Parameter selection (λ, ρ, η), stopping criteria, and sensitivity analyses are not fully described.
- Novelty of the convergence theory is incremental relative to existing inexact ADMM results.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a section on post-processing (symmetrization, PD projection if used) and its cost/effect on accuracy.
- Provide more quantitative accuracy metrics (estimation error norms, ROC curves) on synthetic data, and report how truncating C affects error/support recovery.
- Include a communication/computation breakdown and a simple performance model predicting optimal block sizes and process grids.
- Compare to strong multi-threaded baselines (e.g., QUIC on many cores) and report memory per node.
- Clarify parameter choices and stopping criteria; provide heuristics for η and ρ tied to λ_max(C).

Recommendation: Leaning toward acceptance for NIPS as a strong systems/optimization contribution to large-scale graphical model estimation. While theoretical novelty is limited and statistical evaluation could be deeper, the scalability, engineering, and practical impact are compelling and relevant to the community.
