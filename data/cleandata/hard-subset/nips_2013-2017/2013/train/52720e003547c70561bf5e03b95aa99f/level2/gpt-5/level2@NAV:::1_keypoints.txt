- Tackles sparse precision matrix estimation in p ≫ n via a column-block inexact ADMM for CLIME, designed for shared and distributed memory with block-cyclic data distribution.  
- Proposes linearized ADMM updates with closed-form steps and proves O(1/T) convergence in objective suboptimality and optimality residuals.  
- Implements strategies exploiting low-rank structure (C = AAT), iterate/data sparsity, and introduces a perturbation/truncation result that preserves deviation bounds.  
- Empirically demonstrates near-linear speedups to hundreds of cores and a 10^6-dimensional run, comparing runtime to CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC.  
- Builds on prior CLIME theory and scalable ADMM/QUIC literature, contributing primarily a systems-focused, constrained-problem specialization.  
- Algorithmic derivation and step-size condition are sound and standardly applied, with correct closed-form updates.  
- Perturbation theorem aligns with known concentration results and is theoretically consistent.  
- Empirical scalability is convincing, but statistical accuracy is only lightly evaluated (single precision–recall plot).  
- Limited analysis of communication vs computation breakdowns, memory footprints, and sensitivity to parameters k, ρ, η, and block sizes.  
- Reproducibility would benefit from releasing code and fuller experimental details.  
- Paper is generally clear with helpful algorithms and diagrams, though contains some notation slips/typos.  
- Needs clarification on the symmetrization/positive-definite repair step in the block/distributed setting.  
- Novelty is mainly in cohesive engineering: column-block ADMM mapping to block-cyclic distribution and low-rank multiplications, not in the O(1/T) rate or linearized ADMM itself.  
- High practical significance for ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation and enabling million-scale problems.  
- Pros: strong systems design with clear speedups; solid theoretical guarantees for the inexact ADMM; practical insights on block sizing, block-cyclic layout, and low-rank exploitation.  
- Cons: limited statistical evaluation and unclear trade-offs versus TIGER/QUIC beyond runtime; missing details on symmetrization/PD correction and memory/output handling at extreme scales; no open-source code and limited ablation/communication analysis.  
- Suggestions: release code; detail symmetrization/PD repair and final model storage; add ablations over k/η/ρ and block sizes, communication cost analysis, weak/strong scaling and memory usage; evaluate statistical error vs baselines with matched sparsity and assess truncation heuristic’s effect on estimation error.
