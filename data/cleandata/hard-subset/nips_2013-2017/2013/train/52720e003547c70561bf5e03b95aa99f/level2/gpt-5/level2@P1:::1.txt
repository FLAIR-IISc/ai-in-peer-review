Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via CLIME, focusing on computational scalability to ultra-high dimensions. The authors design an inexact ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column-blocks, yielding closed-form elementwise updates (soft-thresholding and box projection) and reducing the computational bottleneck to matrix multiplications. They establish O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective suboptimality and optimality residuals under a step-size condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. To scale, they (i) exploit low-rank structure of the sample covariance C = AA^T (with p >> n), reducing matmul complexity from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k); (ii) develop parallel and distributed implementations using block-cyclic data/parameter distribution; and (iii) discuss how sparsity in iterates and in (a perturbed) sample covariance could further accelerate computation. Experiments demonstrate strong and near-linear speedups on shared- and distributed-memory systems, including an instance with p = 10^6 (p^2 = 10^12 parameters) completed in ~11 hours on 400 cores. Comparisons with TIGER, CLIME (flare), and DC-QUIC show favorable scalability.

This work builds on: CLIME (Cai et al.), ADMM and inexact/linearized ADMM (Boyd et al., He & Yuan, Wang & Banerjee), GPU/HPC parallelization and block-cyclic layouts (ScaLAPACK), and large-scale inverse covariance methods (QUIC, DC-QUIC). ADMM-based approaches for related constrained problems and distributed frameworks are acknowledged, but prior CLIME solvers have not targeted distributed scalability at this magnitude.

Quality:
- Algorithmic soundness: The inexact ADMM derivation is standard: linearize the quadratic term in the X-update and add a proximal term, enabling closed-form soft-thresholding. The Z-update reduces to a box projection, and the Y-update is standard. The O(1/T) rates (objective gap and residuals) are consistent with non-ergodic rates for (linearized) ADMM; the step-size condition is explicit. The use of low-rank multiplications (C X = A(A^T X)) and block-cyclic distribution are technically appropriate and well-justified for load balance and cache efficiency.
- Theory gaps and assumptions: (1) The convergence results hinge on η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2, but guidance on stable, practical selection of ρ and η is limited; experimental sensitivity to these parameters and stopping criteria is not reported. (2) CLIME typically requires symmetrization of the column-wise solutions; how symmetry/PD is handled in the block setting is not specified. (3) The “sample covariance sparsification” idea is interesting, but Theorem 3 is high-level: it ensures a deviation bound persists after adding sub-exponential perturbations, but does not characterize the end-to-end impact on the resulting CLIME estimator (e.g., estimation error bounds vs. unperturbed CLIME), nor discuss preservation of PSD/symmetry. Empirical validation of this acceleration path is not shown.
- Empirical evaluation: The scalability study is thorough and informative (shared vs distributed, block sizes, cores, 1 vs multiple cores per node), and the p=10^6 result is compelling. Comparisons to TIGER/CLIME/DC-QUIC are helpful but not fully apples-to-apples because methods optimize different objectives; timing at matched sparsity is reasonable, but accuracy comparisons are limited to PR curves only on synthetic data. Reporting objective values or estimation error vs sample size would strengthen claims. Details on memory usage, I/O overhead, and communication costs for the largest runs would aid reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear algorithm box and implementation details. A few points could be clearer:
- Be explicit on symmetrization of Ω̂ and any postprocessing to ensure positive definiteness when needed for downstream use.
- Clarify scaled vs unscaled ADMM notation in the augmented Lagrangian (there are minor notational inconsistencies).
- Provide practical guidance for choosing ρ, η, stopping tolerances, and column-block size k (tradeoffs among cache size, process grid, and sparsity).
- Theorem 3: specify if perturbations preserve symmetry and whether hard-thresholding is the intended perturbation; add a brief proof sketch or intuition.

Originality:
The individual components (linearized ADMM, low-rank matmuls, block-cyclic distribution) are known, but their integration for distributed CLIME with column-block optimization and concrete system design is a meaningful engineering contribution. The column-block approach that promotes BLAS-3 efficiency and the systematic HPC treatment for inverse covariance estimation at this scale are novel in this context. Theoretical rates are standard but appropriately tailored.

Significance:
Scalability to millions of variables and trillions of parameters for precision estimation is impactful for large-scale graphical modeling. The practical framework (with demonstrated strong scaling) is likely to be useful to practitioners, and aspects of the design should transfer to other constrained matrix problems. While statistical optimality is inherited from CLIME, the contribution here is primarily computational/systems.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses an important bottleneck: ultra-large-scale sparse precision estimation.
- Clear algorithmic design with closed-form updates and O(1/T) rates.
- Careful, well-reasoned HPC implementation (block-cyclic distribution, matmul decomposition).
- Strong empirical scalability, including very large problem instances uncommon in prior work.
- Potentially reusable framework for other constrained problems.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited guidance on parameter/stopping choices and their effect on convergence time/accuracy.
- Symmetrization/PD handling for the final precision estimate not described.
- Perturbation/threshholding theory is suggestive but not fully developed or empirically validated.
- Cross-method comparisons mix different objectives; more accuracy metrics would strengthen claims.
- Some notation/derivation details (scaled ADMM form) could be polished.

Suggestions for improvement:
1) Add a short section detailing symmetrization and PD postprocessing, and report any effect on runtime/accuracy.
2) Provide practical defaults and sensitivity studies for ρ, η, k, and stopping tolerances; include plots of residuals vs iterations.
3) Expand the evaluation with estimation error metrics (e.g., Frobenius/spectral error to Ω0) on synthetic data, and report objective values where comparable.
4) For the perturbation/truncation idea, either (a) include a theorem that carries through estimation error rates for CLIME under the proposed truncation, or (b) provide empirical results showing negligible statistical degradation with substantial computational benefit.
5) Report memory, I/O, and communication breakdowns for the largest experiments; include data format and parallel I/O details for reproducibility.
6) Minor: clean up notation in the augmented Lagrangian; ensure symmetry of C + Δ; clarify whether the extrapolation term Vt = ρη(2ĈY − ĈYprev) is essential for speed or can be omitted.

Overall assessment:
A strong systems-oriented contribution that makes CLIME practical at previously unreachable scales, with a sound algorithmic foundation and convincing scalability evidence. While some theoretical and empirical details could be strengthened, the paper is likely to be valuable to the community.

Recommendation: Weak accept to accept.
