- Core contribution: an inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME with a distributed framework and column-block solving to scale sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions.
- Technical novelty: column-block ADMM and optimizations leveraging sparse/low-rank structure to enable efficient distributed matrix operations.
- Theoretical result: convergence analysis for the proposed inexact ADMM algorithm (theoretical guarantees).
- System design: detailed distributed framework with block-cyclic data distribution and memory-hierarchy optimizations for massive-scale problems.
- Empirical claim: demonstrates ability to estimate million-dimensional precision matrices (trillions of parameters) showing promising scalability.
- Limited comparisons: experimental evaluation is incomplete and comparisons to methods like flare and DC-QUIC are cursory.
- Missing details: lack of runtime comparisons on the same datasets and absence of parameter settings for competing algorithms.
- Sparse-structure treatment: discussion and practical exploitation of sparsity/low-rank structures are underdeveloped despite theoretical suggestions (Theorem 3).
- Performance unexplained: shared-memory speedup analysis is partial, with unexplained performance degradation at higher core counts for certain block sizes.
- Related work clarity: paper heavily cites prior work but does not clearly articulate its specific contributions relative to papers like [23].
- Presentation: notation is dense and needs more explanatory text for readers unfamiliar with the formulations.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel scalable approach for CLIME, convergence guarantees, and demonstrated large-scale performance.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental breadth, unclear practical benefits for smaller datasets, and underdeveloped sparsity discussion.
- Essential recommendations: expand experiments across dataset sizes and sparsity levels and report parameter settings for all compared algorithms.
- Major/minor recommendations: clarify reasons for shared-memory performance issues, elaborate practical implications of Theorem 3, and improve notation clarity and comparative discussion to related work.
