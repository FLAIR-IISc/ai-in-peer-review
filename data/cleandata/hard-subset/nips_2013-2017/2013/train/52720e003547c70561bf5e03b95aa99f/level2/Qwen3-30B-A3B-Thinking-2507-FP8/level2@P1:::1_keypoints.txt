- Presents a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using an inexact ADMM solver operating on column blocks to tackle ultra-high dimensional problems.
- Proposes a block cyclic data distribution for balanced load across processors and scalable distributed execution.
- Column-block processing is a key innovation that improves scalability over column-by-column methods by enabling multi-column solves and better cache utilization.
- Leverages sparse and low-rank structure to make block matrix multiplications more efficient.
- Establishes O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective value and optimality conditions with rigorous theoretical analysis.
- Demonstrates linear scalability with the number of cores on both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems.
- Comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real data, including estimating a 1M-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.
- Thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) showing superior scalability for large problems.
- Well-written and clearly structured paper with helpful pseudocode and accessible notation for readers familiar with ADMM and precision matrix estimation.
- Related work section effectively contrasts the approach with parameter servers and GPBS, highlighting better handling of global constraints.
- Original contribution: column-block CLIME and block cyclic distribution extend existing distributed optimization methods meaningfully.
- Significant impact: enables handling millions of dimensions with linear scaling, relevant to many high-dimensional applications.
- Minor weakness: lacks explicit discussion of how the distributed approach affects statistical guarantees of the estimator.
- Minor weakness: could add a more intuitive explanation of practical implications of the O(1/T) rate and further justify benefits of column-block processing beyond matrix multiplication efficiency.
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, significant, and well-supported by theory and experiments.
