## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high dimensions, a core task in various fields like bioinformatics and finance. The authors present a scalable algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, leveraging an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) combined with a distributed computing framework capable of handling datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The core innovation lies in solving the CLIME optimization problem in column-blocks, enabling efficient matrix multiplications and parallelization.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods for ultra-large scale problems. The proposed approach builds upon established techniques (CLIME, ADMM) but significantly extends their scalability through the column-block decomposition and distributed implementation. The theoretical analysis providing convergence rates for the algorithm is a definite strength.  The experimental evaluation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures convincingly demonstrates the algorithm’s scalability, achieving near-linear speedup with increasing core count, and showcasing practical performance on a one-million-dimensional dataset.  The authors rightly position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms, referencing relevant literature.

However, the paper lacks some depth in experimental analysis. While speedups are reported, a more detailed comparison to alternative distributed approaches (beyond the mentioned but briefly discussed DC-QUIC) would strengthen the claims. The impact of different block sizes on performance, beyond what’s briefly touched upon, warrants further investigation.  Furthermore, the practical implications of the statistical guarantees for real-world applications could be elaborated upon.  The notation, while standard, can be dense and might benefit from a more accessible explanation, particularly for readers less familiar with the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach with theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrates strong scalability through comprehensive experiments.
*   Well-written and clearly explains the proposed algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental comparison could be more thorough.
*   Discussion of parameter tuning and sensitivity could be expanded.
*   Notation is dense and could be more accessible.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics. While minor improvements could be made, the novelty, technical soundness, and demonstrated scalability make it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
