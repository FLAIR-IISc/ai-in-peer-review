

This paper proposes a scalable distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, with a focus on solving problems with millions of dimensions. The key contributions include an inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME, a column-block approach that leverages matrix-matrix operations for better cache efficiency, and a distributed framework using block cyclic data distribution.

Strengths:
The paper makes a significant contribution to the scalability of precision matrix estimation. The proposed column-block ADMM approach is well-motivated and addresses a real bottleneck in the field - the quadratic scaling of parameters with dimensionality. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-2) establishes convergence rates, which is valuable for the community. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the ability to estimate a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, substantially outperforming state-of-the-art methods on large-scale problems. The distributed framework using block cyclic distribution is well-justified and leads to better load balancing than alternatives.

Weaknesses:
The theoretical contribution appears somewhat incremental. The convergence rate results are similar to those in [26] and [12], and the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how their analysis differs or improves upon prior work. The experimental comparison with DC-QUIC is not sufficiently quantitative - the claim that "DC-QUIC dramatically slows down when sparsity increases" lacks specific metrics to support this assertion. While the paper mentions using low-rank structure (Section 3.2) to accelerate matrix multiplication, the experimental results don't demonstrate the impact of this technique, making it unclear how much performance improvement it actually provides. The claim of "almost linear scaling" with the number of cores (Figures 3 and 4) is somewhat overstated, as the speedups show diminishing returns at higher core counts, especially in shared-memory environments. The paper should more precisely quantify the scaling behavior.

Clarity and Presentation:
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm is presented with pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and detailed complexity analysis. The notation is consistent throughout. The figures (Figures 1-4) effectively illustrate the distributed framework and scalability results. However, the paper would benefit from a more precise explanation of how the theoretical convergence rates relate to the practical implementation, particularly regarding the choice of parameters like η.

Originality and Significance:
The paper is positioned well within the context of recent work on scaling machine learning algorithms to large-scale problems. While the column-block approach for ADMM is not entirely novel (citing [23] for similar block splitting), the specific application to precision matrix estimation with the CLIME estimator and the detailed analysis of the distributed framework make this work significant. The ability to handle problems of unprecedented scale (1 million dimensions) represents a meaningful advance for the field. The distributed framework could be useful for other large-scale constrained optimization problems beyond precision matrix estimation.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant practical contributions to the scalability of precision matrix estimation. The theoretical results are sound but somewhat incremental, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and impressive for the scale of problems addressed. The main weaknesses are the lack of quantitative comparison with DC-QUIC, insufficient demonstration of the low-rank structure benefits, and slightly overstated claims about linear scaling. The authors should address these points to strengthen the paper.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with demonstrated scalability to unprecedented problem sizes. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics. The minor issues identified are easily fixable and don't undermine the main contribution.
