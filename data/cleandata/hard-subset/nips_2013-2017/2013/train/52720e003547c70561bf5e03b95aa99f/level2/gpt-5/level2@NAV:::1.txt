Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles sparse precision matrix estimation via CLIME in the p ≫ n regime, proposing an inexact ADMM that operates on column blocks and maps cleanly to shared- and distributed-memory systems with block-cyclic data distribution. The authors prove O(1/T) convergence in objective suboptimality and optimality residuals for their linearized ADMM updates, and introduce implementation strategies leveraging low-rank structure (C = AAT) and iterate/data sparsity, including a perturbation/truncation result preserving deviation bounds. Experiments compare against CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate near-linear speedups on hundreds of cores, including a 10^6-dimensional run. This builds on CLIME (Cai et al.), scalable optimization/ADMM (Boyd et al.; Parikh & Boyd GPBS), and large-scale inverse covariance estimation (QUIC/DC-QUIC; Graphical Lasso literature), bringing a systems-focused perspective tailored to constrained problems.

Quality: The algorithmic derivation is sound; the inexact ADMM linearization and step-size condition (η ≥ ρλmax^2(C)) are standard and correctly applied, with clear closed-form updates. The perturbation theorem aligns with known concentration results. Empirical evaluation convincingly establishes scalability; however, statistical accuracy is only lightly evaluated (one precision–recall plot), and there is limited analysis of communication/computation breakdowns, memory footprints, or sensitivity to k, ρ, η and block sizes. Reproducibility would benefit from code and fuller experimental details.

Clarity: Generally clear, with a helpful algorithm box and system diagrams. A few notation slips/typos appear, but organization is good. Please clarify the symmetrization/PD repair step customary in CLIME and how it is implemented in the block setting.

Originality: The O(1/T) rate and linearized ADMM are not new per se; the novelty lies in a cohesive, column-block ADMM specialized to CLIME with block-cyclic distribution and low-rank multiplications, plus a simple perturbation/truncation justification. The contribution is primarily algorithm-engineering and systems scalability.

Significance: High practical significance for ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation; enabling million-scale columns is valuable. Usefulness would be strengthened by downstream tasks and comparisons to state-of-the-art large-scale Graphical Lasso (e.g., BigQUIC) under identical hardware.

Pros (for acceptance):
- Strong, well-motivated systems design with clear gains; near-linear speedups to hundreds of cores.
- Solid theoretical guarantees for the proposed inexact ADMM variant.
- Practical insights on block sizing and block-cyclic distribution; low-rank exploitation.

Cons (against acceptance):
- Limited statistical evaluation; unclear trade-offs vs TIGER/QUIC beyond runtime.
- Missing details on symmetrization/PD correction and memory/output handling at trillion-parameter scale.
- No open-source implementation; limited ablations and communication analysis.

Suggestions: Provide code; include symmetrization/PD details and final model storage strategy; add ablations on k/η/ρ and communication cost; report weak/strong scaling and memory usage; evaluate statistical error vs baselines under matched sparsity; assess the truncation heuristic’s effect on estimation error.
