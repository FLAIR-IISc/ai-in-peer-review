**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper presents a large-scale distributed framework for estimating sparse precision matrices using the CLIME estimator. The framework is based on an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm that decomposes the constrained optimization problem into elementary matrix multiplications and element-wise operations. The authors establish convergence rates for both the objective and optimality conditions and demonstrate the scalability of the algorithm on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on sparse precision matrix estimation, including the CLIME estimator [3] and ADMM algorithms for constrained optimization problems [2, 26]. The authors also draw inspiration from recent advances in scaling up machine learning algorithms to "Big Data" [29, 22, 6, 7, 20, 2, 23, 9].

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of the algorithm. The experimental results demonstrate the scalability of the algorithm on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm and a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its components. However, the paper assumes a significant amount of background knowledge in optimization and machine learning, which may make it difficult for non-experts to follow.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel application of ADMM to sparse precision matrix estimation and demonstrates the scalability of the algorithm on large-scale datasets. However, the idea of using ADMM for constrained optimization problems is not new, and the authors build upon previous work in this area.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for large-scale machine learning and data analysis. The ability to estimate sparse precision matrices on large-scale datasets has applications in many fields, including finance, biology, and social network analysis. The paper demonstrates the potential of the proposed algorithm to scale up to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
