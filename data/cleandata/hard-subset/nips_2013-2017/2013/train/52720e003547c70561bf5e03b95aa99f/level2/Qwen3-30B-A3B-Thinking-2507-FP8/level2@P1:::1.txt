

This paper presents a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, addressing the computational challenges of ultra-high dimensional problems (e.g., millions of dimensions with trillions of parameters). The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm that operates on column blocks rather than individual columns, along with a scalable distributed framework using block cyclic data distribution for load balancing. The paper establishes O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions, and demonstrates linear scalability with the number of cores on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, the column-block approach is a significant innovation that substantially improves scalability over previous column-by-column methods. The authors convincingly demonstrate that solving multiple columns together in blocks, combined with leveraging sparse and low-rank structures, leads to more efficient matrix multiplications and better cache utilization. Second, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, with proper convergence rate proofs that establish both objective convergence and constraint satisfaction rates. Third, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing impressive scalability on both synthetic and real datasets, including estimating a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides pseudocode for the proposed algorithm. The related work section effectively positions this contribution against existing distributed optimization frameworks, highlighting how the authors' approach differs from parameter servers and GPBS by better handling the global constraints in precision matrix estimation. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) on both synthetic and real datasets is thorough and demonstrates clear advantages in scalability for large problems.

Some minor weaknesses could be addressed. The paper mentions the statistical guarantees but doesn't explicitly connect how the distributed approach might affect statistical properties of the precision matrix estimate. A brief discussion of this would strengthen the paper. Additionally, while the convergence rate analysis is technically sound, a more intuitive explanation of the practical implications of the O(1/T) convergence rate compared to existing methods would help readers contextualize the results. The explanation of why column-block processing is beneficial beyond matrix multiplication efficiency could be slightly expanded.

The paper meets all four NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly validate the claims about scalability.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized with clear notation and pseudocode, making it accessible to readers familiar with ADMM and precision matrix estimation.
- Originality: The column-block approach for CLIME and the block cyclic distribution framework represent meaningful extensions to existing work, distinct from previous distributed methods for precision matrix estimation.
- Significance: The ability to handle millions of dimensions with linear scalability to hundreds of cores addresses a critical challenge in high-dimensional statistics, with potential applications across multiple domains.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of large-scale distributed optimization for precision matrix estimation. The proposed framework could be valuable for practitioners working with massive datasets, and the techniques may extend to other large-scale constrained optimization problems. The results on 400 cores estimating a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix are particularly impressive and demonstrate real-world applicability.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant advancement in scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-large dimensions, with a well-executed theoretical foundation and compelling empirical results. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics. The minor suggestions for improvement do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.
