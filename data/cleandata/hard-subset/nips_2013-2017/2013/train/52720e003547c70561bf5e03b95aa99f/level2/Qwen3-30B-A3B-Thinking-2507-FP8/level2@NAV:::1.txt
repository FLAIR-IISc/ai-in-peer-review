

This paper proposes a distributed column-block ADMM algorithm for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The main claims are: (1) an inexact ADMM variant for CLIME with O(1/T) convergence rates; (2) a distributed framework that scales to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters using hundreds of cores; and (3) column-block processing with block cyclic distribution that achieves near-linear scalability.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics by addressing a critical scalability bottleneck in precision matrix estimation. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-2) is sound, establishing convergence rates for both the objective and optimality conditions, which is well-supported by the algorithm design. The experimental evaluation demonstrates impressive scalability: the authors solve a 1 million-dimensional problem (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods like CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC. The comparison on both synthetic and real datasets (Leukemia, Climate) provides convincing evidence of the method's practical utility.

The paper effectively situates its work within the broader context of distributed optimization, correctly citing relevant literature on ADMM variants, distributed frameworks, and precision matrix estimation. The authors demonstrate good field knowledge by addressing key challenges like load balancing and cache efficiency through block cyclic distribution, which is a meaningful improvement over previous approaches.

The main strength is the practical scalability demonstration, particularly the successful handling of ultra-large-scale problems that were previously intractable. The paper acknowledges limitations regarding optimal block size selection and the need for careful resource management in shared-memory environments.

The only significant weakness is the lack of detailed analysis on how the column block size selection impacts convergence rates beyond the empirical observations. While the paper demonstrates scalability with different block sizes, a more thorough theoretical exploration of the optimal block size selection would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound, novel, and practically useful approach to a challenging problem. The theoretical foundations are solid, the implementation is well-executed, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's scalability. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale statistical learning and would be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
