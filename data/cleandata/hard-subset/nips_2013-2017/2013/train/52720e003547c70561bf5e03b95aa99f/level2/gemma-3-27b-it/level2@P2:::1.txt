This paper presents a scalable distributed algorithm for CLIME, a method for sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors address a critical challenge – applying CLIME to ultra-large-scale problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters – by proposing an inexact ADMM variant and a novel distributed framework. The core idea is to solve the CLIME problem in column blocks, leveraging matrix-matrix multiplications and block cyclic data distribution for improved efficiency and scalability.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods in handling massive datasets. The authors correctly identify the quadratic scaling of parameters in precision matrix estimation as a major bottleneck. The proposed approach, breaking down the problem into column blocks and employing matrix multiplications, is a sensible strategy for enhancing scalability. The theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates for the ADMM algorithm, adds rigor to the work.  The experimental evaluation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures demonstrates significant speedups and scalability, particularly in the distributed setting where they successfully estimate a one-million-dimension precision matrix.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** Scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to truly large dimensions is a crucial challenge with practical implications.
* **Novel approach:** Solving CLIME in column blocks and exploiting matrix multiplications is a clever optimization, potentially offering substantial performance gains.
* **Theoretical guarantees:** The established convergence rates for the ADMM algorithm provide confidence in the method's reliability.
* **Strong experimental results:** The demonstrated scalability, especially in the distributed environment, is compelling. Reaching a one-million dimension problem in 11 hours on 400 cores is impressive.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.  The notation is consistent and the algorithm is presented clearly.
* **Comprehensive related work:** The authors provide a good overview of related work in distributed optimization and sparse precision matrix estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Comparison depth:** While comparisons with existing methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) are presented, the analysis could be more in-depth.  For instance, the reasons behind DC-QUIC's performance degradation with increasing sparsity are mentioned but could be explored further. A more nuanced comparison of parameter tuning requirements across methods would also be valuable.
* **Column block size selection:** The paper hints at the importance of choosing an optimal column block size (k), but lacks a comprehensive discussion on how to determine this parameter in practice.  Guidance on selecting k based on problem characteristics (e.g., matrix sparsity, available memory) would be beneficial.  The brief mention of the relationship to cache size is intriguing but needs expansion.
* **Low-rank structure exploitation:**  The discussion on leveraging low-rank structure (Section 3.2) feels somewhat cursory. While the potential benefits are stated, the details of implementing this optimization aren't fully elaborated. Further investigation into how effectively this can be applied in practice would be useful.
* **Limited discussion of communication costs:** In the distributed-memory setting, communication costs are a major factor. While block cyclic distribution is mentioned to mitigate load imbalance, the paper doesn't extensively discuss how communication overhead is minimized. A more detailed analysis of communication patterns and their impact on performance would strengthen the work.
* **Potential for tighter theoretical bounds:** While convergence rates are established, exploring tighter bounds or conditions under which faster convergence can be guaranteed could further enhance the theoretical contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a practically important and challenging problem.
* The proposed algorithm is novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the method.
* The experimental results demonstrate significant scalability and performance gains.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison with existing methods could be more in-depth.
* The guidance on selecting the column block size is limited.
* The exploitation of low-rank structure isn't fully developed.
* Communication costs in the distributed setting aren't thoroughly analyzed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation. The proposed algorithm and distributed framework offer a significant advance in scalability, enabling the analysis of truly large-scale datasets. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly providing more guidance on column block size selection and expanding the discussion of communication costs in the distributed setting.
