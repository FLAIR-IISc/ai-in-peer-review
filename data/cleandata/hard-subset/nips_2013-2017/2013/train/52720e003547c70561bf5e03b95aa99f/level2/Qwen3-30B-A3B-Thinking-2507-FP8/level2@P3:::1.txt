

The paper presents a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using CLIME, targeting ultra-high-dimensional problems (e.g., 1 million dimensions). The core contribution is a column-block ADMM variant with theoretical convergence guarantees (O(1/T)) and a scalable distributed framework leveraging block cyclic data distribution. The method decomposes the problem into independent column-blocks, uses matrix-matrix operations to exploit low-rank structure (via C = AA^T), and achieves near-linear scalability on shared and distributed memory systems. Experimental results demonstrate significant speedups over state-of-the-art methods (e.g., 11 hours for 1M dimensions on 400 cores) and validate linear scaling with cores.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical analysis is rigorous, establishing convergence rates for both objective and feasibility residuals under inexact ADMM. The distributed framework addresses critical scalability bottlenecks in prior work (e.g., memory constraints for single-machine solvers) through block cyclic distribution, which minimizes cache misses and balances workloads. The empirical evaluation is thorough: synthetic data shows superior scalability for large graphs, and real-world datasets (Leukemia, Climate) confirm efficiency gains over CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC. The low-rank exploitation (Section 3.2) is a practical and well-motivated optimization, reducing matrix multiplication complexity from O(p²k) to O(npk). The paper situates its work clearly within NIPS literature (citing relevant ADMM, distributed optimization, and precision estimation papers) and avoids overclaiming novelty.

**Weaknesses:**  
The theoretical justification for covariance matrix perturbation (Theorem 3) requires strengthening. While the paper claims perturbations preserving statistical guarantees are possible via sub-exponential ∆_ij, the proof sketch lacks detail on how the perturbation maintains the required deviation bound ‖C + ∆ - Σ₀‖_∞ ≤ c√(log p/n) for all sample paths. This is critical for the "sparse covariance" claim, yet the argument relies on a high-level probabilistic bound without explicit construction or bounds on ∆_ij. Additionally, the real-data results (Table 1) lack comparison to more recent methods (e.g., [13, 14] post-2013), limiting the significance assessment. The column-block size k is empirically tuned but not theoretically justified—how should k be selected for a given hardware/memory profile? The discussion of cache effects (Section 5.2) is descriptive but does not provide a principled selection criterion.

**Revisions:**  
The paper would benefit from a concise clarification of Theorem 3’s proof, explicitly showing how ∆_ij preserves the deviation bound (e.g., by bounding the tail probability of ∆_ij). A brief analysis of k’s optimal choice (e.g., as a function of cache size, block dimensions, or problem sparsity) would strengthen the framework’s practical utility. Including a comparison to at least one post-2013 method (e.g., [14]’s QUIC variants) in real-data results would better contextualize the significance. These are reasonable additions (1–2 pages) that address the theoretical and practical gaps without requiring new experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The work makes a strong contribution to scalable high-dimensional statistics. It successfully bridges theoretical guarantees with practical distributed implementation, solving a problem previously intractable for millions of dimensions. The scalability claims are well-supported by experiments, and the framework’s generality (e.g., applicability to other constrained optimization problems) enhances its significance. The minor theoretical gap in Theorem 3 and limited real-data benchmarking do not undermine the core technical advances. The paper meets NIPS quality standards: technically sound, clearly written, and significantly advancing the state of the art in scalable precision estimation. It is appropriate for acceptance.
