## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper addresses the important problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in high-dimensional settings, a core task in various fields like genomics and finance. The authors present a distributed algorithm based on the CLIME estimator and an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), designed to scale to datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The key contributions lie in the column-block ADMM variant, leveraging matrix multiplication optimizations, and a scalable distributed framework utilizing block cyclic data distribution.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods for ultra-large-scale problems. The proposed approach is logically presented, with theoretical convergence guarantees provided for the ADMM algorithm. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of column-block processing and the use of block cyclic distribution for load balancing and memory efficiency. The experimental results demonstrate significant scalability, achieving near-linear speedup with increasing core count, and outperforming state-of-the-art methods on large datasets. The inclusion of both shared-memory and distributed-memory experiments strengthens the evaluation.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other distributed estimation techniques beyond those explicitly mentioned (e.g., those based on stochastic gradient descent). While the authors acknowledge the limitations of gradient-based methods for constrained optimization, a brief discussion of their applicability with appropriate modifications would be valuable. The discussion of low-rank structure feels somewhat underdeveloped; while mentioned, its practical impact isn’t fully quantified in the experiments.  Furthermore, the choice of block size *k* seems crucial for performance, but the paper lacks a detailed analysis of how to optimally select *k* based on problem characteristics and hardware configuration. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrates significant scalability and outperforms existing methods.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison with alternative distributed estimation approaches.
*   Underdeveloped discussion of low-rank structure exploitation.
*   Lack of guidance on optimal block size selection.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. While some areas could be strengthened, the demonstrated scalability and performance improvements are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. The authors should consider expanding the related work section and providing more practical guidance on parameter tuning, particularly the block size *k*.
