Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses sparse precision matrix estimation at ultra-high dimensions using CLIME (Cai, Liu, Luo 2011). It proposes a linearized (inexact) ADMM specialized to CLIME that operates on column blocks, enabling BLAS-3 style matrix-matrix multiplications and avoiding formation of the full sample covariance. Convergence rates O(1/T) are established for both objective suboptimality and feasibility residuals under η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2, building on linearized ADMM analyses (e.g., He & Yuan 2012; Wang & Banerjee 2013). The framework leverages the low-rank structure C = AA′ when n ≪ p to reduce multiplications from O(p2k) to O(npk), and exploits sparsity in iterates and in a truncated covariance. A distributed implementation with block-cyclic data/parameter distribution (as in ScaLAPACK) achieves near-linear scaling to hundreds of cores; experiments demonstrate solving p = 10^6 (p^2 = 10^12 parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores. Comparisons are provided to CLIME (flare), TIGER (Liu & Wang 2012), and DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al. 2013).

Relative to prior scalable optimization frameworks (e.g., GraphLab; parameter server; Boyd et al. 2011; Parikh & Boyd GPBS) and inverse covariance solvers (graphical lasso/QUIC; DC-QUIC; interior point for CLIME), the main novelty is a CLIME-specific linearized ADMM that (i) updates column blocks with closed forms (soft-thresholding and box projection), (ii) is amenable to high-performance matrix-matrix kernels and block-cyclic distribution, and (iii) comes with explicit O(1/T) rates for the chosen splitting. The discussion on safe covariance truncation connects to existing thresholding ideas (e.g., Mazumder & Hastie 2012 for glasso) but is framed for CLIME’s deviation-based guarantees.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important scaling challenge for sparse precision estimation; avoids O(p2) storage by never forming C explicitly and uses low-rank multiplications.
- Clear algorithmic decomposition: elementwise operations plus parallel matrix multiplications; column-blocking is well-motivated by cache and BLAS-3 considerations.
- Convergence analysis with explicit rate and parameter condition; residual bounds include a metric induced by η/ρ I − C^2, clarifying when the linearization is contractive.
- Thoughtful systems design: block-cyclic distribution for load balancing, shared vs distributed memory comparisons, and empirical guidance on block sizes and core placement.
- Demonstrates impressive scale (p = 10^6) with near-linear speedups, and compares to several representative baselines on synthetic and real data.

Weaknesses
- Statistical evaluation is limited. Beyond precision/recall on synthetics, there is little assessment of estimation error (e.g., norm error, support recovery stability) on real datasets; TIGER’s superior recall is acknowledged but the implications are not explored.
- The “safe” covariance perturbation/truncation result (Theorem 3) is stated at a high level without actionable guidance on constants, selection of ∆, or empirical validation of its impact on accuracy vs. speed.
- Some notational inconsistencies and minor errors hinder reproducibility: e.g., augmented Lagrangian scaling factors, the definition of box(·) (Algorithm 1 step 7 omits E), and the use of scaled vs. unscaled multipliers.
- Comparisons focus on single-node baselines for competing methods; there is no direct comparison to a strong column-wise ADMM baseline using the same distributed framework to isolate the benefit of column-blocking.
- Communication and memory-computation tradeoffs are not analyzed theoretically; per-iteration communication volume and synchronization costs would help interpret scaling.

Quality
The optimization formulation and linearized ADMM steps are technically sound and align with known analyses for linearized/approximate ADMM. The convergence conditions are reasonable and explicit. The systems implementation choices are standard and sensible. The experimental methodology is generally solid for scaling, but the statistical side could be strengthened, and some claims (safe truncation) would benefit from more rigorous development and experiments.

Clarity
Overall well-organized and readable, with a clear separation of algorithm, theory, structure exploitation, and systems details. A few notation/typo issues should be corrected to avoid confusion (augmented Lagrangian constants; consistent definition of box; indexing of variables in Algorithm 1). More guidance on choosing ρ, η, k, and block sizes would improve clarity.

Originality
The core estimator (CLIME) and ADMM machinery are known, as are linearized ADMM and block-cyclic distribution. The novelty lies in their combination tailored to CLIME with column-block updates, the specific convergence statement for this variant, and a carefully engineered distributed implementation that achieves extreme scale. The covariance perturbation perspective is interesting but underdeveloped.

Significance
High practical significance for large-scale graphical model estimation and distributed convex optimization. The framework is likely to be reused for other constraint-splitting problems with similar linear operators. The ability to solve trillion-parameter CLIME instances is notable.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential revisions:
- Fix notation and algorithmic inconsistencies (augmented Lagrangian factors; scaled dual variable; box projection arguments). Provide a precise, self-contained pseudocode with stopping criteria and parameter defaults.
- Add a brief communication/memory analysis per iteration (messages and words moved for A′X and AY; dependence on k, process grid, and block sizes), to contextualize scaling plots.
- Expand statistical evaluation: report estimation error (e.g., spectral/Frobenius/L1 error) and support recovery/stability on synthetics; on real data, include stability selection or subsampling-based agreement across methods.
- Provide guidance for choosing ρ and η (e.g., back-of-the-envelope scaling with λmax(C) and n) and for selecting k and block sizes given cache and network characteristics.

Optional but beneficial:
- Empirically evaluate the proposed covariance truncation: runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs as a function of the truncation threshold; relate to the deviation bound constants.
- Compare against a column-wise ADMM baseline implemented within the same distributed framework to isolate the gain from column-blocking.
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros: strong systems contribution with clear algorithmic rationale; solid convergence guarantees; impressive scalability; broadly useful framework.
Cons: limited statistical evaluation; under-specified truncation result; minor clarity issues; lack of communication analysis and direct baseline for column-blocking.

Overall: a valuable and timely contribution to scalable precision estimation. With the suggested revisions, the work would be a strong fit for the conference.
