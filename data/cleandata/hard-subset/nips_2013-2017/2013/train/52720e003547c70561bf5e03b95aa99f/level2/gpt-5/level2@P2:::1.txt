Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation at extreme scales by focusing on the CLIME estimator, which is formulated as an LP with separable columns. The authors propose an inexact/linearized ADMM tailored to CLIME that operates on column blocks (k columns at a time), yielding updates that are either elementwise or matrix-matrix multiplications, and a distributed, block-cyclic framework that exploits both sparse iterate structure and the low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = AA^T, with n ≪ p). They establish O(1/T) convergence rates for the objective and optimality residuals under η ≥ ρ‖C‖_2^2. They further argue that small-entries thresholding of C preserves deviation bounds needed for CLIME’s statistical guarantees. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and two real datasets, and demonstrate near-linear scaling in cores; in a distributed setting they report solving p = 10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) in ≈11 hours on 400 cores.

This work builds on and is positioned relative to: (i) CLIME and its theory (Cai et al. JASA, and follow-ups); (ii) scalable precision estimation (e.g., QUIC and DC-QUIC); (iii) distributed optimization and ADMM (Boyd et al.; He & Yuan for linearized/inexact ADMM rates); and (iv) systems/parallelization methods (block-cyclic distribution, ScaLAPACK, parameter server and GraphLab for broader context). The core novelty lies in a CLIME-specific linearized-ADMM with column-blocking and a distributed implementation that emphasizes cache/memory hierarchy and load balance.

Quality:
- Algorithmic soundness: The linearized ADMM update with a proximal parameter η and the box projection for the ∞-constraint is standard and appropriate. The convergence statements (O(1/T) objective and residuals) are reasonable under the stated condition η ≥ ρλ_max(C)^2, and consistent with existing analyses of linearized ADMM/Douglas–Rachford variants. However:
  - Theorem 1 bounds ‖X̄_T‖_1 − ‖X‖_1 for any pair (X,Z) with CX=Z, but does not explicitly require or exploit the box constraint ‖Z−E‖_∞ ≤ λ. As stated, it is not yet a suboptimality bound with respect to the original feasible set unless X is taken to be an optimal feasible solution and the feasibility of (X̄_T, Z̄_T) is discussed. Clarifying feasibility of averaged iterates and explicitly connecting to optimality gap would strengthen the claim.
  - Theorem 2’s residual bound is clear; it uses a KKT point and requires (η/ρ)I − C^2 ≽ 0. It would be helpful to discuss practical stopping criteria derived from these residuals.
- Statistical claims: The truncation/perturbation argument for making C sparse is valuable. The statement via random sub-exponential ∆ is fine but the practically relevant case is deterministic thresholding. The paper eventually notes that choosing |∆_ij| ≤ c√(log p/n) suffices; this follows by triangle inequality without requiring independence of ∆, and should be made the primary justification. An experiment demonstrating negligible statistical impact when using thresholded C would be reassuring.
- Empirical evaluation: The system-level scalability experiments are compelling and detailed (shared vs distributed, block sizes, process grids). For comparisons with TIGER/QUIC/CLIME:
  - Parameters are tuned to match sparsity, which is fair for runtime comparison but does not fully assess statistical accuracy; the precision-recall plots on synthetic help, though TIGER’s superior recall is noted. It would be useful to report edge F1/AUC or error in ‖Ω̂−Ω_0‖ norms across methods.
  - The real-data comparisons report runtime to achieve target sparsity, but without accuracy proxies (e.g., held-out likelihood).
  - Reporting communication volume per iteration and per column-block would clarify scaling limits on clusters.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized, with a concrete algorithm box and implementation details. Some notation and statements could be tightened:
- Explicitly state the stopping conditions used in experiments.
- Clarify how ρ and η are selected in practice (e.g., set η = ρ‖C‖_2^2 or a multiple thereof; how is ‖C‖_2 estimated?).
- Make precise the baseline “column-by-column” implementation used for speedup S_col^k, and whether warm starts across blocks were used.

Originality:
The convergence rate for linearized ADMM is not novel per se, but its specialization to CLIME with a column-block strategy, a clean set of elementwise and matmul primitives, and a distributed block-cyclic implementation for ultra-high dimensions are a meaningful engineering and algorithmic contribution. The explicit discussion of leveraging low rank (n ≪ p) to reduce cost is important in this setting. The truncation argument is a useful practical add-on, though not deeply novel.

Significance:
The ability to estimate sparse precisions at p ≈ 10^6 with near-linear scaling is impactful for large-scale graphical modeling. The proposed framework should be of interest to both ML practitioners and systems/optimization researchers, and the primitives could transfer to other constrained problems.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a principled and practical method.
- Clear algorithm with O(1/T) guarantees tailored to CLIME.
- Thorough systems considerations (block-cyclic, cache, shared vs distributed) and strong scaling results, including trillion-parameter case.
- Demonstrates superior scalability compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

Cons and suggestions:
- Theorem 1 should be clarified to connect to the original constrained problem’s optimality gap; discuss feasibility of averaged iterates and/or provide a bound that incorporates the box constraint.
- Limited statistical evaluation on real data; consider reporting held-out likelihood or stability selection to complement sparsity-matched runtimes.
- Parameter selection (ρ, η, λ) and stopping criteria are under-specified; provide practical guidelines and sensitivity analysis.
- Communication cost analysis per iteration and per block is missing; a brief complexity model would improve reproducibility and help practitioners size clusters.
- Discuss memory footprint for p = 10^6 (A storage, intermediates) and I/O time; indicate whether fault tolerance/checkpointing is supported.
- Compare against parallel column-wise CLIME (p independent LPs solved concurrently) to isolate the benefit of column-blocking versus simple parallelization.

Recommendation:
Overall, this is a strong systems-oriented paper with solid algorithmic grounding and impressive scalability. Addressing the theoretical clarity around Theorem 1 and enriching the empirical evaluation with accuracy metrics and parameter/stopping details would further strengthen it. I lean toward acceptance.
