This paper proposes an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm to solve the linear programming problem inherent in the Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) framework. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) for their proposed method. A key focus of the paper is the algorithm's implementation and evaluation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory parallel architectures. The empirical evaluation includes numerical comparisons against several existing methods, namely DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare, on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper is technically sound and addresses the important and challenging problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The development of a simple, robust, and distributable algorithm for this task is a valuable contribution to the field. The empirical evaluation of the algorithm's performance and scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems is a notable strength of the paper, providing valuable insights into the practical application of ADMM for this class of problems.

However, the significance of the theoretical contribution and the scalability results could be contextualized more clearly. While the proposed method is effective, its novelty appears to be more in the implementation and large-scale evaluation rather than in the core algorithmic development. Specifically:

1.  The application of an inexact ADMM to a problem of this form, while effective, is a relatively standard technique in the optimization literature. The core derivation follows established patterns. The paper would be strengthened by more clearly positioning its contribution in light of this existing work.

2.  The paper highlights the excellent scalability of the proposed method. This impressive performance appears to be a direct consequence of the problem's structure. The CLIME formulation decomposes into component-wise subproblems, with the most computationally intensive step being a matrix-matrix multiplication. As matrix-matrix multiplication is known to be highly amenable to parallelization, the observed scalability is expected. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss this and clarify whether there were any non-obvious challenges or algorithmic insights that were necessary to achieve this performance.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' methodology and results.

**Originality**

The primary originality of this work lies in the thorough implementation and evaluation of the ADMM-based solver for CLIME on modern parallel computing architectures. While the algorithmic components themselves are not entirely new, their application and rigorous empirical analysis in this specific context are interesting and useful for practitioners.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the following minor revision:

*   The description of the numerical experiments could be improved by providing more specific details about the convergence criteria. For instance, the paper compares the runtime of different solvers, but it is not specified to what level of accuracy or tolerance each method was run. Including this information is crucial for a fair and reproducible comparison of the solvers' efficiency.

In summary, this is a solid paper presenting a practical and scalable solver for a significant problem. While the core algorithmic novelty is limited, the careful implementation and extensive evaluation on parallel systems represent a valuable contribution.
