### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of covariance selection at a very large scale, handling up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME (Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation) estimator for determining the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector. CLIME is particularly amenable to distributed computation because it can be solved column-by-column, and its theoretical analysis is noted to be simpler than that of the Graphical Lasso.

The main contribution is a new algorithm for fitting the CLIME estimator. This algorithm departs from the standard column-wise approach by solving the problem block-wise, coupled with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the convergence rate of this algorithm, both in terms of the objective function value and the distance to the optimal solution. The algorithm is designed to exploit special matrix structures, such as sparsity and low-rank properties, to reduce the computational cost.

This method is implemented within a scalable parallel framework designed for both shared and distributed memory architectures. The numerical experiments compare the runtime of the proposed method against its direct competitors on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Furthermore, a detailed study evaluates the algorithm's speedup with respect to varying block sizes and the number of processor cores in both shared and distributed memory settings.

### Comments

This is a well-written and clear paper that makes a significant algorithmic contribution.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Positioning:** The introduction clearly articulates the novelty and contributions of the work. The authors effectively position their method within the existing literature, providing sound and relevant bibliographical references.

2.  **Algorithm Presentation:** The algorithm is presented effectively, striking an excellent balance between the technical details of numerical analysis and linear algebra, and the theoretical guarantees for its convergence rate.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical analysis is thorough and appropriate. The implementation of the algorithm demonstrates impressive performance and scalability, tackling problems of a size that are often out of reach for existing methods. The experiments convincingly show the benefits of the block-wise approach and the parallel implementation.

**Concerns:**

My primary concern revolves around the availability of the implementation. The paper presents a powerful new tool for the community, but its impact and reproducibility will be significantly diminished if the source code is not made publicly available. Given that the authors state the implementation is built upon open-source libraries, releasing the code should be feasible and would be a major service to the field. Without access to the code, it is difficult for other researchers to build upon this work or even verify its impressive results.

### Recommendation

This is a strong algorithmic paper that addresses the critical and practical problem of large-scale covariance selection. The proposed method and its high-performance implementation represent a powerful tool for high-dimensional data analysis.

I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors make their code publicly available. The value of this contribution lies not just in the algorithm itself, but in the potential for its widespread use. Publication should be contingent on a commitment to release the source code to ensure the work has the broad impact it deserves.
