Review- Summary of the paper 
This paper addresses covariance selection at extreme scale (up to millions of features and trillions of parameters). The estimator considered is CLIME, which provides a precision-matrix estimator for Gaussian models and decomposes into column-wise subproblems, making it well suited to distributed computation. Its theoretical analysis is also claimed to be more straightforward than that of the Graphical Lasso. The authors introduce a new algorithm that fits CLIME by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers. They state convergence-rate guarantees for both objective suboptimality and distance to optimality. The method exploits structural properties (sparsity and low rank) during computation to reduce cost. The algorithm is implemented in a scalable parallel framework for both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems. The numerical experiments compare runtime with direct competitors on synthetic and real datasets. A detailed analysis is also provided to assess speedup as a function of block size and number of cores on both shared and distributed memory systems. 
Comments 
The paper is well written and very clear. The novelty and contributions are clearly presented in the introduction, and connections to prior work are well established with sound bibliographic references. The algorithm is introduced effectively, with a good balance between numerical analysis/algebraic details and theoretical convergence guarantees. The empirical evaluation is appropriate, and the implementation shows very impressive performance. 
My only concern is the availability of the method: if the source code (largely built on open-source libraries) is not released to the community, the impact of the work will be limited. 
A solid algorithmic paper addressing the important practical problem of covariance selection at very large scale (millions of variables). Such a powerful tool should be published if it is made available to the community.
