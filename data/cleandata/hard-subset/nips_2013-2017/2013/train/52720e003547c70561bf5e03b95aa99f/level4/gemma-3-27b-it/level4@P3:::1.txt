This paper addresses the important problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation, focusing on scaling the CLIME estimator to datasets with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The authors present a novel inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) and a distributed framework to enable this scalability. The core idea is to solve the CLIME problem in column blocks, leveraging matrix multiplication and a block cyclic data distribution scheme for improved performance. The paper includes theoretical convergence analysis and empirical evaluation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, demonstrating significant speedups compared to existing methods.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in sparse precision matrix estimation, referencing key papers such as [3, 19, 25, 28] that establish theoretical foundations. It also acknowledges recent advances in scaling machine learning algorithms [2, 6, 7, 9, 20, 22, 23, 29] and positions the work within this broader context. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods in handling ultra-high-dimensional problems and motivate the need for a scalable distributed solution. The choice of CLIME as the underlying estimator is well-justified, given its desirable theoretical properties and computational advantages.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Scaling precision matrix estimation to extremely large datasets is a critical challenge with practical applications.
*   **Novel approach:** Solving the CLIME problem in column blocks and utilizing block cyclic data distribution are innovative ideas that contribute to improved scalability.
*   **Theoretical guarantees:** The paper provides convergence rate analysis for the CLIME-ADMM algorithm, demonstrating its theoretical soundness.
*   **Comprehensive evaluation:** The empirical evaluation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures provides strong evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness and scalability. The comparison with existing methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, Flare) is valuable.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation:** The initial sections suffer from scattered notation. A consolidated notation table would significantly enhance readability.  Key parameters like ρ and η are introduced without initial definition.
*   **Justification of Block Cyclic Distribution:** While the authors claim the block cyclic distribution achieves load balance and scalability, the explanation lacks depth. A more detailed justification of *why* this scheme is effective, and potentially a comparison with alternative distribution strategies, would be beneficial.
*   **Column Block Size Selection:** The paper doesn't provide a clear methodology or theoretical guidance for choosing the optimal column block size *k*. The current approach appears ad-hoc. A discussion of the trade-offs involved in selecting *k* (e.g., cache size, communication overhead) is needed.
*   **"Distributed" vs. "Parallel":** The paper describes a parallel implementation, but characterizing it as fully "distributed" is debatable, as the description of inter-process communication is limited. Clarifying this distinction would be helpful. The paper lacks details regarding the communication cost and how it scales with problem size and the number of cores.
*   **Limited discussion of sparsity assumptions:** While the paper touches upon exploiting sparsity, the impact of different sparsity patterns on performance isn't thoroughly investigated. Further exploration of this would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
*   The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm and distributed framework offer a novel and effective solution with strong empirical results.
*   The theoretical convergence analysis provides a solid foundation for the method.
*   The extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates clear scalability advantages over existing approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The justification for the block cyclic data distribution is weak.
*   The lack of guidance for selecting the column block size *k* is a limitation.
*   The characterization of the algorithm as "distributed" may be misleading.
*   Some minor presentation issues (notation, clarity in certain explanations) detract from the overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm and distributed framework are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some aspects (justification of block cyclic distribution, column block size selection) could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses regarding notation, justification of block cyclic distribution, and the discussion of *k* selection in a revised version. Addressing the "distributed" vs. "parallel" terminology would also be beneficial.
