Summary
This paper addresses large-scale sparse precision (inverse covariance) matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact, linearized ADMM algorithm that updates column blocks rather than single columns, enabling heavy use of BLAS-3 matrix–matrix multiplications and simple elementwise operations. They prove O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective and optimality residuals under a stepsize condition η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2. The paper further presents scalable implementations for shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures using block cyclic data distribution (via OpenMP/OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK). The framework exploits either sparsity in iterates and/or sample covariance, or low-rank structure (C = AA′ with n << p) to reduce complexity. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and real datasets, and demonstrate near-linear scaling with cores and the ability to handle p up to one million (≈1e12 parameters) in ≈11 hours on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on CLIME [Cai et al.], which decomposes across columns and has strong statistical guarantees, but avoids explicit positive-definiteness constraints. Prior solvers include interior-point and ADMM variants; the key computational contribution here is solving in column blocks to leverage matrix–matrix multiplications and block-cyclic distribution for load balance. The approach is related to recent distributed optimization frameworks (e.g., GPBS, parameter servers) but tailored to global constraints in CLIME and to BLAS-3 friendly operations. Comparisons include QUIC/DC-QUIC for Graphical Lasso, which rely on different objectives and structures; the authors explain why DC-QUIC benefits from block-diagonal thresholding. The paper also discusses covariance perturbations to induce sparsity while maintaining deviation bounds, and the use of low-rank multiplication AA′X to cut cost from O(p^2 k) to O(npk).

Quality
- Algorithmic soundness: The linearized (inexact) ADMM updates for X and the closed-form Z and Y updates are standard and appropriate. The stepsize condition η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2 is reasonable; it would be helpful to state how η and ρ are chosen in practice and whether backtracking or tuning was required.
- Theory: The O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality and KKT residuals align with known results for linearized ADMM; proofs are deferred to the supplement and appear to follow established techniques. A minor clarity point: ensure consistent use of scaled/unscaled dual variables and constants in the augmented Lagrangian (e.g., ρ/2 vs ρ factors).
- Statistical aspects: The discussion on covariance truncation/perturbation to induce sparsity is interesting. However, the theorem assumes a perturbation ∆ with independent sub-exponential entries; the practical truncation rule depends on C and thus violates independence. Please clarify the conditions under which data-dependent truncation still preserves the required deviation bounds, or provide a direct deterministic bound for entrywise thresholding by c√(log p/n).
- Implementation: The engineering is thoughtful, with clear consideration for cache effects, load balancing via block cyclic distribution, and communication patterns. Reporting the communication volume per iteration and memory usage per node would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a good balance between algorithmic detail and system design. A few suggestions:
- Explicitly state stopping criteria, primal/dual residual tolerances, and typical iteration counts; plot residuals vs time.
- Summarize symmetry/positive-definiteness handling for CLIME (e.g., symmetrization via min(|Ω̂ij|,|Ω̂ji|)); clarify whether the final estimator is symmetrized and PD-projected if needed.
- Standardize notation and scaling constants in the augmented Lagrangian and ADMM updates; define all variables at first use (e.g., ρ, η, V̂).
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “architecture,” “comparison,” “ground truth,” “sparse,” “achieved,” etc.).

Originality
While ADMM for CLIME is not new, the column-blocked, linearized ADMM tailored to BLAS-3 operations, coupled with a practical distributed implementation using block-cyclic distribution, is a meaningful systems/algorithms contribution. The explicit convergence rates for this variant and the detailed analysis of sparse/low-rank acceleration are also valuable. To the best of my knowledge, scaling CLIME to p ≈ 10^6 with demonstrated near-linear parallel efficiency is novel for NIPS 2013.

Significance
The work addresses an important practical problem—estimating massive precision matrices—which arises in many scientific and industrial applications. The demonstrated scalability and availability of a general distributed template for constrained optimization via ADMM could benefit a broad audience. The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on runtime and scaling; adding more accuracy metrics (e.g., held-out likelihood, estimation errors in ℓ1/Frobenius norms) would better quantify the trade-offs versus alternatives on both synthetic and real data.

Empirical evaluation
- Strengths: Clear runtime and scaling comparisons; sensible discussion of why DC-QUIC excels on block-diagonal structures; thorough stress tests across cores, block sizes, and architectures.
- Suggestions: Report λ selection strategy, η/ρ choices, and sensitivity; include accuracy metrics on synthetic data (beyond precision/recall) and some proxy accuracy on real data (stability selection, cross-validated likelihood). Ensure fair multi-threading for baselines where possible. Provide ablation results showing benefits of column blocking vs single-column ADMM on identical hardware.

Reproducibility
The paper relies on standard open-source libraries; to maximize impact, please release code and scripts (or provide an artifact) to reproduce major figures and the million-dimensional experiment, along with configuration details (process grid, block sizes, I/O setup).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem at unprecedented scale.
- Simple, well-motivated algorithmic design with provable convergence.
- High-quality distributed systems engineering (block cyclic distribution, BLAS-3 focus) with strong empirical scaling.
- Clear exposition and useful discussion of exploiting sparsity and low rank.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited reporting on statistical accuracy versus baselines on real data; primarily runtime-centric.
- Some theoretical aspects of covariance perturbation need clarification for data-dependent truncation.
- Missing details on stopping criteria, parameter selection, and symmetrization/PD handling.
- Reproducibility hinges on code availability.

Overall evaluation
A strong algorithmic/systems paper that tackles large-scale sparse precision estimation with a principled and effective approach. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the noted theoretical and experimental details and, ideally, releasing code to the community.
