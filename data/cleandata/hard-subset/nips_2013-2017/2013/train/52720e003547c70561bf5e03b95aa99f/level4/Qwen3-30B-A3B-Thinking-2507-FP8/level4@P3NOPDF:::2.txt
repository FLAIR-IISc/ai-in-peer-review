

This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear programming subproblem in the CLIME estimator, establishing an O(1/T) convergence rate. The authors implement the algorithm across both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures and provide numerical comparisons against DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare. The work addresses a relevant challenge in large-scale sparse precision estimation, where distributed computation is essential.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound, with a clear presentation of the inexact ADMM framework for CLIME. The convergence analysis is rigorous and well-motivated. The experimental design is generally appropriate, though some details require clarification (see Minor Comments).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and readable, with logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm derivation, analysis, and evaluation. Key contributions are clearly articulated.

**Originality:** The primary novelty lies in the empirical evaluation of ADMM’s performance across shared-memory and distributed-memory systems for the CLIME problem. While the inexact ADMM derivation itself follows standard techniques (as noted below), the architecture-specific implementation and scalability assessment provide fresh insights for practitioners.

**Significance:** Solving large-scale sparse precision estimation problems efficiently remains challenging. The proposed distributed ADMM offers a simple, robust, and scalable solution, particularly valuable given the increasing scale of modern datasets. The demonstration of strong parallel performance across architectures is a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Minor Comments:**
1.  The derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard and follows the established ADMM framework for linear programs (e.g., as presented in He et al., "Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers for Linear Programming," 2013). To strengthen the contribution narrative, the authors should briefly contrast their specific adaptation to CLIME's structure with the general case, highlighting any problem-specific optimizations.
2.  The paper correctly notes that CLIME's structure allows component-wise solution of subproblems, making the algorithm inherently parallelizable. However, the claim that "matrix-matrix multiplications can be parallelized well" is slightly misleading. The dominant operation *is* matrix-matrix multiplication (e.g., in the dual step), but the *parallelization potential* stems directly from the component-wise separability of the primal update, not solely from the parallelizability of matrix multiplication. The authors should clarify that the algorithm achieves *ideal* parallelism due to the problem's structure, not because of the properties of matrix multiplication itself.
3.  The experimental setup lacks critical details. For a fair comparison, the authors should specify the termination criteria and achieved accuracy (e.g., relative duality gap, residual norms) for each solver (DC-QUIC, Tiger, CLIME-Flare, and their ADMM) at the point of comparison. Without this, it is difficult to assess the trade-offs between computational efficiency and solution quality.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a solid contribution addressing a practical problem in large-scale statistical learning. The technical execution is strong, and the distributed implementation evaluation is valuable. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract significantly from the paper's core merits. I recommend acceptance. The work provides a useful, scalable tool for precision matrix estimation and offers clear guidance on implementing ADMM effectively in parallel environments. The clarity of the presentation and the relevance of the problem ensure the paper will be valuable to the NIPS community.
