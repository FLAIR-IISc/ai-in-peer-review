

Summary of the paper
This paper addresses the critical challenge of sparse precision matrix estimation in ultra-high-dimensional settings (up to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters), focusing on the CLIME estimator which offers favorable theoretical properties and a column-wise decomposable structure. The authors propose an innovative inexact ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column-blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging both sparse and low-rank structures inherent in precision matrix estimation problems. The theoretical contributions include convergence rate analysis for both the objective function and optimality conditions (O(1/T) rates), while the computational framework employs block cyclic distribution for efficient parallelization across shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The paper demonstrates significant scalability improvements over state-of-the-art methods like CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC, with nearly linear speedup across hundreds of cores. The authors position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning to "Big Data," contrasting their approach with previous distributed optimization frameworks that are ill-suited for constrained problems like precision matrix estimation.

Strengths
The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written, with a strong connection to prior NIPS work on distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 6, 9, 15, 23]) and precision matrix estimation (e.g., [3, 4, 19, 21, 25, 28]). The theoretical analysis is rigorous, establishing meaningful convergence rates while maintaining practical relevance. The implementation details are thorough, with careful consideration of memory hierarchy optimization through block cyclic distribution and the exploitation of sparse/low-rank structures. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating substantial scalability improvements on both synthetic and real datasets (Leukemia, Climate) with clear quantitative comparisons. The paper effectively addresses all four evaluation criteria: the method is technically sound (Quality), clearly presented (Clarity), builds upon but significantly extends prior work (Originality), and solves an important practical problem with demonstrable impact (Significance).

Weaknesses
The only significant concern is the absence of source code availability, which undermines the reproducibility and potential impact of this otherwise excellent work. While the authors reference open-source libraries, they do not provide a link to or description of the actual implementation, making it difficult for other researchers to verify results or build upon this work.

Pro-acceptance arguments
1. Solves a critical problem in high-dimensional statistics with practical significance for large-scale applications
2. Contains strong theoretical guarantees with convergence rates established for both objective and optimality conditions
3. Demonstrates impressive scalability (1 million dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores)
4. Provides thoughtful analysis of both sparse and low-rank structures to optimize computation
5. Clear connection to previous NIPS work and appropriate citations

Con-acceptance arguments
1. Source code not made available to the community, limiting reproducibility and practical utility

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization for statistical estimation. The method represents a significant step forward for precision matrix estimation in ultra-high-dimensional settings, and the implementation framework is well-suited for modern computational architectures. The only barrier to acceptance is the lack of code availability, which should be remedied before publication. If the code is made available, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
