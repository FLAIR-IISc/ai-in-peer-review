The authors present a scalable variant of the Constrained L1-minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) algorithm, referred to as CLIME-ADMM, along with a distributed framework for computations. They also provide empirical results comparing the performance of CLIME-ADMM to state-of-the-art techniques, including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare.

To improve the readability of the paper, it would be beneficial to consolidate the notation into a single table, rather than scattering it throughout Sections 1, 2, and 3. For instance, the definitions of \rho and \eta are not provided in the text, which may cause confusion for readers.

The proposed block cyclic data distribution scheme is an interesting approach, but its ability to achieve load balance and scalability is not immediately clear. Furthermore, there is a lack of empirical results to support this claim. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind this scheme and to include experimental results to demonstrate its effectiveness.

Additionally, the algorithm currently appears to set the column block size in an ad-hoc manner. It would be beneficial to provide some intuition or theoretical guidance on the choice of k, as this could significantly impact the performance of the algorithm.

A more general concern is that the algorithm may not be truly "distributed" in the classical sense, as it does not involve explicit mechanisms for message passing and communication. It may be more accurate to describe this as a parallel implementation.

Minor comments include:

* Section 1: Replace "tunning" with "tuning" in the sentence "where \lambda is a tunning parameter."
* Section 5.1: Replace "tunning" with "tuning" in the sentence "As Tiger is parameter tunning free."
* Section 2: Replace "summerized" with "summarized" in the sentence "CLIME is summerized in."

The authors present a large-scale parallel algorithm for estimating sparse precision matrices using CLIME. The novelty of this approach lies in the estimation of the precision matrix by column blocks, rather than column-by-column. The empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapak is a valuable contribution to the field.
