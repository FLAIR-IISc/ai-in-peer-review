This paper addresses the challenging problem of sparse precision matrix estimation in high-dimensional settings, scaling to millions of variables and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, leveraging its desirable theoretical properties and suitability for parallel computation. They introduce an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm, coupled with a novel block-wise solution approach, and provide convergence rate analysis for both the objective function and optimality conditions. The paper also explores utilizing sparse and low-rank structures to reduce computational cost, and presents a scalable parallel computation framework for shared and distributed memory systems. Comprehensive experimental results demonstrate the algorithm’s efficiency and scalability, comparing its performance to state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets, with detailed analyses of speedup based on block size and core count.

This is a well-written and clear paper. The novelty and contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction, and the work is appropriately situated within the existing literature with comprehensive and relevant citations. The algorithm is presented with a good balance of technical detail concerning numerical analysis and theoretical convergence guarantees. The numerical evaluation is appropriate and demonstrates impressive performance gains.

My primary concern revolves around the accessibility of this method. The significant effort invested in developing and analyzing this algorithm would be greatly amplified by making the source code – which appears to be largely built upon open-source libraries – publicly available to the research community. Without code release, the practical impact of this work is limited.

In summary, this is a strong algorithmic paper that tackles an important and practical problem in covariance selection for very high-dimensional data. If the authors commit to releasing the code, publication is strongly recommended.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Scalability:** The algorithm demonstrably scales to problem sizes previously intractable, opening new avenues for research and application.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The established convergence rates provide confidence in the algorithm’s reliability.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the method and its advantages.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental results are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the algorithm’s performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Code Release:** Without code availability, reproducibility and practical impact are severely limited.
