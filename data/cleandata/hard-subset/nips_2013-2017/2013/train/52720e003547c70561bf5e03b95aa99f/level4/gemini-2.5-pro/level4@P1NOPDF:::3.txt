### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the problem of covariance selection in a very large-scale setting, with up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The paper focuses on the Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) estimator. This estimator, which provides an estimate of the precision matrix for a Gaussian vector, has the advantage of being solvable column by column, making it highly amenable to distributed computation. The authors also note that CLIME's theoretical analysis is simpler than that of the Graphical Lasso.

The authors develop a new algorithm to fit the CLIME estimator by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-by-column, coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers. The paper provides theoretical results on the algorithm's convergence rate, both in terms of the objective function value and the distance to the optimal solution. Special attention is paid to exploiting matrix structures, such as sparsity and low-rank properties, during computation to reduce the overall burden. The proposed algorithm is implemented in a scalable parallel framework designed for both shared and distributed memory systems.

The numerical studies compare the runtime of the proposed method against its direct competitors on both synthetic and real datasets. The paper also includes a detailed analysis of the new algorithm, evaluating its speedup with respect to varying block sizes and number of cores on both shared and distributed memory architectures.

### Comments

This paper is well-written and very clear. The introduction clearly states the novelty and contributions of the method, establishing its connection to existing work with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is presented effectively, striking a good balance between the technical details of numerical analysis and linear algebra and the theoretical guarantees of its convergence rate. The numerical analysis is thorough, and the implementation of the algorithm demonstrates very impressive performance.

My only concern is the availability of the proposed method. The value of this work to the community would be immensely amplified if the source code, which appears to be based on open-source libraries, were made publicly available. Without access to the implementation, the practical impact of this research will be limited.

This is a sound algorithmic paper that addresses the important practical problem of very large-scale covariance selection (with millions of variables). This powerful tool merits publication, and I would strongly recommend acceptance, on the condition that the authors make their implementation available to the research community.
