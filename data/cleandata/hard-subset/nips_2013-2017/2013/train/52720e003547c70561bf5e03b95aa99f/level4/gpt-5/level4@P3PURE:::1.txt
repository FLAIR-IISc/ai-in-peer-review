This paper addresses sparse precision matrix estimation via CLIME, proposes a scalable variant (CLIME-ADMM), and describes a distributed/parallel framework for computation. The method estimates the precision matrix by column blocks rather than column-by-column, and empirical comparisons are provided against state-of-the-art methods including DC-QUIC, TIGER, and Flare. An empirical analysis using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK is presented.

Major comments:
- Notation: The notation is scattered across Sections 1–3. A consolidated notation table would greatly improve readability. In particular, ρ and η are used but do not appear to be defined.
- Block-cyclic data distribution: While the proposed block-cyclic distribution is interesting, it is not clear why this scheme ensures load balance and scalability. The paper does not provide empirical evidence to substantiate these claims. Please include justification (theoretical or empirical) for the load-balancing and scalability benefits of this choice.
- Column block size: The choice of the column block size k appears ad hoc. Some intuition, heuristic guidance, or theoretical rationale for selecting k would be helpful.
- Distributed vs. parallel: The work is presented as distributed, but there is no explicit discussion of message passing or communication mechanisms. As written, it seems more accurate to view this as a parallel implementation. Please clarify the communication model and patterns, or adjust the positioning accordingly.

Minor comments:
- Section 1: “where λ is a tunning parameter” → “where λ is a tuning parameter”
- Section 5.1: “As Tiger is parameter tunning free” → “As TIGER is parameter tuning free”
- Section 2: “CLIME is summerized in” → “CLIME is summarized in”

Overall, the paper presents a large-scale parallel approach to CLIME, with the novelty of estimating by column blocks. The empirical comparisons are useful, but the paper would benefit from clearer, centralized notation; defined symbols (e.g., ρ, η); justification for the block-cyclic distribution and claims about scalability; guidance on choosing k; and a precise description of the communication model if the method is to be considered distributed.
