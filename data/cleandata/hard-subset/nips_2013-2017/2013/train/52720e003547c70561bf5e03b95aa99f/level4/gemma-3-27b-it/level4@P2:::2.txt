This paper introduces an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for solving the linear program associated with the CLIME estimator for sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and demonstrate the algorithm’s implementation across both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, providing numerical comparisons with existing methods like DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a well-defined algorithm for a challenging problem. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck as matrix-matrix multiplication and leverage this for parallelization. The convergence analysis appears reasonable. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM is a standard application of the technique (see, for example, He & Yuan, 2013 – http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm), and the paper doesn’t present significant novelty in the ADMM formulation itself. The strength of the work lies in its scalable implementation and evaluation. A minor comment is that the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the theoretical properties of CLIME itself, perhaps briefly outlining the statistical guarantees that motivate its use.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written. The authors effectively explain the key ideas and algorithmic steps. The notation is consistently used, and the overall presentation is logical. However, greater detail regarding the experimental setup (e.g., specific accuracy metrics, parameter tuning details for competing methods) would improve reproducibility and understanding.

**Originality:** While the ADMM formulation isn’t novel, the application to CLIME and, more importantly, the thorough evaluation on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures are valuable contributions. The authors effectively exploit the inherent structure of the CLIME problem (component-wise updates, dominance of matrix-matrix multiplication) to achieve scalability. The block-cyclic data distribution strategy in the distributed-memory setting seems particularly promising. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison to existing *distributed* sparse precision estimation methods, if any exist.

**Significance:** Estimating sparse precision matrices at large scales is a critical problem with applications in diverse fields. The authors address this challenge with a practical and scalable algorithm. The demonstration of near-linear scalability with the number of cores is a significant result. Although the authors correctly point out that the good scalability largely stems from the underlying structure of CLIME, providing a readily available and efficient implementation is a valuable contribution to the field. It is likely that this algorithm will be useful to researchers and practitioners working with high-dimensional data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem (large-scale sparse precision estimation).
*   Presents a practical and scalable algorithm (CLIME-ADMM).
*   Demonstrates excellent scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The ADMM formulation itself is not novel.
*   The experimental setup could be more detailed to facilitate reproducibility.
*   A more thorough comparison to existing distributed methods (if they exist) would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of sparse precision estimation. While the underlying ADMM technique isn’t novel, the implementation and evaluation are well-executed, and the demonstrated scalability is impressive. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding experimental detail and comparison to related work.
