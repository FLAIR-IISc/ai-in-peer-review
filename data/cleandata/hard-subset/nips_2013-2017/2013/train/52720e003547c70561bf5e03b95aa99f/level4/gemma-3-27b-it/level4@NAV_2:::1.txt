The authors address the problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, presenting a novel ADMM-based algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) and a distributed framework for computation. They demonstrate the algorithm's scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, comparing its performance to state-of-the-art methods like DC-QUIC, Tiger, and implementations within the flare package.

The paper begins by contextualizing the problem within the broader field of sparse precision matrix estimation, referencing relevant work on statistical convergence rates [28, 25, 3, 19, 4] and computational methods [1, 8, 14, 21, 13]. The motivation for tackling ultra-large-scale problems – where the number of parameters scales quadratically with the number of variables – is clearly articulated. The authors correctly identify the strengths of the CLIME estimator, including its statistical guarantees and inherent computational advantages. The proposed CLIME-ADMM builds upon existing ADMM approaches [18] but addresses their limitations in scaling to high dimensions by introducing an inexact ADMM variant and a column-block decomposition strategy. The connection to recent advances in scaling machine learning algorithms [29, 22, 6, 7, 20, 2, 23, 9] is also appropriately drawn.

The strengths of this work lie in its practical focus on scalability and its thorough experimental evaluation. The convergence rate analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) provides a theoretical foundation for the algorithm’s performance. The use of block cyclic data distribution in the distributed-memory framework is a potentially valuable innovation for load balancing, and the exploration of sparse and low-rank structures to further optimize performance is well-justified. The empirical results clearly demonstrate that CLIME-ADMM outperforms existing methods in terms of scalability, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The experimental setup appears comprehensive, utilizing both synthetic and real datasets, and the comparisons to relevant baselines are informative.

However, several areas could benefit from further clarification. The notation is initially scattered and could be consolidated into a dedicated notation table, as suggested. Specifically, the meaning of parameters like ρ and η are not immediately clear upon first encountering them in Section 1. The justification for the block cyclic data distribution needs more elaboration. While the authors claim it achieves load balance and scalability, a more detailed explanation of *why* this is the case, along with empirical evidence specifically demonstrating its advantages over other distribution schemes, would strengthen the argument. Similarly, the choice of the column block size *k* currently appears ad hoc. Providing some theoretical guidance or a systematic approach for selecting *k* would be helpful. 

A point for consideration is whether the presented framework truly constitutes a “distributed” algorithm. The paper focuses heavily on parallelization, but the discussion of message passing and inter-process communication is limited. Clarifying the degree of distribution and perhaps framing it as a highly optimized parallel implementation would be more accurate. 

Minor editorial comments: Section 1, replace "tunning" with "tuning" in "where λ is a tunning parameter". Section 5.1, replace "tunning" with "tuning" in "As Tiger is parameter tunning free". Section 2, replace "summerized" with "summarized" in "CLIME is summerized in".

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The algorithm demonstrably scales to dimensions and parameter counts beyond the reach of existing methods.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – sparse precision matrix estimation in high dimensions – is important in various fields.
*   **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are convincing and support the claims made by the authors.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The convergence rate analysis provides a strong theoretical basis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Detailed Justification:** The reasoning behind the block cyclic data distribution and the selection of the column block size is not fully explained.
*   **Ambiguity Regarding "Distribution":** The degree of distribution could be clarified.
*   **Notation:** Scattered notation makes the paper slightly harder to follow.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation. While some clarifications and further elaboration would be beneficial, the algorithm’s scalability and practical relevance, coupled with its solid theoretical foundation and thorough experimental evaluation, make it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
