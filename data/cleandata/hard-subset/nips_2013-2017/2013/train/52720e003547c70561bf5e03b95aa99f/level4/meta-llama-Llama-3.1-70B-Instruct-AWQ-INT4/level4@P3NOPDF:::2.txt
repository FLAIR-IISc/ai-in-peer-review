This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear program problem in the CLIME estimator. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and demonstrate the algorithm's performance using both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. Numerical comparisons with other methods, such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare, are also provided.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors' approach to solving large-scale sparse precision estimation problems is well-motivated. However, there are a few minor comments to note:

1. The derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard and has been previously discussed in the literature (e.g., http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm). While the authors' presentation is clear, it would be beneficial to acknowledge the existing work in this area.
2. The authors highlight the parallelization potential of ADMM for CLIME due to the problem's structure, which allows for component-wise computation of each subproblem. As matrix-matrix multiplications can be parallelized efficiently, this is a promising aspect of the proposed algorithm.
3. To further enhance the numerical experiments, it would be helpful to specify the accuracy achieved by each solver. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithms' performance.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors present their ideas in a clear and concise manner. The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is an interesting contribution, as it highlights the algorithm's potential for parallelization.

The originality of the paper lies in its evaluation of ADMM in different architectures, which is a valuable addition to the existing literature. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM is standard, and the good scalability of the algorithm is largely due to the simple structure of CLIME.

The significance of this work is evident, as solving large-scale sparse precision estimation problems is a challenging task. A simple yet robust distributed algorithm, such as the one proposed, can be highly beneficial in this context. Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field, and with some minor revisions, it has the potential to make a more substantial impact.
