The authors address the challenge of sparse precision matrix estimation, building upon the CLIME methodology. They introduce CLIME-ADMM, a scalable adaptation, and a distributed computational framework. The paper includes empirical comparisons of CLIME-ADMM against existing state-of-the-art algorithms – DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare.

However, the notation used throughout Sections 1, 2, and 3 is currently presented in a fragmented manner. A consolidated notation table would significantly improve readability. Specifically, symbols like ρ and η are introduced without formal definition within the text. 

The proposed block cyclic data distribution is an interesting approach, but the rationale behind its expected load balance and scalability is not clearly articulated. Furthermore, the manuscript lacks empirical validation of these claims. 

The current method for setting the column block size (k) appears heuristic. Exploring the theoretical or intuitive basis for selecting this parameter would strengthen the work.

A broader point concerns the classification of the algorithm as ‘distributed.’ The paper doesn’t detail any explicit message passing or communication mechanisms, suggesting a more accurate characterization might be a parallel implementation.

Minor corrections:
Section 1: Replace “tunning parameter” with “tuning parameter”.
Section 5.1: Replace “parameter tunning free” with “parameter tuning free”.
Section 2: Replace “CLIME is summerized in” with “CLIME is summarized in”.

In summary, the paper presents a large-scale parallel algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation leveraging CLIME, with the key innovation being precision matrix estimation by column blocks rather than individual columns. Empirical results utilizing OpenMPI and Scalapak are included.
