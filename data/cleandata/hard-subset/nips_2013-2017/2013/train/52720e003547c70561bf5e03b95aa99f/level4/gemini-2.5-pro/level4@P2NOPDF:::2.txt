This paper proposes an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to solve the linear programming problem associated with the Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME). The authors establish a theoretical convergence rate of O(1/T) for the proposed algorithm. A key contribution of the work is the implementation and empirical evaluation of this algorithm on both shared-memory and distributed-memory parallel architectures. The paper presents numerical experiments comparing the method's performance against several existing solvers, including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper is technically sound and addresses the important and challenging problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The development of simple and robust distributed algorithms for this task is a valuable contribution to the field. The main strength of this work lies in its thorough empirical evaluation of the ADMM-based solver across different parallel computing architectures, which provides useful insights for practitioners.

However, the significance of the theoretical contributions is somewhat limited. The application of inexact ADMM to solve the CLIME formulation follows a relatively standard procedure. Furthermore, the impressive scalability demonstrated in the experiments is largely expected, given the specific structure of the CLIME problem. As the authors' method decomposes the problem, the subproblems can be solved component-wise, with the most computationally intensive step being matrix-matrix multiplication—an operation known to be highly parallelizable. While the empirical results are valuable, the paper would be stronger if it acknowledged that the problem structure itself is highly amenable to the parallelization strategy employed.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors' methodology and results.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Contextualizing the Algorithmic Contribution:** The derivation of the inexact ADMM algorithm for the CLIME objective is presented as a primary contribution. However, this derivation appears to be a direct application of existing techniques. The authors should better contextualize their work by acknowledging that this is a standard approach for this class of problems, perhaps by citing relevant foundational work on inexact ADMM. This would help clarify that the primary novelty lies in the large-scale implementation and empirical analysis rather than in the algorithm design itself.

2.  **Attributing Scalability:** The excellent scalability results are a highlight of the paper. It would improve the analysis to explicitly discuss *why* the algorithm scales so well. The CLIME problem's structure, which allows for component-wise updates and relies on easily parallelized matrix-matrix multiplications, is the primary enabler of this performance. A discussion of this point would provide a more nuanced interpretation of the results and properly attribute the scalability to the problem's properties in addition to the algorithmic implementation.

3.  **Experimental Details:** The numerical experiments could be strengthened by providing more specific details about the experimental setup. In particular, the termination criteria and convergence thresholds used for each solver are not specified. To facilitate a more rigorous comparison, please report the final accuracy (e.g., objective value or constraint violation) achieved by each method in the experiments. This would allow readers to better assess the trade-offs between speed and solution quality for the different algorithms.
