Summary
This paper addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact (linearized) ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column blocks rather than column-by-column, enabling efficient use of matrix–matrix multiplications and elementwise operations. They prove O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective suboptimality and optimality residuals under a step-size condition. The framework exploits two types of structure: (i) dynamic sparsity in iterates and (ii) low-rank structure of the sample covariance C = AA′ when n ≪ p, reducing the dominant cost from O(p2k) to O(npk) per block. A key system contribution is a scalable implementation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures using block-cyclic data distribution to balance load and reduce cache misses. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate near-linear speedups and the ability to estimate a precision matrix with p = 106 (≈1012 parameters) on 400 cores in ~11 hours.

Relation to prior work
CLIME [Cai et al., JASA 2011] is a popular estimator with strong statistical guarantees and a separable LP structure across columns. Prior optimization approaches include interior-point methods and ADMM variants, but typically target single-machine settings and do not scale to millions of dimensions. Linearized ADMM (inexact ADMM) is well-studied [e.g., He and Yuan, 2012; Boyd et al., 2011] and has O(1/T) rates under suitable conditions. The proposed contribution lies in a distributed, column-block ADMM design tailored to CLIME and in a practical, high-performance implementation with block-cyclic distribution, drawing on HPC literature (ScaLAPACK, blocked BLAS). The paper also discusses covariance thresholding/perturbation and connections to recent large-scale ML systems (parameter servers, GraphLab), positioning the work within scalable optimization efforts.

Quality
- Theory: The O(1/T) rates for objective and residuals are standard for linearized ADMM; the presented assumptions (e.g., η ≥ ρ λmax(C)2) are reasonable but potentially conservative in practice. It would be helpful to describe how η and ρ are selected in experiments and whether adaptive tuning/backtracking is used.
- Algorithmic design: Solving multiple columns jointly to leverage matrix–matrix kernels is a sound idea for modern architectures. The block-cyclic distribution is appropriate for balancing load and improving cache utilization.
- Experimental evaluation: The scaling studies are informative, covering both shared and distributed settings and reporting two notions of speedup (cores and column-block size). However, additional details on stopping criteria, feasibility levels, and objective values would strengthen the methodological claims. Comparisons include methods that solve different statistical objectives (e.g., DC-QUIC for graphical lasso and TIGER), which limits the interpretability of accuracy and timing comparisons.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized, with a concise derivation of the linearized ADMM updates and a clear description of the distributed framework. A few suggestions:
- Precisely define stopping criteria and report tolerances used (e.g., primal/dual residual norms in ADMM).
- Clarify how η and ρ are set; if λmax(C) is required, explain how it is estimated/distributed.
- When comparing methods, specify how λ (and other tuning parameters) are chosen and report the achieved constraint violation ||CX − I||∞ − λ (if any) to show feasibility.
- Figures: Include axes labels/units and confidence intervals when feasible; ensure that speedup baselines are clearly stated.
- References: Several entries are incomplete or malformed; please standardize and include full bibliographic details.

Originality
The inexact ADMM derivation is standard, as acknowledged. The main novelty lies in the systems/algorithmic engineering: column-block formulation to exploit matrix–matrix multiplications, the use of block-cyclic distribution for load balancing, and a careful distributed implementation that scales to p = 106. Highlighting this systems contribution and contrasting with prior CLIME solvers (and their scaling limits) would clarify the paper’s contribution.

Significance
Efficiently estimating sparse precision matrices at ultra-high dimensions is important in statistics and ML. A robust, distributed CLIME solver that runs on commodity clusters is valuable and likely to be used. The near-linear speedups and million-dimensional experiment are compelling. The work can also inform scalable approaches for other constrained estimators.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Comparisons across models: DC-QUIC and TIGER optimize different objectives than CLIME. For fairness, (i) compare CLIME-ADMM against state-of-the-art CLIME solvers at scale (e.g., flare’s ADMM variant), and (ii) when reporting precision/recall, provide PR/AUC or F1 across a grid, and separate plots per model class. Consider adding runtime-to-target-accuracy curves (e.g., time vs. PR-AUC at a fixed sparsity).
- Accuracy and feasibility: Report objective values and constraint residuals ||CX − I||∞ at termination, along with stopping tolerances. This will address questions about the accuracy achieved by each solver.
- Parameter selection: Provide guidance on ρ and η (fixed vs. adaptive), and on λ selection (e.g., theory-driven λ ∝ √(log p/n) vs. grid search). If λmax(C) is needed, describe how it is estimated in distributed settings.
- Perturbation/thresholding of C: Theorem 3 is interesting but needs tighter statements. Please specify the sub-exponential parameters and scaling of Δ to ensure ||Δ||∞ = O(√(log p/n)) with high probability, and discuss how this affects the bias/variance trade-off. It would be useful to cite covariance thresholding literature and exact thresholding results (e.g., Mazumder & Hastie, 2012) and to provide empirical results showing negligible loss in statistical accuracy when using the perturbed C.
- Symmetry/PD of the estimate: CLIME solutions are not necessarily symmetric/PD. Do you apply the standard CLIME symmetrization step? Please clarify how symmetry/PD is handled in practice and whether this affects scalability.
- Communication costs: Include a brief per-iteration communication analysis (messages/bytes as functions of p, n, k, and process grid) and a breakdown of time per iteration (matrix multiplications vs. elementwise ops vs. communication).
- Memory footprint: Report per-node memory usage for the largest experiments, including storage of A and the O(pk) variables, and discuss how k trades off between compute efficiency and memory/communication.
- Sensitivity to k and n: Provide guidance for selecting k given n, cache sizes, and process-grid dimensions; include a brief sensitivity analysis.

Pros
- Strong scalability on both shared and distributed architectures; clear engineering contributions.
- Simple, practical algorithm relying on efficient matrix kernels; amenable to HPC libraries.
- Theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed linearized ADMM scheme.
- Compelling large-scale experiment (p = 106) demonstrating feasibility.

Cons
- Core optimization idea (linearized ADMM for CLIME) is standard; novelty is primarily systems-level.
- Comparisons mix different model classes; limited reporting on feasibility/objective accuracy.
- Some theoretical claims (perturbation of C) need sharper conditions and empirical validation.
- Incomplete details on parameter tuning, stopping, communication, and per-node memory.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The systems contribution and demonstrated scalability are valuable and relevant to the community, and the paper is technically sound. Strengthening the experimental methodology (accuracy/feasibility reporting, clearer baselines within the CLIME family) and tightening the perturbation result would make the paper more compelling.
