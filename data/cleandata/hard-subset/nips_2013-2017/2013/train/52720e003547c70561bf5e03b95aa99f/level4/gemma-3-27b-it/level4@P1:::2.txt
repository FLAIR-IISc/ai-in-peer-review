This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear program underlying the CLIME estimator for sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and demonstrate the algorithm’s implementation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. Numerical comparisons are provided against existing methods such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm builds on well-established techniques – ADMM and CLIME – and appropriately adapts them to the problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The convergence analysis appears rigorous. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME follows standard approaches, such as those found in He and Yuan (2013) [http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm]. A brief acknowledgement of this existing work would strengthen the presentation. The authors correctly highlight that the computational bottleneck lies in the matrix-matrix multiplications, justifying the focus on parallelization strategies. While the paper provides empirical validation, it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the accuracy achieved by each solver, beyond just runtime comparisons.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The notation is mostly consistent, although expanding the definitions in the notation section slightly would improve readability. The algorithmic description is clear, and the explanations of the distributed computation framework are reasonably detailed.

**Originality:** The primary contribution lies in the efficient implementation and scaling of ADMM for CLIME, specifically in leveraging column-block updates and a block-cyclic data distribution scheme. Evaluating ADMM's performance in both shared-memory and distributed-memory environments is a valuable addition. However, it’s important to acknowledge that the underlying idea of parallelizing ADMM for matrix computations is not novel. The specific advantage of this work stems from its application to the CLIME problem, exploiting its inherent structure for efficient parallelization.

**Significance:** Solving large-scale sparse precision estimation problems remains a significant challenge. This work provides a practical and scalable algorithm that addresses this challenge, potentially making CLIME applicable to datasets previously intractable due to computational limitations. The demonstration of near-linear scalability with the number of cores is particularly encouraging. However, the authors rightly point out that the scalability is largely due to the inherent structure of CLIME, which allows for efficient parallelization of the core matrix operations. The work would be further strengthened by discussing potential applications where this scalability would be particularly beneficial.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem: large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation.
*   Presents a technically sound and well-implemented algorithm.
*   Demonstrates excellent scalability, approaching linear speedup with increasing cores.
*   Provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the method and results.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The core algorithmic idea (ADMM for CLIME) builds heavily on existing work and lacks a major theoretical breakthrough.
*   The convergence analysis, while correct, relies on standard results.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, including a detailed discussion of the accuracy and robustness of the proposed method alongside runtime comparisons.
