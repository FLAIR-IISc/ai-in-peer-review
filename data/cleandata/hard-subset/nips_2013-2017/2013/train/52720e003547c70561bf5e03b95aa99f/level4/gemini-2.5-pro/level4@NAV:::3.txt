**Summary of the paper**

This work addresses the challenge of solving covariance selection problems in massive-scale settings, encompassing millions of features and trillions of parameters. The paper focuses on the CLIME estimator for the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector, which is noted for its column-wise decomposability that makes it highly suitable for distributed computation. The authors also highlight that CLIME's theoretical analysis is more tractable than that of the Graphical-Lasso.

The primary contribution is a novel algorithm for fitting the CLIME estimator. This method departs from traditional approaches by solving the problem in a block-wise fashion, rather than column-wise, and incorporates an inexact direction method of multipliers. The paper establishes theoretical results for the algorithm's convergence rate, both in terms of the objective function and the distance to optimality. To mitigate the computational load, the algorithm is designed to leverage special matrix structures like sparsity and low-rank properties during computation. The implementation is realized within a scalable parallel framework compatible with both shared and distributed memory systems. In the numerical experiments, the algorithm's runtime is benchmarked against its main competitors on synthetic and real-world data. A detailed performance study is also presented, evaluating the speedup achieved by varying block sizes and the number of cores in both shared and distributed memory environments.

**Comments**

The manuscript is well-written and exceptionally clear. The novelty and contributions are explicitly stated in the introduction, which also effectively situates the work within the existing literature with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is introduced effectively, striking a good balance between the technical details of numerical analysis and algebra and the theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation achieves very impressive performance.

My only reservation concerns the accessibility of this method. For a work of this nature, its impact is severely limited if the source code, which is largely based on open-source libraries, is not released to the research community.

This is a sound algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection in extremely high dimensions. Such a powerful tool should be accepted for publication, on the condition that it is made available to the community.
