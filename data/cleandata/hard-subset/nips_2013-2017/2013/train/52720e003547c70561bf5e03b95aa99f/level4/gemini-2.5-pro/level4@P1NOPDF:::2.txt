This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear programming problem inherent in the CLIME estimator. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) for their method. They implement the algorithm on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures and provide numerical comparisons against other methods, including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound.

**Minor Comments:**

1) The application of the inexact ADMM to the CLIME problem follows a standard derivation. See, for example:
http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm

2) The strong performance of the parallel ADMM implementation is largely expected due to the structure of the CLIME problem. Specifically, the solution to each subproblem can be computed component-wise, which makes matrix-matrix multiplication the most computationally expensive operation. Since matrix-matrix multiplication is known to be highly parallelizable, the impressive scalability of the proposed ADMM approach is a direct consequence of this underlying structure.

3) The setup for the numerical experiments could be described in greater detail. For instance, the authors should specify the convergence tolerance or final accuracy achieved by each of the compared solvers to allow for a fairer comparison.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized.

**Originality:** The main novelty of this work lies in its thorough evaluation of an ADMM algorithm for CLIME on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. While the algorithm itself is a straightforward application of existing methods, its empirical analysis in large-scale parallel settings is interesting.

**Significance:** Solving large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation problems is an important and challenging task. Therefore, a simple and robust distributed algorithm is a welcome contribution to the field. The empirical evaluation of ADMM's performance on different parallel architectures is particularly useful for practitioners. However, the overall significance is somewhat tempered by the fact that the theoretical derivation is standard, and the algorithm's excellent scalability is an expected outcome given the highly parallelizable structure of the CLIME problem.
