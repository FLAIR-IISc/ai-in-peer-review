This paper presents a large-scale distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact alternating direction method of multiplier (ADMM) algorithm for CLIME and establish rates of convergence for both the objective and optimality conditions. They also develop a distributed framework for the computations, which scales to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters using hundreds of cores.

The paper relates to previous work on sparse precision matrix estimation, including the CLIME estimator, which has strong statistical guarantees. The authors also draw on recent advances in developing computationally efficient optimization methods for solving statistical estimation problems. However, their focus on designing an efficient distributed algorithm for precision matrix estimation under ultra-large-scale dimensional settings is a novel contribution.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a detailed description of their algorithm and framework, including notation, which is helpful for understanding the technical details. The experimental results are also well-presented, with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and an evaluation of the scalability of their algorithm.

One minor comment is that the notation could be improved by including a table with all the notations for ease of readability. For example, ρ and η are not defined in the text. Additionally, the block cyclic data distribution scheme is interesting, but it is not entirely clear why this scheme would achieve load balance and scalability. Some empirical results or further explanation would be helpful to support this claim.

Another concern is that the algorithm currently sets the column block size on an ad-hoc basis. It would be helpful to have some intuition or theory to guide the choice of k. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the communication and synchronization mechanisms used in the distributed framework. While the authors mention that the framework uses OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK, it is not entirely clear how the data is distributed and how the computations are parallelized.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation, and the authors have made a good effort to clearly present their work. With some minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the NIPS conference.
