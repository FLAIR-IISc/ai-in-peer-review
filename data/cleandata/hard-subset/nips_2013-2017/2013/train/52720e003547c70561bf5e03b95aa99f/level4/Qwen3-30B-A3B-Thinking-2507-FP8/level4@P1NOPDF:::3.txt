

Summary  
This paper addresses covariance selection in ultra-high-dimensional settings (up to millions of features and trillions of precision matrix entries), focusing on the CLIME estimator. CLIME offers a column-wise solvable precision matrix estimator for Gaussian vectors, facilitating distributed computation and simplifying theoretical analysis compared to Graphical Lasso. The authors propose a novel block-wise algorithm for CLIME, combining inexact ADMM with specialized handling of sparse and low-rank structures to accelerate computation. Theoretical convergence rates are established for both the objective function and optimality gap. The method is implemented in a scalable parallel framework supporting shared and distributed memory architectures. Numerical experiments demonstrate runtime advantages over competitors on synthetic and real data, with a detailed analysis of speedup as a function of block size and core count across architectures.  

Comments  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The novelty and contributions are precisely articulated in the introduction, with thorough contextualization against existing work and robust bibliographic references. The algorithmic exposition balances technical depth in numerical analysis/algebra with rigorous convergence guarantees. The numerical validation is comprehensive, and the implementation achieves exceptional performance.  

My sole reservation pertains to reproducibility: the absence of publicly available source code—primarily built on open-source libraries—would significantly undermine the paper’s contribution. Given the paper’s focus on a critical practical problem (scalable covariance selection for massive datasets), the method’s impact is contingent on community access. A sound algorithmic advance of this caliber merits publication only if accompanied by open-source code.
