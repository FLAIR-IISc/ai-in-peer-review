This paper addresses the problem of sparse precision matrix estimation by proposing a scalable variant of the CLIME algorithm, termed CLIME-ADMM. The primary novelty lies in a distributed computational framework that estimates the precision matrix in column blocks rather than column-by-column. The authors present empirical results comparing the performance of their proposed algorithm against state-of-the-art methods such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare, using an implementation based on OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Clarity of Notation:** The notation is scattered throughout Sections 1, 2, and 3, which makes the paper difficult to follow. For instance, parameters such as `\rho` and `\eta` appear to be used without a formal definition in the text. To improve readability and ease of reference, I strongly recommend consolidating all notation into a single table.

2.  **Justification for Block Cyclic Data Distribution:** The proposed block cyclic data distribution scheme is an interesting component of the framework. However, the paper asserts that this scheme achieves better load balancing and scalability without providing sufficient justification. It is not immediately obvious why this would be the case. The claims would be significantly strengthened by including either a theoretical argument or empirical results (e.g., ablation studies) that demonstrate the advantages of this specific distribution strategy over simpler alternatives.

3.  **Guidance on Parameter Selection:** The algorithm's performance seems dependent on the choice of the column block size, `k`. The current description suggests this parameter is set on an ad-hoc basis. The paper would be more impactful if it provided some intuition, theoretical guidance, or an empirical study to guide the selection of `k`. A principled approach for setting this parameter is essential for the method's practical application.

4.  **"Distributed" vs. "Parallel" Implementation:** A more fundamental concern is the characterization of the algorithm as "distributed." A distributed algorithm typically involves an explicit discussion of the message-passing mechanisms, communication overhead, and fault tolerance strategies. The paper focuses primarily on the parallelization of computation across multiple cores or nodes. Therefore, it might be more accurate to frame the contribution as a large-scale *parallel* implementation of CLIME. If the authors maintain the "distributed" claim, the manuscript should be updated to include a detailed discussion of the communication protocols and architecture.

**Minor Comments**

*   Section 1: "...where `\lambda` is a tunning parameter..." should be "...where `\lambda` is a **tuning** parameter...".
*   Section 2: "CLIME is summerized in..." should be "CLIME is **summarized** in...".
*   Section 5.1: "As Tiger is parameter tunning free..." should be "As Tiger is parameter **tuning** free...".
