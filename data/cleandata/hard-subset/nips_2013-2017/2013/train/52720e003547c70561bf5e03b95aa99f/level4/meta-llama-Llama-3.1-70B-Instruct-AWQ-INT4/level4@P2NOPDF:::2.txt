The authors propose an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving linear programs in the CLIME estimator. They establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and implement the algorithm using both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The paper also presents numerical comparisons with other methods, including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

Quality: The paper is technically sound.

Minor comments:

1. The derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME follows standard procedures, as seen in similar works (e.g., http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm). While the derivation is correct, it does not offer new insights into the methodology.

2. The structure of CLIME allows for component-wise computation of each subproblem, with matrix-matrix multiplication being the most expensive operation. As matrix-matrix multiplications can be parallelized efficiently, the ADMM algorithm can be ideally parallelized for CLIME. This is a well-known advantage of such algorithms.

3. To further enhance the numerical experiments, it would be beneficial to specify the accuracy achieved by each solver. Providing this information would allow for a more comprehensive comparison of the methods.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and methodology.

Originality: The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is an interesting contribution, as it explores the algorithm's performance in different computational settings.

Significance: Solving large-scale sparse precision estimation problems is a challenging task. In this context, a simple yet robust distributed algorithm can be highly beneficial. The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is a valuable addition to the field. However, the derivation of inexact ADMM is standard, and the good scalability of the algorithm is largely due to the simple structure of CLIME.
