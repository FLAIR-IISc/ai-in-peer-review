This paper addresses the challenge of sparse precision matrix estimation for ultra-large-scale problems. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, which is known for its strong statistical guarantees and its decomposable structure. The primary contribution is a novel, scalable algorithm called CLIME-ADMM, which is an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers variant. This algorithm is coupled with a large-scale distributed computation framework designed to handle millions of dimensions.

Key ideas of the proposed approach include:
1.  **Column-Block Processing:** Instead of solving the CLIME problem column-by-column, which is standard, the authors propose solving for blocks of columns simultaneously. This reframes the core computation into matrix-matrix multiplications, which can be highly optimized on modern hardware, rather than repeated matrix-vector multiplications.
2.  **Inexact ADMM Formulation:** An inexact ADMM algorithm is developed to solve the column-block optimization problem. The updates consist of element-wise operations and matrix multiplications, making them suitable for parallelization. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees for this algorithm, showing an O(1/T) rate for both the objective value and constraint violations.
3.  **Scalable Distributed Framework:** The paper describes implementations for both shared-memory (using OpenMP) and distributed-memory (using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK) architectures. A key aspect of the distributed framework is the use of block cyclic data distribution to ensure load balancing and efficient use of memory hierarchies.

This work builds upon a rich literature of both statistical estimation of precision matrices (e.g., CLIME by Cai et al., graphical lasso by Friedman et al.) and large-scale optimization methods (e.g., ADMM literature by Boyd et al.). While previous methods for CLIME exist, they were not designed to scale to the massive dimensions targeted here. This paper effectively bridges the gap between statistical theory and high-performance computing to tackle a problem of significant practical and theoretical interest.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Ambition:** The paper tackles a very important and challenging problem: scaling a statistically sound estimator to dimensions (p = 10^6) that are largely intractable for existing methods. The ability to estimate a precision matrix with a trillion parameters is a significant engineering and algorithmic achievement. This work could enable new scientific applications where high-dimensional dependency modeling is crucial.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm is well-founded. The authors provide proofs for its convergence rate, lending theoretical credibility to the method. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing, demonstrating substantial performance gains over state-of-the-art methods like `flare` and DC-QUIC on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The scalability experiments are particularly impressive, showing near-linear speedups with hundreds of cores.

3.  **Novelty:** The combination of a column-block strategy with an inexact ADMM solver for the CLIME problem is novel. While ADMM and block-processing are known techniques, their application and careful implementation in this context, particularly the focus on leveraging optimized matrix-multiplication kernels and block cyclic distribution, represent a solid contribution.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Hyperparameter Selection:** The performance of the algorithm appears to heavily depend on the column block size `k`. The experiments show that the choice of `k` impacts speedup, but the paper offers little guidance on how to select this crucial parameter. Is there a theoretical or empirical heuristic to guide this choice based on problem size, data structure, or hardware characteristics (e.g., cache size, number of cores)? A discussion on this would make the method more accessible and practical for others to use.

2.  **Substantiating the Impact of Block Cyclic Distribution:** The authors claim that block cyclic distribution is a key element for achieving load balance and scalability. While this is a standard and plausible technique in high-performance computing, the paper does not provide direct empirical evidence to support this claim in their specific context. The work would be strengthened by an ablation study that compares the performance of the block cyclic scheme against a simpler data distribution method (e.g., assigning large contiguous blocks to each process). This would isolate and quantify the benefit of this specific design choice.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:**
    *   The notation is introduced across several sections, which can make it difficult for the reader to follow. For instance, the parameters `ρ` and `η` for the ADMM algorithm are used in Algorithm 1 but formally defined later in the text. Consolidating key notations into a dedicated paragraph or table at the beginning of Section 2 would significantly improve readability.
    *   The paper frames the work as a "distributed" algorithm. While it is implemented on a distributed-memory system, the algorithmic description focuses more on the parallelization of computation and less on the communication aspects (e.g., communication costs, synchronization patterns). Explicitly discussing the communication overhead or model would provide a more complete picture and better justify the "distributed" framing.

4.  **Minor Comments:**
    *   Section 1: "where λ is a tunning parameter" -> "tuning"
    *   Section 2: "The ADMM algorithm for CLIME is summerized in Algorithm 1" -> "summarized"
    *   Section 5.1: "As Tiger is parameter tunning free" -> "tuning"

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a well-designed and highly scalable system for a fundamental problem in high-dimensional statistics. The empirical results are impressive and demonstrate a significant advance in the state of the art for large-scale precision matrix estimation. While there are opportunities to improve clarity and provide more guidance on hyperparameter selection, the core contributions are significant and impactful.
