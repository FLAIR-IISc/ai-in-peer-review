**Summary of the Paper**

This manuscript addresses the resolution of covariance selection problems in extremely large-scale settings, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, which provides an estimate of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be efficiently solved column by column, making it more amenable to distributed computations. Theoretical analysis of CLIME is also claimed to be more straightforward compared to the Graphical-Lasso. The authors propose a novel algorithm for fitting CLIME by solving the problem block-wise, coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers. They provide theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm in terms of both the objective function and distance to optimality. Special consideration is given to exploiting special matrix structures, such as sparsity and low rank, to reduce computational burden. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework suitable for both shared and distributed memory systems. Numerical studies compare the algorithm's runtime with its direct competitors on both synthetic and real data, and a detailed analysis evaluates the speedup with respect to block sizes and the number of cores on both shared memory and distributed memory systems.

**Comments**

The manuscript is well-written and exceptionally clear. The introduction clearly articulates the novelty and contributions of the method, establishing a strong connection to existing works through sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is introduced with a suitable balance between technical aspects of numerical analysis and algebra, as well as theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation demonstrates impressive performance.

However, my primary concern is the availability of this method to the community. If the source code, which relies heavily on open-source libraries, is not made available, the impact of this work is severely limited.

Overall, this is a solid algorithmic paper that addresses the critical practical problem of covariance selection in extremely high-dimensional settings. If the authors make the method available to the community, it would be a valuable contribution worthy of publication.
