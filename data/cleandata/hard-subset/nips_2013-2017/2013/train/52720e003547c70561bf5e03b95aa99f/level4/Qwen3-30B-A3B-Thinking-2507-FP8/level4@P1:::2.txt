

This paper proposes a scalable distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, addressing the significant challenge of ultra-high-dimensional problems (up to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters). The authors present an inexact ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column blocks rather than column-by-column, enabling efficient parallelization on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. They establish convergence rates of O(1/T) for both the objective function and optimality conditions, and demonstrate substantial scalability improvements over existing methods.

The work builds on previous research on CLIME (Cai et al., 2011; Cai et al., 2013), which provides strong statistical guarantees but has limited computational scalability. The authors extend the ADMM approach to CLIME (previously applied to similar problems by Hsieh et al., 2011; Cai et al., 2013), but make two key contributions: (1) solving the problem in column blocks rather than individual columns, which enables more efficient matrix-matrix operations, and (2) developing a distributed framework using block cyclic data distribution that achieves excellent load balancing and cache efficiency. This addresses a critical gap in the literature, as existing methods cannot handle problems with millions of dimensions due to memory constraints and poor parallelization.

Quality: The technical content is sound and well-supported. The convergence rate analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the scalability advantages. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is largely standard (as noted by the authors in their references to Boyd et al., 2011 and He & Yuan, 2012). The key innovation lies in the distributed framework and column-block approach, not in the core ADMM algorithm itself. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the column-block approach versus the column-by-column approach, particularly regarding the cache efficiency improvements.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm description is precise, and the experimental setup is adequately detailed. However, the paper would be strengthened by providing more specific details about the numerical tolerances used in the experiments (e.g., the stopping criteria for the ADMM iterations) and the exact accuracy metrics achieved by each method. The comparison with Tiger, CLIME-Flare, and DC-QUIC is well-executed, but the paper should clarify why Tiger (which is parameter-free and achieves minimax rates) is not the preferred method for large-scale problems despite its strong theoretical properties.

Originality: The main contribution is the distributed framework for CLIME that scales to millions of dimensions, which represents a significant practical advance. While the ADMM approach itself is not novel, the column-block implementation and block cyclic distribution scheme provide new insights into efficient parallelization of precision matrix estimation. This is particularly valuable given that the problem size (p = 10^6, p^2 = 10^12 parameters) is far beyond what current state-of-the-art methods can handle. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach scales almost linearly with the number of cores, which is a significant advantage for large-scale applications.

Significance: The problem of ultra-high-dimensional precision matrix estimation is highly relevant to modern machine learning applications in genomics, finance, and climate science, where datasets can easily contain millions of features. The authors' framework enables precision matrix estimation for problems that were previously computationally intractable. The experimental results showing a 11-hour runtime for a 1 million-dimensional problem on 400 cores is compelling evidence of practical significance. However, the paper would benefit from discussing potential applications beyond precision matrix estimation (e.g., how this framework might be adapted to other constrained optimization problems in machine learning).

Strengths:
- Demonstrates impressive scalability to ultra-high-dimensional problems (p = 10^6, p^2 = 10^12 parameters)
- Establishes rigorous convergence rates for the proposed algorithm
- Provides thorough experimental validation across multiple architectures
- Presents a well-designed distributed framework with block cyclic data distribution
- Shows nearly linear speedup with increasing number of cores

Weaknesses:
- The core ADMM algorithm is not novel, which somewhat diminishes the technical contribution
- Lacks sufficient detail on numerical tolerances and accuracy metrics
- The connection to broader applications beyond precision matrix estimation could be strengthened
- The comparison with existing methods could better explain why their approach is superior for large-scale problems

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important practical challenge in high-dimensional statistics. The distributed framework represents a significant step forward for scalable precision matrix estimation, and the experimental results are compelling. While the core ADMM algorithm is standard, the implementation details and scalability results are valuable contributions to the field. The paper would be strengthened with more specific details about the numerical experiments and a clearer articulation of the broader applicability of the framework.

The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of how their framework might be adapted for other large-scale constrained optimization problems in machine learning, as this would further enhance the significance of their contribution. The technical content is sound, the presentation is clear, and the results are well-supported, making this a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale statistical learning.
