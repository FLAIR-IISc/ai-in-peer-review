### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the problem of covariance selection in very large-scale settings, with dimensions reaching millions of features and trillions of parameters. The work focuses on the Constrained L1-Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation (CLIME) estimator for the precision matrix of a Gaussian random vector. A key advantage of CLIME is that the estimation problem can be decomposed and solved column-by-column, which makes it highly amenable to distributed computation. The authors also note that its theoretical analysis is generally more straightforward than that of the graphical lasso.

The authors propose a new algorithm to fit the CLIME estimator by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupling this approach with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers. The paper provides theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm, analyzed in terms of both the objective function value and the distance to the optimal solution. To reduce the computational burden, the algorithm is designed to exploit special matrix structures, such as sparsity and low-rank properties.

This algorithm is implemented in a scalable parallel framework designed for both shared and distributed-memory systems. In the numerical studies, the authors compare their method's runtime against direct competitors on both synthetic and real-world data. A detailed analysis is also provided to evaluate the algorithm's speedup as a function of block size and the number of processing cores in both shared and distributed-memory environments.

### Comments

The paper is well-written and clear. The novelty and contributions are clearly stated in the introduction, and the work is effectively situated within the context of existing literature with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is introduced effectively, striking a good balance between the technical details of numerical analysis and the theoretical guarantees for its convergence rate. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation demonstrates very impressive performance.

My primary concern is the availability of the proposed method. A significant part of this work's contribution is its high-performance implementation, which appears to be built upon open-source libraries. If the source code is not made available to the research community, the practical impact of this work would be severely limited.

Overall, this is a strong algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection in extremely high dimensions. Such a powerful tool merits publication, provided that the implementation is made available to the community.
