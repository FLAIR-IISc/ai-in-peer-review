This paper proposes an inexact Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for solving the linear program inherent in the CLIME (Constrained L1 Minimization Estimator) estimator. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and demonstrate the algorithm’s implementation across both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. Numerical comparisons are presented against existing methods including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a reasonable application of ADMM to the CLIME problem. However, the novelty of the ADMM derivation itself is limited, as similar approaches are readily available (e.g., see http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm). The strength of the work lies in the practical implementation and evaluation, particularly the exploration of shared and distributed memory parallelization. 

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally easy to follow. The ADMM formulation for the CLIME problem is presented clearly.

**Originality:** While the core ADMM approach isn’t novel, the evaluation of this ADMM implementation within both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, and the resulting scalability analysis, *is* a valuable contribution. The investigation of performance on these different architectures differentiates this work.

**Significance:** Large-scale sparse precision estimation remains a significant challenge. Providing a simple and robust distributed algorithm like this one is beneficial to the field. The good scalability observed is directly attributable to the inherent structure of the CLIME problem, which allows for ideal parallelization of the subproblems – primarily componentwise solutions and matrix-matrix multiplications, both known to parallelize efficiently. This connection between the CLIME structure and scalability is well noted by the authors.

**Concerns and Suggestions:**

* **Experiment Detail:** The presentation of numerical experiments could be significantly improved. Currently, it lacks sufficient detail regarding solver accuracy. For each method compared (DC-QUIC, Tiger, CLIME-Flare, and the proposed ADMM), the authors should report metrics quantifying the solution accuracy achieved – for example, primal and dual feasibility tolerances, or a measure of the optimality gap.  Simply presenting runtime comparisons without accuracy information is insufficient. Were the solvers stopped based on a time limit, or a convergence criterion? This needs clarification.
* **Subproblem Parallelization:** The observation that CLIME’s structure enables efficient parallelization is correct and important. However, the paper would benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion of *how* this parallelization is achieved in practice, beyond simply stating that matrix-matrix multiplications parallelize well. For example, what libraries (e.g., BLAS, LAPACK, MPI) are used, and what specific parallelization strategies are employed within these libraries? 
* **Convergence Rate Context:** The stated convergence rate of O(1/T) is correct within the context of inexact ADMM. However, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the implications of this rate for practical performance. Is this rate fast enough for the targeted problem sizes?  How does the inexactness impact the overall performance?
* **Comparison Focus:** While comparisons to DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare are valuable, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the *strengths and weaknesses* of each method relative to the proposed ADMM approach. What scenarios might favor one method over another?



**Overall:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed implementation of ADMM for solving the CLIME problem. The contributions are incremental but valuable, particularly the demonstration of scalability on both shared- and distributed-memory architectures. Addressing the concerns raised regarding experiment detail and providing a more nuanced discussion of the method’s performance relative to competitors would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
