- Addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator and proposes CLIME-ADMM with a distributed computation framework.  
- Introduces an inexact ADMM variant and a column-block decomposition to improve scalability over existing ADMM approaches.  
- Situates work within prior literature on statistical convergence rates and scalable ML algorithms; motivation for ultra-large-scale problems is clear.  
- Provides theoretical convergence-rate analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) underpinning the algorithm’s performance.  
- Proposes block cyclic data distribution for load balancing in the distributed-memory setting and explores exploiting sparse/low-rank structure for further gains.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive (synthetic and real datasets) and compares against DC-QUIC, Tiger, and flare implementations.  
- Results show CLIME-ADMM outperforms state-of-the-art methods in scalability, especially for very high-dimensional problems.  
- Notation is scattered and should be consolidated into a dedicated table; parameters like ρ and η need clearer definitions.  
- The justification for block cyclic data distribution is insufficient; needs explanation of why it achieves load balance and empirical comparisons to other schemes.  
- The choice of column block size k appears ad hoc and requires theoretical guidance or a systematic selection strategy.  
- Unclear whether the framework is truly distributed versus a highly optimized parallel implementation; more detail on message passing and inter-process communication is needed.  
- Minor editorial fixes: replace "tunning" with "tuning" (Sections 1 and 5.1) and "summerized" with "summarized" (Section 2).  
- Arguments for acceptance: demonstrable scalability, practical relevance, solid empirical evaluation, and a strong theoretical foundation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacking detailed justification for distribution/design choices, ambiguity about distribution level, and scattered notation.
