

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions (millions of variables and trillions of parameters) using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm for CLIME that achieves O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions, and develop a scalable distributed framework that processes the precision matrix in column-blocks using block cyclic data distribution. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions such as [13] (DC-QUIC), [14] (CLIME), and [18] (ADMM for CLIME), but significantly advances the state of the art by enabling computation on scales previously considered intractable. The framework is implemented on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, demonstrating near-linear scalability with the number of cores and achieving substantial speedups over existing methods.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis establishing convergence rates. The experimental design is comprehensive, comparing against state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, CLIME-Flare) on both synthetic and real datasets. The authors properly acknowledge the standard nature of the inexact ADMM derivation (as seen in He's work [12]), and the implementation details are thorough. A minor improvement would be to specify the accuracy thresholds achieved by each solver in the experiments, as this would provide more complete context for the performance comparisons.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized with clear technical exposition. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and accessible, and the theoretical results are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor. The experimental section effectively demonstrates the scalability claims, with well-constructed figures showing speedup behavior across different architectures.

Originality: The column-block processing approach for CLIME, combined with the block cyclic data distribution strategy, represents a meaningful technical contribution. While ADMM has been used for precision estimation before [18, 23], the authors' specific implementation for ultra-high dimensions with nearly linear scaling to hundreds of cores is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work, particularly in addressing the scalability limitations of existing methods.

Significance: The ability to estimate precision matrices at the million-dimensional scale (with trillions of parameters) is highly significant for modern high-dimensional statistical applications. The paper demonstrates that their method can solve such problems in reasonable time (11 hours on 400 cores), which represents a substantial practical advance over existing approaches. The distributed framework could be adapted to other large-scale constrained optimization problems, extending its impact beyond the specific application of precision matrix estimation.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, excellent scalability demonstration, well-structured paper, meaningful contribution to ultra-high dimensional statistics.  
Con: The inexact ADMM derivation is standard (not a major novelty), and the paper would benefit from more detailed accuracy metrics in the experimental results.
