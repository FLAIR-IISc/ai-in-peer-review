This paper addresses the challenging problem of covariance selection in high-dimensional settings, scaling to datasets with millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, a method for estimating the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that offers computational advantages over alternatives like Graphical Lasso, particularly its suitability for distributed computation. They present a novel algorithm for fitting CLIME, employing a block-wise solution approach coupled with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the algorithm’s convergence rate, analyzed with respect to both objective function value and distance to optimality. Recognizing the computational burden of large-scale problems, the authors exploit sparse and low-rank matrix structures to improve efficiency.  A scalable parallel implementation, designed for both shared and distributed memory systems, is described. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated through comprehensive numerical experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, comparing runtime against competing methods.  A detailed scalability analysis investigates the impact of block sizes and core counts on both shared and distributed memory architectures.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear in its exposition. The authors clearly articulate the novelty and contributions of their method in the introduction, and provide a thorough and well-referenced discussion of related work. The algorithm is presented with a commendable balance between the necessary technical details of numerical analysis and algebraic considerations, alongside rigorous theoretical convergence guarantees. The numerical evaluation is appropriate and demonstrates impressive performance gains.

My primary concern regards the accessibility of this work. The practical impact of a powerful algorithm designed to address a critical, real-world problem—covariance selection with massive datasets—is significantly diminished without publicly available source code. Given the implementation relies heavily on open-source libraries, making the code accessible to the community would greatly enhance the reproducibility and usability of the research. 

Overall, this is a strong algorithmic paper that tackles an important and increasingly prevalent practical challenge. Publication is strongly recommended *conditional* on the release of the source code to the research community.
