

The authors present a scalable algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, introducing a column-block ADMM variant and a distributed framework that achieves linear scalability with the number of cores. The work extends prior NIPS contributions on precision matrix estimation [3, 13, 14, 19] and distributed optimization [2, 6, 9, 23] by addressing ultra-large-scale problems (up to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters) through column-block processing and block cyclic data distribution.

Strengths:
- Theoretical convergence rates (O(1/T)) for both objective and optimality conditions are rigorously established
- Empirical results demonstrate substantial scalability improvements over state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, Flare) on both synthetic and real datasets
- The column-block approach represents a meaningful extension of prior CLIME solvers [3, 18]
- Implementation using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK is well-documented and provides valuable practical insights
- The framework effectively handles the quadratic parameter scaling problem (p² parameters for p dimensions)

Weaknesses:
- Notation is scattered throughout the paper (e.g., ρ and η are not clearly defined), which hinders readability
- The block cyclic data distribution is presented as innovative but lacks theoretical justification for its load-balancing properties
- The choice of column block size k appears ad hoc without theoretical guidance or systematic analysis
- The algorithm is more accurately described as a parallel implementation rather than a distributed algorithm, as it lacks explicit discussion of message passing and communication protocols
- Minor grammatical errors (e.g., "tunning" instead of "tuning" in Sections 1 and 5.1, "summerized" in Section 2)

Quality: The paper is technically sound with proper theoretical analysis and convincing experimental validation. The convergence rates are well-established, and the scalability results are impressive (1 million dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores). However, the lack of theoretical justification for key design choices slightly impacts the quality.

Clarity: The paper would significantly benefit from a dedicated notation table and clearer explanation of the block cyclic distribution. The scattered notation and minor grammatical errors reduce readability.

Originality: The column-block approach for CLIME represents a novel contribution to precision matrix estimation literature, differentiating it from prior column-by-column approaches [3, 18] and distributed optimization frameworks [9, 23].

Significance: The work addresses a significant practical challenge in high-dimensional statistics with potential to enable precision matrix estimation at previously intractable scales. The demonstrated scalability (1 million dimensions, 1 trillion parameters) has clear implications for applications in genomics, climate science, and other fields.

Pro arguments: Strong theoretical foundation, impressive scalability results, clear presentation of the column-block approach, well-executed experiments, and practical implementation details.

Con arguments: Lack of theoretical justification for key design choices, scattered notation, minor grammatical errors, and terminology issue regarding "distributed" versus "parallel" algorithm.
