### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of covariance selection in very large-scale settings, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the Constrained L1-Minimization Estimator (CLIME), which provides an estimate of a Gaussian vector's precision matrix. CLIME is known to be solvable column-by-column, making it well-suited for distributed computation, and its theoretical analysis is often simpler than that of the Graphical Lasso.

The primary contribution is a new algorithm for fitting the CLIME estimator. This algorithm solves the problem block-wise, rather than column-by-column, and is coupled with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers. The authors provide theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm, both in terms of the objective function value and the distance to the optimal solution. Special attention is given to exploiting matrix structures, such as sparsity and low-rank properties, to reduce the computational cost. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework for both shared and distributed memory systems. In the numerical studies, the method's runtime is compared with its direct competitors on both synthetic and real data. A detailed analysis is also provided to evaluate the algorithm's speedup with respect to block size and the number of cores on both shared and distributed memory architectures.

### Comments

This paper is well-written and very clear. The novelty and contributions of the proposed method are clearly articulated in the introduction, and the connections to existing work are well-established with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is introduced effectively, striking a good balance between technical details from numerical analysis/algebra and theoretical guarantees for its convergence rate. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation demonstrates very impressive performance.

My only concern is the availability of this method. Given that this is a significant algorithmic and implementation-focused contribution, its value to the research community is contingent upon the source code (which is noted to be based on open-source libraries) being made publicly available.

This is a sound algorithmic paper that addresses the important practical problem of high-dimensional covariance selection. Such a powerful tool merits publication, provided it is made available to the community.
