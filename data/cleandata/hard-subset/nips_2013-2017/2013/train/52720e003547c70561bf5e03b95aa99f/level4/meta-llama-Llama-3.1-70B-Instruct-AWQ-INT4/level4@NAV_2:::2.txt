This paper presents a large scale distributed framework for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact alternating direction method of multiplier (ADMM) algorithm for CLIME, and establish rates of convergence for both the objective and optimality conditions. The algorithm is implemented using both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, and is shown to scale to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, using hundreds of cores.

The paper is technically sound, and the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard. The solution of each subproblem can be computed componentwise, and the most expensive operation is matrix-matrix multiplication, which can be parallelized ideally for CLIME. The numerical experiments demonstrate the scalability of the algorithm in both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

The paper is well-organized, and the evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting. Solving large scale sparse precision estimation problems is challenging, and a simple yet robust distributed algorithm is helpful.

However, the originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as the derivation of inexact ADMM is standard, and the good scalability is due to the simple structure of CLIME. Additionally, the setup of the numerical experiments could be further specified, such as the accuracy achieved by each solver.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-organized solution to a challenging problem.
* The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting and helpful.
* The algorithm is shown to scale to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, using hundreds of cores.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as the derivation of inexact ADMM is standard.
* The good scalability is due to the simple structure of CLIME, rather than any novel contribution.
* The setup of the numerical experiments could be further specified.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but encourage the authors to further specify the setup of the numerical experiments, and to highlight any novel contributions beyond the standard derivation of inexact ADMM.
