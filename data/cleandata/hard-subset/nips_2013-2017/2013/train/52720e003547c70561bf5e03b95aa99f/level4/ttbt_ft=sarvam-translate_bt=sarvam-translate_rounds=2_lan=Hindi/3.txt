**Abstract of the Paper**

This paper is related to the solution of covariance selection problems on a very large scale (up to millions of features and trillions of parameters). The considered estimator is CLIME, which is known to provide an estimator of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be solved column by column, thus being more easily adapted to distributed computations. Theoretical analysis is also claimed to be easier than graphical-based methods. The authors have developed a new algorithm to fit CLIME by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise with the wrong direction of the coefficients. Theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm are reported, both in terms of the objective function and the distance to optimality. Special attention is paid to special matrix structures (thin and low rank) with the computation to reduce the computational burden. The algorithm has been implemented within a scalable parallel computing framework for both shared and distributed memory systems. In numerical studies, it is compared to its direct competitors in terms of runtime on both synthetic and real data. A detailed study related to this new algorithm is also provided to evaluate the speed with respect to the block size and the number of cores on both shared and distributed memory systems.

**Notes**

This paper is well-written and very clear. The novelty and contribution brought by the method are clearly stated in the introduction, and the relationship with existing works is clearly established, with a well-referenced bibliography. The algorithm is presented beautifully with a good balance between the technical points of numerical analysis/algebra and the theoretical guarantee for convergence rates. Numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation of the algorithm shows very impressive performance.

My only concern is the availability of such a method: if the source code (mostly based on open-source libraries) is not made available to the community, then there is no point in such work.

A solid algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection when the dimension is very large (up to millions of variables). Such a powerful tool should be rewarded by publication if it is made available to the community.
