This paper presents CLIME-ADMM, a scalable algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation based on the CLIME method. The core contribution is a novel block-wise estimation strategy, where columns of the precision matrix are computed in blocks rather than individually. This approach is implemented in a distributed framework using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK. The authors provide an empirical analysis comparing the performance of CLIME-ADMM against several state-of-the-art techniques, namely DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare.

While the proposed method for scaling the CLIME estimator is interesting, the paper could be significantly improved in several areas concerning clarity, justification, and framing.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarity of Notation:** The presentation suffers from scattered notation, with definitions spread across Sections 1, 2, and 3. This makes the methodology difficult to follow. For instance, the parameters `\rho` and `\eta` do not appear to be formally defined in the text. To enhance readability and comprehension, I strongly suggest consolidating all key notations into a single table.

2.  **Justification for Block Cyclic Data Distribution:** The paper introduces a block cyclic data distribution scheme, claiming it achieves load balancing and scalability. However, the intuition for why this specific scheme is effective is not obvious from the description provided. The paper would be substantially strengthened by including a more detailed explanation or theoretical justification for this choice. Furthermore, this claim is not supported by empirical evidence; the experiments do not include results that specifically validate the load balancing and scalability advantages of this distribution method.

3.  **Guidance on Parameter Selection:** The choice of the column block size, `k`, appears to be ad-hoc. Is there any theoretical guidance or practical intuition to guide the selection of an optimal `k`? A discussion on the algorithm's sensitivity to this parameter, perhaps accompanied by an ablation study in the experiments, would be a valuable addition and would improve the paper's practical utility.

4.  **"Distributed" vs. "Parallel" Framework:** A more general concern is the framing of the algorithm as "distributed." The paper does not discuss key mechanisms inherent to distributed systems, such as message passing protocols, communication overhead, or fault tolerance. The description is more suggestive of a parallel implementation designed for a tightly-coupled cluster environment. The authors should either clarify the distributed aspects of their algorithm by detailing the communication model or consider reframing their contribution as a large-scale parallel algorithm.

**Minor Comments:**

The following typographical errors should be corrected:

*   **Section 1:** "where \lambda is a tunning parameter" -> "where \lambda is a **tuning** parameter"
*   **Section 2:** "CLIME is summerized in" -> "CLIME is **summarized** in"
*   **Section 5.1:** "As Tiger is parameter tunning free" -> "As Tiger is parameter **tuning** free"
