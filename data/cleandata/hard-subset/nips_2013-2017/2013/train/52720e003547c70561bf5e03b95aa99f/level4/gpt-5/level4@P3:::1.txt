Summary
This paper addresses large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. The authors derive an inexact (linearized) ADMM algorithm for CLIME with O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective suboptimality and feasibility residuals, under η ≥ ρλmax(C)2. The algorithm exploits the separable structure of CLIME to work on column blocks (k columns at a time) rather than a single column, enabling the use of matrix–matrix multiplications, better cache behavior, and parallelism across cores. They further show how to leverage problem structure: (i) iterate- and covariance-sparsity to reduce multiplications, and (ii) low-rank factorizations C = AAT to reduce per-iteration cost from O(p2k) to O(npk). A distributed framework based on block cyclic data distribution is proposed for both shared-memory (OpenMP) and distributed-memory (OpenMPI + ScaLAPACK) environments. Empirically, the method outperforms or scales better than CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC in several regimes and demonstrates near-linear speedups, including a 1M-dimensional run (≈11 hours on 400 cores).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on CLIME’s column-wise decomposability and recent ADMM-based solvers (e.g., Boyd et al.; Wang & Banerjee). Compared to interior-point or per-column ADMM, the column-block design emphasizes matrix–matrix kernels and distribution. The block-cyclic strategy follows established HPC practices (ScaLAPACK; blocked BLAS) for load balancing and cache locality. The distributed optimization literature cited (e.g., GPBS, parameter servers, GraphLab) typically targets unconstrained or differently structured problems; the paper positions its contribution as tailored to CLIME’s global constraints.

Quality
- Algorithmic correctness: The linearized ADMM updates and closed-form proximal steps are standard; the O(1/T) rates align with known results for inexact ADMM under appropriate step sizes. However, the paper relies on supplemental proofs; a concise sketch in the main text would improve trust.
- Assumptions/parameters: The requirement η ≥ ρλmax(C)2 is reasonable but not operationalized. Guidance on choosing (ρ, η) and sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Statistical aspects: Theorem 3 motivates thresholding/perturbing C to induce sparsity while retaining deviation bounds. The conditions (sub-Gaussian Ri, sub-exponential ∆ij) are plausible, but the practical procedure (how thresholds are set; impact on estimation error) should be clarified and empirically validated.
- Implementation/engineering: The parallelization choices are sensible, but the paper does not detail communication patterns, collectives, or overlap of communication/computation per ADMM step. A breakdown of time per kernel and per-iteration communication costs would strengthen the case.
- Experiments: The scalability results are compelling. Accuracy is evaluated via precision/recall on synthetic graphs, but additional metrics (e.g., spectral/Frobenius error, held-out likelihood) and more careful fairness controls (matching λ or error, not just sparsity) would help. Stopping criteria and tolerances are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but notation and definitions are scattered. A consolidated notation table would help, especially for ρ, η, soft, box, and the various matrices (A, C, X, Z, Y, U, V). Some figures/tables are referenced but not fully described; a self-contained caption and explicit reporting of q, k, block sizes, and stopping criteria would aid reproducibility. There are several typos and incomplete references (see Minor Comments).

Originality
The combination of (i) inexact ADMM tailored to CLIME, (ii) column-block updates that emphasize matrix–matrix multiplications, and (iii) a scalable block-cyclic distributed implementation is a useful and, in aggregate, novel systems contribution for this problem. The theoretical convergence rate is standard for linearized ADMM, but its specialization to CLIME and the structural exploitation (low-rank/sparsity) are well-motivated.

Significance
Scaling CLIME to p ≈ 10^6 (p^2 ≈ 10^12 parameters) is ambitious and practically relevant for modern high-dimensional applications. If the implementation details and parameter selection strategies are clarified and code is made available, the work could have meaningful impact for practitioners needing global-constraint estimators beyond GLasso variants.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Choice of column block size k: Provide theory- or model-based guidance (e.g., roofline/cost model that trades O(npk) compute vs. memory/communication), or an auto-tuning procedure based on cache sizes and interconnect bandwidth. Explain the dependence of optimal k on n, p, q, and block sizes (pb × nb × kb).
- Distributed details: Specify the communication primitives (e.g., SUMMA-style PDGEMM for A(ATX), collectives used, synchronization points per ADMM iteration). Include a time breakdown (compute vs. comm) and scalability limits.
- Block-cyclic distribution: Add an ablation comparing contiguous vs. block-cyclic layouts and report load-balance metrics (e.g., per-process flops/idle time) to substantiate the claim.
- Positive definiteness and symmetrization: CLIME solutions are not necessarily PD. Describe the post-processing (symmetrization and PD projection, if any) used and whether this affects timing/accuracy.
- Parameter choices: Report how λ, ρ, and η are selected; provide sensitivity plots. Clarify stopping criteria, tolerances, and iteration counts.
- Accuracy metrics: In addition to precision/recall, report estimation error (e.g., ∥Ω̂−Ω0∥F, operator norm), and for real data, likelihood or cross-validated predictive performance.
- Perturbation/thresholding of C: Describe practical thresholds, how often sparsification is applied, and its empirical effect on runtime and accuracy.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important scaling bottleneck in sparse precision estimation with clear engineering advances.
- Demonstrates impressive scalability on both shared- and distributed-memory systems, including million-dimensional runs.
- Provides convergence guarantees and practical structural speedups (low-rank A, iterate sparsity).
- The distributed framework and blocking strategy are broadly applicable to other constrained problems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient detail on communication/memory behavior and lack of ablation to justify block-cyclic superiority.
- Limited guidance on critical hyperparameters (ρ, η, k) and stopping criteria; no auto-tuning.
- Evaluation emphasizes runtime/scalability more than statistical accuracy; comparisons could be more controlled.
- Notation and presentation issues, plus several typos and incomplete references.

Minor comments
- Define ρ and η when first introduced; consider a notation summary table.
- Fix typos: “tunning” → “tuning” (multiple instances), “summerized” → “summarized,” “Scalapak” → “ScaLAPACK,” “Zeon” → “Xeon,” “compution/achetectures” etc.
- Clarify Algorithm 1: define soft and box where they first appear; ensure consistent use of E vs. I; check dimensions in V-updates; unify “low-rank” vs. “sparse” branches.
- Complete and standardize references (years, venues, titles), and remove duplicates (Friedman et al. appears twice with different titles).
- Please report memory usage per process and whether C is ever formed explicitly; emphasize the A-based implementation path.

Overall, this is a strong systems paper with solid potential impact. With improved clarity, parameter guidance, and more detailed distributed-system characterization, it would be a compelling addition to the NIPS program.
