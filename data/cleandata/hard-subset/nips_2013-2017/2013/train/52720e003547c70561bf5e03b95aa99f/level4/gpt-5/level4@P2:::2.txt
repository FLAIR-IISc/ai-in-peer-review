Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. It formulates CLIME as a set of LPs and proposes an inexact ADMM algorithm tailored to a column-block decomposition. Each ADMM iteration consists of elementwise proximal steps (soft-thresholding and box projection) and matrix multiplications; the authors exploit both sparsity and the low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = AA′, with n ≪ p) to reduce matmul cost from O(p2k) to O(npk). They analyze the algorithm and establish O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective suboptimality and feasibility residuals. A distributed framework based on block-cyclic data distribution is developed for both shared-memory and distributed-memory settings, emphasizing load balance and cache efficiency. Experiments compare against DC-QUIC, TIGER, and CLIME-Flare, and demonstrate near-linear speedups and the ability to handle p ≈ 10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) on hundreds of cores.

This work builds on prior advances in sparse precision estimation (e.g., CLIME, graphical lasso, QUIC, DC-QUIC) and distributed optimization via ADMM. The inexact/linearized ADMM update and the O(1/T) rate are consistent with known results on ADMM variants (e.g., Boyd et al.; He and Yuan), while the engineering focus here is on making CLIME scale via column-blocking and parallel matmuls with block-cyclic distribution.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic formulation is correct, the linearized (inexact) ADMM updates are standard for handling coupled quadratic terms, and the convergence guarantees (non-ergodic O(1/T) rates for objective and residuals) match established theory for inexact ADMM under appropriate parameter choices.
- Empirics: The implementation demonstrates strong scalability on both architectures, and the comparisons include representative baselines (TIGER, CLIME-Flare, DC-QUIC).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The algorithm steps and the distributed framework are described clearly, with attention to computational bottlenecks and data layout (block-cyclic distribution).

Originality:
- The derivation of the inexact ADMM updates for CLIME is largely standard. The main novelty lies in the scalable design: column-block ADMM with efficient matrix multiplications leveraging low rank, and a practical, load-balanced block-cyclic distribution enabling near-linear scaling to hundreds of nodes.

Significance:
- Scaling sparse precision estimation to millions of dimensions is impactful. A simple, robust distributed algorithm for CLIME that achieves near-linear speedups is valuable to practitioners handling ultra-high-dimensional problems.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of the computational bottleneck (matmul) and an effective strategy to exploit low-rank structure (C = AA′).
- Practical distributed design with block-cyclic distribution and careful consideration of cache and load balance.
- Strong scalability results, including a challenging p = 10^6 case.
- Theoretical guarantees for the proposed inexact ADMM variant (O(1/T) for objective and residuals).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Novelty of the core optimization method is limited; the linearized ADMM derivation is standard (see, e.g., He and Yuan, 2012; Boyd et al.). Emphasizing what is technically new beyond system design would help.
- Experimental setup needs more specificity:
  - Report stopping criteria, primal/dual residual tolerances, and how feasibility of the infinity-norm constraints is enforced at termination.
  - Provide accuracy metrics across solvers (e.g., constraint violation, objective value, and, when possible, statistical error relative to ground truth) to complement runtime.
  - Clarify parameter selection for λ across methods; note that TIGER is tuning-insensitive, whereas CLIME typically requires λ choice.
- Post-processing details: CLIME does not enforce positive definiteness; describe any symmetrization/PD correction used for downstream evaluation, and its cost.
- Sensitivity: Discuss how performance depends on column-block size k, sparsity patterns, and sample size n, and provide guidance for choosing k in practice.
- Memory footprint: Quantify memory usage per node (A, intermediates, and column-block variables) and the largest problem that fits at given resources.
- Comparisons: Consider including graphical lasso/QUIC without block-diagonal pre-thresholding to assess trade-offs when C is not readily block-diagonal.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important scalability gap in sparse precision estimation with a clean, well-engineered distributed solution.
- Demonstrates convincing empirical scalability on large problems and modern hardware.
- Provides convergence analysis and practical system design insights (block-cyclic distribution, low-rank matmul).

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited methodological novelty beyond adapting known inexact ADMM techniques and established parallel matmul strategies.
- Empirical evaluation could better quantify statistical and optimization accuracy and detail stopping conditions.
- Some practical aspects (PD enforcement, parameter selection, sensitivity) are under-documented.

Minor comments:
- The inexact ADMM formulation and rate claims should be positioned with clearer references (e.g., He and Yuan, 2012; Boyd et al., Foundations and Trends in ML).
- Since each subproblem decouples componentwise after linearization, highlight that elementwise proximal steps dominate lightly, with matmul as the critical, well-parallelized kernel.
- Please report, for each solver, the achieved residuals and final objective, not just runtime.
