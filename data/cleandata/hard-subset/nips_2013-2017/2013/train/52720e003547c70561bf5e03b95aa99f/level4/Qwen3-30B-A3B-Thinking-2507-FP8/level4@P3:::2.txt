

This paper presents a distributed framework for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator, addressing the challenge of scaling to millions of dimensions. The authors propose an inexact ADMM variant that solves CLIME in column-blocks with a block cyclic distribution strategy, enabling efficient parallel computation on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The paper establishes O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions, and demonstrates nearly linear scalability with the number of cores.

The work builds upon CLIME (Cai et al., 2011), which provides strong statistical guarantees for sparse precision estimation, and ADMM (Boyd et al., 2011), a well-established optimization framework. Previous efforts like DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al., 2013) and Tiger (Liu & Wang, 2010) have addressed precision matrix estimation but lack the scalability to handle ultra-high-dimensional problems (e.g., millions of dimensions). The authors position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms to "Big Data," distinguishing their approach from parameter server frameworks (Dean et al., 2012) that are unsuitable for constrained optimization problems like CLIME.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis is rigorous, and the algorithmic development correctly handles the CLIME problem structure. The authors properly address the challenges of distributed computation for high-dimensional problems, including load balancing and cache optimization through block cyclic distribution. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against state-of-the-art methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) on both synthetic and real datasets. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME follows standard procedures (as noted in the draft review), and the theoretical results are extensions of existing convergence analysis techniques rather than novel contributions to optimization theory. The experimental setup could be strengthened by specifying the final accuracy achieved by each solver, particularly for the large-scale experiments.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental results are presented effectively through well-labeled figures and tables. Minor improvements could be made to clarify the relationship between the theoretical convergence rates and practical performance, and to better explain the choice of block sizes for different architectures.

**Originality**: The paper's main contribution lies in the distributed framework for CLIME that enables scaling to millions of dimensions, rather than in the core optimization technique itself. The column-block approach with block cyclic distribution represents a valuable practical innovation that significantly improves cache efficiency and load balancing compared to previous distributed methods like GPBS (Parikh & Boyd, 2014). While the inexact ADMM formulation for CLIME is standard, the authors effectively adapt it to a distributed setting with appropriate parallelization strategies. The work demonstrates how the inherent structure of CLIME (separability into column-wise problems) can be leveraged for efficient parallelization, which is distinct from previous distributed approaches for graphical models.

**Significance**: Solving precision matrix estimation at million-dimensional scale represents a significant practical challenge with important applications in genomics, climate science, and other high-dimensional domains. The authors' framework demonstrates the ability to estimate a one-million-dimensional precision matrix (one trillion parameters) in approximately 11 hours on 400 cores, substantially outperforming existing methods. The almost-linear scalability with the number of cores makes this approach particularly valuable for practitioners dealing with ultra-high-dimensional data. The distributed framework is general enough to be applicable to other large-scale constrained optimization problems, enhancing its broader significance.

**Strengths**: 
- Demonstrates practical scalability to unprecedented problem sizes (1 million dimensions)
- Provides rigorous convergence analysis with O(1/T) rates
- Offers both shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations
- Shows nearly linear speedup with number of cores
- Provides clear empirical comparison with state-of-the-art methods

**Weaknesses**: 
- The core optimization technique (inexact ADMM for CLIME) is not novel but rather a standard application of existing methods
- The experimental section would benefit from more specific details about solver accuracy and convergence criteria
- The theoretical contribution is modest compared to the practical implementation advances

The paper makes a strong practical contribution to the field of large-scale statistical learning. While the optimization technique itself is not novel, the authors' systematic approach to distributed implementation, combined with thorough experimental validation, provides a valuable tool for practitioners working with ultra-high-dimensional data. The framework is particularly well-suited for applications where precision matrix estimation is needed at massive scales, such as in genomics or climate modeling.

This work is well-aligned with NIPS' focus on high-impact contributions to machine learning and statistics. It advances the state of the art in scalable statistical estimation by providing a practical solution to a problem that has remained challenging due to the quadratic growth in parameters with dimensionality. The paper's strengths in practical scalability and thorough experimental validation outweigh its modest theoretical contributions, making it a valuable addition to the literature on large-scale machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
