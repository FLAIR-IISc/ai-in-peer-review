

This paper proposes a scalable distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME (Constrained L1 Minimization for Inverse Matrix Estimation) estimator. The authors present an inexact ADMM variant that processes the precision matrix in column blocks rather than column-by-column, along with a distributed framework designed for high-dimensional settings. The approach leverages the inherent separability of CLIME's optimization problem while addressing computational and memory challenges in ultra-high-dimensional settings (e.g., 1 million dimensions with 1 trillion parameters). The paper establishes convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions, and demonstrates substantial scalability improvements over existing methods like DC-QUIC, Tiger, and flare.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a truly distributed solution for precision matrix estimation at the million-dimensional scale, where previous methods fail due to memory constraints. The column-block approach combined with block cyclic data distribution effectively balances computational load across processors while minimizing cache misses—a critical consideration for large-scale distributed systems. The convergence rate analysis (O(1/T)) for both objective and constraint violations is theoretically sound and aligns with recent advances in inexact ADMM methods. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating linear scalability with the number of cores on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, and showing significant speedups over state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real datasets.

The paper's clarity is generally strong, though the notation could be better organized. The authors define key symbols (ρ, η, etc.) in the text, but these appear scattered across Sections 1-3, making it difficult for readers to quickly reference them. A single notation table would greatly improve readability. The description of the block cyclic distribution scheme is conceptually interesting but lacks sufficient explanation of why this particular distribution achieves better load balancing than alternatives. The authors claim it "minimizes cache misses" but provide no empirical evidence or theoretical justification for this claim. The column block size k appears to be chosen ad hoc (k=100 for experiments), with no guidance on how to select this parameter for different problem settings. While the paper mentions that k depends on "process grid size, block size in matrix multiplication, cache size, and sparsity pattern," this lacks concrete theoretical or empirical support.

The paper's technical quality is strong, with sound theoretical analysis and well-executed experiments. The implementation details (using OpenMP/OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK) are appropriate for the problem domain, and the results convincingly demonstrate scalability. The significance is high, as large-scale precision matrix estimation is increasingly important in genomics, climate science, and other fields dealing with high-dimensional data. The paper effectively positions itself within the broader context of distributed machine learning, distinguishing its approach from related work on parameter servers and graph projection block splitting.

However, the paper's claim of being "distributed" is somewhat misleading. The framework relies on a block cyclic distribution scheme, but the authors provide no details about communication patterns, message passing, or synchronization between processes. The description focuses primarily on data partitioning rather than communication protocols, which is more characteristic of a parallel implementation than a distributed one. This ambiguity is a notable weakness, particularly for a venue like NIPS that values clear technical contributions.

The paper's strengths include:
- Demonstrating feasibility of precision matrix estimation at unprecedented scales (1 million dimensions)
- Providing convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm
- Showing substantial scalability improvements over existing methods
- Offering practical implementation details for large-scale distributed systems

The paper's weaknesses include:
- Lack of clear theoretical guidance for choosing column block size k
- Insufficient explanation of why block cyclic distribution achieves better load balancing
- Ambiguous claims about the algorithm being "distributed" rather than "parallel"
- Scattered notation that hinders readability
- Limited discussion of how the column-block approach affects statistical properties compared to column-by-column processing

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of large-scale statistical estimation. While the "distributed" aspect could be clarified, the core algorithmic innovations and scalability results are compelling. The work advances the state of the art in precision matrix estimation by addressing a critical scalability barrier that has limited previous methods. The experimental results are thorough and well-justified, showing clear advantages over existing approaches in high-dimensional settings.

For acceptance, I recommend the paper be accepted with the following considerations: the authors should clarify the "distributed" nature of their framework by adding details about communication patterns and synchronization, provide theoretical justification for the column block size parameter, and include a notation table to improve readability. These minor revisions would substantially strengthen an already strong contribution to the field. The paper's significance, technical quality, and empirical results strongly support acceptance.
