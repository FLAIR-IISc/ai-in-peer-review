Summary of the paper
The paper tackles large-scale covariance selection with up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. It focuses on the CLIME estimator for Gaussian precision matrices, which can be solved columnwise and is therefore well suited to distributed computation and often simpler to analyze than the Graphical Lasso. The authors propose a new algorithm that solves CLIME blockwise rather than columnwise, coupled with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers. They provide convergence guarantees in terms of both objective value and optimality gap. The method exploits structural properties—sparsity and low rank—throughout the computation to reduce cost. The algorithm is implemented in a scalable parallel framework for both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems. Empirical studies compare runtime against strong baselines on synthetic and real datasets. The paper also offers a thorough ablation of speedups as a function of block size and core count in both shared and distributed settings.

Comments
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The contributions and novelty are explicitly stated, and the connections to prior work are carefully drawn with sound bibliographic support. The algorithmic presentation strikes a good balance between numerical linear algebra details and theoretical convergence guarantees, and the empirical evaluation is appropriate and compelling, demonstrating impressive performance and scalability.

My main concern is software availability. Given the strong emphasis on scalability and the reliance on open-source libraries, the practical impact of this work will be substantially limited if the source code is not released to the community.

Overall, this is a strong algorithmic paper addressing an important practical problem: covariance selection at truly massive scales. It merits publication, provided the implementation is made available to facilitate reproducibility and adoption.
