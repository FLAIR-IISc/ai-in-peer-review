This work presents an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) to solve the linear programming problem associated with the CLIME estimator. The authors establish a theoretical convergence rate of O(1/T). The proposed algorithm is implemented and tested on both shared-memory and distributed-memory parallel architectures, with its performance compared against existing methods including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality:** The technical contributions of this work are solid.

**Minor Comments:**
1) The application of inexact ADMM to the CLIME problem follows a well-established procedure, as seen in prior literature (e.g., http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm).
2) The ADMM algorithm is particularly well-suited for parallelization in the context of CLIME. This is because the problem's structure allows for componentwise computation of subproblem solutions, with the primary computational bottleneck being matrix-matrix multiplication—an operation known to be highly parallelizable.
3) The description of the numerical experiments could be improved by providing more specific details. For instance, the authors should clarify the stopping criteria or the level of accuracy achieved by each of the compared solvers.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is clearly written and well-structured.

**Originality:** The primary novelty of this work lies in its empirical evaluation of the ADMM algorithm across both shared-memory and distributed-memory parallel architectures.

**Significance:** This paper addresses the significant challenge of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. A simple and robust distributed algorithm is a helpful contribution, and the evaluation on different parallel systems is valuable. However, the overall contribution is tempered by the fact that the derivation of inexact ADMM is standard, and the observed scalability is largely an expected outcome given the highly parallelizable structure of the CLIME problem.
