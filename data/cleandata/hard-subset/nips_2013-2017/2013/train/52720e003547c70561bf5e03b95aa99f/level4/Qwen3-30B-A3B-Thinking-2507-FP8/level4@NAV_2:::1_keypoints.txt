- Presents a scalable algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM variant and a distributed framework with linear scalability in cores.
- Extends prior CLIME and distributed optimization work to ultra-large-scale problems (millions of dimensions, trillions of parameters) via column-block processing and block cyclic data distribution.
- Proves rigorous O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions.
- Empirical results show substantial scalability and performance improvements over DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare on synthetic and real data.
- Column-block approach is a meaningful and novel extension of prior column-by-column CLIME solvers.
- Implementation using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK is well-documented and practically useful.
- Framework effectively addresses the quadratic parameter scaling problem (p² parameters for p dimensions).
- Notation is scattered and some symbols (e.g., ρ and η) are not clearly defined, hurting readability.
- Block cyclic data distribution is presented without theoretical justification for its load-balancing properties.
- Choice of column block size k appears ad hoc with no theoretical guidance or systematic analysis.
- The algorithm is more accurately described as a parallel implementation rather than a distributed algorithm due to lack of discussion on message passing and communication protocols.
- Several minor grammatical errors (e.g., "tunning", "summerized") reduce polish.
- Overall technically sound with solid theoretical analysis and convincing experimental validation (1M dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores).
- Clarity would improve with a dedicated notation table and clearer explanation of block cyclic distribution.
- Originality: column-block CLIME is a novel contribution differentiating it from prior approaches.
- Significance: enables precision matrix estimation at previously intractable scales with clear applied relevance.
- Pro arguments: strong theory, impressive scalability, clear column-block presentation, thorough experiments, and practical implementation details.
- Con arguments: lack of theoretical justification for key design choices, scattered notation, minor grammatical issues, and terminology ambiguity between "distributed" and "parallel."
