Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator, which casts the problem as a linear program with an infinity-norm constraint. The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm tailored to CLIME that updates column blocks of the precision matrix at a time. Each iteration consists of elementwise soft-thresholding and box projection, together with matrix multiplications. They prove an O(1/T) convergence rate for both the objective suboptimality and the optimality residuals under a standard condition on the proximal parameter. On the systems side, they develop shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations that exploit (i) dynamic sparsity of iterates, (ii) block-cyclic data distribution for load balance, and (iii) the low-rank structure of the sample covariance C = A A^T to replace O(p^2 k) multiplications by O(n p k) when n ≪ p. Experiments on synthetic and real data show near-linear speedups and the ability to handle up to p = 10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) in roughly 11 hours on 400 cores. Comparisons with DC-QUIC, TIGER, and CLIME in the flare package suggest that the proposed approach is substantially more scalable, particularly at higher dimensionalities and for higher sparsity targets.

This work builds on established literature: CLIME’s statistical guarantees [Cai et al.] and columnwise decomposability; prior solvers using interior-point methods and ADMM; inexact/linearized ADMM analyses and non-ergodic O(1/T) rates (e.g., He and Yuan); and systems work on parallel matrix multiplication and block-cyclic distribution (e.g., ScaLAPACK). The proposed contribution is largely an engineering/design advance—adapting inexact ADMM to a column-blocked CLIME solver and making it scale in distributed environments—rather than introducing a novel optimization method.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound. The inexact ADMM updates are derived in a standard way by linearizing the quadratic term and adding a proximal term, yielding closed-form updates for X (soft-thresholding) and Z (box projection). The convergence analysis is consistent with known non-ergodic O(1/T) rates under appropriate parameter choices (η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2). The systems design choices (block-cyclic distribution, exploiting low-rank structure) are sensible and well-motivated.

However, several experimental details need clarification to fully assess solution quality:
- What stopping criteria and tolerances are used for all methods? Please report primal/dual residuals, KKT violations, or objective gaps, so runtime comparisons reflect comparable accuracy.
- How are λ and other tuning parameters selected for each method, especially on real data where ground truth is unknown? For synthetic data, ensuring matched sparsity is reasonable; still, reporting solution accuracy (e.g., constraint violation, objective value, or estimation error) would help.
- For DC-QUIC, clarifying the thresholding that induces block structure and its impact on accuracy would strengthen the comparison.
- A breakdown of time into matrix multiplication vs elementwise operations, and communication costs on the cluster, would illustrate bottlenecks and portability.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The algorithmic steps are clear, and the rationale for column-block updates and block-cyclic distribution is cogent. It would help to:
- Explicitly state the stopping conditions and how accuracy is matched across methods in all experiments.
- Provide more details on data distribution and block sizes (pb × nb × kb), and how these are selected in practice.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “Comparision,” “acheived,” “reasonble”) and ensure consistent notation (e.g., E vs I in box projection).

Originality:
The derivation of the inexact ADMM scheme for CLIME is standard (see, e.g., inexact/linearized ADMM literature such as He and Yuan; the link http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm contains relevant references). The main novelty lies in the scalable implementation: column-block updates that convert repeated matrix-vector products into matrix-matrix multiplications, exploitation of low-rank structure of C, and a block-cyclic distributed framework with good load balance. This is a useful and nontrivial engineering contribution for ultra-high-dimensional settings.

Significance:
Scaling sparse precision estimation to millions of variables is important. A simple, robust distributed algorithm that achieves near-linear speedups is valuable to practitioners. The ability to leverage both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures broadens impact. The empirical results are compelling; releasing code would further enhance significance.

Pros:
- Addresses a challenging and timely large-scale problem with clear practical impact.
- Solid, standard convergence guarantees with an O(1/T) rate.
- Thoughtful systems design (column blocks, block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplications) leading to strong scalability.
- Comprehensive experiments across architectures, with results at unprecedented scales.

Cons:
- Algorithmic novelty is limited; the inexact ADMM derivation is standard for this problem class, and much of the theory is known.
- Experimental methodology under-reports accuracy/optimality metrics, making cross-method fairness harder to assess.
- Limited discussion/analysis of communication costs and memory footprint at scale.
- Some presentation issues (typos, minor notation inconsistencies).

Minor comments:
1) The inexact/linearized ADMM derivation is standard; see, e.g., He and Yuan and related references (the authors might add more precise citations rather than a general webpage link).
2) Given CLIME’s structure, subproblem updates are componentwise, and the main cost is matrix-matrix multiplication, which parallelizes well; this underlies the strong scaling observed.
3) Please specify the achieved accuracy of each solver (residuals, constraint violation, and objective values) to ensure comparable stopping across methods.

Overall assessment:
A technically sound paper with moderate methodological novelty but strong systems and scalability contributions to an important problem. With improved reporting of accuracy and a clearer comparison protocol, this would be a solid contribution.
