This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multiplier (ADMM) for solving the linear program problem in the CLIME estimator, which is used for large scale distributed sparse precision estimation. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and implement the algorithm using both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The paper provides numerical comparisons with other methods, such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard and can be found in existing literature (e.g., http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm). Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the numerical experiments, such as the accuracy achieved by each solver.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm and its implementation. However, some minor improvements could be made to enhance the clarity of the paper. For example, the authors could provide more details on the setup of the numerical experiments and the accuracy achieved by each solver.

Originality: The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting and contributes to the existing literature on large scale sparse precision estimation. However, the derivation of inexact ADMM is standard, and the good scalability is largely due to the simple structure of CLIME.

Significance: Solving large scale sparse precision estimation problems is challenging, and a simple yet robust distributed algorithm is helpful. The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting and provides insights into the scalability of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel application of ADMM to large scale sparse precision estimation.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm and establish a convergence rate of O(1/T).
* The paper provides numerical comparisons with other methods, such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.
* The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting and contributes to the existing literature.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The derivation of inexact ADMM is standard and can be found in existing literature.
* The paper could provide more details on the numerical experiments, such as the accuracy achieved by each solver.
* The good scalability of the proposed algorithm is largely due to the simple structure of CLIME.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm. While the derivation of inexact ADMM is standard, the evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting and contributes to the existing literature. Therefore, I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions.
