Summary
The paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear programs arising in the CLIME estimator for sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors establish an O(1/T) convergence rate, implement the method on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, and provide numerical comparisons with DC-QUIC, TIGER, and CLIME-Flare.

Quality
The paper is technically sound. The derivation follows standard inexact ADMM methodology and is correctly specialized to the CLIME structure. The empirical study is useful and the system-level implementation appears competent.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus (CLIME) with an algorithm well matched to the separable structure.
- Theoretical guarantee with an O(1/T) convergence rate.
- Practical emphasis on parallel and distributed implementations, addressing real scalability concerns.
- Broad comparisons with established baselines (DC-QUIC, TIGER, CLIME-Flare).

Weaknesses and concerns
- The inexact ADMM derivation, while appropriate, is standard; the novelty lies more in the implementation and evaluation than in algorithmic development.
- Some experimental details are missing, limiting reproducibility and interpretability (e.g., precise stopping criteria and accuracy targets for each solver).
- It would help to clarify whether the O(1/T) rate is ergodic or non-ergodic, and in terms of which residuals/optimality measures.
- Given CLIME’s columnwise separability, the main computational bottleneck is matrix–matrix multiplication; this makes strong scaling somewhat expected. The paper could better articulate what is new about the parallelization beyond leveraging BLAS-friendly kernels and data partitioning.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and generally easy to follow. Explanations of the algorithm, implementation, and experiments are presented clearly, though several experimental details should be specified (see below).

Originality
While the derivation of inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard (see, e.g., the inexact ADMM literature such as the references available at http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm), the evaluation of ADMM on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures for this problem is interesting and practically relevant. The contribution is primarily empirical and systems-oriented rather than algorithmically novel.

Significance
Large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation is important in many applications. A simple, robust, and scalable algorithm is valuable. Demonstrating good scalability on shared and distributed platforms is useful for practitioners. The observed scalability likely benefits from the simplicity and separability of CLIME and the heavy use of matrix–matrix operations, which parallelize well.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theoretical clarification:
  - Specify whether the O(1/T) convergence rate is ergodic (averaged iterates) or non-ergodic, and state the exact metric (e.g., primal/dual residuals, feasibility gap, objective error).
  - Detail how inexactness is controlled (e.g., bounds on inner solves, relation to penalty parameter) and whether penalties or relaxation parameters are adapted.
- Experimental setup and reporting:
  - Report stopping tolerances and accuracy criteria for all solvers (e.g., primal/dual residual thresholds, relative optimality gaps), and ensure they are matched across methods.
  - Provide both accuracy vs. time and accuracy vs. iterations to disentangle algorithmic efficiency from implementation speed.
  - Describe data generation and problem settings fully: dimensions, sparsity levels, condition numbers, regularization parameters (and how they are selected), and any preprocessing.
  - Clarify hardware and software settings: number of threads, BLAS/LAPACK/MKL versions, compiler flags, network topology and bandwidth, and whether competitors use multithreading or GPU acceleration.
  - Include strong and weak scaling plots for both shared and distributed settings, and break down time into computation vs. communication where possible.
  - Report memory usage and, for distributed runs, communication volume per iteration to justify scalability claims.
  - Discuss hyperparameter choices for baselines (e.g., path length, warm starts, screening rules), ensuring fairness. If baselines can exploit multiple cores/nodes, enable and report this.
- Methodological details:
  - Explain how the columnwise separability of CLIME is exploited (e.g., block partitioning across processes/threads) and how load balancing is handled.
  - Identify the most expensive kernels (e.g., matrix–matrix multiplications), indicate whether they are precomputed or reused across iterations, and discuss numerical stability.
  - Comment on the effect of penalty parameter selection on convergence speed and sensitivity.
- Contextualization:
  - Acknowledge prior inexact ADMM results more explicitly and position the contribution as a careful systems evaluation tailored to CLIME’s structure.

Minor comments
- The derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard; see, for example, the inexact ADMM references at http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm.
- Because CLIME’s subproblems decouple componentwise, the main cost is in matrix–matrix multiplications, which parallelize well; this should be emphasized when interpreting scalability.
- Please specify the achieved accuracy and stopping conditions for each solver in the experiments.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed systems paper applying inexact ADMM to CLIME with credible convergence guarantees and useful empirical evaluation across shared and distributed settings. The primary novelty lies in the implementation and thoroughness of the evaluation rather than in new algorithmic ideas. With clearer experimental details and stronger analysis of accuracy and scalability (including communication and fairness across baselines), the paper would make a valuable contribution for practitioners working on large-scale sparse precision estimation. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on the clarifications above.
