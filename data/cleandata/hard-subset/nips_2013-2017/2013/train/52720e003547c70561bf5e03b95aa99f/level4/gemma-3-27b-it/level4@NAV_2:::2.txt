This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear program underlying the CLIME estimator, establishing a convergence rate of O(1/T). The algorithm is implemented for both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, and its performance is compared with existing methods including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME follows a standard approach, as seen in, for example, He & Yuan (2010) [http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm]. Given the componentwise nature of the CLIME subproblems and the dominant cost of matrix-matrix multiplication, the suitability of ADMM for parallelization is well-motivated, leveraging the known efficient parallelization of matrix multiplications. The numerical experimental setup could be further clarified by reporting the achieved accuracy for each solver, beyond just runtime comparisons.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the work is well-presented, and the proposed method is logically explained.

The evaluation of ADMM within both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures represents a valuable contribution. While the derivation of the inexact ADMM is standard, and the scalability benefits largely stem from CLIME’s inherent structure, efficiently addressing large-scale sparse precision estimation remains a significant challenge. The development of a relatively simple and robust distributed algorithm is therefore a positive contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics: scalable sparse precision matrix estimation.
*   Presents a technically sound and well-motivated ADMM-based approach.
*   Demonstrates good scalability on both shared- and distributed-memory architectures.
*   Provides empirical comparisons with relevant existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution (the inexact ADMM derivation) is incremental, building directly on established ADMM theory.
*   The scalability achieved is largely attributable to the underlying structure of CLIME and efficient matrix multiplication, rather than novel algorithmic innovations.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks detailed accuracy reporting for the competing methods, making a complete performance assessment difficult.
