Summary of the Paper:

This paper addresses the resolution of covariance selection problems in extremely large-scale setups, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, known for providing an estimate of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be solved column by column, thereby facilitating distributed computations. They claim that theoretical analysis is also easier to conduct compared to the Graphical-Lasso. The authors have developed a novel algorithm to fit the CLIME by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupled with an inexact augmented Lagrangian method of multipliers. They present theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm, expressed in terms of both the objective function and the distance to optimality. The authors pay special attention to exploiting special matrix structures, such as sparsity and low rank, to reduce the computational burden during calculations. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework, suitable for both shared and distributed memory systems. The numerical studies include comparisons of runtime with direct competitors on both synthetic and real data. A detailed analysis of the new algorithm is also provided, evaluating the speedup in relation to block sizes and the number of cores on both shared memory and distributed memory systems.

Comments:

This paper is well-written and very clear. The novelty and contributions of the method are clearly stated in the introduction, and connections to existing works are clearly established, with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is nicely introduced, striking a good balance between technical points of numerical analysis and algebra, as well as theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation of the algorithm demonstrates impressive performance.

My only concern is the availability of this method: if the source code, which is largely based on open-source libraries, is not made available to the community, the impact of this work will be limited. A sound algorithmic paper that addresses the important practical problem of covariance selection in very high-dimensional settings (millions of variables) deserves publication, provided that the implementation is made available to the community.
