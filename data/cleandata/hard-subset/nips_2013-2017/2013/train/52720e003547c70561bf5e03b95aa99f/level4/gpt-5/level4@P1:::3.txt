Summary
This paper addresses large-scale covariance selection, focusing on estimating sparse precision matrices with the CLIME estimator in ultra–high-dimensional regimes (millions of variables, trillions of parameters). The authors develop an inexact ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column blocks rather than column-by-column, yielding closed-form updates for the main steps and relying only on elementwise operations and matrix multiplications. They establish O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective value and the optimality residuals. The method exploits structural properties to reduce cost: sparsity in iterates and sample covariance, and especially low-rank structure (via C = AA′ with n ≪ p) to replace O(p2k) products by O(npk) products. They implement a scalable parallel framework for both shared- and distributed-memory systems, using block-cyclic data distribution for load balance and cache efficiency. Experiments compare runtime against CLIME, TIGER (flare), and DC-QUIC on synthetic and real datasets, and provide a detailed scalability study varying block size and number of cores. Results show strong speedups and near-linear scaling across hundreds of cores, including an instance with p = 10^6 (p^2 = 10^12) solved in roughly 11 hours on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to existing approaches for sparse precision estimation (graphical lasso, QUIC, CLIME, TIGER) and distributed optimization frameworks (ADMM-based methods, block splitting, parameter-server approaches). The choice of CLIME leverages its separable structure and avoids positive-definiteness constraints, which can be computationally burdensome at scale.

Quality
- The algorithmic development is sound. The inexact ADMM linearization of the quadratic term leads to simple, fully parallelizable updates, and the authors state clear complexity considerations for each step.
- Convergence guarantees are provided, including rates for both the objective and the feasibility residuals, with appropriate conditions on the parameters.
- The implementation details are practical and well motivated: column-blocking, low-rank multiplies, and block-cyclic distribution to achieve load balance and cache efficiency.
- The empirical evaluation is appropriate and convincing. Runtime comparisons on synthetic and real data highlight scalability, and the speedup analysis (as a function of block size and core count) is informative for practitioners.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The algorithm is explained crisply, with clean notation and explicit pseudo-code. The discussion of distributed implementation choices is concrete and accessible. The empirical section is easy to follow and includes relevant baselines.

Originality
While ADMM and CLIME themselves are established, the combination of:
- an inexact ADMM tailored to column-block CLIME with closed-form updates,
- an implementation that reduces to matrix multiplies amenable to low-rank and sparsity acceleration,
- and a carefully engineered block-cyclic distributed framework that scales to the million-variable regime,
constitutes a meaningful and novel contribution. The analysis of convergence for this specific inexact scheme is also valuable.

Significance
The work tackles a practically important problem—scalable sparse precision estimation—in a regime that previous methods have struggled to reach. The demonstrated scalability and near-linear speedups make the method attractive for large scientific and industrial datasets, provided it is accessible to the community.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation for choosing CLIME over alternatives.
- Simple, parallel-friendly updates with provable O(1/T) convergence for objective and residuals.
- Thoughtful exploitation of sparsity and low-rank structure.
- Strong engineering: block-cyclic distribution and careful treatment of shared vs. distributed memory.
- Extensive experiments demonstrating substantial scalability and performance gains.

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Availability: If the source code (built largely on open-source libraries) is not released, the impact of the work will be severely limited. For a systems-oriented contribution of this kind, community access to an implementation is critical.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a practical, scalable solution.
- Solid theoretical guarantees combined with compelling empirical performance.
- Clear exposition and useful engineering insights that others can build on.

Arguments against acceptance
- If the implementation is not made available, reproducibility and adoption by the community will be compromised.

Recommendation
A strong algorithmic and systems paper on large-scale sparse precision estimation. It should be accepted provided the authors make the code available to the community.
