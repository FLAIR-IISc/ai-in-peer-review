Summary
This paper addresses high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator and proposes a scalable inexact ADMM variant (CLIME-ADMM) that operates on column blocks rather than single columns. The method is designed so that each iteration consists of elementwise operations and matrix multiplications, enabling efficient parallelization. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective and feasibility residuals under suitable conditions and present a large-scale framework spanning shared-memory and distributed-memory settings. They exploit low-rank structure (via C = A A^T) and discuss leveraging sparsity in iterates and/or the sample covariance. Empirically, they compare against DC-QUIC, TIGER, and flare (CLIME/TIGER implementations), and demonstrate near-linear speedups, including a million-dimensional instance solved on 400 cores.

Strengths
- Tackles an important and timely problem: scaling sparse inverse covariance estimation to ultra-high dimensions.
- Clear computational design: the column-block ADMM formulation enables batching of columns to exploit efficient matrix–matrix multiplications and memory hierarchies.
- Theoretical guarantees: O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality and constraint residuals with inexact ADMM, with conditions stated.
- Practical contributions: a distributed implementation strategy (OpenMP/OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK), low-rank multiplications for p ≫ n, and empirical results on both shared and distributed environments.
- Comparative experiments indicate strong scalability relative to state-of-the-art methods.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/notation: Notation is introduced across Sections 1–3 in a scattered way. Several key symbols (e.g., rho and eta) are not explicitly defined when first used. A consolidated notation table would improve readability and reproducibility.
- Block-cyclic distribution justification: The argument that block-cyclic data distribution yields better load balance and scalability is plausible but not fully supported. Please provide:
  - A clearer explanation of why block-cyclic helps with dynamic sparsity and cache behavior relative to simple row/column blocks.
  - Empirical evidence isolating this choice (e.g., comparisons against alternative layouts such as contiguous block distributions), including metrics for load imbalance, cache misses, and communication overhead.
- Choice of column-block size k: The selection of k appears ad hoc. Guidance would be valuable, e.g., rules of thumb tying k to process-grid shape, block sizes pb × nb × kb, cache sizes, p/n ratios, and sparsity patterns. A sensitivity analysis of performance vs k would make the approach easier to adopt.
- Distributed algorithm details: Although the framework runs across nodes, the paper does not clearly describe the communication patterns and costs. To make this convincingly “distributed” (not just parallel), please specify:
  - How data and variables are partitioned and redistributed across iterations.
  - The message-passing primitives used (point-to-point vs collectives), per-iteration communication volume, synchronization points, and overlap of communication/computation.
  - An iteration-level complexity model that includes both flops and bandwidth/latency terms, and how these scale with p, n, k, and the process grid.
- Evaluation details: The large-scale experiments are compelling, but it would help to:
  - Report stopping criteria, choices of rho and eta, and how these impact convergence and runtime.
  - Clarify memory footprints per node for A and working variables at scale.
  - Where claims hinge on low-rank multiplications, quantify the speedup vs naive multiplication.

Quality: The algorithmic development is sound, and the convergence analysis aligns with inexact ADMM literature. The empirical results are strong on scaling; however, more detail on communication and parameter choices would strengthen reproducibility and support the “distributed” claim.

Clarity: Generally well written, but organization of notation and additional implementation details (above) would significantly improve clarity.

Originality: The column-block inexact ADMM formulation for CLIME, combined with a block-cyclic distributed design and low-rank multiplications, is a novel and useful combination relative to prior CLIME solvers and related ADMM-based approaches.

Significance: The ability to handle million-dimensional precision estimation with near-linear scaling is impactful and likely to be of interest to practitioners working with massive graphical models.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a challenging large-scale problem with both theory and practice.
- Demonstrates impressive scalability on real systems.
- Provides a flexible framework likely applicable to other constrained problems.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient justification and ablation for the block-cyclic distribution choice.
- Limited guidance on selecting k, rho, and eta.
- Communication patterns and distributed costs are under-specified.

Minor comments (typos/edits)
- Section 1: “tunning” → “tuning”.
- Section 5.1: “parameter tunning free” → “parameter tuning free”.
- Section 2: “summerized” → “summarized”.
- Section 5.1 header: “Comparision” → “Comparison”.
- Section 4: “achitectures” → “architectures”.

Overall assessment
A strong and promising systems-oriented contribution to scalable precision matrix estimation. With clearer exposition of notation, communication, and parameter choices, and with targeted experiments justifying the block-cyclic layout and block-size selection, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
