

This paper proposes CLIME-ADMM, a scalable variant of the CLIME algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation, accompanied by a distributed framework for parallel computation. The authors present empirical comparisons against state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, Flare), demonstrating the approach's efficiency on large-scale datasets. While the core idea of decomposing precision matrix estimation into column blocks (rather than column-by-column) offers a plausible parallelization strategy, the paper requires significant revisions to address clarity, technical rigor, and terminology.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Notation Ambiguity:** Critical symbols (e.g., $\rho$, $\eta$) are introduced without definition in the main text, appearing scattered across Sections 1-3. This severely hinders readability and reproducibility. A dedicated notation table in Section 1 or 2 is essential to define all symbols consistently. For example, the role of $\rho$ in the ADMM updates and $\eta$ in the convergence analysis must be explicitly stated.

2.  **Mischaracterization of "Distributed" Algorithm:** The paper claims a "distributed framework" but fails to specify any explicit communication or message-passing mechanisms between processors. The use of OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK implies a *parallel* implementation, not a *distributed* one (which typically requires explicit inter-node communication protocols and fault tolerance considerations). The authors should either:
    *   Clarify the specific communication patterns (e.g., all-to-all, ring, tree) and message sizes used in the implementation, or
    *   Retitle the framework as a *parallel* (not distributed) algorithm for precision. The current terminology is misleading and undermines the claimed contribution.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Scalability Claims:** The block cyclic data distribution is presented as key to achieving load balance and scalability. However, the paper provides no theoretical justification for why this specific distribution ensures load balance (e.g., for arbitrary problem sizes or data distributions) and crucially, **no empirical evidence** to support this claim. The results in Section 5.2 show speedup and scalability *with respect to the number of processors*, but do not demonstrate *how* the data distribution scheme contributes to this. The authors should either provide a theoretical argument for load balance under block cyclic distribution or include experiments (e.g., measuring load imbalance metrics) to empirically validate the claim.

4.  **Ad Hoc Block Size Selection:** The column block size $k$ is used as a parameter but is set "on an ad hoc basis" without justification. The authors should discuss whether there is theoretical guidance (e.g., based on communication costs vs. computation) or empirical tuning rationale for choosing $k$. The impact of $k$ on performance and scalability should be analyzed.

**Minor Concerns:**

*   Section 1: "tunning" → "tuning" (repeated error).
*   Section 5.1: "parameter tunning free" → "parameter tuning free".
*   Section 2: "CLIME is summerized" → "CLIME is summarized".
*   Section 5.1: "Empricial analysis" → "Empirical analysis".
*   The phrase "a large scale parallel algorithm" should be "a large-scale parallel algorithm" (hyphenation).

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper addresses a relevant problem (scalable sparse precision estimation) and presents a reasonable parallelization strategy using column blocks. The empirical results on large datasets are a strength. However, the current presentation contains significant flaws that prevent a clear evaluation of the method's novelty and technical contribution. The misuse of "distributed" terminology is particularly problematic for the NIPS audience. The lack of defined notation, unsubstantiated scalability claims regarding the data distribution, and the ad hoc choice of block size require substantial revision before the paper can be considered for publication. The core idea of column-block decomposition is potentially useful, but the paper's current form does not convincingly demonstrate its novelty, technical soundness, or superiority over existing parallel approaches.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.** The paper requires major revisions to address the fundamental issues of notation clarity, precise terminology ("distributed" vs. "parallel"), and empirical justification for key claims (load balance, block size choice). Without these, the contribution is obscured and the technical validity is questionable. The authors should reframe the work as a *parallel* algorithm, provide a clear notation table, rigorously justify the data distribution choice with theory or evidence, and justify the block size parameter before resubmission. The current version does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS.
