This paper addresses the challenging problem of sparse precision matrix estimation in high-dimensional settings, scaling to millions of variables and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, leveraging its desirable theoretical properties and potential for distributed computation. They present an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for CLIME, establishing convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions. Furthermore, they develop a scalable distributed framework that utilizes column-block operations and efficient matrix multiplications, and evaluate its performance on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and advantages of the proposed method are clearly articulated, and the connection to existing work is well-established with thorough referencing. The algorithm is introduced with a good balance between technical detail and theoretical justification, including convergence rate analysis. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating impressive performance gains, particularly in scalability. The authors appropriately address the potential for exploiting sparse and low-rank structures to further enhance computational efficiency.

A minor concern is the accessibility of the proposed method. Making the source code available to the research community would significantly enhance the impact and reproducibility of this work. 

Overall, this is a strong algorithmic paper that tackles an important practical problem – covariance selection with very large datasets. If the software is made available, this work warrants publication due to its potential to become a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant practical problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   Presents a theoretically sound algorithm with established convergence rates.
*   Demonstrates substantial scalability through a carefully designed distributed framework.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation validates the effectiveness of the method.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of publicly available source code limits reproducibility and impact.
