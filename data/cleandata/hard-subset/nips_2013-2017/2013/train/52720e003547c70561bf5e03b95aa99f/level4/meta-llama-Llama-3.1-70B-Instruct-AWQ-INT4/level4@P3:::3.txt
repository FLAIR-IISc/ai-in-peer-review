**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of large-scale distributed sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm for CLIME, which can scale to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters using hundreds of cores. The algorithm solves the CLIME problem in column-blocks, leveraging the separable structure of the problem, and utilizes element-wise operations and parallel matrix multiplications. The authors also develop a scalable distributed framework for the computations, which can be applied to both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The CLIME estimator has been previously studied in the context of sparse precision matrix estimation, and its theoretical properties have been established. However, existing solvers for CLIME do not scale well to ultra-high-dimensional problems. The authors' contribution is to develop a scalable distributed algorithm for CLIME, which can handle problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of the proposed algorithm. Theoretical results are established for both the objective function and the residuals of optimality conditions. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm and discuss how to leverage special matrix structures (sparse and low rank) to reduce the computational burden.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clear. The authors provide a good balance between technical points of numerical analysis and algebra, and theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The algorithm is nicely introduced, and the numerical analysis is appropriate.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel algorithm for large-scale distributed sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME estimator. The authors' contribution is to develop a scalable distributed algorithm that can handle problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters.

**Significance**

The paper addresses an important practical problem in machine learning and statistics, namely, the estimation of sparse precision matrices in high dimensions. The proposed algorithm has the potential to be widely used in various applications, including gene expression analysis, climate modeling, and finance.
