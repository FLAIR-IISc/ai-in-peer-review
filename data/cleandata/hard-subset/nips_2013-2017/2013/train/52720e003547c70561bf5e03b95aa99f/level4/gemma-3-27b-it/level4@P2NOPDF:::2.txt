This paper presents an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) tailored for solving the linear program inherent in the CLIME estimator. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) and demonstrate the algorithm’s implementation across both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. They support their work with numerical comparisons against existing methods, including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness. The derivation of the inexact ADMM applied to CLIME appears to follow standard approaches, as evidenced by existing work (e.g., http://math.nju.edu.cn/~hebma/English%20Version.htm).  The authors correctly highlight the potential for significant parallelization within the ADMM framework when applied to CLIME; the componentwise nature of the subproblem solutions, coupled with the dominance of matrix-matrix multiplications – operations known to be highly parallelizable – allows for ideal scalability. 

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow.

**Originality:** The investigation of ADMM’s performance in both shared-memory and distributed-memory environments for this specific problem is a noteworthy contribution.

**Significance:** Large-scale sparse precision estimation remains a significant challenge. This work offers a simple and potentially robust distributed algorithm to address this problem. While the derivation of the inexact ADMM itself is standard, the effective scalability achieved is a direct result of leveraging the inherent structure of the CLIME problem, and the empirical evaluation of this scalability is valuable. 

**Minor Comments/Suggestions for Improvement:**

To further strengthen the paper, the numerical experimental setup could benefit from greater detail. Specifically, reporting the achieved accuracy (e.g., relative error, tolerance) for each solver would provide a more complete comparison and allow readers to better assess the trade-offs between different methods. Including this information would facilitate a more thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithm's performance.
