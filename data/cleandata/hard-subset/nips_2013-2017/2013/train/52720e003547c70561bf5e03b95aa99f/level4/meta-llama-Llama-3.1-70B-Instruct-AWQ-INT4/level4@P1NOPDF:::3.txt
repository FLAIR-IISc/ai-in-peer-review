Summary of the paper 

This paper addresses the resolution of covariance selection problems on a very large scale, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The estimator of interest is the CLIME, known for providing an estimate of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be solved column by column, making it more amenable to distributed computations. The authors claim that theoretical analysis is also easier for CLIME compared to the Graphical Lasso. To fit the CLIME, the authors developed a new algorithm that solves the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupled with an inexact method of multipliers. They provide theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm, expressed in terms of both the objective function and the distance to optimality. The authors pay special attention to exploiting special matrix structures, such as sparsity and low rank, to reduce the computational burden. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework suitable for both shared and distributed memory systems. Numerical studies compare the algorithm's runtime with its direct competitors on both synthetic and real data. A detailed study is also presented to evaluate the speedup with respect to block sizes and the number of cores on both shared memory and distributed memory systems.

Comments 

This paper is well-written and very clear. The novelty and contributions of the method are clearly stated in the introduction, and connections to existing works are well-established with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is nicely introduced, striking a good balance between technical points of numerical analysis and algebra, and theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation of the algorithm demonstrates impressive performance.

My only concern is the availability of the method: if the source code, which is largely based on open-source libraries, is not made available to the community, the impact of this work will be limited. 

This is a sound algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection in very high-dimensional settings (millions of variables). If the authors make the method available to the community, this powerful tool should be rewarded with publication.
