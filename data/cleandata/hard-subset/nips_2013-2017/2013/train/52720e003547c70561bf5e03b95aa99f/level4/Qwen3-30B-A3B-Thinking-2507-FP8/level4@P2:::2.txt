

This paper addresses the challenge of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation in high dimensions, proposing an efficient distributed algorithm based on the CLIME estimator. The authors develop an inexact ADMM variant for CLIME that scales to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters using hundreds of cores. The key innovation lies in solving CLIME in column-blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging matrix-matrix multiplications that can be optimized for cache efficiency and parallelization. The paper establishes O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective and optimality conditions, and evaluates the algorithm on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures with block cyclic data distribution to achieve load balance.

The paper builds on recent work in distributed optimization for machine learning [2, 6, 9, 22, 23], particularly extending the ADMM framework to precision matrix estimation. While [23] introduced graph projection block splitting for distributed optimization, the authors' block cyclic distribution approach provides more flexibility in choosing optimal block sizes to minimize cache misses. Unlike [9] which considered local constraints in graphical model inference, this work handles global constraints inherent in precision matrix estimation. The CLIME estimator [3, 4, 19] is well-established in statistical theory, and the authors appropriately leverage its columnwise separable structure to design their distributed algorithm.

Quality: The technical content is sound and well-executed. The convergence rate analysis is rigorous and correctly establishes O(1/T) rates for both objective function and optimality residuals. The implementation details for shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures are thorough, with appropriate consideration of memory hierarchies and load balancing. The experimental results demonstrate substantial scalability improvements over state-of-the-art methods (DC-QUIC, Tiger, CLIME-Flare) on both synthetic and real datasets. However, the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME follows standard methodology [12, 26, 27] and could be better contextualized with more specific references to prior work on inexact ADMM for similar problems. The numerical experiments would benefit from reporting the final accuracy achieved by each solver (e.g., Frobenius norm error between estimated and true precision matrices), rather than solely focusing on runtime comparisons.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for column-block processing is well-explained, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is sufficiently detailed. The experimental setup is adequately described, though more specifics on parameter choices for different methods would strengthen the comparison. The figures effectively illustrate the scalability results, particularly Figures 3 and 4 showing linear speedup with increasing cores.

Originality: The paper's main contribution is the practical implementation of CLIME in a distributed setting with column-block processing, rather than the theoretical novelty of the ADMM approach itself. While the column-block approach is a reasonable extension of CLIME's inherent separability, the authors demonstrate that this simple modification leads to substantial performance gains through efficient matrix multiplications. The block cyclic distribution strategy for load balancing is a practical contribution that improves upon prior work [5, 15, 16] by providing more flexibility in block size selection. However, the core idea of parallelizing matrix-matrix multiplication for CLIME is well-known and not particularly novel in the broader context of distributed optimization.

Significance: The problem of large-scale sparse precision estimation is highly relevant to modern data analysis, especially in genomics and climate science where datasets can reach millions of dimensions. The authors successfully demonstrate that their method can estimate a one-million-dimensional precision matrix (one trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, substantially outperforming existing methods. The linear scalability with number of cores (Figures 3 and 4) is particularly impressive and addresses a critical bottleneck in the field. While the distributed framework could potentially be applied to other large-scale constrained optimization problems, the paper's focus on precision matrix estimation is appropriately specific and impactful for the NIPS community.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the practical implementation of statistical methods at scale. The distributed framework is well-designed, technically sound, and demonstrates impressive scalability. The main limitation is that the core algorithmic approach (inexact ADMM for CLIME) is not particularly novel, though the implementation details and scalability results are compelling. The paper would benefit from more specific discussion of how the column-block approach differs from prior work on parallelizing CLIME, and more detailed reporting of solution accuracy in the experiments.

Strengths: 
- Demonstrates exceptional scalability (1 million dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores)
- Well-executed convergence rate analysis
- Practical distributed framework with block cyclic distribution for load balancing
- Comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods

Weaknesses:
- Inexact ADMM derivation follows standard methodology
- Limited discussion of how column-block processing differs from prior parallel CLIME implementations
- Experimental results lack final solution accuracy metrics
- The significant scalability gains are partly attributable to CLIME's inherent parallelizability rather than novel algorithmic advances

The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in high-dimensional statistics with a practical solution that could benefit many applications. While the algorithmic novelty is modest, the implementation details, scalability results, and thorough experimental evaluation make this a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the experimental details and contextualize the method within the literature.
