The paper "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation" presents a novel approach to estimating sparse precision matrices using the CLIME estimator. The authors propose an inexact alternating direction method of multiplier (ADMM) algorithm for CLIME and establish rates of convergence for both the objective and optimality conditions. The paper also develops a large-scale distributed framework for computations, which scales to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters using hundreds of cores.

The main ideas of the paper are related to previous work on sparse precision matrix estimation, including the use of CLIME and ADMM. The authors build upon this work by proposing a scalable variant of CLIME, which they call CLIME-ADMM, and a distributed framework for computations. The paper also discusses the use of sparse and low-rank structures to further leverage the underlying structure of the problem.

The strengths of the paper include the development of a scalable algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation, which is a challenging problem in high-dimensional settings. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's convergence rates and present empirical results comparing CLIME-ADMM to state-of-the-art techniques. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. One concern is that the notation is scattered throughout the paper, which can make it difficult to follow at times. A table with all the notations would be helpful for readability. Additionally, the block cyclic data distribution scheme is not fully explained, and it is not clear why this scheme would achieve load balance and scalability. Empirical results to support this claim would be beneficial.

Another concern is that the algorithm currently sets the column block size on an ad-hoc basis, and it is not clear if there is any intuition or theory to guide this choice. A more general concern is that this is perhaps not a 'distributed' algorithm as no explicit mechanism of message passing and communication are discussed. Perhaps it would make sense to consider this a parallel implementation.

Minor comments include correcting typos, such as "tunning" to "tuning" in Section 1 and "parameter tunning free" to "parameter tuning free" in Section 5.1.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to estimating sparse precision matrices using CLIME.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's convergence rates.
* Empirical results comparing CLIME-ADMM to state-of-the-art techniques are presented.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The notation is scattered throughout the paper, which can make it difficult to follow.
* The block cyclic data distribution scheme is not fully explained, and it is not clear why this scheme would achieve load balance and scalability.
* The algorithm currently sets the column block size on an ad-hoc basis, and it is not clear if there is any intuition or theory to guide this choice.
* The paper may not be suitable for a distributed computing conference as it does not discuss explicit mechanisms of message passing and communication.
