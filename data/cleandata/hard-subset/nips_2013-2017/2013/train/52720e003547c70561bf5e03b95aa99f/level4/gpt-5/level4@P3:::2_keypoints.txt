- Proposes an inexact (linearized) ADMM that solves CLIME in column blocks to use matrix–matrix kernels and elementwise ops efficiently.  
- Proves O(1/T) convergence rates for objective suboptimality and optimality residuals under a step-size condition.  
- Exploits two structures: dynamic sparsity in iterates and low-rank sample covariance C = AA′ when n ≪ p to reduce cost from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k) per block.  
- Implements a scalable solver on shared- and distributed-memory systems using block-cyclic data distribution to balance load and reduce cache misses.  
- Experiments show near-linear speedups and estimation of a precision matrix with p = 10^6 on 400 cores in ~11 hours.  
- Situates work relative to prior CLIME solvers and linearized ADMM literature; main novelty is the distributed, column-block ADMM design and high-performance implementation.  
- Theoretical rates are standard for linearized ADMM; step-size assumptions (η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2) are reasonable but potentially conservative.  
- The paper should state how η and ρ are chosen in practice and whether adaptive tuning/backtracking is used.  
- Algorithmic idea of solving multiple columns jointly to leverage BLAS is sound and well suited to modern architectures.  
- Block-cyclic distribution is appropriate for load balancing and cache efficiency in distributed settings.  
- Experimental scaling studies are informative, but lack details on stopping criteria, feasibility tolerances, and reported objective values.  
- Comparisons include methods optimizing different objectives (DC-QUIC, TIGER), which complicates timing and accuracy comparisons.  
- Clarity could be improved by precisely defining stopping criteria, reporting tolerances, and clarifying how η, ρ, and λ are set or estimated/distributed.  
- Figures need axis labels/units, confidence intervals where appropriate, and clear baselines for speedup plots.  
- References contain incomplete/malformed entries and should be standardized with full bibliographic details.  
- Originality lies mainly in systems/engineering: column-block formulation, block-cyclic distribution, and a scalable implementation to million-dimensional problems.  
- Significance is high: a practical distributed CLIME solver for ultra-high dimensions is valuable to statistics and ML communities.  
- Recommend stronger experimental methodology: compare against other CLIME solvers, report PR/AUC or F1 across grids, and include runtime-to-target-accuracy curves.  
- Report final objective values and constraint residuals ||C X − I||∞ at termination and state stopping tolerances to validate accuracy and feasibility.  
- Provide guidance on selecting ρ, η, and λ (theory-driven vs grid search) and explain distributed estimation of λmax(C) if used.  
- Theorem on covariance perturbation needs tighter statements (sub-exponential parameters, scaling of Δ) and empirical validation of statistical loss.  
- Clarify handling of CLIME symmetry/PD issues (symmetrization step) and any impact on scalability.  
- Include per-iteration communication analysis (messages/bytes) and empirical time breakdown (matmult vs elementwise vs communication).  
- Report per-node memory footprint for largest experiments and discuss trade-offs of block size k for compute vs memory/communication.  
- Pros: strong scalability, practical use of matrix kernels, theoretical guarantees, and compelling million-dimensional experiment.  
- Cons: core optimization idea is standard (systems novelty), mixed-model comparisons, incomplete empirical/theoretical details on perturbation, parameters, stopping, communication, and memory.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending stronger experimental rigor (feasibility/accuracy reporting, fair CLIME baselines) and tightened perturbation theory.
