This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multiplier (ADMM) for solving the linear program problem in the CLIME estimator, a widely used method for sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(1/T) for the algorithm and demonstrate its scalability by implementing it using both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their algorithm, including numerical comparisons with other methods such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and CLIME-Flare.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a distributed framework for the CLIME estimator, which can scale to millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters. The authors achieve this by solving the CLIME problem in column-blocks, using a block cyclic distribution of data and parameters to achieve load balance and improve the efficiency in the use of memory hierarchies. The proposed framework is shown to be substantially more scalable than state-of-the-art methods and scales almost linearly with the number of cores.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its implementation. The evaluation of ADMM in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is interesting, and the authors provide a thorough comparison of their algorithm with other methods.

One minor comment is that the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME is standard and can be found in other sources. However, the application of ADMM to the CLIME estimator and its evaluation in shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures is a significant contribution.

To further improve the paper, the authors could provide more details on the setup of the numerical experiments, such as the accuracy achieved by each solver. Additionally, the authors could discuss the implications of their results for other large-scale constrained optimization problems and explore the potential applications of their framework in other domains.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of sparse precision matrix estimation and demonstrates the potential of ADMM for solving large-scale optimization problems in a distributed setting.
