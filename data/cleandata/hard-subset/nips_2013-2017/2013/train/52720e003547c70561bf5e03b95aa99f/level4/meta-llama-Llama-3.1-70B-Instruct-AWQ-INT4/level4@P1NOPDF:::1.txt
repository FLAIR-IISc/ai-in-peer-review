The authors discuss the problem of sparse precision matrix estimation using CLIME and present a scalable variant, CLIME-ADMM, along with a distributed framework for computations. They also provide empirical results comparing the CLIME-ADMM algorithm to state-of-the-art techniques such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare.

One suggestion for improving the clarity of the paper is to consolidate the notation into a single table, rather than scattering it throughout Sections 1, 2, and 3. For instance, the variables \rho and \eta are not explicitly defined in the text. A notation table would enhance readability and facilitate understanding.

The block cyclic data distribution scheme appears to be an interesting approach; however, it is not immediately clear why this method would achieve load balance and scalability. Furthermore, there are no empirical results provided to support this claim.

It seems that the algorithm currently sets the column block size on an ad hoc basis. It would be beneficial to provide some intuition or theoretical guidance on how to choose the value of k.

A more general concern is that this may not be a truly 'distributed' algorithm, as there is no explicit discussion of message passing and communication mechanisms. It might be more accurate to consider this a parallel implementation.

Minor comments include:

* In Section 1, "where \lambda is a tunning parameter" should be replaced with "tuning".
* In Section 5.1, "As Tiger is parameter tunning free" should be replaced with "tuning".
* In Section 2, "CLIME is summerized in" should be replaced with "CLIME is summarized in".

A large-scale parallel algorithm for estimating sparse precision matrices using CLIME is presented. The novelty of this approach lies in estimating the precision matrix by column blocks, rather than column-by-column. The empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapak is also presented.
