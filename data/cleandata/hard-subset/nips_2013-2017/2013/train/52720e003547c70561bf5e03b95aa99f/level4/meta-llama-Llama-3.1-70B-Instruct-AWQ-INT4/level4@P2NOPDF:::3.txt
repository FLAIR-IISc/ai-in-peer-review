Summary of the Paper:

This paper tackles the resolution of covariance selection problems in extremely large-scale settings, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, known for providing an efficient estimator of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be solved column by column, making it more amenable to distributed computations. Theoretical analysis of CLIME is also claimed to be more straightforward compared to the Graphical-Lasso. The authors propose a novel algorithm to fit CLIME by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupled with an inexact direction method of multipliers. Theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm are presented, in terms of both objective function and distance to optimality. The authors pay special attention to exploiting special matrix structures (sparse and low rank) during computations to reduce the computational burden. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework for both shared and distributed memory systems. Numerical studies compare the algorithm's runtime with its direct competitors on both synthetic and real data, and a detailed study evaluates the speedup with respect to block sizes and the number of cores on both shared memory and distributed memory systems.

Comments:

The paper is well-written and clear, effectively conveying the novelty and contributions of the method in the introduction while establishing connections to existing works through sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is nicely presented, striking a good balance between technical points of numerical analysis and algebra, and theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation of the algorithm demonstrates impressive performance.

However, my primary concern is the availability of the method: if the source code, which is largely based on open-source libraries, is not made available to the community, the impact of this work will be limited. A sound algorithmic paper, such as this one, that addresses the important practical problem of covariance selection in very large dimensions (millions of variables) should be rewarded with publication, provided that the implementation is made accessible to the community.
