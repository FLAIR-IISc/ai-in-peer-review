The authors discuss the problem of sparse precision matrix estimation using CLIME and present a scalable variant, CLIME-ADMM, along with a distributed framework for computations. Empirical results comparing the CLIME-ADMM algorithm to state-of-the-art techniques such as DC-QUIC, Tiger, and flare are presented.

The notation is somewhat scattered throughout Sections 1, 2, and 3. A table summarizing all notations would improve readability. For example, ρ and η are not explicitly defined in the text. 

The block cyclic data distribution appears promising, but the rationale behind achieving load balance and scalability is not entirely clear. Furthermore, there are no empirical results specifically demonstrating these benefits.

It is unclear how the column block size *k* is determined. Is there an underlying intuition or theoretical guidance for choosing an optimal *k*?

A potential concern is whether this qualifies as a truly “distributed” algorithm. The paper lacks a discussion of explicit message passing and communication mechanisms. It might be more accurate to characterize this as a parallel implementation.

Overall, this paper presents a large-scale parallel algorithm for estimating sparse precision matrices using CLIME. The primary novelty lies in estimating the precision matrix by column blocks instead of column-by-column. Empirical analysis using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK is presented. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The proposed method demonstrates significant scalability, capable of handling matrices with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, as evidenced by the results on a 1 million dimension problem solved in 11 hours using 400 cores.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors establish convergence rates for both the objective function and optimality conditions of the CLIME-ADMM algorithm.
*   **Practical Implementation:** The framework is implemented with widely used libraries (OpenMP, OpenMPI, ScaLAPACK), making it readily accessible to researchers.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation and positions the contribution within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Explanation of Block Cyclic Distribution:** The justification for the block cyclic data distribution and its impact on load balancing requires further elaboration and empirical validation.
*   **Ad Hoc Block Size Selection:** The method for choosing the column block size *k* is not well-defined or justified.
*   **Distributed vs. Parallel:** The characterization of the algorithm as “distributed” might be misleading given the lack of detailed discussion on inter-process communication.
*   **Notation:** The scattered and undefined notation hinders readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
*   The proposed algorithm demonstrates promising scalability, outperforming existing methods on large-scale datasets.
*   The theoretical analysis provides some guarantees on the convergence of the algorithm.
*   The work contributes a practical implementation with potential for broader impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The explanation for the effectiveness of the block cyclic data distribution is insufficient.
*   The lack of guidance on selecting the column block size *k* weakens the practical applicability of the method.
*   The terminology used (“distributed” algorithm) could be more precise.
*   Minor issues with notation and clarity detract from the overall presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. While there are some areas for improvement, the demonstrated scalability and theoretical analysis suggest that this work is worthy of acceptance. Addressing the concerns regarding the block cyclic distribution, block size selection, and clarifying the nature of the parallelism would strengthen the paper further. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
