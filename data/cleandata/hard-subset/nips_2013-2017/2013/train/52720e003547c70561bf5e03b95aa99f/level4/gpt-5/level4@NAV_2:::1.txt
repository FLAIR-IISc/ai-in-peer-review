The paper addresses sparse precision matrix estimation via CLIME and introduces a scalable CLIME-ADMM variant together with a distributed computation framework. The authors present empirical comparisons against contemporary methods including DC-QUIC, TIGER, and flare.

The notation is dispersed across Sections 1–3; a consolidated notation table would improve readability. In particular, the symbols ρ and η are not clearly defined when first used.

The block-cyclic data distribution strategy is intriguing, but the rationale for why it ensures load balancing and scalability is not fully substantiated. Corresponding empirical evidence explicitly isolating this effect would strengthen the claim.

The choice of column block size appears ad hoc; guidance—either theoretical or empirical heuristics—on selecting k would be valuable.

More generally, the work may be better characterized as a parallel implementation, since explicit communication and message-passing mechanisms typical of distributed algorithms are not described.

Minor comments:
- Section 1: “where λ is a tunning parameter” → “tuning”
- Section 5.1: “As TIGER is parameter tunning free” → “tuning”
- Section 2: “CLIME is summerized in” → “CLIME is summarized in”

Overall, the paper presents a large-scale parallel approach to CLIME-based sparse precision estimation. The key novelty is solving in column blocks rather than one column at a time, with empirical results using OpenMPI and ScaLAPACK.
