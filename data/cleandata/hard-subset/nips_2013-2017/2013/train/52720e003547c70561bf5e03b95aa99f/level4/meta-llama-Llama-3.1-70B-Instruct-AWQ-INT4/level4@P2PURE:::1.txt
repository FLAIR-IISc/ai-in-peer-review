The authors discuss the problem of sparse precision matrix estimation using the CLIME method and introduce a scalable variant, CLIME-ADMM, along with a distributed framework for computations. They present empirical results comparing the CLIME-ADMM algorithm to state-of-the-art techniques, including DC-QUIC, Tiger, and Flare.

To enhance readability, it would be beneficial to consolidate the notation into a single table, rather than having it scattered throughout Sections 1, 2, and 3. For instance, the parameters \rho and \eta are not explicitly defined in the text.

The proposed block cyclic data distribution is an interesting approach; however, it is not immediately clear why this scheme achieves load balance and scalability. Furthermore, there are no empirical results provided to support this claim.

The algorithm appears to set the column block size on an ad hoc basis; it would be helpful to provide some intuition or theoretical guidance on the choice of k.

A more general concern is that this may not be a truly 'distributed' algorithm, as there is no explicit discussion of message passing and communication mechanisms. It may be more accurate to consider this a parallel implementation.

A large-scale parallel algorithm for estimating the sparse precision matrix using CLIME is presented. The novelty of this approach involves estimating the precision matrix by column blocks, rather than column-by-column. Empirical analysis using OpenMPI and Scalapak is presented.
