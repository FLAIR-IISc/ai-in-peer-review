Summary of the paper
The paper tackles covariance selection at ultra-large scales (up to millions of variables and trillions of parameters). It focuses on the CLIME estimator, which yields a precision matrix estimate that decomposes columnwise, facilitating distributed computation; its analysis is also argued to be more straightforward than for the Graphical Lasso. The authors propose a new solver that optimizes CLIME in column blocks rather than one column at a time, combined with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers. They provide convergence-rate guarantees for both objective suboptimality and optimality residuals. The method explicitly exploits structural properties (sparsity and low rank) to reduce computation. A scalable parallel framework is implemented for both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems. Empirically, the paper compares runtime against strong baselines on synthetic and real datasets, and presents an in-depth scaling study showing speedups as a function of block size and number of cores in both computing environments.

Comments
The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. Its contributions and novelty are well framed in the introduction, with solid connections to prior work and appropriate citations. The algorithmic presentation strikes a good balance between numerical linear-algebraic details and theoretical convergence guarantees. The experimental methodology is appropriate, and the implementation demonstrates very impressive performance and scalability.

My only concern is availability: if the source code (built largely on open-source libraries) is not released, the practical impact of this work would be substantially diminished.

Overall, this is a solid algorithmic contribution addressing an important practical problem—large-scale covariance selection. Such a capable tool merits publication, provided it is made accessible to the community.
