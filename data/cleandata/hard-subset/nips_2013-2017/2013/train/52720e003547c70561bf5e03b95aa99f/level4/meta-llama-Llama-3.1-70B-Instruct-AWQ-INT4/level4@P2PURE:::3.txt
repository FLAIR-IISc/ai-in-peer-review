Summary of the paper 
This paper addresses the resolution of covariance selection problems in extremely large-scale settings, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, known for providing an estimator of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be solved column by column, making it more amenable to distributed computations. Theoretical analysis is also claimed to be more straightforward than for the Graphical-Lasso. The authors propose a novel algorithm to fit the CLIME by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupled with an inexact augmented Lagrangian method of multipliers. They provide theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm, expressed in terms of both objective function and distance to optimality. Special attention is paid to exploiting special matrix structures (sparse and low rank) during computation to reduce the computational burden. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework for both shared and distributed memory systems. Numerical studies compare the algorithm's runtime with its direct competitors on both synthetic and real data. A detailed study evaluating the speedup with respect to block sizes and the number of cores on both shared memory and distributed memory systems is also provided.

Comments 
This paper is well-written and very clear, effectively conveying the novelty and contributions of the method in the introduction while establishing connections to existing works through sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is nicely introduced, striking a good balance between technical points of numerical analysis and algebra, as well as theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation of the algorithm demonstrates impressive performance. 
My primary concern is the availability of this method: if the source code, which relies heavily on open-source libraries, is not made available to the community, the impact of this work will be limited. 
This is a sound algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection in very high-dimensional settings (millions of variables). If the authors make the code available to the community, this powerful tool deserves to be published.
