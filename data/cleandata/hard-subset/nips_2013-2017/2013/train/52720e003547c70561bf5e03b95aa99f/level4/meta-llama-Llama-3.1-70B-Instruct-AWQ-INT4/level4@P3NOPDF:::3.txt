This paper addresses the resolution of covariance selection problems on a very large scale, involving up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, known for providing an estimate of the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector that can be solved column by column, making it more amenable to distributed computations. Theoretical analysis is also claimed to be easier for CLIME compared to the Graphical-Lasso. 

The authors have developed a novel algorithm to fit the CLIME by solving the problem block-wise rather than column-wise, coupled with an inexact method of multipliers. Theoretical results on the convergence rate of this algorithm are provided, in terms of both objective function and distance to optimality. Special attention is paid to exploiting special matrix structures, such as sparsity and low rank, to reduce the computational burden. The algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework, supporting both shared and distributed memory systems.

In the numerical studies, comparisons are made in terms of runtime with direct competitors on both synthetic and real data. A detailed study is also provided to evaluate the speedup regarding block sizes and the number of cores on both shared memory and distributed memory systems.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clear, effectively communicating the novelty and contributions of the method. The introduction clearly states the connections to existing works, with sound bibliographical references. The algorithm is nicely introduced, striking a good balance between technical points of numerical analysis and algebra, as well as theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation of the algorithm demonstrates impressive performance.

However, the availability of the method is a concern. If the source code, which is mostly based on open-source libraries, is not made available to the community, the impact of this work will be limited. Nevertheless, this is a sound algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection in high-dimensional settings. If the code is made available, this powerful tool would be a valuable contribution to the community, making it worthy of publication.
