

Summary  
This paper addresses covariance selection in extremely high-dimensional settings (up to millions of features and trillions of parameters), focusing on the CLIME estimator for Gaussian graphical models. CLIME enables column-wise precision matrix estimation, facilitating distributed computation and simplifying theoretical analysis compared to Graphical-Lasso. The authors introduce a novel block-wise algorithm for CLIME, combined with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). Theoretical convergence rates are established for both the objective function and solution distance to optimality. Computational efficiency is further enhanced through specialized handling of sparse and low-rank matrix structures. The method is implemented within a scalable parallel framework supporting both shared and distributed memory architectures. Extensive numerical experiments demonstrate superior runtime performance against competitors on synthetic and real-world datasets. A detailed scalability analysis evaluates speedup as a function of block size and core count across different parallel systems.  

Comments  
The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of contributions and strong contextualization within existing literature through comprehensive, well-chosen references. The algorithmic development strikes an effective balance between technical depth (in numerical analysis and linear algebra) and rigorous convergence guarantees. The empirical validation is thorough, with compelling evidence of the method’s computational efficiency.  

The sole significant concern pertains to reproducibility: the absence of publicly available source code—despite reliance on open-source libraries—undermines the paper’s practical impact. For a computational method targeting large-scale applications, accessibility of the implementation is non-negotiable. Without it, the algorithm’s potential utility remains theoretical.  

This work presents a substantial advance in scalable covariance estimation, directly addressing a critical challenge in modern high-dimensional statistics. The method’s efficiency and theoretical grounding are compelling, and its implementation within a robust parallel framework further strengthens its practical relevance. If the source code is made publicly available, this paper would represent a valuable contribution to the field, deserving of publication at NIPS. The authors are strongly encouraged to release their implementation to maximize the work’s impact.
