Summary of the paper
This paper addresses covariance selection problems in very large-scale settings – with datasets containing up to millions of features and trillions of parameters. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, which offers a computationally attractive alternative to Graphical Lasso for estimating the precision matrix of a Gaussian vector. CLIME’s column-wise solution structure lends itself well to distributed computation, and the authors claim that its theoretical analysis is more tractable. They develop a novel algorithm to fit the CLIME by solving the problem block-wise, coupled with an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers. The paper provides theoretical results regarding the algorithm’s convergence rate, expressed in terms of both the objective function and distance to optimality. The authors pay particular attention to leveraging special matrix structures – sparsity and low rank – to reduce computational burden. Their algorithm is implemented within a scalable parallel computation framework designed for both shared and distributed memory systems. The numerical studies compare runtime performance with direct competitors on both synthetic and real datasets. A detailed study evaluates the speedup achieved by varying block sizes and the number of cores on both shared and distributed memory systems.

Comments
This is a well-written and very clear paper. The novelty and contributions of the method are clearly stated in the introduction, and the connection to existing work is well-established, supported by thorough bibliographical references. The algorithm is presented effectively, striking a good balance between technical details regarding numerical analysis/algebra and the theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The numerical analysis is appropriate, and the implementation demonstrates impressively strong performance.

My primary concern relates to the availability of the method. If the source code – which is largely based on open-source libraries – is not made available to the community, the value of this work is significantly diminished. 

Overall, this is a strong algorithmic paper that tackles the important practical problem of covariance selection in very high-dimensional data (millions of variables). Such a powerful tool deserves publication, contingent upon making it accessible to the wider research community.
