

This paper proposes an inexact alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) for solving the linear program underlying the CLIME estimator, establishing a convergence rate of $O(1/T)$. The approach is implemented across both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, with numerical comparisons against DC-QUIC, TIGER, and CLIME-Flare.  

**Quality**: The technical content is sound, though the derivation of the inexact ADMM for CLIME follows a standard procedure (e.g., as detailed in He and Ma, 2013). The core insight—that CLIME’s structure enables element-wise solution of subproblems, with matrix-matrix multiplication as the dominant cost—implies inherent parallelizability. This scalability stems not from the ADMM framework itself, but from CLIME’s simplicity, which is well-established. The experimental setup lacks specificity: key details such as the achieved accuracy (e.g., relative duality gap) for each solver remain unreported, limiting the interpretability of the comparisons.  

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written.  

**Originality**: The evaluation of ADMM in shared- and distributed-memory settings is novel for CLIME, though the methodological foundation is conventional.  

**Significance**: Solving large-scale sparse precision estimation remains challenging, and the proposed distributed ADMM offers a simple, robust solution. Its scalability is effectively demonstrated, but the primary contribution lies in the practical implementation and architecture evaluation, not in the ADMM derivation or convergence analysis. The work provides a useful, scalable tool for practitioners, though the theoretical novelty is modest.  

**Summary**: The paper presents a technically valid, well-executed implementation of inexact ADMM for CLIME, with compelling empirical evidence of scalability across architectures. However, the derivation is standard, and the experimental reporting requires greater rigor. The significance is moderate: while the distributed algorithm is practically valuable for large-scale problems, the methodological innovation is limited. The work would benefit from clearer experimental details (e.g., accuracy metrics) and a more precise characterization of the contribution relative to existing ADMM applications.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The practical utility and thorough scalability evaluation justify acceptance, though minor revisions to the experimental section are advised.
