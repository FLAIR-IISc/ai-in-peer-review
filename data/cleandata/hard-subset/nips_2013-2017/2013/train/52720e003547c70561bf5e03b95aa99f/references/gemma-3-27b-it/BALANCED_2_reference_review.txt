This paper presents a distributed algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, for estimating sparse precision matrices in ultra-high dimensional settings (p >> n). The core idea is to leverage the separable structure of the CLIME estimator and combine it with an inexact ADMM formulation, optimized for parallel computation across shared and distributed memory architectures. The authors introduce several key innovations, including column-block ADMM updates utilizing matrix-matrix multiplications, and a block-cyclic data distribution scheme for efficient memory access and load balancing. Extensive experimental validation demonstrates the scalability and efficiency of the proposed method, achieving significant speedups compared to existing approaches, even estimating a million-dimensional precision matrix in a reasonable timeframe.

The paper addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional statistics and machine learning – scaling precision matrix estimation to truly massive datasets. The motivation is clearly stated, and the connection to prior work in both statistical estimation and distributed optimization is well-established. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods and present a compelling case for their approach.  The theoretical analysis regarding convergence rates (O(1/T)) is reassuring, although perhaps not entirely surprising given the established theory of inexact ADMM. The contributions – the column-block ADMM formulation, the block-cyclic distribution scheme, and the comprehensive experimental evaluation – collectively represent a substantial step forward in this area. The experimental setup is rigorous, with comparisons to several baseline methods and thorough evaluation of scalability on different architectures. The presented speedups are impressive and convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.  The detailed explanation of the parallelization strategy, including considerations for cache optimization and load balancing, is particularly valuable.

However, there are some areas that could be strengthened. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Depth:** While the convergence rate is stated, the paper lacks a detailed theoretical justification for why the block-wise updates don’t significantly impact convergence compared to a column-wise approach. A deeper analysis of the convergence properties specific to the block structure would strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of parameter tuning (λ, ρ, η) is relatively brief. While the authors mention setting parameters individually, a more systematic exploration of the sensitivity of performance to these parameters, particularly in the context of the block structure, would be beneficial.  How do optimal values of these parameters change with increasing problem size and number of cores?
* **Practical Guidance:** The paper doesn't provide clear guidelines for selecting the optimal block size *k*. While experiments explore different *k* values, a more systematic analysis relating *k* to problem characteristics (p, n, sparsity level, architecture) would be helpful for practitioners. A practical heuristic or rule-of-thumb would be valuable.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons with CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC are provided, it would be beneficial to compare CLIME-ADMM with more recent distributed estimation methods, if available. The landscape of distributed ML is rapidly evolving, and situating the work within the broader context of recent advances would strengthen the contribution.
* **Code Availability:**  The authors mention implementation using OpenMP and OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK. Making the code publicly available would significantly enhance reproducibility and impact.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The demonstrated scalability to a million dimensions with 400 cores is a significant achievement.
* **Novelty:** The combination of column-block ADMM, block-cyclic distribution, and efficient matrix multiplication optimization is novel and effective.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The comprehensive experimental validation on both shared and distributed memory architectures is a major strength.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key ideas and technical details.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The convergence analysis, while not exhaustive, provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed method.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
* Presents a novel and effective algorithm with demonstrated scalability.
* Provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation.
* Contributes to the growing body of work on distributed machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*  Theoretical analysis could be more in-depth, particularly regarding the block structure.
*  Lack of discussion on parameter sensitivity.
*  Limited guidance on selecting the optimal block size.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and distributed machine learning. While some theoretical aspects and practical considerations could be further developed, the demonstrated scalability and efficiency of CLIME-ADMM are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the weaknesses outlined above. I would suggest the authors add a section in the appendix discussing the sensitivity of the performance to the parameters, and a more in-depth discussion on choosing the block size *k*, potentially in the form of a table or a set of guidelines based on problem characteristics. Making the code available would further strengthen the contribution.
