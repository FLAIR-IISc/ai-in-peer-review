

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM approach based on the CLIME estimator. The authors claim to address the computational challenges of precision matrix estimation in ultra-high dimensions (up to millions of variables) by solving the problem in column blocks rather than individual columns, using an inexact ADMM variant and block cyclic data distribution. They present theoretical convergence rates (O(1/T)) and experimental results showing scalability to one million dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates technical soundness in presenting the algorithm and establishing theoretical convergence rates. The theoretical results in Theorems 1 and 2 appear to be correctly derived, though the proof techniques are similar to existing literature (citing [26, 12]). The experimental section shows promising scalability results, particularly on large-scale problems.

However, the paper's quality is hampered by insufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The comparison with existing methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) is limited to synthetic data with small dimensions (p ≤ 10^4) and fails to demonstrate significant advantages on the most challenging high-dimensional settings where the authors claim their method excels. The paper does not adequately address whether the precision matrices they estimate are statistically meaningful or useful for downstream applications. For instance, the synthetic data experiments use sparsity 0.05, which is unrealistically sparse for practical applications with p=10^4.

The paper's claims about leveraging "sparse structure" and "low-rank structure" are underdeveloped. Theorem 3 on perturbing the sample covariance matrix to obtain sparsity lacks practical guidance on how to choose the perturbation parameters in real applications, and the authors don't demonstrate how this perturbation affects the statistical properties of the estimated precision matrix.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm design to experimental validation. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is well-structured, and the notation is consistent throughout. The explanation of the block cyclic distribution is particularly clear.

However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the column block size k affects performance in practice, beyond the limited discussion in Section 5.2. The authors claim "k depends on the size of process grid, block size in matrix multiplication, cache size and probably the sparsity pattern of matrices," but provide no practical guidance for choosing k in real applications.

## Originality
The paper's main technical contribution is the column-block ADMM approach for CLIME, which is a reasonable extension of existing ADMM-based precision matrix estimation methods. However, this contribution is not sufficiently novel in the context of the NIPS community. The authors cite related work on distributed optimization (e.g., [6, 9, 23]), but fail to clearly differentiate their approach from these methods.

The paper does not sufficiently establish how their method differs from DC-QUIC [13], which also uses a divide-and-conquer approach for high-dimensional precision matrix estimation. The authors acknowledge DC-QUIC's limitations with increasing sparsity but don't demonstrate how their method overcomes these limitations in a meaningful way. The "block cyclic distribution" they propose is a standard technique in distributed computing (citing [15, 5, 16]), not a novel contribution.

## Significance
The problem of large-scale precision matrix estimation is indeed significant, but the paper's claimed significance is overstated. While the authors demonstrate impressive scalability to 1 million dimensions, they fail to establish whether the precision matrices they estimate are statistically valid or useful for practical applications. For instance, the paper does not show how the estimated precision matrix improves downstream tasks like classification or clustering.

The experimental results are limited to synthetic data and small-scale real datasets (Leukemia, Climate). The paper claims to estimate a precision matrix with one trillion parameters in 11 hours on 400 cores, but this result is based on synthetic data with fixed n=200, which is unrealistically small for p=10^6. In practice, the sample size n would need to scale with p to obtain meaningful estimates, which the authors don't address.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound distributed algorithm for precision matrix estimation that demonstrates good scalability to large problems. However, the paper fails to sufficiently validate its claims against established baselines in realistic high-dimensional settings, and the claimed advantages over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated. The theoretical contributions are incremental, and the practical significance is overstated.

The paper would be significantly stronger with more comprehensive comparisons against the most recent state-of-the-art methods (including more recent distributed approaches like those in [2, 23]), a more thorough analysis of how the method performs on realistic high-dimensional datasets with appropriate n/p ratios, and clearer guidance on practical parameter choices.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable approach to scaling precision matrix estimation, it fails to provide sufficient evidence of its advantages over existing methods in the settings where they claim to excel. The paper's claims of scalability and performance improvements are not adequately supported by rigorous empirical validation against established baselines in realistic high-dimensional settings. The paper would benefit from extensive additional experiments on larger, more realistic datasets before being considered for publication at NIPS.
