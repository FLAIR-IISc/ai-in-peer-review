Summary
The paper tackles large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation in the low-sample, high-dimensional regime by proposing a distributed inexact ADMM algorithm for the CLIME estimator. The main ideas are: (i) reformulating CLIME into an equality-constrained problem with a box constraint on Z and using a linearized (inexact) ADMM update for X that admits a simple soft-thresholding step; (ii) solving the CLIME problem in column blocks (k columns at a time) to exploit matrix–matrix multiplies (better cache behavior and BLAS-3 efficiency) rather than repeated matrix–vector multiplies; (iii) leveraging structural properties—low sample size n (so C = A A^T is low rank) to rewrite CX as A(A^T X) and reduce matmul cost to O(npk), and, optionally, sparsity (either in iterates or via thresholding/perturbing C); and (iv) a scalable implementation using block-cyclic data distribution on shared- and distributed-memory systems. Theoretical results include O(1/T) ergodic rates for objective suboptimality and primal residuals under a stepsize condition η ≥ ρ λ_max^2(C). Experiments show substantial speedups versus column-wise CLIME solvers, TIGER, and DC-QUIC on moderate problems, and a demonstration at p=10^6 using 400 cores with an 11-hour runtime.

Strengths
- Clear computational focus with a well-motivated shift from column-wise to column-block updates; the reliance on BLAS-3 and block-cyclic distribution is sound and likely to deliver practical speedups.
- Reasonable inexact ADMM design (linearized augmented term plus proximal step) leading to closed-form X and Z updates; the algorithm reduces core operations to elementwise transforms and two matrix multiplies per iteration.
- O(1/T) convergence statements for ergodic objective gap and primal residuals under a standard Lipschitz-type stepsize condition; the analysis is consistent with linearized/inexact ADMM literature.
- Good systems perspective: process grid organization, block-cyclic distribution rationale, and parallel I/O consideration are appropriate for load balance and cache efficiency.
- Empirical evidence of scalability and nearly linear speedups in distributed memory, and a credible large-scale run (p=10^6).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Statistical estimator completeness and symmetrization: CLIME typically requires a symmetrization step to produce a symmetric precision estimate (e.g., min(|Ω_ij|,|Ω_ji|) with appropriate sign). The paper does not describe how symmetry is enforced when solving independent column blocks, nor the final post-processing. Positive definiteness is not enforced by CLIME; some discussion of downstream implications and optional PD projection would improve clarity.
- Theoretical results are modest and somewhat narrowly stated. Theorem 1 uses X̄_T and a bound relative to “any CX=Z,” which is unusual and may obscure convergence to the optimum of (3). Theorem 2 controls ‖CX^T − Z^T‖ and iterate differences but does not provide explicit iteration complexity to reach ε-accuracy in the original CLIME constraints; also, practical stopping criteria are not stated.
- Stepsize selection: η ≥ ρ λ_max^2(C) is required but potentially nontrivial to set at scale. Since λ_max(C) = σ_max^2(A), how is η chosen in practice (e.g., power iteration/upper bounds, backtracking)? This is important for reproducibility and performance.
- Perturbation/thresholding of C: Theorem 3’s thrust (entrywise noise/thresholding preserves sup-norm deviation with high probability) essentially follows by triangle inequality and union bounds under sub-exponential tails and is well-aligned with prior literature on covariance thresholding (e.g., Bickel & Levina, 2008). The novelty is limited, and the paper does not empirically evaluate this idea. If this is advocated as a key acceleration, experiments should quantify trade-offs (runtime vs. accuracy).
- Comparisons and fairness: DC-QUIC solves the graphical lasso, not CLIME; TIGER differs from CLIME statistically and computationally. While comparing runtime and precision–recall at matched sparsity is informative, the baselines are not apples-to-apples. More recent large-scale GLasso solvers (e.g., BigQUIC) and CLIME implementations should be discussed or compared, and column-parallel TIGER could be run on multiple cores for fairness. The million-dimensional demonstration lacks accuracy/quality evaluation and only reports runtime.
- Memory and output size for ultra-large runs: For p=10^6 and typical small n, storing A is plausible, but storing and writing k columns of variables (X, Z, Y, V, V̂) may still be heavy. What is the sparsity level of the final Ω̂ and how is it stored/written? Are there mechanisms to prune during iterations?
- Clarity/organization: Algorithm 1’s indices and step references are occasionally confusing (e.g., Steps 6/9 “Mat-Mul” labels). The norms in Theorem 2 use a matrix-weighted norm; a brief reminder that η/ρ I − C^2 is PSD under the stepsize condition would help. Some references are incomplete; related work on linearized ADMM and covariance thresholding could be strengthened.

Questions for the authors
- How do you ensure symmetry of the final precision estimate across independently solved column blocks? What symmetrization is used in practice?
- How do you set ρ and η at scale without computing λ_max(C) exactly? Any adaptive strategy?
- Can you report per-iteration costs, number of iterations to convergence, and communication time breakdowns for the distributed runs?
- Do you have empirical results on using thresholded C versus the raw C demonstrating accuracy preservation and speed gains?
- For the p=10^6 run, what is the sparsity of Ω̂, and how is the solution stored?

Suggestions
- Include symmetrization/post-processing details and their effect on runtime and accuracy.
- Add stopping criteria and practical parameter selection guidelines.
- Strengthen experiments: (i) add accuracy metrics for large p; (ii) ablations for k and communication; (iii) perturbation/truncation experiments; (iv) stronger baselines where possible.
- Discuss memory footprint and I/O for very large outputs; consider on-the-fly pruning.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a principled and implementable approach.
- Solid systems design leveraging well-understood HPC techniques; convincing speedups.
- The algorithmic decomposition to elementwise ops and matmuls is clean and generalizable.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited statistical/estimation analysis and incomplete treatment of symmetrization.
- Comparisons not entirely fair; missing some relevant baselines; limited accuracy evaluation at scale.
- Theoretical results are standard and do not push beyond O(1/T) ergodic bounds.

Overall
A practically motivated, well-engineered approach that likely advances the state of the art in scalable CLIME, with credible systems contributions. The paper would benefit from clearer estimator details (symmetry/PD), stronger experimental validation of accuracy at scale, and more comprehensive comparisons. I lean borderline accept if the target is systems-oriented scaling of convex estimators; otherwise borderline reject due to evaluation and clarity gaps.
