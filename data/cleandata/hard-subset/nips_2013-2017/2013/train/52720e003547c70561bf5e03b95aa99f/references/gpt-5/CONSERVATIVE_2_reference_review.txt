Summary
The paper targets scalable estimation of sparse precision matrices in the p ≫ n regime via the CLIME estimator. The authors propose a linearized (inexact) ADMM scheme that operates on column blocks rather than single columns, enabling the use of matrix–matrix multiplies and efficient cache-aware implementations. They exploit low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = AAᵀ) to avoid storing C and reduce multiplication cost from O(p²k) to O(npk), and describe a distributed-memory implementation using block-cyclic data distribution. They provide O(1/T) convergence rates for the objective gap (in an ergodic sense) and for residuals (non-ergodic), and discuss how sparsity in intermediate iterates or in C (via thresholding/perturbations) can further accelerate the algorithm. Experiments compare runtime and limited accuracy metrics against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and a large-scale run with p = 10⁶ on 400 cores is reported.

Relation to prior work
ADMM and linearized ADMM have been widely used for large-scale convex problems and for CLIME/graphical model estimation; column-wise decomposition of CLIME is standard. Using A rather than C and leveraging low rank when n ≪ p is also known. Block-cyclic distribution and cache-aware blocking are standard HPC practices (e.g., ScaLAPACK, GotoBLAS). The paper’s main incremental ideas are: (i) a column-block formulation specifically for CLIME combined with linearized ADMM; (ii) an engineering-focused distributed implementation with block-cyclic data distribution; and (iii) a discussion of sparsifying/perturbing C to maintain deviation bounds.

Quality
- Theory: The O(1/T) rates for the linearized ADMM variant are plausible and in line with known results. However, the statements are somewhat nonstandard for CLIME: Theorem 1 bounds the average iterate’s ℓ1 objective relative to an arbitrary feasible X, but does not quantify feasibility or statistical error. Theorem 2 provides a residual bound under η ≥ ρλ²_max(C); the induced seminorm ‖·‖²_{(η/ρ)I − C²} requires (η/ρ)I − C² ≽ 0, which is noted but provides limited practical guidance. No end-to-end bounds are given for the statistical accuracy of the final estimator under the proposed linearization and block decomposition, nor for the perturbed-C variants beyond a deviation statement.
- Perturbation/truncation of C: Theorem 3 assumes a perturbation matrix with independent sub-exponential entries; in practice, deterministic thresholding of C (a function of the data) violates independence. The “special case” justification (truncate entries with |C_ij| ≤ c√(log p/n)) needs a careful proof that the resulting C̃ still satisfies the required high-probability deviation and that the CLIME solution using C̃ achieves the same minimax rates with appropriate λ. This is currently hand-wavy.
- Algorithmic details: The choice of ρ and η is critical (η ≥ ρλ²_max(C)), but computing λ_max(C) is nontrivial at scale; practical estimation, sensitivity, and robustness are not discussed. CLIME typically requires post-symmetrization across columns; with a block-wise solver, how are inter-block symmetry and positive definiteness handled? This is not specified.
- Experiments: The comparisons focus primarily on runtime. The synthetic precision–recall plot is limited and acknowledges TIGER’s superior recall; there is no systematic accuracy evaluation at larger p or under model misspecification, nor any statistical error metrics (e.g., Frobenius, ℓ₁ norm errors) or stability analyses. On real data, only timings to match target sparsity are shown; solution quality is not assessed. Baselines: DC-QUIC (graphical lasso) solves a different estimator; the comparison is useful for speed but not for accuracy. Other strong baselines for large-scale graphical lasso/CLIME (e.g., BigQUIC and related distributed solvers) are not included. The million-dimensional experiment is impressive, but end-to-end accuracy and practical utility of the estimated graph are not demonstrated.
- Communication/computation analysis: The distributed algorithm description is sensible, but the paper lacks communication complexity, synchronization costs, and weak/strong scaling analyses beyond speedup plots. Memory footprint and I/O costs are only sketched.

Clarity
The paper is readable but has several notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., incomplete references, ambiguous norms, missing details for symmetrization and feasibility). Figures/tables are referenced but not fully described in the text. Algorithm 1 uses extrapolated quantities (2V̂_{t+1} − V̂_t) with minimal derivation. Reproducibility details (parameter choices, stopping criteria, λ selection) are insufficient.

Originality
The contributions are largely engineering-oriented combinations of known pieces: linearized ADMM for CLIME, block-cyclic distribution, and low-rank multiplications. The column-block angle is a reasonable twist but not conceptually deep. The theoretical results are incremental relative to existing ADMM literature and do not address statistical guarantees of the final estimator in a new way.

Significance
Scalability for precision estimation at p ≈ 10⁶ is practically important, and the demonstration is noteworthy. However, without stronger evidence on estimation quality, robustness, and fair comparisons to the best large-scale baselines, it is hard to assess real impact. The current form is more of a systems/implementation paper with limited methodological novelty and incomplete validation.

Pros
- Addresses an important large-scale problem with a practical, implementable framework.
- Sensible use of low-rank structure (A Aᵀ) to avoid p×p storage.
- Distributed design with block-cyclic distribution and demonstrated speedups; million-dimensional run is impressive.
- Convergence guarantees for the proposed linearized ADMM variant.

Cons
- Limited novelty; much is a combination of standard techniques.
- Theory does not provide new statistical guarantees for the final estimator; perturbation argument is weak.
- Experimental validation emphasizes speed over accuracy; limited and arguably unfair accuracy comparisons; no large-scale accuracy results.
- Missing details on parameter selection, symmetrization/PD, communication complexity, and reproducibility.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Questions for authors
1. How do you enforce symmetry and (if desired) positive definiteness across column blocks? What post-processing is used?
2. How are ρ and η chosen in practice at scale, given the λ_max(C) condition? How sensitive is convergence/speed to these choices?
3. Can you provide statistical accuracy results (e.g., estimation error, support recovery) for large p, and compare to TIGER and CLIME with proper tuning?
4. Can you justify deterministic thresholding of C rigorously (beyond independence assumptions) and quantify its impact on CLIME’s statistical guarantees?
5. What is the communication complexity per iteration in the distributed implementation? Provide strong/weak scaling with problem size and cores.
6. How does the method compare to state-of-the-art large-scale graphical lasso/CLIME solvers (e.g., BigQUIC or distributed variants)?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The system is promising and the scalability demonstration is valuable, but the methodological novelty is limited and the validation is insufficiently rigorous on statistical accuracy and fairness of baselines. Strengthening the theory for perturbed C, clarifying algorithmic details (symmetry/PD, parameter selection), and adding thorough large-scale accuracy evaluations would substantially improve the paper.
