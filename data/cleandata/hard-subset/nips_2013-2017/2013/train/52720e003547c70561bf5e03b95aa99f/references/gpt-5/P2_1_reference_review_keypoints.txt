- Proposes an inexact ADMM for CLIME that updates column blocks to exploit Level-3 BLAS (matrix–matrix) over repeated mat–vecs.
- Leverages low-rank structure C = A A' (n ≪ p) and optional truncation/sparsity to accelerate computation.
- Provides O(1/T) convergence rates for objective suboptimality and ADMM residuals under standard linearized ADMM conditions.
- Implements shared- and distributed-memory versions with block-cyclic data distribution for load balancing.
- Experiments show near-linear shared/distributed speedups and a p = 10^6 demo (~11 hours on 400 cores).
- Novelty is moderate: column-blocked specialization and distributed engineering rather than new ADMM theory or estimator.
- Perturbation/truncation analysis of C is incremental and aligns with standard covariance concentration results.
- Theory is plausible and matches known linearized ADMM analyses, but practical constants and conditions are relegated to the supplement.
- Column-block design is justified for BLAS3, cache use, and the low-rank trick CX = A(A'X) is the main practical speed driver.
- Block-cyclic distribution is a sensible HPC choice to reduce load imbalance and cache misses.
- Comparisons include CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, but baseline fairness is imperfect (DC-QUIC solves Graphical Lasso).
- Accuracy on real data is not assessed due to lack of ground truth; statistical evaluation beyond synthetic precision/recall is limited.
- Ablations on block size and core counts are helpful, but communication costs, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to η, ρ, λ are under-analyzed.
- Reproducibility would benefit from code release and more implementation details.
- Paper clarity is generally good but marred by typos, notation inconsistencies, missing factors in formulas, and incomplete references.
- Algorithm 1 needs clearer indexing, scaling, and explicit stopping criteria; derivation of the linearized step would help readability.
- Methodological contribution is primarily systems/engineering—enables million-scale CLIME in practice—which is practically significant.
- Scientific/theoretical novelty is modest; convergence guarantees align with existing ADMM results.
- Strengths: addresses major scalability bottleneck, simple implementable algorithm, strong practical scaling, sensible low-rank exploitation, and appropriate convergence guarantees.
- Weaknesses: limited methodological novelty, heavy reliance on n ≪ p, incomplete baseline coverage, missing real-data accuracy metrics, and editorial issues.
- Suggested improvements: fix algorithm/AL expressions, add communication/memory analysis, more ablations (block vs columnwise, low-rank vs full C, truncation), report parameter sensitivity, expand baselines, and clean references.
- Acceptance arguments: important practical capability, solid scalability engineering, and sound convergence analysis.
- Rejection arguments: incremental methodological novelty, under-emphasized statistical accuracy and baselines, and presentation/referencing issues.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept—valuable systems/optimization contribution with impressive scaling that requires clearer presentation and stronger empirical evaluation.
