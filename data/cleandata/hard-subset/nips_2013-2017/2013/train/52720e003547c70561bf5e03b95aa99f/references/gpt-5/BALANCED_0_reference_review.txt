Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation in the n ≪ p regime by designing a distributed solver for CLIME. The authors propose a linearized/inexact ADMM algorithm that solves CLIME in column blocks (k columns at a time) rather than column-by-column, enabling the use of BLAS-3 matrix–matrix multiplications and block-cyclic data distribution for better cache usage and load balance. They exploit low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = AAᵀ with n ≪ p) to reduce matmul cost from O(p²k) to O(npk), and discuss exploiting sparsity of iterates and of a suitably perturbed covariance. They establish O(1/T) convergence rates (ergodic objective suboptimality and residuals) under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)². Experiments show scalability on shared-memory and distributed-memory systems, including a demonstration at p = 10⁶ using 400 cores (≈11 hours), and comparisons to CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and two real datasets.

This work builds on CLIME (Cai et al.), TIGER (Liu & Wang), quadratic approximation/QUIC and DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al.), and distributed ADMM and systems frameworks (Boyd et al., Parikh & Boyd; ScaLAPACK; block-cyclic distribution). Prior CLIME solvers used interior-point or ADMM columnwise; this paper’s main new angle is a system/algorithm co-design that leverages column-blocking and low-rank multiplications for scalability.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical bottleneck: scaling precision estimation to ultra-high dimensions with principled use of distributed linear algebra (block-cyclic distribution, BLAS-3).
- Solid exploitation of low-rank structure (C = AAᵀ) to reduce per-iteration complexity; clear path to near-linear scaling with cores.
- Convergence analysis for the proposed linearized ADMM variant tailored to CLIME with explicit step-size condition.
- Demonstrates impressive scale (p = 10⁶) and provides speedup analyses on both shared and distributed settings.
- Practical engineering insights (column-block size k, cache and load balance considerations), which are likely useful to practitioners.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical correctness
- The augmented Lagrangian is presented without the usual 1/2 factor; indices and updates in Algorithm 1 (e.g., V update using 2V̂_t+1 − V̂_t) are confusing and need tighter justification. It appears to be a linearized ADMM step with an extrapolation; a precise derivation and connections to known linearized ADMM variants would help.
- Theorems 1–2 essentially restate known O(1/T) rates for (in)exact/linearized ADMM with problem-specific constants. Novelty in theory seems limited; proofs are deferred.
- Theorem 3 assumes a random perturbation ∆ independent of C, but the “special case” of deterministic thresholding depends on C. A simpler and more appropriate deterministic statement via triangle inequality could be provided; as written, the independence assumption is mismatched.
- CLIME does not enforce positive definiteness. The standard symmetrization/post-processing step to ensure a PD estimator (often used in CLIME) is not discussed, nor its computational implications at scale.

Clarity
- The pseudocode and notation have inconsistencies and typos; several references are incomplete. Important implementation details are missing: how η is chosen in practice (e.g., estimating λ_max(C)), how k is selected or auto-tuned, and how symmetrization/PD enforcement is handled.
- Some figure/table references (e.g., Table 1) are not included in the excerpt; precision/recall and runtime plots would benefit from clearer captions and axes details.

Originality
- The main contribution is an engineering/systems advance (column-block linearized ADMM + distributed matrix multiplications). Both linearized ADMM and block-cyclic distribution are well-established; the novelty is in their combination for CLIME with careful attention to cache and load balance.
- The statistical “perturb-and-threshold” idea is standard in spirit; the current formalization needs tightening (see above).

Significance
- The scale achieved is compelling, and the approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners needing ultra-large precision estimation.
- However, empirical comparisons could be stronger and fairer: TIGER is embarrassingly parallel across columns but appears to be run mostly in serial; DC-QUIC leverages block structure and could be run in distributed settings; no comparison to other large-scale logdet-based solvers (e.g., GLASSO variants) in distributed environments.
- The large-scale experiments focus on runtime; accuracy at scale (e.g., edge recovery, estimation error) is not evaluated.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong practical impact: a scalable, implementable framework with convincing system design.
- Demonstrated ability to handle unprecedented dimension with clear speedup analyses.
- Clean mapping of CLIME to matrix–matrix ops; likely reusable for other constrained problems.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical contribution is incremental; some derivations and the perturbation result are unclear/misaligned.
- Clarity and presentation issues; missing details limit reproducibility (parameter selection, PD enforcement, memory/I/O management).
- Empirical comparisons may not be entirely fair; limited assessment of statistical accuracy in large-scale settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct the ADMM derivation: include the 1/2 factor, justify the linearization and extrapolation step rigorously, and relate to known linearized ADMM results; specify exact conditions for O(1/T) (ergodic vs non-ergodic).
- Replace Theorem 3 with a deterministic bound: if ||∆||_∞ ≤ c√(log p/n), then the deviation bound for C+∆ holds with updated constants; then discuss how entrywise thresholding of C achieves such a ∆, and implications for statistical guarantees.
- Discuss PD enforcement and symmetrization (as in CLIME), its cost at scale, and how it is incorporated in the pipeline.
- Provide details and, if possible, auto-tuning heuristics for η (e.g., power iteration to estimate λ_max(C)), ρ, and k; include sensitivity analyses.
- Strengthen empirical evaluation: parallelize TIGER across columns; include distributed baselines where possible; report end-to-end memory and I/O costs; add ablations isolating the benefits of column-blocking vs columnwise, and of low-rank vs dense multiplication.
- Report estimation accuracy at larger p (e.g., edge F1, Frobenius/spectral error) to complement runtime; include warm-starts and continuation over λ.
- Improve writing and references; release code or sufficient pseudocode for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses a timely and important scaling problem with a practical and promising framework, and the large-scale results are impressive. However, the novelty beyond careful systems engineering is modest, and there are clarity issues and gaps in theory exposition and experimental fairness. Addressing the above points could elevate the work to a strong systems/ML contribution.
