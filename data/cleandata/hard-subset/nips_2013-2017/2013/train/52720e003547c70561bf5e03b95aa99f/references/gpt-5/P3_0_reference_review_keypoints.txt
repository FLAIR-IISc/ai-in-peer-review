- Proposes a scalable CLIME solver using an inexact/linearized ADMM on column blocks to turn matvecs into matrix–matrix multiplies.  
- Exploits low-rank structure C = A A^T (n ≪ p) and sparsity/truncation to reduce cost and memory.  
- Provides shared-memory and distributed implementations (block-cyclic layout) with near-linear scaling to 400 cores and a p = 10^6 run (~11 hours).  
- Gives O(1/T) ergodic convergence rates for objective and residuals under stepsize condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2.  
- Builds on CLIME and standard ADMM/linearization and leverages HPC practices (BLAS-3, ScaLAPACK); novelty is the engineered, scalable CLIME solver.  
- Strength: clear, practically motivated systems contribution enabling CLIME at unprecedented scales.  
- Strength: algorithmic design converts repeated matvecs to efficient matmuls using column blocks and BLAS-3.  
- Strength: solid parallelization with block-cyclic distribution addressing load balance and cache behavior; concrete OpenMP/OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK details.  
- Strength: provides convergence analysis with an explicit stepsize condition.  
- Strength: strong empirical scalability and demonstrations on synthetic/real data, including low-rank exploitation without forming C.  
- Weakness: probable inconsistency/bug in X-update scaling—definition of V_t appears incorrect and conflicts with linearized ADMM; needs correction/clarification.  
- Weakness: Theorem 3 analyzes random sub-exponential perturbations but does not directly justify the deterministic hard-threshold truncation used in practice.  
- Weakness: paper omits how final CLIME estimates are symmetrized and does not discuss positive-definiteness or downstream implications.  
- Weakness: missing practical details on choosing ρ, η, λ, stopping criteria, tolerances, and how λ_max(C) is estimated at scale.  
- Weakness: baselines and comparisons are limited/unfair—TIGER may be parallelizable, DC-QUIC targets a different objective, and BIGQUIC/QUIC comparisons on smaller p are missing.  
- Weakness: million-dimensional run reports time only; lacks estimation-accuracy metrics (KKT residuals, constraint violation, sparsity, support recovery).  
- Weakness: clarity issues—typos, incomplete references, and inconsistent notation (box(·) and algorithm steps) hinder reproducibility.  
- Quality: theory is standard/reasonable but depends on resolving the X-update scaling; systems design aligns with HPC best practices.  
- Empirics: scalability is strong, but accuracy comparisons are limited and partially mismatched across methods.  
- Originality: methodologically incremental, but the integrated, optimized distributed implementation and low-rank strategy are novel and useful.  
- Significance: high practical impact—enables CLIME at previously infeasible scales for practitioners and distributed-solver research.  
- Key author action items: fix/clarify V_t and X-update scaling, formalize deterministic truncation bounds, specify symmetrization/PD postprocessing, detail parameter selection and stopping rules, and report accuracy metrics at large p.  
- Questions for authors: confirm V_t definition (is V_t = (ρ/η) C(2Y^t − Y^{t−1})?), how ρ/η/λ and λ_max(C) are chosen/estimated, how symmetrization/PD is handled, what stopping criteria/tolerances are used, and can large-p accuracy/support recovery be reported?  
- Pros for acceptance: strong systems contribution, clear speedups, near-linear distributed scaling, and reasonable convergence guarantees.  
- Cons for acceptance: algorithmic clarity bug risk, limited/unfair baselines, and incomplete discussion of symmetrization and parameter selection.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept conditional on resolving the V_t scaling issue and addressing parameter/stopping/symmetrization and accuracy reporting; otherwise borderline.  
- Reviewer confidence: high on optimization and distributed aspects; medium on statistical perturbation/truncation claims.
