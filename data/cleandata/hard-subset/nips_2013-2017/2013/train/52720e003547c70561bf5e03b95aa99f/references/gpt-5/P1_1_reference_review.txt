Summary and contributions
The paper tackles ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation via CLIME, proposing a scalable inexact ADMM algorithm and distributed framework. The core ideas are:
- Reformulate CLIME as a constrained problem with a splitting variable Z, and solve via an inexact/linearized ADMM so that:
  - X-update is decoupled and has a closed-form soft-thresholding step.
  - Z-update is a box projection with a closed form.
  - Only two matrix multiplications per iteration are required.
- Solve CLIME in column blocks (k columns at a time), enabling matrix–matrix multiplies that exploit cache and BLAS-3 efficiencies and can be parallelized over shared and distributed memory with a block-cyclic distribution.
- Leverage problem structure:
  - Low-rank structure: use C = A A’ with n << p to reduce O(p^2 k) multiplies to O(n p k).
  - Sparsity: exploit sparsity in iterates, and optionally sparsify C by thresholding small entries while preserving CLIME’s deviation bounds; a theorem shows sub-exponential perturbations preserve the sup-norm deviation rate.
- Provide O(1/T) convergence rate of objective suboptimality and primal residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2.
- Implement shared-memory and distributed-memory frameworks (OpenMP/OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK) and demonstrate near-linear speedups, including a run with p=10^6 (≈10^12 parameters) in ~11 hours on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CLIME (Cai et al.) and prior ADMM solvers for CLIME and graphical lasso, and on distributed optimization frameworks. Prior CLIME solvers often worked column-by-column and did not address ultra-large scales or distributed settings. This paper contributes an inexact ADMM tailored to CLIME, a column-block strategy to exploit matrix–matrix multiplies, and a distributed block-cyclic implementation drawing on ideas from high-performance computing. The O(1/T) rates mirror standard ADMM/linearized ADMM results but are instantiated for this CLIME splitting. The optional covariance perturbation connects to screening/thresholding ideas in large-scale covariance estimation.

Strengths
- A clear algorithmic design that turns CLIME into operations amenable to BLAS-3 kernels and distribution. The column-block approach is compelling and practically effective.
- Solid convergence analysis with explicit conditions on η and ρ.
- Careful systems considerations: block-cyclic distribution, process grids, parallel I/O, and cache-aware block sizes.
- Strong empirical scalability: both shared and distributed settings, detailed speedup analyses, including a very large p=10^6 demonstration.
- Pragmatic exploitation of low-rank structure inherent in n << p regimes, avoiding explicit storage of C.

Weaknesses and questions
- Comparisons emphasize runtime at matched sparsity levels; accuracy comparisons are limited. On synthetic data, precision–recall is shown, but on real data only runtime is reported for target sparsities. It would be preferable to report estimation error metrics (e.g., spectral/Frobenius error to ground truth on synthetic), or graph recovery AUC, and runtime-accuracy tradeoffs.
- Fairness of baselines: DC-QUIC operates on thresholded block-diagonal approximations; TIGER is tuning-insensitive with different statistical behavior than CLIME. Including a CLIME solver using the same low-rank multiplies (if available) or a strong graphical lasso solver would strengthen claims.
- Statistical perturbation of C: Theorem 3 claims sub-exponential perturbations preserve the sup-norm deviation bound with high probability. However:
  - The constants and dependence on perturbation scale are not explicit, and it seems λ may need to be adjusted to accommodate extra noise; this is not discussed.
  - The truncation strategy relies on knowledge of the deviation bound constant c; in practice c is unknown. How is the threshold chosen?
  - Does the perturbation preserve positive semidefiniteness? (Not required by CLIME, but may matter if reused elsewhere.)
- Symmetrization: CLIME typically symmetrizes the column-wise solution to yield a symmetric precision estimate. The paper does not describe the symmetrization step or its cost in the distributed setting.
- Hyperparameters and stopping: Experimental details about ρ, η (and how λ_max(C) is estimated), stopping criteria, and number of iterations are missing. These are important for reproducibility and to assess the impact of block size k on iteration counts.
- Sensitivity to n: The speedups rely heavily on n << p. It would be useful to report performance when n is moderately large (e.g., n ~ 0.1 p), where O(p^2 k) multiplies are unavoidable.
- Presentation: References are incomplete, with missing years/venues; some notation issues (e.g., factors of 1/2 in augmented Lagrangian), and typos make it harder to verify details.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithm and convergence analysis are plausible and align with known linearized ADMM results; the condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 ensures positive semidefiniteness in Theorem 2. The perturbation theorem is plausible but underspecified; more details are needed to rely on it in practice.
- Empirical evaluation: Strong for scalability, weaker for statistical accuracy and fairness of comparisons.

Clarity
- The high-level ideas and algorithm are understandable, but the paper would benefit from clearer notation, complete references, and a precise description of the final symmetrization, parameter choices, and stopping rules. Some figures/tables are referenced but not fully shown in the excerpt.

Originality
- Combining CLIME with a linearized ADMM, block-column updates, and a distributed block-cyclic implementation tailored to low-rank covariance multiplies is a novel and useful integration. Theoretical rates are standard; the perturbation result is a small theoretical addition but needs tightening.

Significance
- The ability to scale CLIME to p in the millions, with near-linear speedups, is practically significant for large-scale graphical model learning under n << p. The engineering contributions are likely to be useful to practitioners and as a template for other constrained estimators.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report accuracy-runtime tradeoffs on synthetic data with known ground truth (e.g., Frobenius/spectral norms, Hamming error) and PR/AUC, and include real-data stability measures.
- Detail symmetrization strategy and its cost.
- Provide hyperparameter selection, stopping criteria, and iteration counts; discuss sensitivity to ρ and η.
- Clarify and strengthen Theorem 3: state constants, how λ should be adjusted, and provide a practical thresholding rule; discuss PSD implications.
- Discuss performance when n is larger; quantify communication overhead and memory footprints.
- Improve presentation and complete references.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a principled and effective approach.
- Strong systems design with demonstrated large-scale runs and near-linear speedups.
- Clear mapping of CLIME to operations amenable to distributed HPC.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited accuracy evaluation and fairness of baselines.
- Some theoretical claims (covariance perturbation) are underspecified.
- Missing practical details (symmetrization, parameter/stopping choices) affect reproducibility.

Overall: A compelling systems/methods paper that substantially advances the scalability of sparse precision estimation. With strengthened experimental accuracy analysis and clarified theoretical/practical details, it would be a strong NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
