

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM approach for the CLIME estimator. The authors claim to scale to million-dimensional problems (1 trillion parameters) using 400 cores in 11 hours. The key innovations include an inexact ADMM formulation for CLIME, column-block processing to leverage matrix-matrix multiplications, and a block cyclic distribution strategy for load balancing. The paper includes theoretical convergence guarantees and extensive experiments comparing with existing methods.

## Strengths
- The paper addresses a significant practical problem: scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions.
- The column-block approach is a reasonable extension of existing methods and provides a clear path to distributed implementation.
- The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) provide useful convergence guarantees.
- The experimental results demonstrate impressive scalability, particularly in the distributed-memory setting.
- The paper identifies important practical considerations like sparse structure exploitation and low-rank approximations.

## Weaknesses
The paper contains numerous technical and formatting issues that undermine its credibility and completeness:

1. **Formatting and Notation Errors**: The paper contains multiple inconsistent and incorrect mathematical notations:
   - "Ω̂ = Σ−10" appears multiple times (should be Ω̂ = Σ₀⁻¹)
   - "p2 = 1012" (should be p² = 10¹²)
   - "n p" (should be n ≪ p)
   - "Sp++" (should be S₊₊ᵖ)
   - "‖X‖22" (should be ‖X‖₂²)
   - "1η" (should be 1/η)
   - "c1 √ log p n" (should be c₁√(log p/n))

2. **Algorithmic Errors**: 
   - In Algorithm 1, step 5: "Xt+1 = soft(Xt −Vt, 1η )" should be "Xt+1 = soft(Xt − Vt, 1/η)" (missing slash).
   - In Algorithm 1, step 9: "V̂t+1 = CYt+1" should be "V̂t+1 = C'Yt+1" (transpose missing).
   - In Theorem 2, the expression "‖XT −XT−1‖2η ρ I−C2" is unclear and should be properly formatted as "‖XT − XT−1‖₂²/(ηρ)‖I − C‖₂²" or similar.

3. **Missing Details**:
   - The paper claims "For proofs, please see Appendix A in the supplement" but provides no supplement, making it impossible to verify the theoretical claims.
   - The paper states "CLIME-ADMM is shown to be much faster than existing methods" without quantitative comparisons (e.g., "X times faster").
   - The paper claims "scales almost linearly" without defining what "almost linear" means (e.g., 90% of linear speedup?).

4. **Technical Inconsistencies**:
   - In Section 3.2: "Since C = AAT and p n" should be "p ≫ n" (not p n).
   - The paper claims to use "low rank structure" but doesn't explain how the low-rank approximation is obtained in practice.
   - The paper references "block cyclic distribution" but doesn't cite the seminal work by [16, 15, 5] that established this technique.

5. **Experimental Details**:
   - The paper claims to estimate a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores but provides no details on the machine configuration (CPU speed, memory, network bandwidth).
   - The comparison with Tiger on the Leukemia dataset states "Tiger is faster than CLIME-ADMM on small dimensional dataset Leukemia" but doesn't provide specific numbers or time comparisons.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem and makes a reasonable extension of existing methods, the numerous technical and formatting errors significantly undermine the credibility of the work. The paper fails to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (missing supplement, unclear notation), contains algorithmic errors, and makes claims without proper supporting evidence (e.g., "much faster," "almost linear speedup"). The errors are not merely superficial but affect the mathematical correctness of the proposed method and the interpretation of results.

NIPS is a venue that values both technical correctness and clear presentation. The paper's numerous minor but substantive errors, combined with the lack of a supplement to verify the theoretical claims, make it difficult to assess the actual contribution of this work. Given these issues, I recommend rejection.

## Pro/Con Summary
**Pro:**
- Addresses a significant practical problem in high-dimensional statistics
- Reasonable extension of existing CLIME and ADMM approaches
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees
- Demonstrates impressive scalability in experiments

**Con:**
- Numerous inconsistent and incorrect mathematical notations
- Algorithmic errors in the proposed method
- Missing supplement for theoretical proofs
- Lack of quantitative comparison claims ("much faster")
- Unclear definition of "almost linear" speedup
- Missing experimental details (machine configuration, specific speedups)

The paper's technical flaws are substantial enough to outweigh its potential contribution, especially given NIPS' emphasis on rigorous presentation and technical correctness.
