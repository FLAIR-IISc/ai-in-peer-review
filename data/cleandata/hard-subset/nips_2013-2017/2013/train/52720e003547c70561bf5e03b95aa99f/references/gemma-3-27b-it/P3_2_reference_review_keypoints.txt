- Tackles scaling sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation to ultra-high dimensions via a distributed CLIME implementation.
- Key algorithmic contributions: an inexact ADMM variant, block-wise solution leveraging matrix-matrix multiplications, and block-cyclic data distribution for load balancing.
- Theoretical contributions include convergence rate analyses (Theorems 1 & 2) that appear rigorous.
- Strong empirical scalability: claims estimating a million-dimensional precision matrix (trillion parameters) in ~11 hours on 400 cores.
- Detailed performance analysis across shared and distributed memory architectures with clear speedup plots.
- Practical relevance: enables previously intractable high-dimensional precision estimation for real applications.
- Novelty in combining inexact ADMM, block-wise CLIME, and system-level optimizations for constrained global problems.
- Solid attention to implementation details (block-cyclic distribution, cache and load balancing considerations).
- Paper is dense and notation-heavy; needs clearer explanations, diagrams, and possibly a glossary.
- Limited discussion of limitations—sensitivity to block size, sparsity pattern, and regularization parameter λ needs more analysis.
- Comparison to other distributed precision-estimation methods is insufficient.
- Real-world experiments are limited (only Leukemia and Climate datasets); broader, larger datasets would strengthen validation.
- Section 3.1 (perturbing sample covariance) and Theorem 3 feel isolated and need clearer practical interpretation.
- Section 4’s explanation of block-cyclic distribution requires visual aids to improve clarity.
- Algorithm 1 would benefit from rationale for parameter choices (η and ρ).
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on improving clarity and expanding discussion of limitations and comparisons.
