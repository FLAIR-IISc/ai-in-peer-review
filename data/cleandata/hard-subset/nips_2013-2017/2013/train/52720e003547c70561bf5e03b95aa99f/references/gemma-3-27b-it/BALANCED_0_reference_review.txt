This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation, a core task in statistical inference and machine learning. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator and propose a distributed ADMM variant, CLIME-ADMM, designed to scale to millions of dimensions and leverage thousands of cores. The key innovations lie in an inexact ADMM formulation tailored for CLIME, solving the problem in column-blocks, and optimizing matrix multiplication via exploiting inherent low-rank structure.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presenting the limitations of existing methods for ultra-high dimensional precision matrix estimation and positioning CLIME-ADMM as a solution. The theoretical contributions, establishing convergence rates, are valuable. The experimental results are comprehensive, showcasing scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, and demonstrating substantial speedups compared to existing methods, culminating in the estimation of a one million dimension precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores. The authors appropriately relate their work to the broader trend of scaling up machine learning algorithms and provide a thorough overview of related work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The problem of large-scale precision matrix estimation is important and widely applicable.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm is technically well-founded, building on established ADMM techniques and addressing specific challenges of CLIME. The convergence analysis provides a strong theoretical basis.
*   **Scalability:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the scalability of CLIME-ADMM to unprecedented dimensions, showcasing significant speedups with increasing core counts.
*   **Innovation:** The combination of inexact ADMM, column-block solving, and leveraging low-rank structure represents a novel approach to tackling this problem.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The extensive experimental evaluation across different architectures and datasets provides strong empirical validation.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison Depth:** While comparisons are made to CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC, the parameter tuning details for these competitors are not always explicitly stated. A more rigorous comparison, ensuring a fair playing field, would strengthen the results. For instance, it's mentioned Tiger achieves better recall, but doesn't delve into why (parameter selection) besides being "tuning-free".
*   **Low-Rank Assumption:** While exploiting low-rank structure is a good idea, the paper assumes it without extensive justification. A discussion of the prevalence of low-rank structure in real-world covariance matrices, or analysis of the empirical low-rankness of the datasets used, would be beneficial.
*   **Block Size Selection:** The paper mentions optimizing block size, but lacks a detailed discussion of how the optimal block size is determined in practice. Providing guidelines or a rule of thumb for selecting appropriate block sizes would be helpful for practitioners.
*   **Memory Requirements:** The memory requirement of O(pn) is mentioned, but its practical implications for extremely large 'p' and 'n' aren't fully explored. Are there further memory optimization techniques that could be applied?
*   **Implementation Details:** While the authors mention the use of OpenMP and ScaLAPACK, more details about the specific implementation choices (e.g., BLAS library used, communication strategies in the distributed environment) would enhance reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel and technically sound solution.
*   Demonstrates significant scalability and performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Provides a strong theoretical foundation with convergence analysis.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Comparison to competing methods could be more rigorous and detailed.
*   Reliance on the low-rank assumption requires further justification.
*   Lack of detailed guidance on block size selection.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale precision matrix estimation. The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm is well-designed, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Provide more detailed information on parameter tuning for comparison methods.
*   Discuss the prevalence of low-rank structure in covariance matrices and/or analyze the empirical low-rankness of the datasets.
*   Elaborate on the guidelines for selecting optimal block sizes.
*   Address the practical implications of the O(pn) memory requirement.
*   Provide more implementation details to enhance reproducibility.
*   Briefly discuss potential extensions of this work to other constrained optimization problems.
