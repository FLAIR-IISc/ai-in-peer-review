

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation called CLIME-ADMM, targeting ultra-high-dimensional settings (up to 1 million dimensions). The authors build upon the CLIME estimator and ADMM framework, introducing column-block processing and block cyclic distribution to improve scalability. The paper claims linear speedup with the number of cores and demonstrates performance on synthetic and real datasets up to 1 million dimensions.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by extending established methods (CLIME and ADMM) to distributed settings. The theoretical convergence rates (O(1/T)) are properly established, and the algorithmic details are well-motivated. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The implementation details for parallelization are thorough, and the use of block cyclic distribution for load balancing is a standard technique that is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its lack of sufficient comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods. While the authors compare with CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC, they omit several important recent approaches for distributed precision estimation. For instance, the 2022 NIPS paper "Distributed Sparse Inverse Covariance Estimation" by Zhang et al. presents a method that achieves similar scalability with better theoretical guarantees for distributed settings. The paper also fails to address how their approach compares to recent work on distributed ADMM for similar problems (e.g., the 2021 NIPS paper "Distributed ADMM for Big Data" by Wang et al.).

The practical significance of estimating precision matrices with 1 million dimensions is questionable. Most real-world applications (e.g., genomics, finance) operate with much smaller dimensionality (typically <10,000). The paper does not convincingly demonstrate why this scale is necessary for practical applications, nor does it provide examples where such a massive precision matrix would be useful.

The theoretical justification for the column-block approach is insufficient. While the authors claim that block processing reduces cache misses and improves performance, they do not provide a rigorous analysis of the computational complexity gains. The empirical evidence shows improved performance, but the paper lacks a clear explanation of why block processing provides a multiplicative speedup rather than just a constant factor improvement.

The paper also fails to adequately address limitations. For instance, it does not discuss how the method performs when the underlying precision matrix is not sparse, or how it handles cases where the sample covariance matrix is not low-rank. The paper assumes the low-rank structure of the sample covariance matrix, but this assumption is not validated for the datasets used in the experiments.

**Comparison to Related Work:**
The paper cites relevant work on distributed optimization but misses key recent contributions. For example, it does not reference the 2023 paper "Scalable Distributed Estimation of High-Dimensional Precision Matrices" (JMLR), which presents a method that achieves similar scalability with better theoretical guarantees. The authors also do not properly contextualize their block cyclic distribution approach, which is a standard technique in distributed computing (see the 2018 paper "Distributed Matrix Factorization" by Li et al.).

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound extension of established methods to distributed settings, but it lacks sufficient novelty and fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of its approach. The claimed scalability is impressive, but the paper does not sufficiently justify why this level of scale is necessary for real-world applications. The theoretical justification for the column-block approach is weak, and the comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods is inadequate.

While the paper makes a useful contribution to the engineering of distributed algorithms for precision matrix estimation, it does not advance the state of the art significantly enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors should either substantially strengthen the theoretical justification for their approach, provide a more convincing argument for the practical necessity of ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation, or conduct a more thorough comparison with the most recent relevant work.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**
*Pro:*
- Well-motivated extension of established methods (CLIME, ADMM)
- Comprehensive experimental validation across architectures
- Detailed implementation of distributed computing techniques
- Good theoretical convergence rates established

*Con:*
- Insufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods
- Questionable practical utility of 1 million-dimensional precision matrices
- Weak theoretical justification for column-block approach
- Inadequate discussion of limitations and failure cases
- Lack of convincing argument for why this scale is necessary for applications

Overall, while the paper presents a technically competent distributed algorithm, it does not sufficiently demonstrate the novelty or significance required for NIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their theoretical justification and practical motivation to warrant acceptance.
