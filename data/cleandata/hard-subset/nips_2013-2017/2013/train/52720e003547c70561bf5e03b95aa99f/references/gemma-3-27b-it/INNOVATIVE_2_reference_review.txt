This paper tackles the important and increasingly relevant problem of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions. The authors propose CLIME-ADMM, a distributed framework built on an inexact ADMM variant of the CLIME estimator, and demonstrate impressive scalability – estimating a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores. This is a significant achievement and the core contribution of the paper. The key innovations – inexact ADMM tailored to CLIME, column-block updates leveraging matrix multiplication efficiencies, and a block cyclic distribution scheme for parallel computation – are all well-motivated and appear crucial to the observed performance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though dense. The mathematical derivations are reasonably clear, and the experimental setup is described adequately. The authors do a good job of positioning their work within the broader context of large-scale machine learning and related work in distributed optimization. The inclusion of theoretical convergence guarantees, even if standard, adds to the rigor.

The strength of this work lies squarely in its *scalability*.  It directly addresses a bottleneck in high-dimensional statistical inference. While the individual components (ADMM, CLIME, block coordinate descent) aren't groundbreaking on their own, the combination and careful engineering for distributed computation *is* novel and impactful. The experimental results, particularly the 1 million-dimension example, are compelling.  The attention to detail regarding data distribution and memory access optimization (block cyclic distribution) is impressive.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while present, feels somewhat standard and doesn’t fully capture the nuances of the distributed setting and inexact ADMM. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between inexactness and convergence speed would be valuable. The comparison to existing methods, while present, feels somewhat limited.  For example, a more thorough comparison to other distributed sparse solvers (even those not specifically for CLIME) would be helpful.  The practical impact of the sparse and low-rank structure exploitation (section 3) feels slightly undersold. The authors mention benefits, but the extent to which these actually contribute to the observed performance isn't quantified. The empirical evaluation mostly focuses on synthetic data. While this allows for controlled experimentation, showcasing performance on a wider range of real-world datasets would strengthen the claims of practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Significant Scalability:** The demonstrated ability to handle a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix is a major accomplishment.
* **Novel Combination:** The combination of CLIME, ADMM, column-block updates, and a tailored distributed framework is a significant contribution.
* **Practical Relevance:** Addresses a critical bottleneck in high-dimensional statistical inference.
* **Sound Methodology:** The approach is well-motivated and supported by theoretical convergence guarantees.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Theoretical Depth:** The theoretical analysis could be more tailored to the distributed and inexact settings.
* **Restricted Empirical Evaluation:**  More real-world datasets would be beneficial.
* **Understated Impact of Optimizations:** The precise benefit of sparse and low-rank exploitation isn't clearly quantified.
* **Comparison to other distributed solvers:** Comparison with more relevant baselines could strengthen the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and impactful solution. While the theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation could be further strengthened, the demonstrated scalability and the ingenuity of the approach are compelling. I lean heavily toward acceptance. The paper presents a substantial advance in the field and has the potential to enable the analysis of datasets that were previously intractable. I believe the novelty and potential impact outweigh the minor shortcomings.



**Rating:** Accept with minor revisions.  I suggest the authors consider expanding the empirical evaluation to include more real-world datasets and providing a more detailed analysis of the benefits derived from the sparse and low-rank structure assumptions. A discussion about potential limitations and future research directions would also be valuable.
