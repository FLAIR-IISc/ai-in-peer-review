Summary
The paper targets large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation in the n << p regime by proposing a distributed, column-block ADMM variant for CLIME. The main ideas are: (i) linearized (inexact) ADMM X-updates to avoid per-iteration Lasso solves; (ii) solving multiple columns jointly (column blocks) to enable BLAS-3 matrix–matrix multiplications; (iii) exploiting low rank of the sample covariance by using C = A A^T and computing CX as A(A^T X); (iv) implementing shared- and distributed-memory parallelization via block-cyclic data distribution; and (v) stating O(1/T) convergence rates for the objective gap and residuals. The authors also discuss potential speedups from iterate and sample-covariance sparsity, and propose a perturbation/thresholding argument to justify sparsifying C. Experiments show speedups over CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and small real datasets, and a scaling demo to p = 10^6 on 400 cores.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant scalability bottleneck for CLIME by turning columnwise LPs into an ADMM scheme composed of simple, parallelizable primitives.
- The column-block design and block-cyclic distribution are sound engineering choices that leverage BLAS-3, cache efficiency, and load balance; the shared/distributed implementations appear competent, with clear speedup trends.
- The convergence analysis (O(1/T) bounds) is standard but appropriate for the proposed linearized ADMM variant.
- The low-rank trick CX = A(A^T X) is a practical way to reduce complexity when n is small, and is effectively used in experiments.
- Empirical speedups are substantial; the paper demonstrates near-linear scaling in the distributed setting and includes a large-scale p = 10^6 run.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: Linearized/inexact ADMM for composite problems, column-blocking to exploit BLAS-3, and block-cyclic distribution are well-known. The theory (O(1/T)) is standard for this class of methods. The work reads primarily as a systems/engineering paper for CLIME rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution.
- Methodological gaps:
  - Choice of parameters (λ, ρ, η), stopping criteria, and their impact on accuracy/runtime are not specified. The condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 requires estimating λ_max(C); how is this done at scale?
  - CLIME does not enforce positive definiteness. The standard symmetrization/post-processing steps (and their cost and effect on statistical performance) are not discussed.
  - Communication complexity is not analyzed; a simple cost model relating k, grid shape, and communication would be important for predicting scalability and guiding practitioners.
- Statistical validation is weak:
  - Comparisons tune each method to match a target sparsity, which can bias the evaluation and obscures accuracy-time tradeoffs. No likelihood or held-out risk, no support recovery AUC, and no error norms to ground truth are reported (except a single precision–recall plot where TIGER dominates recall).
  - The large-scale (p = 10^6) demo reports only runtime; no measure of solution quality (support size, NNZ, symmetrization, held-out log-likelihood) is provided. Claiming “one trillion parameters” is misleading without reporting actual NNZ and storage; a dense 10^12-parameter object is infeasible to store.
- Questionable perturbation/sparsification argument:
  - Theorem 3 states that adding independent sub-exponential noise Δ preserves the sup-norm deviation rate. Without explicit control of ‖Δ‖∞ at the √(log p / n) scale and a dependence on its tail parameters, the statement is too loose; moreover, (C + Δ) may lose PSD, and independence across entries ignores covariance structure. The practical takeaway reduces to standard hard-thresholding of small entries of C, which is already known; this section feels both underspecified and not used in experiments.
- Clarity and correctness issues:
  - Notation and algorithmic inconsistencies (e.g., box(·) definition with/without E; norms switch between elementwise and operator-weighted; the term ‖·‖_{(η/ρ)I − C^2} requires positivity that depends on λ_max(C); some lines/variables in Algorithm 1 are hard to follow).
  - Several references are incomplete or duplicated; some prior scalable graphical model solvers are not adequately contrasted.
  - The paper would benefit from a cleaner, reproducible description of the implementation (I/O layout, memory footprint per process, output format).

Quality
Technically competent in terms of optimization and parallel implementation, but the statistical side is under-evaluated, and some claims (perturbation theorem, 10^12 parameters) are not carefully substantiated. The theoretical results are standard. Experimental validation focuses on speed, with insufficient accuracy metrics and incomplete baselines.

Clarity
Readable overall, but with multiple notational/algorithmic inconsistencies and incomplete references. Important practical details (parameters, stopping, communication) are missing. Reproducibility is limited.

Originality
Incremental. The combination (CLIME + linearized ADMM + column blocks + block-cyclic distribution) is a reasonable engineering synthesis, but not a novel algorithmic or theoretical contribution.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners who need to scale CLIME, especially when n << p and distributed resources are available. However, without stronger evidence on statistical quality and more comprehensive comparisons, the impact is limited.

Questions for the authors
- How are λ, ρ, and η selected in practice? What stopping criteria are used? Please report sensitivity studies.
- Do you perform the standard CLIME symmetrization/refitting? If so, how is it implemented at scale, and what is its cost and effect?
- Provide a communication/memory cost model per iteration as functions of p, n, k, and the process grid. What are the dominant bottlenecks?
- For the p = 10^6 run: what are n, λ, final NNZ of Ω, storage format, and held-out log-likelihood? How is output written and validated?
- Can you add AUC or likelihood-based metrics and report time–accuracy tradeoffs against TIGER and DC-QUIC?

Pros
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a practical, parallel implementation.
- Solid engineering choices and clear speedups; distributed scaling is convincing.
- Straightforward algorithm with standard convergence guarantees.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; standard theory.
- Weak statistical validation; large-scale demo lacks quality metrics.
- Questionable/underdeveloped perturbation theorem; not used empirically.
- Missing methodological details and clarity issues.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper makes a useful engineering contribution, but the novelty is limited and the empirical/statistical validation does not meet NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the statistical evaluation, clarifying methodology and communication costs, and tightening the theory around sparsification would substantially improve the work.
