Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation (Gaussian graphical models) in the n ≪ p regime by developing a scalable distributed algorithm for the CLIME estimator. It reformulates CLIME’s LP with a variable split and solves the resulting constrained problem via an inexact/linearized ADMM that updates column blocks of the precision matrix simultaneously. Key design choices are: (i) a linearized X-update with soft-thresholding; (ii) a box projection for Z; (iii) exploiting low-rank structure C = AA′ to reduce matrix multiplications from O(p2k) to O(npk); and (iv) block-cyclic data distribution to balance load and minimize cache misses on shared- and distributed-memory systems. The paper derives O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality (ergodic) and residuals (non-ergodic) under η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2. It discusses leveraging sparsity of iterates and of (possibly perturbed) sample covariances, offers a perturbation argument for thresholding C, and presents experiments comparing to flare’s ADMM implementations (CLIME/TIGER) and DC-QUIC, as well as strong scaling studies. The authors report solving p = 10^6 with 400 cores in ~11 hours.

This work connects to a line of optimization for sparse inverse covariance estimation: graphical lasso (e.g., Friedman et al.; Ravikumar et al.), QUIC/DC-QUIC (Hsieh et al.), and CLIME (Cai et al.). It builds on ADMM literature (Boyd et al.) and distributed ML frameworks, emphasizing that CLIME’s separable structure and lack of PD constraint are advantageous at scale.

Strengths
- Addresses an important systems problem: scaling sparse precision estimation to the million-variable regime with careful algorithm–systems co-design.
- Sound use of inexact/linearized ADMM yielding closed-form elementwise updates and matrix–matrix multiplies amenable to high-performance kernels; O(1/T) rates are standard but appropriate and stated clearly.
- Sensible exploitation of C’s low rank (n ≪ p), reducing the computational bottleneck, and a pragmatic block-cyclic distribution for load balancing.
- Empirical scalability demonstrated across shared- and distributed-memory environments, with clear speedup analyses and a compelling p = 10^6 case study.
- Useful discussion on leveraging iterate sparsity and on covariance thresholding to induce sparsity in C.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- The theory covers algorithmic convergence but not the statistical properties of the returned estimator under the inexact/linearized updates and finite iterations. The results on objective suboptimality are ergodic (in X̄T), whereas the algorithm outputs the last iterate Xt; practitioners need guidance on whether to average and how this affects statistical guarantees.
- The condition η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2 is required, but there is no guidance for choosing (ρ, η) in practice or estimating λmax(C) efficiently at scale (e.g., via power iterations on A).
- The covariance perturbation argument (Theorem 3): the random independent sub-exponential ∆ model is a mismatch with the deterministic thresholding procedure subsequently advocated; the special-case justification reduces to a triangle inequality bound, but the presentation is misleading. Also, truncation based on C itself induces dependence that the theorem’s assumptions do not cover.
- No discussion of symmetrization/PD correction for CLIME outputs. In practice, CLIME requires symmetrization and often a PD projection for downstream tasks; the computational and statistical effects at scale are not reported.
- Experimental comparisons prioritize runtime and sparsity matching but do not report estimation error (e.g., Frobenius/spectral norm, Hamming error) vs. n, nor downstream likelihoods. TIGER is claimed minimax and indeed performs well in recall; however, there is no evaluation across sample sizes, graph structures, or noise levels.
- Fairness/coverage of baselines: comparisons are on a single machine; distributed baselines such as BigQUIC (Hsieh et al., KDD’14) are not included. DC-QUIC’s dependence on block-diagonal structure is explained, but a version exploiting block structure within your own framework would be a natural ablation. Memory footprint and communication time breakdowns are not quantified.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized, but there are several notation/dimension slips (e.g., in Section 4, U is stated as n×k though U = CX should be p×k; some shapes around W, U are inconsistent). Algorithm 1’s V-update over-relaxation (2 V̂t+1 − V̂t) is not motivated or cited.
- Important implementation details are deferred or omitted: precise stopping criteria; parameter choices (ρ, η, λ); whether column-block solutions are symmetrized across blocks; and how k is selected beyond heuristic cache/process-grid considerations.
- References have many missing bibliographic details.

Originality
- Using ADMM for CLIME and linearizing to obtain closed-form updates is known; O(1/T) rates are standard in the ADMM literature. The main novelty lies in the systems aspects: column-block updates with matrix–matrix multiplies, careful low-rank exploitation, and block-cyclic distribution. This constitutes a solid engineering contribution but limited algorithmic novelty.

Significance
- For practitioners facing p up to 10^6 and n small, a robust, distributed CLIME implementation with demonstrated scaling is valuable. However, the lack of statistical accuracy assessments and missing comparisons to more recent large-scale glasso solvers limit the broader impact. The framework likely generalizes to other constrained problems, which is promising.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
1. Provide guidance and ablations on choosing ρ and η; report sensitivity and an efficient procedure to estimate/upper-bound λmax(C) at scale.
2. Clarify whether outputs are averaged (ergodic) or last-iterate; if the latter, provide non-ergodic objective guarantees or empirical evidence that last-iterate is sufficient statistically.
3. Include symmetrization/PD projection steps and report their cost and effect on accuracy and runtime.
4. Tighten the covariance perturbation section: replace Theorem 3 with a deterministic truncation bound via triangle inequality and high-probability bounds on C − Σ0; discuss dependence issues explicitly.
5. Expand experimental evaluation: estimation error vs. n; robustness across graph topologies and edge densities; memory/communication breakdown; and comparisons against distributed/quasi-distributed graphical lasso solvers (e.g., BigQUIC) if feasible.
6. Fix dimension typos and fully specify Algorithm 1 (over-relaxation rationale, stopping, parameter selection).
7. Discuss how k should be chosen given cache sizes and process grids; provide a simple heuristic or autotuning.
8. Report the n used in the million-dimensional run and the per-node memory footprint.

Pros
- Scalable, practical framework with strong engineering.
- Solid convergence rates and a clean algorithm.
- Clear speedup gains and demonstration at unprecedented p.

Cons
- Limited algorithmic novelty; missing statistical accuracy evaluations.
- Parameter selection and symmetrization issues not addressed.
- Some clarity/notation problems; incomplete baselines.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is promising and close to being a strong systems contribution; addressing the statistical evaluation, parameter/symmetrization details, and a clearer, more rigorous treatment of the covariance sparsification would substantially strengthen it.
