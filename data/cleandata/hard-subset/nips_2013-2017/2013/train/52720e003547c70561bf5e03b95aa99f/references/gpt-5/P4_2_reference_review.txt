Summary
The paper targets ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation, focusing on the CLIME estimator, which solves a column-separable L∞-constrained l1 LP. The authors propose a linearized (inexact) ADMM algorithm that updates blocks of columns (size k) jointly rather than one column at a time. The block formulation enables two key computational strategies: (i) replacing repeated matrix–vector multiplies with matrix–matrix multiplies that better exploit cache hierarchies and BLAS-3 efficiency, and (ii) leveraging the low-rank structure of the sample covariance C = A A^T (with n ≪ p) so CX can be computed as A(ATX) at cost O(npk). They further propose exploiting sparsity during iterations and a statistically justified “coarsening” of C by sup-norm thresholding to induce sparsity while preserving the CLIME deviation bound.

On the systems side, they present shared-memory and distributed-memory implementations. The distributed version uses a block-cyclic distribution to balance load and reduce cache misses during matrix multiplications, with parallel I/O for data distribution. Convergence results for the linearized-ADMM scheme show O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality (ergodic) and constraint residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate scalability to p = 10^6 (total parameters 10^12), finishing in ~11 hours on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work: CLIME and its statistical guarantees are from Cai et al. (JASA); TIGER offers tuning-insensitive optimal rates; QUIC/DC-QUIC efficiently solves graphical lasso problems. ADMM and linearized/inexact ADMM are standard (Boyd et al.; He & Yuan). The proposed contributions lie in an ADMM variant specialized to CLIME with block-column updates, low-rank multiplications, and a distributed block-cyclic implementation; this appears distinct from prior CLIME solvers (interior-point or per-column ADMM) and from graphical lasso solvers (QUIC, BigQUIC/DC-QUIC).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The linearized ADMM is standard; the majorization condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 ensures convergence. The O(1/T) bounds for objective and residuals are plausible extensions of known analyses. However, there are notational inconsistencies around the definition/scaling of Vt in the linearized step: in (8)–(9) and Algorithm 1, Vt is written as ρ η C(…); to match the gradient of the quadratic penalty, it should scale like (ρ/η) C(…), otherwise the soft-threshold update is mis-scaled. This likely is a typo but should be corrected to avoid confusion about correctness.
- The perturbation result (Theorem 3) is consistent with union bounds for sub-exponential noise, but the special case of data-dependent thresholding (truncating entries of C below c√(log p/n)) would benefit from a more explicit argument or citation (e.g., covariance thresholding literature such as Bickel & Levina, 2008), since ∆ then depends on C.
- Empirics: The scalability studies are thorough (shared and distributed, speedups Scol_k and Score_q). Comparisons include runtime and precision/recall on synthetic data; real-data results focus on runtime at matched sparsity. Since DC-QUIC solves a different objective (graphical lasso), reporting only matched sparsity is reasonable for runtime, but any quality comparisons should be qualified accordingly.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear about algorithmic steps and system design. Useful details include process grid choice, block sizes, and the use of BLAS-3.
- Some notation/typographical issues: inconsistent scaling of Vt; minor formatting problems in equations; occasional grammatical errors; incomplete references (missing years/venues). Clarify whether Ω̂ is symmetrized post hoc, as CLIME typically requires.
- Convergence statements should explicitly state whether bounds are ergodic vs non-ergodic, and clarify the norm weighting in Theorem 2.

Originality
- Novelty lies in: (i) a linearized-ADMM solver tailored to CLIME with block-column updates and closed-form proximal steps; (ii) leveraging A for low-rank multiplies; (iii) a scalable block-cyclic distributed implementation; and (iv) a perturbation perspective for sparsifying C while preserving deviation bounds. While linearized ADMM and block-cyclic distribution are known, their integration for CLIME at this scale appears new. Prior CLIME solvers are typically per-column and not designed for distributed memory at this scale.

Significance
- The ability to run CLIME at p = 10^6 is significant for large-scale graphical modeling and for benchmarking distributed optimization with global constraints. The system/algorithm co-design (block-column updates and low-rank multiplies) is likely to be useful beyond CLIME. The work primarily advances computational scalability; statistical advances are modest (the perturbation perspective is incremental but practical).

Specific suggestions
- Fix the scaling/definition of Vt in (8)–(10) and Algorithm 1; ensure dimensions and constants yield the correct linearization of (ρ/2)||CX − Z + Y||_F^2.
- State explicitly the stopping criteria, tolerance choices, and how λ, ρ, η are selected in practice; discuss how λ_max(C) is estimated at scale (e.g., power method on A).
- Discuss symmetrization/PD enforcement for Ω̂ (standard in CLIME) and its computational cost at scale.
- For Theorem 3, clarify the dependence of ∆ on data and cite covariance thresholding results (e.g., Bickel & Levina, 2008; Rothman et al., 2009) if using data-dependent truncation.
- Expand on communication complexity per ADMM iteration and overlap of communication/computation; report wall-clock per iteration and number of iterations to convergence for large runs.
- Clean up references (complete bibliographic info).

Pros
- Clear, practical algorithmic design with closed-form updates and provable O(1/T) convergence.
- Strong systems contribution: block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplies, and demonstrated scaling to 10^6 variables with near-linear speedups.
- Empirical evidence on both shared and distributed memory, with comparisons to strong baselines.

Cons
- Notational/scaling inconsistency in the linearized step; needs correction for full technical clarity.
- Limited discussion of PD/symmetry post-processing for CLIME and downstream implications.
- Statistical novelty limited; the perturbation result is useful but not deeply explored.
- Reference list and some presentation aspects are incomplete/messy.

Overall, the paper provides a solid and useful advancement in scalable precision estimation with CLIME, with minor technical/presentation issues that are addressable. The contribution is primarily computational/systems-oriented and appears within NeurIPS scope (optimization and scalable ML).
