## Peer Review: Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper presents a distributed algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, for estimating sparse precision matrices in high-dimensional, low-sample settings – a critical problem in modern statistics and machine learning. Building upon the CLIME estimator, the authors address the scalability limitations of existing methods by leveraging inexact ADMM, column-block processing, and a block cyclic data distribution scheme for parallel computation. The paper demonstrates strong theoretical convergence guarantees and empirical performance on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, achieving impressive speedups and successfully estimating a precision matrix with one million dimensions and one trillion parameters.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the well-established CLIME estimator [Cai et al., 2012] and prior work utilizing ADMM for precision matrix estimation [e.g., Mazumder et al., 2012]. It acknowledges the increasing focus on scaling machine learning algorithms [e.g., Dean et al., 2012] and contrasts its approach with parameter server architectures [Li et al., 2014] and graph projection block splitting [Parikh & Boyd, 2013], highlighting the suitability of their method for constrained optimization problems. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of scaling up machine learning to “Big Data” challenges.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical scalability bottleneck:** The paper tackles a significant challenge in precision matrix estimation - the computational cost for ultra-high dimensional data. Existing methods simply do not scale to the problem sizes considered in this work.
* **Novel combination of techniques:** The integration of inexact ADMM, column-block processing, and block cyclic data distribution is a clever and effective approach to parallelization and memory management.  The justification for using column blocks (despite the inherent column-wise separability of CLIME) through optimized matrix multiplication is well-reasoned.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides convergence rate analysis for both the objective function and constraint residuals, lending confidence to the algorithm’s performance.
* **Compelling empirical results:**  The experiments demonstrate significant speedups and scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. Estimating a 1 million dimension precision matrix in 11 hours with 400 cores is a remarkable achievement.
* **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easier to follow the technical details. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concrete implementation overview.
* **Practical considerations:** The discussion of sparse and low-rank structures, and how to exploit them for further performance gains, are practical and insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art:** While comparisons are provided, the selection of baseline algorithms (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) doesn't fully represent the most advanced existing techniques for large-scale precision matrix estimation.  It would be beneficial to compare against more recent, scalable methods if they exist.  The discussion of why these methods were chosen is minimal.
* **Parameter sensitivity analysis:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., λ, ρ, η, block size). A more detailed analysis of these parameters and their impact on accuracy and runtime would strengthen the work.
* **Overly optimistic experimental setup:**  The choice to *assume* low-rank structure in the experimental evaluation is somewhat misleading. While the authors motivate this assumption, it's not always valid in real-world scenarios. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance *without* relying on this assumption would be more compelling.
* **Minor clarity issues:**  Some of the notation and explanations could be made more concise.  For instance, a clearer explanation of the "block cyclic distribution" would be helpful.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results. The work is complete and demonstrates a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

**Clarity:**  The paper is generally clearly written, but could benefit from minor improvements in notation and explanations.

**Originality:** The combination of techniques used in CLIME-ADMM is novel and addresses a significant challenge in the field.

**Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact a wide range of applications in areas such as genomics, finance, and image processing. The ability to estimate precision matrices for datasets with millions of dimensions opens up new possibilities for analyzing complex data.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant scalability challenge in precision matrix estimation.
* Presents a novel and effective combination of techniques for parallelization and memory management.
* Demonstrates strong theoretical and empirical results.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* Lack of thorough parameter sensitivity analysis.
* Reliance on low-rank structure assumption in experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale precision matrix estimation. The authors have developed a scalable and efficient algorithm that addresses a critical bottleneck in existing methods. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the suggestions regarding more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and a more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity.
