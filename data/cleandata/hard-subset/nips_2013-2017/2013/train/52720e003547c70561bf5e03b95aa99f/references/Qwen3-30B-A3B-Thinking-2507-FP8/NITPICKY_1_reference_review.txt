

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM variant of the CLIME estimator. The authors address the challenge of scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions (e.g., p = 10^6) by leveraging column-block processing, inexact ADMM, and block cyclic data distribution. The paper claims to achieve linear scalability with the number of cores and demonstrates empirical speedups on both synthetic and real datasets.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to the problem of large-scale precision matrix estimation, which is an important problem in high-dimensional statistics. The column-block approach with block cyclic distribution is a sensible way to address the memory constraints of ultra-high-dimensional problems. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) provide convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm. The experimental results on scalability (Figures 3 and 4) are well-presented and show promising speedups.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Formatting Issues
1. **Mathematical Notation Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent mathematical notation throughout. For example:
   - "S p ++" should be "S_p^{++}"
   - "p2 = 1012" should be "p² = 10¹²" or "p^2 = 10^12"
   - "‖A‖1" should consistently be "‖A‖₁"
   - "1√n" should be "1/√n"
   - "R̄ = 1n ∑ iRi" should be "R̄ = (1/n) ∑_i R_i"

2. **Algorithm 1 Errors**: 
   - The X-update step should be "soft(Xt - Vt, 1/η)" not "soft(Xt -Vt, 1η )"
   - The Z-update should be "box(Ut+1 + Yt, E, λ)" (three arguments), not "box(Ut+1 + Yt, λ)" as written
   - The definition of "soft" in Step 5 is inconsistent with the algorithm description

3. **Theoretical Claims**: 
   - The paper claims "rates of convergence... are both O(1/T)", but Theorems 1 and 2 only provide bounds of the form "O(1/T)" without establishing the exact convergence rate
   - Theorem 1 has an unclear expression: "‖X̄T ‖1 − ‖X‖1 ≤ η‖X‖22 T" should be "‖X̄_T‖₁ - ‖X‖₁ ≤ (η‖X‖₂²)/T"
   - Theorem 2 contains an unclear expression: "‖XT −XT−1‖2η ρ I−C2"

4. **Experimental Details**: 
   - The paper claims to estimate a 1 million dimension precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, but doesn't explain how they handle the memory requirements for such a large matrix (8 TB for double precision)
   - The paper doesn't clarify why they're using "flare" instead of the original CLIME implementation, which would provide a fairer comparison
   - The comparison with Tiger on large datasets lacks details about why Tiger doesn't scale (e.g., does it require more memory or does it have a different convergence rate?)

5. **Terminology**: 
   - "groud truth" appears multiple times (should be "ground truth")
   - "sparsity of the underlying graph to be 0.05" should be "sparsity of the underlying graph to be 0.05" (missing "o")
   - "p n" is used to denote "n << p" but this is confusing notation

### Methodological Concerns
1. The paper claims that "CLIME-ADMM is shown to be much faster than existing methods," but doesn't provide a clear comparison to the state-of-the-art methods for ultra-high-dimensional problems. The comparison with DC-QUIC is limited to smaller dimensions.

2. The paper states that "the memory requirement includes O(pn) for A and O(pk) for the other six variables," but for p = 10^6, n = 200, k = 500, this would be about 1.6 GB for A and 4 GB for the other variables. However, the precision matrix itself (p^2 = 10^12 elements) is not addressed, which would require 8 TB for double precision.

3. The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of why block cyclic distribution is better than other distributions for their specific problem, nor does it discuss the trade-offs between different block sizes.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a theoretically sound algorithm, the numerous technical and formatting inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical notation errors are particularly concerning as they could lead to misinterpretation of the algorithm and theoretical results. The experimental section also lacks sufficient detail to validate the claims about scalability to one million dimensions.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these minor but pervasive issues to be substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper's claims about scalability to ultra-high dimensions are not adequately supported by the experimental details provided, and the theoretical results are presented with inconsistent notation that makes them difficult to verify.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these technical and formatting issues before submission. As it stands, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for technical precision and clarity, which are critical for a high-quality scientific contribution.

**Recommendation: Reject**
