Summary
The paper targets ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation by proposing a distributed, column-block variant of CLIME solved via an inexact ADMM scheme. The key ideas are: (i) linearizing the quadratic penalty in the ADMM X-update with a proximal term to obtain elementwise soft-thresholding, (ii) operating on column blocks (k columns at a time) to replace repeated matrix–vector multiplies with more efficient BLAS-3 matrix–matrix multiplies, (iii) exploiting low-rank structure of the sample covariance via C = A A^T so CX can be computed as A(A^T X) with cost O(npk) when n ≪ p, and (iv) a block-cyclic data distribution to balance load and reduce cache misses in both shared- and distributed-memory settings. They prove O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality and residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2, and present strong-scale experiments showing near-linear speedup and a striking demo estimating a 1e6-dimensional precision matrix in ~11 hours on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
Statistically, the work builds on CLIME (Cai, Liu, Luo 2011) and subsequent theory (Cai, Liu, Zhou 2012; Cai & Liu 2011; Liu & Wang TIGER 2012/2015). Computationally, it is related to LP/ADMM solvers for CLIME, interior-point and ADMM solvers (Toh, flare), and to scalable solvers for graphical lasso such as QUIC/DC-QUIC/bigQUIC, covariance thresholding (Mazumder & Hastie), and scalable ML frameworks (parameter servers, GraphLab) and distributed ADMM. The block-cyclic distribution and BLAS-3 emphasis connect to classic HPC literature (ScaLAPACK, cache blocking).

Strengths
- Sound decomposition: turning columnwise CLIME into column-blocks yields natural, cache-friendly matrix–matrix operations and high parallel efficiency.
- Practical linearized ADMM: the X-update becomes soft-thresholding; the remaining steps are either elementwise or matmul. The O(1/T) rate under a clear condition (η ≥ ρ λ_max^2) is standard but appropriate.
- Systems design: a thoughtful block-cyclic distribution, analysis of sparsity/low-rank routes, and shared vs distributed comparisons. The low-rank trick (A(A^T X)) is a strong practical lever when n ≪ p.
- Scale: Demonstration on p = 10^6 is rare for precision estimation; the reported speedups and the 11-hour run on 400 nodes are impressive.
- Empirics: Competitive accuracy on synthetic graph recovery versus CLIME/TIGER/DC-QUIC; good scalability plots; real-data timing comparisons.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theorem 3 (perturbation to induce sparsity) is shaky as stated. If Δ has sub-exponential entries independent of n, then with high probability ‖Δ‖∞ does not shrink with n and will dominate c sqrt(log p / n) unless Δ is scaled with n. The special case (|Δ_ij| ≤ c sqrt(log p / n)) suffices for thresholding, but the general statement with random sub-exponential Δ needs assumptions tying its scale to n (or a proof correcting constants). As written, it risks being incorrect.
- Clarity and correctness issues:
  - Augmented Lagrangian uses ρ both in the linear dual term and the quadratic penalty; the “scaled dual variable” convention is not clearly specified and differs from standard ADMM write-ups. Please standardize notation and correct factors (1/2) to avoid ambiguity.
  - The extrapolated V-update V^{t+1} = (ρη)(2ĈY^{t+1} − ĈY^t) is unusual in vanilla ADMM. The convergence proof should explicitly handle this extrapolation (or remove/explain it).
  - Symmetrization/PD: CLIME solutions are typically symmetrized post hoc (min of transpose pair) and may not be PD; this is not discussed.
  - Stopping criteria and tuning of ρ, η, and k are not specified beyond a condition on η. Practical guidance is needed.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - Baselines: Comparison to recent large-scale graphical lasso solvers (bigQUIC) is missing; while models differ (CLIME vs log-det), practitioners will want a runtime/accuracy tradeoff comparison at scale. Also, a more direct comparison to the best existing CLIME solvers (beyond flare/TIGER) would strengthen claims.
  - Accuracy: For the million-dimensional demo, n is presumably small. The statistical utility of estimating 10^12 parameters with n ≪ p is questionable; discuss sparsity levels, false discovery control, and whether only graph structure (nonzeros) is materialized.
  - Communication: There is no analysis of communication cost per iteration, overlap, or scalability limits. The choice to use 1 core per node (to avoid shared-memory contention) suggests the implementation is sensitive to NUMA/cache effects; quantify.
  - Output/storage: A full p×p solution is infeasible to store at p = 10^6. How are results materialized (sparse format, top-k edges)? What are the final sparsity levels and total nonzeros?
- Reproducibility: Important implementation details (block sizes, data distribution, I/O format, stopping rules) and code availability are not provided.

Quality
The algorithmic components are reasonable and the convergence analysis (for the linearized ADMM without extrapolation) is standard and likely sound; however, the extrapolated V-update and Theorem 3 need clarification/correction. The systems aspects are substantive, but the lack of communication modeling and some unclear implementation choices reduce rigor.

Clarity
Generally understandable, but several notation inconsistencies, typos, and missing constants hinder readability. Figures/tables are referenced but not always well-explained in text. The paper would benefit from a polished description of the algorithm with standard ADMM notation, explicit stopping rules, and a clear separation of “engineering heuristics” vs “provable” parts.

Originality
The combination—column-block CLIME via linearized ADMM with BLAS-3 and block-cyclic distribution—is a sensible engineering advance rather than a fundamentally new optimization method. The low-rank multiplication is standard. If fixed, the truncation result (Theorem 3) could be a useful practical justification of covariance thresholding in CLIME, but as stated it is problematic.

Significance
Scaling CLIME to p ≈ 10^6 is impressive and potentially impactful for applications where only graph structure is needed and n is small. The work advances the state of practice for distributed sparse precision estimation and could be adopted by practitioners. Theoretical innovation is modest.

Questions for the authors
1) Please clarify/correct Theorem 3. Under what distribution/scale of Δ (as a function of n, p) does the deviation bound hold? Is Δ deterministic thresholding sufficient for your purposes?
2) Provide precise augmented Lagrangian/ADMM derivation, including whether Y is scaled/unscaled, and justify the extrapolated V-update in the convergence proof.
3) What are the stopping criteria and parameter selection rules for ρ, η, and k? Sensitivity?
4) How is the p×p solution stored or used at large p? What sparsity levels (nnz) are observed?
5) Can you report per-iteration communication cost and strong/weak scaling including message counts and data volume?
6) Please compare against bigQUIC or provide rationale if infeasible.

Pros
- Strong systems contribution with demonstrated scale
- Simple, implementable algorithm with BLAS-3 cores
- Sensible exploitation of low rank; near-linear speedups
- Standard O(1/T) rates under clear condition

Cons
- Theorem 3 likely incorrect as stated; needs fixing
- Some ADMM derivation/notation inconsistencies
- Limited baseline comparisons and communication analysis
- Practical details (stopping, tuning, output) under-specified

Recommendation: Weak reject. With a corrected Theorem 3, clearer ADMM derivation, and stronger experimental/comms analysis, this would be a compelling systems paper for NeurIPS.
