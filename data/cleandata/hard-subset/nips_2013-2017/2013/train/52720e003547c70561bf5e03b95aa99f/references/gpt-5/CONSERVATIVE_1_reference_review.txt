Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. It proposes an inexact ADMM variant that operates on column blocks rather than single columns, enabling use of BLAS-3 matrix–matrix multiplications and block-cyclic data distribution for better cache utilization and load balancing. It further exploits low-rank structure of the sample covariance (C = AA′, with n ≪ p) to reduce the cost of matrix multiplications from O(p2k) to O(npk), and discusses potential speedups from sparsity in iterates and a “perturbed” sparse covariance. Theoretical results claim O(1/T) convergence rates for objective and residuals under η ≥ ρλmax(C)2. Experiments compare runtime and speedups against CLIME/ADMM in flare, TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and present scaling on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, including a demonstration with p = 10^6 on 400 cores.

Relative to prior work: ADMM for CLIME (including column-wise decomposition) and related large-scale solvers have appeared (e.g., flare; ADMM surveys; QUIC/DC-QUIC for Glasso). Linearized/inexact ADMM and O(1/T) rates are known. Block-cyclic distribution and block-level BLAS optimizations are standard HPC techniques (e.g., ScaLAPACK). The paper’s main contribution is engineering the combination for CLIME at large scale and demonstrating empirical speedups.

Quality
Strengths
- Sound choice of estimator (CLIME) with established statistical guarantees; avoiding PSD constraints simplifies large-scale optimization.
- A practical, scalable implementation strategy: column-block updates, low-rank matmuls via A(A′X), and block-cyclic distribution. The design is consistent with HPC best practices and maps well to shared/distributed memory.
- Clear derivation of a linearized ADMM update with elementwise proximal step, yielding simple per-iteration operations.
- Empirical scaling trends are encouraging: near-linear speedups with more cores for suitably chosen block sizes; the million-dimensional run demonstrates feasibility.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Limited novelty in algorithms/theory. Linearized ADMM with O(1/T) rates is standard; block-level batching to leverage BLAS-3 and block-cyclic distribution is a well-known HPC pattern. The paper’s theoretical results are essentially restatements of known inexact ADMM consequences with problem-specific notation.
- Theorem 3 (perturbation/sparsification of C) is problematic. The stated result assumes Δ independent of C, but the proposed truncation (data-dependent thresholding of small |Cij|) violates independence. No precise error bound is provided that relates the perturbed C to CLIME estimation error under data-dependent sparsification, nor is a corresponding adjustment of λ justified. As written, this section risks overstating statistical safety of truncation.
- Convergence statements are weak and standard: (i) objective gap bound depends on unknown ∥X∥F and is ergodic; (ii) residual bound involves a norm weighted by (η/ρ)(I − C^2), which requires η ≥ ρλmax(C)^2 and raises questions about conditioning and practical parameter selection. No convergence-to-KKT non-ergodic rate is shown beyond standard ADMM results.
- Experimental evaluation focuses on runtime/speedups with limited statistical assessment. Apart from a small precision/recall plot where TIGER performs best (as expected), there is no time–accuracy tradeoff or error vs λ analysis, and parameter tuning is set to match sparsity rather than target statistical risk, which complicates fairness. It would be important to show, for matched accuracy, how the proposed solver compares in wall-clock time.
- Missing or unclear baselines. For Glasso, BigQUIC and other modern large-scale solvers are relevant; even though the estimator differs, they are de facto standards for large sparse GGMs. Within CLIME, a straightforward parallel column-wise ADMM baseline (one column per core without block aggregation) would clarify the incremental gain from block updates. Also compare to ADMM-based distributed frameworks (beyond GPBS) and communication-avoiding formulations.
- Systems analysis is shallow. There is no communication-cost model, no breakdown of compute vs communication, no profiling, and limited guidance on choosing k, block sizes (pb × nb × kb), or ρ, η. The “million-dimension in 11 hours” claim lacks accuracy metrics, hardware/network details, and storage considerations for the 10^12 parameters (e.g., was the full matrix materialized? symmetrized? projected to SPD?).
- Practical/statistical details are missing: CLIME solutions are not necessarily symmetric/PD—what post-processing was used? What stopping criteria and tolerances were used, and how do they affect runtime and accuracy? How is memory managed for writing/reading blocks at scale?

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are typos, notation inconsistencies, and some awkward phrasing. Several figures/tables are referenced without sufficient detail in the text. The references are incomplete and inconsistently formatted. Algorithm 1 could be clearer about inputs/outputs, stopping, and parameter choices.

Originality
- The main novelty lies in engineering a block-wise ADMM for CLIME with HPC-friendly data distribution. The algorithmic and theoretical components are largely known. The perturbation idea, if made rigorous for data-dependent truncation, could be interesting, but as presented it is not convincing.

Significance
- Practically, a scalable CLIME solver that runs at p ~ 10^6 is useful. However, without stronger statistical evaluation, clearer comparisons to strong baselines, and a more rigorous systems analysis, the contribution feels incremental. The work may be more appropriate for a systems/HPC venue unless the statistical aspects are substantially strengthened.

Pros
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck for CLIME.
- Sensible use of low-rank structure and BLAS-3; good empirical scaling.
- Simple per-iteration operations; potentially reusable framework.

Cons
- Limited novelty; theory largely standard; perturbation theorem questionable.
- Sparse evaluation of statistical accuracy; fairness of comparisons unclear.
- Missing baselines and detailed systems analysis; reproducibility concerns.

Questions for the authors
1) Please justify Theorem 3 for data-dependent truncation of C (|Cij| thresholding). Can you provide a high-probability bound on ∥(C̃ − Σ0)∥∞ and the resulting CLIME error with appropriate λ?
2) What stopping criteria and tolerances are used? How sensitive are results to ρ and η? How do you choose k and block sizes beyond heuristics?
3) How do you enforce symmetry/PD, if at all? Do you symmetrize or project post hoc?
4) For the 10^6-dimensional experiment, what accuracy was achieved, what was stored, and what were the hardware/network details?
5) Can you include a parallel column-wise CLIME-ADMM baseline and time–accuracy tradeoff plots?

Recommendation
Overall score: 4 (reject). While the engineering is solid and the scaling results are promising, the paper’s novelty is limited, the perturbation-based sparsification claim is not rigorously justified, and the experimental/statistical evaluation is insufficient for NeurIPS. Strengthening the theory for data-dependent sparsification, adding thorough accuracy–time benchmarks and stronger baselines, and providing a detailed systems analysis would significantly improve the paper.
