- Paper proposes a distributed column-block CLIME solved via an inexact linearized ADMM with BLAS-3 matrix–matrix operations and a block-cyclic data layout.  
- Key algorithmic ideas: linearize the ADMM X-update to get elementwise soft-thresholding, operate on k-column blocks to use BLAS-3, exploit low-rank covariance C = A A^T to compute CX as A(A^T X), and use block-cyclic distribution to balance load/cache.  
- Proves O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality and residuals under the condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2.  
- Strong-scale experiments show near-linear speedup and an 11-hour run to estimate a p = 10^6 precision matrix on 400 cores.  
- Builds on CLIME literature and connects to LP/ADMM solvers, scalable graphical-lasso solvers (QUIC/bigQUIC), and classic HPC techniques (ScaLAPACK, cache blocking).  
- Strength: column-block decomposition yields cache-friendly matmul and high parallel efficiency.  
- Strength: linearized ADMM makes X-update soft-thresholding and keeps other steps elementwise or matmul, with a standard O(1/T) rate.  
- Strength: thoughtful systems design (block-cyclic layout, low-rank trick) and shared vs distributed comparisons.  
- Strength: rare large-scale demonstration (p = 10^6) with impressive reported speedups.  
- Strength: empirics show competitive accuracy vs CLIME/TIGER/DC-QUIC and good scalability/timing plots.  
- Weakness: Theorem 3 (perturbation/truncation) is likely incorrect as stated—sub-exponential Δ won’t shrink with n unless its scale depends on n.  
- Weakness: ADMM notation/confusion—ρ used inconsistently; scaled dual-variable convention not clearly specified; factors (1/2) ambiguous.  
- Weakness: Unusual extrapolated V-update (V^{t+1} = (ρη)(2ĈY^{t+1} − ĈY^t)) is not standard; convergence proof must explicitly handle it or remove it.  
- Weakness: Symmetrization and positive-definiteness of CLIME solutions not discussed.  
- Weakness: Stopping criteria and tuning rules for ρ, η, and block size k are not specified; practical guidance missing.  
- Weakness: Missing baseline comparisons to large-scale graphical-lasso solvers (bigQUIC) and strongest CLIME solvers; runtime/accuracy tradeoffs at scale not fully explored.  
- Weakness: Statistical utility of estimating p×p when n ≪ p is questionable—sparsity levels, false discoveries, and whether only graph structure matters need discussion.  
- Weakness: No analysis of per-iteration communication cost, overlap, or scalability limits; implementation appears sensitive to NUMA/cache but not quantified.  
- Weakness: Storage/output at p = 10^6 infeasible—no details on sparse formats, top-k edges, observed sparsity/nnz.  
- Weakness: Reproducibility issues—missing implementation details (block sizes, data distribution, I/O, stopping rules) and no code availability.  
- Quality assessment: algorithmic/convergence (without extrapolation) is standard and likely sound, but extrapolated V-update and Theorem 3 need fixes.  
- Clarity: paper is generally understandable but has notation inconsistencies, typos, missing constants, and insufficient explanation of figures/tables.  
- Originality: sensible engineering/system combination rather than a new optimization method; low-rank trick is standard.  
- Significance: impressive practical scaling and useful for applications needing graph structure with small n, but theoretical novelty is modest.  
- Key questions for authors: clarify/fix Theorem 3; provide precise AL/ADMM derivation and justify V-update; give stopping/tuning rules and sensitivity; explain storage/observed sparsity; report per-iteration communication costs and scaling; compare to bigQUIC or justify infeasibility.  
- Pros summary: strong systems contribution, simple implementable algorithm with BLAS-3, effective low-rank exploitation, near-linear speedups, standard O(1/T) rates.  
- Cons summary: incorrect Theorem 3 as stated, ADMM derivation/notation issues, limited baselines/communication analysis, under-specified practical details.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject; would be compelling if Theorem 3 is corrected, ADMM derivation clarified, and experiments/communication analysis strengthened.
