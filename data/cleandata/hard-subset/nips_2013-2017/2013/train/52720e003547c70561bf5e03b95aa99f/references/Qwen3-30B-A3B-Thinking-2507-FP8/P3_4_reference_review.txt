

# Peer Review: Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

This paper addresses a critical challenge in high-dimensional statistics: scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-large dimensions (millions of variables). The authors propose a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm that solves the problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging block cyclic distribution for load balancing and using sparse/low-rank structures to accelerate matrix multiplications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Practical Contribution:** The paper tackles a real bottleneck in precision matrix estimation, where the number of parameters scales quadratically with dimension. The demonstrated scalability to 1 million dimensions (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores represents a substantial advance over existing methods. This addresses a genuine need in fields like genomics and climate science where datasets are increasingly massive.

2. **Well-Motivated Technical Approach:** The column-block processing strategy is well-justified. The authors correctly identify that while CLIME naturally decomposes column-wise, processing multiple columns together leverages more efficient matrix-matrix multiplications (using cache-friendly block operations) rather than repeated matrix-vector operations. The block cyclic distribution for load balancing is a practical and well-executed solution to a common parallel computing challenge.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments comprehensively compare against relevant baselines (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) on both synthetic and real datasets. The scalability analysis across shared-memory (20 cores) and distributed-memory (400 cores) architectures is thorough, demonstrating nearly linear speedups. The paper effectively shows that ADMM, while less efficient for small problems, excels at scaling to massive dimensions.

4. **Practical Insights:** The analysis of iterate sparsity (Section 3.1) and the perturbation approach for making the sample covariance matrix sparse are particularly valuable for practitioners. The paper correctly identifies that statistical guarantees for CLIME only require a deviation bound on the sample covariance, which can be maintained even with certain perturbations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** While the convergence rate analysis (O(1/T) for both objective and residuals) is correctly established, it's largely standard for inexact ADMM (citing [12, 26]). The paper doesn't establish new theoretical guarantees specific to the column-block approach, which is the main technical innovation.

2. **Block Size Impact Unclear:** The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate how block size (k) affects solution quality. While they show scalability, it's unclear if the block size impacts the accuracy of the estimated precision matrix. This is particularly important for statistical applications where solution quality matters as much as speed.

3. **Low-Rank Assumption Ambiguity:** The paper assumes C = AAT with p >> n for low-rank advantages, but doesn't clearly state the n/p ratio in experiments. The benefits of low-rank structure are most significant when n is small compared to p, but this relationship isn't quantified in the results.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The comparison with existing methods is good but could include more recent approaches like the distributed methods in [23] or [9], which also use ADMM for graphical models. The paper mentions "recent related work" but doesn't fully distinguish their approach from these alternatives.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong technical contribution that makes precision matrix estimation feasible for real-world "Big Data" applications. The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the practical insights are valuable for practitioners. While the theoretical novelty is modest, the practical contribution is substantial and directly addresses a significant bottleneck in the field.

The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on scalable machine learning algorithms. The paper demonstrates how to bridge statistical theory with distributed computing, which is increasingly important as datasets grow in size. The framework could be extended to other large-scale constrained optimization problems, enhancing its significance.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to scaling high-dimensional statistical methods to real-world sizes, with strong empirical validation and a clear path to practical implementation. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the scalability of the approach.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include a more detailed analysis of how block size k affects both computational efficiency and solution accuracy.
2. Clarify the relationship between n and p in the experiments, particularly for the low-rank advantage.
3. Briefly discuss the trade-offs between the perturbation approach (making C sparse) and maintaining statistical guarantees.
4. Add a comparison with the most recent distributed methods for large-scale precision matrix estimation.

This paper represents an important step toward making high-dimensional statistical methods feasible for modern "Big Data" applications, and it should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
