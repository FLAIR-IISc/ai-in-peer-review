This paper tackles the important problem of scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensional settings, a critical bottleneck in many modern applications. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator and present a distributed ADMM variant (CLIME-ADMM) along with a scalable parallel computation framework. The core ideas – inexact ADMM, column-block updates leveraging matrix multiplication, and block cyclic data distribution – are compelling and represent a significant step towards tackling this challenging problem.

The strength of this work lies in its ambition and potential impact. Estimating a million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores is a substantial achievement. The proposed algorithm builds upon existing work but introduces key innovations that genuinely address the scalability limitations of prior approaches. The theoretical analysis establishing convergence rates (O(1/T)) is reassuring, and the exploration of sparse and low-rank structure within the computations is insightful, hinting at avenues for further optimization. The connection to the broader “Big Data” scaling effort in machine learning is well-placed.

However, while the paper presents a strong overall concept, several areas could benefit from further development and clarification. The empirical evaluation, while impressive in its scale, lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and comparisons to alternative distributed methods (beyond those mentioned briefly). For instance, a more thorough comparison to distributed implementations of graphical lasso or other ADMM-based approaches would strengthen the argument for CLIME-ADMM’s superiority. The discussion of sparse structure, while promising, feels somewhat disjointed; the perturbation theorem (Theorem 3) is interesting but its practical impact isn't fully demonstrated, and the connection to improved computational complexity isn't explicitly quantified in the experiments. Similarly, the benefits of low-rank structure (exploiting C=AA’) are mentioned but the experimental results don’t convincingly showcase the speedup achieved.  The notation, while generally clear, could be improved (e.g., clearer definition of block indices for matrices). The paper also relies heavily on citations without thoroughly explaining *how* the cited work contributes to their approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of inexact ADMM, column-block updates, and block cyclic distribution for CLIME is a novel approach to a significant problem.
*   **Scalability:** Demonstrating the ability to estimate a million-dimensional precision matrix in a reasonable timeframe is a remarkable achievement.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** Convergence rates are established, providing some confidence in the algorithm's behavior.
*   **Potential Impact:**  Successfully scaling precision matrix estimation unlocks applications in numerous fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Detail:** Hyperparameter tuning and comparisons to other distributed methods could be more comprehensive.
*   **Incomplete Validation:** The benefits of sparse and low-rank structure aren’t fully demonstrated experimentally.
*   **Presentation:** Some sections (sparse structure) feel less integrated and more like future directions than fully developed contributions.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It is not completely clear how much each contribution (inexact ADMM, block updates, block cyclic distribution) contributes to the overall performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the areas for improvement, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the demonstrated scalability, outweighs the minor flaws in the experimental validation and presentation. The paper introduces a compelling new direction for tackling ultra-high-dimensional precision matrix estimation, and the potential impact is substantial. I lean towards **acceptance**, with a strong recommendation for the authors to address the points raised regarding empirical detail and a more thorough exploration of sparse and low-rank structure in a future iteration of the work. The authors should include a table that breaks down the execution time of each step in the algorithm, to allow the reader to better understand the computation cost.
