Summary
The paper targets ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. The main contribution is a scalable inexact ADMM variant that operates on column blocks rather than one column at a time, enabling the use of matrix–matrix multiplications and block-cyclic data distribution across many cores/machines. Key components include: (i) a linearized (inexact) ADMM update for CLIME with simple elementwise proximal steps and O(1/T) convergence bounds for objective suboptimality and feasibility residuals; (ii) leveraging low-sample regimes to rewrite CX as A(A′X), reducing cost from O(p^2 k) to O(npk); (iii) exploiting iterate sparsity and proposing a coarsening/thresholding of the sample covariance C with a deviation-bound argument to maintain CLIME’s statistical guarantees; and (iv) careful shared- and distributed-memory implementations with block-cyclic distribution for load balancing. Experiments compare against CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and real datasets, and demonstrate near-linear speedups and a 1-million dimensional run (roughly 1 trillion parameters) in ~11 hours on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
ADMM for precision estimation (including CLIME) is known, and linearized/inexact ADMM with O(1/T) rates is standard. QUIC/DC-QUIC scale the graphical lasso through second-order approximations and block-diagonalization. GPBS and parameter-server approaches target different classes of constraints. The novelty here lies in making CLIME viable at previously unreachable scales via column-block updates, low-rank multiplication, and HPC-minded distribution and I/O. The “coarsening” perspective builds on deviation bounds used in prior covariance thresholding work but is repurposed here to justify sparse approximations of C for CLIME.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important and challenging problem: sparse precision estimation at million-dimensional scale.
- Thoughtful algorithm–systems co-design: column-block ADMM to replace repeated mat-vec with mat-mat; block-cyclic distribution to avoid load imbalance; low-rank A(A′X) multiplications in the n ≪ p regime.
- Simple, implementable updates with clear convergence bounds and parameter conditions (η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2).
- Empirical scalability: strong speedups in both shared and distributed settings; an impressive 1e6-dimensional demonstration.
- Useful practical guidance (block sizes, process grids) and discussion of caching and data distribution effects.
- The coarsening/thresholding argument for C provides a principled path to exploit sparsity without (theoretically) sacrificing statistical guarantees.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is mostly in engineering and integration: linearized ADMM, column-blocking, and block-cyclic distribution are each known; the contribution is their combination for CLIME and its systemization. The theoretical results (O(1/T)) are standard for linearized ADMM.
- Theorem 3 is underdeveloped: the independence/sub-exponential noise model doesn’t directly match the deterministic thresholding procedure; the needed adjustment to λ when using C+Δ is not fully specified; the impact on estimation error bounds is not quantified.
- Limited discussion of postprocessing: CLIME solutions are not necessarily symmetric/PD; the paper does not state whether/how symmetrization and PD enforcement are performed at scale.
- Practicalities of trillion-parameter outputs are not discussed: storage format of Ω̂, sparsity levels achieved at p=1e6, I/O cost, and how memory limits are handled across nodes.
- Experimental scope: comparisons emphasize runtime; statistical accuracy is thin (one PR curve on synthetic) and lacks distributed competitors at scale. TIGER can also be parallelized; this is only partially acknowledged. No ablations on the impact of coarsened C in practice.
- Some notation/clarity issues (e.g., norms defined elementwise; a few typos) and limited guidance on tuning (ρ, η, k) and stopping criteria.

Quality
Technically solid for the algorithmic part, with standard but correct convergence guarantees. Systems aspects are carefully thought out and credible. The statistical coarsening result needs tightening and better integration with algorithmic tuning and practice. Experiments convincingly demonstrate scalability but are less thorough on accuracy and on comparisons in distributed settings.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, though the manuscript would benefit from: (i) an explicit symmetrization/PD postprocessing description; (ii) concrete memory/communication budgets and I/O details for the 1e6 run; (iii) more explicit guidance for choosing ρ, η, k, block sizes, and stopping rules.

Originality
Moderate. The key ideas are known individually; the novelty is the column-block ADMM design for CLIME plus a scalable HPC implementation enabling unprecedented scale. The coarsening angle is potentially interesting but currently light.

Significance
High from a systems/ML engineering standpoint: making CLIME feasible at million dimensions is impactful and can influence practice. Methodologically, the advances are incremental. Given the demonstrated scale and practical utility, the contribution is meaningful.

Questions for authors
- How do you symmetrize and (optionally) enforce PD at scale? What is the overhead?
- What n was used for the 1e6 experiment, what was the final sparsity of Ω̂, and how was the result stored?
- How do you choose λ when using a perturbed/thresholded C, and what is the empirical impact on accuracy?
- Breakdown of compute vs. communication time in distributed runs? Any overlap/pipelining?
- Any results on using iterate sparsity for further speedups?

Pros (for acceptance)
- Enables a bold new scale for CLIME (1e6 dims), with near-linear speedups.
- Clean, practical algorithm with implementable updates and credible guarantees.
- Strong systems design (block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplies) with careful empirical validation.

Cons (for rejection)
- Methodological novelty is limited; convergence theory is standard.
- Coarsening theorem and its practical integration are underdeveloped.
- Limited statistical evaluation and distributed baselines; missing details on symmetry/PD and memory/I/O at scale.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite limited methodological novelty, the paper makes a bold and practically significant step in scaling sparse precision estimation to million-dimensional problems, with a well-engineered algorithm–systems contribution likely to be useful to the community.
