This paper addresses the important problem of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensional settings. The authors propose a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm, focusing on optimizations for both the ADMM solver and the distributed computation framework. While the problem is relevant and the authors demonstrate scalability to a million dimensions, I find the paper lacking in rigorous comparison to existing methods and offering insufficient justification for the algorithmic choices.

The core idea of using column blocks in ADMM combined with a block cyclic distribution strategy is reasonable, but the benefits are presented as largely self-evident. The claim that solving multiple columns together leverages matrix multiplications more efficiently requires more in-depth analysis. The authors mention existing literature on efficient matrix multiplication ([15, 5, 11]), but do not provide a detailed comparison of computational costs between the proposed block approach and standard column-wise updates, particularly in the context of sparse matrices. A theoretical justification for the block size selection would also strengthen the paper. Similarly, the perturbation technique described in Section 3.1 is stated as a possibility but lacks concrete demonstration of its practical impact and statistical guarantees in a distributed setting. 

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the authors demonstrate scalability to a million dimensions, the comparisons to existing methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) are limited. The synthetic data results (Figure 2) are presented with little explanation of the experimental setup and parameter tuning. The comparison on real datasets (Table 1) is similarly cursory, and the statement that CLIME-ADMM is “substantially more scalable” feels unsupported by the data. A more thorough comparison with well-optimized implementations of existing algorithms (even if not perfectly scalable to the same dimensions) is crucial. Furthermore, the reported speedups on shared and distributed memory architectures (Figures 3 & 4) need more context. For instance, what is the communication overhead in the distributed setting? How does the performance vary with different network configurations? Are the results reproducible with different random seeds?

The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) provides convergence guarantees, but the conditions (e.g., η ≥ ρλ2max(C)) appear restrictive and their practical implications are not discussed. A more insightful analysis of the convergence rate in relation to the problem parameters would be valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant and challenging problem.
*   Proposes a potentially scalable algorithm for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation.
*   Provides convergence guarantees for the proposed ADMM variant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of detailed computational complexity analysis and justification for algorithmic choices.
*   Insufficient experimental validation and comparison with established methods.
*   Restrictive theoretical conditions and limited discussion of their practical implications.
*   Limited exploration of the impact of the proposed perturbation technique.
*   Discussion of scalability is present, but lacks details regarding communications and other overheads.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant problem with the potential to impact large-scale data analysis.
*   The proposed algorithm demonstrates scalability to a million dimensions, indicating a viable approach for ultra-high dimensional data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of rigorous comparison with existing methods and detailed analysis weakens the claims of superiority.
*   The theoretical analysis is limited, and the practical implications of the convergence conditions are unclear.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

I am currently leaning towards **reject**. While the problem is important and the authors demonstrate scalability, the paper lacks the rigor and thoroughness expected of a NIPS publication. The claims of efficiency and scalability need to be supported by more comprehensive experiments and detailed analysis. A significant revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. I am skeptical of the improvements without a more thorough examination of the tradeoffs and comparisons.
