## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper tackles the important problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in ultra-high dimensional settings, a challenge that increasingly arises in modern data analysis. The authors propose a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm, building on existing work but aiming for scalability to problems with millions of variables and trillions of parameters. The core ideas – inexact ADMM with column-block updates and leveraging sparse/low-rank structure – are potentially valuable. However, despite the significance of the problem and the technical detail presented, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor issues that cumulatively detract from its overall quality.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the bottleneck in existing CLIME solvers – the requirement to load the entire sample covariance matrix into memory. It proposes a distributed ADMM approach, solving the CLIME problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column. This builds directly on the work of Cai et al. [3] for CLIME and existing ADMM implementations [18], but aims for a scalability beyond their reach. The use of inexact ADMM [27, 12] and leveraging matrix multiplication optimizations [15, 5, 11] are sensible approaches.  The connection to the broader landscape of scaling machine learning algorithms [29, 22, 6, 7, 20, 2, 23, 9] is also appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Estimating precision matrices in ultra-high dimensions is a vital problem.
* **Clear Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 provides a relatively clear outline of the proposed CLIME-ADMM variant.
* **Scalability Focus:** The emphasis on scalability is commendable, and the reported results on a 1 million-dimensional problem are impressive.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** Providing convergence rate theorems (Theorems 1 & 2) is a strong point, demonstrating a degree of theoretical grounding.

**Weaknesses:**

This paper is riddled with minor, yet pervasive, issues. While individually not fatal, their accumulation presents a serious problem.

1. **Notation Inconsistencies:** The notation is inconsistent throughout. For example, the definition of matrix norms varies (elementwise vs. spectral norms are not clearly distinguished). `X ∈ <p×k` is used without consistent explanation (does this denote a *subset* of columns, or a matrix with those dimensions?). Similarly, `Aij` representing an element *and* a block is confusing.  The definition of `~Aij` is vague – what precisely does this represent within the broader matrix A and the block cyclic distribution?
2. **Minor Technical Imprecisions:** Several technical statements require clarification.  For example, the claim that solving multiple columns together in blocks is "not worthwhile" *before* showing its efficiency is weak. The justification for using block cyclic distribution is not fully compelling – the advantage over other block distributions is not convincingly demonstrated. The discussion of low-rank approximation (Section 3.2) lacks detail on *how* this is implemented, and its interaction with the ADMM algorithm.  The statement that CX = A(ATX) for low-rank C is incorrect - C = AAT. A(ATX) would yield AATX.
3. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Rigor in Experimental Evaluation:** The claim of "almost linear" scalability requires more thorough justification. While the speedup plots are presented, they lack error bars and a clear comparison to the theoretical expected speedup. The comparison to other algorithms (Section 5.1) is incomplete. Why are certain methods excluded from certain datasets? The choice of parameter settings for comparison isn't clearly explained, making it difficult to judge the fairness of the comparison. The runtime reported for the 1 million-dimension problem (11 hours on 400 cores) is given without detailed breakdown of the individual steps.  Was this *wall-clock* time, or CPU time?
4. **Poor Writing & Editing:** The writing quality is uneven. Several sentences are convoluted and difficult to understand. Numerous grammatical errors and typos exist throughout the text (e.g., "inner product in elementwise").  The extensive use of citations within sentences disrupts the flow of reading.
5. **Unnecessary Repetition:** Ideas are repeated multiple times throughout the paper without adding new information.
6. **Appendix References:** References to "Appendix A" and "Appendix B" are made without the appendices being provided in the submitted material.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**Against:**

* Numerous minor flaws in notation, technical details, and writing quality.
* Overstated claims regarding scalability and efficiency.
* Incomplete and potentially biased experimental evaluation.
* Lack of clarity in several key aspects of the algorithm and implementation.

**For:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem.
* Presents a novel approach to scaling CLIME estimation.
* Provides some theoretical analysis, including convergence rate results.
* Demonstrates promising scalability in experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its potential, I recommend **rejection**.  The paper presents a technically sound approach, but the abundance of minor flaws – ranging from notation inconsistencies to imprecise technical statements and poor writing – significantly detract from its quality. While the core ideas are interesting and the scalability results are promising, the paper needs substantial revision to address these issues before it can be considered suitable for NIPS. A more rigorous evaluation, clearer writing, and attention to detail are essential. The current state of the manuscript suggests a lack of polish and thoroughness that is not commensurate with NIPS standards.
