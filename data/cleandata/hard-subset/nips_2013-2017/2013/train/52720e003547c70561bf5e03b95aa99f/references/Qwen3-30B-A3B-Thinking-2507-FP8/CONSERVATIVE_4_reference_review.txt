

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a distributed ADMM-based algorithm for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation, focusing on scaling the CLIME estimator to millions of dimensions. The authors introduce a column-block processing approach instead of column-by-column processing, leveraging block cyclic distribution for parallelization and claiming linear scalability with the number of cores. They provide theoretical convergence rates and experimental results demonstrating speedups on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a clear contribution to the computational scalability of precision matrix estimation, addressing a genuine challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
2. The column-block processing approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence properties.
3. The implementation details for parallelization using block cyclic distribution are well-described and represent a practical contribution to the field.
4. The experimental results demonstrate reasonable speedups on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, particularly for large-scale problems.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of statistical validation**: The paper's primary focus on computational efficiency comes at the expense of statistical validation. The authors claim "strong statistical guarantees" but provide no evidence that their method produces more accurate precision matrix estimates compared to existing methods. The synthetic experiments only compare runtime, not statistical quality (e.g., Frobenius norm error, spectral norm error, or support recovery rates).

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The comparison with CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC is limited. The paper doesn't show how the precision matrices produced by CLIME-ADMM compare statistically to those of other methods. For example, Figure 2(b) shows precision-recall curves but doesn't address how the quality of the estimated graphs compares when using the same sparsity parameter.

3. **Unsubstantiated scalability claims**: The claim of estimating a "one million dimension precision matrix" in 11 hours on 400 cores is not sufficiently validated. The paper lacks details about the specific dataset used, the sparsity of the true precision matrix, and whether the estimated matrix satisfies necessary statistical properties. Without statistical validation, this claim remains purely computational.

4. **Theoretical claims not supported by experiments**: The paper claims O(1/T) convergence rates but doesn't provide empirical evidence of these rates in the experimental section. The experiments focus on runtime, not convergence behavior.

5. **Minimal discussion of limitations**: The paper fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, they claim "matrix multiplication can be further simplified as block-by-block operations," but don't discuss how this approach might fail when the underlying precision matrix lacks the assumed sparsity or low-rank structure.

## Specific Concerns

1. **Theoretical vs. practical significance**: The paper's theoretical contribution (convergence rates) is minimal compared to the extensive literature on ADMM convergence. The practical contribution (column-block processing) is incremental and not sufficiently justified against existing block-based approaches like GPBS mentioned in the paper.

2. **Missing baseline comparisons**: The authors compare with CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC, but don't include a comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for distributed precision matrix estimation, such as those using GPU acceleration or more advanced distributed optimization techniques.

3. **Questionable statistical claims**: The paper claims "strong statistical guarantees" but provides no evidence that their method actually achieves these guarantees in practice. The statistical theory of CLIME (cited as [3]) is well-established, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that their distributed implementation preserves these properties.

4. **Insufficient detail on "sparse, low-rank structure"**: Section 3.1 claims to leverage sparsity and low-rank structure, but the experimental results don't show how this actually improves performance. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the perturbation techniques described in Theorem 3 are used effectively in practice.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound computational approach to scaling precision matrix estimation, but it falls short on the critical criterion of statistical validation. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven computational approaches without sufficient statistical evidence, I find this paper to be a marginal contribution. The authors have focused primarily on computational scalability without adequately demonstrating that their method produces statistically valid precision matrices.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Including statistical validation metrics (Frobenius norm error, spectral norm error, support recovery rates) for the estimated precision matrices
2. Providing a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods that includes both computational efficiency and statistical accuracy
3. Including detailed results for the "one million dimension" claim, showing both runtime and statistical properties of the solution
4. Clarifying how their column-block approach differs from existing block-based methods and why it's superior

Without these improvements, the paper does not demonstrate that it advances the state of the art in precision matrix estimation beyond the computational scalability claims, which are not sufficient for NIPS acceptance given the field's emphasis on both statistical and computational contributions.

**Overall Recommendation: Reject**
