## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to scaling up a key statistical estimation problem – sparse precision matrix estimation – to the ultra-high dimensional regime. The authors tackle a critical bottleneck in applying these methods to modern datasets, offering a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated distributed algorithm based on CLIME and ADMM. While the paper is dense and could benefit from some clarity improvements, the technical depth and promising results warrant a strong positive recommendation.

**Summary:**

The paper addresses the challenge of estimating sparse precision matrices (inverse covariance) when the number of variables (p) is extremely large (millions) and the number of samples (n) is limited (n << p). Existing methods struggle with the computational demands of such problems.  The authors build upon the CLIME estimator, leveraging its desirable statistical properties and inherent structure. They propose a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm that scales to millions of dimensions and thousands of cores by utilizing inexact ADMM with column-block updates and efficient matrix multiplication strategies. They demonstrate theoretical convergence guarantees and show substantial speedups in their experiments.  This work fits well within the broader trend of scaling machine learning algorithms to handle "Big Data", but specifically targets the constraints of constrained optimization problems unlike existing parameter server approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Estimating sparse precision matrices is fundamental in many fields (genomics, finance, etc.). Scaling these methods to ultra-high dimensions is crucial for unlocking their potential with real-world datasets.
* **Sound Methodology:**  The choice of CLIME as the base estimator is justified by its statistical properties. The inexact ADMM formulation is a clever adaptation for scalability, and the use of column-block updates with optimized matrix multiplication is insightful.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides convergence analysis for the proposed algorithm, demonstrating the theoretical soundness of the approach.  The proof technique is clearly stated and builds on existing literature.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate significant speedups compared to existing methods, and successfully estimate a precision matrix with one million dimensions using 400 cores in a reasonable timeframe (11 hours).  The comparison with other methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) is comprehensive.
* **Detailed Implementation Details:** The description of the distributed framework, including the block cyclic data distribution, is thorough and provides valuable insights into the practical considerations of scaling the algorithm.
* **Clear connection to related work:** The authors appropriately position their work within the context of existing approaches for scaling machine learning algorithms, particularly highlighting the limitations of prior methods for constrained optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Density:** The paper is quite dense and technical.  Some sections, particularly the mathematical derivations, could benefit from more detailed explanations and intuitive motivations.  The notation, while standard, is heavy and can be difficult to follow without careful attention.
* **Limited Exploration of Parameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention parameter tuning, the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to the choice of parameters (e.g., λ, ρ, η, block size k) is not thoroughly investigated. A more systematic parameter study would strengthen the empirical results.
* **Assumptions on Data Structure:** The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structure is somewhat disjointed.  While the potential benefits are highlighted, the practical implementation details and the impact on performance are not fully elaborated. The low-rank assumption is stated but more exploration around its frequency in practical applications would be helpful.
* **Lack of Comparisons on Real-World Datasets with Ground Truth:**  The experiments primarily focus on synthetic data. While the real-world datasets are used for runtime comparisons, the lack of ground truth makes it difficult to assess the accuracy of the estimated precision matrices.
* **Limited Discussion of Memory Usage:**  While memory requirements are mentioned, a more detailed analysis of the memory footprint of the algorithm and its scalability with respect to problem size would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves a crucial problem that is becoming increasingly relevant with the growth of high-dimensional data.
* Proposes a novel and technically sound algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrates significant scalability and performance improvements through rigorous experiments.
* Provides valuable insights into the practical considerations of distributed optimization.
* Contributes to the body of knowledge on scaling machine learning algorithms for "Big Data".

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Density and lack of clarity may hinder understanding for some readers.
* Limited parameter sensitivity analysis.
* Dependence on synthetic data for primary performance evaluation.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** Despite the minor weaknesses, the technical strengths, significance, and promising experimental results of this paper clearly outweigh its drawbacks. The contribution is novel, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. This work is a significant step forward in enabling the application of sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensional datasets and should be presented at NIPS.  I recommend the authors address the clarity issues in a revision to maximize the impact of this valuable contribution.  Specifically, adding more intuitive explanations, streamlining the notation, and providing a more detailed parameter sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
