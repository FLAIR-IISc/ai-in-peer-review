

# Peer Review: "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper addresses the important problem of ultra-high-dimensional precision matrix estimation (up to 1 million dimensions) using a distributed computational framework. The authors propose a Column Block ADMM variant for the CLIME estimator, which solves the problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging sparse and low-rank structures to improve scalability. They demonstrate significant speedups on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, with the ability to estimate a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish O(1/T) convergence rates for both the objective function and constraint residuals (Theorems 1-2), which is a solid theoretical foundation. The algorithm design cleverly exploits the structure of the CLIME problem, particularly the column-wise separability and the potential for sparse structure in intermediate iterates.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed, demonstrating scalability across problem sizes and architectures. The comparison with existing methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) shows clear advantages for large-scale problems. However, the statistical guarantees when using the perturbed covariance matrix (Section 3.1) need more thorough discussion. While Theorem 3 provides probabilistic guarantees for the perturbation, the paper doesn't address how this affects the final precision matrix estimate's statistical properties beyond the convergence rate.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm is presented with pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the notation is consistently defined. The theoretical results are properly contextualized with references to the supplement. The figures (2-4) effectively illustrate the scalability results. One minor weakness is that the relationship between block size and convergence rate isn't thoroughly explored - the paper shows that larger blocks improve speedup but doesn't explain the theoretical basis for this observation.

## Originality

The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing the scalability challenge for precision matrix estimation in the ultra-high-dimensional regime (p = 10^6), which is beyond the reach of current methods. The column block approach is a novel twist on existing ADMM methods for CLIME, and the use of block cyclic distribution for load balancing represents a significant practical contribution. While the paper builds on CLIME [3], ADMM [1], and distributed optimization [23], the specific combination and implementation for ultra-high-dimensional problems is novel.

## Significance

The problem addressed is highly significant for modern data analysis, where precision matrix estimation is crucial for understanding complex relationships in high-dimensional data. The ability to handle 1 million dimensions represents a substantial advance over existing methods. The framework could be valuable for applications in genomics, climate science, and other fields with massive datasets. The authors demonstrate that their method scales almost linearly with the number of cores (Figures 3-4), which is a major practical contribution.

## Strengths

1. Addresses a critical scalability challenge in precision matrix estimation
2. Provides rigorous theoretical convergence guarantees
3. Demonstrates impressive empirical scalability (1 million dimensions in 11 hours)
4. Offers a practical distributed framework with implementation details
5. Properly acknowledges limitations and related work

## Weaknesses

1. The impact of block size on solution quality isn't thoroughly explored
2. The paper doesn't compare with the most recent distributed methods for precision matrix estimation (e.g., [22, 23])
3. The theoretical analysis doesn't fully explain why larger block sizes improve speedup
4. The communication overhead in distributed settings is only addressed through speedup metrics, not through detailed analysis
5. The statistical implications of the covariance matrix perturbation (Section 3.1) need more discussion

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale statistical estimation. The proposed framework effectively addresses the scalability gap for precision matrix estimation in the ultra-high-dimensional regime. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened with a deeper exploration of block size effects, and the comparison with the most recent methods could be expanded, the paper's empirical results and practical contributions are compelling.

The work advances the state of the art in distributed statistical computation and is well-suited for NIPS. The authors have demonstrated both the theoretical soundness and practical utility of their approach, with clear evidence of scalability to problems that were previously intractable. I recommend acceptance.

## Specific Suggestions

1. Expand the discussion on the relationship between block size, convergence rate, and solution quality
2. Include a comparison with the most recent distributed methods for precision matrix estimation
3. Add a brief analysis of communication overhead in distributed settings
4. Clarify the statistical implications of using the perturbed covariance matrix

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with significant practical impact. It represents a meaningful contribution to the field of large-scale statistical computation.
