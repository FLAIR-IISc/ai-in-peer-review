Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses large-scale estimation of sparse precision matrices via the CLIME estimator. It reformulates CLIME’s column-wise linear programs into column-block problems and proposes an inexact ADMM algorithm that (i) linearizes the quadratic term to yield a soft-thresholding step for X and a simple box projection for Z, and (ii) concentrates the heavy computation into matrix–matrix multiplications. The method exploits two structural properties: low-rank sample covariance (C = AA′ with n ≪ p) to reduce multiplications from O(p2k) to O(npk), and sparsity in iterates or in a “perturbed” covariance to further accelerate multiplications. For scalability, the paper implements block-cyclic data distribution and uses shared-memory (OpenMP) and distributed-memory (OpenMPI/ScaLAPACK) systems, showing near-linear speedups and solving a one-million-dimensional problem on 400 cores within 11 hours.

The paper builds on statistical estimation via CLIME [Cai et al., JASA 2011], which has established convergence guarantees, and on optimization methods for sparse inverse covariance estimation, including QUIC [Hsieh et al., NIPS 2011], divide-and-conquer QUIC (DC-QUIC) [Hsieh et al., NIPS], interior-point and ADMM-based solvers. It adopts known inexact ADMM linearization techniques [e.g., He & Yuan; Parikh & Boyd] and known HPC practices (block-cyclic distribution, BLAS-3 kernels). The experimental comparisons include CLIME (flare), Tiger (tuning-insensitive estimator with minimax rates), and DC-QUIC.

Strengths
- Clear separation of statistical model (CLIME) and computational strategy, enabling scale to p in the millions without storing C explicitly.
- Algorithmic design aligns with hardware: column-block ADMM yields matrix–matrix multiplications amenable to BLAS-3 and block-cyclic distribution, providing good load balance and cache efficiency.
- Convergence analysis (O(1/T) for objective gap and residuals) under a natural condition η ≥ ρ λmax(C)2, consistent with inexact ADMM literature.
- Practical structural accelerations: low-rank multiplications via A(A′X), and discussion of when sparsifying C (or iterates) preserves CLIME guarantees.
- Comprehensive scalability study on both shared- and distributed-memory systems; the 1M-dimensional demonstration is significant.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- The inexact ADMM updates and convergence conditions appear correct and consistent with standard scaled ADMM analyses. The norm in Theorem 2, with weight (η/ρ)I − C2, necessitates η ≥ ρ λmax(C)2, which is stated.
- The statement of Theorem 3 assumes independent sub-exponential perturbations for Δ but later uses deterministic thresholding as a “special case.” The theorem as written does not cover deterministic truncation; the guarantee needed is simply a deterministic sup-norm bound on Δ that preserves ‖(C+Δ)−Σ0‖∞ ≤ O(√(log p / n)). Suggest clarifying the argument and removing unnecessary independence assumptions.
- Practical details affecting correctness are omitted: (i) CLIME solutions are not guaranteed positive definite; standard practice symmetrizes columns and sometimes reprojects—this procedure and its impact on results are not described. (ii) Stopping criteria (primal/dual residual tolerances, or T) are not specified; these materially affect runtime and accuracy. (iii) Parameter selection for η and ρ is only constrained by η ≥ ρ λmax(C)2; how λmax(C) is estimated in practice, and the sensitivity to η/ρ choices, is not discussed.
- Comparisons focus on runtime and, on synthetic data, precision/recall at matched sparsity. For real data, matching target sparsity does not guarantee comparable statistical quality; held-out likelihood or KKT residuals could provide a more objective metric.

Clarity
- The method and implementation are generally well organized. Some notation and minor typos could be improved (e.g., consistent use of scaled ADMM form; explicit statement of the indicator term for the box constraint in the augmented Lagrangian). Define all variables when first used in Algorithm 1 (e.g., E in box(·) step).
- Provide explicit pseudocode for the distributed multiplication and a brief communication-cost discussion (e.g., collective operations per iteration), as scalability hinges on this.

Originality
- The principal novelty is engineering: column-block inexact ADMM instantiated for CLIME, with low-rank multiplications and block-cyclic distribution at scale. The O(1/T) rate for inexact ADMM itself is known; the contribution is adapting it to CLIME with closed-form X and Z updates and demonstrating extreme-scale practicality.
- The “perturb-and-threshold C” observation is a reasonable practical note, though not fundamentally new; making the conditions precise increases its value.

Significance
- Enabling CLIME at p ≈ 10^6 is impactful for high-dimensional structure learning and related applications. The near-linear speedups and reliance on standard HPC libraries suggest the approach is usable by practitioners.
- The work complements prior advances such as QUIC/DC-QUIC (graphical lasso family) by scaling a different estimator (CLIME) with distinct statistical properties.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 3 to cover deterministic thresholding; state the exact sufficient condition on Δ (e.g., ‖Δ‖∞ ≤ c √(log p/n)).
- Describe symmetrization/PD enforcement procedures used to construct the final precision estimate from column blocks.
- Specify stopping criteria and report primal/dual residuals vs. time; include sensitivity to η and ρ and how λmax(C) is estimated (e.g., power method on A).
- Provide communication complexity per iteration and its impact on scaling; report strong/weak scaling with explicit n, p, k.
- Add accuracy metrics on real data (e.g., Gaussian log-likelihood on held-out samples) in addition to sparsity matching.
- Where feasible, include comparisons to additional strong baselines (e.g., QUIC with covariance screening, graphical lasso with exact thresholding of connected components) under the same stopping criteria.

Arguments for acceptance
- Demonstrates a practical, scalable solution to a core problem, with convincing large-scale experiments and near-linear scaling.
- Sound adaptation of inexact ADMM to CLIME with closed-form updates and provable convergence.
- Implementation details (column-blocking, block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplications) are well aligned with modern HPC practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical contributions beyond known inexact ADMM rates are limited; novelty is primarily systems-oriented.
- Some methodological details (PD enforcement, stopping criteria, parameter selection) and aspects of Theorem 3 need clarification.
- Real-data evaluation emphasizes runtime at matched sparsity without broader statistical metrics; baseline coverage could be expanded.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and practically significant systems contribution that enables CLIME at unprecedented scales, with minor theoretical clarifications and additional evaluation details recommended.
