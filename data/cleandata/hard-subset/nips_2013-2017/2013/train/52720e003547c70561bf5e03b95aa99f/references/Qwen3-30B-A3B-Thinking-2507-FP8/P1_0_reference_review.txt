

# Peer Review: "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary

This paper addresses the critical challenge of estimating sparse precision matrices in ultra-high-dimensional settings (millions of dimensions), where traditional methods become computationally intractable. The authors extend the CLIME estimator to a distributed framework using an inexact ADMM approach that solves the problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column. Key innovations include leveraging sparse/low-rank structures in matrix multiplications, implementing a block cyclic distribution scheme for load balancing, and achieving nearly linear scalability on thousands of cores. The authors demonstrate their method can estimate a 1-million-dimensional precision matrix (trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, significantly outperforming existing methods.

The paper builds upon recent advances in distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 23, 6]) and precision matrix estimation (e.g., [3, 19, 28]), but makes a significant contribution by addressing the scalability gap for problems with p > 10^5. Unlike previous distributed methods that focus on data parallelism, their column-block approach exploits the inherent structure of precision matrix estimation.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness & Novelty:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation with convergence rate analysis (O(1/T) for both objective and residuals), which is a significant contribution beyond just experimental results. The column-block approach is a novel and effective way to leverage parallelism while maintaining the structure of the CLIME problem.

**Technical Innovation:** The authors successfully combine several techniques (inexact ADMM, block cyclic distribution, sparse/low-rank exploitation) to solve a problem previously considered intractable. Their implementation details (e.g., how to choose optimal block sizes, how to handle cache misses) are particularly valuable for practitioners.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experiments comprehensively compare against state-of-the-art methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) across synthetic and real datasets. The scalability results on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures are convincing, with clear demonstration of nearly linear speedups on hundreds of cores.

**Practical Impact:** The ability to handle trillion-parameter problems has clear implications for real-world applications in genomics, climate science, and other fields where high-dimensional precision matrices are needed.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Motivation:** While the convergence analysis is technically sound, the motivation for the inexact ADMM approach (compared to exact ADMM) could be strengthened. The paper states that the inexact approach "can be made substantially more efficient" but doesn't provide clear theoretical justification for why the inexact version would maintain convergence properties while improving performance.

**Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the choice of block size k affects both convergence rate and scalability. The experiments show that larger k sometimes leads to better speedups, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to optimally choose k for different problem sizes.

**Comparison Depth:** While the paper compares with three existing methods, it doesn't engage with more recent distributed optimization methods for precision matrix estimation (e.g., [23, 26]). The connection to recent NIPS work on distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 6, 9]) could be more explicitly articulated.

**Tuning Parameter Impact:** The paper doesn't discuss how the choice of λ (the tuning parameter in CLIME) impacts the scalability of the distributed framework. For ultra-high-dimensional problems, the choice of λ could significantly affect the sparsity pattern and thus the efficiency of sparse matrix operations.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Theoretical Clarity:** Consider adding a brief discussion on why the inexact ADMM approach is appropriate for this problem, and how it differs from exact ADMM in terms of convergence properties.

2. **Block Size Selection:** Provide more guidance on selecting the optimal block size k. The paper shows that k=100 works well for the experiments, but doesn't explain how to determine this parameter for new problems.

3. **Tuning Parameter Impact:** Discuss how the choice of λ affects the sparsity pattern and thus the efficiency of the sparse matrix operations. This is particularly important for real-world applications where λ is often selected based on statistical considerations.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** Consider adding a brief comparison with the distributed optimization framework in [23], which also uses ADMM for graphical model inference, to better position your contribution.

5. **Memory Considerations:** The paper mentions memory requirements but doesn't discuss how the block cyclic distribution affects memory usage patterns across cores. This is particularly important for distributed-memory implementations.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and distributed optimization. The problem addressed (ultra-large scale precision matrix estimation) is important and timely, and the authors provide a technically sound, novel, and well-supported solution. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the scalability of the approach, and the theoretical analysis provides important guarantees.

While there are some areas where the paper could be strengthened (particularly regarding theoretical motivation for the inexact ADMM and guidance on parameter selection), these do not undermine the core contribution. The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental challenge in modern machine learning with both theoretical and practical significance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing the state of the art in scalable optimization for high-dimensional statistical problems. The authors have made a substantial contribution to making precision matrix estimation feasible at scales previously considered impossible.
