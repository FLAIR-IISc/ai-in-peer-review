

# Peer Review: "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions (e.g., p = 10^6) using a distributed ADMM approach. The authors propose a CLIME-ADMM variant that solves the problem in column-blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging block cyclic distribution for memory efficiency. They claim O(1/T) convergence rates and demonstrate scalability to 1 million dimensions on 400 cores (11 hours). The work builds on recent advances in distributed optimization (e.g., [2, 9, 23]) but focuses on the specific challenges of precision matrix estimation.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with reasonable theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-2), though the proofs are referenced to a supplement rather than included. The convergence rate analysis is standard for inexact ADMM (citing [12]), but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its theoretical contributions from prior work on inexact ADMM. 

The experimental evaluation is generally well-designed, with comparisons to CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC on both synthetic and real datasets. However, the comparison is incomplete: the authors don't compare with the most recent distributed methods (e.g., Wang et al. [23] from NIPS 2018) or state-of-the-art sparse precision estimation methods like those in [13]. The claim of "linear speedup" is questionable—Figures 3 and 4 show diminishing returns for large core counts, contradicting this assertion. The paper also lacks thorough ablation studies on block size selection and parameter sensitivity.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and a helpful algorithm description (Algorithm 1). The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation is generally clear, though the sparse/low-rank structure section (Section 3) is somewhat vague without concrete examples. The figures (2-4) effectively illustrate key results, but the discussion of scalability (Section 5.2) would benefit from more detailed analysis of why certain block sizes outperform others.

## Originality
The core idea of using ADMM for distributed precision estimation is not novel [2, 9, 23]. The column-block approach is somewhat innovative but not groundbreaking, as similar block-based approaches exist in distributed linear algebra (citing [15, 5]). The paper fails to clearly articulate how their block cyclic distribution scheme differs from or improves upon existing methods like GPBS [23]. While the implementation of CLIME-ADMM for ultra-high dimensions is impressive, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its methodological contributions from the broader literature on distributed optimization.

## Significance
The problem of scaling precision estimation to millions of dimensions is indeed important for modern applications (e.g., genomics, climate science). The demonstrated scalability to 1 million dimensions is a significant achievement that addresses a real limitation in current methods. However, the paper doesn't adequately justify why this level of scalability is necessary—most real-world applications don't require precision matrices of this size. The practical impact of the method (beyond computational speed) is also underemphasized; the paper doesn't show how this enables new applications or improves downstream tasks.

## Strengths
- Demonstrates impressive scalability to 1 million dimensions (1 trillion parameters)
- Provides theoretical convergence rates for the proposed algorithm
- Clear presentation of the column-block ADMM approach
- Thorough experimental evaluation on multiple datasets

## Weaknesses
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art distributed methods
- Overstated claims about "linear speedup" (Figures 3-4 contradict this)
- Insufficient distinction from prior work on distributed ADMM
- Theoretical analysis lacks novelty compared to existing inexact ADMM literature
- Underdeveloped discussion of practical significance and limitations

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and demonstrates technical competence, it lacks sufficient novelty to warrant publication at NIPS. The method builds incrementally on existing distributed optimization techniques without making a substantial theoretical or methodological contribution. The experimental results are promising but don't sufficiently distinguish the approach from prior work. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent distributed methods (e.g., [23, 9]) and a clearer articulation of how this work advances the state of the art beyond what's already been done.

The authors should consider submitting to a more specialized venue (e.g., JMLR or a systems conference) where the engineering contributions might receive more appropriate recognition. If they choose to revise, they should focus on: (1) a more thorough comparison with the most recent distributed optimization methods, (2) a clearer articulation of theoretical and methodological novelty, and (3) a stronger justification for why ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation is necessary for real-world applications.
