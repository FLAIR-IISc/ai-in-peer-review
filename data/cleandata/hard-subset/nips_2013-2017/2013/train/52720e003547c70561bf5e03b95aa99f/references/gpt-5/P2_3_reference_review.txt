Summary
The paper targets ultra-high-dimensional sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation under n << p by proposing a distributed, column-block ADMM solver for the CLIME estimator. The core ideas are: (i) reformulate CLIME with a splitting variable to separate the l1 objective from the infinity-norm constraint; (ii) use a linearized (inexact) ADMM update for X leading to a closed-form soft-thresholding step; (iii) solve k columns at a time to replace repeated matrix–vector multiplies by matrix–matrix multiplies; (iv) exploit structure—low rank of the sample covariance (C = AA^T) and potential sparsity of iterates/C—to reduce multiplication cost from O(p^2 k) to O(npk); and (v) implement a scalable shared-memory and distributed-memory system with block-cyclic data distribution to balance load and minimize cache misses. They prove O(1/T) rates for objective and residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2, suggest a perturbation/thresholding scheme for C that preserves deviation bounds needed by CLIME, and demonstrate strong scalability (including a p = 10^6, p^2 = 10^12 run on 400 cores in ~11 hours). Comparisons vs CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC show better scalability on large problems.

Relation to prior work
ADMM has been applied to CLIME and related estimators, typically column-wise. Linearized/inexact ADMM with O(1/T) convergence is well known. QUIC/DC-QUIC targets graphical lasso; TIGER provides parameter-free statistical optimality but not distributed scaling. On the systems side, block-cyclic layouts and ScaLAPACK-style distribution are standard for dense linear algebra. This paper’s distinct angle is bringing column-block linearized ADMM and a carefully engineered distributed implementation to CLIME at unprecedented scales.

Quality
- Algorithmic soundness: The linearized ADMM steps and closed-form proximal updates are standard and appropriate. The step size condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 ensures stability of the linearization.
- Theory: The O(1/T) rates for the ergodic objective/residuals are consistent with the linearized ADMM literature. However, Theorem statements and notation are sometimes imprecise (e.g., the norm involving 2η/ρ I − C^2 needs clearer definition to ensure PSD weighting). The “perturbation” theorem for sparsifying C is plausible but underspecified (sub-exponential parameters, independence, and scaling required for the bound are not fully stated).
- Empirics: The scalability demonstrations are compelling, including both shared and distributed regimes, speedup vs block size k, and core counts. However, statistical accuracy is not deeply evaluated: synthetic comparisons tune to the same sparsity rather than equal-regularization or equal-accuracy; real-data results focus on clock time at target sparsities without accuracy metrics. Stopping criteria, ρ/η choices, and communication/memory costs are not reported in detail. The million-dimensional run lacks accuracy diagnostics and resource footprints.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but marred by notational and dimensional inconsistencies and typos: e.g., dimensions of A, U, and W switch in Section 4; some steps in Algorithm 1 are ambiguously annotated; symbols V, V̂, Ŷ appear with limited cross-referencing; Theorem 2 uses unclear weighted norms. Some references are incomplete or misformatted. These issues impede reproducibility, especially for the distributed implementation details.

Originality
The systems contribution—column-block ADMM formulation geared to matrix–matrix multiplies, low-rank exploitation of C, and a block-cyclic distributed implementation for CLIME—is useful and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated at this scale for CLIME. The optimization method and convergence rates themselves are standard. The “sparse perturbation” observation is essentially covariance thresholding framed via deviation bounds; it does not add substantial novelty.

Significance
Scaling sparse precision estimation to p ≈ 10^6 with principled optimization and near-linear speedups is significant for practitioners facing extreme dimensions. The framework (linearized ADMM + block-cyclic distribution + low-rank multiplications) is likely reusable for other constrained large-scale problems. The main limitation is that the statistical evaluation is thin; demonstrating that such extreme-scale estimates are accurate and useful would bolster impact.

Pros
- Addresses an important bottleneck (ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation) with a principled, scalable algorithm/system.
- Clear mapping to efficient primitives (matrix–matrix, elementwise ops); avoids storing C explicitly.
- Strong scalability evidence (shared and distributed), including million-dimensional run.
- Ability to exploit both low-rank and sparsity; practical block-cyclic distribution for load balance.
- Standard, interpretable convergence guarantees for the proposed linearized ADMM.

Cons
- Algorithmic/theoretical novelty is limited; O(1/T) rates for linearized ADMM are known.
- Clarity issues: dimensional inconsistencies, ambiguous notation, and some theorem statements need tightening.
- Empirical evaluation emphasizes runtime over statistical accuracy; fairness of comparisons (tuning to same sparsity) is debatable.
- Sparse-perturbation theorem is underspecified and adds little beyond known covariance thresholding ideas.
- Missing details on hyperparameter selection, stopping criteria, communication/computation breakdown, and symmetrization/PD post-processing of CLIME.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Clarify dimensions and notation throughout (A, U, W shapes; consistent p×n for A; fix Section 4).
- Make Theorem 2 precise (define the weighted norm; ensure PSD of the weight; state assumptions).
- Specify stopping criteria, ρ and η selection, and sensitivity; report primal/dual residual curves.
- Provide accuracy-vs-time plots (e.g., objective gap, constraint violation, or statistical error) and equal-regularization comparisons.
- Detail communication/computation breakdown and per-node memory usage for the large runs.
- Include ablations showing the benefit of column-blocking vs column-wise with highly optimized BLAS.
- Describe symmetrization/PD correction for CLIME and its cost.
- Tighten and properly reference the covariance thresholding/perturbation result.

Overall recommendation: Weak Accept (6/10). The paper’s primary contribution is a solid, well-engineered system that scales CLIME to unprecedented sizes with reasonable theoretical backing. The novelty in optimization theory is modest and the clarity/empirical rigor need improvement, but the demonstrated scalability is valuable to the NeurIPS community.

Confidence: 0.7
