This paper addresses the important problem of scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensional settings. The authors focus on the CLIME estimator and propose a distributed ADMM variant, leveraging both inexact ADMM updates and block-wise column processing to achieve scalability. While the problem is relevant and the proposed approach has potential, the paper falls short of convincing me of its significant contribution due to a lack of comprehensive comparison against established, highly-optimized baselines and insufficient detail regarding practical implementation considerations.

The core idea of applying ADMM to CLIME and exploiting column-wise separability is reasonable. The proposed improvements – inexact ADMM and block-wise updates – seem plausible for enhancing scalability. However, the paper relies heavily on theoretical convergence guarantees and scalability arguments without demonstrating convincingly that these translate to practical speedups *over well-engineered existing methods*. The theoretical analysis, while seemingly sound, feels somewhat detached from the practical challenges of large-scale optimization.  The claims of almost linear scaling with the number of cores are significant, but the experimental validation, while extensive, primarily focuses on demonstrating speedups *relative to other ADMM implementations of CLIME*, not to optimized solvers like those built around interior-point methods or other specialized algorithms. 

The comparison with existing methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) is insufficient. While comparisons are presented, they don’t fully capture the state-of-the-art. For instance, the comparison against `flare`’s ADMM implementation is useful, but doesn’t address whether CLIME-ADMM outperforms highly optimized solvers *specifically designed* for large-scale sparse estimation, which may utilize different techniques or hardware acceleration (e.g., specialized BLAS libraries). Furthermore, the discussion of DC-QUIC feels somewhat dismissive – a more thorough evaluation against its best configurations seems warranted.  The statement that DC-QUIC slows down dramatically with increased sparsity needs more justification; the authors should demonstrate this slowdown explicitly and compare against CLIME-ADMM’s performance in that regime.

The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structure is also somewhat superficial. While the idea of perturbing the covariance matrix to induce sparsity is interesting, the theorem presented (Theorem 3) feels largely theoretical; it doesn’t explain *how* this perturbation is applied in practice and whether it introduces significant artifacts or impacts the accuracy of the estimation. Similarly, the use of low-rank structure is mentioned, but the experimental results don't explicitly show the gains achieved by exploiting this structure. 

The distributed computing framework description lacks crucial details. The discussion of block cyclic distribution is good, but a more detailed explanation of the data partitioning strategy, communication costs, and synchronization overhead would be helpful.  The optimal block size seems heavily dependent on hardware characteristics (cache size, network bandwidth), yet the paper doesn’t provide clear guidance on how to choose this parameter effectively for different architectures.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Presents a plausible approach based on ADMM and block-wise updates.
*   Provides theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrates scalability on synthetic data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficient comparison against highly optimized, state-of-the-art baselines.
*   Lack of practical detail regarding implementation choices (e.g., block size selection).
*   Superficial discussion of exploiting sparse/low-rank structure.
*   Limited justification for the benefits of the proposed perturbation technique.
*   Scalability claims need stronger empirical support.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and the proposed approach *could* be beneficial for very large-scale problems.
*   The theoretical results are sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of a strong empirical comparison to established methods weakens the claim of significant advancement.
*   The paper feels incomplete – it lacks the practical details needed to effectively deploy and utilize the proposed framework.
*   The benefits of the proposed optimizations aren't convincingly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a technically sound approach to a relevant problem, it doesn't provide sufficient evidence to convince me of its significant contribution to the field. The lack of a thorough comparison against established baselines and the absence of crucial implementation details raise serious concerns about its practical utility. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address these weaknesses and resubmit a revised version with a more compelling empirical evaluation. A more detailed exploration of the sparsity and low-rank assumptions and how they translate into concrete performance improvements would also strengthen the paper.
