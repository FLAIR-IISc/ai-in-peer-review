## Peer Review: Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper presents a distributed algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, for estimating sparse precision matrices in ultra-high-dimensional settings (p >> n). Building on the CLIME estimator, the authors address the computational bottleneck of scaling to millions of variables and trillions of parameters. Their key innovations include an inexact ADMM variant optimized for CLIME, solving the problem in column blocks instead of individually, and leveraging efficient matrix multiplication techniques. They demonstrate scalability on shared and distributed memory architectures, achieving impressive results—estimating a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores.  The work positions itself within the broader trend of scaling machine learning algorithms to "Big Data".

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon existing work in sparse precision matrix estimation (e.g., [3, 19, 28]) and distributed optimization techniques (e.g., [2, 6, 23]).  It acknowledges the limitations of existing methods like CLIME and ADMM when applied to truly massive datasets.  The authors correctly position their work within the context of recent efforts to scale machine learning algorithms, referencing relevant works on parameter servers, graph projection block splitting, and distributed ADMM.  The focus on utilizing block structure and leveraging efficient matrix multiplication distinguishes it from simpler parallel implementations.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical challenge:** Scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions is a significant practical problem.
* **Technical soundness:** The proposed algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, is well-motivated and appears technically sound, with convergence rate analysis provided. The use of inexact ADMM and column-block solving are insightful optimizations.
* **Scalability:**  The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the scalability of the algorithm on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures. The reported performance on a 1 million-dimensional dataset is impressive.
* **Theoretical analysis:** The inclusion of convergence rate theorems (Theorems 1 & 2) strengthens the paper's contribution.
* **Practical considerations:**  The discussion of sparse structure exploitation and low-rank approximations (Section 3) demonstrates a good understanding of practical optimization strategies. The detailed discussion of block cyclic distribution in Section 4 is valuable.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is reasonably clear, and the algorithm is presented in a concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison:** While comparisons are made to existing algorithms, they are somewhat limited in scope. The choice of comparison algorithms (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) is reasonable, but a more thorough comparison against other scalable methods, if they exist, would be beneficial. The comparison focuses more on runtime and less on the quality of the estimated precision matrix (e.g., false positive/negative rates in graph recovery).
* **Parameter sensitivity:** The performance of CLIME-ADMM is likely sensitive to parameters like the regularization parameter λ, the ADMM penalty parameter ρ, and the block size k. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to choose these parameters in practice.
* **Low-rank assumption:** Leveraging the low-rank structure of the sample covariance matrix is a good optimization, but the authors don't provide extensive justification for *why* this assumption is likely to hold in many practical settings. Further discussion on the conditions under which this assumption is valid would be useful.
* **Missing details:** Some details are glossed over. For example, the perturbation matrix Δ in Theorem 3 could be elaborated upon.  The discussion of cache optimization could be more concrete.
* **Theoretical results could be clearer**: The assumptions on η in Theorems 1 and 2 seem restrictive, and the implications of these conditions for practical application could be discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant problem in high-dimensional statistics and machine learning.
* The proposed algorithm is technically sound and demonstrably scalable.
* The theoretical analysis provides confidence in the algorithm's convergence.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison with existing methods is somewhat limited.
* The parameter sensitivity of the algorithm is not fully addressed.
* The assumption of low-rank structure requires further justification.
* Some details could be elaborated upon.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors address a significant practical challenge with a well-designed algorithm and provide convincing experimental evidence of its scalability. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1. **Expand the comparison:**  Include a more thorough comparison against other scalable precision matrix estimation methods, if possible.  Provide more detailed results on the accuracy of the estimated precision matrix.
2. **Parameter tuning guidance:**  Provide more guidance on how to choose the parameters λ, ρ, and k in practice.
3. **Justify low-rank assumption:**  Elaborate on the conditions under which the low-rank assumption is likely to hold.
4. **Clarify theoretical results**: Provide a more intuitive explanation of the assumptions involved in Theorems 1 and 2, and discuss their practical implications.
5. **Add more details**: Clarify and expand the details on the perturbation matrix in Theorem 3 and the cache optimization strategies.
