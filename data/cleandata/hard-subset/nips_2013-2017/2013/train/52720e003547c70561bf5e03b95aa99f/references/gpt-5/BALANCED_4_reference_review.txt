Summary
The paper addresses ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation via the CLIME estimator. It proposes a distributed, column-block variant of ADMM for solving the CLIME linear program, with three main ideas: (i) a linearized (inexact) ADMM update that reduces each iteration to elementwise proximal steps and matrix–matrix multiplications; (ii) solving multiple columns at once (“column blocks”) to leverage BLAS-3 efficiency and cache behavior; and (iii) scalable implementations in both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures using block-cyclic data distribution and parallel I/O. The authors establish O(1/T) ergodic convergence rates for the objective and residuals under a standard stepsize condition. They further discuss exploiting structure: dynamic sparsity in iterates and static sparsity/low rank of the sample covariance; in particular, they use C = A A^T and n << p to reduce the main multiplication to O(npk). A perturbation/truncation argument suggests thresholding small entries in C does not hurt CLIME’s statistical guarantees. Empirically, they report substantial speedups over competing methods (CLIME via interior point/ADMM, TIGER, DC-QUIC) and demonstrate scalability up to p = 10^6 on 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on the CLIME estimator (Cai, Liu, Luo 2011; Cai, Liu, Zhou 2012) and on ADMM-based optimization (Boyd et al.). ADMM variants for precision estimation (including per-column solvers) and fast Graphical Lasso solvers (e.g., QUIC, DC-QUIC) are relevant NeurIPS precedents. Using low-rank multiplications for covariance-based problems and block-cyclic distributions (ScaLAPACK) are classical in HPC. The idea of truncating sample covariances has a close connection to thresholded covariance estimation (e.g., Bickel and Levina, 2008) and covariance thresholding for block decomposition in Graphical Lasso (Mazumder and Hastie).

Strengths
- Practical scalability: The combination of column-block updates, BLAS-3 heavy kernels, and block-cyclic distribution is well-motivated and effective. Demonstrating p=10^6 with 400 cores is impressive.
- Algorithmic simplicity: The linearized ADMM leads to closed-form proximal updates plus standard matrix multiplications.
- Convergence guarantees: O(1/T) rates under a Lipschitz-like condition are appropriate and align with recent ADMM theory.
- Systems perspective: Careful discussion of shared- vs distributed-memory behavior, cache/memory considerations, and load balancing.
- Structure exploitation: The low-rank trick via A A^T is natural in the n << p regime; the discussion of sparsity and blocking is useful.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: Column-block ADMM, low-rank matmuls, and block-cyclic distribution are established techniques; the main contribution reads as a strong systems/engineering package rather than a new optimization/statistical method. The convergence results are standard for linearized ADMM.
- Clarity and correctness issues: There are typos/inconsistencies in the derivation (e.g., scaling in Vt: the text defines Vt ∝ ρηC(·) whereas the algorithm uses ρ/η; Theorem 2’s norm “‖·‖^2_{η/ρ I − C^2}” is unclear). References are incomplete; some details are missing in figures/tables.
- Limited statistical evaluation: Comparisons across different estimators (CLIME, TIGER, QUIC variants) are done primarily via speed and sparsity-matched precision/recall; there is little systematic assessment of estimation error or robustness. Choice of λ, stopping criteria, and symmetrization for CLIME are not detailed.
- Perturbation/sparsification theory (Theorem 3) is under-justified: Adding sub-exponential noise ∆ to C and still meeting the same deviation bound requires careful dependence control and scaling assumptions; citing and aligning with known results on covariance thresholding (e.g., Bickel–Levina) would make this more rigorous.
- Practical concerns unaddressed: For p=10^6, storing or outputting Ω̂ (even sparse) is nontrivial; the paper does not discuss output formats, symmetrization of CLIME, PD correction, or memory for intermediate and final results.

Quality
Technically sound in the core algorithm and systems design; the ADMM derivation needs careful cleanup to avoid confusion. Convergence claims are reasonable. The perturbation result needs more formal conditions or should be reframed conservatively. Experimental methodology is reasonable for scalability, less so for statistical accuracy across models with different objectives. Reproducibility could be improved (code/parameters/stopping criteria not provided).

Clarity
Overall readable, but the manuscript contains several typos, inconsistent notation, and incomplete references; some figure/table details are missing. Algorithm 1 should be carefully checked for scaling constants. Theorems should be fully stated with precise norms and assumptions.

Originality
Moderate. The systems integration and column-block ADMM framing for CLIME are useful but not conceptually groundbreaking. Convergence rate and low-rank exploitation are standard.

Significance
High practical relevance for large-scale precision estimation; the ability to handle million-dimensional problems is compelling. Scientific impact would be stronger with clearer theoretical guarantees for the proposed sparsification and more extensive accuracy comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix derivation inconsistencies (Vt scaling) and clarify Theorem 2 norms; include a short proof sketch to highlight the key inequalities and the η ≥ ρ λ_max^2(C) condition.
- Discuss symmetrization of the CLIME solution and whether PD is enforced post hoc; clarify output storage strategies at p=10^6 and sparsity levels achieved.
- Provide full experimental details (λ selection, stopping criteria, tolerances, iteration counts) and release code if possible.
- Strengthen statistical evaluation: report estimation errors (e.g., Frobenius/spectral norms to Ω0), ROC/PR curves, and sensitivity to n/p and graph structures; ensure cross-method fairness.
- Reframe Theorem 3 or add rigorous conditions; cite thresholded covariance literature (e.g., Bickel and Levina, 2008) and relate your truncation heuristic to known bounds.
- Compare against more recent large-scale Graphical Lasso solvers (e.g., BigQUIC) and nodewise regression–based estimators, and discuss pros/cons relative to your approach.
- Provide a brief communication/computation cost model to guide choices of k, block sizes, and process grids.

Pro arguments for acceptance
- Strong engineering contribution with demonstrated scalability to regimes not commonly reported.
- Clear path for reuse in other constrained problems with similar structure.
- Solid, if standard, convergence guarantees for the proposed linearized ADMM variant.

Con arguments for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; heavy reliance on known techniques.
- Clarity/notation errors and incomplete references.
- Limited statistical validation and theoretical support for covariance perturbation/truncation.
- Missing practical details (symmetrization, output/storage) at extreme scales.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The work is promising and practically relevant, but the current presentation has correctness/clarity gaps and limited novelty for NeurIPS. With tighter theory/derivations, stronger and fairer empirical validation, and improved exposition, it could be competitive in a future submission.
