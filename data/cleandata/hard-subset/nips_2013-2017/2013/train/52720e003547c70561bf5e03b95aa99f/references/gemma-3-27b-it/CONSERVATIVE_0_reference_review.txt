This paper addresses the important problem of scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions. The authors present CLIME-ADMM, a distributed algorithm built on an inexact ADMM variant of the CLIME estimator. The core idea of solving the problem in column blocks and leveraging matrix multiplication optimizations is reasonable. However, while the paper demonstrates scalability to a million dimensions, the presentation lacks sufficient rigor and comparative evaluation to convince me of its significance relative to existing methods.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. The decomposition of CLIME into column-wise LPs is well-known, and the authors correctly identify the memory bottleneck of existing solvers. The proposed approach of column-block ADMM and distributed computation is a logical extension. However, the discussion of the theoretical convergence rates (Theorems 1 and 2) feels cursory. While stating O(1/T) convergence is useful, it lacks specific details regarding the dependence on problem parameters (p, n, sparsity level) and the specific constants involved. The proofs are relegated to a supplementary appendix, hindering quick assessment.

The section on leveraging sparse and low-rank structure presents potentially valuable optimizations. However, the discussion feels fragmented. The idea of perturbing the covariance matrix to induce sparsity is interesting but lacks a clear justification for the chosen perturbation scheme. The claim that statistical guarantees hold even with perturbation needs stronger backing beyond a theorem statement and a pointer to the appendix. The use of low-rank approximations is mentioned but lacks detail on how the rank is chosen and the impact on accuracy.

The description of the scalable parallel computation framework is thorough, detailing block cyclic distribution and its advantages. Figures 1(a) and 1(b) are helpful in visualizing the parallelization strategy. However, the rationale behind the choice of block size (pb × nb × kb) remains unclear. The explanation relies heavily on avoiding cache misses but doesn't provide concrete guidelines or analysis.

The experimental results are the weakest aspect of the paper. While demonstrating scalability to a million dimensions is impressive, the comparison with existing methods is insufficient. The comparison in Section 5.1 focuses primarily on runtime. While speed is important, accuracy is paramount in precision matrix estimation. The precision and recall results shown are limited and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the estimator’s performance. The setup of the experiments is not described in enough detail, specifically the parameter tuning for Tiger and DC-QUIC. The scaling experiments in Section 5.2 are largely confined to synthetic data. The results on real datasets (Table 1) are presented without sufficient context and do not provide a clear understanding of the benefits of CLIME-ADMM. The claim of "linear speedup" should be supported with more rigorous analysis of efficiency. Furthermore, the fact that using multiple cores *slows down* the computation in the shared memory setting (Table 2) is alarming and requires a detailed explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem (scaling precision matrix estimation).
*   Presents a reasonable algorithmic approach (column-block ADMM).
*   Demonstrates scalability to a large problem size (million dimensions).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks rigorous theoretical analysis, with convergence rates presented without sufficient detail.
*   Insufficient comparative evaluation against established methods, particularly regarding accuracy.
*   Experimental setup is not clearly described, hindering reproducibility.
*   Conflicting results in the shared-memory experiments require explanation.
*   The discussion of sparsity and low-rank structure is fragmented and lacks concrete guidance.

**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be technically sound but lacking in convincing evidence of its significance. The ideas presented are incremental improvements over existing approaches and don't represent a major breakthrough. The lack of thorough evaluation and the concerning results in the shared-memory experiments raise concerns about the robustness and practical applicability of the proposed method. I recommend **rejection** due to insufficient novelty and inadequate validation. The authors should significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more detailed theoretical analysis, and address the issues raised regarding the shared-memory performance.
