Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation via the CLIME estimator. It proposes an inexact, column-block ADMM algorithm that: (i) linearizes the coupled quadratic penalty to obtain closed-form soft-thresholding updates; (ii) solves multiple columns at a time to exploit matrix–matrix multiplies and cache-friendly blocking; (iii) leverages low-rank structure (C=A A^T with n≪p) to replace O(p^2k) multiplies by O(npk); and (iv) implements a scalable shared- and distributed-memory framework using block-cyclic data distribution. The paper proves O(1/T) ergodic convergence for objective suboptimality and residuals under a step-size condition. It also justifies truncating small entries of the sample covariance by showing that certain perturbations preserve the deviation bound required by CLIME’s statistical guarantees. Experiments compare to CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate near-linear speedups and a 1M-dimensional run in ~11 hours on 400 cores.

Relative to prior work, ADMM has been used for CLIME/graphical model estimation, and linearized/inexact ADMM O(1/T) rates are known. Prior numerical work such as QUIC/DC-QUIC addresses the graphical lasso. The present contribution is a CLIME-specific, block-column inexact ADMM with a carefully engineered distributed implementation (block-cyclic distribution, leveraging A rather than C) that targets extreme scales.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear scalability bottleneck in sparse precision estimation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude scale (p=10^6).
- Sound algorithmic design: linearized ADMM gives simple, elementwise updates; block-column processing harnesses matrix–matrix operations and cache blocking; low-rank multiplies are natural in n≪p settings.
- Convergence guarantees (O(1/T)) under a transparent step-size condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2; the norm weighting in Theorem 2 aligns with standard linearized ADMM analyses.
- Systems contributions are well thought out: block-cyclic distribution to mitigate load imbalance from dynamic sparsity; discussion of cache effects and per-node vs per-core usage; practical I/O.
- Empirical evaluation includes both accuracy (precision/recall curves) and scalability (speedups vs k and cores), and compares to relevant baselines (TIGER, CLIME, DC-QUIC).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical novelty is modest: the O(1/T) rate and linearized ADMM template are standard; proofs seem to adapt known arguments. The main novelty is engineering and problem-specific tailoring.
- The perturbation/truncation theorem (Theorem 3) assumes independent sub-exponential Δ; the proposed thresholding Δ depends on C and thus is data-dependent. The appendix may address this, but as written the “special case” is not covered by the independence assumption. Clarify conditions under which data-dependent truncation preserves the deviation bound.
- Baselines: DC-QUIC is included, but other large-scale graphical lasso solvers (e.g., BigQUIC) are not compared; though the objective differs from CLIME, such comparisons would contextualize ultimate end-to-end capability for structure learning at scale.
- Statistical evaluation is limited: beyond PR curves on synthetic data, there is little quantification of estimation error (e.g., operator/Frobenius error vs n, λ), or robustness to model mismatch, which would reassure that engineering choices (e.g., covariance truncation) do not degrade accuracy.
- Parameter choices: the paper does not discuss how ρ and η are tuned in practice; η requires (an upper bound on) λ_max(C). A brief discussion of estimating λ_max via power iterations with A would help reproducibility. Stopping criteria and tolerance settings are also not specified.
- CLIME symmetry/positivity: CLIME solutions are not necessarily symmetric/PD; the paper does not discuss symmetrization or post-processing to ensure PD if needed for downstream tasks.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The algorithm and systems design are technically sound; the convergence claims are consistent with known linearized ADMM theory. The empirical evidence for scalability is strong and includes careful ablations (block size, cores, shared vs distributed). Some theoretical aspects (perturbation result’s assumptions; communication cost modeling; parameter selection) could be strengthened or clarified. Overall, this is a complete piece of work with honest discussion of where speedups come from, and practical implementation details.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. Algorithm 1 is concrete. A few notational/typographical issues (e.g., missing E argument in box in one place; the weighted norm in Theorem 2) should be cleaned up. The systems section could benefit from a succinct communication-computation cost model.
- Originality: The combination—CLIME-specific linearized, block-column ADMM; systematic exploitation of low-rank sample covariance; and a block-cyclic distributed implementation—appears novel in aggregate. The theoretical rate is standard; the main originality lies in the distributed design for CLIME and the perturbation argument motivating covariance truncation.
- Significance: High practical significance for ultra-high-dimensional precision estimation (million+ variables). The approach should be of interest to researchers and practitioners needing scalable graphical model structure learning. The ideas (block-column ADMM, block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplies) are transferable to other constrained matrix problems.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the scope of Theorem 3 for data-dependent truncation and whether additional arguments (e.g., union bounds plus concentration of C) ensure the deviation bound after truncation.
- Provide guidance on setting ρ and η and on estimating λ_max(C) efficiently in the distributed setting; report stopping criteria used in experiments.
- Discuss symmetry enforcement and any post-processing to ensure PD if required.
- Add a brief communication/time complexity model per iteration under block-cyclic distribution.
- If possible, include estimation error vs n on synthetic data and a comparison to at least one large-scale graphical lasso solver to contextualize trade-offs.

Pros
- Demonstrates truly large-scale precision estimation with near-linear scaling.
- Simple, effective algorithmic primitives with clear convergence guarantees.
- Thoughtful distributed systems design (block-cyclic, low-rank multiplies).
- Empirical results are convincing on scalability; code architecture choices are well motivated.

Cons
- Limited theoretical novelty; perturbation result needs clearer applicability to data-dependent truncation.
- Statistical accuracy evaluation is light; missing discussion of parameter/stopping choices.
- Baseline coverage could be broader.

Overall recommendation
Lean toward accept for NeurIPS as a strong systems-and-algorithms contribution to scalable statistical estimation. While the theoretical advances are incremental, the paper addresses an important problem with a well-engineered, principled solution and compelling empirical scale, and the techniques are broadly applicable. Strengthening clarity around the perturbation result and practical parameterization would further improve the paper.
