

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM variant of the CLIME estimator. The authors aim to scale precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions (up to 1 million variables) through parallel computation and block cyclic distribution. The key contributions include an inexact ADMM algorithm that processes multiple columns simultaneously, a distributed framework for shared- and distributed-memory architectures, and theoretical convergence rates of O(1/T) for both objective and residuals.

## Strengths

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough description of the algorithm and theoretical foundations. The authors provide a detailed experimental evaluation comparing their method against state-of-the-art approaches on both synthetic and real datasets. The scalability results (achieving linear speedup with up to 400 cores for p=10^6) are impressive and demonstrate practical utility for large-scale problems. The theoretical analysis of convergence rates appears sound, and the paper properly references prior work in distributed optimization and sparse precision estimation.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that undermine its contribution and significance:

1. **Lack of compelling motivation for extreme scale**: The paper claims to scale to "one million dimensions" (1 trillion parameters) but fails to demonstrate why this scale is meaningful for real-world applications. Most precision matrix estimation problems in genomics, finance, and other domains involve dimensions in the hundreds or thousands, not millions. The authors provide no convincing evidence that the 1 million dimension case represents a meaningful practical problem rather than a theoretical benchmark.

2. **Inadequate statistical validation**: The paper claims "statistical guarantees" for their method but doesn't properly address whether the distributed implementation preserves the statistical properties of the standard CLIME estimator. The perturbation argument in Section 3.1 (Theorem 3) is hand-wavy and doesn't show how the distributed approach affects the final precision matrix estimate. There's no comparison of statistical properties (e.g., error rates, false discovery rates) between the distributed method and standard implementations.

3. **Misleading performance comparisons**: The comparison with Tiger (a method designed for small-to-moderate dimensions) is misleading. Tiger is not intended for distributed computation, so the comparison unfairly disadvantages the distributed approach. The paper doesn't compare against distributed methods designed for precision matrix estimation, such as those in [23] or [9]. The claim that CLIME-ADMM is "much faster" is based on limited comparisons without proper statistical validation.

4. **Underdeveloped novelty**: The column-block approach for ADMM is not novel—similar ideas have been explored in distributed optimization for other problems. The block cyclic distribution scheme is a standard technique in distributed linear algebra (cited in [15, 5, 11]) and isn't sufficiently distinguished from prior work. The main contribution appears to be applying existing techniques to precision matrix estimation rather than developing fundamentally new methods.

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims "CLIME-ADMM is shown to be much faster than existing methods" without providing sufficient evidence. The experimental results (Table 1) show modest improvements on small datasets but don't demonstrate the claimed superiority on the large-scale problems the paper purports to solve. The 11-hour runtime for p=10^6 is impressive but doesn't address whether this is actually useful for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a technically sound distributed implementation of CLIME with good scalability results, it fails to demonstrate that this approach solves a meaningful problem at the claimed scale. The lack of statistical validation, misleading comparisons, and overstatement of significance are significant concerns. The paper would be significantly stronger if it had addressed why 1 million dimensions are necessary for real applications, provided proper statistical validation of the distributed approach, and compared against relevant distributed methods.

The methodological approach is largely derivative of established distributed optimization techniques, and the paper doesn't convincingly show how this combination advances the state of the art beyond what's already known. For a NIPS paper, the significance of the contribution doesn't match the claimed scale of the problem. The results are impressive for the specific benchmark they test, but they don't demonstrate a meaningful advancement for the broader precision matrix estimation community.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence that the approach solves a meaningful problem at the claimed scale, lack of statistical validation, and inadequate novelty relative to existing distributed optimization techniques. The paper would benefit from stronger motivation for the extreme scale, proper statistical validation, and a more nuanced comparison with relevant distributed methods.
