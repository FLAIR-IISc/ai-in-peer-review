## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sound approach to a very important problem – scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensional datasets. The authors address a significant bottleneck in the field by providing a distributed implementation of the CLIME estimator, coupled with smart algorithmic optimizations like inexact ADMM and block-wise processing.  While the paper is dense and could benefit from increased clarity in some sections, the technical contributions are substantial and the experimental results are convincing. I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions, contingent on addressing clarity issues and potentially expanding the discussion of limitations.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of estimating sparse precision matrices (inverse covariance) in the high-dimensional, low-sample regime, a common scenario in modern data analysis.  Existing methods struggle to scale beyond a few thousand variables due to the quadratic parameter complexity.  The authors focus on the CLIME estimator, known for its statistical guarantees and computational advantages, but adapt it for massive scale through a distributed framework. 

The work builds upon existing distributed optimization techniques, particularly ADMM, but differentiates itself through: (1) an inexact ADMM variant tailored to CLIME, enabling efficient parallelization; (2) a block-wise solution approach to leverage matrix-matrix multiplications; and (3) a carefully designed distributed computing framework utilizing block cyclic data distribution to optimize memory access and load balancing.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms, referencing relevant papers on parameter servers and distributed ADMM, but clearly highlight the novelty of their approach in handling constrained optimization with global constraints.  The connection to previous work on sparse and low-rank structure exploitation is well-placed.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations for the inexact ADMM convergence rates (Theorems 1 & 2) appear rigorous and well-justified. The rationale for block-wise processing and the benefits of leveraging matrix multiplication are convincingly explained.
* **Scalability:** The experimental results are the strongest aspect of the paper.  Demonstrating the ability to estimate a million-dimensional precision matrix with a trillion parameters in 11 hours on 400 cores is a significant achievement. The detailed performance analysis on both shared and distributed memory architectures is thorough and insightful. The speedup plots clearly show the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The problem addressed is highly relevant to numerous applications, and the authors have provided a practical solution that can handle datasets previously intractable with existing methods.
* **Novelty:** The combination of inexact ADMM, block-wise CLIME solution, and a carefully designed distributed framework represent a novel and effective approach to tackling a challenging problem.
* **Attention to Implementation Details:**  The discussion of block cyclic distribution and its impact on load balancing and cache performance demonstrates a strong understanding of practical implementation concerns.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Density:** The paper is quite dense and mathematically involved.  Some sections, particularly the discussion of the distributed framework and block cyclic distribution, could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative diagrams. The notation is heavy, and a dedicated glossary might be useful.
* **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The authors briefly mention potential limitations related to the choice of block size and the sparsity pattern, but a more thorough discussion of these limitations and potential future directions would strengthen the paper. For example, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the regularization parameter λ?
* **Comparison to Alternative Distributed Methods:** While the paper compares to several centralized methods, a more direct comparison to other distributed precision estimation algorithms (if they exist) would be valuable.  
* **Real-World Datasets:** The real-world experiments are limited. While the inclusion of Leukemia and Climate datasets is a start, expanding these experiments with larger, more complex datasets would further demonstrate the practicality of the approach.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1 (Sparse Structure):** The discussion of perturbing the sample covariance matrix is intriguing.  However, the theorem presented (Theorem 3) feels somewhat isolated.  A clearer explanation of how this perturbation would be applied in practice and a more detailed analysis of its impact on the final precision matrix estimate would be helpful.
* **Section 4 (Scalable Parallel Computation Framework):** The explanation of block cyclic distribution is somewhat difficult to follow.  A visual representation (e.g., a diagram illustrating the block distribution) would significantly improve clarity.
* **Algorithm 1:** While the algorithm is clearly presented, a brief explanation of the rationale behind the choices of η and ρ would be beneficial.
* **Section 5 (Experimental Results):**  The discussion of the experiments could be more focused. For instance, in the shared-memory experiment, explaining *why* certain column block sizes fail to achieve linear speedup would provide greater insight.




**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical scalability bottleneck in precision matrix estimation.
* Demonstrates significant performance improvements on a large-scale problem.
* Presents a novel and technically sound combination of algorithmic and system-level optimizations.
* The experimental results are compelling and well-documented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and presentation.
* The discussion of limitations is somewhat limited.
* The comparison to other distributed methods is not extensive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field, and the proposed method has the potential to enable a wide range of applications.  Addressing the clarity issues and expanding the discussion of limitations would further strengthen the paper and make it more accessible to a wider audience.  I believe the potential impact of this work justifies its acceptance to NIPS.
