This paper addresses the crucial problem of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensional settings, a key bottleneck in modern statistical learning. The authors propose a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm with several innovations: an inexact ADMM formulation, column-block processing for improved matrix multiplication efficiency, and a block cyclic data distribution scheme for enhanced parallelization. While the core idea is strong and potentially impactful, the presentation suffers from a surprising number of minor flaws and inconsistencies, hindering a clear assessment of the contributions.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. However, the notation is immediately cumbersome. Defining “<p×k” as 'k columns of a p-dimensional matrix' feels inelegant and unnecessary. This notation is used inconsistently throughout. Further, while referencing prior work, the authors frequently omit crucial details—for example, stating that methods "do not scale well" without quantifying *how* they fail or providing specific comparisons.

The algorithmic description (Section 2) is the weakest part of the paper. Algorithm 1 is presented without sufficient explanation. The meaning of “Mat-Mul: { sparse : ... low rank : ... }" is ambiguous. Is this conditional execution, or separate implementations? This needs clarification. The explanation of the inexact ADMM update (8) is terse and doesn’t fully justify the linearization. The rationale for choosing η ≥ ρλ2max(C) isn’t clearly explained within the text – it’s simply stated. The soft and box thresholding operations are defined at the end of the section, interrupting the flow.

Section 3, on leveraging sparse and low-rank structure, feels disjointed. Theorem 3, regarding perturbations to the sample covariance matrix, is vaguely stated ("suitable constants ∆ij") and the connection to practical implementation isn't clear. While theoretically interesting, the theorem feels dropped in without sufficient elaboration. The discussion of low-rank structure is more promising but lacks a concrete example demonstrating the speedup achieved.

Section 4, detailing the scalable parallel framework, is better written, but still suffers from inconsistencies. The description of the block cyclic distribution relies heavily on Figures 1(b) and 1(c), which are not optimally informative. The explanation of how the block size pb × nb × kb is chosen feels ad-hoc. The reasoning behind using a single core per node in the distributed memory experiments (despite the potential for shared-memory gains within a node) isn’t fully justified and feels like an attempt to sidestep potential scaling issues.

The experimental results (Section 5) are presented with limited detail. The figure captions are often lacking specific information (e.g., number of runs, confidence intervals). The comparisons with existing algorithms are not entirely fair – the authors acknowledge Tiger's parameter-free nature but don't adequately address its strengths in terms of statistical performance. The scalability results (Figures 3 and 4) are promising, but the y-axis labels ("Speedup Scolk" and "Speedup Scoreq") are confusing and should be clarified. The statement that CLIME-ADMM took 11 hours to estimate a 1 million dimension matrix on 400 cores is impressive, but a comparison to the theoretical lower bound of computation is missing, making it difficult to assess the true efficiency. The discussion of the comparison between 1 core vs. 4 cores per node in the distributed memory setting seems contradictory – it’s unclear why increasing cores *decreases* performance in some cases.

Throughout the paper, minor formatting issues abound. Variable names are sometimes inconsistent (e.g., X vs. X̂). There are occasional grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The use of boldface for matrices and non-boldface for elements is standard, but sometimes broken.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community.
*   The proposed algorithm has the potential to significantly improve scalability in high-dimensional precision matrix estimation.
*   The inexact ADMM formulation and column-block processing are novel ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is riddled with minor flaws, inconsistencies, and ambiguities.
*   The algorithmic description is unclear and incomplete.
*   The experimental results lack sufficient detail and analysis.
*   The justification for certain design choices (e.g., using a single core per node) is weak.
*   The writing quality needs significant improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential significance of the work, the numerous flaws in presentation and detail detract from its overall quality. While the core ideas are promising, they are obscured by a lack of clarity and rigor. I would lean towards **rejection**, but with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors need to address the issues outlined above to demonstrate the true value of their contribution. A significant rewrite, focusing on clarity, completeness, and rigorous analysis, is required before this paper could be considered for publication at NIPS. The level of polish is currently insufficient for acceptance.
