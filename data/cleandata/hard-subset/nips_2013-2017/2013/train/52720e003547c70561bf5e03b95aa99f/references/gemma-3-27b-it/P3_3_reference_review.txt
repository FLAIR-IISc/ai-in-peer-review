## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to scaling up sparse precision matrix estimation, a crucial task in high-dimensional statistics and machine learning. The authors tackle a genuinely challenging problem – estimating precision matrices with millions of dimensions – and propose a well-motivated and technically sound approach based on a distributed ADMM algorithm tailored to the CLIME estimator. While the paper is dense and could benefit from some clarification, the strong theoretical justification, comprehensive experimental validation, and demonstrated scalability make it a strong candidate for acceptance to NIPS.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of estimating sparse precision matrices (inverse covariance matrices) in the “low sample, high dimension” setting (n < p). Existing methods struggle with ultra-large scale problems due to memory limitations and computational costs. The authors build upon the CLIME estimator, known for its statistical guarantees and computational advantages (specifically its separable structure).  They propose a distributed ADMM (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers) variant, CLIME-ADMM, coupled with a scalable distributed framework.  

The paper appropriately positions itself within the growing body of work on scaling up machine learning algorithms, referencing parameter servers, distributed ADMM, and techniques for parallelizing matrix operations. They correctly highlight the limitations of existing approaches (like MapReduce and gradient descent-based methods) for constrained optimization problems like CLIME.  The connection to existing work on sparse and low-rank structure exploitation is well established, and the authors build upon and extend these ideas. The referencing is generally thorough, although a more explicit comparison to recent distributed estimation frameworks beyond ADMM would be valuable (e.g., those leveraging stochastic gradient descent with specialized communication schemes).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The core strength of this work lies in its demonstrated scalability.  The authors successfully estimate a precision matrix with one million dimensions on 400 cores in a reasonable timeframe (11 hours), showcasing a substantial advancement over existing methods.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The theoretical analysis of the inexact ADMM algorithm provides convergence guarantees, which are crucial for establishing the reliability of the method. The analysis showing O(1/T) convergence for both objective and residual error is a strong point.
* **Clever Optimization:** The combination of inexact ADMM, column-block updates, and leveraging low-rank structure in matrix multiplication are insightful optimizations.  Solving in column blocks, despite seemingly counterintuitive, appears to be a key enabler of performance.
* **Detailed Implementation & Experimental Evaluation:** The paper offers a detailed description of the distributed framework and presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets. The comparisons against established methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) are informative.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion on block cyclic data distribution for load balancing in the distributed environment demonstrates a strong understanding of practical implementation challenges.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Readability:** The paper is quite dense and technical. The notation is heavy, and some explanations could be more intuitive. For example, the motivation behind solving in column blocks feels somewhat buried within the explanation of matrix multiplication efficiency.  
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The role of key hyperparameters (e.g., ρ, η, k) and their influence on performance are not thoroughly discussed. While the paper mentions setting η ≥ ρλ2max(C), the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be helpful to understand.
* **Limited Comparison to Distributed Alternatives:** While the paper acknowledges the existence of other distributed machine learning frameworks, a more in-depth comparison to alternatives specifically designed for distributed optimization (beyond just ADMM) would strengthen the contribution.
* **Real-Data Insights:** The experiments on real datasets (Leukemia and Climate) primarily focus on runtime. Providing insights into the estimated precision matrices (e.g., network structure, biological interpretation) could further highlight the practical utility of the method.



**4. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach with strong theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrates significant scalability with compelling experimental results.
* Offers practical insights into implementing distributed algorithms for precision matrix estimation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and readability.
* A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity is needed.
* A more comprehensive comparison to other distributed optimization frameworks would be beneficial.




**5. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity and hyperparameter sensitivity issues.  A minor expansion of the comparison to alternative distributed optimization frameworks would also be valuable.  The paper’s contribution to scaling up sparse precision matrix estimation is significant, and the technical soundness and thorough evaluation justify its inclusion in the NIPS program.
