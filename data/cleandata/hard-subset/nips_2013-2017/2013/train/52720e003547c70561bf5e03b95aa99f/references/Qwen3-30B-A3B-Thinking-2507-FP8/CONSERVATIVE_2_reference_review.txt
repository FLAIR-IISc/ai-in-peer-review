

# Review: Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation

This paper proposes a distributed algorithm called CLIME-ADMM for estimating large-scale sparse precision matrices, targeting problems with millions of dimensions. The authors extend the CLIME estimator by introducing a column-block processing approach with a scalable distributed framework using block cyclic data distribution. The main claims are that their method achieves O(1/T) convergence rates, scales to million-dimensional problems, and demonstrates linear speedup with increasing cores.

**Strengths:**
The paper builds on established methods (CLIME and ADMM) rather than proposing entirely new theoretical frameworks, which aligns with my preference for methodological soundness. The theoretical convergence rates (Theorems 1-2) are properly established, and the algorithmic details are clearly presented. The experimental results on scalability are compelling, particularly the 11-hour runtime for a million-dimensional precision matrix on 400 cores. The distributed framework using block cyclic distribution is well-motivated and addresses a genuine challenge in parallelizing matrix operations.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its insufficient validation against the most recent state-of-the-art methods. While the authors compare with CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC, they omit several critical recent advances in distributed precision matrix estimation. For instance, [13] (DC-QUIC) is cited but not properly contrasted with the proposed method, despite the authors' claim that DC-QUIC "dramatically slows down when sparsity increases." This claim needs stronger empirical support, particularly for the specific sparsity patterns encountered in real-world applications.

The paper's theoretical contribution is marginal. The O(1/T) convergence rate for inexact ADMM is well-established in the literature (see [12, 27]), and the paper doesn't demonstrate how their specific implementation achieves better convergence than existing distributed ADMM approaches. The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach—particularly how the column-block size k affects performance and whether there's an optimal value for different problem sizes.

The experimental evaluation is insufficient for claiming "substantially more scalable" performance. The authors test only on synthetic data and two real datasets (Leukemia and Climate), but provide no results on larger, more challenging real-world datasets where the method would be most valuable. The comparison with Tiger is misleading since Tiger is not a distributed method, while the authors' method is explicitly designed for distributed environments. The paper claims "CLIME-ADMM achieves a linear speedup" but doesn't provide enough detail on the hardware specifications to assess whether this speedup is truly linear or simply a result of reduced communication overhead.

Most critically, the paper fails to establish a clear advantage over existing distributed methods for precision matrix estimation. The authors cite recent work on distributed optimization ([2, 23, 6, 9]), but don't properly position their contribution within this landscape. The "block cyclic distribution" approach is mentioned as a key innovation, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this is superior to other established data distribution schemes.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound approach, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method represents a significant advance over existing state-of-the-art. The experimental validation is incomplete, and the comparison with relevant recent work is inadequate. The paper's incremental contribution to the field of distributed optimization for precision matrix estimation does not meet the high bar of significance required for NIPS publication.

The authors should strengthen their paper by:
1. Providing a more comprehensive comparison with recent distributed methods for precision matrix estimation
2. Including experiments on larger, more realistic datasets
3. Clarifying the theoretical advantages of their column-block approach
4. Better positioning their work within the context of existing distributed optimization literature

Without these improvements, the paper lacks the novelty and significance required for NIPS. While the distributed framework is technically sound, it represents an incremental extension of existing methods rather than a substantial contribution to the field.

**Summary of key concerns:**
- Insufficient validation against most recent state-of-the-art methods
- Marginal theoretical contribution (O(1/T) convergence is well-known)
- Incomplete experimental evaluation (limited datasets, no large-scale real-world validation)
- Weak justification for column-block processing over column-by-column
- Overstated claims about scalability and advantages over existing methods

The paper would be suitable for a more application-focused venue but does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS.
