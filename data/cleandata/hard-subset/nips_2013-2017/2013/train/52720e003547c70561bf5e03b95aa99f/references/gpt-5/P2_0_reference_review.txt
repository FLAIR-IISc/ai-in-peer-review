Summary
The paper proposes a scalable distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation via CLIME. It reformulates CLIME as a block-column optimization and develops a linearized (inexact) ADMM scheme whose updates decompose into elementwise soft/box-thresholding and matrix multiplications. The key computational ideas are: (i) solving multiple columns at once to leverage cache-friendly matrix–matrix multiplies; (ii) exploiting low-rank structure of the sample covariance C = A A^T (with n << p) to reduce multiplications from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k); and (iii) a block-cyclic data distribution for load balance in distributed memory. Theoretical results establish O(1/T) convergence for objective and residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. The paper also discusses exploiting sparsity by perturbing/thresholding C while preserving sup-norm deviation guarantees. Experiments show speedups versus CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate scaling to p = 10^6 dimensions (≈10^12 parameters) on 400 cores in ~11 hours.

Relation to prior work
ADMM is widely used for large-scale optimization; prior work applied it to precision estimation (e.g., GLasso, CLIME) and to distributed optimization (e.g., GPBS, parameter servers). QUIC/DC-QUIC tackle GLasso with second-order methods and block structure exploitation. The proposed method targets CLIME specifically, uses an inexact/linearized ADMM step (well-known), and concentrates on distributed engineering (block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O) and column-blocking to exploit matrix–matrix multiplications. The low-rank trick for C = A A^T is standard in low-sample regimes, but applied here at very large scale. The paper positions itself with these threads appropriately, though some references are incomplete.

Quality
- Theory: The linearized ADMM updates and O(1/T) convergence bounds are standard and technically sound, given the assumption η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. The results control ergodic objective gap and primal feasibility residuals; this is the right guarantee for ADMM in this setting. However, the theoretical section does not quantify communication/computation complexity per iteration or the dependence of iteration counts on accuracy; guidance on parameter choices (ρ, η) is light.
- Perturbation/sparsification of C: Theorem 3 asserts that adding sub-exponential perturbations preserves a sup-norm deviation bound, enabling thresholding C to sparsify. This seems plausible in order terms, but the assumptions (independence of Δ from the data; constants; PSD of C+Δ) and implications for feasibility/conditioning are under-specified. Since CLIME does not require PD of C, loss of PSD might not be fatal, but more care and empirical validation would help.
- Empirics: The engineering is convincing. The shared-memory vs distributed-memory analyses are thoughtful, with clear explanations of cache contention and load balancing. Scaling to p = 10^6 is an impressive milestone. Comparisons include CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC. However, these methods solve different estimators (CLIME vs GLasso vs TIGER) and the evaluation harmonizes sparsity rather than accuracy; PR curves are shown only on synthetic data. A more apples-to-apples comparison (e.g., CLIME vs CLIME with different solvers; time-to-same-KKT residual) would better isolate algorithmic advantages.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with clear algorithm pseudocode and a good description of the distributed framework. The block-cyclic distribution and matrix multiplication pipeline are well explained. There are some typos and incomplete references; Theorem statements and norms (e.g., the weighted norm in Theorem 2) could be clarified. Practical choices (ρ, η, stopping criteria, communication costs) should be stated more explicitly. The perturbation section would benefit from a crisper statement of conditions.

Originality
- Algorithmic novelty is moderate: linearized ADMM and column-blocking are known ideas; the novelty lies in their tailored integration for CLIME and the large-scale distributed implementation with block-cyclic distribution. The perturbation perspective for sparsifying C in the CLIME setting is an interesting twist, albeit incremental.
- The main contribution is a systems/algorithms integration that enables extreme-scale CLIME, with some theory to justify the ADMM variant.

Significance
For practitioners needing sparse precision estimation at extreme dimensionalities, this work is valuable. Demonstrating CLIME at p = 10^6 with near-linear scaling and careful distributed design advances the practical state of the art. The ideas (column-blocking, low-rank multiplication, block-cyclic distribution) are likely reusable in other constrained problems. The theoretical results, while standard, provide necessary convergence assurances.

Pros
- Tackles an important, challenging scaling problem with clear practical impact.
- Simple, parallelizable updates; effective use of column-blocks and low-rank structure.
- Thoughtful distributed design (block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O) with empirical validation.
- Convergence guarantees appropriate for the algorithm.
- Strong empirical scaling to million-dimensional problems.

Cons
- Comparisons mix different estimators; need more controlled CLIME-vs-CLIME baselines and accuracy-vs-time metrics.
- Parameter selection (ρ, η), stopping criteria, and comms/compute complexity not fully specified.
- Perturbation/thresholding theory is underdeveloped; PSD and independence assumptions unclear.
- Some clarity issues and incomplete references.

Questions for authors
- Provide heuristic or adaptive rules for ρ and η; report sensitivity.
- Specify stopping criteria (primal/dual residual tolerances) and how they scale with p, n.
- Quantify communication volume per iteration and strong/weak scaling efficiency.
- Clarify assumptions and guarantees in Theorem 3 (independence of Δ, PSD, constants), and show empirical effects of sparsifying C on accuracy and runtime.
- Include a pure CLIME baseline (same estimator, different solver) with time-to-target-KKT residual.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper’s main value is an effective, carefully engineered distributed method that pushes CLIME to unprecedented scales, supported by reasonable theory. Strengthening the experimental comparisons (CLIME-to-CLIME) and clarifying the perturbation result would further solidify the contribution.

Confidence: High on technical/algorithmic assessment; medium on novelty vs closely related distributed ADMM work due to some missing references.
