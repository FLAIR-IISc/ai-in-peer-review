

**Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"**

**Summary**: This paper proposes a distributed ADMM-based algorithm for large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation, focusing on the CLIME estimator. The authors claim to address the challenge of scaling precision matrix estimation to millions of dimensions through column-block processing, block cyclic distribution, and inexact ADMM. They demonstrate scalability on shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, claiming to estimate a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours on 400 cores.

**Strengths**:
- The paper addresses a relevant challenge in high-dimensional statistics.
- The column-block approach extends existing ADMM-based methods in a reasonable way.
- Theoretical convergence rates are established for the proposed algorithm.
- The experimental results show promising scalability for large-scale problems.

**Weaknesses**:

1. **Critical Mathematical Errors**:
   - The paper states "with a million dimensions p = 10^6, the total number of parameters to be estimated is a trillion, p^2 = 10^12." This is fundamentally incorrect. For a symmetric precision matrix, the number of parameters is p(p+1)/2 ≈ 5 × 10^11, not 10^12. This error appears multiple times (including in the abstract and conclusion), undermining the paper's credibility.
   - The paper claims "the sample covariance matrix can be computed as C = AA^T" where "ai = 1/√n (Ri - R̄)". This is mathematically incorrect; the correct formula is C = (1/n)AA^T. This error in the problem setup affects all subsequent analysis.

2. **Formatting and Notation Inconsistencies**:
   - Inconsistent use of mathematical notation: "S p ++" should be "S^p_{++}", "p2" should be "p^2", "λmax(C)" should be "λ_{max}(C)".
   - In Algorithm 1, step 5 is written as "Xt+1 = soft(Xt -Vt, 1η )" which should be "Xt+1 = soft(Xt - Vt, 1/η)".
   - The paper uses "<p×k" to denote matrix dimensions but should use "ℝ^{p×k}".
   - Inconsistent formatting of "Ω̂" (sometimes with extra characters).

3. **Experimental Misrepresentations**:
   - The paper claims to estimate "a precision matrix of one million dimension and one trillion parameters" - this is incorrect (5 × 10^11 parameters, not 10^12). This misstatement appears in multiple places, including the abstract and conclusion.
   - The paper states "the entire computation took about 11 hours" without specifying the hardware configuration, making it impossible to assess the claimed performance.
   - In Section 5.2, the paper claims "the entire computation took about 11 hours" but doesn't provide details on the dataset size or other experimental conditions.

4. **Theoretical Claims**:
   - The paper claims "the rates of convergence of the objective to the optimum as well as residuals of constraint violation are both O(1/T)" but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the conditions under which this holds.
   - The proof techniques referenced to "Appendix A in the supplement" are not provided in the submission.

5. **Technical Details**:
   - In Algorithm 1, step 9 states "V̂t+1 = CYt+1" but earlier defines "V̂t = CYt", creating an inconsistency.
   - The paper claims "the number of parameters scales quadratically with the number of variables" (true for the full matrix), but then incorrectly uses p^2 instead of p(p+1)/2 in all calculations.

**Recommendation**: I recommend rejection. The fundamental mathematical errors (particularly the parameter count error) are severe and undermine the paper's main claims. The inconsistent notation and critical errors in the problem setup indicate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. While the paper addresses an important problem and has some promising ideas, these errors make it impossible to assess the validity of the claims.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:

**Pro**:
- Addresses a relevant problem in high-dimensional statistics
- Proposes a reasonable extension of existing ADMM-based methods
- Provides theoretical convergence rates
- Demonstrates promising scalability in experiments

**Con**:
- Fundamental mathematical errors (parameter count, covariance calculation)
- Inconsistent and incorrect notation throughout
- Claims of performance based on incorrect parameter counts
- Insufficient experimental details for reproducibility
- Errors in the algorithm description (e.g., step 5 in Algorithm 1)

The paper's errors are not minor; they are foundational to the main claims. The misstatement of parameter counts alone is a critical error that would require substantial revision before the paper could be considered for publication. The authors should carefully re-examine their mathematical foundations before resubmitting.
