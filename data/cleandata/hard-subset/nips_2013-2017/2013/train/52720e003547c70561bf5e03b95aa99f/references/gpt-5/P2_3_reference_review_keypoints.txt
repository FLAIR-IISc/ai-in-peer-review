- Proposes a distributed column-block linearized ADMM solver for CLIME to scale sparse precision estimation to ultra-high dimensions (p ≈ 10^6).  
- Core algorithmic ideas: split variable to separate ℓ1 objective from ∞-norm constraint; linearized ADMM update with closed-form soft-thresholding.  
- Systems ideas: process k columns at a time to use matrix–matrix multiplies, exploit C = AA^T low rank to reduce cost to O(n p k), and use block-cyclic distribution for load balance and cache efficiency.  
- Theoretical claim: O(1/T) ergodic rates under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 and a perturbation/thresholding scheme for sparsifying C that preserves CLIME deviation bounds.  
- Empirical claim: strong shared- and distributed-memory scalability, including a 1e6-dimensional run on 400 cores (~11 hours); shows better scalability vs CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC.  
- Relation to prior work: ADMM and linearized ADMM convergence are standard; QUIC/DC-QUIC and TIGER are related baselines; novelty is mainly the systems-scale application to CLIME.  
- Algorithmic quality: linearized ADMM steps and closed-form proximal operators are appropriate and stabilized by the step-size condition.  
- Theoretical concerns: theorem statements and notation are sometimes imprecise (e.g., weighted norm PSD conditions); the perturbation theorem lacks full specification of distributional/scaling assumptions.  
- Empirical concerns: statistical accuracy is weakly evaluated (comparisons tuned to same sparsity rather than equal regularization/accuracy); stopping criteria, ρ/η choices, and communication/memory cost breakdowns are missing.  
- Reproducibility/clary issues: dimensional inconsistencies, ambiguous annotations in Algorithm 1, unclear symbol cross-references, and some misformatted references impede reproducibility.  
- Originality: systems and engineering contributions (column-block ADMM, low-rank exploitation, block-cyclic distributed implementation) are novel at this scale; optimization and covariance-thresholding ideas are mostly known.  
- Significance: scaling CLIME to p ≈ 10^6 with near-linear speedups is practically significant and potentially reusable for other large constrained problems.  
- Pros: addresses an important scaling bottleneck; leverages efficient primitives and avoids storing C explicitly; combines low-rank and sparsity exploitation; provides interpretable convergence guarantees.  
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty; clarity and notation problems; empirical focus on runtime over statistical validity; sparse-perturbation result underspecified; missing hyperparameter/communication/memory and PD/symmetrization details.  
- Suggested fixes: clarify dimensions/notation, precisely define weighted norms in Theorem 2, state perturbation assumptions, and fix references.  
- Suggested experiments/details: report stopping criteria and sensitivity to ρ/η, show primal/dual residual curves and accuracy-vs-time plots, equal-regularization comparisons, communication/computation and per-node memory breakdowns, ablations vs column-wise BLAS, and symmetrization/PD correction cost.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept (6/10) — strong engineering and scalability, but modest theoretical novelty and need for clearer presentation and stronger statistical evaluation.
