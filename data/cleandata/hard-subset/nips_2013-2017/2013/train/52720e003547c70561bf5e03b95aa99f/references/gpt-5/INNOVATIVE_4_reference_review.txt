Summary
This paper tackles ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation by rethinking how to solve CLIME at scale. The authors propose an inexact ADMM tailored to CLIME that operates on column blocks (k columns at a time) rather than the traditional column-by-column approach. The algorithm linearizes the quadratic term to enable simple soft-thresholding updates and reduces the dominant cost to matrix multiplications. Two structural accelerations are emphasized: (i) exploiting low-rank structure of the sample covariance via C = AA^T (with n ≪ p), thereby replacing O(p^2 k) multiplies by O(npk), and (ii) optionally coarsening/thresholding C while preserving the sup-norm deviation bound needed by CLIME, supported by a perturbation theorem. The systems contribution is substantial: a block-cyclic distribution scheme to achieve load balance and cache efficiency on distributed-memory systems, with scalable parallel I/O and process grids. Theoretical results establish O(1/T) rates for both objective suboptimality (ergodic) and residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2. Experiments demonstrate strong speedups in shared and distributed memory; notably, the authors estimate a 1,000,000-dimensional precision matrix (1e12 parameters) in ~11 hours on 400 cores, and show favorable scaling compared to CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC on smaller problems.

Relation to prior work
CLIME is a well-studied estimator with strong statistical guarantees (Cai et al.). Prior solvers include IPM- and ADMM-based columnwise approaches and QUIC/DC-QUIC for Graphical Lasso. The inexact/linearized ADMM and O(1/T) rates are standard, but the combination of column-block updates, low-rank multiplications, and a carefully engineered block-cyclic distributed implementation is novel in this context. The perturbation result connects to covariance thresholding ideas (e.g., Mazumder & Hastie), but is tailored to preserving the CLIME deviation requirement. Compared to frameworks like parameter servers or GPBS, the paper addresses global constraints with a distribution strategy matched to matrix multiplications.

Strengths
- Bold and impactful scaling: Demonstrates estimation at p = 10^6, a clear advance over prior systems.
- Clear algorithmic decomposition: elementwise prox + matrix-matrix multiplies; amenable to vectorization and distribution.
- Practical systems design: block-cyclic data distribution, parallel I/O, process grid choices, and attention to cache behavior.
- Structural accelerations: leveraging low rank (C = AA^T) to reduce complexity; plausible covariance coarsening rationale with a supporting theorem.
- Solid though standard theory: O(1/T) convergence for objective and residuals with explicit parameter conditions.
- Empirical scalability: near-linear speedups in distributed memory for moderate/large k; convincing large-scale timing results.

Weaknesses
- Theory is largely incremental: the ADMM rate and linearization conditions are standard; no novel statistical guarantees for the estimator beyond CLIME’s and the perturbation lemma (which is not validated empirically).
- Limited statistical evaluation at scale: beyond PR curves on small/medium datasets, there is little assessment of estimation error, constraint feasibility, or support recovery for large p; stopping criteria and tolerance reporting are unclear.
- Missing discussion of symmetry/PD: CLIME solutions often require symmetrization; the paper does not discuss symmetrization/PSD enforcement or downstream implications.
- Parameter selection: guidance for λ, ρ, η, and especially block size k is ad hoc; sensitivity analyses are minimal.
- Comparison fairness: baselines are single-machine or small-scale; no distributed baselines (e.g., distributed Graphical Lasso frameworks) are included for the large-scale regime.
- The perturbation/coarsening idea (Theorem 3) is not exercised in experiments; practical recipes and trade-offs are not quantified.
- Clarity/notation: a few inconsistencies (e.g., box() signature, missing E in Algorithm 1 line); minor typos.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The algorithmic derivations are consistent, and the convergence assumptions are explicit. The systems implementation appears competent and thoughtfully engineered. The empirical section convincingly demonstrates systems scalability but is weaker on statistical accuracy and fairness across methods at scale.

Clarity
Generally well organized with clear motivation. Some notation slips and minor inconsistencies should be fixed. More detail on stopping criteria, tolerance satisfaction, parameter selection, and symmetrization would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately novel. The inexact ADMM and column-block idea are not fundamentally new, but the combination with low-rank multiplications and a block-cyclic distributed framework for CLIME, along with a million-dimensional demonstration, is a meaningful advance in scalability.

Significance
High practical significance: the ability to handle p in the millions is rare and valuable, opening up previously infeasible precision estimation tasks. Methodologically, the work provides a reusable design pattern for constrained matrix problems in distributed settings.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- How do you ensure symmetry/PD in the final estimate (symmetrization rule)? Impact on performance?
- What are the stopping criteria and constraint feasibility tolerances achieved at scale?
- Provide more guidance on selecting k, ρ, η, and λ; include sensitivity analyses.
- Quantify communication cost and its scaling; include roofline-style or bandwidth/bottleneck analysis.
- Validate Theorem 3 empirically with coarsened C, showing speed vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- Consider releasing code and scripts for reproducibility.

Pros
- Demonstrates truly unprecedented scale for sparse precision estimation.
- Clever exploitation of low-rank structure and matrix-matrix multiplies.
- Thoughtful distributed systems design with strong empirical speedups.
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees and a useful perturbation lemma.

Cons
- Statistical evaluation limited, especially at large scale.
- Theory incremental; perturbation idea not empirically validated.
- Missing discussion of symmetrization/PD and parameter selection.
- Baseline comparisons at large scale are limited.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some gaps in statistical validation and mostly standard theory, the paper introduces a bold and practically important systems/algorithmic contribution that significantly advances the state of the art in scalable precision estimation.

Confidence
High for systems/scalability assessment; moderate for theoretical/statistical aspects.
