- Summary: Proposes a scalable distributed solver for CLIME using block-column reformulation and a linearized (inexact) ADMM whose updates reduce to elementwise soft/box-thresholding and matrix multiplications; exploits column-blocks, low-rank C = A A^T, and block-cyclic data distribution, with O(1/T) convergence and empirical scaling to p=10^6 on 400 cores.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on well-known ADMM and low-rank tricks and relates to QUIC/DC-QUIC and distributed optimization literature; novelty is in engineering and integration for extreme-scale CLIME rather than new core algorithms.

- Theory: Provides standard O(1/T) ergodic convergence bounds under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2, but lacks iteration-complexity dependence on accuracy and per-iteration communication/computation costs.

- Perturbation/sparsification: Claims sup-norm guarantees when perturbing/thresholding C, but assumptions (independence of Δ, PSD of C+Δ, constants) and implications for conditioning/feasibility are under-specified and need more detail and validation.

- Empirics: Strong engineering evidence and scaling analyses (shared vs distributed memory); shows large speedups and million-dimension scaling, but comparisons mix different estimators and lack apples-to-apples CLIME-vs-CLIME baselines and time-to-target-accuracy metrics.

- Clarity: Generally readable with clear pseudocode and system descriptions; some typos, incomplete references, and unclear theorem/notation and practical parameter/stopping choices.

- Originality: Moderate — algorithmic pieces (linearized ADMM, column-blocking, low-rank trick) are known, but their tailored integration and distributed implementation for extreme-scale CLIME is a useful systems contribution.

- Significance: Valuable for practitioners needing extreme-scale sparse precision estimation; engineering ideas are likely reusable and the theoretical guarantees are adequate for the deployed algorithm.

- Pros: Tackles a hard scaling problem; simple parallel updates; effective use of column-blocks and low-rank structure; thoughtful distributed design with strong empirical scaling and convergence guarantees.

- Cons: Evaluation mixes different estimators; insufficient guidance on parameter selection (ρ, η), stopping criteria, and communication complexity; perturbation theory is underdeveloped; clarity and references need improvement.

- Questions for authors: Provide heuristics/adaptive rules and sensitivity for ρ and η; specify stopping criteria and scaling with p,n; quantify per-iteration communication volume and scaling efficiency; clarify assumptions and constants in Theorem 3 and empirically show effects of sparsifying C; include a pure CLIME baseline with time-to-target-KKT residual.

- Recommendation: Borderline accept — strong systems/engineering contribution that advances practical CLIME scaling but needs stronger controlled comparisons and clearer perturbation/parameter details.
