

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation in ultra-high dimensional settings (up to millions of dimensions). The authors focus on the CLIME estimator and introduce CLIME-ADMM, a variant that solves the problem in column-blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging block cyclic distribution for efficient parallelization. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) an inexact ADMM algorithm with O(1/T) convergence rates, (2) a column-block approach that exploits matrix-matrix multiplications to reduce cache misses, and (3) a scalable distributed framework for shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures.

The work builds upon recent NIPS papers on distributed optimization [2, 9, 23] and precision matrix estimation [3, 13, 14, 19]. It distinguishes itself from prior work by addressing the specific challenges of ultra-high dimensional precision estimation, where even moderate-sized problems exceed the memory capacity of a single machine. The paper carefully compares with CLIME, Tiger, and DC-QUIC, showing that CLIME-ADMM scales better to large dimensions while maintaining statistical performance.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses an important problem in modern statistics and machine learning: ultra-high dimensional precision matrix estimation. The authors provide compelling motivation for why this problem matters, noting that with p=10^6, the number of parameters to estimate is 10^12.

2. The technical contribution is sound and well-supported. The theoretical convergence rates (Theorems 1-2) are properly established with references to related work. The algorithm design is carefully motivated by the problem structure, particularly the sparse and low-rank properties of precision matrix estimation problems.

3. The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing. The paper demonstrates scalability to 1 million dimensions (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores, significantly outperforming existing methods. The shared-memory and distributed-memory scalability results (Figures 3-4) clearly show near-linear speedup with the number of cores.

4. The paper provides valuable implementation details for distributed computing frameworks, including the block cyclic distribution strategy to minimize cache misses and improve load balancing. This is particularly useful for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper could provide a more thorough comparison with the most recent distributed methods for precision matrix estimation, particularly those from NIPS 2014-2015. While it references [2, 9, 23], it doesn't explicitly compare with other recent distributed optimization approaches that might be applicable.

2. The discussion of low-rank structure (Section 3.2) is somewhat brief. While the paper shows that the low-rank property of the sample covariance matrix can reduce computation complexity, it doesn't provide empirical results demonstrating the impact of this approach on the actual runtime for large-scale problems.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method would perform when the underlying precision matrix is not sparse, which is a common concern in real-world applications. While the paper acknowledges the sparsity assumption, it could better discuss the robustness of the method to deviations from this assumption.

4. The distributed-memory experiments (Section 5.2) could include more detailed analysis of communication overhead, which is crucial for understanding the scalability limits of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization for high-dimensional statistics. The problem is well-motivated, the technical approach is sound, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's scalability. The theoretical guarantees and careful design of the algorithm to leverage problem structure are particularly strong.

The paper's main contribution is the practical framework for ultra-high dimensional precision matrix estimation, which is a significant step beyond the current state of the art. The method's ability to scale to 1 million dimensions (1 trillion parameters) is impressive and represents a substantial advance over existing methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in modern statistics with clear relevance to big data applications
- Provides a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution
- Demonstrates impressive scalability results (1M dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores)
- Offers valuable implementation insights for distributed optimization
- Clear theoretical guarantees and thorough experimental validation

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Could provide more thorough comparison with the most recent distributed methods
- The low-rank structure discussion could be expanded
- Limited discussion of robustness to non-sparse precision matrices
- Some details about communication overhead in distributed experiments are missing

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong contribution to the field. The technical content is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate significant practical value. While there are minor areas for improvement, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on statistical machine learning and distributed optimization, and it advances the state of the art in scalable precision matrix estimation.

Given the paper's technical soundness, novelty in addressing ultra-high dimensional settings, and significant practical impact, I recommend acceptance.
