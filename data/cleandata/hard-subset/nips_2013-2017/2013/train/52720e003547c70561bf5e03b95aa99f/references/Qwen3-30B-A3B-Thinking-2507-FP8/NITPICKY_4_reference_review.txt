

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM approach for the CLIME estimator. The authors address the scalability challenge of precision matrix estimation in ultra-high-dimensional settings (e.g., p = 10^6) by leveraging column-block processing, block cyclic distribution, and exploiting sparse/low-rank structures. The method demonstrates linear scalability on both shared-memory and distributed-memory architectures, achieving a 11-hour runtime for p = 10^6.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on distributed optimization for large-scale statistical problems. The column-block approach is well-motivated, and the theoretical convergence guarantees (Theorems 1-2) are properly established. The experimental results demonstrate impressive scalability, particularly on the million-dimensional problem. The integration of sparse/low-rank structure exploitation (Section 3) is practical and well-justified.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notation Errors
The paper contains numerous minor but distracting formatting errors that undermine technical credibility:

1. Inconsistent notation: "Ω0 = Σ−10" (should be Ω0 = Σ0⁻¹), "‖A‖22" (should be ‖A‖₂²), "‖A‖∞ = max1≤i≤p,1≤j≤n |Aij |" (should be max_{1≤i≤p,1≤j≤n} |A_{ij}|).

2. Algorithm 1 has critical formatting issues: "soft(Xt −Vt, 1η )" (should be soft(Xt - Vt, 1/η)), and inconsistent spacing in "1η" throughout.

3. Inconsistent use of mathematical symbols: "Xt+1 = soft(Xt −Vt, 1η )" versus "Vt = ρη (2V̂t − V̂t−1)" (missing spaces).

### Theoretical Inconsistencies
1. Theorem 1 states "‖X̄T ‖1 − ‖X‖1 ≤ η‖X‖22 T" (should be η‖X‖₂²/T), and Theorem 2 uses inconsistent notation (‖XT −XT−1‖2η ρ I−C2 vs. standard norms).

2. The algorithm initialization (step 3) includes "V̂0 = 0" but Theorems 1-2 assume X₀ = Z₀ = Y₀ = 0, creating a mismatch between theory and implementation.

### Missing Technical Details
1. The paper claims "the entire computation took about 11 hours" for p=10⁶ but fails to specify the sample size (n) or sparsity level, which are crucial for reproducibility.

2. The block size parameters (pb×nb×kb = 10×10×1 for k ≤ 10 and 10×10×10 for k > 10) are chosen without justification, making it difficult to replicate the experimental results.

3. Theorem 3 claims "if |Cij| ≤ c √log p/n, then it can be safely truncated to 0" but never specifies the constant c, which is essential for practical implementation.

### Related Work Issues
1. The paper cites "Tiger" as a method but does not properly reference it (should cite Liu & Wang's "Tiger: A tuning-insensitive approach...").

2. The reference to [10] as "Molecular classification of cancer" is inconsistent with the paper's citation style (it's listed as "dAspremont" in references).

### Experimental Ambiguities
1. Section 5.2 states "For q cores, we use the process grid q2 × 2 since p n" - this is confusingly written (should be q × q or q² × 2).

2. In Table 2, the comparison between "1 core per node" and "4 cores per node" lacks statistical significance measures (standard deviations).

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a valuable contribution to scalable precision matrix estimation, the numerous minor but pervasive technical and formatting errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. These issues are not merely typographical - they represent fundamental inconsistencies in the mathematical presentation that could mislead readers attempting to understand or reproduce the method.

The paper would be substantially improved by:
1. Correcting all mathematical notation and formatting inconsistencies
2. Providing complete experimental details (n, sparsity, block sizes)
3. Justifying the arbitrary block size choices
4. Properly citing all related work

Given the paper's technical depth and potential impact, I recommend a major revision to address these issues before publication. The current version's technical inconsistencies are too numerous to overlook, and they detract from the paper's otherwise strong contribution to the field.
