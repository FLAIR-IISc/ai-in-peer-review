Summary
The paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation based on CLIME, targeting ultra-high dimensions (up to p = 10^6). The core contributions are: (i) an inexact ADMM variant for CLIME where the quadratic term is linearized and a proximal term is added, yielding elementwise updates for X and Z and reducing the heavy computation to matrix-matrix multiplications; (ii) solving CLIME in column blocks (k columns at a time) to exploit cache-friendly BLAS3 operations; (iii) leveraging either sparsity in iterates or the low-rank factorization C = AA′ (n ≪ p) to replace O(p^2k) multiplies with O(npk); and (iv) a shared- and distributed-memory framework using block-cyclic distribution for load-balancing. The authors give O(1/T) convergence rates (objective gap and residuals under η ≥ ρ λmax(C)^2), propose a covariance “coarsening” via thresholding justified by a deviation bound, and report scalability: estimating a 10^6-dimensional precision matrix in 11 hours using 400 cores.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CLIME [Cai, Liu, Luo 2011] and subsequent statistical results, and contrasts with Glasso solvers (e.g., QUIC/DC-QUIC) and TIGER. On the optimization side, it uses inexact/linearized ADMM ideas (e.g., Boyd et al., He & Yuan) and distributed BLAS/ScaLAPACK with block-cyclic distribution, aligning with HPC practices (Goto & Van de Geijn; Lam et al.). The block approach appears new for CLIME and targets distributed scalability, whereas many prior works target single-node or modest parallelism.

Strengths
- Practical significance: addresses a real bottleneck—memory/compute scaling for CLIME—by avoiding explicit C and relying on A and BLAS3/GEMM-friendly blocking.
- Algorithmic design: the linearized ADMM per block yields closed-form soft-thresholding and box-projection; the block-cyclic distribution is a sensible strategy for load balance.
- Convergence: provides O(1/T) rates for both objective and residuals under clear conditions on η and ρ, with proofs (in supplement).
- Engineering: comprehensive consideration of shared vs distributed memory, blocking, and cache effects; demonstrates near-linear speedups and large-scale runs.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and clarity:
- Scaled vs unscaled ADMM inconsistency: Eq. (4) uses Lρ = ||X||1 + ρ〈Y, CX − Z〉 + (ρ/2)||CX − Z||^2 while later Y is called a “scaled dual variable,” and the update is Yt+1 = Yt + CXt+1 − Zt+1. In the scaled form, the linear term with ρ〈Y, ·〉 should disappear. This inconsistency needs correction.
- V update factor error: From (8)–(9), Vt = (ρ/η)C(2Yt − Yt−1) and V̂t = CYt, so Vt = (ρ/η)(2V̂t − V̂t−1). Algorithm 1 (Step 10) shows Vt+1 = ρη(2V̂t+1 − V̂t), which appears dimensionally inconsistent (η should be in the denominator, not multiplied).
- Box operator signature mismatch: Algorithm 1 Step 7 uses Zt+1 = box(Ut+1 + Yt, λ), but (10) and the definition include E (Z is projected onto ||Z − E||∞ ≤ λ). The pseudocode omits E.
- Dimensions/sign conventions: In Sec. 4, A is stated as n×p and U ∈ n×k, but earlier A ∈ Rp×n and U = CX ∈ Rp×k (consistent with U = A(A′X)). The dimensionality in Sec. 4 is inconsistent.
- Convergence statements: Theorem 1 states ||X̄T||1 − ||X||1 ≤ (η||X||^2F)/T “for any CX = Z.” As written, this bound is hard to interpret (it should compare to the optimal feasible X*, not an arbitrary feasible pair, or explicitly clarify the primal-dual gap notion used). Theorem 2 uses a seminorm ||·||η/ρ I − C^2, requiring η ≥ ρλ^2max(C). Please clarify whether the residual bound is on the last iterate or ergodic averages, and whether objective optimality is guaranteed under these assumptions.
- Symmetry and post-processing: Standard CLIME requires a symmetrization step across columns (e.g., Cai–Liu–Luo rule). The block-based scheme solves independent subproblems; it is unclear how symmetry is enforced across blocks and how the final symmetric estimator is formed and stored.
- Parameter selection: λ, ρ, η, and k critically affect accuracy and convergence; no guidance is provided (e.g., estimating λmax(C) to set η, stopping criteria, tolerance thresholds). This hinders reproducibility.

Statistical aspects:
- Covariance “coarsening”: Theorem 3 claims sub-exponential perturbations preserve sup-norm deviation. However, the practical procedure advocated appears to be deterministic thresholding of C at level c√(log p/n). Please clarify whether ∆ is deterministic or random, how constants are chosen, and whether such truncation affects bias in finite samples and the feasibility/accuracy of the CLIME constraints. The result as stated is loose and lacks practical prescription.
- Comparisons and fairness: Runtime comparisons tune each method to achieve a target sparsity, which can bias wall-time vs. accuracy tradeoffs. Precision-recall plots are provided but details (early stopping, tolerances, choice of λ grid, symmetrization) are missing. TIGER is known to be tuning-insensitive; discussing statistical error (Frobenius/L1/operator) would strengthen claims.

Experimental/reporting issues:
- Missing/garbled tables/figures: Table 1 and Table 2 are incomplete; numbers like “200×1 0.37 0.68 …” appear out of place; Figures are referenced but not shown here. It is hard to assess empirical support.
- Storage of outputs at p = 10^6: How is the 10^12-parameter estimate stored? Are results streamed in blocks to disk? What is the sparsity of the final solution? This is critical for credibility at this scale.
- Implementation details: Termination criteria, iteration counts, precise hardware (memory per node, interconnect), and the exact block sizes (pb×nb×kb) and their tuning rationale are not fully specified.

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “achetectures,” “exisiting,” “pertubed,” “colletion,” “spase,” “reguarlized”); incorrect notation (||·||22 instead of ||·||F^2); inconsistent variable shapes and missing arguments in function definitions; several references are incomplete/incorrect. These detract from clarity and hinder reproducibility.

Pros
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a pragmatic, HPC-aware design.
- Sensible use of low-rank factorization and block-cyclic distribution; near-linear speedups demonstrated.
- Closed-form updates via linearized ADMM enable efficient kernels.

Cons
- Multiple technical inconsistencies/errors in the algorithm derivation and pseudocode (scaled ADMM form, V update, dimensions, box operator).
- Missing critical details on parameter selection, symmetrization, termination, and output storage at extreme scales.
- Empirical section is difficult to assess due to missing/garbled tables/figures and limited statistical validation.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation: Reject
While the engineering idea is promising and potentially impactful, the manuscript in its current form contains enough technical inconsistencies, missing details, and presentation issues to preclude acceptance. With corrected derivations/pseudocode, clarified statistical and implementation details (especially symmetry, parameter choices, and storage), and fully reported experiments, this could become a solid contribution. My recommendation is to revise substantially and resubmit. Confidence: High.
