This paper presents a distributed algorithm, CLIME-ADMM, for estimating sparse precision matrices in ultra-high-dimensional settings. The core idea is to leverage an inexact ADMM formulation combined with block-wise processing and block cyclic data distribution to achieve scalability. The authors position their work within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms and provide a thorough related work section.

While the problem addressed is important, and the authors demonstrate significant scalability in their experiments, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of sufficient detail in several crucial areas, leading me to recommend rejection. My concerns stem from a perceived lack of rigor in the presentation, inconsistencies in notation, and a tendency to gloss over potentially problematic implementation details.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The empirical results demonstrate that CLIME-ADMM scales well to high dimensions and can leverage a large number of cores. The reported execution time for a one-million-dimensional problem is impressive.
*   **Problem Relevance:** Estimating sparse precision matrices is a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning with numerous applications. The focus on ultra-high-dimensional settings is timely and important.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of related work in precision matrix estimation, distributed optimization, and scalable machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For instance, the definition of matrix norms changes slightly (elementwise vs. standard norms) without explicit clarification. The use of boldface for matrices and non-boldface for elements (A vs. Aij) is standard, but the introduction of boldface lowercase for blocks (Aij) is unconventional and confusing.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Theoretical Analysis:** While Theorems 1 and 2 are presented, the assumptions required for their validity (e.g., η ≥ ρλ2max(C)) are not adequately justified or discussed in the context of practical application.  Furthermore, the relationship between the convergence rate and practical performance isn’t clearly established.  The proofs, deferred to a supplement, are crucial but not readily assessable within the scope of this review.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Implementation:** The paper lacks crucial implementation details. For example, the choice of block size (pb x nb x kb) in the distributed memory implementation is mentioned, but the rationale behind it is weak. The impact of different block sizes on performance isn't thoroughly investigated, and the interplay between block size, cache performance, and communication overhead isn’t sufficiently explained. The use of ScaLAPACK is noted but lacks discussion regarding any tuning or optimization applied for this specific problem.
*   **Ambiguous Claims about Low-Rank Structure:** The discussion of leveraging low-rank structure (Section 3.2) is vague.  Simply stating that the computational complexity reduces to O(npk) isn't enough. A detailed explanation of how the low-rank approximation is applied in practice, including any associated error analysis, is missing.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The statement that CLIME-ADMM achieves "almost linear" scaling with the number of cores is an oversimplification. Figure 3(b) and 4(b) clearly show deviations from ideal linear scaling, particularly at higher core counts.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** Step 6 of Algorithm 1, “Z-update: Zt+1 = box(Ut+1 + Yt, λ)”, is concerning.  The box constraint should likely be ‖Zt+1 − E‖∞ ≤ λ, not ‖Ut+1 + Yt − E‖∞ ≤ λ, which seems logically flawed.  This is a significant issue.
*   **Redundant Information:**  Some sections, particularly the introduction, contain repetitive information and could be more concise.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Scalable algorithm for a relevant problem.
*   Thorough related work section.
*   Potential for significant impact if the claims hold up.

**Con:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Lack of rigor in theoretical analysis and implementation details.
*   Overstated claims regarding scalability.
*   Significant technical issue in Algorithm 1 (Step 6).
*   The overall presentation lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.



In conclusion, while CLIME-ADMM demonstrates promising scalability, the numerous issues detailed above prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper requires substantial revision, including more rigorous analysis, clearer presentation, detailed implementation descriptions, and a careful review of technical correctness. The current manuscript falls short of the standards expected for a NIPS publication.
