Summary
The paper presents a scalable algorithmic and systems framework for estimating sparse precision matrices via the CLIME estimator in ultra–high-dimensional settings (up to p = 10^6). The authors reformulate CLIME as a set of linear programs separable by columns and propose an inexact (linearized) ADMM tailored to column blocks: the X-update is a proximal soft-thresholding step after linearizing the quadratic penalty; the Z-update is a box projection (∞-norm constraint), and the dual update follows standard ADMM. The key practical ideas are: (i) solving multiple columns at once (column-blocking) to replace repeated matrix–vector products with more efficient matrix–matrix multiplies; (ii) exploiting low-rank structure C = A A^T to reduce the cost of multiplications to O(npk) when n ≪ p; and (iii) a block-cyclic distributed data layout (ScaLAPACK style) to balance load and limit cache misses. Theoretical results establish O(1/T) rates for objective suboptimality and residuals under η ≥ ρ λ_max^2(C). A secondary result argues one can sparsify/perturb C (e.g., threshold small entries) while preserving the sup-norm deviation bound required for CLIME’s statistical guarantees. Experiments compare against CLIME (flare), TIGER, and DC-QUIC, and demonstrate strong scaling on shared- and distributed-memory systems, culminating in a 1e6-dimensional run on 400 cores in ~11 hours.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, practically relevant bottleneck: precision estimation in regimes where p is in the millions and p^2 parameters cannot be materialized.
- Sound and appropriate algorithmic choice: linearized (inexact) ADMM leads to elementwise proximal updates and reduces the heavy step to matrix multiplications that can be optimized and parallelized.
- Clear and plausible scalability strategy: column-blocking plus low-rank multiplications and block-cyclic distribution. The design aligns with established HPC best practices.
- Convergence analysis: O(1/T) ergodic-type bounds for objective and residuals under a simple condition on η; consistent with inexact ADMM literature.
- Strong empirical scaling results on both shared-memory and distributed-memory systems; the 1e6 demo is impressive.
- The perturbation/thresholding perspective on C explains when covariance sparsification can be statistically safe.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: inexact ADMM, block-cyclic layouts, and leveraging mat–mat multiplies are well-known; the main novelty is their integration for CLIME and the column-block idea. The perturbation theorem is useful but relatively straightforward and not integrated into experiments.
- Limited statistical evaluation: comparisons prioritize runtime; accuracy is reported via precision/recall at matched sparsity levels, but without a comprehensive trade-off (e.g., estimation error vs time) or discussion of optimization error vs statistical error (tolerances ensuring optimization error negligible relative to c√(log p/n)).
- Baseline comparability: DC-QUIC and TIGER solve different estimators (graphical lasso / regression-based) than CLIME; matching sparsity helps but the comparison may favor certain methods in different regimes. Missing comparisons to other large-scale CLIME/ADMM implementations.
- Missing or under-specified details that matter in practice: 
  - Symmetrization of the final precision estimate (CLIME typically symmetrizes the column-wise solutions); it is not discussed how this is performed at scale.
  - Choice and estimation of λ_max(C) for setting η ≥ ρ λ_max^2(C) in huge problems (e.g., power iteration cost/accuracy).
  - Communication costs and strong-scaling breakdown analysis beyond empirical plots; no analytical communication model.
- Some claims (e.g., Theorem 3) are stated broadly. The role of independence between C and Δ, tail parameters for sub-exponential Δ, and the constants are not made precise here; and the approach is not validated empirically.

Quality
- The optimization method is technically sound and consistent with the ADMM literature; the convergence statements are plausible under the stated condition and with standard proofs (as cited).
- The systems aspects conform to established HPC practices; empirical results are convincing on scaling.
- Statistical claims for CLIME’s estimator are background; the perturbation argument is directionally correct but needs tighter, explicit conditions to be wholly convincing.
- Evaluation could better quantify the effect of optimization tolerance on statistical performance and provide more apples-to-apples baselines.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful algorithm box and complexity discussion.
- Some important implementation details (symmetrization; λ_max estimation; stopping criteria; communication patterns) should be made explicit.
- Minor typos and occasional ambiguities (e.g., notation around block distributions) but overall readable.

Originality
- Incremental but useful. The combination of column-block inexact ADMM, low-rank multiplies, and block-cyclic distribution for CLIME is, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated at this scale, but the components are standard.

Significance
- High practical impact for large-scale precision estimation. The ability to handle p ~ 10^6 with near-linear scaling is compelling for applications in genomics, climate, and beyond.
- The approach could generalize to other separable constrained estimators.

Suggestions and questions
- Please detail the symmetrization step for Ω̂ and its cost/distribution. How does it affect runtime and memory at scale?
- Provide a stopping rule that ties optimization error to statistical error (e.g., ensure residual ≤ O(√(log p/n))). Empirically show that tightening tolerance past that threshold does not change support recovery.
- Clarify how η and ρ are chosen, and how λ_max(C) is estimated at scale (e.g., distributed power iteration).
- Add a simple communication cost model for the two main multiplies A^T X and A W under block-cyclic distribution, and relate it to the observed speedups.
- Evaluate the covariance sparsification idea (Theorem 3) empirically: measure speedup vs estimation degradation for different thresholds.
- Expand baselines if possible (e.g., more CLIME solvers) and report accuracy–time Pareto curves rather than matching sparsity only.

Recommendation
- Verdict: Weak accept.
- Justification: Strong systems contribution with solid optimization underpinnings and impressive scaling results on an important problem. Novelty is moderate and some methodological/statistical details could be tightened, but the practical impact and quality of engineering make it a worthwhile contribution.

Confidence
- 4/5.
