Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation, focusing on the CLIME estimator. It proposes an inexact, column-block ADMM solver that (i) linearizes the quadratic term to obtain closed-form updates with O(1/T) convergence for both the objective and feasibility residuals, (ii) batches columns into blocks to exploit high-throughput matrix–matrix multiplies (GEMM) and cache-friendly block-cyclic distribution, and (iii) leverages low-rank structure (n ≪ p) by computing C X via A(A′X) where C = A A′, reducing complexity from O(p^2 k) to O(n p k). The paper further argues one can coarsen/threshold C while preserving the deviation bound needed for CLIME’s statistical guarantees (Theorem 3), thereby enabling additional sparsity in the core matrix multiplies. It describes shared-memory and distributed implementations (OpenMP/OpenMPI+ScaLAPACK), demonstrates near-linear scaling across hundreds of cores, and reports solving p = 10^6 (≈ 10^12 parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores. Comparisons are provided to CLIME (column-wise), TIGER, and DC-QUIC.

This work connects to: CLIME and its guarantees (Cai et al.), ADMM and inexact/linearized ADMM (Boyd et al.; He & Yuan), scalable ML and distributed ADMM variants (e.g., GPBS), and prior large-scale graphical model solvers (QUIC/DC-QUIC). The novelty is not in the statistical estimator but in a scalable optimization/architecture design specialized for CLIME with accompanying convergence analysis and a justification for covariance coarsening.

Strengths
- Bold scaling and systems contribution: The column-block formulation that turns repeated GEMV into GEMM, along with block-cyclic distribution and low-rank multiplies, is a well-motivated and effective recipe. The p = 10^6 demonstration is impressive and, to my knowledge, significantly beyond prior CLIME solvers.
- Clean algorithmic design: The linearized (inexact) ADMM leads to simple elementwise proximal steps and matrix multiplies; the O(1/T) rates for both objective suboptimality and feasibility are appropriate for this class of methods.
- Practical insights: Clear articulation of when to exploit low-rank (n ≪ p) vs sparsity (iterate sparsity and coarsened C), and of how block sizes/process grids interact with cache and communication.
- Relevance: Distributed, constrained optimization (not easily handled by parameter servers) is important; this framework could influence other large-scale constrained estimators.

Weaknesses
- Incremental on the theory side: The O(1/T) rates are standard for (linearized) ADMM; the convergence analysis as presented does not appear to yield sharper or problem-specific bounds. Theorem 3, while useful, is rather high-level and does not quantify the impact of coarsening on the downstream estimation error beyond the classical deviation bound; more concrete prescriptions (threshold levels, trade-offs) would strengthen the contribution.
- Limited statistical evaluation at scale: The largest-scale experiments focus on timing/speedup. There is little assessment of statistical accuracy (e.g., graph recovery/AUC) beyond moderate p. It remains unclear how accuracy degrades when aggressively coarsening C or when choosing large k.
- Comparisons: DC-QUIC is a strong baseline, but the comparison is limited (and benefits DC-QUIC when block-diagonalization is effective). TIGER is included but is a different estimator; it is faster on small problems and stronger in recall, consistent with prior work. More discussion on fairness of tuning (λ, ρ, η, stopping criteria) is needed.
- Some clarity gaps and minor inconsistencies: Algorithm 1 step numbering and notational glitches; incomplete reporting in some tables; no explicit symmetrization/PD postprocessing discussion for CLIME; memory/per-node footprint breakdown would help reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The algorithm is correct, the linearization condition (η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2) is reasonable albeit potentially conservative, and the implementation choices are aligned with HPC best practices (GEMM, block-cyclic, parallel I/O). Empirical methodology could be more thorough on accuracy, and tuning/stopping criteria should be specified.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The systems details are explained with enough depth to be implementable by readers familiar with distributed linear algebra. Some polishing would improve readability (fix typos/figures/tables, unify notation, detail PD symmetrization, and add a brief hyperparameter/tolerance selection guide).

Originality
Moderate. Column-block ADMM, linearized ADMM, low-rank multiplies via A(A′X), and block-cyclic distribution are known individually; the novelty lies in their integration for CLIME with convergence guarantees, a coarsening justification, and execution at unprecedented scale.

Significance
High on the systems/optimization-for-stats front. Providing a practical path to million-dimensional precision estimation broadens the applicability of CLIME and similar constrained estimators. The framework is likely reusable.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please detail λ, ρ, η selection and stopping criteria used across experiments, and perform a sensitivity analysis.
- Report memory per node and communication costs; include breakdowns to guide practitioners.
- Discuss and evaluate symmetrization/PD enforcement post-CLIME.
- Provide an empirical study of covariance coarsening (threshold levels vs accuracy/speed) to support Theorem 3.
- Include an ablation on k (column block size) vs accuracy and runtime beyond speedups; clarify failure modes when n is not small.
- Consider releasing code or pseudocode for the distributed kernels.

Pros for acceptance
- Demonstrates a substantial leap in scale for a widely used estimator.
- Clear, practical algorithm-engineering contributions with sound analysis.
- Likely to be useful to both ML and HPC communities.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical advances are limited; O(1/T) is standard.
- Statistical accuracy at extreme scale and under coarsening is underexplored.
- Some experimental and reporting details are missing.

Overall recommendation: Accept (7/10)
Confidence: 3/5
