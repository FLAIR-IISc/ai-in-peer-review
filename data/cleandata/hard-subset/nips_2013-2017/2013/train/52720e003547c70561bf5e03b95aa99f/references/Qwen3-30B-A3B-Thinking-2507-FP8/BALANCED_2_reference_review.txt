

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper addresses the important problem of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions (millions of variables) using a distributed framework. The authors propose a CLIME-ADMM variant that solves the precision matrix estimation problem in column blocks rather than column-by-column, leveraging matrix-matrix multiplication and block cyclic data distribution for scalability. The paper demonstrates impressive scalability, estimating a 1 million-dimensional precision matrix (1 trillion parameters) in 11 hours on 400 cores.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Practical Contribution**: The paper tackles a genuine bottleneck in high-dimensional statistics—scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-large dimensions. The demonstrated scalability (1M dimensions in 11 hours on 400 cores) is impressive and addresses a real need in modern applications.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The proposed column-block ADMM approach is well-motivated and theoretically supported with O(1/T) convergence rates for both objective function and constraint residuals. The analysis is rigorous and builds on established ADMM theory.

3. **Innovative Framework**: The use of column blocks and block cyclic distribution for load balancing represents a meaningful improvement over previous column-by-column approaches. The paper effectively demonstrates how this approach leverages matrix multiplication efficiency and cache locality.

4. **Thorough Experimental Validation**: The experiments comprehensively compare against state-of-the-art methods (CLIME, Tiger, DC-QUIC) on both synthetic and real datasets, showing clear scalability advantages for large dimensions. The shared-memory and distributed-memory scalability results are particularly convincing.

## Weaknesses
1. **Incomplete Statistical Analysis**: While the paper provides convergence guarantees for the algorithm, it doesn't address how distributed computation affects the statistical properties of the estimator. In distributed settings, does the estimator maintain the same statistical guarantees (e.g., minimax rates) as the centralized version? This is critical for statistical validity.

2. **Missing Distributed Sample Covariance Computation**: The paper assumes the sample covariance matrix C is available, but doesn't address how to compute it efficiently in a distributed setting. For truly large-scale data, computing C from raw data would be a major bottleneck that the framework doesn't address.

3. **Comparison with Distributed Methods**: The related work section mentions distributed ADMM approaches like [23] and [9], but doesn't sufficiently differentiate the current approach. A more thorough comparison with recent distributed precision matrix estimation methods would strengthen the novelty claim.

4. **Limited Real-World Impact Discussion**: While the scalability is impressive, the paper doesn't clearly articulate what new applications or problems become feasible with this approach. A concrete example of a previously intractable problem that can now be solved would strengthen the significance argument.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong technical contribution to scaling precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions. The column-block approach and distributed framework are well-motivated and effectively demonstrated. However, the lack of statistical analysis for the distributed setting and missing discussion of distributed sample covariance computation are significant omissions that affect the paper's completeness.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Address Statistical Properties**: Add a brief discussion (or theoretical analysis) of how distributed computation affects the statistical guarantees of the estimator. Does the estimator maintain the same convergence rates as the centralized version?

2. **Include Distributed Covariance Computation**: Describe how the framework could be extended to compute the sample covariance matrix in a distributed manner. This is essential for truly large-scale applications where data cannot be centralized.

3. **Strengthen Related Work**: More explicitly contrast with recent distributed methods like [23] and [9], highlighting specific technical differences and advantages of the column-block approach.

4. **Expand Significance Discussion**: Add a paragraph discussing concrete applications that become feasible with this approach (e.g., gene expression networks with 1M+ genes, climate data analysis with 1M+ locations).

The paper is strong in its technical contribution and scalability demonstration, but needs to better address the statistical implications of distributed computation and the full pipeline from raw data to precision matrix. With these improvements, it would be a valuable contribution to the field.

**Pro arguments for acceptance**: Strong technical contribution to a significant problem, impressive scalability results, sound theoretical foundation, well-executed experiments.

**Con arguments for rejection**: Incomplete statistical analysis for distributed setting, missing discussion of distributed covariance computation, limited articulation of real-world impact.

Overall, this is a promising contribution that would be significantly stronger with the suggested improvements. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
