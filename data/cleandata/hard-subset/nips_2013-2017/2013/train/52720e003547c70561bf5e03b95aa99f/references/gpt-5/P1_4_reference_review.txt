Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles scalable estimation of sparse precision matrices via CLIME, focusing on ultra-high-dimensional regimes (p up to 10^6). The authors propose an inexact ADMM algorithm tailored to CLIME that operates on column blocks (k columns at a time), rather than column-by-column, to exploit matrix–matrix multiplications and modern BLAS/ScaLAPACK efficiencies. They present a parallel/distributed implementation with block-cyclic data distribution for load balancing, show O(1/T) convergence rates for objective suboptimality and feasibility residuals, and describe how to exploit (i) sparsity in iterates and potentially sparsified covariance, and (ii) low-rank structure of the sample covariance (since C = A A^T with n ≪ p). Experiments compare against CLIME, TIGER, and DC-QUIC on synthetic and two real datasets, and demonstrate scaling to p = 10^6 on 400 cores in ~11 hours.

This work builds on: (i) CLIME and its statistical guarantees (Cai et al.), (ii) scalable optimization via ADMM (Boyd et al.), (iii) fast precision estimation methods such as QUIC/DC-QUIC, and (iv) distributed linear algebra with block-cyclic partitioning (ScaLAPACK). The idea of exploiting block-diagonal structure via covariance thresholding has precedent (e.g., Mazumder & Hastie for graphical lasso); the paper argues similar ideas can be leveraged for CLIME.

Contributions
- An inexact ADMM variant for CLIME with closed-form per-iteration updates (soft-thresholding and box projection), plus two matrix multiplies per iteration. Convergence rates O(1/T) are provided under η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2.
- Column-block formulation to replace repeated matrix–vector products by matrix–matrix products, improving cache efficiency and scalability.
- Parallel/distributed framework with block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O, and empirical scalability on shared and distributed memory.
- Heuristic/statistical rationale for perturbing/thresholding the sample covariance to induce sparsity while maintaining deviation bounds, and thus CLIME’s statistical guarantees.
- Empirical results demonstrating large speedups and very large-scale runs.

Quality
- Theory: The O(1/T) convergence rate for an inexact/linearized ADMM is standard but appropriate. The sufficient condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 ensures the proximal metric is PSD; this should be stated clearly where Theorem 2 shows a weighted norm involving (η/ρ) I − C^2. The “perturbed covariance” result (Theorem 3) is plausible at a high level but underspecified: it assumes independent sub-exponential perturbations and concludes the same sup-norm deviation order. However, (a) independence from the data, (b) constants, and (c) the practical choice of thresholds to induce sparsity deserve more precise statements and connections to known covariance thresholding results (e.g., Bickel & Levina; Mazumder & Hastie for graph structure). As written, the theorem reads more like a sketch than a rigorous, immediately applicable prescription.
- Algorithmic soundness: The column-block decomposition is correct (CLIME is column-separable), and the low-rank trick (A(A^T X)) is well motivated for n ≪ p. The update sequence in Algorithm 1 is coherent; however, some notation inconsistencies exist (e.g., factors of 1/2 in the augmented Lagrangian; elementwise norms called “matrix inner product in elementwise form”), and the V-update relies on an extrapolation expression that could be explained better to avoid off-by-one confusion.
- Experiments: The scalability study is thorough and useful (S_col^k and S_core^q, shared vs distributed, block sizes, process grids). The comparisons on accuracy are limited: on synthetic data, only precision/recall vs sparsity is shown, tuned to match sparsity, which can bias comparisons. On real data, only runtimes are reported; no model selection or downstream goodness-of-fit is discussed. Important details are missing for reproducibility: stopping criteria, number of iterations, choice/sensitivity of ρ and η, λ selection protocol (CLIME’s parameter-free variant is discussed but seemingly not used), memory footprint, communication volume, and n for the 10^6 example.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but needs tightening:
- Notation/numerical constants in the augmented Lagrangian and updates should be made consistent; define precisely the weighted norms in Theorem 2.
- Several typos and grammatical issues; references are incomplete in places.
- Figures/tables should be legible and fully described; include full experimental settings (n, k, λ, ρ, η, stopping).
- The perturbation theorem should specify conditions and practical guidance for thresholding (and cite closely related work).

Originality
The main algorithmic ingredients (ADMM, inexact/linearized proximalization, block-cyclic distribution, low-rank multiplications) are known, and ADMM has been applied to CLIME/graphical models. The novelty lies in combining column-block ADMM with a carefully engineered distributed implementation and demonstrating extreme-scale instances. The theoretical convergence is standard; the perturbation idea is interesting but close to existing covariance thresholding notions and not fully developed.

Significance
From a systems/optimization perspective, the work addresses a relevant bottleneck: scaling sparse precision estimation to millions of variables without forming C. The ability to run at p = 10^6 is noteworthy and likely of interest to practitioners who need feasibility-first estimators with simple linear algebra kernels. The statistical side would benefit from stronger empirical evaluation beyond runtime, but the engineering contribution alone is likely to be useful.

Pros
- Addresses an important large-scale problem with a clear, implementable method.
- Good exploitation of linear algebra structure (matrix–matrix ops, low-rank factorization) and block-cyclic distribution for load balance.
- Demonstrates strong scalability, including to 10^6 dimensions.
- Convergence guarantees appropriate for the proposed inexact ADMM.

Cons
- Novelty is moderate; several components are known, and theory is standard.
- The covariance perturbation/statistical justification is underdeveloped.
- Experimental evaluation under-emphasizes statistical accuracy and fairness (e.g., tuning to match sparsity; limited baselines; no results on real-data fit).
- Important implementation details (stopping, parameter selection, memory/comms model) are missing; references are incomplete.

Suggestions/questions for the authors
1) Provide stopping criteria and sensitivity to ρ and η; include convergence plots (objective, primal/dual residuals) vs time and cores.
2) Clarify Theorem 2’s norms and constants; ensure Lagrangian factors are consistent throughout.
3) Expand and ground Theorem 3: specify independence assumptions, constants, and relate to known covariance thresholding literature; give a concrete thresholding rule and its effect on runtime/accuracy.
4) Report statistical metrics on real datasets (e.g., likelihood on held-out data, stability selection, sparsity–accuracy curves).
5) Compare to stronger baselines: non-DC QUIC on moderate p; block-diagonal speedups for your method; possibly BigQUIC-style implementations.
6) Discuss how early stopping affects statistical error for CLIME (accuracy vs feasibility violation).
7) Provide code or pseudo-code sufficient for reproduction, including block sizes, process grids, and parallel I/O details.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper’s systems contribution and demonstrated scale are compelling for NIPS, but improving clarity, statistical evaluation, and the covariance perturbation justification would strengthen the work.
