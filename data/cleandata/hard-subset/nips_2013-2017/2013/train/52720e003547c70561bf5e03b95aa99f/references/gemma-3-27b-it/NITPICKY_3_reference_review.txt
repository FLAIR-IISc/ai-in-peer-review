## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

This paper tackles the important and increasingly relevant problem of estimating sparse precision matrices in ultra-high dimensional settings. The authors present a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm, building upon existing ADMM approaches, and propose innovations in inexact ADMM implementation and column-block solving strategies to achieve scalability. While the problem is significant and the proposed method shows promise, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of thoroughness that ultimately detract from its overall contribution and leave me leaning towards rejection.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the bottleneck in precision matrix estimation as the computational cost scaling with p², where p is the dimensionality.  Leveraging the separability of the CLIME objective function, the authors build upon prior work using ADMM [18] to parallelize computations. Their key contributions are an inexact ADMM implementation tailored to CLIME and solving the problem in column blocks to exploit matrix multiplication efficiencies [15, 5, 11]. This approach is positioned within the broader context of scaling machine learning algorithms [29, 22, 6, 7, 20, 2, 23, 9], noting the inadequacy of MapReduce and limitations of existing parameter server architectures [6].  The work acknowledges and builds upon prior CLIME implementations [3, 28] and recent advances in parameter-free methods [4, 19].

**Strengths:**

* **Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant and represents a practical challenge in numerous domains.
* **Scalability Focus:** The emphasis on scalability to millions of dimensions is commendable, and the distributed framework addresses a key limitation of existing methods.
* **Technical Contributions:** The inexact ADMM and column-block strategies are logical extensions to the existing literature and represent potential efficiency gains.
* **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to handle a one million-dimensional problem, offering some evidence of scalability.

**Weaknesses:**

This paper is riddled with numerous minor imperfections that, collectively, significantly diminish its quality. My concerns are categorized below:

* **Notational Inconsistency & Imprecision:** The notation is inconsistent. For example,  "X ∈ <p×k" is introduced to denote k columns of Ω̂, but later "Xij" denotes a *block* of the matrix. Using bold lowercase for blocks (as suggested in the notation section itself) would resolve this. Furthermore, the definition of matrix norms feels unnecessarily verbose and includes redundant information.
* **Lack of Rigor in Justification:** The claim that solving in column blocks is "not worthwhile" without their approach is unsupported. The advantages of matrix multiplication are stated but lack a detailed analysis of computational complexity compared to repeated matrix-vector multiplications. The authors mention "cache misses" but provide no analysis of optimal block sizes or experimental evidence supporting their choice.
* **Insufficient Detail in Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 lacks crucial details. What is the initial value of η?  How is the block size 'k' selected? The soft-thresholding and box constraint functions are defined inline, disrupting the flow of the algorithm description. More importantly, the *actual implementation* of sparse matrix multiplication isn't discussed. Are specialized sparse linear algebra libraries used? 
* **Experimental Design Deficiencies:** The comparison with existing methods is incomplete. Tiger is acknowledged as achieving optimal rates, but the comparison focuses primarily on runtime, neglecting statistical accuracy metrics (e.g., false positives, false negatives in graph recovery). The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding the generation of synthetic data and parameter tuning for each algorithm.  The claim of "linear speedup" requires careful scrutiny –  is this strong or weak scaling? The discussion of Table 2 is also weak. Simply stating it's slower doesn’t elucidate why, or suggest improvements.
* **Overly Assertive Claims:** Phrases like "almost linearly" regarding scalability are vague and require more quantitative justification.
* **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The introduction repeats information and is overly lengthy. The related work section is exhaustive but lacks critical analysis of how this work *specifically* advances the field.
* **Typos & Grammatical Errors:** Numerous minor typos and grammatical errors are present throughout the paper (e.g., “Scolk” is used without definition before appearing in figures, “Mat-Mul” is not a standard term).

**Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
* Demonstrates the feasibility of scaling CLIME to large dimensions.
* Presents potentially efficient algorithmic improvements.

**Against:**

* Numerous minor flaws in notation, justification, algorithm description, and experimental design.
* Lack of rigorous analysis supporting key claims.
* Overly verbose and repetitive writing.
* Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms in terms of statistical accuracy.
* The level of polish and attention to detail is significantly below that expected of a NIPS publication.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the authors identify an important problem and propose a potentially valuable solution, the paper's numerous deficiencies prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of rigor, incomplete experimental validation, and numerous minor flaws indicate that the work is not yet ready for a competitive venue like NIPS.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the concerns raised in this review. A substantial rewrite focusing on clarity, rigor, and completeness is necessary before resubmission.  A more thorough comparison to related methods, coupled with a more detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability, would also significantly strengthen the contribution.
