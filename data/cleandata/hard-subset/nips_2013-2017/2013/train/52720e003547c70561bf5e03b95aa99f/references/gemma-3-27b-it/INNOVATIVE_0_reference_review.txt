This paper tackles the important problem of scaling sparse precision matrix estimation to ultra-high dimensions, a crucial need as datasets continue to grow. The core idea – leveraging column-block ADMM with careful attention to matrix multiplication and data distribution – is a strong one, and the reported 11-hour estimation of a million-dimensional precision matrix is genuinely impressive. This represents a significant engineering feat and a substantial step forward in addressing a practical bottleneck. The theoretical analysis, while not deeply novel, provides a reasonable foundation for the empirical results.

The strength of this work lies in its *practical* impact. While the theoretical results (O(1/T) convergence) are standard for ADMM, the clever combination of inexact ADMM, block updates, and a block-cyclic data distribution scheme demonstrably overcomes limitations of existing methods. The attention to cache efficiency and parallelization is commendable. The discussion of leveraging sparse and low-rank structure, though not fully realized in the experiments, points towards promising future directions. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details, although the notation is quite dense.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The novelty, while substantial in *implementation* and *system-level optimization*, feels incremental from a purely *algorithmic* standpoint. The ADMM framework itself is well-established, and the core algorithmic contributions – inexact ADMM and block updates – are adaptations of existing techniques to the specific CLIME problem. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper’s impact hinges on the successful scaling, rather than a fundamentally new estimation approach. The experimental validation, while strong for a million dimensions, feels somewhat limited. More extensive comparisons with alternative distributed methods (beyond those mentioned) and a deeper exploration of the parameter sensitivity (λ, ρ, η) would be beneficial.  The low-rank structure exploitation feels superficial – simply mentioning it and stating it *can* be used isn’t compelling. More concrete results demonstrating its impact are needed. The discussion of perturbations to induce sparsity also lacks detailed justification and experimental evidence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Significant practical achievement:** The ability to estimate a million-dimensional precision matrix in a reasonable timeframe is a substantial accomplishment.
* **Novel system-level optimization:** The combination of techniques – inexact ADMM, block updates, block-cyclic distribution – provides a genuinely novel and effective solution.
* **Well-written and clearly explained:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is accessible and understandable.
* **Addresses an important problem:** Scaling precision matrix estimation is crucial for handling modern high-dimensional datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Incremental algorithmic novelty:** The core algorithmic ideas are adaptations of existing techniques.
* **Limited experimental validation:** More extensive comparisons and parameter sensitivity analysis are needed.
* **Superficial exploration of low-rank structure:** The potential benefits are mentioned but not demonstrated.
* **Perturbation justification lacking:** Insufficient detail on why and how to perturb the covariance matrix.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper warrants acceptance, leaning towards a strong accept. While the algorithmic novelty is incremental, the engineering achievement and demonstrable scalability are highly significant. The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in the field and presents a practical solution that will likely be valuable to many researchers. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses regarding experimental validation and more thoroughly explore the potential of low-rank structure exploitation in a future iteration of the work. I am willing to overlook the lack of groundbreaking algorithmic innovation given the substantial practical impact.
