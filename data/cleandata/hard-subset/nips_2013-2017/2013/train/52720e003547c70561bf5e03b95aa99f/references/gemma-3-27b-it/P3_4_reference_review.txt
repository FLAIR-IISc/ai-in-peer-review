## Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the challenging problem of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The authors develop a distributed CLIME-ADMM algorithm designed to scale to problems with millions of dimensions and trillions of parameters, a regime often inaccessible to existing methods. The technical innovations, including inexact ADMM, column-block processing, and careful consideration of data distribution strategies, are well-motivated and demonstrate a deep understanding of the interplay between algorithm design and hardware limitations. While the paper is lengthy and some details are relegated to the supplement, the core ideas are clearly presented and supported by empirical results. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of estimating the precision matrix (inverse covariance) in high-dimensional settings where the number of samples is limited.  This is a core problem in statistics and machine learning with applications ranging from gene expression analysis to portfolio optimization. The authors build upon the CLIME estimator, a popular method with strong statistical guarantees, but recognize its limitations in scalability.  They propose a distributed ADMM-based algorithm (CLIME-ADMM) to overcome these limitations, leveraging the separable structure of CLIME and optimizing for parallel computation.

The work builds on a rich history of research in precision matrix estimation, including graphical lasso [Yuan, 2007], CLIME [Cai et al., 2011], and related optimization techniques [Hsieh et al., 2014]. It also connects to the broader area of scaling machine learning algorithms, referencing work on parameter servers [Dean et al., 2012], distributed ADMM [Boyd et al., 2011], and parallel matrix multiplication [Choi, 2008].  The authors correctly position their contribution as addressing a critical bottleneck in applying these techniques to truly massive datasets, and they clearly delineate how their approach differs from existing parallelization strategies. The focus on column-block processing and the detailed discussion of data distribution are novel contributions within this landscape.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its demonstrated scalability. The experiments on a 1 million-dimensional problem, completed in 11 hours on 400 cores, are impressive and highlight the practical impact of the proposed algorithm.
* **Technical Depth:** The authors provide a thorough technical analysis, including convergence rate analysis for the ADMM algorithm (Theorems 1 and 2) and insightful considerations of leveraging sparse and low-rank structures.
* **Practical Innovations:** The column-block processing and the use of block cyclic distribution are well-motivated and contribute to the algorithm's efficiency.  The discussion of cache optimization is particularly valuable.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing CLIME-ADMM to several state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets. The analysis of speedup in shared and distributed memory environments provides a clear understanding of the algorithm’s performance characteristics.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, despite its length. The authors make a good effort to explain complex concepts in a clear and accessible manner.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The paper is quite dense and can be challenging to follow in its entirety.  Some details are relegated to the supplement, which makes it harder to get a complete picture of the algorithm.
* **Parameter Tuning:** While the authors highlight the benefits of CLIME's inherent structure, the practical tuning of parameters (e.g., ρ, η, block size) for optimal performance is not thoroughly discussed.  Guidance on selecting these parameters for different problem instances would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison to Specialized Methods:** The comparison to other methods is solid, but it would be beneficial to include a comparison against more specialized algorithms designed for specific sparse matrix structures if such methods exist.
* **Real Data Analysis:** The real-data analysis is limited to runtime comparisons.  A more in-depth analysis of the estimated precision matrices (e.g., examining the recovered network structure) would strengthen the results.



**4.  Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling precision matrix estimation.
* Demonstrates impressive scalability on a challenging problem instance.
* Provides a technically sound and well-motivated algorithm with convergence guarantees.
* Offers practical innovations that improve performance and efficiency.
* Empirical results support the claims of the paper.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and could benefit from simplification.
* Parameter tuning guidance is lacking.
* Real data analysis could be more thorough.



**5.  Suggestions for Improvement (Minor Revisions):**

* **Simplify the presentation:** Consider streamlining the paper by moving less essential details to the supplement.
* **Provide parameter tuning guidelines:** Include a section discussing how to choose appropriate values for the algorithm’s parameters (ρ, η, block size) based on the characteristics of the data.
* **Expand real data analysis:**  Present a more detailed analysis of the estimated precision matrices on the real datasets, including visualization of the recovered network structure or comparison to known relationships.
* **Clarify assumptions:** Explicitly state the assumptions about the data distribution (e.g., sub-Gaussianity) and their impact on the algorithm’s performance.

**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale sparse precision matrix estimation. The proposed CLIME-ADMM algorithm is a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working with high-dimensional data. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**
