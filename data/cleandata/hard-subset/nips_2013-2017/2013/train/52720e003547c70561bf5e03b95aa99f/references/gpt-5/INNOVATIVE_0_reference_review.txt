Summary
The paper tackles ultra–high-dimensional sparse precision matrix estimation by designing a distributed, block-parallel solver for the CLIME estimator. The core contributions are: (i) a linearized (inexact) ADMM variant for CLIME with elementwise-prox updates for X and Z and O(1/T) convergence guarantees for objective gap and residuals; (ii) solving CLIME in column-blocks (k columns at a time) to turn repeated matrix–vector products into BLAS3 matrix–matrix multiplies, improving cache efficiency and enabling better parallelization; (iii) leveraging low-rank structure of the sample covariance C=A A^T (with n≪p) to reduce the dominant cost from O(p^2k) to O(npk); (iv) a distributed implementation with block-cyclic data distribution (ScaLAPACK-style) that achieves near-linear scaling to hundreds of cores; and (v) a perturbation argument (Theorem 3) justifying truncation/sparsification of small entries of C while preserving deviation bounds needed by CLIME’s statistical guarantees. Experiments compare against flare/TIGER and DC-QUIC on moderate p, and demonstrate scalability to p=10^6 (reporting ~11 hours on 400 cores for k=500).

Strengths
- Ambitious scale: Running CLIME to one million dimensions is a bold systems contribution for sparse precision estimation.
- Solid algorithmic engineering: The column-block ADMM plus low-rank matmul and block-cyclic distribution are well-motivated and align with best practices in HPC (BLAS3, cache-aware blocking).
- Convergence guarantees: The inexact ADMM analysis (O(1/T) rates under η≥ρλ_max^2(C)) is standard but appropriate, and the weighted residual bound is clearly stated.
- Practical structure exploitation: Clear discussion of how iterate sparsity and low-rank C can be used to accelerate the heavy steps. The low-rank trick (using A instead of C) is particularly impactful in n≪p.
- Empirical scaling: Careful speedup studies in both shared-memory and distributed-memory settings, with an honest discussion of cache/resource contention and block-size effects.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is mostly in engineering and packaging: Linearized ADMM for CLIME, column grouping, BLAS3 use, and block-cyclic distribution are all known ideas; the paper’s novelty is their combination and scale, not a fundamentally new optimization or statistical concept.
- Limited head-to-head comparisons at scale: The large-scale experiments are primarily about runtime/scaling; there is little accuracy/quality evaluation at very large p, nor comparisons to the strongest large-scale GLasso/QUIC-style solvers at similar scales (DC-QUIC is included but benefits from block diagonal thresholding; the paper does not explore similar structure exploitation in their own method).
- Theorem 3 is plausible but incremental: Showing that sub-exponential perturbations preserve the required ∞-norm deviation bound (and thus permit truncation) is useful but unsurprising; constants and practical guidance for choosing thresholds/c are not discussed.
- Clarity/Presentation: Several notation/formatting issues (Algorithm 1 steps and symbols, missing details on stopping criteria and parameter selection for ρ, η, λ). Symmetrization/PD post-processing for CLIME is not discussed; storage/output format for the trillion-parameter solution is unclear (sparse write-out?).

Quality
- The optimization development and proofs (as summarized) are technically sound and consistent with inexact/linearized ADMM literature. The condition η≥ρλ_max^2(C) makes the weighted norm PSD.
- The systems aspects (block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O, process grid design, cache blocking) are credible and consistent with ScaLAPACK practices.
- Experimental methodology is fair for scaling, but the statistical/graph recovery evaluation is limited to moderate p with synthetic and a couple of real datasets; the million-dim run is reported without accuracy/statistics or downstream utility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but polishing is needed: unify and label algorithm steps, define stopping criteria and parameter choices, and clarify the “V”/“V-hat” updates. Some references are incomplete. Include the symmetrization step customary for CLIME and its cost implications. Provide communication-cost per iteration and the overlap strategy.

Originality
- Moderate. The combination of column-block linearized ADMM for CLIME with low-rank multiplications and a distributed, cache-aware implementation is a useful systems contribution, but the optimization and statistical components largely build on known results. The perturbation lemma is a mild extension of standard deviation-bound arguments.

Significance
- High for practitioners facing enormous p, where engineering a solver that truly scales and uses HPC idioms is valuable. The demonstration at p=10^6 will likely spur follow-on work and adoption. From a theory or algorithmic-novelty perspective, the advance is incremental.

Questions for the authors
1) How are ρ and η chosen in practice? Any adaptive schemes or sensitivity analysis?
2) What are the stopping criteria (primal/dual residual thresholds, max iters)? How do they scale with p?
3) How is symmetrization and PD post-processing of CLIME handled at scale? Is the output stored sparsely; what is the final memory footprint?
4) Can your framework exploit block-diagonal structure in C similarly to DC-QUIC for further speedups? Any experiments?
5) For Theorem 3, please specify constants and provide practical guidance for truncation thresholds; do you implement this in the large-scale runs?
6) Communication analysis: quantify per-iteration communication volume and show strong/weak scaling with different network topologies.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold and practically important scaling result for sparse precision estimation.
- Clear, effective HPC design that others can build upon for constrained problems.
- Convergence analysis is appropriate; implementation details (blocking, distribution) are useful to the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Algorithmic/statistical novelty is limited; largely an engineering advance.
- Presentation gaps (parameters, stopping, symmetrization, reproducibility).
- Limited accuracy/quality evaluation at the largest scales and limited comparisons to the strongest baselines at scale.

Recommendation
Weak accept. While the methodological novelty is modest, the paper presents a credible, effective, and well-engineered framework that enables a qualitatively new scale for CLIME-based precision estimation. The contribution is likely to influence practice and future work on large-scale constrained optimization. Addressing clarity issues and adding details on parameter selection, symmetrization, and communication costs would strengthen the paper. Confidence: medium.
