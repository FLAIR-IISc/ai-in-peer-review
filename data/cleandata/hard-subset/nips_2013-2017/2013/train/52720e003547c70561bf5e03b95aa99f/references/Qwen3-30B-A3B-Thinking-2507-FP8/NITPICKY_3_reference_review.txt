

# Review of "Large Scale Distributed Sparse Precision Estimation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a distributed algorithm for sparse precision matrix estimation using a column-block ADMM approach for the CLIME estimator. The authors claim their method scales to million-dimensional problems (10^6 dimensions, 10^12 parameters) with linear scalability across hundreds of cores. The work builds on existing CLIME and ADMM methods but introduces column-block processing and block cyclic distribution for better cache utilization. The paper includes theoretical convergence guarantees, a scalable parallel framework for shared and distributed memory, and empirical results showing improved scalability over existing methods.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics and presents a well-motivated approach to scaling precision matrix estimation. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) provide convergence guarantees for the proposed inexact ADMM variant. The experimental results demonstrate good scalability across different architectures, and the column-block approach with block cyclic distribution is a reasonable technical contribution. The problem is clearly relevant to the machine learning and statistics communities.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Formatting and Notation Inconsistencies
The paper contains multiple formatting errors that undermine technical credibility:
- Inconsistent use of mathematical notation: "Ω0 = Σ−10" (should be Ω₀ = Σ₀⁻¹), "p2 = 1012" (should be p² = 10¹²), "1√n" (should be 1/√n).
- In Algorithm 1, the matrix multiplication section has inconsistent formatting: "Mat-Mul: { sparse : Ut+1 = CXt+1 low rank : Ut+1 = A(A′Xt+1)" lacks proper spacing and mathematical notation.
- Theorem 1 contains a critical typo: "‖X̄T ‖1 − ‖X‖1 ≤ η‖X‖22 T" should be "≤ η‖X‖₂² / T" (missing division symbol).
- Theorem 2 contains unclear notation: "‖XT −XT−1‖2η ρ I−C2" which appears to be missing proper mathematical formatting.

### 2. Technical Errors and Ambiguities
- The CLIME estimator definition in the introduction has inconsistent norm notation: "‖CΩ̂− I‖∞" should be "‖CΩ̂ - I‖∞" (missing space) or "‖CΩ̂ - I‖_∞" (proper norm notation).
- In Section 3.2, the paper states "the computational complexity of matrix multiplication reduces from O(p²k) to O(npk)" but fails to clarify that this assumes n << p (which is standard in high-dimensional statistics, but the paper should explicitly state this).
- The claim that "CLIME-ADMM scales almost linearly with the number of cores" contradicts Figure 3(b), which shows non-linear speedup on 20 cores (where speedup is significantly less than linear).
- The paper claims to estimate "a precision matrix of one million dimension and one trillion parameters in 11 hours by running the algorithm on 400 cores," but fails to explain how they handle the memory requirements for a 10^6 × 10^6 matrix (10^12 entries), which would require ~8 TB of memory for double-precision values.

### 3. Methodological Concerns
- The "low-rank" approach is poorly justified: the paper states "C = AAT and p n" but then claims "CX = A(ATX)" which is correct, but the complexity reduction from O(p²k) to O(npk) is only beneficial when n << p (which is true), but the paper doesn't clearly establish why n is small in their experiments (n=200 is mentioned later, but not emphasized).
- The theoretical convergence rate claims are overstated: Theorems 1 and 2 provide bounds that are O(1/T), but the paper doesn't explicitly state this is the convergence rate (the bounds contain O(1/T) terms, but don't claim the rate is O(1/T)).
- The comparison with Tiger in Table 1 is misleading: The paper states "Tiger is faster than CLIME-ADMM on small dimensional dataset Leukemia" but provides no specific timing data for the high-dimensional case to support their scalability claim.

### 4. Experimental Presentation
- Figure 2(a) shows scalability only up to 10^4 dimensions, which is far from the claimed million-dimensional capability, making the scalability claim unsubstantiated.
- The paper claims "CLIME-ADMM is shown to be much faster than existing methods" but provides no clear evidence of speedup for the million-dimensional case (only mentions the 11-hour runtime without comparison to other methods at that scale).
- The distributed-memory results in Figure 4(a) show speedups that plateau, which contradicts the claim of "almost linear" scaling.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some valid technical insights, the numerous formatting errors, mathematical inconsistencies, and unsubstantiated claims significantly undermine its credibility. The paper makes a strong claim about scaling to million-dimensional problems but fails to provide adequate evidence or technical justification for this claim. The theoretical results contain critical typos that suggest carelessness in preparation.

The paper's minor flaws (formatting, notation, minor mathematical errors) collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. Despite the paper's potential significance, the technical shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains too many formatting and mathematical errors that undermine the technical credibility of the work. The claims about scalability to million-dimensional problems are not properly supported by the experimental results presented. The paper would require significant revision to address these fundamental issues before being considered for publication at NIPS.
