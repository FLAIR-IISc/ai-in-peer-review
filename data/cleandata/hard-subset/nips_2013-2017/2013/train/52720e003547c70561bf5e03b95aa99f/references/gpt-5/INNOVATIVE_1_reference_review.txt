Summary
This paper tackles ultra–large-scale sparse precision (inverse covariance) estimation by developing a distributed, column-block variant of CLIME solved via an inexact/linearized ADMM. Key ideas: (i) solve CLIME in column blocks to leverage matrix–matrix multiplies (vs the usual column-by-column matrix–vector multiplies), (ii) exploit low-rank structure of the sample covariance C = A A^T in the low-sample, high-dimensional regime (p >> n) to reduce O(p^2k) costs to O(npk), (iii) use block-cyclic data distribution and parallel I/O to achieve load balance and cache efficiency on shared and distributed architectures, and (iv) optionally sparsify/perturb the sample covariance while preserving deviation bounds needed for CLIME’s statistical guarantees. The authors provide O(1/T) convergence guarantees for objective suboptimality and residuals for their inexact ADMM, and demonstrate strong scaling up to 400 cores, including a striking run estimating a 1M×1M precision matrix in ~11 hours.

Strengths
- Bold, impactful scaling: Moving sparse precision estimation to the 10^6-dimensional regime is impressive and practically relevant. The column-block design that enables batching into BLAS-3 operations and the low-rank factorization tricks are well-motivated for p >> n.
- Solid systems design: The block-cyclic distribution, parallel I/O, and separation of elementwise vs matrix-multiply steps are sound and likely generalizable to other constrained problems.
- Theory support: The linearized ADMM update has closed-form proximal steps and comes with clear O(1/T) rates. The condition η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2 is explicit.
- Practical insights: Useful discussion on iterate sparsity, low-rank vs sparsity tradeoffs, and architecture-dependent behaviors (shared vs distributed memory), including empirical speedups with varying k and core counts.
- Perturbation/sparsification idea for C: The observation that deterministic truncation respecting ∞-norm deviation bounds preserves CLIME’s guarantees is a useful practical trick for accelerating multiplies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty vs prior ADMM/CLIME work: ADMM for CLIME and linearized ADMM are known; the main novelty is column-block batching + HPC distribution. This is a strong engineering contribution but methodologically incremental. The perturbation theorem is plausible but only lightly developed; the presented theorem assumes random Δ whereas the practical truncation is deterministic (although a simple triangle inequality argument suffices to justify the deterministic case).
- Statistical aspects underplayed: CLIME solutions are not guaranteed PD; standard symmetrization/PD projection steps are not discussed. How do the authors form the final Ω̂ (symmetrization, PD correction), and at what cost at 10^6 scale?
- Some theoretical presentation issues: Theorem 2 contains a term ‖XT − XT−1‖^2_(η/ρ I − C^2); unless ‖C‖ < 1 this weighting may not be PSD. This needs clarification/typo fix and a precise statement of the norm weighting.
- Hyperparameter guidance: Choice of ρ and η (beyond η ≥ ρ λ_max(C)^2), and practical estimation of λ_max(C) at scale are not detailed. A brief description (e.g., power iteration, conservative bounds) would improve reproducibility.
- Experimental evaluation: 
  - Comparisons are mostly runtime; statistical accuracy is only evaluated on synthetic graphs by matching sparsity. For real data, there is no downstream task or likelihood-based metric. 
  - Baseline coverage could be stronger (e.g., BIG-QUIC or other recent large-scale graphical lasso/CLIME implementations where applicable).
  - The “1 trillion parameters” claim is compelling but leaves open how Ω̂ is stored/output (I/O costs, symmetry/PD processing).
- Clarity/typos: Several notation slips (e.g., missing E argument in box, occasional mismatched dimensions) and minor editing issues reduce readability.

Questions for the authors
1. Symmetry/PD: How do you enforce symmetrization of the CLIME solution at scale, and do you perform a PD projection? What is the added overhead and impact on runtime/memory?
2. Parameter choice: How are ρ, η, and λ chosen in practice? How do you estimate or bound λ_max(C) efficiently at scale?
3. Deterministic truncation: Please provide a clean, deterministic statement showing that hard-thresholding C at τ ≍ √(log p/n) preserves the required ∞-norm deviation bound and how this impacts the choice of λ in CLIME.
4. Output and storage: For p = 10^6, how is Ω̂ stored and written? Is the final solution sparse enough to be kept in compressed form, and what is the resultant memory footprint?
5. Ablations: Can you quantify the separate gains from (a) column-blocking (k>1), (b) low-rank multiplies, and (c) block-cyclic distribution vs simple block partitioning?
6. Iteration counts: How many ADMM iterations are typically needed to reach practical tolerances across scales, and how do tolerances map to statistical error?

Relation to prior work
- The work builds on CLIME and ADMM variants previously used for column-wise solutions, and on HPC block-cyclic distribution and BLAS-3 acceleration. Relative to QUIC/DC-QUIC, this paper focuses on CLIME (no log-det) and makes different tradeoffs (no PD constraint, but simpler per-iteration operations and better batching).
- The O(1/T) rate for linearized/inexact ADMM is aligned with known results; the application to CLIME column-blocks is a reasonable specialization.

Clarity and reproducibility
- The high-level algorithm is clear, but the paper would benefit from a more careful, self-contained pseudo-code (with all arguments), correction of notational issues in Theorem 2, and a brief parameter guide. Public code or more implementation details (block sizes, I/O formats, stopping criteria) would enhance reproducibility.

Significance
- From a systems/scale perspective, the contribution is significant: it demonstrates a path to 10^6-dimensional precision estimation with near-linear scaling. Methodologically, it is an incremental but pragmatic advance. Given the potential to unlock new applications that require massive graphs, the impact could be high.

Recommendation: Weak accept
I value the bold scaling approach and practical, well-engineered design that opens up a new operational regime for sparse precision estimation. While some methodological components are incremental and certain theoretical/clarity issues need polishing, the overall contribution and empirical scale justify acceptance. I encourage the authors to address the noted concerns in the final version.
