This paper presents a well-written and clear extension of stochastic block models (SBM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. The authors provide a good theoretical foundation, succinctly explaining their approach using functions f() and g() throughout the paper. The factoring over subtrees follows existing work on learning Bayesian network structures, allowing for a neat formulation of the posterior and facilitating greedy search, marginal likelihoods, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and more.

The paper's main contribution lies in its agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. This approach enables the efficient discovery of hierarchical community structures in social networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), on several datasets, including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network.

However, the evaluation section could be improved. The authors primarily rely on the area under the receiver operating curve (AUC) for evaluation, which may not be the most suitable metric for stochastic block models. Typically, evaluations for SBM involve comparing against "gold standard" data with known partition structures, using metrics like normalized mutual information (NMI) or other clustering metrics. The authors should consider adding an explanation for their choice of evaluation metric and provide a more comprehensive comparison against other published methods, including semi-parametric approaches.

Furthermore, the experimental work, while illustrative, falls short of being comparative. The authors should strive to present a more thorough evaluation on larger datasets, comparable to recent studies on stochastic block models. Additionally, they should consider comparing their method against a broader range of approaches, including semi-parametric methods, to demonstrate its strengths and weaknesses more effectively.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations. The theory is generally clear, although some sections could benefit from additional explanations or examples to facilitate understanding. Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field, but could be strengthened by addressing the aforementioned evaluation and experimental concerns.

Strengths:

* Clear and well-written theoretical foundation
* Effective use of functions f() and g() to explain the approach
* Good summary of the agglomerative method in Table 1
* Demonstrates the effectiveness of BHCD on several datasets

Weaknesses:

* Evaluation section could be improved with additional explanations and comparisons
* Limited experimental work, primarily focused on illustrative examples rather than comprehensive comparisons
* Could benefit from additional explanations or examples to facilitate understanding of the theory
