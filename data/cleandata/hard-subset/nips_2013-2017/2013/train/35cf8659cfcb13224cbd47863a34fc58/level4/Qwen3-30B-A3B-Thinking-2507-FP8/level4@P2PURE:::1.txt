

The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modeling, drawing key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper: 1) the hierarchy specifies a mixture over consistent partitions of nodes, and 2) this hierarchy can be learned efficiently via a greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results are shown on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, with quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity. The paper is exceptionally well written, with a clear exposition of the core ideas.

Originality. The main concepts are derived from the Bayesian rose tree work, but adapting them to the relational data setting required significant effort, particularly in preserving the same computational complexity.

Significance. Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially to network models, is a critical challenge for practitioner adoption. While the basic algorithm's n² scaling may prove prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse variant should remain feasible. It would be valuable to include timings for larger real-world networks: NIPS-234 takes only tens of seconds—how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Gene networks typically involve tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are substantially larger; how does inference scale in such cases? Additionally, it would be beneficial to assess how close the greedy method comes to the optimal tree. This could be investigated at the largest scale where the optimal tree is still computable via exhaustive search. Is theoretical support possible, e.g., via submodularity arguments?
