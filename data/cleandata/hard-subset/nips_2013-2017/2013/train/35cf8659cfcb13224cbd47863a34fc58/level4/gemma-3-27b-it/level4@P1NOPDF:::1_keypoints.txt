- Proposes a hierarchical extension of the Information Retrieval Metric (IRM) using Bayesian rose tree ideas to define a mixture over consistent node partitions.  
- Learns the hierarchy with an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure adapted from the Bayesian rose tree work.  
- Shows qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234.  
- Inference is computationally much cheaper than the original IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.  
- Paper is very well written and the core ideas are clearly explained.  
- Core ideas are borrowed from Bayesian rose trees, but adapting them to relational data required significant effort to maintain comparable computational complexity.  
- Scaling BNP and network models is important; the basic algorithm’s O(n^2) scaling may be prohibitive for very large networks, though the sparse version could be feasible.  
- Requests timings on larger, real-world networks and specifically asks how long inference takes on the full NIPS dataset and how inference would scale to gene or large social networks.  
- Suggests evaluating how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree by comparing against exhaustive search at tractable scales and exploring possible theoretical guarantees (e.g., submodularity).  
- Lists minor corrections: change “whilst… but” to “while… but” or “although”; “pair of treeS” → “pair of trees”; “A fruitful next steps” → “Fruitful next steps”.  
- Overall recommendation: contribution is significant despite familiarity with underlying ideas and the paper should be accepted.
