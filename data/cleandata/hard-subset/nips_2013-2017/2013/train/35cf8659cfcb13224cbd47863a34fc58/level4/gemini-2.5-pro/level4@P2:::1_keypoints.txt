- Introduces BHCD: applies Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data to discover hierarchical community structures via a tree-structured mixture over stochastic blockmodels.  
- Proposes a greedy agglomerative O(n² log n) algorithm that learns the hierarchy in a single bottom-up pass as a computationally efficient alternative to MCMC/variational inference.  
- Designs a likelihood and caching scheme for relational data to maintain O(n² log n) complexity and offers a sparse variant for empirical speedups.  
- Empirically evaluated qualitatively on Sampson’s monastery and full NIPS co-authorship, and quantitatively on a NIPS subset, showing comparable predictive performance to IRM with large speed gains.  
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides sufficient derivations (e.g., recursive marginal likelihood) and algorithmic detail for experts.  
- Core framework is adapted from Bayesian Rose Trees, but the non-trivial adaptation and engineering for relational data is a meaningful contribution.  
- Main significance: addresses scalability of Bayesian nonparametric network models, demonstrating ~100× speedup on NIPS-234 while retaining accuracy.  
- Weakness: O(n² log n) scaling may still be prohibitive for very large graphs; needs timing results for the sparse algorithm on full NIPS (n=2864) or larger networks.  
- Weakness: Greedy inference has no optimality guarantees; should compare greedy solutions to optimal trees on small networks to quantify loss.  
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical discussion on properties (e.g., submodularity) that might justify or explain greedy performance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical and scalable approach, large empirical speed-up, clear adaptation of an existing elegant framework, and well-presented experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited conceptual novelty (direct application of prior method) and incomplete empirical substantiation of scalability to very large graphs.  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong contribution with valuable practical speedups; identified weaknesses are suggestions for future work rather than fatal flaws.
