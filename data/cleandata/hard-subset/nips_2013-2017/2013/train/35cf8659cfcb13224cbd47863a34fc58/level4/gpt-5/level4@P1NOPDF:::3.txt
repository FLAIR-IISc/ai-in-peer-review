The paper is well written, with strong spelling, grammar, and correct citations—well above average in this respect. However, in NIPS style it is preferable not to begin a sentence with a citation. The theory is presented succinctly; for example, the consistent use of f() and g() throughout is clear and economical.

The work studies stochastic block models (SBMs) and extends the simple single-table instance of the IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose Trees. This lets the CRP formulation of the IRM be replaced by the simpler Eq. (7). The factorization over subtrees follows ideas from Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Sec. 3), where the posterior neatly factorizes under a constrained class of structures. As a result, greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction can all be carried out cleanly.

Evaluation for SBMs is often conducted against gold-standard data with known partition structure, using metrics such as NMI and related clustering scores to compare against the ground-truth clustering. You report AUC, and I had to think about how this applies here since I am more familiar with NMI-based evaluations for SBMs. I presume you hold out a subset of edges and then predict whether they should be present (link prediction), scoring with AUC. If so, please state this clearly and describe the protocol (holdout rate, negative sampling, repeated splits, etc.). In any case, the evaluation needs a more explicit explanation.

Recent SBM evaluations are run on larger datasets—larger than your full NIPS corpus—so the small-scale experiment in Figure 4 is a good start but should be complemented with results on larger datasets. It is also not sufficient to compare only against Bayesian nonparametric methods; there are many published semi-parametric baselines (some cited) that should be included to provide a fair and competitive comparison.

The core contribution is the agglomerative method; Table 1 summarizes it well. The theoretical development and algorithms are clear, though the underlying concepts and procedures are relatively simple. Overall, the experiments are illustrative rather than comparative, and thus are not yet adequate to substantiate the empirical advantages of the approach. Strengthening the evaluation (clarifying the AUC setup, adding NMI on datasets with known partitions, scaling to larger graphs, and including semi-parametric baselines) would considerably improve the paper.
