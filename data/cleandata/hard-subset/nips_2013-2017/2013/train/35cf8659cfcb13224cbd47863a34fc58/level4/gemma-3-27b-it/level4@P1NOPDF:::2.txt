The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, aiming to infer a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities based on observed links between them. They introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid partitions of the entities. Empirical results on two social connectivity datasets demonstrate that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for this type of task.

I believe this paper addresses an interesting problem that will resonate with a significant portion of the NIPS audience. Bayesian nonparametric methods, while conceptually appealing, are often computationally expensive in practice. Simultaneously, social scientists are increasingly employing sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis, and a hierarchical extension to a popular network discovery model like the IRM would be a valuable contribution. This paper engages with both of these aspects.

The key insight is that restricting allowed partitions to a tree structure allows for efficient computation of the marginal likelihood, a significant advantage over the IRM, which must consider an exponential number of possible partitions. However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative story was somewhat unclear to me. Given its greedy nature, it's not obvious whether the algorithm performs true posterior inference over a distribution of trees, or represents a point estimate (MAP or ML). I may have missed something, and I recommend the authors clarify Section 4.

The results are impressive. Across several performance measures (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performed comparably to, or slightly better than, the IRM. While the absolute improvements in these scores may not be dramatic, the reported runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

Overall clarity and writing quality could be improved. First, there are distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126). Second, the prose is sometimes difficult to follow. For example, lines 47-50 in the introduction contain vague phrasing (what are ‘local changes’ in this context? What ‘iterations’ are being referred to – iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The authors also do not sufficiently describe the datasets used for training (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), which could confuse readers unfamiliar with them. I found some of the mathematical notation somewhat unnecessarily complex, such as Eq. 5. Figure 3 is also confusing; unfamiliarity with the Monastery dataset makes it difficult to visually assess which method performs best. A more widely-recognized dataset might be more informative. Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, required multiple readings to fully understand. 

However, if these issues are addressed, I believe the proposed approach is novel enough and the results are strong enough to justify publication. 

The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure, essentially a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning algorithm insightful and the experimental results compelling, but the writing quality needs improvement.
