This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core contribution is to adapt the framework of Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data. The model is conceptualized as a tree-structured mixture over a potentially exponential number of stochastic blockmodels, where the tree defines a prior over partitions consistent with its hierarchical structure. A key advantage of this formulation is that the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree can be calculated exactly and efficiently via dynamic programming. For inference, the authors propose a greedy agglomerative algorithm that builds the hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion, starting with each node as its own community. This approach is computationally efficient, with a worst-case complexity of O(n^2 log n), and importantly, is independent of the number of communities. The method is evaluated qualitatively on the Sampson's monastery network and the full NIPS co-authorship dataset, and quantitatively on a subset of the NIPS data, demonstrating a significant speedup (two orders of magnitude) over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or superior predictive accuracy.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the model, which extends prior work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering to the relational setting, is clear and correct. The proposed greedy inference algorithm is well-described and justified by the local Bayes factor score. The claims of computational efficiency and strong predictive performance are well-supported by experimental results comparing BHCD to the widely-used IRM. The authors are careful to present results for several variants of their model (e.g., sparse and binary-restricted versions) and honestly compare their performance. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a novel model, an efficient inference algorithm, and a thorough empirical evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, and the background on stochastic blockmodels provides the necessary context. The core model in Section 3 is explained logically, building from the marginal likelihood of a standard blockmodel to the tree-structured mixture model. The figures, particularly Figure 1 illustrating the tree-consistent partitions, are very helpful for understanding the mechanics of the model. The description of the agglomerative algorithm is also clear and easy to follow. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The main theoretical components—using a tree to define a mixture over partitions and learning it via a greedy agglomerative procedure—are drawn directly from previous work on Bayesian Rose Trees. However, the application and adaptation of these ideas to relational data and community detection is novel and significant. There is a non-trivial contribution in designing the model likelihood for relational data using block-specific sufficient statistics and, critically, in developing the caching scheme that allows the sufficient statistics to be updated in O(1) time per merge consideration, thereby preserving the O(n^2 log n) complexity of the original algorithm in this new domain.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial challenge in the field: scaling Bayesian nonparametric models to large network datasets. Many existing methods, such as MCMC-based inference for the IRM, are notoriously slow, which limits their practical application. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster while maintaining strong performance, this work makes hierarchical Bayesian community detection much more accessible to practitioners. The O(n^2 log n) complexity of the base algorithm may still be a bottleneck for truly massive networks, but the "sparse" variant presented should scale much better in practice for the sparse networks common in real-world applications. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon and used by other researchers working on scalable inference for network models.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of scalable inference for hierarchical community detection in networks.
*   The proposed method (BHCD) offers a massive speed improvement (two orders of magnitude in experiments) over a standard baseline (IRM) with comparable predictive performance.
*   The paper is very clearly written, making a sophisticated model accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.
*   Represents a successful and novel adaptation of powerful ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering to the network modeling domain.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons) & Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Scalability on Larger Networks:** While the speedup on the NIPS-234 dataset is impressive, the O(n^2 log n) worst-case scaling remains a concern for larger networks. The paper would be significantly strengthened by reporting the runtime for the "Full NIPS" experiment (n=2864) and providing a more detailed discussion of the practical scalability limits of both the standard and sparse versions. How would inference scale to networks with tens of thousands of nodes?
*   **Optimality of Greedy Inference:** The greedy algorithm provides no guarantee of finding the optimal hierarchy. While empirically effective, the paper would benefit from an analysis of how close the greedy solution is to the optimum. This could be investigated on small synthetic networks where the optimal tree can be found via exhaustive search.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Could the authors provide any theoretical support for the greedy merge criterion? For example, is there any connection to submodular function optimization that might provide insight into the quality of the solution? While not essential, such a discussion would add depth.

**Minor Corrections:**
*   Line 48, page 1: "whilst... but" is slightly redundant. Consider rephrasing.
*   Algorithm 1 caption area: "pair of treeS" should be "pair of trees".
*   Section 6, last sentence: "A fruitful next steps" should be "fruitful next steps".

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant practical contribution. The adaptation of the Bayesian Rose Tree framework to network data is elegant and the resulting inference algorithm is impressively fast. While there are open questions regarding scalability and optimality, the work is a solid step forward for scalable Bayesian network analysis. The paper is well-executed and clearly presented, and I recommend its acceptance.
