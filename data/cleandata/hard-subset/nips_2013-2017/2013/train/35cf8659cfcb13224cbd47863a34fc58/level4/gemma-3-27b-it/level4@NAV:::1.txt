The authors introduce a hierarchical extension to the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modeling, leveraging concepts from Bayesian rose trees. Specifically, they utilize a hierarchical structure to define a mixture over coherent node partitions and employ an efficient, greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this hierarchy. The work includes qualitative analyses on the Sampson’s monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, alongside quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference procedure demonstrates substantially reduced computational cost compared to standard IRM, while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains its core concepts. 

While the underlying ideas build upon the Bayesian rose tree literature, adapting these concepts to the relational data setting, and preserving computational efficiency, represents a valuable contribution.

Scaling Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) models, particularly to network data, is a crucial challenge for practical application. Although the quadratic scaling of the presented algorithm may limit its use with very large networks, the sparse version offers potential for continued feasibility. It would be beneficial to include runtime measurements on larger, real-world networks.  For example, while inference on NIPS-234 completes in seconds, the runtime for the full NIPS dataset is not reported.  Considering the scale of typical biological (e.g., gene regulatory networks with tens of thousands of nodes) and social networks, investigating the scalability of inference is important. 

Further investigation into the quality of the greedy tree approximation would be valuable. Examining its performance relative to the optimal tree, determined via exhaustive search at the largest feasible scale, could provide insights. Exploring potential theoretical guarantees for the method, such as through submodularity analysis, would also strengthen the work.
