This paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and the overall algorithmic structure are essentially unchanged from Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted in a straightforward way to handle network data, and with some additional work, sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently. The presentation is generally clear, and the experiments compare favorably to a baseline with flat community structure; however, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are missing.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. Section 3 is particularly dense; there may be a simpler way to notate and explain Eq. (9) and the components that build on it. Because the empirical comparisons are limited to models without hierarchical latent structure, the experiments primarily demonstrate the benefit of modeling hierarchy for relational data, rather than establishing advantages over existing hierarchical approaches. In particular, at least two relevant hierarchical models are not discussed: the multiscale community blockmodel for network exploration (Ho et al., 2012), which uses a nested CRP, and the Mondrian process (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a stochastic process yielding hierarchical structures over k-d trees. These Bayesian nonparametric models could potentially discover more refined latent structure and should be considered in the related work and, ideally, in comparisons.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The model here is effectively a Bayesian rose tree paired with a standard block-model likelihood. The main innovations lie in how the new likelihood alters the recursive updates of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative procedure. The fact that the likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time is a noteworthy strength. That said, the description of how this is achieved is not sufficiently clear: there is an allusion to dynamic programming before Eq. (10), but the details are hard to follow. Similarly, the equations for recursively updating sufficient statistics are presented with limited explanation of why they are correct, making it difficult to verify or reproduce the method.

TERMINOLOGY:
The use of “nonparametric” is questionable in this context. Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose tree approach as model selection and explicitly contrast it with nonparametric methods. I agree with their interpretation.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative analysis on the Sampson dataset is solid, and the comparison to the IRM nicely illustrates the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. The main limitation remains the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models, which would more directly test the advantages of the proposed hierarchy. There is some discussion of scalability, but none of the evaluated networks are especially large.

Overall, the paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with block-model likelihoods for networks, extending efficient agglomerative clustering to this setting. It demonstrates effective hierarchical community discovery, but its contribution would be stronger with clearer algorithmic exposition (especially around Eqs. (9)–(10) and the sufficient-statistics updates) and empirical comparisons to established hierarchical network models.
