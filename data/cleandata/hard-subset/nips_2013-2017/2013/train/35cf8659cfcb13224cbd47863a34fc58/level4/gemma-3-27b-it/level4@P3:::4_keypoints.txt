- Proposes an efficient greedy agglomerative method combining Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for hierarchical community detection.  
- Enables efficient recursive updates of sufficient statistics and reports O(Nodes + Edges) time for likelihood computation.  
- Presentation is generally clear, with Section 3's dense notation ultimately effective.  
- Experiments show favorable comparisons to a flat community baseline and demonstrate benefits of modeling hierarchy.  
- Lacks comprehensive comparisons to other hierarchical network models, weakening claims of novelty and relative performance.  
- Should compare and contrast with hierarchical Bayesian methods like Ho et al. (2012) and Roy et al. (2009).  
- Main innovation is adapting recursive sufficient-statistic updates to the blockmodel likelihood within a greedy agglomerative framework.  
- Explanation of the claimed O(Nodes + Edges) efficiency and the dynamic programming details is insufficiently detailed.  
- Correctness of the recursive updating equations is presented but not thoroughly justified or explained.  
- Use of the term “nonparametric” is debatable; rose trees may be better framed as model selection rather than strictly nonparametric.  
- Qualitative experiment on Sampson’s monastery network is reasonable and shows hierarchy benefits versus IRM.  
- Scalability claims are weak because experiments use moderately sized networks and don’t demonstrate challenges for modern compute.  
- The algorithm is computationally efficient, especially for dense networks, and is a principled Bayesian approach.  
- Paper is well-written and presents core ideas accessibly.  
- Recommend acceptance with minor revisions: add comparisons to hierarchical models, clarify efficient likelihood computation (dynamic programming), and reconsider “nonparametric” terminology.
