The authors present a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling, building on key ideas from the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) paper: (1) the hierarchy specifies a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes; and (2) the hierarchy can be learned using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. They provide qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity. The paper is very well written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear.

Originality. Although the main ideas are drawn from the BRT work, adapting them to the relational data setting required nontrivial effort, particularly to maintain the same computational complexity.

Significance. Scaling BNP models—especially for networks—is an important challenge if these methods are to be broadly useful to practitioners. While the O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for very large networks, the sparse variant should remain feasible. It would be helpful to include timings on larger real-world networks. NIPS-234 takes only tens of seconds; how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Typical gene networks have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are larger still: how would inference scale in these regimes?

It would also be valuable to assess how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be investigated at the largest scale for which the optimal tree can still be found by exhaustive search. Is it possible to provide any theoretical support for the method—for example, guarantees based on submodularity or related properties?

While the ideas presented here are familiar from the BRT literature, the contribution remains significant, and I recommend acceptance.
