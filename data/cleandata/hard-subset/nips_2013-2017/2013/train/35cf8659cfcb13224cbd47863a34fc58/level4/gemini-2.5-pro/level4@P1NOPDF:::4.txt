This paper proposes a scalable, agglomerative approach for clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over the (possibly non-binary) trees and the overall algorithmic structure are adopted directly from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The main contribution is the modification of the likelihood to handle network data, which in turn allows for the efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is very clear, and the experiments show favorable results against a baseline model with a flat community structure. However, the work is limited by a lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK**
The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. However, the presentation in Section 3 is particularly dense; the notation and explanation surrounding Equation (9) and its subsequent development could be simplified for greater clarity.

A more significant issue is that the experimental comparisons are limited to models that do not incorporate hierarchy in their latent structure. Consequently, while the results effectively demonstrate the benefit of modeling hierarchy for relational data, they do not establish whether the proposed method is superior to other existing hierarchical approaches. For example, the authors overlook at least two relevant Bayesian nonparametric models that also discover hierarchical latent community structures. The "Multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) utilizes a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009) defines a novel stochastic process that uses hierarchical partitions of space as its prior. A discussion of how the proposed model relates to these methods would significantly strengthen the paper.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY**
The model is essentially a Bayesian rose tree combined with a standard block-model likelihood. The primary innovation, therefore, lies in adapting the recursive updates of sufficient statistics for this new likelihood within the greedy agglomerative clustering framework. A valuable property of this approach is that the likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time.

Unfortunately, the description of this computation lacks clarity. The paper alludes to dynamic programming before Equation (10), but the details of the implementation are difficult to discern. Similarly, regarding the recursive updating of sufficient statistics, the paper provides the relevant equations but offers little intuition or derivation to support their correctness.

**TERMINOLOGY**
It is questionable whether the "nonparametric" terminology is appropriate for this model. Blundell et al. [12] originally framed the rose tree approach as a form of model selection, explicitly contrasting it with nonparametric methods in their discussion. This reviewer agrees with their interpretation, which seems more fitting.

**EXPERIMENTS**
The qualitative experiment on the Sampson monastery dataset is illustrative, and the comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively demonstrates the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. However, as noted above, the primary limitation of the experimental section is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models. Furthermore, while the paper discusses scalability, none of the networks used in the evaluation are particularly large-scale, leaving the claims about scalability not fully substantiated.

In summary, the paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic block model likelihood for networks and successfully generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this new scenario. This allows for effective hierarchical community discovery in the conducted experiments, but the absence of comparisons to other state-of-the-art hierarchical network models is a major weakness.
