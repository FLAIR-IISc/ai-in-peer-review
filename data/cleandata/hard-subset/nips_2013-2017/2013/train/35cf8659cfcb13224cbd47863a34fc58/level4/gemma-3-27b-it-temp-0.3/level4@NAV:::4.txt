This work presents an agglomerative approach for scalable network clustering into hierarchical communities. The model builds upon existing Bayesian rose tree methodology [12], retaining the prior structure and algorithmic framework, while adapting the likelihood function for network data and maintaining efficient sufficient statistic caching. The paper is clearly presented, and experimental results demonstrate favorable performance compared to a baseline flat community model; however, it lacks comparisons to other hierarchical network models.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:**

The paper is generally well-written and appears technically sound. Section 3’s presentation is particularly dense, and simplifying the notation and explanation of equation (9) and its subsequent derivations could improve clarity. While the experiments effectively demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets, the limited comparison set – focusing on models *without* inherent hierarchical structure – prevents assessment of performance relative to existing hierarchical methods. Specifically, the paper does not engage with relevant Bayesian nonparametric models like the nested Chinese Restaurant Process utilized in Ho et al. (2012) or the Mondrian Process described by Roy et al. (2009), both of which offer alternative approaches to discovering refined latent hierarchical structures.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:**

The core model can be viewed as a Bayesian rose tree combined with a standard stochastic block model likelihood. Consequently, the primary innovations lie in adapting the sufficient statistic updates for recursive agglomerative clustering within this new likelihood framework. The O(Nodes + Edges) computational complexity is a positive attribute, but its description lacks sufficient detail; the reference to dynamic programming before equation (10) is insufficient to fully understand the implementation. Furthermore, while equations are provided for recursive updating, the justification for their correctness is not clearly articulated.

**TERMINOLOGY:**

The use of “nonparametric” terminology is debatable. Consistent with Blundell et al. [12], framing this approach as model selection, and contrasting it with true nonparametric methods, appears more accurate.

**EXPERIMENTS:**

The qualitative experiment on the Sampson network is adequate, and the comparison to the IRM effectively illustrates the advantages of model averaging for prediction. However, the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models remains a significant limitation. While scalability is discussed, the networks used in the experiments are not particularly large-scale. 

In summary, this work successfully integrates prior research on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering techniques to this setting. This enables effective hierarchical community discovery, but would benefit from more comprehensive benchmarking against existing hierarchical network models.
