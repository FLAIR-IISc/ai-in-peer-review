This paper is well-written, with excellent spelling, grammar, and proper citations, exceeding average expectations. However, it would be beneficial to avoid starting sentences with citations in the NIPS style. The theoretical aspects are concisely presented, effectively utilizing notations such as f() and g() throughout the paper.

The paper explores stochastic block models (SBM) and enhances the simple single table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This integration enables the replacement of the CRP formulation of IRM with the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees aligns with existing work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood's JMLR 2004 section 3, where the posterior factorizes neatly based on the chosen class of constrained structures, facilitating greedy search, marginal likelihoods, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and more.

Evaluations of SBM typically involve comparison with "gold standard" data, where the partition structure is known. Metrics like NMI and other clustering measures are commonly used for this purpose. Although the paper reports using AUC, it was initially unclear how this was applied, given the familiarity with NMI evaluations for SBM. Presumably, the approach involves deleting links and then predicting their addition. This warrants further explanation. Recent SBM evaluations have utilized larger datasets, exceeding the size of the full NIPS dataset; therefore, presenting results for a larger dataset would be beneficial. The comparison against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient, as there are numerous published semi-parametric methods, some of which are mentioned in the paper.

The algorithm's contribution lies in its agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper extends the simple single table instance of an IRM by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees, presenting good, clear theory and algorithms, albeit based on a simple concept. However, the experimental work is more illustrative than comparative, rendering it inadequate.
