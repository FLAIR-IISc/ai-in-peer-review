

The authors propose a hierarchical extension of the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modeling, adapting key concepts from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) literature: (1) using the hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions, and (2) learning this hierarchy via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The method is evaluated on Sampson's monks, the full NIPS dataset, and the NIPS-234 dataset, demonstrating computational efficiency (significantly cheaper than IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

**Quality/Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The core ideas are clearly articulated, and the technical exposition is precise and accessible.

**Originality.** While the foundational concepts derive from the BRT work, adapting them to relational data—particularly ensuring the same computational complexity as the original IRM—represents non-trivial effort. The adaptation to network structures is the paper's primary contribution.

**Significance.** Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially to network data, is crucial for practitioner adoption. The proposed method addresses this by reducing the computational burden from the standard $O(n^2)$ complexity. However, the paper leaves critical scalability questions unanswered:  
- The NIPS-234 dataset runs in tens of seconds; how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset?  
- For larger real-world networks (e.g., gene networks with $10^4$+ nodes, social networks with millions), what are the practical scaling limits? The sparse variant is promising, but empirical evidence on large-scale networks is missing.  
- How close does the greedy method get to the optimal tree? It would be valuable to assess this gap on the largest datasets where exhaustive search for the optimal tree is feasible.  
- Is there theoretical justification for the greedy approach? Submodularity arguments or approximation guarantees would strengthen the method's foundation.

**Minor Corrections:**  
- Line 48, Page 1: "whilst... but" → "while" (or "whereas").  
- "pair of treeS" → "pair of trees".  
- "A fruitful next steps" → "A fruitful next step".

**Overall Recommendation.** Despite the ideas being inspired by the BRT work, the adaptation to network modeling, the demonstrated computational efficiency, and the clear presentation constitute a significant contribution. The paper addresses an important practical challenge in BNP modeling. While the scalability questions warrant further investigation, they do not undermine the core contribution. The paper should be accepted.
