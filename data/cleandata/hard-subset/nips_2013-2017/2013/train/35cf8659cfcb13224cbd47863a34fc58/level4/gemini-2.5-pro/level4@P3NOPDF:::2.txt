This paper introduces a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model. The goal is to infer a hierarchical structure over entities, rather than a flat clustering, based on observed network links. To achieve this, the authors propose a learning algorithm based on greedy agglomerative clustering. This algorithm constructs a tree that constrains the set of possible partitions, allowing for efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of the data. The model is evaluated on two social network datasets, where it is shown to be significantly faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a prominent baseline for this task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses an important and interesting problem. Inferring hierarchical structure from relational data is a fundamental task with broad applications, particularly in the social sciences. As sophisticated Bayesian models become more prevalent in these fields, a hierarchical extension to a popular network model like the stochastic block model is a welcome contribution that will be of interest to a significant portion of the NIPS audience.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The key technical insight—restricting the space of possible partitions to those consistent with a tree structure—is clever. This constraint circumvents the primary computational bottleneck of models like the IRM, which must contend with an exponential number of possible partitions. By enabling the efficient calculation of the marginal likelihood, the authors offer a practical solution to the often-prohibitive runtime of Bayesian nonparametric methods.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experimental results are strong. The proposed method performs comparably or slightly better than the IRM across several key metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy). While the absolute improvements in these scores are modest, the reported runtime is orders of magnitude faster. This trade-off—achieving state-of-the-art or superior performance with a significant reduction in computational cost—is a major practical advantage.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of the Learning Algorithm (Section 4):** The primary area for improvement is the clarity of the connection between the proposed learning algorithm and the generative Bayesian model. The learning procedure is a greedy agglomerative algorithm. It is unclear how this relates to principled Bayesian inference. Is this algorithm intended to approximate MAP estimation over the space of trees? Or is it a heuristic for selecting a single tree structure, upon which inference is then performed? The authors should explicitly state the inferential goal of their algorithm and justify why this greedy approach is a reasonable method for achieving it. A more detailed explanation in Section 4 is crucial for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.

2.  **Writing and Presentation Quality:** The overall clarity of the manuscript could be significantly improved.
    *   **Prose and Terminology:** The manuscript would benefit from a thorough proofreading to address several grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., line 126). Certain passages are ambiguous; for instance, the introduction's reference to "local changes" and "iterations" (lines 47-50) is unclear. The authors should specify whether "iterations" refers to steps in an MCMC algorithm or another process.
    *   **Dataset and Figure Clarity:** The descriptions of the datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) are minimal. While familiar to some researchers, providing a brief background would make the paper more accessible to a broader audience. Similarly, Figure 3 is difficult to interpret without prior knowledge of the Monastery dataset. It is not immediately clear from the visualization which method performs best. The authors might consider using a more widely understood dataset for this key visualization or, at a minimum, improving the figure's caption and annotation to guide the reader's interpretation.
    *   **Mathematical Notation:** Some mathematical expressions could be simplified or better motivated. For example, Equation 5 felt unnecessarily complex and could potentially be presented more intuitively.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a novel and promising approach for hierarchical relational modeling. The core idea of using a tree structure to enable efficient computation is insightful, and the empirical results, particularly the significant runtime improvements over the IRM, are compelling. The primary weakness lies in the lack of clarity regarding the theoretical justification for the greedy learning algorithm and the overall quality of the presentation.

However, these issues are addressable. If the authors can substantially revise Section 4 to clarify the inference procedure and improve the overall writing and presentation as suggested, I believe this paper would be a valuable contribution to the conference. The novelty of the approach and the strength of the results warrant publication, provided these clarity issues are resolved.
