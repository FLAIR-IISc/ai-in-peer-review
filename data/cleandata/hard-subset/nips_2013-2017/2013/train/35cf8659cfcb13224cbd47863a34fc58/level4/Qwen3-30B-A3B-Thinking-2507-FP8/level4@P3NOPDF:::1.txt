

The paper introduces a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, leveraging key concepts from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) framework. Specifically, it proposes using a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent node partitions and employs a greedy agglomerative procedure for efficient learning. The authors demonstrate qualitative results on Sampson's monks and the full NIPS dataset, alongside quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Crucially, their method achieves comparable predictive performance to the IRM at significantly lower computational cost.  

**Quality and Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and logical exposition of the core ideas. The motivation, methodology, and results are presented cohesively, making the technical contributions accessible. Minor grammatical issues (e.g., "computational much cheaper" → "computationally cheaper") and phrasing inconsistencies (e.g., "practioners" → "practitioners") in the draft should be corrected.  

**Originality (7/10):** While the foundational concepts are adapted from the BRT work, the adaptation to relational data—particularly the maintenance of computational efficiency (O(n²) for dense graphs, O(n log n) with sparsity)—represents a non-trivial contribution. The authors successfully bridge a gap between hierarchical Bayesian modeling and scalable network analysis, though the novelty hinges on this specific application rather than a new theoretical framework.  

**Significance (8/10):** Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models to large networks remains a critical challenge for practical adoption. This work provides a compelling step toward that goal, offering a computationally efficient alternative to the IRM. However, the paper leaves open key scalability questions:  
- The NIPS-234 dataset (n≈234) runs in tens of seconds, but the full NIPS dataset (n≈1,500) is not timed. How does inference scale to this size?  
- Real-world applications (e.g., gene networks with 10k+ nodes, social networks with millions) demand larger-scale validation. The sparse variant is promising, but concrete timings on such networks would strengthen the case for practical utility.  
- The greedy agglomerative procedure’s suboptimality warrants investigation. How close does it get to the optimal tree? Exhaustive search on smaller datasets (e.g., n<50) could provide empirical bounds, and a theoretical analysis (e.g., submodularity guarantees) would add rigor.  

**Minor Corrections:**  
- Line 48, Page 1: "whilst... but" → "while" (redundant).  
- "pair of treeS" → "pair of trees".  
- "A fruitful next steps" → "A fruitful next step".  

**Overall Assessment:**  
Although the core ideas derive from the BRT work, the adaptation to relational data—particularly the computational efficiency and scalability focus—represents a meaningful contribution. The paper’s clarity, strong empirical results, and relevance to a pressing challenge in network modeling make it a valuable addition to the literature. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the computational advantages are significant for practitioners. Addressing the scalability and theoretical gaps (e.g., timing on larger networks, submodularity analysis) would further elevate the work, but these are reasonable suggestions for future work rather than critical flaws.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The contribution is significant for scalable network modeling, the writing is exemplary, and the results are compelling. The minor concerns raised are common for a methodological paper and do not detract from the core value. This work provides a practical, efficient tool for relational data analysis, advancing the applicability of BNP models in real-world settings.
