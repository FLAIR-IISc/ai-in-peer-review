This is a well-written paper with strong spelling, grammar, and citation practices—well above average. Please avoid beginning sentences with citations per NIPS style. The theory is concise; the consistent use of functions such as f() and g() helps readability, though some parts verge on being too terse.

The paper studies stochastic block models (SBMs) and extends the single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose Trees. This allows the CRP formulation in the IRM to be replaced by the simpler expression in Eq. (7). The posterior’s factorization over subtrees parallels results from Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Sec. 3), where constraints on structure yield clean factorization. This in turn enables efficient greedy search, straightforward computation of marginal likelihoods, reuse of sufficient statistics, and practical procedures for link prediction.

The principal algorithmic contribution is the agglomerative procedure, clearly summarized in Table 1. The theoretical development and algorithms are clear and well presented, though the overall idea is relatively simple.

The experimental evaluation is the weakest part. SBM performance is commonly assessed on datasets with known partitions using metrics such as NMI and related clustering measures. You report AUC instead, which is nonstandard in this context without a clear protocol. I infer that you may perform link prediction by withholding a subset of edges and evaluating recovery, but the paper needs to specify the setup precisely: how train/test splits are constructed, whether non-edges are sampled as negatives and how, how class imbalance is handled, and how uncertainty is integrated into predictions. Without this, the AUC results are hard to interpret or compare.

Recent SBM evaluations typically include much larger datasets than the full NIPS example you present. The small-scale experiment in Figure 4 is informative, but to support the claims it should be complemented with larger benchmarks that stress scalability and robustness. In addition, comparisons limited to Bayesian nonparametric baselines are insufficient. There is a substantial literature on semi-parametric and other competitive methods (some of which you cite); these should be included to position the contribution more convincingly. Reporting both clustering-quality metrics (e.g., NMI, adjusted Rand index) on labeled datasets and link-prediction metrics (e.g., AUC/AP) with a clearly defined protocol would give a more complete picture.

In summary:
- Strengths: Excellent writing; clear, succinct theory; a neat extension of the IRM via Bayesian Rose Trees that yields a factorized posterior and enables efficient agglomerative inference; practical implications for likelihood computation and link prediction.
- Weaknesses: Limited and small-scale empirical evaluation; unclear AUC protocol; narrow baseline set that omits prominent semi-parametric methods; lack of results on larger datasets and standard clustering metrics when ground truth is available.

Recommendations for improvement:
- Clarify the link-prediction evaluation in full detail (edge holdout strategy, negative sampling, number of trials, confidence intervals).
- Add experiments on larger, widely used SBM benchmarks to demonstrate scalability and robustness.
- Include comparisons to strong semi-parametric and other non-BNP baselines.
- When possible, report NMI/ARI against known partitions in addition to AUC/AP.
- Minor style point: avoid starting sentences with citations in accordance with NIPS style.

Overall, the paper presents a clear and elegant agglomerative approach with a solid theoretical foundation, but the empirical validation is more illustrative than comparative and is currently not sufficient to fully substantiate the claims.
