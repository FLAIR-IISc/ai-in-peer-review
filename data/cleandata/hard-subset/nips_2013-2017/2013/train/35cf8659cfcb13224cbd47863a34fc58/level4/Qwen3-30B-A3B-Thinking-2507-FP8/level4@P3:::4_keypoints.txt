- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method extending Bayesian rose trees to relational data via a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels.  
- Introduces a novel caching scheme and recursive sufficient-statistics updates that yield O(n² log n) time complexity for inference.  
- Claims comparable accuracy to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving roughly two orders of magnitude faster inference.  
- Clear connection to Bayesian hierarchical clustering prior work, but insufficient engagement with key hierarchical network models.  
- Omits important comparisons to models like the Multiscale Community Blockmodel and the Mondrian Process, which would be meaningful baselines.  
- Technical formulation is well structured, with the main innovation being efficient recursive updating of sufficient statistics.  
- The claimed O(n+m) marginal likelihood computation and derivations (eqs. 9–13) lack sufficient detail and justification.  
- Section 3 is dense; the notation for sufficient statistics is cumbersome and could be simplified for clarity.  
- Experimental evaluation demonstrates speed and qualitative results (Sampson, NIPS co-authorship) but does not show superiority over other hierarchical models.  
- Experiments do not assess whether discovered hierarchies are more interpretable or accurate than alternatives, nor do they include sufficiently large networks to fully demonstrate scalability.  
- Originality is limited: BHCD adapts existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering techniques to networks rather than proposing a fundamentally new modeling approach.  
- The use of the term “nonparametric” is questionable and needs clearer justification given prior work’s distinctions.  
- Clarity and organization are generally good, but the paper needs more explanatory detail, simpler notation, and a small illustrative example for the recursive updates.  
- Strengths: clear algorithm presentation, substantial computational speedup over IRM, reasonable qualitative results, conjugate-prior motivation, and practical sparse-network handling.  
- Weaknesses: missing comparisons to relevant hierarchical methods, insufficient explanation of the dynamic programming / sufficient-statistics updates, debatable nonparametric claim, limited discussion of interpretability, and limited large-scale experiments.  
- Recommended revisions: compare to Multiscale Community Blockmodel and Mondrian Process, expand derivations and explanation of sufficient-statistics updates, clarify nonparametric claims, and include larger or more diverse experiments to establish significance.
