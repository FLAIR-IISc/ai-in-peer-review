This is a well-written paper with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations – well above average in these respects. A minor stylistic point: in NIPS submissions, it’s generally best to avoid beginning sentences with citations. The theory is presented succinctly in places, for example, through the consistent use of f() and g() notation throughout the paper.

The paper investigates stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the basic single-table instance of an infinite relational model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows for the replacement of the CRP formulation of the IRM with the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees mirrors work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as that by Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior factorizes elegantly based on the chosen class of constrained structures, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction.

Evaluation of SBMs is commonly performed using “gold standard” data, where the partition structure is known, and metrics like NMI and other clustering measures are used to compare results to the ground truth. You report using AUC, which initially puzzled me, as I’m more familiar with NMI evaluations in the context of SBM. I understand you are likely deleting links and then attempting to predict whether they should be added back – is this correct? Regardless, this procedure requires a clear explanation. Recent SBM evaluations have been conducted on much larger datasets than those presented here (including larger than the full NIPS dataset), so while the small evaluation shown in Figure 4 is valuable, it would be strengthened by presentation on a larger scale. Simply comparing against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient, as numerous published semi-parametric methods exist, some of which you mention.

The core contribution of this algorithm lies in its agglomerative approach, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. 

In essence, the paper extends the single-table IRM by integrating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. The theory and algorithms are clear and well-presented, although the underlying concepts and algorithms are relatively straightforward. The experimental work is more illustrative than comparative and, as such, is not entirely convincing. A more comprehensive evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.
