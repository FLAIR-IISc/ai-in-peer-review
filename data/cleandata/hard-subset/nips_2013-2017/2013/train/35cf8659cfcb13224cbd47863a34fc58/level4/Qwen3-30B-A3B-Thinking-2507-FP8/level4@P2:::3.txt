

This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, introducing the Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method. The authors build upon stochastic blockmodels (SBMs) and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), extending previous work on hierarchical community detection to develop a more efficient algorithm that maintains probabilistic modeling while significantly reducing computational complexity.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it formulates a hierarchical mixture of SBMs where communities are organized in a tree structure, with each internal node representing a community and leaves representing individual vertices. Second, it develops an efficient greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that learns the hierarchical community structure in O(n² log n) time, where n is the number of vertices. This is significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, including IRM, which scales poorly with network size. Third, the paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the model, including marginal likelihood calculations using dynamic programming and a principled approach to link prediction.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing the computational complexity of their approach and demonstrate through experiments that BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster. The experimental evaluation includes qualitative analysis on Sampson's monastery network, comparative analysis on a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and exploration of the full NIPS network. The authors correctly use AUC and predictive probability as evaluation metrics, though they could have provided more explanation about how these metrics relate to hierarchical community detection.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the technical developments. The use of f(·) and g(·) to abstract the on-diagonal and off-diagonal block models simplifies the presentation. The authors also provide a good comparison of computational complexity in Table 1, clearly showing how BHCD's complexity differs from other methods.

Regarding originality, the paper extends previous work on hierarchical community detection in a novel way. While hierarchical community detection has been explored before, the authors' approach of combining Bayesian hierarchical clustering with SBMs using a tree-structured prior represents a significant advance. The paper effectively differentiates itself from related work, particularly the IRM and its extensions, by emphasizing the computational efficiency of their approach. However, the paper could better clarify how this work differs from the Mondrian process [12] and other Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in network analysis: discovering meaningful hierarchical community structures in large networks. The authors demonstrate that BHCD can handle networks of realistic size (e.g., the full NIPS network with 2,864 vertices), which is a practical contribution to the field. The speed advantage of BHCD over IRM makes it a valuable tool for applications where computational efficiency is critical. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope, focusing on relatively small networks compared to the scale of networks that could benefit from this approach.

The main weaknesses of the paper are:
1. The experimental evaluation is limited in scope. The authors compare against IRM but do not benchmark against recent semi-parametric methods for hierarchical community detection that have been developed since the IRM. The evaluation on the NIPS-234 subset is illustrative but not comprehensive enough to demonstrate the full value of the method.
2. The explanation of evaluation metrics (AUC) is insufficient. While AUC is a standard metric for link prediction, the paper does not clearly explain how it's applied to hierarchical community detection.
3. The paper could better contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of hierarchical community detection methods. While it references relevant work, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate from approaches like the Mondrian process [12] or the work by [11] on hierarchical community detection.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. The clear theoretical development of the BHCD model, including the tree-structured prior and efficient inference algorithm.
2. The well-executed experimental evaluation demonstrating the computational advantage of BHCD over IRM.
3. The practical utility of the method, particularly for large-scale network analysis where computational efficiency is critical.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound and well-written approach to hierarchical community detection that offers significant computational advantages over existing Bayesian methods. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the core contribution is valuable and represents a meaningful step forward in the field. I believe the paper meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical contribution with well-supported claims
- Significant computational advantage over existing methods
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Practical utility for real-world network analysis

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental evaluation on larger datasets
- Insufficient explanation of evaluation metrics
- Could better differentiate from related work in hierarchical community detection

The paper is within scope for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in network analysis with a novel computational approach. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the experimental evaluation and clarify the evaluation metrics.
