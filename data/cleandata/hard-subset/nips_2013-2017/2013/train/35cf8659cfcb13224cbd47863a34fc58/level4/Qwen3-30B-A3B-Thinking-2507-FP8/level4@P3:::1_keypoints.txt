- Introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community detection in networks.  
- Adapts Bayesian Rose Tree framework to relational data, maintaining a full probabilistic model over tree-consistent partitions.  
- Uses a greedy agglomerative inference procedure with O(n² log n) time complexity and worst-case scaling independent of number of nested communities.  
- Demonstrates comparable predictive performance to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being ~100× faster in experiments.  
- Provides clear theoretical foundation including marginal likelihood calculations and dynamic programming for efficient inference.  
- Experimental evaluation includes multiple datasets (Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship) with qualitative visualizations and quantitative predictive metrics.  
- Method is technically sound and well-supported, with reproducible presentation and helpful supplementary material.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and precise in definitions and algorithm description.  
- Main novelty is extending tree-structured Bayesian clustering to relational data with computational efficiencies; idea builds on prior Bayesian Rose Trees work.  
- Significant practical impact by scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods to larger networks; BHCD sparse variant can handle many real-world sparse graphs.  
- Limitations: greedy optimization may not find global optimum and lacks theoretical guarantees on optimality.  
- Limited scaling analysis on very large networks; additional timing on full NIPS and much larger graphs would strengthen claims.  
- Needs clearer theoretical analysis of the greedy procedure (e.g., submodularity) and clarity on hyperparameter γ’s effect on tree structure.  
- Suggested experiments: timing on full NIPS dataset and small-scale exhaustive comparisons to quantify greedy quality loss.  
- Minor editorial fix: line 48 wording ("whilst" usage).  
- Overall assessment: valuable, technically strong contribution with significant computational advantages—worth acceptance pending more scaling/theoretical analysis.
