Proposes an agglomerative method for scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and the algorithmic framework are essentially unchanged from Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted in a straightforward way to networks, and with some additional work, sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently. The presentation is generally clear, and the experiments compare favorably to a flat community baseline; however, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are missing.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. The exposition in Sec. 3 is particularly dense; a simpler notation and explanation for Eq. (9) and the subsequent derivations that rely on it would help.

Because comparisons are limited to models without hierarchical latent structure, the experiments primarily show that modeling hierarchy benefits relational datasets, but they do not establish whether the proposed approach outperforms existing hierarchical methods. For example, there are at least two relevant papers that model hierarchical community structure: the “Multiscale Community Blockmodel for Network Exploration” (Ho et al., 2012), which employs a nested CRP, and the “Mondrian Process” (Roy et al., 2009), a stochastic process that induces hierarchical structures over k-d trees as a prior. The paper does not mention these Bayesian nonparametric models, which could potentially discover more refined latent structure.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The model here is essentially a Bayesian rose tree coupled with a standard stochastic block-model likelihood. The main innovations lie in how this new likelihood changes the recursive updates of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative procedure. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a valuable practical feature.

Unfortunately, the description of these computational aspects is not very clear. There is an allusion to dynamic programming before Eq. (10), but the details are difficult to follow. Similarly, for the recursive updates of sufficient statistics, several equations are provided but with little explanation or intuition, making it hard to verify why they are correct.

TERMINOLOGY:
It is questionable whether “nonparametric” is the right term here. Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose tree approach as model selection and explicitly contrast it with nonparametric methods; I agree with their interpretation.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative experiment on the Sampson dataset is appropriate, and the comparison to the IRM nicely demonstrates the benefits of averaging over many hypotheses for prediction. The largest limitation remains the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models. While scalability is discussed, none of the evaluated networks are especially large.

In sum, the paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block-model likelihoods for networks and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this setting. This yields effective hierarchical community discovery in the reported experiments, but the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models is a notable shortcoming.
