- Proposal: BHCD — a Bayesian nonparametric tree-structured mixture over SBMs that marginalizes exactly over tree-consistent partitions using node-specific stopping probabilities.

- Inference for a fixed tree: beta–Bernoulli conjugacy yields a dynamic-programming recursion for p(D|T) computable in O(n + m).

- Model search: greedy agglomerative merging with three merge types (join, absorption), local Bayes-factor scores, and caching for O(1) inner updates; overall O(n^2 log n).

- Sparse variant: forbids merging subtrees with no connecting edges to improve practicality.

- Prediction: missing-link probabilities computed by a recursive mixture along root-to-leaf paths.

- Experiments: qualitative recovery on Sampson’s network, link prediction on NIPS-234 (much faster than IRM, comparable/better accuracy, similar to LFRM but below ILA), and qualitative hierarchy on full NIPS (2,864 nodes); multiple random restarts used.

- Relation to prior work: extends Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data via SBMs, offering a computationally cheaper alternative to IRM and hierarchical IRM variants.

- Strength (quality): probabilistic formulation and DP recursion are sound; within/between prior choices are sensible.

- Strength (quality): agglomerative search is well engineered and complexity claims are plausible; sparse variant is practical.

- Limitation (quality): greedy local search has no global optimality guarantees.

- Limitation (quality): hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed with no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference.

- Limitation (quality): experiments focus on undirected, unweighted data despite claimed generality to other data types.

- Clarity: paper is well written with helpful figures, but experimental setup lacks some reproducibility details (held-out protocol, variance across restarts).

- Originality: adaptation of tree-as-mixture idea to SBMs with an efficient, cache-friendly scoring function is a useful and nontrivial contribution.

- Significance: offers an attractive accuracy–speed trade-off and complexity independent of number of communities, making it practically useful for scaling Bayesian nonparametrics on networks.

- Scalability caveat: worst-case O(n^2 log n) may be large for very big graphs; more timing/memory profiling on large networks is needed.

- Pros summary: principled model with exact marginalization; simple, efficient agglomerative inference; strong empirical speedups over IRM; interpretable hierarchies.

- Cons summary: greedy search without guarantees; fixed hyperparameters; limited baselines run by authors; scalability and parallelization not thoroughly profiled; claimed generality not empirically validated.

- Key suggestions: report timing/memory on full NIPS and larger graphs (including merge counts and effect of restarts); add hyperparameter sensitivity or empirical Bayes; compare directly to modern baselines (LFRM/ILA); assess robustness of greedy search (variance, optimality gaps); clarify held-out link protocol and report confidence intervals; include directed/weighted experiment if feasible.

- Minor corrections: fix capitalization/grammar in abstract and sections, correct typos (e.g., “Which pair of tree(s) to merge”), standardize Algorithm 1 capitalization, mention R = 50, standardize punctuation/spacing around equations, and complete references.

- Recommendation: Accept — well executed adaptation of Bayesian Rose Trees to network SBMs with compelling empirical and computational results, pending suggested analyses and minor edits.
