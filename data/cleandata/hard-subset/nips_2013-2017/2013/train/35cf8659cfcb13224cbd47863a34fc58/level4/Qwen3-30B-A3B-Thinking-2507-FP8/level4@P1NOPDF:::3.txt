

This paper is well-written with correct grammar, spelling, and citations. The theoretical exposition is clear in parts, particularly through consistent notation like f() and g(). However, avoid beginning sentences with citations in NIPS style.

The paper extends the single-table instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose tree theory, enabling a simpler formulation (Equation 7) that replaces the CRP-based IRM. The factorization over subtrees aligns with established work on Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3), where posterior factorization under constrained structures facilitates efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, sufficient statistic handling, and link prediction.

The evaluation methodology requires clarification. Standard SBM evaluation uses "gold standard" partitions with metrics like NMI, but the paper reports AUC. It is unclear how AUC is applied here—presumably by removing edges and predicting their presence, but this must be explicitly explained. The current presentation assumes familiarity with this approach, which is not universal.

The experimental evaluation is limited. Figure 4 uses a small dataset (NIPS), but recent SBM work employs significantly larger datasets. While the small-scale results are illustrative, the paper must demonstrate performance on larger datasets to establish broader relevance. Additionally, comparing only against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient; the evaluation should include established semi-parametric alternatives (some of which are cited), as these represent the current state-of-the-art in the field.

The core contribution—the agglomerative algorithm—appears well-summarized in Table 1. The theoretical development is sound and clearly presented, though the algorithm itself is conceptually straightforward. The experimental section, however, is primarily illustrative rather than comparative. It lacks rigorous benchmarks against relevant baselines on meaningful data scales, weakening the empirical claims. This limits the paper's impact, as the proposed method’s advantages over existing approaches remain unquantified in a convincing manner.
