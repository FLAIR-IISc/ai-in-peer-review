

The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors extend Bayesian nonparametric methods to the hierarchical setting by modeling communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. This differs from previous work that either focused on flat community structure (like the Infinite Relational Model, IRM) or limited hierarchical approaches with computational constraints. The key technical contribution is an efficient greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that computes the marginal likelihood of the tree-structured model in O(n² log n) time, significantly faster than IRM's O(n²K²) complexity where K is the number of communities.

The paper makes several important contributions to the literature. First, it provides a principled Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery that avoids the combinatorial explosion of possible partitions by restricting to tree-consistent partitions. Second, it demonstrates that the marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently using dynamic programming, a significant improvement over previous hierarchical Bayesian approaches. Third, the authors provide empirical evidence that BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster, particularly on sparse networks where the sparse variant (BHCD sparse) excels.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. It builds on the IRM framework [5-7] while addressing its computational limitations, and it extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering work of Heller et al. [13,14] to relational data. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work, including the Mondrian process [12] and recent hierarchical community detection methods [8-11]. The paper's approach to hierarchical community detection represents a meaningful step beyond flat community models, addressing a gap in the literature where most Bayesian approaches remain limited to flat structures.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical soundness: The model is well-motivated, with clear generative process and marginal likelihood derivation. The dynamic programming approach for efficient inference is technically solid.
2. Practical significance: The computational efficiency (O(n² log n) vs. IRM's O(n²K²)) makes the method applicable to larger networks than previous Bayesian approaches, addressing a critical bottleneck in the field.
3. Empirical validation: The experiments on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data demonstrate both qualitative and quantitative advantages over IRM. The speed-accuracy tradeoff is particularly compelling.
4. Clarity of core contribution: The greedy agglomerative algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-specified and the complexity analysis is thorough.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity of Section 4: The connection between the generative model and the greedy learning procedure requires more explicit explanation. The paper claims the method is "probabilistic" but doesn't clarify whether it approximates the posterior or finds a MAP estimate. A clearer statement about the optimization objective would help readers understand what the algorithm is actually doing.
2. Writing quality: Several grammatical errors and awkward phrases appear throughout (e.g., "in the worst case, Our algorithms" on line 126). The description of the datasets (Sampson's monastery, NIPS co-authorship) lacks sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with these networks.
3. Figure 3: The presentation of results on Sampson's network is confusing without clear labels or explanation of the "best" method. A visual comparison that clearly shows which method produces the most interpretable hierarchical structure would be valuable.
4. Mathematical exposition: Equation (5) and the tree-consistent partition formalism could be made more accessible with a brief example. Some mathematical details (like the sufficient statistics) could be simplified for clarity.

The paper addresses all four NIPS criteria effectively. Quality is strong with sound theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results. Originality is clear through the novel combination of Bayesian nonparametric methods with hierarchical community discovery. Significance is demonstrated by the practical speed improvements and applicability to real-world networks. Clarity is mostly good but would benefit from the suggested improvements.

The authors should clarify the relationship between their greedy algorithm and the underlying Bayesian model. The paper states that "BHCD produces good results two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM," but it's unclear whether the comparison is with one Gibbs step of IRM or a full MCMC chain. A more precise comparison would strengthen the empirical claims.

For improvement, I recommend:
1. Adding a brief paragraph explicitly stating whether the algorithm approximates the posterior, finds a MAP estimate, or uses another optimization approach.
2. Providing more context for the datasets (e.g., "Sampson's monastery network is a well-known social network dataset from a 1960s study of monastic relationships" and "NIPS co-authorship network contains 2864 authors with 9466 co-authorship edges").
3. Revising Figure 3 to include clear labels showing which method produces the most coherent hierarchical structure.
4. Correcting grammatical errors and simplifying overly technical mathematical explanations where possible.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear theoretical contributions and significant practical implications for community detection in social networks. The method represents a meaningful advancement over previous Bayesian hierarchical approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency. With minor revisions to improve clarity and precision, this work would make a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
