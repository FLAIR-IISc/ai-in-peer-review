Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, leveraging key ideas from Bayesian rose trees (BRT). The hierarchy defines a mixture over all partitions consistent with the tree, and is learned via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors present qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS coauthorship dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is substantially cheaper than standard IRM inference while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality and Clarity
The paper is very well written and the main ideas are clearly presented. The connection to BRT is explained succinctly, and the description of the agglomerative procedure is accessible and well motivated. Figures and experiments, where shown, support the narrative.

Originality
While the core concepts are adapted from BRT, the extension to relational data is nontrivial. Preserving favorable computational complexity in the network setting and translating the mixture-over-partitions interpretation to IRM-style likelihoods constitute meaningful technical contributions.

Significance
Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models to large networks remains a crucial challenge for practical adoption. The proposed approach, especially with the sparse variant, has the potential to make IRM-like models more tractable. The empirical results indicate that the method can match IRM predictive performance at lower computational cost. This is a valuable step toward scalable, principled network modeling.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framework: using a tree to induce a mixture over consistent partitions is elegant and well grounded.
- Efficient inference: the greedy agglomerative procedure is intuitive and appears to offer significant speedups.
- Solid empirical evidence: results on NIPS and Sampson’s monks are appropriate and show competitive performance with reduced cost.
- Good exposition: the paper is easy to follow and well structured.

Weaknesses and Questions
- Scalability claims would be stronger with more comprehensive timing and memory results on larger real-world networks. NIPS-234 takes tens of seconds; what are the absolute runtimes for the full NIPS dataset and for denser graphs of similar size? How does performance scale empirically with nodes and edges?
- The n^2 complexity of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for large networks. More detail on the sparse implementation would be helpful: what are the assumptions (e.g., bounded average degree), and what is the empirical scaling in the sparse regime?
- Optimality of the greedy tree: it would be useful to quantify how close the greedy solution gets to the optimal tree. On small datasets where exhaustive search is feasible, how often does the greedy procedure recover the optimum, or how far is it in objective value?
- Theoretical support: can the authors provide any guarantees or structural properties? For example, is the objective submodular or approximately submodular in any setting, enabling approximation bounds for the greedy merge heuristic? Even partial results or negative findings would be informative.
- Evaluation clarity: please specify which predictive metrics are used (e.g., held-out likelihood, AUC for link prediction), whether hyperparameters were tuned, and how uncertainty is handled when comparing models.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Include timing and memory usage across datasets of increasing size and sparsity, ideally with plots showing wall-clock and complexity versus number of nodes and edges. Report parallelization details, if any.
- Provide results for a larger real-world sparse network (e.g., a subset of a social or gene interaction network) to demonstrate applicability beyond NIPS-scale graphs. Reporting the runtime on the full NIPS dataset would also help calibrate expectations.
- Add a small-scale study comparing the greedy tree against an optimal or near-optimal solution (via exhaustive search or strong heuristics) to assess the quality of the greedy merges.
- Offer theoretical insights, even if modest: e.g., monotonicity of the merge objective, sufficient conditions where greedy is optimal, or bounds under specific likelihood assumptions.
- Discuss practical guidance: when should one prefer the hierarchical model over standard IRM; how to set or learn hyperparameters; and how sensitive performance is to initialization and merge criteria.

Minor Corrections
- Page 1, line 48: replace “whilst ... but” with either “whilst ...” or “..., but ...” (not both).
- “pair of treeS” → “pair of trees”
- “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step”

Overall Assessment
This is a strong and timely paper. It adapts BRT ideas to relational modeling in a principled way and demonstrates meaningful computational gains with competitive predictive accuracy. With additional empirical scaling evidence and a brief discussion of theoretical properties, the contribution would be even more compelling. Despite the familiarity of some ideas from BRT, the adaptation to networks and the attention to computational efficiency make the work significant. I recommend acceptance.
