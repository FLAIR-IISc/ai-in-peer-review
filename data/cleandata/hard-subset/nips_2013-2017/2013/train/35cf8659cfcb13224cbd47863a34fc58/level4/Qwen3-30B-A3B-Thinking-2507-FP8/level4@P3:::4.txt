

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. BHCD builds upon Bayesian rose trees [12] and extends them to relational data using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors present a novel caching scheme that enables efficient dynamic programming computation of the marginal likelihood, resulting in O(n² log n) time complexity where n is the number of vertices. The method is designed to scale better than existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), achieving comparable accuracy with two orders of magnitude faster inference.

The paper makes a clear connection to prior work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and extends it to relational data. However, the relationship to key hierarchical network models is underdeveloped. While the authors cite the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5-7] and mention extensions that incorporate hierarchy [8-11], they omit important Bayesian nonparametric hierarchical network models such as the Multiscale Community Blockmodel [11] and the Mondrian Process [12]. The Mondrian Process specifically defines a novel stochastic process that results in hierarchical structures over K-d trees, which would provide a meaningful comparison point for evaluating the novelty and effectiveness of BHCD.

In terms of technical quality, the paper presents a well-structured model with clear mathematical formulation. The key innovation appears to be the efficient recursive updating of sufficient statistics that enables the O(n² log n) time complexity. While the authors claim the marginal likelihood can be computed in O(n+m) time, this is not adequately explained in the paper. The derivation of equations (9)-(13) lacks sufficient detail to fully understand why the recursive updates are correct, which is particularly problematic given the paper's focus on computational efficiency. The presentation in Section 3 is dense, and the notation for the sufficient statistics (equation 9) could be simplified to improve clarity.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models. While the experiments demonstrate that BHCD outperforms IRM in speed while maintaining comparable accuracy, they do not establish whether BHCD is better than existing methods specifically designed for hierarchical network structure. The qualitative experiment on Sampson's monastery network shows reasonable results, and the comparison to IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset demonstrates the computational advantages, but the experiments fail to address whether the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD is more meaningful or accurate than that produced by other hierarchical methods.

Regarding originality, the paper does not present a fundamentally new approach to hierarchical community discovery but rather adapts existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering techniques to network data. The key contribution appears to be the efficient computational scheme, but the paper does not clearly distinguish this from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The authors' use of "nonparametric" terminology is questionable, as Blundell et al. [12] explicitly contrast their model selection approach with nonparametric methods. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why BHCD should be considered nonparametric.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections and clear presentation of the algorithm. However, the mathematical details in Section 3, particularly regarding the recursive sufficient statistics, would benefit from additional explanation. The paper would be more accessible if the authors provided a simpler notation for the sufficient statistics updates and included a small illustrative example.

Strengths:
- Clear presentation of the core algorithm
- Demonstrated computational advantages over IRM (two orders of magnitude faster)
- Good qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network
- Well-motivated use of conjugate priors for computational efficiency
- Practical implementation details (e.g., handling sparse networks)

Weaknesses:
- Lack of comparison to relevant hierarchical network models (e.g., Ho et al. 2012, Roy et al. 2009)
- Insufficient explanation of the dynamic programming approach and sufficient statistics updates
- Questionable use of "nonparametric" terminology
- Limited discussion of whether the discovered hierarchies are more interpretable or useful than those from other methods
- Experiments do not test the method on sufficiently large networks to demonstrate scalability

The paper makes a capably integrated contribution that generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering to hierarchical community discovery in networks. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models significantly limits the ability to assess whether BHCD offers a meaningful advance over existing approaches. The computational efficiency is a clear strength, but the significance of the work would be substantially enhanced if the authors demonstrated that their hierarchical models produce better or more interpretable structures than existing methods.

Pro: Strong computational efficiency, clear presentation, good qualitative results
Con: Lacks comparison to relevant hierarchical methods, questionable nonparametric claim, insufficient explanation of key technical details

The paper would benefit from a revision that includes comparisons to the Multiscale Community Blockmodel [11] and Mondrian Process [12], a clearer explanation of the sufficient statistics updates, and a more precise discussion of the nonparametric nature of the approach. With these improvements, the paper could make a more significant contribution to the field of hierarchical community detection. As it stands, the paper demonstrates a useful computational advance but does not clearly establish how it advances the state-of-the-art in hierarchical community discovery.
