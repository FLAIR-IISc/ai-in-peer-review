This paper introduces a Bayesian hierarchical model for community discovery in social networks, effectively extending the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by leveraging ideas from Bayesian rose trees. The core innovation lies in representing the hierarchical community structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, coupled with a computationally efficient greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this hierarchy. The authors demonstrate the method’s performance on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network (NIPS-234), and the full NIPS co-authorship network, highlighting significant speed improvements over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts. The authors effectively bridge the gap between the Bayesian rose tree framework and relational data modeling, successfully adapting existing techniques to a new domain. This adaptation, particularly maintaining favorable computational complexity, represents a noteworthy contribution. 

The originality of the work stems from this successful adaptation and application of Bayesian rose trees to relational data. While building upon existing ideas, the authors’ contribution is substantial, providing a practical and efficient method for hierarchical community detection. 

The significance of this work is considerable. Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models, particularly for network data, remains a major challenge. This paper addresses this challenge head-on, offering a computationally tractable solution that could make such models more accessible to practitioners. The reported speed-up compared to the IRM is impressive. However, further investigation into scalability is warranted. While the method performs well on NIPS-234 (tens of seconds), it would be beneficial to understand its performance on larger, real-world networks. Specifically, providing timings for the full NIPS dataset and discussing potential limitations for networks with tens of thousands of nodes (e.g., gene networks or large-scale social networks) would strengthen the paper. Exploring the performance of the sparse version on larger networks is particularly important.

It would also be valuable to assess the quality of the solutions found by the greedy algorithm. Investigating how closely the greedy approach approximates the optimal tree (determined via exhaustive search, if feasible for smaller instances) would provide insights into its efficacy. Furthermore, exploring any theoretical guarantees regarding the algorithm’s performance—perhaps leveraging concepts like submodularity—could enhance the paper’s theoretical foundation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear and well-written presentation of a complex topic.
*   Successful adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to relational data.
*   Significant computational speed-up compared to existing methods.
*   Comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art techniques.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian network modeling: scalability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of scalability to very large networks.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality. 
*   No exhaustive search performed to determine the quality of solutions.
