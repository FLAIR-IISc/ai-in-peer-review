

The authors present a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, extending the stochastic blockmodel framework to allow for tree-structured partitions. Their approach, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), employs a greedy agglomerative model selection procedure to efficiently learn hierarchical community structures without requiring the number of communities to be known in advance. The method demonstrates significant computational advantages over existing approaches, achieving comparable or better accuracy than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being two orders of magnitude faster, with worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n) that is independent of the number of communities.

This work addresses an important gap in the literature by providing an efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery, which should interest both machine learning researchers and social scientists analyzing network data. The key technical insight lies in restricting the space of possible partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, enabling efficient computation of marginal likelihoods through dynamic programming rather than requiring exponential enumeration of all possible partitions as in the IRM.

The empirical results are compelling, showing that BHCD achieves performance metrics (AUC, predictive log likelihood, accuracy) comparable to or exceeding IRM while operating significantly faster. The runtime advantage is particularly notable given that BHCD requires only a single pass through the data, whereas IRM typically requires many iterations to converge.

However, the paper would benefit from several clarifications. The relationship between the greedy learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model is not clearly articulated, leaving uncertainty about whether the approach corresponds to posterior inference, MAP estimation, or another form of optimization. Section 4, which describes the core algorithm, requires multiple readings to understand and would benefit from clearer exposition. Additionally, the writing contains several distracting spelling and grammatical errors, and some sections (such as Equation 5 and Figure 3) are unnecessarily obscure. The paper would be more accessible with clearer descriptions of the datasets (Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship network) and more intuitive explanations of the mathematical formalism.

With these improvements to the exposition, the paper would present a strong contribution to the field, offering both theoretical insights and practical utility for network analysis. The method's efficiency and meaningful hierarchical structure discovery make it particularly valuable for large-scale network analysis tasks, and the results demonstrate a significant step forward in scalable Bayesian community discovery.
