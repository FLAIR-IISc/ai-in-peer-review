The authors present a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modelling using the key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper: 1) that the hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes 2) that this hierarchy can be learnt using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset, and full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset are presented. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM, whilst obtaining similar predictive performance.

The paper is well-written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The authors effectively summarize the main ideas of their paper and relate them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The paper builds upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, extending these approaches to relational data.

The strengths of the paper include its ability to scale Bayesian nonparametric models to larger networks, which is an important challenge if these methods are to be used by practitioners. The proposed method, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), is a significant contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on several datasets, including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset.

The weaknesses of the paper include the lack of theoretical support for the method. It would be beneficial to provide some theoretical analysis of the method, such as submodularity, to understand its limitations and potential biases. Additionally, the authors could provide more information on the computational complexity of the method, particularly for larger networks. While the sparse version of the algorithm may remain feasible for larger networks, it would be useful to provide some timings on larger real-world networks.

The paper could be improved by addressing the following questions: How close to the optimal tree does the greedy method get? Is it possible to provide any theoretical support to the method? How would inference scale for larger networks, such as gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes or real social networks with millions of nodes?

Arguments For Acceptance:
- The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric models for network data.
- The proposed method, BHCD, is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while obtaining similar predictive performance.
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on several datasets.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- The paper lacks theoretical support for the method.
- The authors do not provide enough information on the computational complexity of the method for larger networks.
- The greedy method may not always find the optimal tree, and it would be beneficial to provide some analysis of its limitations.

Overall, while the ideas presented in this paper are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is still significant, and this paper should be accepted.
