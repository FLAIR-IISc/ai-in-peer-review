The authors propose a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, drawing inspiration from the Bayesian rose tree paper. This approach utilizes a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and employs an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure for learning. The authors present qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Notably, the proposed inference method is computationally more efficient than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality and clarity: The paper is well-written, and the key ideas are clearly presented.

Originality: Although the main ideas are adapted from the Bayesian rose tree work, the authors have made significant efforts to tailor these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially network models, is an essential challenge for their practical application. While the basic algorithm's n^2 scaling may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version is expected to remain feasible. However, it would be beneficial to provide timings for larger real-world networks. For instance, the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds, but how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Furthermore, gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are even larger; it is essential to investigate how inference would scale in such scenarios.

Additionally, it would be valuable to assess the greedy method's performance in approximating the optimal tree. This could be explored by comparing the results to the optimal tree found through exhaustive search at the largest scale possible. Providing theoretical support for the method, such as in terms of submodularity, would also be beneficial.

Overall, while the ideas presented in this paper are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is still significant, and this paper should be accepted.
