- Proposes a Bayesian rose-tree prior combined with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for discovering hierarchical (non-binary) communities.  
- Inference uses a greedy agglomerative algorithm with efficient caching of sufficient statistics; worst-case scaling is quadratic in n and p(D|T) is computable in O(n + m).  
- Method builds on Bayesian rose trees and flat blockmodels (IRM/MMSB); connections to other hierarchical models (multiscale blockmodel, Mondrian) are noted but not compared empirically.  
- Model and algorithm are technically sound; conjugate Beta–Bernoulli likelihoods enable closed-form marginal likelihoods.  
- Dynamic programming recursion for tree marginal likelihood is a key strength but is tersely explained and would benefit from a worked example or pseudocode.  
- Recursive updates for sufficient statistics in agglomeration (join vs. absorption) are correct but under-explained and need derivations or intuitive justification.  
- Experimental methodology is reasonable: Sampson case study and NIPS-234 show faster runtimes and competitive predictive performance versus IRM, but datasets are not large.  
- Lack of direct empirical comparisons to other hierarchical network models leaves relative accuracy and interpretability unclear.  
- Exposition is generally clear but Section 3 and notation around eq. (9) are dense; key definitions like σ¬chSS need intuitive explanation and illustrations.  
- The O(n + m) computation claim should include a short derivation and explicit complexity argument (what is cached and how traversal accumulates terms).  
- Main novelty is tailoring rose-tree agglomerative clustering to relational data with local merge scores and efficient caching rather than a wholly new model.  
- The term “nonparametric” is misleading; the approach is better framed as Bayesian model selection over trees, not a fully nonparametric process.  
- Significance lies in computational efficiency (O(n^2 log n) inference, large speedups over IRM) and interpretable non-binary hierarchies, but impact would increase with hierarchical baseline comparisons.  
- Experiments: Sampson yields sensible qualitative clusters; NIPS-234 shows faster convergence than IRM; full NIPS visualization is interesting but lacks quantitative evaluation.  
- Pros: practical, efficient method; exact tree marginal likelihood via dynamic program; competitive predictive performance; interpretable hierarchies.  
- Cons: limited comparisons to hierarchical baselines, dense exposition around key recursions, debatable “nonparametric” framing, and no truly large-scale empirical evaluation.  
- Suggestions: add worked example/pseudocode for p(D|T) and eq. (13) updates, include at least one hierarchical baseline comparison, and clarify nonparametric vs. model-selection positioning.
