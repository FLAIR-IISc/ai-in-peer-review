- Contribution: Introduces a Bayesian nonparametric model (tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels) for hierarchical community discovery in networks.  
- Model: Internal tree nodes represent communities, leaves are vertices, and a tree-induced prior yields a restricted partition family.  
- Inference/algorithm: Uses dynamic programming for exact marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions and a greedy agglomerative procedure merging subtrees via local Bayes factors.  
- Merge operations: Bottom-up search uses three merge types (join and two absorptions) scored using local sufficient statistics with careful caching.  
- Complexity: Achieves overall O(n^2 log n) runtime independent of number of communities; a sparse variant further reduces work on sparse graphs.  
- Empirical performance: Comparable or slightly better predictive accuracy than IRM on NIPS-234 and Sampson’s monastery, with orders-of-magnitude speedups (seconds vs hundreds–thousands of seconds).  
- Interpretability: Recovers meaningful, often non-binary hierarchical groups (e.g., monastery data) aligned with prior analyses.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on stochastic blockmodels, Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and Bayesian Rose Trees; contrasts with costly IRM/MCMC approaches by restricting to tree-consistent partitions.  
- Novelty: Novel combination of a tree-induced prior over blockmodel partitions with an efficient agglomerative Bayesian scoring scheme.  
- Theoretical soundness: Likelihood/prior factorization and dynamic-programming derivation are sound and well-motivated.  
- Main methodological concern: The connection between the greedy agglomerative algorithm and formal Bayesian inference is underspecified—unclear if it optimizes a well-defined tree-level objective or is heuristic.  
- Clarity issues: Paper needs editing for grammar and precision; some passages and terms (e.g., “local changes”, “iterations”) are vague and datasets are under-described.  
- Presentation weaknesses: Equations (e.g., Eq. 5), figures (Figure 3), captions, and mathematical details need clearer motivation and explanation.  
- Sensitivity/robustness gaps: Limited discussion of hyperparameter choices, tie-breaking, restarts, variability across runs, and ablation studies (e.g., sparse vs non-sparse, binary vs non-binary).  
- Suggested improvements: Clarify the optimization objective of the agglomerative procedure, fix ambiguous phrasing, describe datasets and success metrics, explain tie-breaking/restarts and hyperparameter sensitivity, and add ablations.  
- Overall assessment: Timely, useful, and practical contribution with strong speed–accuracy trade-offs; recommend acceptance pending clarifications on Bayesian grounding and improved clarity.
