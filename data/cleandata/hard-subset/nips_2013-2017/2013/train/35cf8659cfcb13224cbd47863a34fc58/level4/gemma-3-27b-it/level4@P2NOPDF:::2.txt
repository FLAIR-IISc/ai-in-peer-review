This paper introduces a hierarchical extension to the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, aiming to discover hierarchical structures within networks rather than simple flat clusterings. The authors propose an efficient learning algorithm, based on agglomerative clustering, to identify a tree representing plausible partitions of the entities. Empirical evaluation on two social network datasets demonstrates that this approach achieves comparable or improved accuracy to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while offering significantly faster runtime.

I believe this work addresses an important and timely problem with broad appeal to the NIPS community. Bayesian nonparametric methods, while theoretically attractive, often suffer from computational inefficiency. Simultaneously, researchers in social sciences are increasingly adopting sophisticated Bayesian models for network analysis, making a hierarchical extension of a popular model like the IRM a valuable contribution. This paper sits at the intersection of these trends.

The core insight – restricting the search space to tree-structured partitions – enables efficient marginal likelihood computation, a significant advantage over the IRM’s consideration of an exponential number of potential partitions. However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model requires further clarification. The greedy nature of the algorithm raises questions about whether it performs true posterior inference, or approximates a MAP or ML estimate. I recommend the authors enhance the clarity of Section 4 to address this point.

The presented results are promising. The proposed method achieves performance comparable to, or slightly exceeding, the IRM across several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy). While the absolute improvements in these metrics may be modest, the reported orders-of-magnitude reduction in runtime compared to IRM inference is substantial. 

The overall presentation could benefit from significant improvements in clarity and writing quality. Numerous spelling and grammatical errors distract from the content (e.g., line 126). Furthermore, the prose is occasionally vague and difficult to follow. For instance, lines 47-50 in the introduction use imprecise language ("local changes," "iterations" – iterations of what?). The description of the datasets used for evaluation (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) lacks sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical notation, such as Eq. 5, appears unnecessarily complex. Figure 3 is also difficult to interpret, particularly for those unfamiliar with the Monastery dataset; a more widely known dataset might provide clearer visual comparison. Section 4, detailing the core contribution, requires careful and repeated reading for full comprehension. 

Addressing these issues would strengthen the paper considerably. However, even in its current form, the novel approach and strong empirical results suggest this work is worthy of publication. The authors present an innovative Bayesian model for graph edge structure, effectively merging agglomerative clustering with the IRM framework. While the core learning algorithm is insightful and the experimental results are encouraging, refinement of the writing is necessary.
