The paper proposes a scalable, agglomerative approach to clustering networks into hierarchical communities, leveraging a prior over trees that is essentially unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The algorithm structure is also largely unmodified, with the primary innovation being the extension of the likelihood to accommodate network data. This modification allows for efficient caching of sufficient statistics, albeit with some additional computational effort. The presentation is clear, and the experimental results demonstrate favorable performance compared to a baseline model with a flat community structure. However, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are noticeably absent.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. Section 3 is particularly dense, and it may be beneficial to explore alternative notations and explanations for equation (9) and its subsequent developments. The experimental comparisons are limited to models that do not incorporate hierarchy in their latent structure, which primarily serves to illustrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets. Notably, the paper does not compare its approach to other hierarchical methods, such as the multiscale community blockmodel for network exploration (Ho et al., 2012) and the Mondrian Process (Roy et al., 2009). These Bayesian nonparametric models, which also discover hierarchical latent structures, are not mentioned, and their omission is a significant limitation.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The proposed model appears to be a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. Consequently, the primary innovations lie in the modified likelihood and its impact on the recursive updating of sufficient statistics in the greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a desirable feature. However, the description of this process is not entirely clear, with only a brief allusion to dynamic programming before equation (10). Furthermore, the recursive updating of sufficient statistics is not thoroughly explained, with the provided equations being somewhat opaque.

TERMINOLOGY:
The use of the term "nonparametric" in this context is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose tree approach in terms of model selection and contrast it with nonparametric methods in their discussion. This interpretation is more accurate, and the authors may wish to reconsider their terminology.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative experiment on Sampson's data is satisfactory, and the comparison to the IRM is useful in demonstrating the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses when making predictions. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a significant limitation. While the paper discusses scalability, the networks tested are not particularly large, which undermines the significance of this aspect.

Overall, the paper successfully integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This enables effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models is a notable limitation.
