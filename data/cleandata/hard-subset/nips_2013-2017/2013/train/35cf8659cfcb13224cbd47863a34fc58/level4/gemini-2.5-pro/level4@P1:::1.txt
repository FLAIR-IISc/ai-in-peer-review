This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient Bayesian nonparametric method for uncovering hierarchical structures in network data. The model is elegantly formulated as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where the tree defines a prior over a set of consistent partitions of the network's nodes. Building on ideas from the Bayesian Rose Tree model, the authors propose a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm to learn the hierarchy in a single pass over the data. The paper presents qualitative results on the Sampson's monastery and full NIPS co-authorship datasets, and quantitative link prediction experiments on a subset of the NIPS data. The central claim is that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than competing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving comparable or better predictive accuracy.

**Quality and Clarity**
The paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly organized. The authors provide a lucid exposition of their model, its connection to stochastic blockmodels and previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and the details of the agglomerative inference algorithm. The derivations are easy to follow, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and provides a convincing case for the method's utility.

**Originality**
The core ideas are adapted from previous work on Bayesian Rose Trees. However, the application and extension of this framework to relational data are novel and non-trivial. A key contribution lies in successfully adapting the greedy inference scheme to handle the sufficient statistics for network data, thereby maintaining the O(n^2 log n) computational complexity of the original algorithm. This represents a valuable and original contribution to the literature on Bayesian network analysis.

**Significance**
The primary significance of this work lies in addressing the crucial challenge of scalability for Bayesian nonparametric models of network data. By providing a method that is significantly faster than MCMC-based approaches like the IRM, the authors make sophisticated hierarchical modeling more accessible to practitioners. The results on the NIPS-234 dataset are impressive, showing that BHCD can achieve strong predictive performance in seconds, whereas the IRM requires thousands of seconds. This work is likely to be built upon and used by other researchers in the field.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**
While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Scalability to Larger Networks:** The O(n^2 log n) complexity of the main algorithm may still prove prohibitive for the very large networks common today. While the sparse version is noted to be faster in practice, its worst-case complexity remains the same. The experiments on the NIPS-234 dataset (234 nodes) are compelling, but it would be very helpful to see runtime figures for the full NIPS dataset (2,864 nodes) to get a better sense of how the method scales. More broadly, how do the authors envision this approach scaling to networks with tens of thousands of nodes or more, such as those found in genomics or large-scale social media analysis?

2.  **Optimality of the Greedy Approach:** The greedy agglomerative procedure is key to the model's efficiency. It would strengthen the paper considerably to include an analysis of how close the solutions found by this greedy method are to the optimal tree under the model's posterior. This could perhaps be investigated on small networks where an exhaustive search for the highest-likelihood tree is feasible. Furthermore, are there any theoretical guarantees that can be provided for the greedy procedure, for instance, by relating the objective function to the optimization of a submodular function?

**Minor Corrections**
*   In Section 4, paragraph 2, "Which pair of tree to merge" should be "Which pair of trees to merge".
*   In Section 6, the final sentence, "fruitful next steps" would be more grammatically conventional than "a fruitful next steps".

**Conclusion**
Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution. Although the core ideas build upon existing work, their adaptation to the domain of relational data is both novel and effective. The proposed BHCD method offers a practical and principled way to perform Bayesian hierarchical community detection at a scale that was previously challenging for similar models. The questions regarding scalability and optimality are suggestions for future work rather than critical flaws. The paper is clear, technically solid, and addresses an important problem. I strongly recommend acceptance.
