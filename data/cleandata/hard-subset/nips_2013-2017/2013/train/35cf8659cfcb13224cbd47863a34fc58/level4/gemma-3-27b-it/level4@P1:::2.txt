The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to the hierarchical setting, in order to infer a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. They propose a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree which represents valid possible partitions of the entities. They then present empirical results from two corpora of social connectivity data and conclude that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for tasks of this kind. 

I believe this paper tackles an interesting issue that a significant fraction of the NIPS audience will be interested in. On the technical side, Bayesian nonparametric methods, while attractive conceptually, are often computationally expensive. On the problem domain side, social scientists are increasingly using sophisticated Bayesian models for analyzing data, so a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be well-received. This paper addresses both of those issues. 

The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. The IRM must essentially consider the exponential number of possible partitions of the data. However, I was left somewhat confused about how the learning procedure for the trees relates to the generative story behind the Bayesian model. Since it is greedy, it’s not clear whether it performs true posterior inference over a distribution of trees, or finds a point MAP or ML estimate. I may have misunderstood, and I would recommend that the authors clarify Section 4. 

I found the results to be impressive. In terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performed comparably to or slightly better than the IRM, the closest competitor. While the asymptotic improvements in these scores aren't dramatic, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference. 

I think the overall clarity and writing quality could be improved. First, there are several distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126). Second, the prose is sometimes difficult to understand. For instance, lines 47-50 in the introduction contain vague phrasing (what are ‘local changes’ in this setting? What ‘iterations’ are being referred to—iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The authors do not clearly describe the datasets they train on (Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS authorship network), which could confuse readers unfamiliar with them. I occasionally found the math somewhat dense and unnecessary, such as Equation 5. I also found Figure 3 confusing. Being unfamiliar with the monastery dataset, it is visually unclear which method is performing best. Perhaps a dataset more readers will be familiar with would be helpful. Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, required several readings to understand. 

If these issues could be remedied, I believe the proposed approach has enough novelty and strong enough results to warrant publication. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to hierarchical community detection, offering a speed improvement over existing Bayesian methods like IRM.
*   Solid empirical results demonstrating competitive performance on benchmark datasets.
*   The theoretical insight of leveraging a tree structure for efficient marginal likelihood computation is compelling.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity of writing needs improvement, including grammar, prose, and mathematical notation.
*   The connection between the greedy learning procedure and Bayesian inference requires further clarification.
*   Some figures (e.g., Figure 3) could be improved for clarity and accessibility.
