Review- The paper is well written, with strong spelling, grammar, and correct citations—well above average in presentation. That said, in the NIPS style, avoid starting sentences with a citation. The theory is concise in places, for example the use of f() and g() throughout.

The work addresses stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the simple single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. This lets the CRP-based IRM formulation be replaced by the simpler Eq. (7). The factorization over subtrees echoes results from Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3), where the posterior cleanly factorizes under constrained structure classes; consequently, greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, handling sufficient statistics, link prediction, etc., can all be carried out neatly.

Evaluation of SBM is often performed on “gold standard” data where the partition is known, using metrics such as NMI and related clustering measures that compare against ground-truth clusters. You report AUC, and I had to think through how that applies, as I am more accustomed to NMI-based SBM evaluations. I assume you hold out some edges and test whether they are recovered—if so, please make this explicit; the protocol needs to be clearly explained. Also, recent SBM evaluations use larger datasets than your full NIPS example; the small evaluation in Figure 4 is fine but should be complemented with a larger dataset. It is not sufficient to compare only against Bayesian non-parametric methods, as there are many published semi-parametric approaches, some of which you cite.

The key contribution of the algorithm is the agglomerative procedure, which Table 1 summarizes well.

Overall, the paper extends the single-table IRM by adding Bayesian Rose trees. The theory and algorithms are clear, though conceptually straightforward. The experiments are illustrative rather than comparative and thus are not really adequate.
