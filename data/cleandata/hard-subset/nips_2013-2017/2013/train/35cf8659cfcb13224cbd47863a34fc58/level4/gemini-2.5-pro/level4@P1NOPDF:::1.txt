The authors propose a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling. The approach builds upon key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree literature, namely: 1) using the hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and 2) learning this hierarchy via an efficient, greedy agglomerative procedure. The paper presents qualitative results on the Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, along with quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference method is computationally much cheaper than the standard IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the exposition of its key ideas is clear.

**Originality:** While the main ideas are drawn from the Bayesian rose tree literature, the authors have made a valuable contribution in adapting them to the relational data setting, particularly in preserving the method's computational efficiency.

**Significance:** Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models, particularly for network data, is an important challenge for their practical application. While the O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. To that end, it would have been helpful to see runtime results on larger, real-world networks. For instance, the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process; how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Furthermore, how would inference scale to gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes, or social networks that are larger still?

In addition, it would be valuable to understand how close the greedy method's solution is to the optimal tree. This could be investigated on a scale small enough to allow for finding the optimal tree via exhaustive search. It would also strengthen the paper to provide theoretical support for the method, for instance, by exploring connections to submodularity.

**Minor Corrections:**

*   Please check for consistent pluralization (e.g., "pair of trees").
*   The phrase "A fruitful next steps" is ungrammatical; please revise to "A fruitful next step" or "Fruitful next steps."

Although the core ideas are familiar from the Bayesian Rose Tree literature, the contribution is nonetheless significant. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
