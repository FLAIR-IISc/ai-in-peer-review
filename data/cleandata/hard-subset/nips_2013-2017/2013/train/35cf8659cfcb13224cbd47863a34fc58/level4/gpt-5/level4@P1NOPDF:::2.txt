The authors propose a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model that infers a hierarchy, rather than a flat clustering, of entities from observed links. They introduce an agglomerative clustering–based learning algorithm that selects a tree representing valid partitions of the entities, and present results on two social connectivity datasets, concluding that the method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely used baseline for this task.

This paper addresses a problem of clear interest to the NIPS community. Conceptually appealing as they are, Bayesian nonparametric methods are often slow in practice; at the same time, social scientists are increasingly adopting sophisticated Bayesian tools for network analysis. A hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model therefore seems timely and useful, and this paper speaks to both the methodological and application-side concerns.

The key insight is that by restricting partitions to those consistent with a tree, the marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently. In contrast, the IRM effectively faces an exponential number of possible partitions. However, I am confused about how the tree-learning procedure relates to the model’s generative story. Because the algorithm is greedy, it does not appear to perform posterior inference over a distribution of trees, nor is it obviously a point MAP or ML estimator. I may be misunderstanding, but Section 4 should clarify the connection between the proposed objective, the search procedure, and the underlying Bayesian model.

The empirical results are strong. Across several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method is comparable to or slightly better than the IRM. While the absolute gains in these scores are modest, the reported runtime improvements are orders of magnitude, which is compelling.

That said, the clarity and writing quality need improvement. There are several distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126). Parts of the prose are vague or hard to parse—for instance, lines 47–50 in the introduction: what exactly are “local changes” in this setting, and what do “iterations” refer to (MCMC iterations)? The datasets (monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not described clearly enough for readers unfamiliar with them. Some of the mathematics feels unnecessarily opaque (e.g., Eq. 5). Figure 3 is difficult to interpret—without prior familiarity with the monastery dataset, it is visually unclear which method performs best; using a more familiar dataset might help. Finally, Section 4, which contains the core contribution, required multiple readings to understand.

If these issues are addressed, the contribution is novel and the results appear strong enough to warrant publication. In summary, the paper presents a novel model of graph edge structure that can be viewed as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. The learning algorithm is insightful and the experiments are promising, but the exposition needs significant polishing for clarity.
