

The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for inferring hierarchical community structure in social networks using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The work extends Bayesian nonparametric methods by replacing the Infinite Relational Model's (IRM) Chinese Restaurant Process prior with a tree-based prior, enabling efficient greedy agglomerative inference. BHCD leverages Bayesian Rose tree theory [13,14] to factorize the marginal likelihood over subtrees, achieving O(n² log n) time complexity—two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining comparable accuracy. The paper demonstrates BHCD on Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data, with supplementary results on hierarchical structure discovery.

**Quality**: The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear factorization of the marginal likelihood (eq. 7-8) and rigorous derivation of the agglomerative algorithm (Algorithm 1). The use of conjugate priors for computational efficiency is well-justified, though the paper could better address limitations in the Beta-Bernoulli assumption (e.g., sensitivity to hyperparameters α, β, δ, λ). Experimental validation is adequate for the presented datasets but lacks scalability testing on larger networks beyond NIPS-234 (234 nodes), which is insufficient for a NIPS submission where recent SBM work evaluates on networks with thousands of nodes [e.g., 18, 21]. The AUC-based link prediction evaluation is not clearly explained—authors should clarify how held-out edges are selected (e.g., via edge deletion) and why AUC is appropriate for hierarchical community detection versus standard clustering metrics like NMI.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with precise notation (e.g., f(·), g(·) for on/off-diagonal blocks), clear algorithm descriptions, and effective use of figures (e.g., Fig. 1, 3). The generative process for tree-consistent partitions (eq. 5-7) is logically presented. Minor improvements could include: (1) avoiding sentence-starting citations (e.g., "As in [13]..." → "Building on [13]..."), and (2) expanding the AUC explanation in Sec. 5.2 to align with standard SBM evaluation practices.

**Originality**: The core contribution—applying Bayesian Rose tree theory to hierarchical community detection—is novel. It significantly advances beyond prior work [8,9,10,12] by avoiding the computational bottlenecks of tree-augmented IRM or depth-limited hierarchies. The greedy agglomerative approach (Sec. 4) is a clean extension of [14], and the theoretical factorization (eq. 10) is a key innovation. However, the paper does not sufficiently contrast with recent hierarchical SBM methods like [11] or [21], which also address scalability.

**Significance**: BHCD offers a practical solution for large-scale hierarchical community discovery, with clear computational advantages (Table 1) and real-world applicability to co-authorship networks (Fig. 5). The speedup over IRM is compelling for dense networks, and the sparse variant (BHCD sparse) improves efficiency on sparse data. However, the significance is partially undermined by limited experimental scope: the NIPS-234 results (Fig. 4) are presented as "comparable to LFRM" but lack comparison to state-of-the-art semi-parametric methods [e.g., 18, 21], which are now standard for SBM evaluation. The hierarchical structure on full NIPS (Fig. 5) is illustrative but not rigorously validated against domain knowledge.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
*Pro*:  
- Strong theoretical contribution with clear algorithmic innovation (O(n² log n) vs. IRM’s O(n²K²)).  
- Excellent writing, precise notation, and well-structured exposition.  
- Practical speedup (2×) with competitive accuracy on co-authorship networks.  
- Sparse variant improves efficiency for real-world sparse networks.  

*Con*:  
- Experimental evaluation insufficiently addresses scalability (no tests on networks >1,000 nodes).  
- AUC explanation for link prediction lacks methodological clarity.  
- Limited comparison to recent semi-parametric SBM methods (e.g., [18, 21]).  
- Hierarchical results (Fig. 5) are descriptive but not rigorously validated against ground truth.  

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to Bayesian hierarchical modeling but falls short of NIPS expectations in experimental rigor. With expanded evaluation on larger datasets and clearer methodological details for link prediction, it would be a strong candidate. As-is, it is **borderline for acceptance** due to the critical gap in evaluation scope.
