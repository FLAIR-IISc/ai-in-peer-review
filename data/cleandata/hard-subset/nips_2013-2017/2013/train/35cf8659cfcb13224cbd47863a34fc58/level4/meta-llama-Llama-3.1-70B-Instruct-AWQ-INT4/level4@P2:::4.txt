This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that can learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model in a single pass through the data.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The presentation in Section 3 is particularly clear, although some notations and explanations could be simplified. The authors provide a good overview of previous work on community discovery and Bayesian approaches to inferring community structure.

The model builds upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, extending these approaches to relational data. The authors use a tree to define a prior on partitions, where only partitions that are consistent with the tree are included in the sum. This allows them to evaluate the marginal likelihood exactly.

The authors describe an efficient scheme for inferring hierarchical community structure with their model. The algorithm is structurally the same as that in previous work on Bayesian rose trees, but with additional care needed to cache sufficient statistics. The algorithm has a time complexity of O(n^2 log n), where n is the number of vertices.

The authors demonstrate BHCD on several data sets, including Sampson's monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network. They compare their method to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and show that BHCD can make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks.
* The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their model and algorithm.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of BHCD in discovering hierarchical community structure.

Weaknesses:

* The paper lacks comparisons to other hierarchical network models, which makes it difficult to evaluate the novelty and significance of the proposed model.
* The authors could provide more details on the dynamic programming approach used to compute the marginal likelihood.
* The terminology "nonparametric" is questionable, as the model is based on a parametric approach.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks.
* The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their model and algorithm.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of BHCD in discovering hierarchical community structure.

Arguments for rejection:

* The paper lacks comparisons to other hierarchical network models.
* The terminology "nonparametric" is questionable.
* The authors could provide more details on the dynamic programming approach used to compute the marginal likelihood.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The authors should provide more comparisons to other hierarchical network models and clarify the terminology used in the paper. Additionally, they could provide more details on the dynamic programming approach used to compute the marginal likelihood.
