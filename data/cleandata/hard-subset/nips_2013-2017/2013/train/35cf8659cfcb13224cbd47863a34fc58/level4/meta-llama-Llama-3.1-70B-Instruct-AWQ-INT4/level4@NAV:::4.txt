This paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities by integrating prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods. Although the prior over trees and algorithm structure are largely unchanged from previous work on Bayesian rose trees [12], the likelihood has been modified to accommodate networks, and sufficient statistics can still be efficiently cached with some additional effort. The presentation is clear, and experimental results compare favorably to a baseline model with a flat community structure; however, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are noticeably absent.

**Significance & Related Work**

The paper is well-written and appears to be technically sound. Section 3's presentation could be improved, particularly in notating and explaining equation (9) and its subsequent components. The experimental comparisons are limited to models without hierarchical latent structures, demonstrating the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets but not necessarily outperforming existing hierarchical methods. Notably, the paper overlooks two Bayesian nonparametric models, the "multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009), which also model latent community structures hierarchically.

**Originality & Clarity**

The proposed model appears to be a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood, with innovations primarily in the recursive updating of sufficient statistics in the greedy agglomerative clustering. The likelihood computation in O(Nodes + Edges) time is a notable feature. However, the description of this process is unclear, with an allusion to dynamic programming before equation (10) that lacks detail. The recursive updating of sufficient statistics is also not well-explained, with equations provided but insufficient context for understanding their correctness.

**Terminology**

The use of the term "nonparametric" in this context is questionable, as Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose tree approach in terms of model selection, contrasting it with nonparametric methods. This interpretation is supported.

**Experiments**

The qualitative experiment on Sampson is satisfactory, and the comparison to the IRM is useful in demonstrating the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses for predictions. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a significant limitation. While there is some discussion on scalability, the tested networks are not particularly large.

**Overall**

The paper effectively integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering methods to hierarchical community discovery in networks. However, the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models is a notable limitation.
