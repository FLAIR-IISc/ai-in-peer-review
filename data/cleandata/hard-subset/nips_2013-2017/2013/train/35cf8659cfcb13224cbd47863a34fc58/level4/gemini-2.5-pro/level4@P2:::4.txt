This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient method for learning hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to combine a tree-structured prior, based on the Bayesian rose tree model [14], with a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood for relational data. The main contribution is an efficient, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm for inferring the hierarchy. This greedy model selection procedure runs in a single pass over the data, with a worst-case time complexity of O(n^2 log n) for a network with n vertices. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their method is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a popular Bayesian nonparametric model for flat community detection, while achieving comparable predictive accuracy.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Efficiency and Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its computational efficiency. The paper presents a compelling case for BHCD as a practical tool for network analysis. The greedy algorithm, with its clever caching of sufficient statistics, provides a significant speedup over iterative inference methods like MCMC used in models such as the IRM. The experimental results in Figure 4 strongly support the claim that BHCD can produce high-quality results in a fraction of the time required by the IRM, making it a valuable contribution for practitioners and researchers working with medium-to-large networks.

2.  **Model Soundness and Integration:** The paper does a good job of integrating two well-established lines of research: Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and stochastic blockmodels [1]. The combination is natural and results in a coherent probabilistic model for hierarchical community structures. The extension of the agglomerative clustering algorithm from Heller and Ghahramani [13] to handle the blockmodel likelihood is technically sound.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed for demonstrating the method's advantages over a non-hierarchical baseline. The comparison with the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset is thorough, evaluating log predictive probability, accuracy, and AUC over time. This clearly highlights the speed and performance benefits of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis on the classic Sampson's monastery network shows that the algorithm discovers meaningful and interpretable community structures.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical comparison to other hierarchical network models. The paper positions itself as a method for discovering hierarchical community structure, yet it is only compared against the IRM, a model for flat partitions. While the paper cites relevant hierarchical models like the Mondrian Process [12] and the Multiscale Community Blockmodel [11], it does not discuss their relationship to BHCD in detail or, more importantly, compare against them experimentally. This omission makes it difficult to assess the paper's true contribution. The experiments demonstrate the benefit of modeling hierarchy over not modeling it, but they do not establish whether BHCD is a state-of-the-art approach *among* hierarchical models.

2.  **Incremental Originality:** The novelty of the work is somewhat limited. The tree prior and the greedy agglomerative algorithm are adapted directly from Blundell et al. [14], while the likelihood is a standard stochastic blockmodel. The primary technical innovation appears to be the derivation of the efficient caching scheme that allows the sufficient statistics to be updated in O(1) time within the inner loop of the greedy algorithm. While this is a useful engineering contribution, the conceptual advance is modest.

3.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Technical Sections:** Certain parts of the paper are difficult to follow, hindering reproducibility.
    *   In Section 3, the paper states that the marginal likelihood (Eq. 6) can be calculated in O(n+m) time via dynamic programming, but it provides no details, pseudo-code, or even a clear intuition for how this is achieved.
    *   In Section 4, the recursive updates for the sufficient statistics (Eq. 13) are presented without derivation or explanation, making it hard to verify their correctness. A more detailed exposition in an appendix would be beneficial.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Terminology:** The use of the term "Bayesian nonparametric" is debatable. The original Bayesian rose tree paper [14] frames the approach as a form of Bayesian model selection over an unbounded space of tree structures, explicitly contrasting it with methods based on stochastic processes like the CRP. Aligning the terminology with the original work would be more precise.
*   **Experiments:** The scalability claims would be stronger if the method were tested on a network substantially larger than the full NIPS dataset (2,864 vertices). Given the O(n^2 log n) complexity, performance on denser or larger graphs would be of interest.
*   **Improving the Paper:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including an experimental comparison to at least one other Bayesian hierarchical community detection model, such as the Mondrian Process [12] or the model from Herlau et al. [8]. This would properly contextualize the performance of BHCD and validate its significance more rigorously.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a fast and effective algorithm for discovering hierarchical communities in networks by capably integrating prior work on Bayesian rose trees and stochastic blockmodels. Its primary contribution is its computational efficiency, which is well-supported by strong experimental results against a flat-structure baseline. However, the work is hampered by a lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models, which limits its perceived significance, and by a lack of clarity in key technical descriptions. While the method is certainly useful, its placement relative to the state of the art in hierarchical modeling is not established.
