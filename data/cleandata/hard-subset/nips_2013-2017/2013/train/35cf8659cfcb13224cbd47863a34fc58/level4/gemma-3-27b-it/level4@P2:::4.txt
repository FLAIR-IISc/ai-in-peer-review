This paper proposes an efficient agglomerative approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea builds upon Bayesian rose trees [12], adapting them to relational data by employing a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The algorithm maintains an efficient recursive update of sufficient statistics during greedy clustering, enabling scalability. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable performance compared to a flat community structure baseline; however, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are missing.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:**

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. The notation and explanation in Section 3, particularly surrounding equation (9) and its subsequent development, are dense and could benefit from simplification to improve readability. 

The experiments primarily demonstrate the benefit of modeling hierarchical structure for relational datasets, but do not directly compare against existing hierarchical methods. Specifically, the paper neglects to mention relevant Bayesian nonparametric models capable of discovering refined latent structures, such as the “multiscale community blockmodel for network exploration” [18] which utilizes a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the “Mondrian Process” [19] which employs a novel stochastic process resulting in hierarchical structures over K-d trees as its prior. Addressing these omissions would better contextualize the contribution.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:**

The proposed model can be viewed as a Bayesian rose tree with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. Thus, the primary innovations lie in adapting the model for networks and developing efficient recursive updates of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm. The resulting O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood calculation is a valuable feature. However, the description of this efficiency is somewhat lacking; the allusion to dynamic programming before equation (10) is insufficient to fully understand the details. Similarly, while equations are provided for recursive statistic updates, the underlying rationale for their correctness is not thoroughly explained.

**TERMINOLOGY:**

The use of “nonparametric” terminology is questionable. As noted by Blundell et al. [12], the rose tree approach is better framed as model selection, contrasting it with truly nonparametric methods. This distinction is relevant and should be acknowledged.

**EXPERIMENTS:**

The qualitative experiment on Sampson’s monastery network is adequate, and the comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively demonstrates the advantages of incorporating hierarchical structure for predictive performance. The most significant limitation remains the lack of comparison with other hierarchical network models. While the paper discusses scalability, the networks used in the experiments are not exceptionally large.

**Summary:**

The paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This allows for effective hierarchical community discovery as demonstrated in the experiments, but its impact is limited by the absence of comparison to competing hierarchical network models.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and clearly written approach to hierarchical community discovery.
*   The proposed algorithm is efficient, with a favorable time complexity for likelihood calculation.
*   Experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the model compared to a flat-community baseline.
*   The work extends existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering techniques to relational data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks comparison to other hierarchical network models.
*   The explanation of the efficient likelihood calculation is somewhat unclear.
*   The terminology “nonparametric” might be misleading.
*   The experiments do not demonstrate scalability on very large networks.
