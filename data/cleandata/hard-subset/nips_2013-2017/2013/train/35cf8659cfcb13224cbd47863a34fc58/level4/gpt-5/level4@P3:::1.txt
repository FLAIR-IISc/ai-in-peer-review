Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model represents a tree-structured mixture over stochastic blockmodels (SBMs): a tree T defines a set of partitions Φ(T) consistent with the hierarchy, and the data likelihood marginalizes exactly over these partitions using a prior p(φ|T) that depends on node-specific stopping probabilities πS. Using conjugate beta–Bernoulli SBMs with different priors for within- versus between-cluster edges, the marginal likelihood p(D|T) admits a dynamic-programming recursion that can be evaluated in O(n + m) for a fixed tree.

Learning proceeds via greedy agglomerative model selection. Starting from singletons, the algorithm merges a pair of subtrees at each step using a local Bayes factor that compares three merge types: join (new parent of both), or absorption (one subtree becomes a child of the other). Careful caching of sufficient statistics makes inner-loop updates O(1), yielding overall O(n^2 log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities. A sparse variant forbids merging subtrees with no connecting edges. Predictions for missing links are computed by a recursive mixture along the path from root to leaves.

Experiments include: qualitative recovery on Sampson’s monastery network; quantitative link prediction on the NIPS-234 coauthorship subset (BHCD and its sparse variants are roughly two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM while achieving comparable or better accuracy, and similar to previously reported LFRM results, though below ILA); and qualitative hierarchical structure on the full NIPS graph (2,864 nodes). The method uses multiple random restarts to mitigate tie-breaking variability.

Relation to prior work: BHCD builds directly on Bayesian Rose Trees [Blundell et al., 2010] by extending the tree-as-a-mixture-of-partitions idea to relational data via SBMs, providing an alternative to the IRM [Kemp et al., 2006] and its hierarchical variants [e.g., Herlau et al., 2011], with substantially lower computational cost.

Evaluation

Quality
- The probabilistic formulation is sound, and the dynamic-programming recursion for p(D|T) is clearly derived and efficient for a fixed tree. The beta–Bernoulli conjugacy and the differing within/between priors are sensible for community detection.
- The agglomerative search is well engineered (local Bayes factors, caching, three merge operators) and the complexity analysis is plausible. The sparse variant is a practical improvement.
- Main limitations: the search is greedy and local with no global optimality guarantees; hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed and no sensitivity analysis or inference is provided; experiments focus on undirected, unweighted data despite the generality claimed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally very well written, with clear exposition of the model, recursion, and algorithm. Figures (e.g., partition sets induced by a tree, qualitative matrices) help.
- The experimental section communicates the speed/accuracy trade-offs well, though more detail on setup (e.g., held-out edge protocol, variance across restarts) would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The core conceptual framework (hierarchically mixing over tree-consistent partitions) follows Bayesian Rose Trees, but adapting it to SBMs for network data with an efficient, local, cache-friendly scoring function is a useful and nontrivial contribution. The sparse variant and the practical demonstration at scale further strengthen novelty relative to hierarchical IRM work.

Significance
- Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models for networks is important. BHCD offers a compelling accuracy–speed trade-off, with complexity independent of the number of discovered communities and practical performance far faster than IRM. This should be useful in practice.
- That said, worst-case O(n^2 log n) may still be large for very big graphs. Demonstrations on larger real-world networks, or profiling on the full NIPS graph (wall-clock time, memory), would better substantiate scalability claims.

Pros
- Clear, principled model connecting trees and SBMs; exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Simple, efficient agglomerative inference with careful caching; sparse variant is practical.
- Strong empirical speedups over IRM with competitive predictive performance.
- Interpretable hierarchies on real datasets.

Cons
- Greedy local search without guarantees; no analysis of optimality gaps or consistency.
- Fixed hyperparameters; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference.
- Limited comparative baselines run by the authors (IRM only); references to ILA/LFRM are indirect.
- Scalability is argued but not thoroughly profiled on large graphs; memory and parallelization not discussed.
- Generality (directed/weighted/missingness patterns) is claimed but not empirically validated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide timing and memory usage on the full NIPS dataset and, if possible, a larger sparse network; include number of merges considered, heap operations, and effect of R restarts on runtime and variance.
- Add sensitivity analyses over α,β,δ,λ,γ; consider simple empirical Bayes or hierarchical priors to set them, or at least justify defaults.
- Compare directly to at least one additional modern baseline beyond IRM (e.g., LFRM or ILA) on the same folds to substantiate accuracy claims.
- Assess robustness of the greedy search: (a) report variability across restarts, (b) on small graphs, compare to exhaustive or near-exhaustive search to quantify optimality gaps, and (c) discuss whether the local score has any desirable properties (e.g., submodularity-like behavior) or provide counterexamples.
- Clarify the held-out protocol for link prediction (edge sampling, class imbalance handling) and report confidence intervals.
- If feasible, include a brief experiment on directed or weighted networks to demonstrate generality.

Recommendation
Accept. While the ideas build on Bayesian Rose Trees, the adaptation to relational data with an efficient, scalable inference scheme is well executed and empirically compelling. The paper is clear, and the contributions are likely to be useful to practitioners working with large networks. Addressing the suggested analyses and minor edits would further strengthen the final version.
