The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities based on observed links. They present a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid entity partitions. Empirical results on two social connectivity datasets demonstrate that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for network discovery.

I believe this paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem for a significant portion of the NIPS audience. Bayesian nonparametric methods, while conceptually appealing, often suffer from computational inefficiency. Simultaneously, social scientists are increasingly employing sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis, and a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model like the IRM would be valuable. This work touches upon both of these aspects. 

The key insight is that restricting allowed partitions to a tree structure allows for efficient computation of the marginal likelihood, a significant advantage over the IRM, which must consider an exponential number of possible partitions. However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative story was somewhat unclear to me. Given that the learning is greedy, it’s not obvious whether it performs true posterior inference over a distribution of trees, or represents a point MAP or ML estimate. Clarification of this point would be beneficial, and I recommend the authors improve the clarity of Section 4 accordingly. 

The results are impressive. Across several performance metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performs comparably to, or slightly better than, the IRM. While the absolute improvements in these scores may be modest, the reported runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

The overall clarity and writing quality could be improved. First, the manuscript contains distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126). Second, the prose is occasionally difficult to follow. For example, lines 47-50 in the introduction contain vague phrasing—what are ‘local changes’ in this context? To what ‘iterations’ does the text refer (e.g., iterations of an MCMC algorithm)? The authors also do not adequately describe the datasets used for evaluation (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), potentially confusing readers unfamiliar with them. I occasionally found the mathematical notation somewhat convoluted and unnecessary, such as in Eq. 5. Furthermore, Figure 3 is difficult to interpret; lacking familiarity with the monastery dataset, it is unclear which method performs best. A more widely recognized dataset might be more informative. Finally, Section 4, presenting the core contribution, required multiple readings for full comprehension.

However, if these issues are addressed, I believe the proposed approach offers sufficient novelty and demonstrates strong results to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be viewed as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering, inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning algorithm to be insightful and the experimental results compelling, but the writing quality requires improvement.
