

The paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical clustering of networks, building upon Bayesian rose trees [12] with a modified likelihood for network data. While the core model structure and prior remain largely unchanged from prior work, the authors adapt the likelihood to handle networks and demonstrate efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear overall, and experiments show benefits of hierarchical modeling over flat community structures (e.g., IRM), but the work lacks critical comparisons to established hierarchical network models, significantly limiting its significance.

**Significance & Related Work**  
The paper is well-written and technically sound, though Section 3 is dense. The notation for Equation (9) and its extensions could be simplified for better accessibility. Crucially, the experimental evaluation omits comparisons to two prominent Bayesian nonparametric hierarchical models: Ho et al.’s (2012) multiscale community blockmodel (using a nested Chinese Restaurant Process) and Roy et al.’s (2009) Mondrian Process (which induces hierarchical structures via k-d trees). These models explicitly address hierarchical community discovery, making their omission a major gap. Without benchmarking against them, the paper cannot claim superiority over existing hierarchical methods—its experiments only establish that hierarchical modeling (vs. flat) is beneficial for relational data, not that the proposed method outperforms alternatives.

**Originality & Clarity**  
The core innovation lies in adapting sufficient statistic updates for recursive agglomerative clustering under the modified likelihood. While the O(N + E) time complexity for likelihood computation is a notable advantage, the technical explanation is inadequate. The allusion to dynamic programming before Equation (10) is insufficient; the recursive update mechanism lacks intuitive motivation and detailed justification. The equations provided do not clarify *why* the updates are correct or how they generalize the rose tree framework. A more transparent derivation would strengthen the contribution.

**Terminology**  
The use of "nonparametric" is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] explicitly frame rose trees as a *model selection* approach, contrasting it with nonparametric methods (e.g., Dirichlet processes). The authors’ model does not involve infinite mixture components or adaptive complexity; it relies on a fixed tree structure with model selection. Adopting "nonparametric" here misrepresents the method and risks confusion with standard nonparametric Bayesian models.

**Experiments**  
The Sampson network analysis is appropriately qualitative, and the IRM comparison effectively illustrates the benefits of hypothesis averaging in predictions. However, scalability claims are under-supported: the networks tested (e.g., Sampson, Karate) are small, and no large-scale comparisons (e.g., with 10k+ nodes) are provided. The absence of comparisons to hierarchical models (Ho 2012, Roy 2009) remains the most critical limitation, as it prevents assessing whether the method delivers state-of-the-art hierarchical community discovery.

**Overall Assessment**  
The work effectively integrates Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block models for hierarchical network clustering, enabling efficient agglomerative inference. The technical approach is sound, and the scalability claim is plausible for moderate-sized networks. However, the lack of comparison to key hierarchical alternatives (Ho 2012, Roy 2009) fundamentally undermines the paper’s contribution. Without this, the method’s novelty and practical value remain unestablished. The terminology error further weakens the positioning. While the core algorithm is well-motivated and the presentation is generally clear, the paper would benefit from a more precise framing of its relationship to existing nonparametric hierarchical models and a thorough evaluation against them. As presented, the work is incremental and fails to demonstrate clear advantages over prior hierarchical approaches. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions addressing these gaps.
