

The paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical clustering of networks into communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and algorithm structure are largely unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted straightforwardly for networks, and sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently with minor additional effort. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable comparison to a baseline model with flat community structure. However, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are lacking.  

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:**  
The paper is generally well written and technically sound. Section 3 is particularly dense; a simpler notation or explanation for Equation (9) and its dependencies would improve accessibility. Crucially, the experiments only compare against non-hierarchical models, demonstrating the benefits of hierarchy for relational data but failing to assess whether the method outperforms existing hierarchical approaches. For instance, two relevant Bayesian nonparametric models—Ho et al. (2012) on multiscale community blockmodels using a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and Roy et al. (2009) on the Mondrian Process for hierarchical K-d tree structures—are not mentioned. These models could potentially discover more refined latent structures, making their omission a significant gap.  

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:**  
The core model is a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. Innovations lie solely in how the modified likelihood alters recursive sufficient statistic updates during greedy agglomerative clustering. The O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood computation is a notable strength. Unfortunately, the description of this efficiency is inadequate: an allusion to dynamic programming before Equation (10) lacks detail, and the recursive sufficient statistic equations lack intuitive justification.  

**TERMINOLOGY:**  
The use of "nonparametric" is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] explicitly frame rose trees as a model selection approach, contrasting it with nonparametric methods in their discussion. This interpretation is appropriate.  

**EXPERIMENTS:**  
The qualitative Sampson experiment is acceptable, and the comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively illustrates the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses. The primary limitation remains the absence of comparisons to hierarchical network models. While scalability is discussed, none of the tested networks are particularly large.  

In summary, the work capably integrates Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for hierarchical network clustering, enabling effective community discovery. However, the lack of comparisons to established hierarchical network models constitutes a critical deficiency.
