This paper presents a novel agglomerative approach for inferring stochastic block models (SBMs). The key innovation is the use of Bayesian Rose Trees to extend the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM), replacing the standard Chinese Restaurant Process formulation with a more direct and factorizable posterior probability (as shown in Equation 7). This factorization, which is analogous to techniques used in learning Bayesian network structures, enables an efficient greedy search algorithm for model discovery. The paper provides clear theoretical derivations and presents the corresponding algorithms for this new method.

**Strengths**

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear prose, correct citations, and strong grammatical structure, placing it well above average in terms of presentation. The theoretical development is presented succinctly and is easy to follow. The authors' formulation, which factors the posterior over subtrees, is elegant and builds effectively on established work in learning graphical models (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004). This factorization neatly facilitates greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, and link prediction. The core contribution—the agglomerative algorithm—is a simple yet powerful concept, and it is summarized effectively in Table 1.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

While the theoretical contribution is clear, the experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous and has several significant weaknesses that undermine the paper's claims.

1.  **Unclear Evaluation Metric:** The choice of Area Under the Curve (AUC) as the primary evaluation metric for an SBM is non-standard and requires detailed explanation. Typically, SBMs are evaluated against gold-standard partitions using clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI). The current draft leaves the reader to guess how AUC was computed—presumably via a link prediction task where a subset of links is held out and the model's ability to predict them is measured. This procedure must be explicitly described, and its relevance as a primary metric for community structure discovery must be justified.

2.  **Insufficient Scale of Experiments:** The scale of the datasets used for evaluation is a concern. While the NIPS co-authorship dataset is a classic benchmark, recent literature on SBMs and related models routinely employs much larger graphs to demonstrate scalability and performance. The results in Figure 4 are informative, but demonstrating the proposed method's performance on larger, more challenging datasets is essential to establish its practical utility in the context of modern network analysis.

3.  **Narrow Experimental Comparison:** The experimental comparison is too narrow. The authors compare their method primarily against other Bayesian non-parametric approaches. However, the field includes numerous effective semi-parametric and parametric methods for community detection, some of which are already cited in the paper. A convincing evaluation would require benchmarking against a broader and more representative set of state-of-the-art algorithms to properly contextualize the performance of the proposed method. As it stands, the evaluation feels more illustrative of the algorithm's mechanics than comprehensively comparative, which is not adequate for a top-tier publication.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions**

*   While the paper is well-written, I would suggest avoiding starting sentences with a parenthetical citation (e.g., "[1] shows..."), as this is generally discouraged in the NIPS style. It is stylistically better to write "Author et al. [1] show...".
*   The use of `f()` and `g()` to represent components of the model is succinct, but a brief reminder of what they represent in the text near their first use could further aid readability.
