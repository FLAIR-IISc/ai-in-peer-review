

The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Integrated Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, leveraging key concepts from the Bayesian rose tree framework: (1) using the hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent node partitions, and (2) learning this hierarchy via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. They demonstrate qualitative results on Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, alongside quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference is computationally cheaper than IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.  

**Quality/Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and accessible exposition of the core ideas.  

**Originality.** While the foundational concepts draw from the Bayesian rose tree work, significant effort was required to adapt these ideas to relational data, particularly in preserving computational efficiency.  

**Significance.** Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models to network structures is crucial for practical adoption. Although the basic algorithm’s $O(n^2)$ scaling may limit applicability to very large networks, the sparse variant should remain feasible. The authors should provide timing data for larger real-world networks: NIPS-234 takes tens of seconds—how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Real-world gene networks typically involve tens of thousands of nodes, and social networks are orders of magnitude larger. How does inference scale in these regimes? Additionally, it would be valuable to assess how close the greedy method comes to the optimal tree. Could the authors investigate this at the largest scale where the optimal tree is computationally tractable via exhaustive search? Finally, could they provide any theoretical justification for the method, such as submodularity guarantees?
