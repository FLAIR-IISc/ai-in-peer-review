This paper introduces a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model. The goal is to infer a hierarchical, rather than flat, clustering of entities based on their observed network links. The authors propose a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering, which constructs a tree to constrain the space of possible partitions. Empirical results on two social network corpora suggest that the proposed method is significantly faster and achieves comparable or superior accuracy to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used baseline.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Relevance:** The paper addresses an important problem that will be of interest to a broad segment of the NIPS community. On the methods side, it tackles the practical scalability issues that often hinder Bayesian nonparametric models. On the application side, it provides social scientists and network analysts with a hierarchical extension to a popular model for community discovery.
*   **Novel Technical Contribution:** The key insight—restricting the set of allowable partitions to those consistent with a tree structure to enable efficient computation of the marginal likelihood—is both clever and effective. This presents a compelling alternative to methods like the IRM, which must contend with an exponentially large search space.
*   **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experimental results are strong. While the improvements in accuracy, AUC, and predictive log-likelihood over the IRM are modest, the claimed runtime improvements are orders of magnitude faster. This trade-off makes the proposed method a highly practical and attractive option.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The primary weaknesses of this paper lie in its clarity and presentation. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the submission.

1.  **Clarity of the Learning Algorithm (Section 4):** The connection between the generative Bayesian model and the greedy, agglomerative learning algorithm is unclear. As the learning procedure is greedy, it is not obvious how it relates to principled posterior inference (e.g., MCMC) or point estimation (e.g., MAP/ML). Does the algorithm approximate posterior inference over tree structures, or is it better understood as a heuristic search? Clarifying this relationship is crucial and would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding. Section 4, which details this core contribution, was difficult to follow and would benefit from substantial revision.

2.  **Overall Writing Quality and Precision:**
    *   The manuscript contains several distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126).
    *   The prose can be imprecise. For example, the introduction (lines 47-50) uses vague terms like "local changes" and "iterations" without sufficient context. It is unclear if "iterations" refers to steps in an optimization algorithm or an MCMC sampler.
    *   The datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) are not adequately described. Readers unfamiliar with these specific corpora may struggle to understand the context of the experiments. A brief description of the entities and link types would be beneficial.
    *   The presentation of some mathematical concepts could be simplified. For instance, Equation 5 seemed unnecessarily complex and could potentially be explained more intuitively in the text.

3.  **Clarity of Visualizations:** Figure 3, which displays results on the Monastery dataset, is difficult to interpret. For a reader unfamiliar with this dataset's ground truth, it is not visually apparent which method is performing better. Using a more widely known dataset or improving the figure's annotations could enhance its impact.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach for hierarchical clustering in networks. The core idea is insightful, and the experimental results, particularly the computational speedup, are compelling. The authors effectively propose a model that can be viewed as a Bayesian-inspired version of agglomerative clustering. However, the paper's quality is currently diminished by significant issues with clarity, especially concerning the theoretical justification for the learning algorithm and its connection to the Bayesian model. The writing also requires substantial revision to correct errors and improve overall readability.

If the authors can thoroughly address these concerns—particularly by rewriting Section 4 to clarify the learning procedure and by polishing the manuscript to improve its presentation—I believe the paper would represent a strong contribution worthy of publication.
