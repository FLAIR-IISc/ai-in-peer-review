

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors extend the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by introducing a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, enabling efficient Bayesian inference of hierarchical community structure. The work builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches while addressing computational limitations of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, particularly the IRM and its hierarchical extensions. Unlike previous methods that require iterative inference procedures or make restrictive assumptions about hierarchy depth, BHCD employs a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that processes the network in a single pass.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The theoretical development is sound, with clear mathematical formulation of the model (equations 2-8), and the authors provide a thorough explanation of how the tree-structured prior enables efficient inference. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-designed, with a careful analysis of time complexity (O(n² log n)) and a novel caching scheme that allows O(1) computation per inner loop. The experimental results are compelling, showing BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of variations (BHCD sparse, binary restrictions) and their trade-offs.

The paper excels in clarity. The mathematical notation is well-defined and consistently applied throughout, with helpful examples and figures (Figures 1-5) that effectively illustrate the model and results. The writing is precise, and the organization follows a logical progression from problem statement through theory to experiments. The authors successfully relate their work to prior NIPS contributions (e.g., [12], [13], [14]) and clearly distinguish their approach from related work.

Regarding originality, the paper presents a novel combination of tree-structured priors with stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community detection. While the core idea of greedy hierarchical clustering has been explored before [3,4], the Bayesian formulation with efficient inference represents a significant technical contribution. The authors effectively position their work against recent NIPS papers on hierarchical community detection [8,9,10,11], demonstrating how BHCD addresses key limitations of previous approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial. Efficient hierarchical community discovery has broad applications in social network analysis, and the authors demonstrate BHCD's practical utility through experiments on real-world networks (Sampson's monastery, NIPS co-authorship). The speed advantage over existing Bayesian methods makes the approach particularly valuable for large-scale network analysis, where computational efficiency is often a bottleneck.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. The experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. While the authors compare BHCD to IRM on the NIPS-234 dataset, they do not evaluate against non-Bayesian methods that have been shown to perform well on hierarchical community detection (e.g., [3,4,17]). The paper mentions "gold standard" data as a common evaluation method for SBM, but the explanation of how AUC is computed (Section 5) is unclear. The authors should clarify whether they deleted edges and then predicted their presence, and how this differs from standard clustering metrics like NMI. The experiments on Sampson's monastery (Figure 3) are qualitative and lack quantitative comparison to the "gold standard" four-group structure identified in [17]. 

The authors claim BHCD "achieves comparable or better accuracy" than IRM, but the NIPS-234 results (Figure 4) show only marginal improvement in predictive performance. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods on larger datasets, as the authors themselves note that "recent evaluations of SBM run on bigger data sets, bigger than your full NIPS." The limited experimental scope (only two datasets, with the second being relatively small at 234 nodes) weakens the significance claim.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of limitations. While the authors note that BHCD "does not seem to be a reasonable behaviour as communities that are completely disconnected should not be merged," they don't explore how this might affect real-world applications where disconnected communities could exist. The tie-breaking strategy (random selection in case of equal scores) is mentioned but not evaluated for robustness.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery
- Efficient inference algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees
- Clear presentation and well-executed experiments demonstrating speed advantages
- Good relation to previous work and clear differentiation from existing methods

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient experimental evaluation (limited to two datasets, lacks comparison to non-Bayesian methods)
- Unclear explanation of evaluation metrics (AUC)
- Claims of "comparable or better accuracy" are not sufficiently substantiated
- Limited discussion of limitations and failure cases

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to the field of network analysis. While the algorithmic contribution is solid, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive experimental evaluation and clearer explanation of evaluation methods. The theoretical development is strong, but the empirical validation falls short of the significance claimed. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address these experimental limitations in the camera-ready version.
