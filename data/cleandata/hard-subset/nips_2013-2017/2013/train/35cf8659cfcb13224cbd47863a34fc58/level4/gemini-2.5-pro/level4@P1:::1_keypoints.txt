- Introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric method for hierarchical community detection.  
- Model: tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels where the tree defines a prior over consistent partitions.  
- Inference: greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm adapted from Bayesian Rose Trees to learn the hierarchy in a single pass.  
- Experiments: qualitative results on Sampson's monastery and full NIPS co-authorship, quantitative link prediction on a NIPS subset.  
- Main claim: BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than IRM while achieving comparable or better predictive accuracy.  
- Quality/clarity: technically sound, well-written, clearly organized with readable derivations and thorough evaluation.  
- Originality: builds on Bayesian Rose Trees but provides a novel, non-trivial extension to relational data and sufficient-statistic handling.  
- Complexity: retains O(n^2 log n) complexity of the original algorithm, with a sparser practical variant.  
- Significance: addresses scalability for Bayesian nonparametric network models and makes hierarchical modeling more practical for practitioners.  
- Empirical significance: impressive runtime and predictive performance on NIPS-234 (seconds vs thousands for IRM).  
- Weakness — scalability: O(n^2 log n) may be prohibitive for very large networks; need runtimes on full NIPS (2,864 nodes) and discussion of scaling to tens of thousands of nodes.  
- Weakness — optimality: greedy agglomerative procedure lacks analysis of closeness to posterior-optimal trees; recommend exhaustive small-network comparisons or theoretical guarantees (e.g., submodularity links).  
- Minor corrections: fix typos ("Which pair of tree to merge" → "trees"; "a fruitful next steps" → "a fruitful next step(s)").  
- Conclusion: strong, novel, and effective paper that merits acceptance; scalability and optimality analyses suggested as future work.
