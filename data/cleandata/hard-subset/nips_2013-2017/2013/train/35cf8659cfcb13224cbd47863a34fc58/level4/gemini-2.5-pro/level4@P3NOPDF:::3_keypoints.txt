- Contribution: Proposes a novel agglomerative method for inferring SBMs by using Bayesian Rose Trees to extend the IRM and replace the CRP with a factorizable posterior (Eq. 7).  
- Theoretical novelty: Posterior factorization (analogous to Koivisto & Sood, 2004) enables efficient greedy search and tractable marginal likelihood calculations.  
- Algorithmic contribution: Presents a simple, powerful agglomerative greedy search algorithm summarized in Table 1.  
- Presentation: Exceptionally well-written with clear prose, correct citations, and succinct theoretical derivations.  
- Practical benefits: Factorization facilitates greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, and link prediction.  
- Major concern — evaluation metric: Using AUC as the primary metric is non-standard for SBMs; the paper fails to describe how AUC was computed or justify its relevance versus clustering metrics like NMI.  
- Major concern — dataset scale: Experiments use relatively small/limited datasets (e.g., NIPS co-authorship); larger, more challenging graphs are needed to demonstrate scalability and practical utility.  
- Major concern — experimental comparisons: Baselines are too narrow (mainly Bayesian nonparametrics); must compare against a broader set of state-of-the-art parametric and semi-parametric community detection methods.  
- Minor style: Avoid starting sentences with parenthetical citations; prefer "Author et al. [1] show..."  
- Minor clarity: Remind readers what f() and g() denote near their first use to aid readability.
