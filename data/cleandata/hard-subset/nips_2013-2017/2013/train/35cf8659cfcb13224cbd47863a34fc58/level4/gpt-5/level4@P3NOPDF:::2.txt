Summary
This paper generalizes the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a tree-structured hierarchy of entities from observed links rather than a flat clustering. The authors propose a greedy agglomerative procedure that searches over trees representing valid partitions and exploits the tree constraint to compute marginal likelihoods efficiently. Experiments on two social connectivity corpora indicate that the method is competitive with, and often slightly better than, the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) on AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, while being orders of magnitude faster.

Strengths
- Relevance and impact: The problem is important and of clear interest to the NIPS community. Hierarchical structure is natural in social and relational data, and a scalable Bayesian approach is valuable.
- Technical insight: Restricting partitions to those induced by a tree enables efficient computation of the marginal likelihood, avoiding the combinatorial explosion of the IRM’s partition space.
- Empirical results: The method matches or improves upon IRM performance across several metrics while dramatically reducing runtime, addressing a common criticism of Bayesian nonparametrics (i.e., practical slowness).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Inference vs. generative story: It is unclear how the greedy tree-learning procedure relates to the Bayesian model’s generative assumptions. As written, the algorithm does not appear to perform posterior inference over trees, nor is it clearly a point MAP/ML estimator. The paper should make explicit what objective is optimized at each step, whether any approximation to the marginal likelihood is used, and how this procedure is justified within the model.
- Clarity of Section 4: The core contribution—the agglomerative algorithm and its scoring—was difficult to follow and required multiple readings. Key definitions and steps should be presented more systematically.
- Presentation and writing quality: There are numerous spelling and grammar errors (e.g., at least one around line 126). Some phrasing is vague, especially in the introduction (lines 47–50): “local changes” and “iterations” need concrete definitions (are these MCMC iterations? greedy merge steps?).
- Dataset descriptions: The paper assumes familiarity with the monastery and NIPS authorship datasets. Readers unfamiliar with these corpora will struggle to interpret the results without concise descriptions of their size, link semantics, sparsity, and evaluation splits.
- Mathematical exposition: Some derivations (e.g., Eq. 5) feel heavier than necessary relative to the intuition they convey. Without clear guidance on why the expression matters for the algorithm, it obscures rather than clarifies.
- Figures: Figure 3 is hard to interpret. It is not visually obvious which method performs best, and the legend and captions do not sufficiently guide the reader.

Requests for clarification
- Objective and guarantees: What exactly is the objective optimized by the agglomerative procedure? Is it the exact marginal likelihood under the hierarchical model, a bound, or a heuristic score? Does the greedy search define a consistent estimator under the model, or is it purely algorithmic?
- Search details: What are the initialization, merge criteria, and stopping conditions? Are there tie-breaking rules or backtracking mechanisms to mitigate local optima?
- Complexity: Provide a precise complexity analysis per merge and per dataset, and report wall-clock times under matched hardware. Clarify which IRM implementation and hyperparameters were used to ensure a fair runtime comparison.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to prior settings in the hierarchical model? Include a brief sensitivity analysis if possible.
- Evaluation protocol: Clarify train/test splits, link prediction setup, and whether results are averaged over multiple random splits.

Suggestions for improvement
- Rewrite Section 4 with a clear, step-by-step description of the algorithm, including:
  - A succinct pseudocode block (or enumerated steps).
  - A precise definition of the scoring function for merges and how it arises from the model.
  - An intuitive explanation of why restricting to tree-consistent partitions enables efficient marginal likelihood computation.
- Streamline mathematical presentation by moving lengthy derivations (e.g., Eq. 5) to an appendix and adding high-level intuition in the main text.
- Clarify terminology early (e.g., “local changes,” “iterations”) and consistently refer back to these terms when discussing algorithms and experiments.
- Improve dataset descriptions with a compact summary of characteristics (nodes, edges, directed/undirected, density, and the prediction task). If space allows, include a small, widely-known dataset to aid reader intuition.
- Redesign Figure 3 to be self-contained: larger fonts, clearer legends, and visual cues (e.g., bolded best scores, consistent axes). Consider adding numeric summaries in the caption.
- Thorough proofreading to remove typos and grammatical errors.

Evaluation
Overall, this paper presents a novel and practical hierarchical extension of the stochastic block modeling framework, with a simple and fast agglomerative learning procedure and strong empirical performance relative to a prominent baseline. While the performance gains are modest in absolute terms, the substantial runtime improvements make the contribution significant for scalable Bayesian relational modeling. The primary barrier to acceptance is clarity: the relationship between the algorithm and the Bayesian model needs to be explicit, and the exposition—especially in Section 4—should be tightened. If these issues are addressed, I believe the paper is suitable for publication.
