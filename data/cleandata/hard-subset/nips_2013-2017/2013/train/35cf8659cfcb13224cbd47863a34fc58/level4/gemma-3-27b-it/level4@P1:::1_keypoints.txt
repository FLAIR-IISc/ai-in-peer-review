- Proposes a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model using Bayesian rose tree ideas: hierarchy as a mixture over consistent partitions and greedy agglomerative learning.  
- Shows qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery and full NIPS co-authorship, quantitative results on NIPS-234; inference is much cheaper than IRM with comparable predictive performance.  
- Paper is very well written with clear exposition, good notation, and sufficient implementation detail.  
- Core ideas are adapted from Bayesian rose tree work, with substantial effort required to adapt them to relational data while preserving computational efficiency.  
- The combination of Bayesian nonparametrics with a scalable greedy inference procedure is a noteworthy contribution.  
- Scalability concerns: O(n^2) baseline may be prohibitive for very large networks; sparse version may help but larger-network timings (full NIPS, gene regulatory, social networks) are missing.  
- Suggest empirically assessing how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree (e.g., exhaustive search at tractable scales) and seeking theoretical guarantees (submodularity/approximation bounds).  
- Minor corrections: fix phrasing ("whilst... but" → "while, but"), "pair of treeS" → "pair of trees", "A fruitful next steps" → "A fruitful next step", and consistent capitalization of "rose trees".  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated hierarchical community detection; computationally efficient compared to existing Bayesian nonparametrics; effective experiments; clear writing and organization.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability to massive networks not fully demonstrated; lacking theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm; limited comparison to other scalable community detection algorithms.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — contribution is significant despite overlap with prior rose tree ideas.
