Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model views a hierarchy as a tree-structured mixture over stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). Instead of summing over all partitions as in the IRM with a CRP prior, BHCD restricts to tree-consistent partitions and defines a prior via node-specific stopping probabilities πS (Equation 7), following the spirit of Bayesian Rose Trees. This yields an exact dynamic-programming recursion for the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) in terms of on-diagonal and off-diagonal marginal likelihoods, f and g, with conjugate Beta–Bernoulli instantiation for efficiency.

Learning proceeds via a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that merges trees using a local Bayes factor (Equation 12), considering three merge types (join and two absorptions). Caching sufficient statistics enables O(1) inner-loop updates, giving O(n^2 log n) worst-case time independent of the number of communities. A “sparse” variant forbids merging disconnected components. The method also provides fast link prediction via a top-down traversal mixing f and g with responsibilities rS (Equation 14). Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network, a 234-author NIPS co-authorship subset, and the full NIPS 1–17 network show qualitatively interpretable hierarchies and link prediction performance competitive with, but much faster than, the IRM. The approach sits between flat community models (SBM, MMSB) and more complex hierarchical Bayesian models (IRM with trees, Mondrian process, ILA/LFRM), offering a simpler, scalable alternative. The factorization over subtrees resembles structure learning in Bayesian networks (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, §3), where constraints induce clean marginal decompositions that support greedy search and efficient handling of sufficient statistics.

Strengths
- Technical simplicity and efficiency: clear derivation of the tree-conditioned marginal likelihood and a well-engineered agglomerative search with O(1) updates and O(n^2 log n) runtime. The worst-case complexity is independent of the number of communities.
- Practical scalability: two orders of magnitude faster than IRM in the reported settings, with an additional sparse variant that exploits network sparsity.
- Coherent probabilistic formulation: exact marginalization for a fixed tree; principled link prediction via Equation 14; interpretable hierarchies on real data.
- Careful algorithmic design: three merge operators (join/absorb), tie-handling via restarts, and caching of σ-statistics.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation clarity: Link prediction protocol needs to be precisely specified. Please detail how train/test edges and non-edges are split, the handling of class imbalance, whether negative examples are subsampled, the number of held-out folds, and how AUC/accuracy are computed. My assumption is that a subset of observed and absent links is held out and predicted, but this must be stated explicitly.
- Limited benchmarks: Results focus on IRM and a subset of NIPS-234. Recent SBM evaluations commonly include “gold standard” partitions (synthetic SBMs or real networks with known communities), reporting NMI/ARI against ground truth. Including such benchmarks would strengthen the case. It would also help to compare against widely used scalable semi-/parametric community methods (e.g., Louvain/Infomap or modularity-based baselines) and to test on larger graphs than the full NIPS dataset, as many recent works report results at larger scales.
- Sensitivity and robustness: The method depends on hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) and on tie-breaking with R restarts. Please report sensitivity analyses and the effect of R. Clarify differences between “BHCD,” “BHCD sparse,” and their binary-only variants, beyond runtime; discuss trade-offs in predictive accuracy and interpretability.
- Scope and generality: While the text notes that directed/weighted cases are possible, experiments and derivations focus on undirected unweighted graphs with conjugate priors. A brief discussion on extending f/g beyond Beta–Bernoulli and the computational implications (including non-conjugate likelihoods as in [7]) would be useful.
- Presentation: The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized. Avoid starting sentences with citations per NIPS style. Minor typos (e.g., stray capitalization in “Our,” spacing in “wherem n2”) should be fixed. Table 1 should define K, F, and IR and clarify whether complexities include constants due to caching and heap maintenance.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The model and inference are technically sound, with correct factorization, dynamic programming, and a coherent Bayes factor for local merges. Claims on runtime and empirical speedups are plausible, but empirical validation remains limited in breadth. Clarifying the evaluation protocol is essential.
- Clarity: Generally well written. The use of f and g streamlines exposition. Please expand the evaluation methodology and better distinguish model variants in figures and captions.
- Originality: Combining Rose Tree–style hierarchical priors with SBMs and an efficient agglomerative search is a neat, practical contribution. It is a straightforward idea but fills a useful niche between flat IRM and more complex hierarchical models.
- Significance: The approach promises practical scalability for hierarchical community discovery and fast link prediction. Significance would be stronger with broader, standardized benchmarks, comparisons beyond IRM, and robustness analyses.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, principled, and fast hierarchical community model with exact marginalization per tree.
- Strong runtime advantages and competitive predictive performance.
- Clear algorithmic contribution (agglomerative search with cached sufficient statistics).

Cons:
- Evaluation is largely illustrative; lacks standard clustering metrics on datasets with known partitions and broader baselines.
- Link prediction methodology is under-specified.
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and restart variance.

Overall recommendation
A well-executed, efficient method with clear potential. I encourage acceptance if the authors substantially strengthen and clarify the empirical evaluation (protocol details, broader benchmarks, and sensitivity analyses). Otherwise, the paper remains a solid methodological contribution but with an empirical section that feels incomplete.
