This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structures in networks. The core contribution is the novel application of the framework from Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data. The model defines a tree-structured mixture over a potentially exponential number of stochastic blockmodels, where the tree itself specifies a prior over consistent partitions. The authors develop a greedy agglomerative algorithm for model selection, which learns this hierarchical structure in a single bottom-up pass. This approach is presented as a computationally efficient alternative to MCMC or variational methods commonly used for models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), promising significant speed improvements with comparable predictive accuracy. The method is evaluated qualitatively on the Sampson's monastery network and the full NIPS co-authorship dataset, and quantitatively on a subset of the NIPS data, demonstrating its efficiency and effectiveness.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is technically sound and clearly written. The exposition of the model, from the stochastic blockmodel likelihood to the hierarchical mixture defined by the tree, is logical and easy to follow. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Equation 10) and the description of the agglomerative model selection procedure (Algorithm 1) provide sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics. The claims of computational efficiency and predictive performance are well-supported by the experiments comparing BHCD to the IRM. The authors are also transparent about the greedy nature of their inference algorithm.

### Originality

The primary conceptual framework—using a tree to define a mixture over consistent partitions and learning it greedily—is adapted directly from the Bayesian Rose Trees work [14]. However, the contribution here is the non-trivial adaptation of this framework to the domain of relational data and community detection. The authors have successfully designed the likelihood and caching scheme required for relational data to maintain an efficient O(n² log n) complexity, which represents a solid engineering and modeling contribution for network analysis. The paper does a good job of situating its work with respect to the IRM and other hierarchical community detection methods.

### Significance

The main significance of this work lies in addressing the crucial challenge of scalability for Bayesian nonparametric models in network analysis. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating that BHCD can achieve comparable predictive performance to the IRM in a fraction of the time (two orders of magnitude faster on the NIPS-234 dataset). This speed-up makes hierarchical Bayesian community detection significantly more practical for researchers and practitioners working with moderately sized networks. The method provides a valuable point in the trade-off space between model complexity, inference accuracy, and computational cost.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Scalability Demonstration:** The O(n² log n) scaling of the primary algorithm, while a major improvement over iterative methods, may still be a bottleneck for truly large-scale networks (e.g., those with tens of thousands of nodes or more). The paper introduces a "sparse" variant that is empirically faster. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include timing results for this sparse algorithm on the full NIPS dataset or other larger, sparse real-world networks. This would provide a clearer picture of the model's practical limits and better substantiate the claims of scalability. For instance, how long does inference take for the full NIPS network (n=2864)?

2.  **Optimality of Greedy Search:** The greedy nature of the inference algorithm is pragmatic, but it provides no guarantee of finding an optimal or near-optimal hierarchy. It would be valuable for the authors to provide some insight into the quality of the greedy solutions. For example, on a small network where an exhaustive search is feasible, how does the likelihood of the greedily-found tree compare to the optimal one? This would help quantify how much is lost for the sake of speed.

3.  **Theoretical Justification:** A brief discussion on whether the objective function exhibits any properties that might support the greedy approach (e.g., submodularity) would add theoretical depth. Even if such properties do not hold, acknowledging this and its implications would be useful.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   Addresses the important and practical problem of scalable inference for Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
*   Demonstrates a significant (two orders of magnitude) speed-up over a standard baseline (IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy.
*   Presents a clear and well-executed adaptation of an existing elegant framework (Bayesian Rose Trees) to the important domain of network analysis.
*   The paper is well-written, well-organized, and the experimental results are clearly presented.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The core modeling idea is a direct application of a previous method to a new data type, making the conceptual novelty somewhat limited.
*   The claims about scalability to larger networks are not fully substantiated with experiments, as the O(n² log n) complexity remains a limitation for very large graphs.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong contribution. While the foundational ideas are derived from prior work on Bayesian Rose Trees, their application to network modeling is a valuable and significant step forward. The impressive empirical speed-up over the IRM makes this a noteworthy paper for practitioners interested in network analysis. The weaknesses identified are primarily suggestions for future exploration rather than fatal flaws. I recommend acceptance.
