## Review of NIPS_2013-2017/2013: Hierarchical Relational Modelling

This paper presents a hierarchical extension to the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modelling, drawing inspiration from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) literature. The authors leverage two key ideas from the BRT approach: first, utilizing a hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions, and second, employing an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure to learn this hierarchy. The method is evaluated on the Sampson’s monks dataset, the full NIPS dataset, and quantitatively on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference procedure demonstrably reduces computational cost compared to standard IRM, while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and presents the core concepts with commendable clarity. The exposition is logical and facilitates understanding of the proposed hierarchical extension.

**Originality:** While the foundational ideas originate from the Bayesian rose tree work, the authors have undertaken substantial effort to adapt these concepts to the relational data setting. Maintaining computational efficiency during this adaptation is a noteworthy accomplishment. The challenge of applying Bayesian nonparametric methods like BRT to large-scale network data is significant, and this work represents a valuable step towards addressing it.

**Significance:** Scaling Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) models, and specifically extending them to network models, is a crucial challenge for practical adoption. Although the quadratic scaling of the basic algorithm might become prohibitive for very large networks, the proposed sparse version holds promise for continued feasibility. The paper would benefit from further investigation into scaling behaviour. While timings are provided for the NIPS-234 dataset (tens of seconds), reporting inference time on the full NIPS dataset would be informative. Furthermore, the relevance of this work extends to real-world networks which often encompass tens of thousands of nodes (e.g., gene networks) or even millions (e.g., social networks). Demonstrating or discussing how inference would scale to these sizes is important.

A further point of interest concerns the quality of the greedy hierarchical construction. Investigating how closely the greedy method approximates the optimal tree structure would strengthen the work. This could be explored by comparing the greedy solution against exhaustive search results on the largest network scale for which exhaustive search remains computationally tractable. Ideally, the method could also benefit from theoretical justification, potentially framed in terms of submodularity or other relevant properties, that could provide guarantees on its performance.

**Overall:**

Despite the familiarity of the underlying ideas from the BRT literature, the contribution presented in this paper is significant. The adaptation of these concepts to relational data, coupled with the focus on computational efficiency, constitutes a valuable advance. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and demonstrates promising results. I recommend acceptance.
