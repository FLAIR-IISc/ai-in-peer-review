The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling. The approach adapts key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree literature, using a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent node partitions and learning this hierarchy via an efficient, greedy agglomerative procedure. The paper includes qualitative results on the Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, alongside quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference method is computationally much cheaper than the standard IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

**Quality and Clarity**
The paper is well-written, and its key ideas are explained clearly.

**Originality**
While the main ideas are drawn from existing work on Bayesian rose trees, their adaptation to the relational data setting represents a non-trivial contribution, particularly the work done to maintain computational efficiency.

**Significance**
The problem of scaling Bayesian non-parametric models, especially for network data, is an important challenge for their practical application. While the O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for large networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. The evaluation would be strengthened by demonstrating this scalability. For instance, while the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process, what is the runtime for the full NIPS dataset? More importantly, how would the inference scale to real-world networks, such as gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes or even larger social networks?

Additionally, an analysis of the greedy method's approximation quality would be valuable. It would be insightful to understand how close the learned tree is to the optimal tree, which could be investigated on datasets small enough to permit an exhaustive search. Is it possible to provide any theoretical support for the greedy approach, for example, by leveraging concepts like submodularity?

**Recommendation**
Although the core concepts are adapted from the Bayesian rose tree literature, the contribution is significant. I recommend that this paper be accepted.
