- Paper proposes an agglomerative hierarchical clustering for networks by adapting Bayesian rose trees with a modified network likelihood and efficient caching of sufficient statistics.  
- Presentation is generally clear, but Section 3 is dense and Equation (9) notation could be simplified for accessibility.  
- Experiments show hierarchical modeling improves over flat models (e.g., IRM) on small networks like Sampson and Karate.  
- Critically missing comparisons to established hierarchical Bayesian models (Ho et al. 2012 multiscale community blockmodel and Roy et al. 2009 Mondrian process), which undermines claims of superiority.  
- Core technical novelty is adapting sufficient-statistic updates for recursive agglomerative clustering; O(N+E) likelihood computation is a practical advantage.  
- The derivation and motivation for the recursive/dynamic updates (around Equation (10)) are insufficiently explained and lack intuitive justification.  
- Use of the term "nonparametric" is misleading—rose trees are a model-selection approach, not an infinite-component nonparametric model.  
- Scalability claims are under-supported because experiments use only small networks and no large-scale (e.g., 10k+ nodes) benchmarks.  
- Sampson analysis is a reasonable qualitative example and the IRM comparison highlights benefits of hypothesis averaging.  
- Overall, the approach is plausible and technically sound but incremental; omission of key hierarchical baselines and terminology errors fundamentally weaken the contribution.  
- Recommendation: reject pending substantial revisions to include comparisons with Ho 2012 and Roy 2009, clearer derivations, corrected terminology, and larger-scale experiments.
