Proposes an agglomerative method for scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and the overall algorithmic framework closely follow Bayesian rose trees [12]. The main change is a straightforward likelihood for network data; with some additional work, sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently. The paper is generally clear, and experiments outperform a flat community baseline. However, comparisons to alternative hierarchical network models are missing.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. Section 3 is quite dense; the exposition around equation (9) and its subsequent use could benefit from simpler notation and clearer explanation. Because the empirical comparisons focus on models without hierarchical latent structure, the experiments largely demonstrate the value of modeling hierarchy versus not doing so, rather than establishing superiority over other hierarchical approaches. In particular, at least two relevant hierarchical Bayesian nonparametric models are not discussed: the Multiscale Community Blockmodel (Ho et al., 2012), which uses a nested CRP, and the Mondrian Process (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a stochastic process inducing hierarchical structures over k-d trees. Both could discover more nuanced hierarchical structure and should be included in the related work and, ideally, in empirical comparisons.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The proposed model is essentially a Bayesian rose tree equipped with a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The primary novelty lies in how the likelihood enables recursive updates of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative procedure. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges), which is a strong practical advantage. That said, the description of the dynamic programming ideas (alluded to before equation (10)) is too terse to be easily reproducible. Similarly, while equations for recursively updating sufficient statistics are provided, the presentation offers limited intuition or derivations to justify their correctness. More detail, pseudo-code, or a worked example would improve clarity and reproducibility.

TERMINOLOGY:
Labeling the approach as “nonparametric” is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] frame rose trees as a model selection method and explicitly contrast them with nonparametric approaches. I agree with that interpretation, and the paper should either avoid the term “nonparametric” or carefully justify its use.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative analysis on the Sampson network is informative, and the comparison to the IRM highlights the benefits of averaging over many hypotheses for prediction. The main shortcoming is the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models, which would better situate the contribution and test whether the proposed method’s hierarchical structure is competitive. While the paper discusses scalability, the datasets used are not especially large, leaving open questions about performance on truly massive graphs. Reporting wall-clock times, memory usage, and scaling behavior across larger and more diverse networks would strengthen the empirical case.

Overall assessment:
The paper effectively integrates Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for networks and extends efficient agglomerative clustering to hierarchical community discovery. The approach appears sound and yields promising results. However, the contribution would be substantially strengthened by clearer algorithmic exposition (especially for dynamic programming and sufficient-statistic updates), a more careful use of terminology, and empirical comparisons against existing hierarchical network models such as the Multiscale Community Blockmodel and the Mondrian Process.
