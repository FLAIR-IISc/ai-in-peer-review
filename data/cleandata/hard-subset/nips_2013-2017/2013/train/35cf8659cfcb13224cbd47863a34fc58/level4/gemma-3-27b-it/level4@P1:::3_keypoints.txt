- Paper is well-written with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations; minor style: avoid starting sentences with citations.  
- Theory is presented succinctly, with consistent use of f() and g() notation.  
- Extends single-layer IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees to replace CRP with a simpler formulation (eq. 7).  
- Factorization over subtrees parallels work like Koivisto & Sood, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihoods, and link prediction.  
- Main algorithmic contribution is an agglomerative approach to hierarchical community discovery (summarized in Table 1).  
- Algorithm offers substantial speed improvements over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (supported by Figure 4 and Table 1).  
- Theoretical foundation is sound and builds on established Bayesian network and SBM concepts.  
- Experimental evaluation is illustrative rather than comprehensive and thus not fully convincing.  
- Use of AUC for link prediction needs clearer explanation, especially relative to common SBM metrics like NMI.  
- Evaluation datasets are small (full NIPS dataset) compared to recent larger benchmarks; results on larger datasets are needed.  
- Insufficient comparisons to a broader set of relevant methods, notably semi-parametric approaches.  
- Contribution is incremental—builds on existing ideas rather than offering a transformative advance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, novel approach to a relevant problem, speed gains, clear theory.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited evaluation scope, inadequate AUC explanation, small-scale experiments, and only incremental novelty.
