Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchically nested communities in networks. The model views a network as generated by a tree-structured mixture over stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). Each internal node in the tree corresponds to a community; leaves are vertices. A key modeling idea is to restrict attention to partitions consistent with a given tree and to define a prior over partitions via a stop/continue process at each internal node, with πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| favoring non-binary trees (rose trees). With conjugate Beta-Bernoulli likelihoods (with different hyperparameters for within- and between-community edges), the marginal likelihood for a fixed tree can be computed exactly by dynamic programming in O(n + m) time (Eq. 10). 

Learning proceeds via greedy agglomerative model selection: starting from singleton trees, each iteration merges a pair of subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12), considering join and two absorption variants. A caching scheme yields O(1) updates per inner loop, giving overall O(n^2 log n) time per run and making the cost independent of the number of discovered communities. Sparse and binary-only variants are described, as well as restarts to mitigate tie-breaking variability. The paper also provides a recursive predictive distribution for link prediction (Eq. 14).

Relative to prior work, BHCD connects Bayesian rose trees [Blundell et al., UAI 2010; Heller and Ghahramani, ICML 2005] with blockmodels/IRM-like likelihoods [Kemp et al., 2006] to produce an efficient hierarchical community model. It contrasts with IRM extensions to hierarchies [e.g., Herlau et al., 2013] and other nonparametric latent feature/attribute models [Miller et al., 2009; Palla et al., 2012], aiming for speed over expressive power. The factorization over tree-consistent partitions and subtree-local scoring mirrors ideas in structure learning for Bayesian networks (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004).

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized exposition; notation using f(·) and g(·) keeps derivations compact.
- Conceptually simple model that nonetheless captures hierarchical structure; exact marginalization for a fixed tree.
- Greedy agglomerative search with three merge types and careful caching is elegant and efficient; runtime independent of the number of communities.
- Practical predictive scheme with linear cost in tree depth.
- Empirical speed: on NIPS-234, BHCD variants achieve competitive AUC/accuracy orders of magnitude faster than IRM; qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery are sensible and interpretable.
- Discussion acknowledges limits and future directions (non-conjugate likelihoods, sparsity, triangle statistics).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation scope. Experiments focus primarily on speed vs IRM; comparisons to strong non-BNP or semi-parametric baselines (e.g., modularity maximization, spectral or degree-corrected SBMs) are missing. Including such baselines, even if slower or non-hierarchical, would better contextualize accuracy.
- Metrics and protocol clarity. Link prediction setup needs precise description: fraction of held-out edges/non-edges, sampling scheme for negatives, number of folds, whether node pairs are stratified, and whether hyperparameters and restarts are tuned on training folds. Please add this in Section 5. For datasets with accepted “gold” partitions (e.g., Sampson), also report NMI/ARI to complement AUC.
- Dataset scale. The full NIPS network is shown qualitatively, but no quantitative evaluation (predictive or recovery) is provided. Including predictive performance on the full graph or on a larger public network would strengthen the significance claim.
- Stability and uncertainty. The method relies on greedy merges and tie-breaking; restarts are used but a stability analysis is not provided. Reporting variability across restarts (e.g., likelihood spread, variation of information between trees) and a simple model-averaged predictor vs best-run predictor would increase confidence.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity. Fixed Beta priors and γ are used; no sensitivity or learned hyperparameters. Briefly assess robustness to α,β,δ,λ,γ or include empirical Bayes estimates.
- Fairness of baselines. Clarify IRM implementations (MCMC vs greedy), initialization, number of iterations, and whether their computational budgets are matched (e.g., wall-clock parity) to ensure a fair speed/accuracy comparison.
- Minor clarity issues. Avoid beginning sentences with citations (NIPS style). Define responsibilities rS before use. Ensure consistent notation for σ¬chSS and for symmetric networks. Consider adding a schematic to illustrate the three merge types (join vs absorption).

Quality
The model and inference are technically sound. The prior and likelihood factorization are well-justified, and the dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) is correct. The merge scoring (Eq. 12) is a reasonable local approximation enabling efficiency, though global optimality is not guaranteed. The experimental methodology is reasonable but underspecified in places (see above).

Clarity
The paper is generally very clear and well written. The compact notation is effective; figures help interpret results. A few editorial improvements as noted above would further polish the presentation.

Originality
The combination of rose-tree priors with SBMs for hierarchical community discovery and the specific agglomerative search with subtree-local Bayes factors and caching is, to my knowledge, novel and distinct from prior IRM-based hierarchical models. The contribution is primarily algorithmic/engineering rather than a fundamentally new probabilistic construct, but it is a useful and nontrivial synthesis.

Significance
The method’s speed and scalability make it attractive for exploratory analysis of large networks where hierarchical structure is expected. With a broader and clearer empirical evaluation, BHCD could be a practical addition to the toolkit. As it stands, the significance is moderate: strong on efficiency, but with limited evidence of state-of-the-art predictive or recovery performance across diverse datasets.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Elegant and efficient algorithm with clear derivations and practical impact.
- Exact marginalization for a fixed tree and well-motivated hierarchical prior.
- Compelling speedup over IRM with competitive predictive performance.
- Readable and reproducible description (modulo protocol details).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow and small-scale; limited baselines and metrics.
- Limited analysis of stability, sensitivity, and uncertainty.
- Claims about scalability would benefit from quantitative results on larger graphs and sparser settings.

Actionable recommendations
- Specify the link prediction protocol and add recovery metrics (e.g., NMI/ARI) where ground truth is available.
- Include at least one larger network and additional baselines (e.g., degree-corrected SBM, spectral clustering, modularity).
- Report variability across restarts and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Clarify IRM baseline settings and ensure comparable computational budgets.
- Minor edits for style and notation consistency.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and clearly presented paper with a useful and efficient approach to hierarchical community discovery. I view it as a solid contribution on the algorithmic side; strengthening and clarifying the empirical evaluation would significantly improve its impact.
