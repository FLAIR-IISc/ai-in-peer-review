Proposes an agglomerative approach to scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and the algorithmic structure are essentially unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is modified in a straightforward way to handle networks, and with some additional work the sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently. The presentation is very clear, and the experiments compare favorably to a baseline model with flat community structure, but comparisons to other hierarchical network models are missing.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. The presentation in Sec. 3 is particularly dense; I wonder if there is a simpler way to notate and explain (9) and the parts that build on it.
Because the comparisons are limited to models without hierarchical latent structure, the experiments mainly demonstrate that modeling hierarchy benefits relational datasets, but not whether this approach outperforms other existing hierarchical methods. For example, at least two papers model latent community structure hierarchically: the “Multiscale Community Blockmodel for Network Exploration” (Ho et al., 2012) uses a nested CRP, and the “Mondrian Process” (Roy et al., 2009) defines a stochastic process that yields hierarchical structures over K-d trees as a prior. The paper does not mention these two Bayesian nonparametric models, which could potentially discover a more refined latent structure.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The model here appears to be a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. Thus, the innovations lie in how this new likelihood changes the recursive updating of sufficient statistics in the greedy agglomerative clustering. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a nice feature. Unfortunately, the description of this computation is not very clear—there is an allusion to dynamic programming before (10), but the details are hard to follow. Similarly, for the recursive updates of sufficient statistics, the equations are given with limited explanation of why they are correct.

TERMINOLOGY:
It is questionable whether the term “nonparametric” is appropriate here—Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose-tree approach as model selection and contrast it with nonparametric methods in their discussion. I agree with their interpretation.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative experiment on Sampson is fine, and the comparison to the IRM nicely illustrates the benefits of averaging over many hypotheses when making predictions. Again, the biggest limitation is the lack of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. There is some discussion of scalability, but none of the networks tested are especially large.

Capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block-model likelihoods for networks, and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this setting. This enables effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but comparisons to other hierarchical network models are lacking.
