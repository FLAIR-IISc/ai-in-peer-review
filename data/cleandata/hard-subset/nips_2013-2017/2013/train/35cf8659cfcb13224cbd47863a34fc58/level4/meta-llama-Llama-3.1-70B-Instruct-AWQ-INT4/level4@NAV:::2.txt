The authors introduce a hierarchical extension of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, enabling the inference of a hierarchical clustering structure of entities given observed links between them. They develop a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to identify a tree representing valid entity partitions. The authors then present empirical results from two social connectivity datasets, demonstrating that their approach outperforms the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) in terms of both speed and accuracy.

This paper addresses a topic of interest to a significant portion of the NIPS audience. From a technical standpoint, Bayesian nonparametric methods, despite their conceptual appeal, often suffer from slow practical performance. The proposed hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be well-received by social scientists increasingly employing sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis.

A key contribution of this work is the efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of the data by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, in contrast to the IRM, which must consider an exponential number of possible partitions. However, the relationship between the learning procedure and the generative Bayesian model is unclear, particularly given the greedy nature of the algorithm. It is uncertain whether the approach constitutes true posterior inference or a point estimate. Clarification of Section 4 would be beneficial.

The empirical results are impressive, with the proposed method performing comparably to or slightly better than the IRM across several performance metrics (AUC, predictive llh, accuracy). Although the asymptotic improvements may not be substantial in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is significantly faster than IRM inference.

To enhance the overall clarity and writing quality, several issues should be addressed. The text contains distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126) and occasionally obscure prose (e.g., lines 47-50 in the introduction). The authors should provide clearer descriptions of the datasets used (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) and avoid unnecessary mathematical complexity (e.g., Eq. 5). Figure 3 is confusing, and using a more familiar dataset may be beneficial. Section 4 requires multiple readings to understand.

If these issues are resolved, the proposed approach has sufficient novelty and strong results to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel Bayesian model of graph edge structure inspired by the IRM, which can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering. The core learning algorithm is insightful, and the experimental results are strong, but the writing quality requires improvement.
