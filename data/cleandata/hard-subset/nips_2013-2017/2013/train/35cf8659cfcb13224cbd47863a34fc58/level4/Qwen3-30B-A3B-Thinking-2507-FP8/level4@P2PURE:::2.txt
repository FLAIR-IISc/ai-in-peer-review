

The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a hierarchical clustering of entities rather than a flat partition when conditioned on observed links. They introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid partitions of the entities. Empirical results on two social connectivity corpora demonstrate that their method achieves comparable or slightly superior performance (in AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy) to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while operating orders of magnitude faster.  

This paper addresses an issue of significant interest to a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. Technically, Bayesian nonparametric methods—though conceptually appealing—are often computationally intensive in practice. Domain-wise, social scientists increasingly employ sophisticated Bayesian models for network analysis, making a hierarchical extension to a prominent network-discovery model timely. The paper effectively engages both aspects.  

The key insight—that restricting partitions to tree structures enables efficient marginal likelihood computation—effectively contrasts with the IRM’s need to consider exponentially many partitions. However, I remain unclear on how the tree-learning procedure aligns with the generative Bayesian model. As it is greedy, it cannot constitute true posterior inference over tree distributions or yield a MAP/ML estimate. This ambiguity requires clarification in Section 4.  

The results are compelling: the method matches or slightly exceeds IRM on key metrics, with runtime improvements being substantial. While absolute gains in performance metrics are modest, the speed advantage is significant.  

The paper’s clarity and writing quality require substantial improvement. Spelling and grammatical errors are distracting (e.g., line 126). Vague phrasing appears in the introduction (lines 47–50: "local changes" and "iterations" lack context—e.g., are these MCMC iterations?). Dataset descriptions (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) are insufficient for unfamiliar readers. Some mathematical exposition (e.g., Equation 5) is unnecessarily obtuse. Figure 3 is confusing without familiarity with the monastery dataset; a more widely recognized dataset might aid interpretation. Section 4, presenting the core contribution, demands multiple readings for comprehension.  

Addressing these issues would strengthen the paper significantly. Despite the writing shortcomings, the approach demonstrates sufficient novelty, and the results are robust enough to warrant publication. The proposed model—effectively a Bayesian variant of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM—offers an insightful learning algorithm and strong empirical validation, though the writing quality must be improved.
