This paper proposes an agglomerative approach to scalably clustering networks into hierarchical communities, leveraging a Bayesian rose tree prior and a stochastic block model likelihood. The prior over trees and algorithm structure are largely unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12], while the likelihood has been modified to accommodate networks. Additionally, the authors have successfully adapted the caching of sufficient statistics to maintain efficient computation.

The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable performance compared to a baseline model with a flat community structure. However, a notable limitation is the lack of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. A more comprehensive evaluation would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. Section 3, in particular, presents a dense explanation of the model, and it may benefit from a simpler notation and explanation of equation (9) and its underlying components. This would enhance the clarity and accessibility of the presentation.

While the experiments illustrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets, they do not provide a thorough comparison to other hierarchical methods. For instance, the "multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009) are two Bayesian nonparametric models that also capture hierarchical latent community structures. The authors should acknowledge and discuss these models to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach's position within the existing literature.

The model presented in this paper can be seen as an extension of the Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. The primary innovations lie in the modifications to the likelihood and the recursive updating of sufficient statistics in the greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm. The likelihood can be computed efficiently in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a notable feature. However, the description of this process could be improved, particularly in the context of dynamic programming and the recursive updating of sufficient statistics. Additional explanations or equations would facilitate a deeper understanding of these components.

The use of the term "nonparametric" in this context is questionable. As Blundell et al. [12] note, the rose tree approach can be framed in terms of model selection, which contrasts with nonparametric methods. This interpretation is worth considering in the context of this paper.

The qualitative experiment on Sampson's data is well-executed, and the comparison to the IRM highlights the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses when making predictions. Nevertheless, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models remains a significant limitation. While the authors discuss scalability, the networks used in the experiments are not particularly large, and further evaluation on more extensive datasets would be beneficial.

In conclusion, this paper effectively integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. The proposed approach allows for effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but a more comprehensive comparison to other hierarchical network models is necessary to fully assess its strengths and limitations.
