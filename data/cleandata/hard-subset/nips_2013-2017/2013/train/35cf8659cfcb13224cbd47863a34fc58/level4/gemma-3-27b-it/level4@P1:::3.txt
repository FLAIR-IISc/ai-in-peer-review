This is a well-written paper with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations – significantly above average in these aspects. A minor stylistic point is to avoid beginning sentences with citations, as is customary in NIPS submissions. The theory is presented succinctly, particularly through the consistent use of f() and g() throughout the paper.

The paper explores stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the standard, single-layer instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows for a replacement of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation inherent in the IRM with the simpler equation (7). The factorization over subtrees mirrors work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior factorizes neatly based on the chosen constrained structure, facilitating efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, and link prediction.

Evaluation of SBMs is frequently conducted against “gold standard” datasets where the partition structure is known. Metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering measures are commonly used for comparison against these known clusterings. You report using Area Under the Curve (AUC), which initially required some consideration given my familiarity with NMI evaluations for SBMs. I understand you are deleting a few links and then predicting their presence, but this procedure would benefit from a clearer explanation within the paper. Furthermore, recent evaluations of SBMs have been performed on larger datasets than your full NIPS dataset; while the results presented in Figure 4 are valuable, presenting results for a larger dataset would strengthen the evaluation. It is also insufficient to only compare against Bayesian non-parametric methods, as numerous published semi-parametric methods exist, some of which you acknowledge.

The primary contribution of this algorithm lies in its agglomerative approach, which is well summarized in Table 1. 

In essence, the paper extends the basic single-layer IRM by incorporating the framework of Bayesian Rose trees. The theory and algorithms are presented clearly, although the core concept and algorithmic approach are relatively straightforward. The experimental work serves as an illustration, rather than a thorough comparative analysis, and therefore is not entirely convincing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound approach to hierarchical community discovery.
*   The proposed algorithm offers a significant speed improvement over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (as demonstrated in Figure 4 and Table 1).
*   The theoretical foundation is well-presented and builds upon established concepts in Bayesian networks and stochastic blockmodels.
*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in network analysis with a novel approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks comparison to a wider range of relevant methods (including semi-parametric approaches).
*   The explanation of the AUC evaluation metric is insufficient and requires clarification.
*   The scale of the evaluation datasets (especially for the full NIPS dataset) is relatively small compared to current benchmarks in the field.
*   The presented work builds on existing ideas, and the novelty, while present, is incremental rather than transformative.
