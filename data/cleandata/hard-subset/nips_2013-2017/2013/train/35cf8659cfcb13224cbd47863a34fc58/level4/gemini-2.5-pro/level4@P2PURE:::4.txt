This paper proposes a scalable, agglomerative approach for clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The method builds directly on prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12], adopting its prior over non-binary trees and its fundamental algorithmic structure. The main contribution lies in adapting the likelihood function for network data and developing a method for efficiently caching sufficient statistics. While the paper is clearly presented and the experiments demonstrate advantages over a baseline model with a flat community structure, a key limitation is the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK**
The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. However, the presentation in Section 3 is particularly dense; the explanation surrounding Equation (9) and its subsequent development could be simplified for better readability.

A more significant concern is the limited scope of experimental comparisons. By only comparing against a non-hierarchical baseline, the experiments demonstrate the value of modeling hierarchy but fail to benchmark the proposed method against other existing hierarchical models. The paper notably omits any comparison or discussion of relevant Bayesian nonparametric approaches, such as the multiscale community blockmodel based on a nested CRP (Ho et al., 2012) and the Mondrian Process (Roy et al., 2009), which also discover hierarchical latent structures.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY**
The proposed model is effectively an application of the Bayesian rose tree framework with a standard stochastic block-model likelihood. The primary innovation, therefore, lies in deriving the recursive updates for the sufficient statistics required by the greedy agglomerative algorithm under this new likelihood.

A key feature is the O(|V| + |E|) time complexity for computing the likelihood. Unfortunately, the description of this computation is unclear. The paper alludes to dynamic programming before Equation (10), but the details are difficult to follow. Similarly, while the equations for recursively updating sufficient statistics are provided, the paper lacks sufficient intuition or derivation to explain why they are correct.

**TERMINOLOGY**
The use of the term "nonparametric" for this model is questionable. The original Bayesian rose tree paper by Blundell et al. [12] presents the method as a form of model selection, explicitly contrasting it with nonparametric Bayesian approaches. This reviewer agrees with the original paper's characterization.

**EXPERIMENTS**
The qualitative experiment on the Sampson monastery dataset is reasonable, and the quantitative comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) successfully highlights the predictive advantages of using a hierarchical model. However, the most significant experimental weakness is the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. Additionally, while the paper claims scalability as a benefit, the experiments are conducted on networks of modest size, which is insufficient to fully substantiate this claim.

**SUMMARY**
The paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic block model likelihood and generalizes the associated efficient agglomerative clustering method to this new domain. While experiments demonstrate effective hierarchical community discovery, the work is limited by a lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models.
