Summary
The paper proposes an agglomerative method for scalable hierarchical community detection in networks. It largely adopts the Bayesian rose tree prior and search strategy from Blundell et al. [12], and introduces a stochastic block model (SBM)-style likelihood for network data. With careful caching of sufficient statistics, the algorithm remains efficient. The exposition is generally clear and the approach performs well against a flat baseline. However, the work omits comparisons to existing hierarchical network models, limiting the strength of the empirical claims.

Strengths
- Sensible and useful integration of Bayesian rose trees with an SBM likelihood, enabling hierarchical community discovery in relational data.
- Clear articulation of the overall approach; the connection to greedy agglomerative clustering is straightforward and practical.
- Efficient likelihood computation (O(Nodes + Edges)) and attention to caching of sufficient statistics are attractive for scalability.
- Experiments show benefits over a flat IRM-like baseline and present interpretable qualitative results (e.g., Sampson).

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to relevant hierarchical network models (e.g., nested-CRP-based multiscale community blockmodels and the Mondrian Process), which undermines claims about the advantages of modeling hierarchy.
- Section 3 is dense: the notation around Eq. (9) and downstream expressions could be simplified or better motivated. The dynamic programming allusion before Eq. (10) and the recursive updates of sufficient statistics are not explained with sufficient detail or intuition.
- The “nonparametric” terminology is questionable for this approach, aligning with Blundell et al.’s interpretation of rose trees as model selection rather than a genuinely nonparametric prior.
- Scalability claims are not fully substantiated: datasets are modest in size, and there is limited analysis of runtime, memory usage, or the impact of the caching scheme.

Significance and Related Work
The paper convincingly demonstrates that hierarchical structure can improve modeling and prediction in relational data compared to flat community models. However, the contribution would be more compelling with direct comparisons to other hierarchical network methods. In particular:
- Multiscale community blockmodel for network exploration (Ho et al., 2012), which uses a nested CRP to induce hierarchical structure.
- The Mondrian Process (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a stochastic process inducing hierarchical partitions (albeit over metric spaces; the connection to networks could be clarified or justified).
A discussion situating the proposed model relative to these (and other hierarchical network models) would strengthen the narrative, and empirical comparisons on shared datasets would make the advantages more concrete.

Originality and Clarity
The technical novelty lies primarily in adapting a standard SBM likelihood to the rose tree framework and in engineering efficient recursive updates for sufficient statistics. While this is a reasonable and useful contribution, the paper would benefit from:
- Clearer notation and exposition around Eq. (9) and subsequent derivations; a compact, step-by-step explanation (possibly with a running example) would help.
- An explicit dynamic programming formulation for the merge operations, with pseudocode and complexity analysis.
- Intuitive justification for the recursive sufficient-statistics updates (e.g., which conditional independences and conjugacies are used), along with a brief proof sketch or appendix derivation.

Terminology
Referring to the approach as “nonparametric” seems misleading. As in Blundell et al. [12], the method is better seen as a model-selection procedure over tree structures rather than a Bayesian nonparametric prior with a well-defined infinite limit. I recommend aligning the terminology accordingly to avoid confusion.

Experiments
- Qualitative analyses (e.g., Sampson) are informative, and the comparison to an IRM baseline highlights the value of hierarchical averaging for prediction.
- To bolster empirical credibility, consider:
  - Including baselines that explicitly model hierarchy (e.g., nested-CRP-based models, Mondrian-Process-inspired hierarchies where appropriate).
  - Reporting scalability metrics: wall-clock time, memory use, and the contribution of caching to speedups; performance on larger graphs would be particularly convincing.
  - Providing sensitivity analyses (hyperparameters, priors), and ablations (e.g., without certain merge operations or caching) to understand robustness.
  - Clarifying evaluation protocols for link prediction (train/test splits, negative sampling, uncertainty quantification).

Suggestions for Improvement
- Simplify and clarify Section 3: streamline the notation around Eq. (9); add a compact derivation and intuition for the recursive updates; include pseudocode for the agglomerative procedure and caching strategy.
- Calibrate the “nonparametric” claim, consistent with [12].
- Expand related work to include hierarchical network models, and add empirical comparisons on standard benchmarks.
- Strengthen scalability evidence with larger datasets and detailed runtime/memory profiling.
- Provide implementation details sufficient for reproducibility.

Overall Assessment
The paper effectively integrates the Bayesian rose tree framework with an SBM likelihood to produce a practical algorithm for hierarchical community discovery. The approach is elegant, computationally efficient, and shows clear benefits over flat baselines. However, the lack of engagement with existing hierarchical network models—both in related work and in experiments—limits the impact of the contribution. With clearer exposition of the dynamic programming and sufficient-statistics updates, and with stronger comparative experiments, this work could be a valuable addition to the literature. As it stands, it is promising but would benefit from addressing the above weaknesses before publication.
