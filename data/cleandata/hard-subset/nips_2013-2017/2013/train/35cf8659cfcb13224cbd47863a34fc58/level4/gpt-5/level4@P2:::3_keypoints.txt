- Paper introduces BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model that places a tree-induced prior over partitions, defines a tree-structured mixture of SBMs, uses a DP for exact marginal likelihood, and a greedy agglomerative search (three merge ops) with a sparse variant and recursive link-prediction.

- Positions BHCD as an extension of Bayesian hierarchical clustering/Rose Trees to relational data, replacing IRM’s CRP prior and relating to flat/hierarchical SBMs, IRM, LFRM, ILA, and the Mondrian process.

- Clear, well-organized exposition with concise notation and helpful abstractions for within-/between-block likelihoods.

- Sound probabilistic construction: principled tree prior πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| and an O(n + m) DP to compute exact marginal likelihoods over trees.

- Simple, efficient inference: local Bayes-factor scores depending only on candidate subtree data, three intuitive merge operations, caching for O(1) inner updates and overall O(n^2 log n) complexity independent of the number of communities.

- Practical impact demonstrated: faster link-prediction than IRM on NIPS-234 and interpretable hierarchies on example datasets.

- Evaluation protocol unclear: missing details on fraction of edges held out, negative sampling vs full complement, fold construction, hyperparameter tuning, and averaging over restarts — needs specification for reproducibility.

- Limited baselines: should compare to degree-corrected SBM, Louvain/Leiden, spectral clustering, and hierarchical SBMs (and report comparable predictive/cluster metrics) to strengthen empirical claims.

- Scale of experiments is limited: add larger benchmarks (e.g., SNAP) and report runtime/memory on sparse graphs to validate scalability.

- Missing structural recovery metrics: report NMI/VI or hierarchical clustering metrics on ground-truth or synthetic planted hierarchies in addition to AUC.

- Algorithmic details need clarification: explain heap invalidation/updating for the “global” variant or drop it; provide ablations on merge operations (join vs absorptions) and the sparse-merge constraint.

- Sensitivity and robustness are under-explored: investigate hyperparameter sensitivity (α, β, δ, λ, γ), number of restarts R, and variability from tie-breaking across runs.

- Theoretical discussion lacking: add remarks on consistency or bounds (or counterexamples) for the greedy procedure to contextualize guarantees vs other greedy hierarchical methods.

- Technical quality is high and derivations appear sound; main shortcomings are empirical breadth and experimental transparency.

- Writing and clarity are good overall; fix minor style issues (sentences starting with citations), ensure Algorithm 1 is self-contained, standardize capitalization, and report hardware/runtime measurement protocol.

- Originality: novel and neat adaptation of Bayesian Rose Trees/BHC ideas to relational SBMs, distinct from IRM-based and Mondrian approaches.

- Significance: promising practical tool if validated at scale; independence from number of communities and strong runtimes are appealing.

- Arguments for acceptance: elegant, principled model with exact tree-marginal likelihood, simple scalable inference, and interpretable results with good runtimes.

- Arguments against acceptance: empirical evaluation is limited and missing key baselines/details, and robustness/algorithmic variants appear fragile.

- Overall: technically solid and well-written with a useful contribution, but currently illustrative rather than definitive — needs more comprehensive, transparent experiments to be fully convincing.
