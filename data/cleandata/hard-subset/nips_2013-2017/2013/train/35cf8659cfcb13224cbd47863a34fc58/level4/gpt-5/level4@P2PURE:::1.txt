The authors present a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling that builds on the key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) work: (1) the hierarchy induces a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes; and (2) the hierarchy can be learned via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. They provide qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/clarity. The paper is very well written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear.

Originality. Although the main ideas originate from the BRT framework, adapting them to relational data—and doing so while preserving computational complexity—required nontrivial work.

Significance. Scaling BNP models, especially for network data, is crucial for practical impact. While the O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for very large networks, the sparse variant should remain feasible. It would be helpful to report timings on larger real-world networks. NIPS-234 runs in only tens of seconds; how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are much larger still—how would inference scale in those regimes?

It would also be useful to assess how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be evaluated at the largest problem sizes where exhaustive search remains feasible. Can any theoretical support be provided for the method (e.g., submodularity-based guarantees)?
