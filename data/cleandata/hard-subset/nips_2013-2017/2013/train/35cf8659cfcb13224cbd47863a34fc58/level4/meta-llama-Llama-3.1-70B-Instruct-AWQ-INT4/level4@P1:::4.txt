The paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical approach to community discovery in social networks, building upon prior work on Bayesian rose trees and stochastic block models. The presentation is clear, and the experiments demonstrate favorable results compared to a baseline model with flat community structure. However, the comparisons to other hierarchical network models are limited.

The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. The presentation in Section 3 is dense, and it may be beneficial to simplify the notation and explanation of equations (9) and subsequent parts. The use of a tree to define a prior on partitions allows for exact evaluation of the marginal likelihood, which is a notable contribution.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to model hierarchical community structure in social networks efficiently. However, the experiments mostly demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets rather than comparing the proposed approach to other hierarchical methods. For instance, the paper does not mention the "multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) or the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009), which are Bayesian nonparametric models capable of discovering refined latent structure.

The originality of the paper lies in the adaptation of the Bayesian rose tree approach to network data and the development of efficient agglomerative clustering methods. The likelihood can be computed in O(n + m) time, which is a desirable feature. However, the description of this aspect could be improved, and the recursive updating of sufficient statistics could be better explained.

The terminology used in the paper is mostly clear, but the use of the term "nonparametric" may be questionable. As Blundell et al. (2012) pointed out, the rose tree approach can be phrased in terms of model selection, contrasting it with nonparametric methods.

The experiments are well-designed, and the qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network are fine. The comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) is also informative, demonstrating the benefits of averaging over many hypotheses when making predictions. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a notable limitation. The discussion on scalability is also limited, as the networks tested are not particularly large.

Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of community discovery in social networks, but it would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to existing hierarchical network models.
