This paper presents a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is to represent the network as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, leveraging a greedy agglomerative approach for efficient model selection. This allows for a probabilistic account of the data, offering a computationally attractive alternative to methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate that BHCD scales favorably – quadratically in the number of vertices, and independently of the number of communities – and achieves comparable or superior accuracy to the IRM while being significantly faster.

The work builds upon existing Bayesian approaches to hierarchical clustering [13, 14], and cleverly adapts them to relational data. The use of a tree prior, as opposed to directly employing a Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) as in the IRM, allows for efficient calculation of the marginal likelihood. This design choice is reminiscent of work in Bayesian network structure learning [Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004], where constrained structures facilitate tractable inference. The authors effectively leverage this connection, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computations, and link prediction. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-defined and the derivation appears logically sound. The use of dynamic programming for likelihood calculation is a significant optimization.
*   **Efficiency:** The reported computational complexity (O(n<sup>2</sup> log n)) and the empirical speedup of two orders of magnitude over the IRM are strong advantages. The scalability is a key contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model and algorithm. The notation is generally consistent, and the use of f() and g() functions to represent likelihood components is concise.
*   **Novelty:** While building on established techniques, the combination of Bayesian Rose trees with stochastic blockmodels and the efficient agglomerative inference procedure is a novel approach.
*   **Well-motivated:** The introduction effectively establishes the importance of hierarchical community detection and highlights the limitations of existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the speed of BHCD, is limited in scope. The comparison to IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset is valuable, but the dataset size (234 vertices) is relatively small. A more comprehensive evaluation on larger, more complex networks is needed to fully assess the performance.
*   **Link Prediction Explanation:** The paper mentions using AUC for link prediction but lacks a clear explanation of the methodology.  How are links deleted and re-introduced for evaluation?  This needs to be clarified for readers unfamiliar with this approach.
*   **Comparison to Semi-Parametric Methods:** The paper focuses its comparison on Bayesian nonparametric methods. However, a discussion of how BHCD compares to relevant semi-parametric alternatives would provide a more complete picture of its strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The sensitivity of the model's performance to the choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) is not discussed. An analysis of this sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Starting a sentence with a citation:** In the introduction, the sentence “Stochastic blockmodels: Some first steps, P. Holland, K.B. Laskey, S. Leinhardt, Social Networks, 5:109137, ,” should be rewritten to avoid starting with a citation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in network analysis.
*   The proposed method offers a significant improvement in computational efficiency compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches.
*   The theoretical foundation is solid and the model is clearly explained.
*   The paper demonstrates the potential of the method through illustrative experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and does not fully demonstrate the method’s scalability and robustness on large datasets.
*   The lack of detailed explanation regarding the link prediction evaluation and hyperparameter sensitivity weakens the empirical analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and promising paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The proposed method is technically sound, efficient, and well-motivated. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the results presented are encouraging. I believe that addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper. 

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the experimental evaluation to include larger datasets and more comprehensive comparisons.
2.  Provide a detailed explanation of the link prediction evaluation methodology.
3.  Discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings.
4.  Rewrite the sentence that begins with a citation in the introduction.
