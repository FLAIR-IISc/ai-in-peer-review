

The paper is well-written with correct spelling, grammar, and citations. While the theoretical presentation is concise in places (e.g., using consistent notation like f() and g() throughout), avoid beginning sentences with citations in NIPS style. The work extends the single-table instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by integrating Bayesian Rose tree theory, enabling the replacement of the CRP formulation with the simpler Equation (7). The subtree factorization aligns with established Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3), where the posterior factorizes neatly under constrained structures, facilitating greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, sufficient statistic handling, and link prediction.  

Evaluation of SBM typically uses "gold standard" data with known partitions, employing metrics like NMI. Your use of AUC requires clarification: it appears you delete edges and predict their re-addition, but this is not immediately evident to readers familiar with standard SBM evaluation (e.g., NMI). A detailed explanation is essential. Additionally, recent SBM evaluations use larger datasets than your full NIPS dataset; while Figure 4’s small-scale results are acceptable, the evaluation should be extended to a larger dataset. Comparing only against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient, as numerous semi-parametric methods exist (some cited in the paper).  

The algorithm’s contribution lies in its agglomerative approach, well-summarized in Table 1. The theoretical foundation is clear and well-motivated, though the core concept and algorithms are relatively simple. The experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, making it inadequate for a strong evaluation.
