- Paper introduces BHCD: a hierarchical IRM extension using Bayesian Rose Trees to build a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels via a greedy agglomerative algorithm (O(n² log n)).  
- Empirical claim: BHCD is ~200× faster than IRM with comparable predictive accuracy on Sampson’s network, NIPS-234, and full NIPS co-authorship.  
- Quality/Clarity: Exceptionally well-written; model, algorithm, and experiments clearly presented; DP marginal likelihood and sufficient-statistic caching are well-justified; only minor grammar issues.  
- Originality: Non-trivial adaptation of BRT to relational data that maintains computational efficiency and includes a sparse-network extension.  
- Significance: Addresses a key scalability bottleneck—complexity independent of community count—but lacks empirical evidence beyond 2,864 nodes and omits full NIPS runtime.  
- Theoretical gap: No analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality or approximation guarantees (e.g., submodularity), leaving the optimality gap unquantified.  
- Strengths: Clear algorithm and complexity analysis, strong qualitative and quantitative validation, practical BHCD sparse variant, and well-motivated hyperparameter choices.  
- Weaknesses: Limited scalability analysis for very large networks (>10k nodes), missing full NIPS runtime, and no theoretical guarantees for the greedy procedure.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept (suitable for NIPS); technical soundness and clarity outweigh current scalability/theoretical gaps.  
- Requested revisions: Report full NIPS (2,864-node) inference times, discuss feasibility for 10k+ networks, and include a brief analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality gap (e.g., small-scale exhaustive search).
