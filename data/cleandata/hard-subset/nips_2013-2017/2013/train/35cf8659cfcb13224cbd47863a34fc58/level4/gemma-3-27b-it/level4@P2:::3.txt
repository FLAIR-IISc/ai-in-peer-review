This paper presents a well-written and technically sound Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors effectively combine stochastic blockmodels (SBMs) with Bayesian Rose trees, offering a compelling alternative to existing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The theoretical development is concise and elegant, particularly the use of f() and g() functions to streamline the notation. The core idea—replacing the Chinese Restaurant Process formulation of the IRM with a tree-based prior (eq. 7)—allows for efficient computation, a key strength highlighted throughout the paper. This factorization over subtrees mirrors work in Bayesian network structure learning, such as Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, Section 3), enabling effective greedy search and marginal likelihood calculations.

The paper clearly summarizes related work, positioning its contribution within the existing literature on community detection. It correctly identifies the limitations of flat community models (SBM, mixed membership SBM) and previous hierarchical approaches, particularly concerning computational cost. The authors accurately highlight the advantages of their greedy agglomerative approach and its favorable scaling properties, as summarized in Table 1.

A significant strength of the work lies in the computational efficiency of the proposed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) algorithm. The authors demonstrate substantial speedups compared to the IRM, achieving comparable or better accuracy. The use of cached sufficient statistics further optimizes performance, especially for sparse networks (BHCD sparse). 

However, the experimental evaluation, while illustrative, is somewhat limited. The use of AUC for evaluation requires clearer explanation. While the authors presume a link deletion/prediction strategy, this should be explicitly stated for clarity. Furthermore, while the comparison to Bayesian nonparametric methods is valuable, the field also includes numerous semi-parametric methods that should be acknowledged and potentially compared against. The experiments on the NIPS dataset, though demonstrating speed advantages, would be more convincing if conducted on a larger dataset. The comparison against IRM is strong, but broadening the benchmark to include other established methods would strengthen the paper’s claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The model is mathematically well-defined and the derivations are clear.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** BHCD offers significant speed improvements over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow.
*   **Novel Combination:** The combination of SBMs and Bayesian Rose trees is a novel and potentially valuable approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation lacks a comprehensive comparison to a wider range of methods and utilizes a relatively small dataset.
*   **AUC Explanation:** The methodology for using AUC as an evaluation metric requires clarification.
*   **Lack of NMI or similar metrics:** The paper does not report the use of NMI or other standard clustering evaluation metrics common for assessing SBM performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to hierarchical community detection.
*   The computational efficiency gains are significant and represent a valuable contribution.
*   The theoretical framework is well-developed and elegantly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and does not fully demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method across a broad range of scenarios.
*   The lack of a comprehensive benchmark against a wider set of methods weakens the claims of improved performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The combination of theoretical rigor and practical efficiency is commendable. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focused on expanding the experimental evaluation and clarifying the AUC evaluation methodology.
