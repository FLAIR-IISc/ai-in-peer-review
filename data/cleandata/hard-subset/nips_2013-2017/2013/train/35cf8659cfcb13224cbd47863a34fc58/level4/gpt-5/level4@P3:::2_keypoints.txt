- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model that uncovers hierarchical community structure using rose trees and exact marginal likelihoods via beta–Bernoulli blockmodels.

- Learning is greedy agglomerative model selection with three local merge operations, using cached sufficient statistics and local Bayes factors.

- Proposes a sparse variant that forbids merges between disconnected subgraphs to speed up inference.

- Link prediction uses recursive mixing of on/off-diagonal posteriors along tree paths.

- Empirical results on Sampson, NIPS-234, and full NIPS show comparable or better predictive performance than IRM and much faster runtime (worst-case O(n^2 log n)).

- Builds on stochastic blockmodels, IRM, Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and Rose Trees, extending these ideas to relational data.

- Key strength: conceptually clear and practical with exact tree marginal likelihoods and simple, fast greedy search.

- Key strength: scalable implementation with O(1) inner-loop updates and a sparse variant for sparse graphs.

- Key strength: interpretable multi-resolution hierarchies and well-structured predictive recursion.

- Key strength: empirical speedups (reported two orders of magnitude) while matching or improving predictive metrics.

- Main weakness: algorithm is greedy model selection, not posterior inference; paper needs to clarify objective and implications for uncertainty quantification.

- Runtime claims are inconsistent and need reconciliation with matched wall-clock comparisons and clarification of IRM baseline implementation.

- Baselines are limited (mostly IRM); paper should compare to hierarchical IRM variants, Mondrian models, LFRM/ILA or include ablations/discussion of modeling trade-offs.

- Hyperparameters are fixed without justification or sensitivity analysis; needs empirical Bayes, defaults, or hyperparameter experiments.

- Random restarts and tie-breaking effects are not quantified; report variability across runs and overhead of restarts.

- Recommend making the sparse constraint default or providing guidance on when unconstrained BHCD is preferable.

- Presentation needs proofreading, clearer notation, more self-contained Section 4, pseudo-code, running examples, and clearer figures (e.g., Eq.5, Figure 3).

- Quality: technically sound dynamic programming and agglomerative search, but lacks posterior uncertainty and broader empirical evaluation.

- Originality: incremental extension of Rose Trees to relational data but a useful, efficiently implemented contribution.

- Significance: practical and likely useful to practitioners due to favorable speed–accuracy trade-offs.

- Suggestions: clarify learning objective, detail datasets/evaluation, standardize runtime claims with error bars, add ablations, include synthetic ground-truth experiments, and proofread.

- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on improved clarity, expanded baselines, and clarified claims.
