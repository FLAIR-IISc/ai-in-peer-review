This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric model for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The proposed model can be understood as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key contribution is an efficient, greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm that learns this hierarchical structure in a single bottom-up pass. This approach contrasts with existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for relational data, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which typically rely on iterative, sample-based inference schemes that can be computationally intensive. The authors present empirical results on two social network datasets, demonstrating that their method achieves comparable or superior accuracy to the IRM while being orders of magnitude faster.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in the application of Bayesian nonparametric methods to network analysis: computational scalability. Many existing methods, while theoretically elegant, are too slow for large-scale networks. By proposing a greedy model selection algorithm with a worst-case quadratic time complexity that is independent of the number of communities, the authors provide a practical tool that could be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners analyzing network data. The demonstrated two-orders-of-magnitude speedup over the IRM is a substantial practical contribution.

2.  **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The core technical idea is both novel and insightful. By restricting the space of possible data partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data given the tree can be calculated exactly and efficiently using dynamic programming (Eq. 10). This avoids the need to sum over an exponential number of partitions, which is a major challenge for models like the IRM. The adaptation of Bayesian agglomerative clustering to relational data, including the novel caching scheme to achieve O(n² log n) complexity, is a solid technical contribution.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is compelling. On the NIPS co-authorship dataset, BHCD achieves predictive performance (in terms of log predictive probability, accuracy, and AUC) comparable to the IRM in a fraction of the time. The ability to generate reasonable predictions before a standard MCMC-based IRM completes even a single iteration highlights the method's efficiency. The qualitative results on the Sampson's monastery network also appear reasonable and demonstrate the model's ability to uncover interpretable hierarchical structures.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of the Inference Procedure:** The main weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear connection between the probabilistic model presented in Section 3 and the greedy learning algorithm in Section 4. Section 3 describes a well-defined generative process and a method for calculating the marginal likelihood p(D|T). However, the agglomerative algorithm is a greedy search procedure that builds the tree T bottom-up by maximizing a local Bayes factor at each step. It is not clear what this procedure optimizes globally. Is the final tree an approximation of the maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood (ML) tree? Or should the algorithm be viewed as a heuristic search for a useful model structure, rather than formal posterior inference? A more explicit discussion of the theoretical grounding of the learning algorithm and its relationship to the Bayesian model would significantly strengthen the paper's quality.

2.  **Overall Presentation and Clarity:** While the core ideas are strong, the quality of the writing could be improved to make the paper more accessible and impactful.
    *   **Vague Phrasing:** The introduction contains some imprecise statements. For example, the description of why current methods are slow (lines 47-50) mentions "many small changes to the model" and "a large number of iterations." It would be clearer to state explicitly that this refers to local proposals in MCMC or variational methods.
    *   **Dataset Descriptions:** The paper assumes the reader is familiar with the experimental datasets. Brief descriptions of Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data—including their size, sparsity, and the nature of the relationships they represent—would make the results more self-contained and understandable to a broader audience.
    *   **Mathematical Notation:** Some of the notation is dense and could be clarified. For instance, Equation 5, which defines the set of tree-consistent partitions, is presented formally but might benefit from a more intuitive, textual explanation alongside it.
    *   **Figure Interpretation:** Figure 3 is difficult to interpret without prior familiarity with the monastery dataset. It is not immediately obvious which discovered hierarchy is "better" or how they align with the ground-truth groups mentioned in the text. Adding quantitative metrics or a more guided visual comparison would help demonstrate the effectiveness of the different BHCD variants.
    *   **Minor Errors:** The draft contains several minor grammatical and spelling errors (e.g., "Our algorithms scale quadratically" in the abstract, "rose trees" in the figure caption) that should be corrected in a final version.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a novel, efficient, and effective method for discovering hierarchical communities in networks. The combination of a tree-constrained probabilistic model with a fast, greedy agglomerative inference algorithm is a valuable contribution to the field. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate a significant speed advantage over established methods like the IRM without sacrificing predictive accuracy.

The primary concerns relate to the clarity of the presentation. Specifically, the authors should better articulate the theoretical justification for their inference algorithm and its connection to the probabilistic model. Addressing the other points regarding prose, dataset descriptions, and figure clarity would also substantially improve the manuscript.

Despite these weaknesses in presentation, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the results are significant. I believe this work would be of considerable interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors thoroughly revising the manuscript to improve clarity, particularly concerning the rationale for the inference algorithm.
