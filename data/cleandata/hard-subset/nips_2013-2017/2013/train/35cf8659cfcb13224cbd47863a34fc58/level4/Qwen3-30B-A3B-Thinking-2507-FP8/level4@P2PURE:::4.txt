

Proposes an agglomerative approach for scalably clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and algorithm structure are essentially unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is modified in a straightforward manner to handle networks, and additional work ensures sufficient statistics remain efficiently cacheable. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable comparison to a baseline model with a flat community structure. However, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are lacking.  

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:  
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. The presentation in Section 3 is particularly dense; it is unclear whether a simpler notation or explanation for Equation (9) and its dependencies could be employed. Since comparisons are limited to models that do not incorporate hierarchy in their latent structure, the experiments primarily illustrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy for relational datasets, but do not establish whether the approach outperforms existing hierarchical methods. For instance, at least two papers model the latent community structure hierarchically: "Multiscale Community Blockmodel for Network Exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) uses a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009) defines a stochastic process yielding hierarchical structures over K-d trees. The paper fails to reference these Bayesian nonparametric models, which may discover more refined latent structures.  

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:  
The model considered here is a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. Thus, the innovations lie solely in how the modified likelihood alters the recursive update of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative clustering. The likelihood computation operates in O(Nodes + Edges) time, a notable feature. Unfortunately, the description is inadequate: an allusion to dynamic programming precedes Equation (10), but the details remain difficult to grasp. Similarly, while equations for recursive sufficient statistic updates are provided, they lack sufficient explanation to clarify their correctness.  

TERMINOLOGY:  
The use of "nonparametric" terminology is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose tree approach in terms of model selection and contrast it with nonparametric methods in their discussion; this interpretation is supported.  

EXPERIMENTS:  
The qualitative experiment on Sampson is adequate, and the comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively demonstrates the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. The primary limitation remains the absence of comparison to other hierarchical network models. While scalability is discussed, none of the networks tested are particularly large.  

The paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering to this scenario. This enables effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, though comparisons to alternative hierarchical network models are missing.
