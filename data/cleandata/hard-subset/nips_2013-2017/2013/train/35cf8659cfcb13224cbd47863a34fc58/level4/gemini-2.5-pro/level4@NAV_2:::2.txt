This paper introduces a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The proposed model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), can be viewed as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key contribution is an efficient, greedy agglomerative algorithm for model selection that builds the hierarchy in a single bottom-up pass. This approach is contrasted with prior work like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which infers a flat community structure, and other hierarchical extensions that are often computationally expensive. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their method is significantly faster than IRM while achieving comparable or superior predictive performance on social network data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem. The inference of hierarchical structure in networks is of great interest to the machine learning and social sciences communities. Furthermore, developing computationally efficient alternatives to sampling-based Bayesian nonparametric methods is an important research direction. A method that offers a significant speed-up over a widely used model like the IRM is a valuable contribution.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The core technical idea—restricting the set of possible partitions to those consistent with a tree structure to enable efficient, exact computation of the marginal likelihood via dynamic programming—is elegant. The paper builds effectively on previous work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering and adapts it to the domain of relational data. The derivation of the agglomerative learning algorithm with its efficient caching scheme appears technically sound.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The claim of being "two orders of magnitude faster" than the IRM is well-supported by the experiments on the NIPS co-authorship data (Figure 4). Achieving this speed while maintaining comparable or slightly better accuracy, log predictive probability, and AUC is a strong result.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of the Learning Algorithm:** The relationship between the greedy model selection algorithm in Section 4 and the probabilistic model in Section 3 could be made more explicit. Since the agglomerative procedure is greedy, it does not perform full posterior inference over the space of trees. It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify what objective the algorithm is optimizing. Is it intended to find a point estimate approximating the maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood (ML) tree? A brief discussion of the implications of this greedy approximation would strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarity of the Manuscript:** The overall quality of the writing could be improved to make the paper more accessible.
    *   There are several minor typographical and grammatical errors that a careful proofread could resolve (e.g., line 126).
    *   The prose is sometimes ambiguous. For instance, in the introduction (lines 47-50), terms like "small changes" and "iterations" could be specified more clearly (e.g., "MCMC proposals" or "Gibbs sweeps").
    *   The experimental datasets (Sampson's Monastery, NIPS co-authorship) are not described, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with this prior work. A brief sentence describing the nodes and edges for each would be helpful.
    *   Some mathematical definitions, such as Equation 5, are rather formal and could be supplemented with more textual intuition to aid understanding.
    *   Figure 3 is difficult to interpret for a reader not already familiar with the Sampson monastery dataset, making it hard to visually validate the quality of the discovered hierarchies.
    *   Section 4, which details the core algorithmic contribution, is dense and required several readings. Expanding the description of the algorithm's flow, particularly the merge types and the update steps (Eq. 13), would improve clarity.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel and efficient method for a well-motivated problem. The core idea is insightful, and the experimental results demonstrating a significant speed-up over a strong baseline are impressive. While the paper's primary weaknesses relate to the clarity of the exposition, these are addressable. I believe the work's novelty and significance merit publication, and I would recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors improving the clarity as suggested above.
