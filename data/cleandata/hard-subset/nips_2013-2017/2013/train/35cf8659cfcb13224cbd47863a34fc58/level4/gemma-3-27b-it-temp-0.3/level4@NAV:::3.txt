The paper is exceptionally well-written, demonstrating strong command of language, grammar, and citation practices. The writing quality is significantly above average, though adherence to NIPS style regarding in-text citations could be improved (avoiding sentence-initial citations). The theoretical development is concise and effective, notably through the consistent use of notation like f() and g().

This work builds upon stochastic block models (SBM) by extending the standard single-table instance of an Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework with the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows for a simplification of the IRM formulation, replacing the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) with the more streamlined equation (7). The resulting factorization over subtrees aligns with established research in Bayesian network structure learning, such as Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where posterior factorization benefits from constrained structure classes, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, sufficient statistics handling, and link prediction.

Evaluation of SBM typically relies on “gold standard” datasets with known partition structures, utilizing metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) to compare against ground truth clusterings. The authors report using Area Under the Curve (AUC), which initially required clarification. I understand the approach involves link deletion and subsequent prediction of link re-addition; however, this procedure requires a more detailed explanation within the paper. Furthermore, while the evaluation presented in Figure 4 is valuable, recent SBM evaluations frequently employ larger datasets than those used here (including those presented at NIPS). Therefore, extending the evaluation to a larger dataset is recommended. Comparison to Bayesian non-parametric methods alone is insufficient; the inclusion of relevant semi-parametric methods, some of which are already referenced, would strengthen the analysis.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. 

In summary, the paper presents a clear and theoretically sound extension of IRM using Bayesian Rose trees. However, while the theory and algorithms are well-defined, they represent a relatively simple concept. The experimental evaluation, while illustrative, lacks the comparative depth necessary to fully demonstrate the method’s advantages.
