- Generalizes the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to infer hierarchical (tree) partitions rather than flat clusters.  
- Proposes an agglomerative learning algorithm that selects a tree encoding valid partitions.  
- Experiments on two social-network datasets (monastery networks and NIPS authorship) show the method is faster and slightly more accurate than the IRM.  
- Timely and relevant: addresses computational and modeling needs for Bayesian network analysis used by social scientists.  
- Key technical idea: restricting partitions to tree-consistent ones enables efficient marginal-likelihood computation versus the IRM’s exponential partition space.  
- Unclear how the greedy, agglomerative algorithm aligns with the Bayesian generative model—does not appear to perform posterior inference or yield a MAP/ML estimate under a clear objective.  
- Section 4 needs a clearer, explicit statement of the objective the algorithm optimizes and its relationship to the posterior.  
- Empirical results are strong across AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, with dramatic runtime speedups (orders of magnitude) over IRM.  
- Presentation issues: spelling/grammar errors, vague phrasing (e.g., “local changes,” “iterations”), and overly dense mathematical exposition.  
- Datasets are insufficiently described; Figure 3 and some examples are hard to interpret without prior knowledge—add dataset details and a worked example.  
- Overall: novel and compelling contribution that merits publication if exposition and clarity are substantially improved.
