- Proposes BHCD: a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative algorithm that learns a probabilistic hierarchy in a single bottom-up pass.  
- Extends Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data and develops an efficient caching scheme for sufficient statistics to achieve quadratic scaling.  
- Strength: Computationally efficient (worst-case O(n^2 log n)), independent of number of communities, and orders-of-magnitude faster than MCMC/IRM in experiments.  
- Strength: Technically sound integration of Rose Trees with a blockmodel likelihood; the algorithmic generalization is well-executed.  
- Strength: Generally clear and well-organized presentation.  
- Weakness: Limited experimental comparison—only compared to flat IRM, lacking evaluations against other hierarchical network models.  
- Weakness: Missing related work discussion, e.g., multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al. 2011) and the Mondrian Process (Roy & Teh 2009).  
- Weakness: Some technical details are under-explained—O(n+m) likelihood computation and recursive updates (Eq. 13) lack sufficient derivation or intuition.  
- Weakness: Limited originality—largely a direct application of Bayesian Rose Trees to a new likelihood with engineering improvements for caching.  
- Weakness: Terminology issue—labeling the method “nonparametric” is debatable; it is better framed as greedy model selection over a discrete tree space.  
- Weakness: Scalability claims are not fully substantiated because experiments use moderately sized networks rather than truly large-scale datasets.  
- Overall: Produces fast, high-quality hierarchical community discovery versus flat baselines, but its significance is reduced by missing comparisons to related hierarchical methods.
