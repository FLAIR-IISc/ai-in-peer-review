The manuscript is presented with a high degree of clarity, exhibiting strong writing, proper grammar, and accurate citations that place it well above the average submission. As a minor stylistic note, the authors may consider revising sentences that begin directly with a citation, a convention common in the NIPS format. The theoretical exposition is concise, for instance, through the consistent use of general notations such as f() and g().

This paper addresses Stochastic Block Models (SBM) by extending the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM). This is achieved by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees, which allows the conventional Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation within the IRM to be substituted with the more direct expression in equation (7). The method of factoring over subtrees builds upon prior work in learning Bayesian network structures (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3). By selecting an appropriate class of constrained structures, the posterior factorizes elegantly. This property, in turn, enables the straightforward application of greedy search, the calculation of marginal likelihoods, the management of sufficient statistics, and link prediction tasks.

The evaluation methodology raises some questions. Typically, SBMs are assessed against "gold standard" data with known partitions, using clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) to compare against the ground truth. This paper reports performance using AUC, and the protocol for this is not immediately clear. It is presumed that this involves a link prediction task where a subset of edges is removed and the model's ability to predict their existence is measured. This aspect of the evaluation requires explicit explanation. Furthermore, the scale of the experiments could be improved. While the results in Figure 4 are informative, recent SBM evaluations are often conducted on datasets much larger than the NIPS corpus used here. Including a larger-scale experiment would strengthen the paper. Finally, the choice of baselines is somewhat limited; a robust comparison should extend beyond Bayesian non-parametric methods to include the many powerful semi-parametric approaches available, some of which are already cited by the authors.

The core contribution is the novel agglomerative algorithm, which is well-summarized in Table 1. In conclusion, the paper presents a clear theoretical and algorithmic framework for extending the single-table IRM with Bayesian Rose trees. Although the concept and implementation are straightforward, the theory is sound. The primary weakness lies in the experimental validation, which feels more illustrative than rigorously comparative and is therefore not entirely adequate.
