This paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations, making it well above average in terms of presentation. However, it would be beneficial to avoid starting sentences with citations, as per the NIPS style. The theoretical aspects of the paper are succinctly presented, with a clear and consistent use of notation, such as f() and g() throughout the text.

The paper focuses on stochastic block models (SBMs) and extends the simple single table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows for a simpler formulation of the IRM, replacing the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) with equation (7). The factoring over subtrees is reminiscent of work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood's JMLR 2004 paper, section 3. This approach enables the posterior to factorize neatly based on the class of constrained structures chosen, facilitating greedy search, marginal likelihoods, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and other tasks.

The evaluation of SBMs is often performed against "gold standard" data with known partition structures, using metrics such as Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering metrics to compare against known clusterings. The paper reports using Area Under the Curve (AUC) for evaluation, which is an unconventional choice for SBM evaluation. It appears that the authors deleted a few links and then attempted to guess whether they should be added back, but this approach requires further explanation. Recent evaluations of SBMs have been performed on larger datasets, so it would be beneficial to present results on a larger dataset in addition to the small evaluation shown in Figure 4. Furthermore, comparing against only Bayesian non-parametric methods is not sufficient, as there are many published semi-parametric methods that should be considered.

The main contribution of this algorithm is the agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper provides a clear and well-presented theory and algorithm, although the concept and algorithm are relatively simple. However, the experimental work is primarily illustrative rather than comparative, which is not adequate for a thorough evaluation of the method.
