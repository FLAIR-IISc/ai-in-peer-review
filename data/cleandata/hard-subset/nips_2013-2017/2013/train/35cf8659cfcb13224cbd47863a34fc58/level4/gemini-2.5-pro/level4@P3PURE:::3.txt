The paper is well-written, with clear prose, good spelling, and correct citations, placing it well above average in presentation. As a minor stylistic point, sentences should not begin with a citation in the NIPS format. The theoretical exposition is concise, for instance using generic notation such as f() and g() throughout.

The central contribution of this work is an extension of the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for Stochastic Block Models (SBMs) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose Trees. This allows the standard Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of the IRM to be replaced by the simpler factorization presented in Equation (7). The resulting factorization over subtrees is analogous to established techniques for learning Bayesian network structures (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004), where the posterior factorizes neatly based on the class of constrained structures chosen. This property, in turn, enables the straightforward application of greedy search, marginal likelihood calculation, and efficient handling of sufficient statistics for tasks like link prediction. The proposed agglomerative method is the core algorithmic contribution and is well-summarized in Table 1.

The experimental evaluation, however, has several significant weaknesses. First, the choice of evaluation metric is unclear. Standard evaluation for SBMs on "gold standard" data with known partitions typically involves clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI). The authors report Area Under the Curve (AUC), but the methodology for calculating it is not explained. I presume this involves a link prediction task where a subset of links is held out, but this procedure requires explicit clarification.

Second, the scale of the experiments is limited. While the results shown in Figure 4 are illustrative, recent SBM evaluations are often performed on datasets significantly larger than the NIPS co-authorship network used here. Demonstrating the method's performance on a larger scale would be necessary to strengthen the paper's claims.

Finally, the comparison is not comprehensive enough. The evaluation is largely restricted to other Bayesian non-parametric methods, omitting the many published semi-parametric methods, some of which are already cited in the paper.

In summary, the paper presents a clear theoretical extension of the IRM using Bayesian Rose Trees, resulting in a straightforward and elegant agglomerative algorithm. However, the experimental work is more illustrative than rigorously comparative, and is therefore not adequate to convincingly demonstrate the proposed method's advantages over the state of the art.
