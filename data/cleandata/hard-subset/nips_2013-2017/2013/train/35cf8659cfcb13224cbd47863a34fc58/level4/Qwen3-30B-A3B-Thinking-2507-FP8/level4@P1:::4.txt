

This paper proposes an efficient agglomerative approach for hierarchical community discovery in social networks. The core method builds upon Bayesian rose trees [12] by adapting them to network data through a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The key innovation lies in efficiently caching sufficient statistics for recursive updates during greedy model selection, enabling a runtime complexity of O(n² log n) where n is the number of vertices. The paper demonstrates that the proposed method, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), achieves comparable or better accuracy than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being two orders of magnitude faster.

The paper's strengths include a clear overall presentation and strong empirical results. The experiments on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data effectively demonstrate the method's ability to discover interpretable hierarchical structures. The comparison to IRM is particularly valuable as it shows the advantages of hierarchical modeling for relational data, and the authors' careful analysis of predictive performance through multiple runs (with R=50 restarts) provides robust evidence of the method's reliability.

However, the paper has several significant shortcomings that limit its contribution. First, the related work section fails to properly contextualize the method within existing hierarchical network models. The authors overlook two key Bayesian nonparametric approaches: Ho et al.'s Multiscale Community Blockmodel (2012), which uses a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and Roy et al.'s Mondrian Process (2009), which defines a hierarchical structure over K-d trees. These models are directly comparable to BHCD and would provide essential context for evaluating the paper's novelty. The authors' claim of novelty is significantly weakened by this omission.

Second, the paper misuses the term "nonparametric" to describe their approach. As Blundell et al. [12] explicitly clarify in their discussion, Bayesian rose trees operate through model selection rather than nonparametric methods. The authors' approach is fundamentally a greedy model selection procedure with a specific tree prior, not a nonparametric Bayesian method. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's technical credibility.

Third, the explanation of the recursive sufficient statistics updates is confusing. While the paper mentions dynamic programming before equation (10), the details of how statistics are updated (equations 13) lack sufficient intuition. The reader must work hard to understand why these particular updates are correct. A more intuitive explanation or concrete example would greatly improve clarity.

Section 3, which describes the Bayesian hierarchical model, is particularly dense. The notation in equations (9) and subsequent sections is cumbersome and could be simplified for better readability. For instance, the explanation of σ¬chSS could be made more intuitive by explicitly connecting it to the tree structure.

Regarding significance, the method's computational efficiency is a notable contribution, especially for dense networks where the runtime complexity is O(n² log n). However, the paper's claim about scalability is somewhat limited, as the networks tested (Sampson's monastery, NIPS-234) are relatively small. The authors would strengthen their case by demonstrating performance on larger networks.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to hierarchical community discovery, but its novelty is overstated due to the omission of relevant prior work and mischaracterization of the method. The technical approach is well-executed with clear experimental results, but the lack of comparison to other hierarchical models and the mislabeling of "nonparametric" are significant drawbacks.

In summary, the paper presents an efficient algorithm for hierarchical community discovery that outperforms IRM in speed while maintaining comparable accuracy. The method is clearly described and well-executed, but the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models, the mischaracterization of the approach as "nonparametric," and the confusing explanation of the recursive sufficient statistics updates prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. The authors should address these issues to strengthen their submission.
