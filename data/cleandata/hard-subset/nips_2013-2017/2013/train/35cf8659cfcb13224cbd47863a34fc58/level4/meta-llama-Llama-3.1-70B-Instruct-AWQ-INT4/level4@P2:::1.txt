The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, leveraging key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper. Specifically, they utilize the hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and learn this hierarchy using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors demonstrate qualitative results on Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset.

The paper is well-written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The main ideas are taken from the Bayesian rose tree work, but the authors have made significant efforts to adapt these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity.

The significance of this work lies in scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models to network models, an important challenge if these methods are to be used by practitioners. While the n^2 scaling of the basic algorithm may prove prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should presumably remain feasible. It would be beneficial to include timings on larger real-world networks to demonstrate the algorithm's efficiency.

To further strengthen the paper, it would be valuable to investigate how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be explored at the largest scale that allows for exhaustive search to find the optimal tree. Additionally, providing theoretical support for the method, such as in terms of submodularity, would be a valuable contribution.
