The authors present a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, designed to infer hierarchical relationships between entities from observed network connections, rather than a simple flat clustering. Their approach utilizes an agglomerative clustering algorithm to efficiently explore the space of possible entity partitions, represented as a tree structure. Empirical evaluation on two social network datasets demonstrates that their method achieves comparable or improved accuracy to the widely-used Infinite Relational Model (IRM), with significantly faster runtime.

This work addresses a compelling problem relevant to a substantial portion of the NIPS community. Bayesian nonparametric methods, while theoretically appealing, often suffer from computational limitations. Simultaneously, the increasing adoption of sophisticated Bayesian models within the social sciences creates a need for extensions to established network discovery techniques like the IRM. This paper effectively bridges both of these concerns.

A key contribution lies in leveraging the tree structure to enable efficient marginal likelihood computation, contrasting with the IRM’s need to consider an exponentially large number of partitions. However, the connection between the greedy tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model is not entirely clear. It is unclear whether the learning process constitutes true posterior inference or a point estimate (MAP/ML), and clarification in Section 4 would be beneficial.

The reported results are promising, with the proposed method achieving competitive performance against the IRM across multiple metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy). While the absolute gains in these metrics are modest, the substantial reduction in runtime compared to IRM inference is noteworthy.

The manuscript would benefit from improvements in clarity and presentation. Numerous spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126) are distracting. Certain passages, such as lines 47-50 of the introduction, contain ambiguous phrasing ("local changes," "iterations"). A more detailed description of the datasets used (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) is needed for readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical notation (e.g., Eq. 5) appears unnecessarily complex, and Figure 3 is difficult to interpret, particularly for those unfamiliar with the Monastery dataset; a more widely recognized dataset might improve clarity. Section 4, detailing the core contribution, requires careful reading for full comprehension.

Despite these issues, the proposed approach exhibits sufficient novelty and strong empirical results to warrant publication, provided the authors address the concerns regarding clarity and presentation. The work introduces a novel Bayesian model for graph structure, effectively combining aspects of agglomerative clustering and the IRM, and presents a compelling learning algorithm supported by robust experimental findings.
