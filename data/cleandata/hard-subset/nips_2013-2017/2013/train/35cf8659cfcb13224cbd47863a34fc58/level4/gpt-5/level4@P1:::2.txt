Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper generalizes the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic blockmodel to discover hierarchical, rather than flat, communities in relational data. The model imposes a tree over entities and defines a prior over partitions consistent with that tree, yielding a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to those conforming to a tree, the marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently via dynamic programming, in contrast to the IRM, which effectively deals with an exponential number of partitions. The authors propose a greedy agglomerative procedure to select the tree (hierarchy) and evaluate on Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network. They report that their method (BHCD) runs orders of magnitude faster than the IRM while achieving comparable or slightly better predictive performance.

Quality:
The modeling idea is sound and the computational contribution is clear: tree-consistent partitions enable tractable, exact marginal likelihood evaluation, and a carefully cached agglomerative search gives O(n^2 log n) worst-case runtime independent of the number of communities. The empirical comparison shows competitive AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy relative to the IRM, with substantial speedups. However, I am uncertain how the greedy tree-learning procedure relates to the stated Bayesian generative model. Because the search is greedy, it does not appear to perform posterior inference over trees or to correspond to a clear MAP/ML objective; if it does, this connection was not evident. Clarifying Section 4 to explain precisely what objective is optimized (and whether the procedure approximates posterior inference, a MAP tree, or a model selection criterion) would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
The paper would benefit from a thorough revision for grammar and style (e.g., around line 126), and from clearer prose in places (e.g., lines 47–50: what exactly are “local changes” and what “iterations” are being referenced—MCMC iterations?). The datasets are not sufficiently described for readers unfamiliar with them; brief summaries of Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data would help. Some mathematical presentation feels heavier than necessary (e.g., Eq. 5), and Figure 3 is hard to interpret without prior familiarity with the monastery dataset; clearer labeling or a more familiar example would improve accessibility. Section 4, which contains the core algorithmic contribution, required several readings to follow; more intuitive explanation and a small running example would help.

Originality:
The combination of a tree-structured prior over partitions with a blockmodel likelihood, coupled with an efficient agglomerative search using cached sufficient statistics, is a novel and appealing approach to hierarchical community discovery. The work is well positioned relative to the IRM and related hierarchical extensions.

Significance:
Hierarchical community structure is important in social networks, and the ability to learn such structure with significantly improved computational efficiency is valuable. The results suggest practitioners could obtain good predictive performance at a fraction of the computational cost of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.

Strengths:
- Clear and useful insight: tree-constrained partitions enable efficient marginal likelihood computation.
- Practical, scalable algorithm with O(n^2 log n) runtime independent of the number of communities.
- Strong empirical results: comparable or slightly better predictive performance than IRM at much lower runtime.
- Addresses a problem of broad interest to the NIPS community.

Weaknesses:
- The relationship between the greedy search and the Bayesian generative model is not clearly articulated; it is unclear whether the procedure corresponds to posterior inference or a principled model selection criterion.
- Writing and presentation issues (grammar, vague phrasing, heavy math in places) hinder readability.
- Dataset descriptions and figure interpretability (especially Figure 3) are insufficient for readers not already familiar with the datasets.
- Section 4, the core contribution, is difficult to parse and would benefit from clearer exposition and examples.

Recommendation:
If the authors substantially improve the clarity of Section 4, explain the connection between the search procedure and the Bayesian objective, fix the writing issues, and better describe datasets and figures, I believe the paper’s novelty and strong empirical performance would warrant publication.

Overall assessment:
A novel Bayesian model for hierarchical communities with an insightful, efficient agglomerative learning algorithm and strong empirical results, but in need of significant writing and exposition improvements.
