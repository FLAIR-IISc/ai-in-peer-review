This paper presents a compelling generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, aiming to infer hierarchical relationships between entities from observed network connections rather than a simple flat clustering. The authors propose a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to efficiently select a tree structure representing possible entity partitions. Empirical results on two social connectivity datasets demonstrate that their method achieves comparable or improved accuracy relative to the widely-used Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while offering significant speed improvements.

I believe this work addresses a significant and timely problem with strong relevance to the NIPS community. Bayesian nonparametric methods are theoretically attractive, but often suffer from computational cost. Simultaneously, social scientists are increasingly adopting sophisticated Bayesian models for network analysis, and a hierarchical extension to a popular model like the IRM is a valuable contribution. This paper effectively bridges these two areas.

The key insight – restricting the space of possible partitions to a tree structure – enables efficient marginal likelihood computation, a substantial advantage over the IRM’s need to consider an exponential number of partitions. However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative story requires clarification. The proposed algorithm’s greedy nature raises questions about whether it performs true posterior inference, or if it’s best characterized as a heuristic approximation (e.g., a point MAP or ML estimate). I recommend the authors address this point and improve the clarity of Section 4 accordingly.

The experimental results are impressive. Across several performance metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performs comparably to or slightly better than the IRM. While the absolute improvements in these scores may be modest, the reported orders-of-magnitude reduction in runtime compared to IRM inference is a compelling benefit.

The overall presentation, however, would benefit from revision. Numerous minor errors in spelling and grammar distract from the core ideas (e.g., line 126). Furthermore, the prose is occasionally unclear. For example, lines 47-50 of the introduction contain vague phrasing – terms like “local changes” and “iterations” are not well-defined in this context. The datasets used for evaluation (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not adequately described, potentially hindering understanding for readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical formulations, such as Equation 5, appear unnecessarily complex. Figure 3 is also visually unclear; without prior knowledge of the Monastery dataset, it’s difficult to discern which method is performing best. A more widely recognized dataset for illustration might improve clarity. Section 4, detailing the core contribution, required multiple readings to fully grasp. 

Despite these issues, the proposed approach exhibits sufficient novelty and the experimental results are strong enough to warrant publication, *provided* the authors address the clarity concerns outlined above. The work represents a thoughtful combination of agglomerative clustering and Bayesian modeling, offering an insightful learning algorithm and strong empirical validation. Improving the writing quality will significantly enhance the paper’s accessibility and impact.
