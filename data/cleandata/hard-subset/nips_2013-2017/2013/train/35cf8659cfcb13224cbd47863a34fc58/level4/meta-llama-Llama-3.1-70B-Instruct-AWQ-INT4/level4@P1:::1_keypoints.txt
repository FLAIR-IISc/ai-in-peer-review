- Proposes a hierarchical extension of the IRM using Bayesian rose tree ideas: hierarchy as a mixture over consistent partitions and greedy agglomerative learning.  
- Presents qualitative results (Sampson’s monks, full NIPS) and quantitative results (NIPS-234); inference is much cheaper than IRM with similar predictive performance.  
- Quality and clarity: very well written with clear exposition, accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian nonparametrics.  
- Originality: adapts BRT concepts to relational/network data while maintaining computational complexity — a notable adaptation.  
- Significance: provides a computationally efficient method for hierarchical community discovery, addressing BNP scaling challenges.  
- Concern: basic algorithm has n^2 scaling, which may be prohibitive for very large networks; sparse version may mitigate this.  
- Missing experiments: no timing results on larger real-world networks (e.g., runtime on full NIPS); unclear how inference scales to gene/social networks.  
- Evaluation gap: need to assess how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree (suggest exhaustive search at the largest feasible scale).  
- Theoretical gap: could strengthen the paper with theoretical support for the greedy method (e.g., submodularity).  
- Minor corrections: fix typos — "whilst... but"→"while... but"; "pair of treeS"→"pair of trees"; "A fruitful next steps"→"A fruitful next step".  
- Overall recommendation: contribution is significant despite familiarity with BRT ideas; paper should be accepted.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel IRM extension, computational efficiency/scalability, and demonstrated effectiveness on multiple datasets.  
- Arguments against acceptance: n^2 scaling concern, need for larger-scale experiments, and lack of theoretical justification.
