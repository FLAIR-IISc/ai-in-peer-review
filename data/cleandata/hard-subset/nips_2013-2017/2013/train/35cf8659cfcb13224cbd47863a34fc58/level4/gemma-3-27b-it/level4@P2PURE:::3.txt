This paper is well-written, with strong attention to detail in spelling, grammar, and citations – significantly above average in these respects. A minor stylistic note: in NIPS submissions, avoid beginning sentences with citations. The theoretical development is concise, notably through the consistent use of f() and g() notation.

The work extends the standard single-table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose tree theory, allowing for a simplification of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of IRM to the more direct equation (7). This factorization over subtrees parallels research on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior factorizes elegantly based on the chosen constrained structure class, facilitating greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction. 

Evaluation on Stochastic Block Models (SBM) is typically performed using “gold standard” data, leveraging known partition structures and clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) for comparison. The reported use of AUC was initially unclear, as I am more familiar with NMI-based SBM evaluations. I understand the approach involves deleting links and attempting to predict their reinstatement, but this process requires a more detailed explanation. Furthermore, recent SBM evaluations utilize larger datasets than those presented here (including the full NIPS dataset). While the evaluation in Figure 4 is a good starting point, results on a larger dataset would strengthen the analysis. Comparing solely against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient; a comparison to relevant semi-parametric methods – some of which are already acknowledged in the paper – is necessary. 

The key contribution of this work lies in the proposed agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. 

In summary, the paper presents a clear and theoretically sound extension of the IRM using Bayesian Rose trees, although the underlying concepts and algorithms are relatively simple. The experimental work, however, is more illustrative than comparative and, therefore, currently lacks sufficient depth.
