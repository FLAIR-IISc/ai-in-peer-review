### Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for identifying hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to replace the Chinese Restaurant Process prior on partitions, as used in the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), with a prior defined over a tree structure, drawing on the theory of Bayesian Rose Trees. This formulation models the data as a mixture over all partitions consistent with a given hierarchy.

Inference is performed not via MCMC but through a greedy agglomerative algorithm that builds the hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion. At each step, the algorithm merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor, resulting in a computationally efficient procedure. The authors claim this approach is significantly faster than sampling-based methods for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance. The theoretical complexity and empirical results on several datasets are presented to support these claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents its core concepts effectively. The theoretical development, particularly the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood in Equation (10) and the derivation of the merge score in Equation (12), is elegant and easy to follow. Table 1 provides a concise and compelling summary of the method's computational advantages.

2.  **Efficient Inference:** The main contribution is a fast, deterministic algorithm for a Bayesian hierarchical model. The agglomerative search procedure, which cleverly caches sufficient statistics for O(1) updates in the inner loop, provides a practical and scalable alternative to MCMC-based inference. The O(n² log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, is a significant advantage over many competing Bayesian methods.

3.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** The paper successfully combines the stochastic blockmodel (SBM) with the Bayesian Rose Tree framework. This is a novel approach for hierarchical community detection that allows for the exact calculation of the marginal likelihood over a constrained (tree-consistent) set of partitions, avoiding the approximation or sampling typically required.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental work, while illustrative, is not comprehensive enough to fully substantiate the paper's claims.
    *   **Baselines:** The comparison is almost exclusively against the IRM. To properly assess the trade-off between speed and accuracy, the authors should compare against a wider range of state-of-the-art methods, including the fast, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering techniques mentioned in the introduction (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004) and other scalable SBM inference methods.
    *   **Dataset Scale:** The main quantitative evaluation on link prediction (Figure 4) is performed on a relatively small network of 234 nodes. While the method's primary advantage is scalability, its predictive performance on larger networks (like the full NIPS dataset) is not quantitatively evaluated. A qualitative visualization is provided, but this does not demonstrate if the speed benefits translate to strong predictive performance on larger graphs where such efficiency is most needed.
    *   **Evaluation Protocol:** The evaluation protocol for SBMs often involves assessing cluster recovery (e.g., using Normalized Mutual Information) on synthetic networks with a known ground-truth partition structure. The authors instead focus on link prediction using AUC. While valid, the precise experimental setup for this is not explained. For instance, it is unclear how the held-out data was selected and how AUC was computed. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to interpret the results fully.

2.  **Context of Originality:** While the application of Bayesian Rose Trees to SBMs is novel, the underlying algorithmic principle—using a decomposable score to perform efficient greedy search over a constrained set of structures—is a well-established technique in related fields. For example, similar principles for factorizing posteriors are used for learning Bayesian network structures (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004). Acknowledging this connection would provide a more complete context for the paper's algorithmic contribution.

### Detailed Comments

*   The theoretical exposition is concise, for instance, by using the general functions `f(·)` and `g(·)`. While this keeps the derivation general, it might be helpful for readers to see the main equations (e.g., (10) and (12)) written out explicitly for the Beta-Bernoulli case to improve clarity.
*   The paper is generally very well written. As a minor stylistic point, it is conventional in NIPS style to avoid beginning sentences with a citation, such as "[9] and [10] propose...". This can be easily rephrased (e.g., "Methods have been proposed by [9, 10] that are limited to...").

In conclusion, this paper presents a clear, elegant, and computationally efficient algorithm for hierarchical community detection. The core contribution is a fast agglomerative inference scheme for a novel SBM variant. However, the experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous to firmly establish the method's performance relative to the broader state of the art, particularly on larger networks. The work is promising, but would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive and clearly described set of experiments.
