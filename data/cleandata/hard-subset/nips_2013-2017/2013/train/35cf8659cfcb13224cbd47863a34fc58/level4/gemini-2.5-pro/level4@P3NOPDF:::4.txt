This paper proposes a scalable, agglomerative approach for clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The method capably integrates the Bayesian rose tree prior [12] with a stochastic block model likelihood. The main contribution lies in adapting the efficient, greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to this network setting by developing a method for recursively updating sufficient statistics. While the presentation is generally clear and experiments show the benefits of a hierarchical model over a flat one, the paper's primary weakness is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network clustering models.

**Significance and Strengths**

The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. The proposed model is a sensible and well-motivated combination of the Bayesian rose tree framework and the stochastic block model, providing a principled method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. A key strength is the development of an efficient algorithm for this model. The derivation of an O(Nodes + Edges) likelihood calculation is a notable feature, and the ability to recursively update sufficient statistics allows the greedy agglomerative algorithm to scale. The qualitative experiment on the Sampson's monastery dataset is illustrative, and the quantitative comparison against the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively demonstrates the predictive advantages of modeling and averaging over a distribution of hierarchical structures compared to a flat community model.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite its strengths, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its impact.

1.  **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The most critical limitation is the experimental evaluation. By only comparing against a non-hierarchical baseline (the IRM), the experiments primarily serve to demonstrate that hierarchy is a useful modeling assumption for relational data. However, they fail to establish how the proposed method performs relative to other state-of-the-art hierarchical community detection models. The paper omits any discussion of or comparison to relevant Bayesian nonparametric approaches that also learn hierarchical latent structure. For instance, the "Multiscale Community Blockmodel" (Ho et al., 2012), which uses a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a prior over k-d trees, are both highly relevant and capable models. Without these crucial comparisons, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers a meaningful advantage over existing techniques.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** While the overall presentation is clear, certain key technical sections are difficult to follow. The presentation in Section 3, particularly surrounding Equation (9) and its dependent components, is quite dense. The notation could be simplified or supplemented with a more intuitive explanation to improve readability. Furthermore, the description of the efficient likelihood computation is insufficient. The authors allude to a dynamic programming approach before Equation (10) but do not provide enough detail for the reader to fully grasp its implementation. Similarly, while equations for the recursive updates of sufficient statistics are provided, the paper lacks a clear derivation or intuition to help the reader understand their correctness.

3.  **Originality:** The core conceptual components of the model—the prior over trees and the agglomerative search algorithm—are adopted directly from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The innovation is therefore focused on the integration of a standard block-model likelihood and the corresponding adaptation of the update rules for the sufficient statistics. While this is a valid contribution, the paper would be stronger if it more explicitly framed its novelty in this context.

4.  **Terminology:** The use of the term "nonparametric" is questionable. The authors of the original Bayesian rose tree paper [12] frame their approach as a form of Bayesian model selection over an unbounded space of tree structures, explicitly contrasting it with traditional nonparametric methods in their discussion. This reviewer agrees with their interpretation, and using the "nonparametric" label here may be misleading.

5.  **Scalability Claims:** The paper emphasizes scalability, but the experiments are conducted on networks of moderate size. The claims of scalability would be substantially more convincing if the method were demonstrated on at least one significantly larger real-world graph.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a solid integration of the Bayesian rose tree framework with a stochastic block model likelihood for networks. The authors successfully generalize an efficient agglomerative clustering algorithm to this new domain, which is a valuable engineering contribution. However, the lack of crucial experimental comparisons to other hierarchical network models severely limits the paper's ability to demonstrate a clear advance over the state-of-the-art. With improved clarity in its technical sections and a more thorough experimental evaluation, this work could represent a much stronger contribution.
