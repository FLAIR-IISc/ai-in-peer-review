This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks using a Bayesian nonparametric model. The model, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), combines a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on several datasets, including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with a clear presentation of the model and algorithm. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, including the use of conjugate priors and the dynamic programming method for computing the marginal likelihood of the tree. The experiments are also well-designed, with a comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and an evaluation of the model's performance on held-out data.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to scale to large networks, with a time complexity of O(n^2 log n) for the greedy agglomerative algorithm. The authors also provide a variation of the algorithm, called BHCD sparse, which is more efficient for sparse networks.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors do not provide a comparison to other hierarchical network models, such as the multiscale community blockmodel or the Mondrian process. This makes it difficult to evaluate the performance of the BHCD model relative to other state-of-the-art methods. Additionally, the authors do not provide a detailed explanation of the dynamic programming method, which may make it difficult for readers to understand the details of the algorithm.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on prior work on Bayesian rose trees and stochastic blockmodels. The innovation of the paper lies in the combination of these two approaches and the development of an efficient algorithm for computing the marginal likelihood of the tree. However, the use of the term "nonparametric" to describe the model is questionable, as it is not clear that the model meets the traditional definition of nonparametric.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis and community detection. The BHCD model is a powerful tool for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, and the authors' experiments demonstrate its effectiveness. However, further work is needed to evaluate the performance of the model relative to other state-of-the-art methods and to provide a more detailed explanation of the algorithm.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks.
* The model is well-written and technically sound, with a clear presentation of the algorithm and experiments.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on several datasets.
* The model is able to scale to large networks, with a time complexity of O(n^2 log n).

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not provide a comparison to other hierarchical network models.
* The authors do not provide a detailed explanation of the dynamic programming method.
* The use of the term "nonparametric" to describe the model is questionable.
* Further work is needed to evaluate the performance of the model relative to other state-of-the-art methods.
