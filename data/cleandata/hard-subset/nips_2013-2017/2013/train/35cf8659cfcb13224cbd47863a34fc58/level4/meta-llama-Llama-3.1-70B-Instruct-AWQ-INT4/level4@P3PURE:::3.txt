The paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations, significantly exceeding the average standard. However, it is worth noting that starting a sentence with a citation is not in line with the NIPS style.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are succinctly presented, with a clear and consistent notation, such as the use of f() and g() throughout the paper. The authors extend the simple single-table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This extension allows for the replacement of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of IRM with the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees follows the work on learning Bayesian network structures, as seen in Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior factorizes neatly based on the class of constrained structures chosen. This enables the use of greedy search, marginal likelihoods, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and other techniques.

The evaluation of stochastic block models (SBM) often relies on "gold standard" data with known partition structures. In such cases, metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering metrics can be used to compare against known clusterings. The authors report using Area Under the Curve (AUC) for evaluation, which is less common for SBM. It appears that the authors delete a few links and then attempt to predict their addition; however, this approach requires further explanation. Recent evaluations of SBM have been performed on larger datasets, and it would be beneficial to present results for a larger dataset in addition to the small evaluation shown in Figure 4. Furthermore, comparing only against Bayesian non-parametric methods is not sufficient, as there are many published semi-parametric methods, some of which are mentioned in the paper.

The primary contribution of this algorithm is the agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper extends the simple single-table instance of an IRM by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees, presenting good, clear theory and algorithms. However, the concept and algorithms are relatively simple. The experimental work is more illustrative than comparative, and therefore, not entirely adequate.
