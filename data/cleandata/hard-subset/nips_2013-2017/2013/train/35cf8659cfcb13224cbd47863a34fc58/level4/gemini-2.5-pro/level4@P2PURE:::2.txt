This paper proposes a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model. The goal is to infer a hierarchy of entities, rather than a flat clustering, from observed relational data. The authors introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree that constrains the space of possible partitions. They present empirical results on two social connectivity corpora, concluding that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely used baseline for this task.

The paper addresses a problem of significant interest to the NIPS community. The work is well-motivated, as it tackles two relevant challenges: the practical inefficiency often associated with Bayesian nonparametric methods and the growing demand from social scientists for more sophisticated tools for network analysis. A hierarchical extension to a popular network discovery model is therefore a timely contribution.

The paper's central insight is that by constraining partitions to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently, avoiding the need to consider the exponential number of partitions faced by the standard IRM. However, the connection between the proposed tree-learning procedure and the generative model is unclear. Specifically, because the agglomerative algorithm is greedy, it is not obvious how it relates to principled posterior inference over tree structures, or if it corresponds to a MAP or ML point estimate. I may have misunderstood this point, and I strongly recommend that the authors clarify their inference procedure in Section 4.

The empirical results are impressive. On several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performs comparably or slightly better than its closest competitor, the IRM. While the improvements in these scores are modest in absolute terms, the primary advantage is computational: the method is reportedly orders of magnitude faster than inference in the IRM.

The primary weakness of the paper is its overall clarity and quality of writing. The following issues should be addressed:
*   There are several distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126).
*   The prose can be difficult to follow. For example, the phrasing in lines 47-50 of the introduction is vague: it is unclear what "local changes" or "iterations" (e.g., of an MCMC algorithm?) refer to in this context.
*   The datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) are not described in sufficient detail, which may hinder readers unfamiliar with them.
*   Some mathematical notation, such as Equation 5, seems unnecessarily obtuse.
*   Figure 3 is confusing; for a reader unfamiliar with the monastery dataset, it is difficult to visually assess which method performs best. A more familiar dataset might be a better choice for visualization.
*   As mentioned earlier, Section 4, which details the paper's core contribution, required multiple readings to be understood.

Despite these concerns regarding presentation, the proposed approach is novel and the empirical results are strong. If the authors can thoroughly address the clarity issues, I believe the paper would be a valuable contribution and would warrant publication. In summary, the authors propose a novel model for relational data that can be viewed as a Bayesian hierarchical clustering method inspired by the IRM. The core learning algorithm is insightful and the experimental results are compelling, but the paper's potential is currently hindered by a need for significant improvement in the quality of its writing and presentation.
