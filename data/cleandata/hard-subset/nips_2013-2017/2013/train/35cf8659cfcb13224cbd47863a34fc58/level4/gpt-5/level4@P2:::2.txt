This paper introduces a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model represents a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where internal nodes correspond to communities and leaves to individual vertices. A dynamic-programming formulation enables exact evaluation of the marginal likelihood restricted to partitions consistent with a given tree. Learning proceeds via a greedy agglomerative procedure that merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor, yielding a full hierarchical clustering with associated predictive distributions. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and subsets of the NIPS co-authorship network indicate comparable or slightly better predictive performance than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being orders of magnitude faster.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on stochastic blockmodels and their mixed-membership variants, as well as Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees. It contrasts with IRM-based approaches, including hierarchical IRM extensions, which typically require expensive MCMC or iterative greedy search over an exponential partition space. By constraining partitions to those induced by a tree, the authors retain a probabilistic interpretation and obtain efficient, exact marginalization over the restricted partition family.

Key technical idea: Constrain the partition space to be tree-consistent and define a prior over partitions induced by a top-down generative process on the tree. This yields a factorized marginal likelihood that can be computed recursively. Learning uses a bottom-up agglomerative search over three merge operations (join and two absorptions), scoring each candidate via a Bayes factor based only on local sufficient statistics. Careful caching yields an overall O(n^2 log n) runtime independent of the number of communities; a “sparse” variant prohibits merges between disconnected subgraphs to further reduce computation on sparse networks.

Empirical findings: On the NIPS-234 network, the method achieves AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy comparable to or slightly better than IRM, reaching strong performance in seconds where IRM takes hundreds to thousands of seconds. On the monastery data, BHCD recovers interpretable groups aligned with prior analyses, with non-binary trees typically fitting better than binary-only alternatives.

Quality: The modeling assumptions and likelihood/prior factorization are sound, and the dynamic-programming derivation is appropriate. The agglomerative search is a reasonable model-selection strategy, and the local scoring rule is well-motivated computationally. However, the connection between the greedy procedure and Bayesian inference remains underspecified: it is not clear whether the algorithm is optimizing a well-defined objective (e.g., a tree-level marginal likelihood) or should be viewed as a heuristic. Clarifying this would strengthen the technical footing.

Clarity: The exposition is generally informative but would benefit from editing for grammar and precision. Several typos remain, and some passages are vague (e.g., references to “local changes” and “iterations” in the introduction—are these MCMC iterations?). The monastery and NIPS datasets should be described briefly for readers unfamiliar with them. Equations such as Eq. 5 could be motivated more intuitively or moved to an appendix. Figure 3 is hard to interpret without clearer captions and guidance on what constitutes a better outcome.

Originality: The combination of a tree-induced prior over blockmodel partitions with an efficient agglomerative Bayesian scoring scheme is novel and differentiates the work from both IRM variants and purely heuristic hierarchical clustering.

Significance: A fast, probabilistic method for hierarchical community discovery is valuable to both researchers and practitioners, especially given the speed–accuracy trade-offs versus IRM. The approach is likely to be used for exploratory analysis and link prediction in large networks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly articulate the objective optimized by the agglomerative procedure. Is the final tree a MAP estimate under the defined prior and marginal likelihood? If not, state the approximation and its implications.
- Clarify ambiguous phrasing in the introduction and elsewhere (e.g., define “local changes” and “iterations”).
- Briefly describe the datasets and what constitutes success on each; improve figure captions to aid interpretation.
- Explain the role of tie-breaking and restarts, and report variability across runs.
- Discuss hyperparameter choices and sensitivity; consider simple empirical Bayes or hyperparameter inference.
- Where possible, add ablations (e.g., with/without sparse constraint; binary vs non-binary) and report their impact on runtime and accuracy.

Pros:
- Addresses an important problem with a principled probabilistic approach.
- Key insight enables exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions and efficient computation.
- Strong empirical speedups with competitive predictive performance.
- Produces interpretable hierarchical structure.

Cons:
- The link between the greedy learning algorithm and Bayesian inference is not fully clarified.
- Writing quality and clarity need improvement; several typos and vague passages.
- Some figures and mathematical details are hard to follow without additional explanation.
- Limited discussion of sensitivity and robustness (hyperparameters, restarts).

Overall assessment: This is a timely and useful contribution that combines a novel modeling idea with a practical, scalable algorithm. With revisions to improve clarity and a clearer positioning of the learning procedure within the Bayesian framework, I would support acceptance.
