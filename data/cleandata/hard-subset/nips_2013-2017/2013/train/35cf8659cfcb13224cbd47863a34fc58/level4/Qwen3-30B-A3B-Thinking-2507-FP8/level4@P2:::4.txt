

The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient agglomerative method for learning hierarchical community structure in networks. BHCD extends Bayesian rose trees [12] to relational data by adapting the likelihood to a hierarchical stochastic blockmodel. It proposes greedy model selection that computes the marginal likelihood in O(n + m) time via dynamic programming, with worst-case runtime O(n² log n). The core innovation lies in efficiently caching sufficient statistics to enable O(1) updates during tree merges, significantly accelerating inference compared to existing Bayesian hierarchical methods.

**Quality & Technical Soundness**  
The technical approach is sound: the model correctly formalizes hierarchical community structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (Section 3), and the inference algorithm (Algorithm 1) efficiently computes marginal likelihoods using recursive sufficient statistics (Equations 9–13). The runtime analysis is rigorous, and experiments demonstrate two orders of magnitude speedup over IRM while maintaining comparable accuracy on NIPS co-authorship data (Section 5). However, the paper lacks critical comparisons to hierarchical Bayesian models, which undermines the assessment of technical novelty and significance. Specifically, it omits key works like Ho et al. (2012) *Multiscale Community Blockmodel* (which uses a nested CRP for hierarchical communities) and Roy et al. (2009) *Mondrian Process* (which defines a hierarchical tree prior via K-d trees). Without contrasting BHCD’s hierarchy discovery with these methods, it is unclear whether the approach offers meaningful advantages over existing hierarchical Bayesian nonparametric models.

**Clarity & Presentation**  
The paper is exceptionally clear in its core algorithmic contributions (Sections 3–4), with well-structured pseudocode and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The presentation of the marginal likelihood recursion (Equation 10) and sufficient statistic updates (Equation 13) is a strength. However, Section 3’s notation for tree-consistent partitions (Equation 5) and the derivation of σ¬chSS (Equation 9) is dense. A simpler notation or concrete example would aid readability. The qualitative analysis of Sampson’s monastery network (Figure 3) is well-executed, and the scalability claims are convincingly supported by runtime comparisons (Table 1).

**Originality**  
The model itself is not novel: it directly combines Bayesian rose trees [12] with a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The primary innovation is the efficient recursive update of sufficient statistics for network data, which enables scalability. Yet, the paper mischaracterizes this as "nonparametric" (Section 3), while Blundell et al. [12] explicitly distinguish rose trees (model selection-based) from nonparametric methods. This terminology is misleading and should be corrected. Crucially, the *lack of comparison to hierarchical Bayesian baselines* (Ho et al. 2012, Roy et al. 2009) prevents establishing whether BHCD’s hierarchical structure is genuinely novel or merely a scaling improvement over existing methods.

**Significance**  
The significance is partially realized: BHCD’s speed (O(n² log n) vs. IRM’s O(n²K²)) makes hierarchical community discovery feasible for moderate networks, and the scalability claim is well-supported (Section 5). However, the significance of *hierarchical structure itself* is underexplored. The experiments show that modeling hierarchy improves predictions over flat models (IRM), but do not demonstrate that BHCD’s *hierarchical approach* outperforms other hierarchical methods. Without this comparison, the paper fails to establish whether the proposed method advances the state of the art in *hierarchical* community discovery. The qualitative analysis of the full NIPS network (Figure 5) is promising but not rigorously evaluated against hierarchical baselines.

**Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance**  
*Pro:*  
- Clear, technically sound algorithm with rigorous runtime analysis.  
- Significant speedup over IRM (2 orders of magnitude) for hierarchical discovery.  
- Effective experimental validation on real-world networks (Sampson, NIPS-234).  
- Well-written presentation of core contributions (Sections 3–4).  

*Con:*  
- Critical omission of comparisons to hierarchical Bayesian models (Ho et al. 2012, Roy et al. 2009), undermining claims of novelty and significance.  
- Misuse of "nonparametric" terminology (contradicts Blundell et al. [12]).  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations (e.g., scalability on very sparse networks beyond NIPS-234).  
- Theoretical novelty is minimal; the paper builds directly on [12] with a network-specific likelihood adaptation.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a technically valid contribution to *scalable* hierarchical community discovery, but its significance is diminished by the lack of comparison to hierarchical Bayesian baselines. To strengthen the work, the authors should: (1) add experiments against Ho et al. (2012) and Roy et al. (2009); (2) clarify the "nonparametric" claim; and (3) discuss how BHCD’s hierarchy differs from these methods. Without these revisions, the paper’s novelty is overstated, and its significance is not fully justified. As-is, it is a solid incremental contribution but not a compelling advance for NIPS. I recommend *weak rejection* pending major revisions addressing these gaps.
