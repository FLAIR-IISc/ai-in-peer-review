The authors present a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, designed to uncover hierarchical relationships within entities based on observed network connections. Their approach employs an agglomerative clustering algorithm to efficiently explore the space of possible entity partitions, represented as a tree structure. Empirical evaluation on two social network datasets demonstrates that their method achieves comparable or improved accuracy to the widely-used Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while offering significantly faster computation.

This work addresses a compelling problem with relevance to a broad segment of the NIPS community. While Bayesian nonparametric methods offer conceptual advantages, their practical computational cost is often a limitation. Simultaneously, the increasing adoption of sophisticated Bayesian models within the social sciences creates a need for extensions to established network discovery techniques. This paper directly engages with both of these points.

A key innovation lies in leveraging the tree structure to enable efficient marginal likelihood computation, a significant advantage over the IRM’s need to consider an exponential number of partitions. However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model was not entirely clear to me. The greedy nature of the algorithm raises questions about its ability to perform true posterior inference or approximate a maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate. Clarification of this aspect in Section 4 would be beneficial. 

The reported results are promising. The proposed method achieves competitive performance with the IRM across multiple metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), and notably, exhibits substantially faster runtime – a potentially crucial advantage. 

However, the manuscript would benefit from improvements in clarity and presentation. Numerous grammatical and spelling errors detract from the reading experience (e.g., line 126). Furthermore, certain passages, such as lines 47-50 in the introduction, are vaguely worded and require more precise definitions (e.g., "local changes," "iterations"). A more thorough description of the datasets (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) is also necessary for readers unfamiliar with these resources. Certain mathematical expressions (e.g., Eq. 5) appear overly complex, and Figure 3 is difficult to interpret, particularly for those not familiar with the Monastery dataset; an alternative, more widely-recognized dataset might improve comprehension. Section 4, detailing the core contribution, requires multiple readings for full understanding.

Despite these issues, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the results justify publication, pending revisions to address the identified clarity and presentation concerns.
