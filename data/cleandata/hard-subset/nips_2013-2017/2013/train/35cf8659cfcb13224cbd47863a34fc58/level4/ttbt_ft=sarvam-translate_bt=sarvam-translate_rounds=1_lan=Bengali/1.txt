The authors have presented a hierarchical extension of IRM for network modeling, using some key concepts from the Bayesian rose tree research paper: 1) The hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over a continuous partition of nodes. 2) This hierarchy can be learned through an efficient greedy agglomerative approach. Qualitative results have been presented on the Sampson's monks dataset, the complete NIPS dataset, and the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed assumption is much less expensive than IRM, while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity: The paper is very well written, and the explanation of the key concepts is clear.

Originality: The main concepts are taken from work on the Bayesian rose tree, but some work has been done to adapt these concepts to relational data settings, especially to maintain the same computational complexity.

Importance: Scaling BNP models, especially network models, is a significant challenge if these methods are used by users. Although the n^2 scaling of the basic algorithm can be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version will likely be effective. It would be good to see some time scales in larger real-world networks. It takes a few seconds for NIPS-234: how long does it take for the complete NIPS? Gene networks typically have thousands of nodes, and real social networks are much larger: how will the assumption scale?

Also, it would be good to know how close the greedy method is to an optimal tree. It can be tested on the largest scale to see if you can still find an optimal tree by exhaustive search. Is it possible to provide any theoretical support for the method? For example, in the case of Subramodularity?
