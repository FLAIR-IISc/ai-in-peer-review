- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model using rose-tree priors and SBMs to discover hierarchically nested communities.
- Restricts partitions to tree-consistent ones with a stop/continue prior favoring non-binary (rose) trees.
- Uses conjugate Beta-Bernoulli likelihoods and dynamic programming to compute marginal likelihood for a fixed tree in O(n + m) time.
- Learns trees via greedy agglomerative merges (join and two absorption variants) scored by local Bayes factors.
- Caching yields O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time per run, independent of discovered community count.
- Provides sparse/binary variants, restarts for tie-breaking, and a recursive predictive distribution for link prediction.
- Connects rose-tree priors with IRM-like blockmodel likelihoods, trading expressive power for speed.
- Conceptual novelty: combining rose trees with SBMs and an efficient subtree-local agglomerative search is novel.
- Clear, well-organized exposition and compact notation aid understanding and reproducibility.
- Exact marginalization for fixed trees and a simple model that captures hierarchies are strengths.
- Empirical results show large speedups over IRM variants with competitive AUC/accuracy on tested datasets.
- Practical predictive scheme with cost linear in tree depth is useful for large trees.
- Evaluation scope is narrow; missing comparisons to strong non-BNP baselines (e.g., modularity, spectral, degree-corrected SBMs).
- Link prediction protocol is underspecified—needs details on held-out fraction, negative sampling, folds, stratification, and tuning.
- Missing quantitative evaluations on the full NIPS network and larger public graphs to substantiate scalability claims.
- Stability concerns: relies on greedy merges and tie-breaking; lacks analysis of variability across restarts or model-averaging.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is unaddressed; priors and γ are fixed with no robustness or empirical Bayes assessment.
- Fairness of IRM baselines is unclear—need clarification of implementations, initialization, iterations, and matched computational budgets.
- Minor clarity/notation issues (citation placement, undefined symbols, symmetry conventions); suggest schematic for merge types.
- Methodologically sound: prior/likelihood factorization and dynamic programming are correct; merge scoring is a reasonable local approximation.
- Significance is moderate: strong on efficiency and exploratory utility but limited by empirical evidence for predictive/recovery performance.
- Pros: elegant, efficient algorithm with exact marginalization and practical impact.
- Cons: limited empirical breadth, little analysis of stability/sensitivity, and need for larger-scale quantitative results.
- Recommended actions: specify link-prediction protocol and recovery metrics (NMI/ARI), add larger datasets and baselines, report restart variability and hyperparameter sensitivity, clarify IRM settings, and fix minor editorial issues.
- Overall: solid algorithmic contribution that would benefit substantially from a broader, clearer empirical evaluation.
