

This paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical clustering of networks into communities. The core model—based on a prior over non-binary trees and an algorithm structure—remains largely unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted straightforwardly for networks, with additional effort ensuring efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear, and experiments show favorable results compared to a flat-community baseline. However, the work lacks comparisons to existing hierarchical network models, a significant limitation.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:**  
The paper is well-written and technically sound, though Section 3 is dense. The notation and explanation of Equation (9) and its extensions could be simplified. Crucially, the experimental comparisons are restricted to non-hierarchical models, demonstrating only that hierarchical modeling benefits relational data—not that the proposed method outperforms existing hierarchical approaches. Two relevant Bayesian nonparametric models are omitted: Ho et al. (2012) on the multiscale community blockmodel (using a nested Chinese Restaurant Process) and Roy et al. (2009) on the Mondrian Process (which defines a hierarchical prior over K-d trees). These methods could discover more refined latent structures, and their absence weakens the claim of novelty.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:**  
The core contribution lies in adapting sufficient statistics for recursive updates within the greedy agglomerative clustering, driven by the new likelihood. While the O(Nodes + Edges) computational efficiency is a strength, the description of this mechanism is inadequate. The allusion to dynamic programming before Equation (10) is vague, and the recursive update equations lack intuitive justification. Readers are left unclear on *why* the updates are valid, hindering reproducibility and understanding.

**TERMINOLOGY:**  
The use of "nonparametric" is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] explicitly frame the rose tree approach within model selection, contrasting it with nonparametric methods. The current paper aligns with this interpretation, making the terminology inconsistent.

**EXPERIMENTS:**  
The qualitative Sampson experiment is adequate, and the comparison to the independent relational model (IRM) effectively shows benefits of hypothesis averaging. However, the scalability discussion lacks context: none of the tested networks are large-scale. More critically, the absence of benchmarks against hierarchical models (e.g., Ho et al. 2012, Roy et al. 2009) prevents assessing whether the method advances the state of the art in hierarchical network analysis.

**OVERVIEW:**  
The work capably integrates Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block models for hierarchical community discovery, enabling efficient agglomerative clustering. While technically sound and well-presented, its primary limitation is the lack of comparison to established hierarchical network models. Without such comparisons, the paper fails to establish whether its approach offers meaningful advantages over existing methods designed specifically for hierarchical structure. The omission of key related work (Ho et al. 2012, Roy et al. 2009) further diminishes its contribution.
