The paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations, making it well above average in terms of presentation. One minor suggestion is to avoid starting sentences with citations, as per the NIPS style. The theoretical development is succinct and effectively utilizes notation, such as f() and g() throughout the paper.

This work extends the simple single-table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees, allowing for a simpler formulation of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) as shown in equation (7). The factoring over subtrees is reminiscent of learning Bayesian network structures, as seen in the work of Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior distribution can be neatly factorized based on the class of constrained structures chosen. This enables efficient computation of greedy search, marginal likelihoods, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and other tasks.

The evaluation of stochastic block models (SBM) often relies on "gold standard" data with known partition structures, where clustering metrics such as Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) can be used for comparison. While the paper reports using the Area Under the Curve (AUC) metric, it is not immediately clear how this is applied in the context of SBM evaluation, as AUC is not typically used for this purpose. It appears that the approach involves deleting links and then attempting to recover them, but a more detailed explanation is necessary to clarify this point.

Furthermore, recent evaluations of SBM have been conducted on larger datasets, exceeding the size of the full NIPS dataset used in this work. Therefore, it would be beneficial to present results on a larger dataset to demonstrate the scalability of the proposed method. Additionally, the comparison to Bayesian non-parametric methods is not sufficient, as there are many published semi-parametric methods that should also be considered, some of which are mentioned in the paper.

The main contribution of this algorithm is the agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper provides good, clear theory and algorithms, although the concept and algorithms themselves are relatively simple. However, the experimental work is primarily illustrative rather than comparative, and therefore not entirely adequate.
