The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, incorporating key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper. Specifically, they utilize a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and propose an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure for learning this hierarchy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Notably, the proposed inference method is computationally cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality and Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The authors have done an excellent job of presenting their work in a concise and understandable manner.

Originality: Although the main ideas are borrowed from the Bayesian rose tree work, the authors have made a significant effort to adapt these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity. This adaptation is not trivial and demonstrates the authors' understanding of the underlying concepts.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially network models, is an essential challenge if these methods are to be widely adopted by practitioners. While the basic algorithm's n^2 scaling may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. However, it would be beneficial to include timings on larger real-world networks to demonstrate the method's scalability. For instance, the authors mention that the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process, but they do not provide information on the computational time required for the full NIPS dataset. Furthermore, gene networks typically consist of tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are even larger; it is crucial to investigate how inference would scale in these scenarios.

Additionally, it would be valuable to assess how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be explored at the largest scale where the optimal tree can still be found through exhaustive search. Providing theoretical support for the method, such as in terms of submodularity, would also be desirable.

In conclusion, while the ideas presented in this paper are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is still significant. The authors have made a substantial effort to adapt these ideas to the relational data setting, and their work should be recognized. This paper should be accepted.
