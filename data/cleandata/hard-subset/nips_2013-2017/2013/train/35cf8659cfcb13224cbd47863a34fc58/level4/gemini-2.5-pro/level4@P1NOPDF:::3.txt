The paper is well-written, with clear prose and correct citations, making it easy to follow. The theoretical exposition is particularly succinct. A minor stylistic point is that sentences should generally not begin with a citation in the NIPS format.

This paper addresses the problem of learning stochastic block models (SBMs) by extending the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The core contribution is the integration of Bayesian Rose Trees, which elegantly replaces the standard Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation with the simpler and more direct objective in Equation (7). The factorization over subtrees is a sound approach that builds upon established work in learning Bayesian network structures (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004). This factorization enables efficient greedy search, calculation of marginal likelihoods, and straightforward handling of sufficient statistics for tasks like link prediction. The proposed agglomerative method, which is the central algorithmic contribution, is summarized well in Table 1.

The experimental evaluation, however, raises several significant concerns.

First, the choice of AUC as the primary metric is unconventional for SBMs, where performance is typically measured against a gold-standard partition using clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI). The paper should explicitly describe how AUC is calculated; I presume it is for a link prediction task on a held-out set of edges, but this setup must be clarified for the results to be interpretable.

Second, the scale of the datasets is limited. While the results in Figure 4 are illustrative, current state-of-the-art evaluations for SBMs are typically performed on much larger graphs than those presented here, including the full NIPS co-authorship graph. Demonstrating the method's scalability and performance on larger data is crucial to establishing its practical utility.

Third, the set of baselines is not comprehensive. The comparison is restricted to Bayesian non-parametric methods, omitting many relevant and high-performing semi-parametric approaches, some of which are already cited in the paper. A more thorough comparison is needed to properly situate the proposed method's performance within the broader literature.

In summary, the paper presents a clear and elegant extension of the IRM using Bayesian Rose Trees, resulting in a novel agglomerative inference algorithm. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the core concept and resulting algorithms are relatively straightforward. The primary weakness of the paper is its empirical evaluation, which feels more illustrative than comparative. Due to the concerns regarding the evaluation metric, dataset scale, and limited baselines, the experiments are not sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the proposed method's advantages.
