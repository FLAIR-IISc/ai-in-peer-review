The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, leveraging key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper. Specifically, they utilize the hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and learn this hierarchy using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The paper demonstrates qualitative results on Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset.

The proposed model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), is a tree-structured mixture of potentially exponentially many stochastic blockmodels. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that can learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model in a single pass through the data. Notably, BHCD's worst-case run-time scales quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but is independent of the number of nested communities. In practice, the authors report that BHCD is two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM while achieving comparable or better accuracy.

The paper is well-written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their model, including the stochastic blockmodel, the Bayesian hierarchical community model, and the agglomerative model selection procedure. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of BHCD in discovering hierarchical community structure and making predictions about unobserved connectivity in social networks.

In terms of quality and clarity, the paper is strong. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and algorithms, making it easy to follow their reasoning. The writing is free of major errors, and the notation is consistent throughout the paper.

Regarding originality, while the main ideas are taken from the Bayesian rose tree work, the authors have done significant work in adapting these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity. The extension of the IRM to hierarchical community discovery is a notable contribution, and the authors' efficient inference procedure is a significant improvement over existing methods.

The significance of this work lies in its ability to scale Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models to larger networks, which is an important challenge in the field. While the basic algorithm's n^2 scaling may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. The authors' results on the NIPS-234 dataset demonstrate the potential of BHCD for real-world applications.

However, there are some areas for improvement. It would be beneficial to see some timings on larger real-world networks to better understand the scalability of BHCD. Additionally, investigating how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree by exhaustive search could provide valuable insights into the method's performance. Providing theoretical support for the method, such as in terms of submodularity, would also strengthen the paper.
