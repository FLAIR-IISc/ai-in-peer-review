The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to the hierarchical setting, in order to infer a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. This approach addresses an interesting issue that a significant fraction of the NIPS audience will be interested in, as Bayesian nonparametric methods are often attractive from a conceptual point of view, but can be slow in practice.

The paper tackles an important problem in the domain of social network analysis, where scientists are increasingly using sophisticated Bayesian models for analyzing data. A hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be appreciated by this audience. The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. This is a significant improvement over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which must essentially consider the exponential number of possible partitions of the data.

However, the relationship between the learning procedure of the trees and the generative story behind the Bayesian model could be clarified. Since the procedure is greedy, it is unclear whether it can be seen as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or finding some kind of point MAP or ML estimate. The authors should try to improve the clarity of Section 4 to address this issue.

The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed method performing comparably or slightly better than the IRM in terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive llh, accuracy). While the asymptotic improvements in these scores are perhaps not that impressive in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

To improve the overall clarity and writing quality, the authors should address several issues. First, there are distracting spelling and grammar errors throughout the text. Second, the prose is sometimes hard to understand, with vague phrasing and unclear references. For instance, the authors do not clearly describe the content of the datasets they train on (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), which could confuse readers who are not already familiar with them. Additionally, some of the math is somewhat obtuse and unnecessary, such as Eq. 5. Finally, Figure 3 is confusing, and the authors may consider using a more familiar dataset to illustrate their results.

In terms of novelty and results, the proposed approach has enough merit to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. The core learning algorithm is insightful, and the experimental results are strong. However, the quality of writing needs to be improved to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an interesting and important problem in the domain of social network analysis.
* The proposed approach is novel and has enough merit to warrant publication.
* The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed method performing comparably or slightly better than the IRM.
* The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The writing quality needs to be improved to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* The relationship between the learning procedure of the trees and the generative story behind the Bayesian model could be clarified.
* Some of the math is somewhat obtuse and unnecessary.
* Figure 3 is confusing, and the authors may consider using a more familiar dataset to illustrate their results.
