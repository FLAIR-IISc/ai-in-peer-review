The paper is well-written, with clear prose, correct grammar, and proper citations, placing it well above average in terms of presentation. As a minor stylistic point, the authors should avoid beginning sentences with citations to better adhere to the NIPS style. The theoretical exposition is concise, at times perhaps overly so, as exemplified by the use of generic function notations like f() and g() throughout the paper.

The paper addresses stochastic block models (SBMs) by extending the single-table instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) with the theory of Bayesian Rose Trees. A key advantage of this approach is that it replaces the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of the IRM with the simpler expression in Equation (7). The factorization over subtrees is analogous to prior work on learning Bayesian network structures (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3). In that context, the posterior factorizes cleanly for certain classes of constrained structures, which similarly enables efficient greedy search, computation of marginal likelihoods, management of sufficient statistics, and link prediction in the proposed model.

The experimental evaluation has several weaknesses. SBMs are typically evaluated against gold-standard datasets with known partitions using clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI). The authors instead report Area Under the Curve (AUC), a choice that is not immediately obvious for this task and requires clarification. I presume this was calculated via a link prediction task where a subset of links was held out, but this methodology is not explained and must be explicitly detailed. Furthermore, the scale of the experiments is a concern. While the results in Figure 4 are informative, recent SBM literature features evaluations on much larger datasets. The experimental validation would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the method's performance on a larger-scale problem. Finally, the comparison to only Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient. The authors should also include comparisons against relevant semi-parametric approaches, some of which are already cited in the paper, to provide a more comprehensive performance analysis.

In summary, the paper's main contribution is a clear and straightforward agglomerative method, which is well-summarized in Table 1. While the theory and algorithms for extending IRMs are cleanly presented, the concept itself is simple. This simplicity is not supported by a sufficiently rigorous evaluation; the experimental work is more illustrative than comparative and is therefore not adequate to fully demonstrate the method's value.
