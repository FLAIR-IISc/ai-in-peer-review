- Proposal: Introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), extending the IRM with a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community discovery.
- Inference: Uses a greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm that processes the network in a single pass, avoiding iterative inference and depth restrictions.
- Theory: Mathematical formulation is sound and clearly presented (eqs. 2–8), with a tree-structured prior enabling efficient inference.
- Algorithm design: Algorithm 1 is well-designed, with time complexity analysis O(n² log n) and a novel caching scheme yielding O(1) inner-loop updates.
- Empirical speed: Experiments show BHCD is ~100× faster than IRM while achieving comparable or better accuracy.
- Model variations: Authors discuss meaningful variants (BHCD sparse, binary restrictions) and trade-offs.
- Clarity: Writing, notation, figures, and organization are excellent and relate well to prior NIPS work.
- Originality: Novel combination of tree-structured priors and SBMs in a Bayesian hierarchical framework; significant technical contribution despite prior greedy clustering work.
- Significance: Method is practically useful for large-scale network analysis due to efficiency; demonstrated on real networks (Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship).
- Limited datasets: Experimental evaluation is narrow (only two datasets, NIPS-234 is small), weakening empirical claims.
- Missing baselines: No comparison to strong non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods known to perform well ([3,4,17]).
- Evaluation clarity: AUC computation and experimental setup (edge deletion/prediction vs. clustering metrics like NMI) are insufficiently explained.
- Qualitative results: Sampson monastery results are qualitative without quantitative comparison to known four-group gold standard.
- Marginal accuracy gains: NIPS-234 shows only marginal predictive improvement over IRM, undermining claims of better accuracy.
- Limitations under-discussed: Paper does not sufficiently analyze failure modes (e.g., merging disconnected communities) or the robustness of tie-breaking strategies.
- Recommendation: Technically strong and worth accepting if authors expand experiments, clarify evaluation metrics, and discuss limitations in the camera-ready version.
