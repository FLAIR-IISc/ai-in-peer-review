- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model using tree-structured priors and marginal likelihoods over tree-consistent partitions.  
- Adapts the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) framework with conjugate blockmodel likelihoods (e.g., Beta–Bernoulli) and a prior favoring non-binary trees for weak hierarchies.  
- Inference via greedy agglomerative model selection with three merge types (join + two absorptions) using local Bayes factors to preserve BRT’s computational profile.  
- Algorithm worst-case runtime O(n^2 log n) (independent of community count) and tree-likelihood DP evaluation in O(n + m); sparse variant avoids merging disconnected components.  
- Experiments: qualitative recovery on Sampson’s network, predictive performance on NIPS-234 competitive with LFR and better than IRM while much faster, and a large hierarchy on full NIPS coauthorship.  
- Extends IRM-like relational models to hierarchical structure by mixing over tree-consistent partitions and using BRT-style greedy agglomeration.  
- Technical formulation and inference are clearly specified; DP recursion and local Bayes factor scoring are well motivated.  
- Empirical claims on runtime and scalability are plausible and supported by timing plots on NIPS-234, though larger-scale results are missing.  
- Suggest reporting absolute runtimes for full NIPS and at least one larger real-world sparse network to better demonstrate scalability.  
- Paper is well written and clear; generative process, recursion, and merge operators are transparently described.  
- Originality: core methods derive from BRT, but adapting them to relational blockmodels with preserved computational guarantees is a meaningful engineering contribution.  
- Significance: scales Bayesian nonparametric network models practically and shows good speed–accuracy trade-offs vs IRM, though O(n^2 log n) may limit very large graphs.  
- Questions for authors: report full NIPS end-to-end time and memory, tests on >10^4 nodes, greedy approximation quality vs optimal trees, theoretical support for greedy objective, stability across restarts, and hyperparameter sensitivity/heuristics.  
- Pros: clear adaptation of BRT to relational data, strong speedups vs IRM with competitive predictive performance, and interpretable hierarchical outputs.  
- Cons: limited large-scale empirical evaluation, no theoretical guarantees for greedy search, and novelty mainly in adaptation/engineering over prior BRT ideas.  
- Minor corrections: several typos and reference fixes (e.g., capitalization, grammar, “Physical Review E”, “Eric P. Xing”).  
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested additional large-scale experiments and analysis of greedy search optimality to strengthen the paper.
