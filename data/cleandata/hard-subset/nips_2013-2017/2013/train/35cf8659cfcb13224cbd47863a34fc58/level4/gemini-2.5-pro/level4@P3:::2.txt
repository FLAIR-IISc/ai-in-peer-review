This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to define a prior over network partitions that is constrained by a tree structure, where leaves represent entities (vertices) and internal nodes represent nested communities. This restriction allows the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree to be computed efficiently via dynamic programming. The authors propose a greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to perform model selection over the space of possible trees. This algorithm iteratively merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor, resulting in a single hierarchical structure. The method is evaluated on two social network datasets, Sampson's Monastery and the NIPS co-authorship network. The results demonstrate that BHCD achieves predictive performance comparable or superior to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being substantially faster, with reported speed-ups of two orders of magnitude.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a significant challenge in machine learning and computational social science: scalable inference for complex network models. While Bayesian nonparametric methods like the IRM are conceptually elegant, their computational cost often limits their application to small or medium-sized networks. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster than a standard baseline while maintaining competitive accuracy, this work represents a valuable practical contribution that researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon.

2.  **Originality:** The approach is a novel and clever combination of existing ideas. It extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller and Ghahramani (2005) to relational data and formulates the learning problem as a greedy model selection procedure. The insight that restricting partitions to be tree-consistent makes the marginal likelihood tractable for this data type is the key contribution. The use of a local Bayes factor to guide the agglomerative search is also an elegant formulation.

3.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The comparison with the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship network is particularly compelling. Achieving comparable AUC, accuracy, and predictive log-likelihood scores in seconds, when the IRM requires thousands of seconds, is an impressive result that clearly demonstrates the significance of the proposed method's efficiency.

**Weaknesses / Arguments for Rejection**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The primary weakness of this paper is its clarity. The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and a lack of detail that makes it difficult to fully understand. This obscures the technical contributions and detracts from the paper's overall quality. Specific issues are detailed below.

2.  **Inference Procedure Justification:** The connection between the probabilistic model in Section 3 and the greedy learning algorithm in Section 4 could be made clearer. As the algorithm is a greedy search, it is not performing true posterior inference over the distribution of trees. The authors should explicitly state that the goal is to find a single, high-quality tree structure that explains the data well, and briefly discuss the limitations of a greedy approach (e.g., sensitivity to local optima) compared to MCMC-based inference.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Writing Quality:** The paper requires a thorough proofreading pass to correct numerous grammatical and spelling errors (e.g., "Our algorithms scale quadratically" in the abstract, "rose trees typically better explain the data" in Section 5). The prose is often dense and could be simplified. For instance, the description of other methods' drawbacks in the introduction (lines 47-50) is vague. Phrases like "make many small changes" and "large number of iterations" could be replaced with more precise terms like "rely on local MCMC proposals" and "require many Gibbs sampling iterations to converge."

*   **Clarification of the Algorithm (Section 4):** This section describes the paper's main algorithmic contribution but is challenging to follow. The definition of the set of tree-consistent partitions in Equation 5 is formal but not intuitive; a clearer textual explanation or a simple illustrative example would greatly aid comprehension. Similarly, more intuition is needed to explain how the merge score in Equation 12 is calculated and how the three merge types (join, absorb left, absorb right) are chosen.

*   **Dataset Descriptions:** The datasets are not sufficiently described for readers who are not already familiar with them. The paper should include a brief description of the Sampson's Monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks, explaining what the nodes and edges represent.

*   **Figure Clarity:** Figure 3 is difficult to interpret. For a reader unfamiliar with the monastery dataset, it is not visually clear which method is performing best or what the discovered block structures signify in relation to the known ground truth. The authors should consider annotating the figure to highlight the known groups or providing a more quantitative evaluation of the discovered hierarchies for this dataset.

**Summary**

The authors propose a novel, efficient, and effective method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is insightful, and the experimental results demonstrating a significant speed-up over the IRM are a strong contribution. However, the paper is currently hampered by significant issues with clarity and presentation. The writing needs substantial revision to improve grammar and flow, and key parts of the methodology require better explanation. If the authors can thoroughly address these clarity issues, this paper would represent a solid contribution to the NIPS conference.
