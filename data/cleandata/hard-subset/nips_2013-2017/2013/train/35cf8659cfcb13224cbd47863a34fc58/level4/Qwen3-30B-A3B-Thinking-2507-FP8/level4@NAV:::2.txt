

The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting for inferring hierarchical entity groupings from observed link data, rather than flat clusterings. They introduce a learning algorithm using agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid partitions of entities. Empirical results on two social connectivity datasets demonstrate that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely adopted model for such tasks.  

This paper addresses an issue of significant interest to a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. Technically, while Bayesian nonparametric methods are conceptually appealing, they often suffer from high computational costs in practice. Domain-wise, social scientists increasingly employ sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis, making a hierarchical extension to a prominent network-discovery model timely and valuable. The paper engages with both aspects.  

The key insight lies in efficiently computing the marginal likelihood by restricting partitions to tree structures, avoiding the exponential partition space considered by IRM. However, I remain uncertain about how the greedy tree-learning procedure aligns with the Bayesian generative model’s posterior inference. It does not appear to perform true posterior inference over tree distributions, nor does it yield a MAP or ML estimate. Clarification of Section 4 is recommended.  

The results are compelling: the proposed method achieves comparable or slightly superior performance to IRM across metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), with runtime improvements of orders of magnitude. While absolute performance gains are modest, the speed advantage is substantial.  

The paper’s clarity and writing require significant improvement. Notable issues include distracting spelling/grammar errors (e.g., line 126), vague phrasing in the introduction (lines 47–50: unclear references to "local changes" and "iterations"), insufficient dataset descriptions (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship), unnecessarily obtuse mathematics (e.g., Eq. 5), and an unclear Figure 3. Section 4, containing the core contribution, demanded multiple readings for comprehension.  

Addressing these issues would strengthen the paper substantially. Despite the writing shortcomings, the proposed approach demonstrates sufficient novelty and the results are robust enough to warrant publication. The authors present a novel graph-edge model as a Bayesian variant of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM, with an insightful core algorithm and strong empirical results. The writing quality, however, needs improvement.
