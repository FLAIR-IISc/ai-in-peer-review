- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model using trees to define mixtures over tree-consistent partitions for hierarchical community discovery in networks.  
- Uses conjugate beta-Bernoulli blockmodels with different priors for within- and between-cluster edges and computes exact marginal likelihood via dynamic programming.  
- Inference via greedy agglomerative merges (join and absorption) scored by local Bayes factors with cached sufficient statistics for efficiency; sparse variant forbids merging disconnected subtrees.  
- Prediction for unobserved links computed by recursive traversal of the learned hierarchy.  
- Builds directly on Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT) and adapts those ideas to relational/stochastic blockmodel data.  
- Compared empirically to IRM (MCMC and greedy) and related to LFRM/ILA; shows qualitative hierarchies on Sampson and full NIPS, and quantitative results on NIPS-234.  
- Demonstrates comparable or better predictive performance than IRM at much lower computational cost, with worst-case O(n^2 log n) inference independent of number of communities.  
- Strength: clear, coherent exposition and well-motivated model construction.  
- Strength: elegant reuse of BRT to obtain a tractable mixture over partitions for relational data.  
- Strength: practical, scalable inference with caching and a well-motivated sparse variant.  
- Strength: good empirical speedups and predictive quality versus IRM; hierarchies appear interpretable.  
- Strength: exact marginal likelihood on a fixed tree via DP and efficient prediction are notable technical points.  
- Weakness: moderate originality—core hierarchical-mixture and agglomerative search derive from BRT; novelty is adapting to blockmodels.  
- Weakness: limited empirical scope—NIPS-234 small; no comprehensive timing/memory/scale results for larger graphs.  
- Weakness: no analysis of greedy search optimality, sensitivity to tie-breaking/restarts, or variability across runs.  
- Weakness: lack of theoretical guarantees (approximation bounds, consistency conditions, submodularity-like properties).  
- Weakness: limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and lack of experiments with non-conjugate likelihoods.  
- Quality: technically sound derivations and plausible complexity claims, but more detail on memory/update costs would help.  
- Clarity: well written; suggest adding implementation details for caching, explicit prediction complexity, and reporting tie-breaking/restart strategies and variability.  
- Originality: adaptation to relational data and preserved O(n^2 log n) behavior is nontrivial; contribution is engineering/modeling rather than paradigm-shifting.  
- Significance: important step for scalable Bayesian nonparametric network models, but O(n^2) base scaling may limit very large-graph applicability.  
- Key suggestions: report end-to-end runtimes and memory on full NIPS and larger graphs; measure greedy vs optimal on small graphs; provide hyperparameter sensitivity and restart variability analyses; discuss possible submodularity or theoretical properties; consider non-conjugate likelihood comparisons.  
- Acceptance pros: clear adaptation of BRT to networks, strong empirical speedups, interpretable results, and scalable inference with exact marginal likelihood on fixed trees.  
- Acceptance cons: limited novelty, missing large-scale timing and optimality/variance analyses, and no theoretical guarantees for greedy procedure.  
- Minor corrections: fix typos/phrasing—“computationally much cheaper,” “practitioners,” “pair of trees,” and revise “A fruitful next steps” and avoid “whilst … but.”  
- Overall recommendation: recommend acceptance — useful and well-presented adaptation despite limited novelty.
