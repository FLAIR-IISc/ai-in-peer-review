

The authors introduce Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a scalable Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. Building upon the Bayesian Rose Trees framework [14], BHCD leverages tree-structured mixtures of stochastic blockmodels and employs a greedy agglomerative model selection procedure to learn hierarchical partitions efficiently. The method demonstrates significant computational advantages over existing approaches like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), achieving comparable predictive performance while operating two orders of magnitude faster. Experiments on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network (NIPS-234), and the full NIPS dataset validate the approach’s effectiveness.

Quality/Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear exposition of technical concepts. The model formulation, algorithm description, and experimental setup are logically organized and sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The connection to prior work on hierarchical clustering and Bayesian community detection is well-articulated.

Originality: While the core algorithmic framework adapts ideas from Bayesian Rose Trees [14], the adaptation to relational data with maintained computational efficiency represents a non-trivial contribution. The paper effectively addresses challenges in extending hierarchical clustering to probabilistic relational models, particularly through the novel caching scheme enabling O(n² log n) inference. The work distinguishes itself from prior hierarchical Bayesian models [8, 9, 10, 11, 12] by avoiding the computational bottlenecks of MCMC or greedy search over tree structures.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models to large networks is a critical challenge for practical adoption. BHCD’s O(n² log n) complexity (with sparse variant O(n + m)) offers a compelling practical advantage, especially for dense networks where IRM scales poorly with community count. However, the paper would benefit from addressing scalability on larger networks: while NIPS-234 (234 nodes) runs in tens of seconds, the full NIPS dataset (2,864 nodes) requires additional timing data to assess feasibility for real-world social networks (often >10⁴ nodes). A theoretical analysis of the greedy procedure’s optimality (e.g., via submodularity) would strengthen the significance claim, as the current experiments only provide empirical evidence of near-optimal results.

The paper’s contribution is significant given the practical barriers to deploying hierarchical Bayesian models in network analysis. The computational efficiency and reasonable predictive performance make BHCD a valuable tool for practitioners. While scalability on very large networks requires further investigation, the proposed method represents a meaningful step toward deployable hierarchical community detection. The work is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS.
