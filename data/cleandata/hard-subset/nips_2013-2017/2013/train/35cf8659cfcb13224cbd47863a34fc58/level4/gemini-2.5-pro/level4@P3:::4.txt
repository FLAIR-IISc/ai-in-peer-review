This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient method for learning hierarchical community structures in networks. The core of the work is to extend previous Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, particularly the Bayesian Rose Tree model [14], by adapting them for relational data. The model is framed as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where each node in the learned hierarchy corresponds to a community. The primary contribution is a greedy agglomerative algorithm that builds this hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion. By developing a novel caching scheme for the blockmodel's sufficient statistics, the authors achieve an impressive worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), independent of the number of communities.

### Strengths

1.  **Efficiency and Scalability:** The main strength of the paper is the development of a fast and scalable algorithm for a relevant problem. The demonstration that BHCD achieves comparable or better predictive accuracy than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) two orders of magnitude faster is compelling. The caching of sufficient statistics (Equation 13) that enables O(1) updates during the greedy search is a solid technical contribution that makes this efficiency possible.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, and the proposed solution is explained logically. The connection to prior work on both stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering is clearly articulated.

3.  **Probabilistic Framework:** The method provides a full probabilistic account of the data and the discovered hierarchy. This allows for principled model selection via Bayes factors (which guide the greedy search) and a coherent method for generating predictive distributions for link prediction (Section 4.2).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the lack of experimental comparison to other hierarchical network models. The empirical evaluation is confined to the IRM, a model that discovers flat community structures. While this successfully demonstrates the benefit of modeling hierarchy, it fails to establish the advantages of BHCD relative to other existing hierarchical approaches. The authors cite relevant work like the Mondrian Process [12] and the multiscale community blockmodel [11], but without a direct comparison, it is difficult to assess where BHCD stands in the state-of-the-art of hierarchical network modeling.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** While the paper is mostly clear, certain technical sections could be improved for better reader understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The claim of an O(n+m) dynamic programming algorithm for computing the tree likelihood is made just before Equation (10), but the details of this algorithm are not provided. A more thorough explanation of this recurrence would strengthen the paper.
    *   The update equations for the sufficient statistics in Equation (13) are central to the algorithm's efficiency but are presented without much derivation or justification. Expanding on why these updates are correct would be beneficial.

3.  **Originality:** The conceptual framework is a direct application of the Bayesian Rose Tree model [14] to a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The main novelty lies in the efficient implementation and the specific caching scheme derived for relational data. While this is a valuable engineering and integration effort, the work is more of an effective adaptation of existing ideas than a presentation of a fundamentally new modeling or inference paradigm.

4.  **Terminology:** The use of the term "Bayesian nonparametric" may be slightly imprecise. As discussed in the original Bayesian Rose Tree paper [14], this agglomerative approach is more accurately characterized as a form of Bayesian model selection over a combinatorially large space of tree structures, rather than a nonparametric model in the typical sense of having a complexity that can grow with the data.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a fast, practical, and well-motivated algorithm for the important problem of hierarchical community discovery.
*   The efficient caching scheme for the agglomerative algorithm is a sound technical contribution.
*   Experimental results show a dramatic speed improvement over a widely-used baseline (IRM) while maintaining competitive performance.
*   The paper is clearly written and presents a complete piece of work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient, as it lacks comparisons to other state-of-the-art hierarchical models. This makes it hard to gauge the significance of the contribution.
*   The conceptual novelty is somewhat limited; the work is primarily an application of an existing algorithm [14] to a new likelihood.
*   Key technical details concerning the likelihood calculation and sufficient statistic updates could be explained more thoroughly.

Overall, this paper presents a capable integration of prior work on Bayesian Rose Trees with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods, resulting in an efficient and effective algorithm for hierarchical community discovery. The primary limitation is the experimental evaluation, which does not sufficiently compare against other hierarchical methods.
