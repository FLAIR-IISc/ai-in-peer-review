The paper is well written, with solid grammar, formatting, and appropriate citations; it is clearly above average in presentation. Please avoid opening sentences with a citation per NIPS style. The theoretical development is concise, e.g., the consistent use of f() and g() throughout.

The work examines stochastic block models (SBMs) and generalizes the single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. This allows replacing the CRP-based IRM marginalization with the simpler partition prior in Eq. (7). The factorization over subtrees mirrors ideas from Bayesian network structure learning (cf. Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Sec. 3), where the posterior decomposes nicely under constrained structure classes; consequently, greedy search, exact marginal likelihoods, efficient handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction can all be carried out cleanly.

SBM evaluation is often performed against “gold standard” datasets with known partitions using NMI and related clustering metrics. You report AUC, which is less standard in this context; I infer you hold out a subset of edges/non-edges and evaluate link prediction—if so, please make the protocol explicit. Recent SBM evaluations use datasets larger than your full NIPS graph; the NIPS-234 results in Figure 4 are informative but should be complemented with experiments at larger scale. It is also insufficient to compare only to Bayesian nonparametric baselines; several published semi-parametric methods (some cited) should be included for a fairer comparison.

The main contribution is the agglomerative procedure; Table 1 succinctly captures the computational advantages.

In sum, the paper extends the single-table IRM with Bayesian Rose trees. The theory and algorithms are clear and well presented, albeit conceptually straightforward. The experimental section is illustrative rather than broadly comparative, and thus not yet fully adequate.
