

The paper proposes a hierarchical extension of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, enabling inference of hierarchical cluster structures rather than flat partitions from observed network data. The authors develop an agglomerative learning algorithm to efficiently select tree-structured partitions, leveraging the key insight that tree constraints allow tractable marginal likelihood computation—unlike the IRM, which must explore exponentially many partitions. Empirical results on social network datasets (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) demonstrate the method’s superior speed (orders of magnitude faster than IRM) and competitive accuracy (AUC, predictive likelihood, classification accuracy).  

The work addresses two timely concerns: (1) the practical computational burden of Bayesian nonparametric methods, and (2) the growing use of hierarchical models in social network analysis. The core innovation—reducing inference complexity via tree-structured partitions—is conceptually sound and well-motivated. The empirical results are compelling, particularly given the significant runtime advantage, which addresses a major limitation of existing approaches. The proposed method can be viewed as a Bayesian analog of agglomerative clustering, offering a principled alternative to heuristic hierarchical network analysis.  

However, the connection between the proposed learning algorithm and the underlying Bayesian generative model requires clarification. The greedy tree construction procedure does not correspond to posterior inference over trees, nor does it yield a MAP estimate. This disconnect is a critical gap: the method’s theoretical grounding as a Bayesian model is undermined if the learning process cannot be reconciled with the generative story. Section 4 should explicitly address how the algorithm relates to the Bayesian framework (e.g., is it approximating a specific posterior? What loss function is optimized?). Without this, the Bayesian interpretation remains superficial.  

The manuscript also suffers from several presentation issues that impede understanding:  
- **Writing quality**: Multiple spelling/grammar errors (e.g., "nonparamtric" on line 126) and awkward phrasing (e.g., "local changes" and "iterations" in lines 47–50 lack context). The introduction ambiguously references "iterations" without specifying if they denote MCMC steps or algorithmic cycles.  
- **Dataset description**: The Monastery networks and NIPS authorship datasets are not adequately contextualized. Briefly defining their domain (e.g., "Monastery networks represent historical religious community interactions; NIPS authorship captures co-authorship patterns in machine learning publications") would aid readers unfamiliar with these sources.  
- **Mathematical clarity**: Equation (5) and parts of Section 4 are unnecessarily dense. Simplifying the exposition or deferring technical details to an appendix would improve accessibility.  
- **Figure 3**: The figure lacks clear labeling of methods and performance metrics, making it difficult to assess results without cross-referencing text. A more intuitive visualization (e.g., using a familiar dataset like Facebook or Twitter networks) would strengthen the presentation.  

Despite these issues, the method’s novelty and empirical strengths are significant. The runtime efficiency combined with competitive accuracy addresses a clear gap in scalable hierarchical network modeling. With revisions to clarify the algorithm’s Bayesian justification, improve writing quality, and enhance figure/dataset descriptions, the paper would make a valuable contribution.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions). The technical contribution is novel, the results are robust, and the speed advantage is substantial. The writing and clarity issues are fixable and do not undermine the core advance. The authors should:  
1. Reconcile the greedy tree algorithm with the Bayesian model in Section 4.  
2. Correct grammatical/spelling errors and simplify ambiguous phrasing.  
3. Provide brief context for datasets and improve Figure 3’s interpretability.  
4. Streamline overly technical equations (e.g., Eq. 5).  

This work will interest the NIPS community, particularly researchers focused on scalable Bayesian network modeling. The proposed approach offers a practical pathway to hierarchical clustering in large-scale network data, and the authors’ empirical validation is thorough. With the suggested revisions, it merits publication.
