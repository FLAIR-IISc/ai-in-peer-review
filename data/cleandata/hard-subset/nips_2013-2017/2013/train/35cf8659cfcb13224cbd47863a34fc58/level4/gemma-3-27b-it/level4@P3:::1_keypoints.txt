- Paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and a greedy agglomerative learning procedure.  
- Method offers substantially reduced computational cost relative to the IRM while maintaining comparable predictive performance.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian nonparametric network models (IRM) and Bayesian rose tree literature, adapting rose trees to relational data.  
- Related hierarchical community detection work is appropriately cited and situated.  

Strengths:
- Exceptionally clear and well-written manuscript with logical mathematical exposition.  
- Major contribution to scalability: much more computationally efficient than existing Bayesian nonparametric hierarchical community methods.  
- Sound methodology: tree-structured prior plus greedy agglomeration balances flexibility and feasibility.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets (Sampson, NIPS co-authorship) with convincing comparison to IRM on NIPS-234.  

Weaknesses:
- Scalability to very large networks remains a concern; sparse algorithm still O(n^2 log n) and may be prohibitive for millions of nodes.  
- Greedy algorithm may be suboptimal; paper lacks empirical or theoretical bounds on the gap to optimal solutions.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is underexplored; more thorough sensitivity analysis needed.  

Suggested experiments/analyses:
- Report runtime on the full NIPS dataset to better demonstrate scalability.  
- Provide empirical comparisons to optimal solutions on small networks or investigate theoretical properties (e.g., submodularity).  
- Perform detailed hyperparameter sensitivity studies.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem (scalable Bayesian hierarchical community discovery) with a novel, well-motivated approach.  
- Demonstrates large efficiency gains without sacrificing predictive accuracy.  
- Clear exposition makes the complex method accessible.  
- Experimental results provide strong supporting evidence.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Remaining scalability concerns for extremely large networks.  
- Lack of theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality and limited hyperparameter exploration.  

Overall assessment:
- Strong, technically sound, and well-validated paper that makes a significant contribution; recommended for acceptance despite scalability and optimality questions.
