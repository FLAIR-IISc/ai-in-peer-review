The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to the hierarchical setting, enabling the inference of a hierarchical structure rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. They develop a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid possible partitions of the entities. The authors then present empirical results from two corpora of social connectivity data and conclude that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for tasks of this kind.

I believe this paper addresses an interesting issue that will resonate with a significant fraction of the NIPS audience. From a technical standpoint, Bayesian nonparametric methods, although conceptually attractive, often suffer from slow performance in practice. Furthermore, social scientists are increasingly using sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis, making a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model a valuable contribution. This paper effectively bridges these two areas.

The key insight of this work is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. In contrast, the IRM must consider an exponential number of possible partitions of the data. However, I found the relationship between the learning procedure for the trees and the generative story behind the Bayesian model to be somewhat unclear. Since the learning procedure is greedy, I do not think it can be seen as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or finding a point MAP or ML estimate. I may have misunderstood this aspect, and I would recommend that the authors clarify Section 4 to address this concern.

I found the results to be impressive, with the proposed method performing comparably or slightly better than the IRM, the closest competitor, in terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy). Although the asymptotic improvements in these scores may not be substantial in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

While the proposed approach has sufficient novelty and strong results to warrant publication, I think the overall clarity and writing quality could be improved. The text contains distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126). Additionally, the prose is sometimes hard to follow, such as the vague phrasing in lines 47-50 of the introduction (e.g., what are 'local changes' in this setting, and what are the 'iterations' referring to, such as iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The authors should clearly describe the content of the datasets used for training (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), as unfamiliar readers may find this information confusing. I occasionally found the math to be obtuse and unnecessary, such as Eq. 5. Furthermore, Figure 3 is confusing, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the monastery dataset. It would be helpful to use a more familiar dataset. Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, required multiple readings to understand.

If these issues are addressed, I believe the proposed approach has enough novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning algorithm to be insightful, and the experimental results to be strong, but the quality of writing is in need of improvement.
