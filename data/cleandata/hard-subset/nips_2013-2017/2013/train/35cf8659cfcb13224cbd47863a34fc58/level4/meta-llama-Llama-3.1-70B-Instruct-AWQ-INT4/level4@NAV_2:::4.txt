This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, leveraging a Bayesian nonparametric model. The authors extend previous work on Bayesian rose trees by modifying the likelihood to accommodate network data and efficiently caching sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable comparisons to a baseline model with flat community structure. However, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are limited.

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. The presentation in Section 3 is dense and could benefit from simpler notation and explanation, particularly for equations (9) and subsequent parts. The authors' use of a tree to define a prior on partitions allows for exact evaluation of the marginal likelihood, which is a notable contribution.

The significance of this work lies in its ability to model hierarchical community structure, which is beneficial for relational datasets. However, the paper fails to mention other Bayesian nonparametric models that can discover hierarchical latent structure, such as the multiscale community blockmodel and the Mondrian Process. Comparisons to these models would strengthen the paper's claims.

The model's originality lies in its application of Bayesian rose trees to network data, with innovations in the recursive updating of sufficient statistics. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a valuable feature. However, the description of this aspect could be improved, and the recursive updating of sufficient statistics requires more explanation and justification.

The terminology used in the paper is questionable, as the "nonparametric" label may not be entirely accurate. The authors' approach is more akin to model selection, as noted in prior work on Bayesian rose trees.

The experiments are well-executed, with a qualitative evaluation on Sampson's monastery network and a comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The benefits of averaging over many hypotheses when making predictions are clearly demonstrated. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a significant limitation. The discussion on scalability is also limited, as the networks tested are not exceptionally large.

In conclusion, this paper effectively integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks, allowing for efficient hierarchical community discovery. However, further comparisons to other hierarchical network models are necessary to fully evaluate the approach's strengths and limitations.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks.
* The authors extend previous work on Bayesian rose trees to accommodate network data.
* The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable comparisons to a baseline model.
* The approach allows for efficient hierarchical community discovery.

Arguments con acceptance:

* Comparisons to other hierarchical network models are limited.
* The paper fails to mention other Bayesian nonparametric models that can discover hierarchical latent structure.
* The terminology used is questionable, and the approach may be more akin to model selection.
* The discussion on scalability is limited, and the networks tested are not exceptionally large.
