This paper presents a well-written and technically sound extension to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. The writing quality is excellent, with strong grammar, spelling, and correct citations – significantly above average for submitted work. A minor stylistic note: the NIPS style discourages beginning sentences with citations. The theoretical development is concise and effectively utilizes mathematical notation, such as the consistent use of *f()* and *g()*, enhancing readability.

The core contribution lies in augmenting the standard single-table IRM with Bayesian Rose tree theory, allowing for a simplification of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation previously used in IRM, as evidenced by equation (7). This approach draws parallels to research on learning Bayesian network structures – specifically, the factorization over subtrees as demonstrated in work like Koivisto and Sood (JMLR, 2004, Section 3). The authors correctly highlight how this factorization enables efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, and the handling of sufficient statistics, all of which are crucial for tasks like link prediction.

The paper’s evaluation focuses on Stochastic Block Models (SBM), a common benchmark for community detection algorithms. Typically, SBM evaluations leverage “gold standard” datasets with known partition structures, employing metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) to assess clustering performance. The authors, however, report results using Area Under the Curve (AUC), which initially required clarification. I inferred that the AUC is calculated by removing links and then predicting their reinstatement, but this process requires explicit explanation within the paper. While the evaluation presented in Figure 4 provides a good initial demonstration, the field has progressed towards evaluating SBM algorithms on significantly larger datasets than those presented here (larger than the NIPS dataset). Extending the evaluation to a larger dataset would strengthen the empirical validation. Furthermore, comparison to Bayesian non-parametric methods alone is insufficient; the paper should also benchmark against relevant semi-parametric methods, several of which are acknowledged in the related work.

Table 1 effectively summarizes the key aspects of the proposed agglomerative method, clearly illustrating its functionality. 

In summary, the paper offers a solid theoretical contribution by extending the IRM with a well-justified and clearly presented Bayesian framework. The algorithms are straightforward and easy to understand. However, the experimental results are primarily illustrative rather than comprehensively comparative. A more extensive evaluation, particularly on larger datasets and against a wider range of competing methods, would significantly enhance the impact of this work. The explanation of the AUC evaluation metric *must* be clarified.
