This paper proposes an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model is a tree-structured mixture of potentially exponentially many stochastic blockmodels. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that take just one pass through the data to learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model. 

The main idea of the paper is to extend the simple single table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by adding the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of IRM to be replaced by the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees follows the work on learning Bayesian network structures, where the posterior factorises neatly based on the class of constrained structures chosen. 

The paper is well-written with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations. The theory is succinct in parts, for instance using f() and g() throughout the paper. However, the authors should avoid starting a sentence with a citation in the NIPS style.

The evaluation of the stochastic block model (SBM) is often done against "gold standard" data where the partition structure is known. Scores like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering metrics can be used for this where you compare against the known clusterings. The authors report using Area under the receiver operating Curve (AUC) and it is not immediately clear how this could be done, since the reviewer is familiar with NMI evaluations for SBM. The authors should provide more explanation for this. 

The contribution of this algorithm is the agglomerative method and Table 1 sums it up well. However, the experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, so it is not really adequate. The authors should compare their method against other published semi-parametric methods, not just Bayesian non-parametric methods.
