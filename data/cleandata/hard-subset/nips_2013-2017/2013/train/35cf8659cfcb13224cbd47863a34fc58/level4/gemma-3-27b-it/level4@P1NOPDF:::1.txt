The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Information Retrieval Metric (IRM) for network modeling, leveraging key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper: 1) the hierarchy specifies a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and 2) this hierarchy can be learned using an efficient, greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results are shown on the Sampson’s monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, with quantitative results presented for the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the original IRM, while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity: The paper is very well written, and the exposition of the core ideas is clear.

Originality: The core ideas are drawn from the Bayesian rose tree work, but adapting them to the relational data setting required significant effort, particularly in maintaining comparable computational complexity.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models—and network models in particular—is a crucial challenge for practical application. While the n<sup>2</sup> scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for very large networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. It would be helpful to see timings on larger, real-world networks. The NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process: how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are far larger still: how would inference scale with these datasets? 

Furthermore, it would be valuable to understand how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree structure. This could be investigated by comparing its performance to exhaustive search at the largest scale still computationally tractable for finding the optimal tree. Is it possible to provide any theoretical guarantees for the method, such as relating to submodularity?

Despite the familiarity of the underlying ideas from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is still significant, and this paper should be accepted.
