This paper proposes an efficient, greedy agglomerative approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The method adapts the Bayesian rose tree framework, a prior over tree-structured partitions, to relational data by using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The primary contribution is a bottom-up algorithm for learning the community hierarchy that takes a single pass through the data. By developing an efficient caching scheme for the sufficient statistics, the algorithm achieves a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n). The model is evaluated on several network datasets, where it is shown to be significantly faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable predictive accuracy.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method appears technically sound.
*   The primary strength is the capable integration of prior work on Bayesian rose trees [14] with a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood for networks. This combination results in a fast and practical algorithm for hierarchical community discovery.
*   The development of an efficient caching scheme to update sufficient statistics recursively (Section 4) is a key technical contribution that allows the greedy agglomerative algorithm to scale.
*   The experiments provide a compelling comparison to the IRM. They clearly demonstrate the significant speed advantage of the proposed method (BHCD), which obtains strong predictive results orders of magnitude faster than a standard MCMC-based inference scheme for a flat community model.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison to Related Work:** A significant weakness is the lack of comparison, both in the discussion and experimentally, to other Bayesian models for hierarchical community detection. The experiments effectively demonstrate the benefit of modeling hierarchy over a flat model (IRM), but they do not show the advantage of this specific approach over other existing hierarchical alternatives. For instance, the authors should consider discussing and comparing their work to the "Multiscale community blockmodel" [11] and the "Mondrian Process" [12], both of which offer flexible, hierarchical priors for relational data. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the paper's contribution to the state of the art in hierarchical network modeling.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** The presentation could be improved in a few key areas.
    *   Section 3, which introduces the model, is quite dense. The claim that the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) can be calculated in O(n+m) time via dynamic programming is an important one, but the details of this calculation are omitted, making it difficult for the reader to verify.
    *   Similarly, while the update equations for the sufficient statistics are provided in (13), a more detailed explanation or derivation of why these updates are correct would strengthen the paper's clarity and improve its reproducibility.

3.  **Use of Terminology:** The use of the term "nonparametric" is questionable in this context. As the original Bayesian rose tree paper [14] notes, this approach is better framed as a form of Bayesian model selection over a nested set of tree-structured models. The authors of [14] explicitly contrast their approach with truly nonparametric methods. Adopting this more precise terminology would better position the work within the literature.

4.  **Experimental Scale:** While scalability is a central motivation for the work, the networks tested are not especially large. The "Full NIPS" dataset has fewer than 3,000 vertices. Demonstrating the method's performance on a substantially larger network would provide stronger evidence to support the claims of scalability.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a clear and effective method for hierarchical community discovery by combining the Bayesian rose tree framework with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The main contribution is the generalization of an efficient greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to this new model, enabled by a novel caching of sufficient statistics. While the experiments show significant speed and performance benefits over the flat IRM, the work would be substantially strengthened by a more thorough discussion of, and comparison to, other existing hierarchical network models. Improving the clarity of some of the technical derivations would also be beneficial. Overall, this is a solid contribution that capably integrates existing lines of work to produce a fast and useful algorithm.
