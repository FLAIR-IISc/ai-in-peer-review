- Proposes a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling inspired by Bayesian rose trees.  
- Uses a hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions.  
- Learns the hierarchy via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure.  
- Demonstrates qualitative results on Sampson’s Monks and the full NIPS dataset.  
- Provides quantitative results on NIPS-234.  
- Inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.  
- Paper is well written and core ideas are explained clearly.  
- Adaptation from Bayesian rose trees to relational data is nontrivial and makes a meaningful contribution.  
- Scaling BNP models for networks is important; base algorithm’s O(n^2) scaling may be prohibitive for very large graphs.  
- Recommends including timing results on larger real-world networks and reporting full NIPS runtime.  
- Questions how inference scales (time and memory) for very large gene and social networks and suggests the sparse variant may help.  
- Suggests studying how close the greedy agglomerative search is to the optimal tree via exhaustive search on small datasets and seeking theoretical support (e.g., submodularity/approximation guarantees).  
- Lists minor editorial corrections (redundant conjunction, “treeS” → “trees”, “A fruitful next steps” → singular or rephrased).  
- Overall: adaptation and efficiency gains are significant and useful; recommends acceptance.
