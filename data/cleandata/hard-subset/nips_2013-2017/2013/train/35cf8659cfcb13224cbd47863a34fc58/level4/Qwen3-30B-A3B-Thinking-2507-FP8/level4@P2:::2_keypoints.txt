- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric method for hierarchical community detection using a tree-structured mixture of blockmodels.  
- Proposes a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm (BHCD) with worst-case time complexity O(n² log n).  
- Restricting partitions to tree-consistent ones enables exact marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming.  
- Significant computational improvement over IRM and similar Bayesian hierarchical methods that scale poorly.  
- Empirically achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM while being ~100x faster in practice.  
- Runtimes are independent of the number of nested communities, improving scalability for large networks.  
- Technical approach is sound with well-justified complexity analysis.  
- Experiments are comprehensive, including qualitative analysis (Sampson’s monastery) and quantitative comparisons (NIPS co-authorship).  
- Thorough baseline comparisons with IRM, LFRM, and ILA strengthen empirical claims.  
- Main limitation: greedy algorithm does not perform full posterior inference and may miss the global optimum.  
- Clarity issues: grammatical errors, unclear phrasing (e.g., “local changes,” “iterations”), and dense presentation in Section 4.  
- Insufficient dataset description for Sampson’s monastery, making some figures confusing to unfamiliar readers.  
- Mathematical exposition (Equation 5, tree-consistent partitions) could be made more accessible.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify generative model vs. greedy learning, add dataset context, improve math/algorithm presentation, and discuss additional limitations.  
- Overall assessment: novel, practically significant, and suitable for NIPS pending minor revisions to improve clarity.
