The authors present a hierarchical extension of Bayesian nonparametric stochastic blockmodels to recover nested community structure from observed links, rather than a single flat partition. They introduce a greedy agglomerative procedure that selects a tree encoding the set of allowable partitions, and evaluate the approach on two social network datasets. They report that their method is substantially faster and achieves accuracy comparable to or better than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a common baseline for this problem.

I view the problem as timely and of broad interest to the NIPS community. Methodologically, Bayesian nonparametric models are appealing but often computationally heavy in practice. From an application perspective, social scientists increasingly adopt Bayesian tools for network analysis, and a hierarchical variant of a widely used model is likely to be well received. This work speaks to both concerns.

The central idea is that constraining partitions to those consistent with a tree permits efficient evaluation of the marginal likelihood. In contrast, the IRM effectively ranges over an exponentially large partition space. That said, I found the connection between the greedy tree-construction procedure and the paper’s generative model insufficiently clear. Because the search is greedy, it does not appear to correspond to posterior sampling over trees or to an explicit MAP/ML estimator, though I may be misreading the intent. Clarifying Section 4 would help.

The empirical results are strong. Across AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, the proposed approach performs on par with or slightly better than IRM. While the absolute performance gains are modest at convergence, the runtime advantages are striking—reported to be orders of magnitude faster than IRM.

The presentation would benefit from editing and clarification. There are several typos and grammatical issues (e.g., line 126). Some passages are vague or ambiguous—for example, lines 47–50 in the introduction (what precisely are “local changes” and what do “iterations” refer to—MCMC iterations?). The datasets (Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS co-authorship) are not described in sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with them. Portions of the math felt heavier than necessary for exposition (e.g., Eq. 5), and Figure 3 is hard to interpret without prior familiarity with the monastery data; a more widely known dataset or additional guidance might help. I also had to read Section 4 multiple times to fully parse the algorithm, which is the core contribution.

If these issues are addressed, the method’s novelty and the strength of the results support publication.

The paper introduces a new Bayesian model for graph edges that effectively serves as a Bayesian analogue of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the main learning procedure insightful and the experiments compelling, though the writing quality needs attention. The work presents a Bayesian hierarchical approach to graph structure learning related to IRM, with an elegant greedy algorithm and solid empirical performance, but the manuscript would benefit from clearer exposition.
