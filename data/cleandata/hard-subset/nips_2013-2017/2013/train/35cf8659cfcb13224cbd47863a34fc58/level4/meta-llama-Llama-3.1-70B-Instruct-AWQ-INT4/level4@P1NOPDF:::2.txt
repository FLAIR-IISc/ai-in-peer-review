The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to the hierarchical setting, allowing for the inference of a hierarchical structure rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. They introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree that represents valid possible partitions of the entities. Empirical results from two corpora of social connectivity data demonstrate that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for tasks of this kind.

I believe this paper tackles an interesting issue that will resonate with a significant fraction of the NIPS audience. On the technical side, Bayesian nonparametric methods, while conceptually attractive, are often slow in practice. On the problem domain side, social scientists are increasingly using sophisticated Bayesian models for analyzing data, and a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be well-received. This paper addresses both of these concerns.

The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. In contrast, the IRM must consider an exponential number of possible partitions of the data. However, I was left somewhat confused about how the learning procedure of the trees relates to the generative story behind the Bayesian model. Since the algorithm is greedy, I do not think it can be seen as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or finding a point MAP or ML estimate. But I may have misunderstood. I would recommend that the authors improve the clarity of Section 4 to address this concern.

I found the results to be impressive. In terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performed comparably or slightly better than the IRM, the closest competitor. While the asymptotic improvements in these scores may not be impressive in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

I think the overall clarity and writing quality could be improved. First, there are a series of distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126). Second, the prose is sometimes hard to understand. For instance, lines 47-50 in the introduction contain vague phrasing (what are 'local changes' in this setting? What are the 'iterations' referring to? Iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The authors do not clearly describe the content of the datasets they train on (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), which could confuse readers who are not already familiar with them. I occasionally found the math somewhat obtuse and unnecessary, such as Eq. 5. I also found Figure 3 confusing, as it is visually unclear which method is performing best. Perhaps a dataset that more readers will be familiar with would be helpful. Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, required several readings to understand.

If these issues could be remedied, however, I believe the proposed approach has enough novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning algorithm to be insightful and the experimental results to be strong, but the quality of writing is in need of improvement.
