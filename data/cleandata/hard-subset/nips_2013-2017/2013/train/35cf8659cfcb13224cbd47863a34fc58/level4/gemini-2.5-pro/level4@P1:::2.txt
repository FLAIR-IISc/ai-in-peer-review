This paper introduces a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The proposed method, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), extends the stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, allowing it to infer a nested community structure rather than a flat partitioning of entities. Inference is performed using a greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm that builds a tree representing the hierarchy. This tree structure constrains the set of possible partitions, which is the key to the model's computational efficiency. The authors evaluate their method on two social network datasets, demonstrating that it is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or superior predictive accuracy.

**Strengths**

This paper addresses an important problem that will be of interest to a significant portion of the NIPS community. From a technical standpoint, it tackles the common issue of computational inefficiency in Bayesian nonparametric models. From a problem-domain perspective, it provides a useful hierarchical extension to a popular network analysis model, which is valuable for researchers in computational social science and other fields who are increasingly adopting sophisticated Bayesian methods.

The core technical contribution is the insight that by restricting the space of partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, the marginal likelihood can be calculated efficiently via dynamic programming. This avoids the need to sum over an exponentially large space of partitions, a key bottleneck for models like the IRM.

The empirical results are another major strength. On several performance metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy), the proposed method performs on par with or slightly better than the IRM. While the improvements in accuracy are modest, the dramatic speedup—achieving these results orders of magnitude faster than IRM—is a very significant and compelling contribution.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

My main concern relates to the clarity of the inference procedure described in Section 4. The connection between the greedy, bottom-up tree-building algorithm and the probabilistic model is not clearly explained. Since the algorithm is greedy, it does not appear to be performing full posterior inference over the space of trees, nor is it explicitly framed as finding a maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood (ML) point estimate. Clarifying the statistical interpretation of the learned tree would significantly strengthen the paper.

Beyond this major point, the paper's overall clarity could be improved in several ways:
*   The prose is occasionally difficult to follow due to vague phrasing. For example, the introduction's description of prior work mentions "many small changes" and "iterations" without specifying what these refer to (e.g., MCMC steps).
*   The manuscript contains several distracting spelling and grammatical errors that would benefit from careful proofreading.
*   The datasets (Sampson's Monastery and NIPS co-authorship) are not sufficiently described for readers unfamiliar with them, making it harder to appreciate the experimental context.
*   The presentation of results could be improved. Figure 3, for instance, is difficult to interpret without prior knowledge of the monastery dataset, making it hard to visually assess which variant of the model performs best.
*   Some mathematical formulations, such as Equation 5, could be made more intuitive with additional explanation.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a novel and efficient method for hierarchical community detection. The core idea of using a tree-structured prior to enable efficient marginal likelihood computation is insightful, and the experimental results, particularly the significant speedup over existing methods, are strong. However, the paper is held back by a lack of clarity, most critically concerning the relationship between the learning algorithm and the probabilistic model. If the authors can address this point and the other suggestions for improving clarity and presentation, I believe this would be a valuable contribution to the conference.
