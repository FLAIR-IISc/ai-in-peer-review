This paper generalizes the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering from observed links. The authors introduce an agglomerative learning algorithm that selects a tree encoding valid partitions of the entities, and they present experiments on two social network datasets. They report that the method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a common baseline for this task.

The topic is timely and relevant to a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. Conceptually appealing Bayesian nonparametric methods are often slow in practice; at the same time, social scientists increasingly use Bayesian models for network analysis. A hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model is therefore valuable, and this work addresses both the computational and modeling aspects.

The key idea is that constraining partitions to those consistent with a tree allows efficient computation of the marginal likelihood. In contrast, the IRM must, in principle, contend with an exponential number of partitions. However, I am unclear about how the greedy tree-learning procedure aligns with the paper’s generative Bayesian story. Because the algorithm is agglomerative and greedy, it does not appear to perform posterior inference over trees or to yield a clear MAP/ML estimate under a well-defined objective—though I may be misunderstanding. Clarifying the connection between the model and the learning algorithm would strengthen the paper; in particular, Section 4 would benefit from a more explicit explanation of the objective optimized by the algorithm and how it relates to the posterior.

The empirical results are strong. Across several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed approach performs comparably to or slightly better than the IRM. While the absolute performance gains are modest, the reported runtime speedups are dramatic—orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference—which is a compelling advantage in practice.

That said, the paper’s clarity and writing could be improved. There are several distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., around line 126). Some phrasing in the introduction (lines 47–50) is vague; for example, the terms “local changes” and “iterations” are not well defined—are these referring to MCMC iterations or something else? The datasets (monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not described in sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical exposition feels heavier than necessary (e.g., Eq. 5), and Figure 3 is difficult to interpret without prior knowledge of the monastery dataset, making it hard to see which method is best. Section 4, which presents the main technical contribution, required multiple readings for me to follow; clearer structure, definitions, and a worked example would help.

If these presentation issues are addressed, the contribution appears sufficiently novel and the results strong enough to merit publication. In summary, the paper proposes a novel hierarchical model for graph edges that can be viewed as a Bayesian variant of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning idea insightful and the experiments compelling, but the exposition and writing need meaningful refinement.
