This paper introduces a hierarchical extension to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network analysis, adapting core concepts from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) framework. Specifically, the proposed model leverages the hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions and employs an efficient, greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this structure. The authors demonstrate their method with qualitative results on the Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, complemented by quantitative evaluation on the NIPS-234 dataset. A key finding is that the proposed inference procedure is significantly more computationally efficient than the standard IRM, yet it achieves comparable predictive accuracy.

**Quality/Clarity**
The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and effective exposition of the central ideas.

**Originality**
While the foundational concepts are drawn from the Bayesian rose tree literature, the authors have made a non-trivial contribution in adapting these principles to the domain of relational data. A notable achievement is the preservation of the original's computational complexity within this new context.

**Significance**
The challenge of scaling Bayesian non-parametric (BNP) models for network analysis is a critical one for their practical adoption, and this work makes a valuable contribution in this area. However, the O(n^2) complexity of the base algorithm could be a bottleneck for larger networks. While the authors mention a sparse version, its scalability is not fully demonstrated. To strengthen the claims, it would be beneficial to include timing experiments on larger, real-world graphs. For instance, given that the NIPS-234 dataset is processed in seconds, what is the runtime for the full NIPS dataset? Furthermore, how would the inference scale to networks on the order of gene-interaction maps (tens of thousands of nodes) or large social networks?

Additionally, it would be valuable to understand how close the greedy method's solution is to the optimal tree. This could be explored empirically by comparing the greedy solution to one found via exhaustive search on a problem small enough for the latter to be tractable. From a theoretical standpoint, could the authors provide any guarantees for the greedy approach, perhaps by establishing connections to concepts like submodularity?
