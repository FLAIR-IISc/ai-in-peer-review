This paper presents a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling, drawing on key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) framework: (1) the hierarchy specifies a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and (2) the hierarchy can be learned via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors provide qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity. The paper is very well written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear.

Originality. Although the main ideas originate from the BRT work, adapting them to the relational data setting required nontrivial effort, particularly to preserve the computational complexity.

Significance. Scaling BNP models—especially for network data—is an important challenge for practical adoption. While the O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should presumably remain feasible. It would be valuable to include timings on larger real-world networks. NIPS-234 runs in only tens of seconds; how long does full NIPS take? Gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are much larger still—how would inference scale in these settings?

It would also be helpful to understand how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be assessed at the largest scale for which the optimal tree can still be found by exhaustive search. Is any theoretical support available for the method (e.g., guarantees related to submodularity)?

Minor corrections:
- Line 48, page 1: “whilst … but” → avoid the redundant conjunction
- “pair of treeS” → “pair of trees”
- “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps”

Overall assessment. While the ideas are familiar from the BRT literature, the contribution here is still significant due to the careful adaptation to relational data and the demonstrated computational gains. I recommend acceptance.
