

The paper is well-written with correct grammar, spelling, and citations. However, avoid beginning sentences with citations in NIPS style. The theoretical contributions are clear in parts, though the consistent use of abstract functions (e.g., $f()$ and $g()$) could be clarified for broader accessibility.  

The work extends the single-table instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by integrating Bayesian Rose trees, enabling a simplified CRP formulation (Equation 7). This approach leverages structural factorization principles from Bayesian network learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004), allowing efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, sufficient statistic handling, and link prediction. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the algorithmic presentation is clear.  

The evaluation methodology requires significant clarification. While stochastic block model (SBM) performance is commonly assessed using "gold standard" partitions (e.g., via normalized mutual information, NMI), the paper reports AUC results. It is unclear how AUC is computed for SBM—specifically, whether it involves deleting edges and predicting their re-occurrence. This methodological gap must be explicitly explained. Furthermore, the experimental validation (Figure 4) relies solely on the NIPS dataset, which is relatively small by contemporary standards. Recent SBM benchmarks use substantially larger datasets; thus, the current evaluation lacks scalability evidence. Comparisons should extend beyond Bayesian non-parametric methods to include recent semi-parametric approaches (some cited in the paper), as the current scope is insufficient for establishing competitive advantage.  

The agglomerative algorithm (summarized in Table 1) constitutes the core contribution, and its description is concise. However, the experimental section falls short of rigorous benchmarking. The results are illustrative rather than comparative, failing to demonstrate how the proposed method advances upon established baselines. For instance, the absence of ablation studies or comparisons against state-of-the-art SBM solvers weakens the empirical claims.  

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically grounded extension of IRM using Bayesian Rose trees, with clear algorithmic exposition. However, the evaluation lacks depth and context: the AUC methodology is ambiguous, the dataset size is inadequate for modern SBM challenges, and comparative analysis is insufficient. Addressing these gaps would significantly strengthen the contribution. The theoretical insights are valuable, but the experimental validation must be expanded to meet NIPS standards for empirical rigor.
