

The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities from observed links. They introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid partitions of entities, and demonstrate empirical results on two social connectivity corpora. The method outperforms the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) in both speed and accuracy, with runtime improvements of orders of magnitude while maintaining comparable or slightly better performance on metrics including AUC, predictive likelihood, and accuracy.

This paper addresses an interesting problem that will interest a significant portion of the NIPS audience. Technically, it tackles the practical inefficiency of Bayesian nonparametric methods, which are conceptually appealing but often slow. Domain-wise, social scientists increasingly employ sophisticated Bayesian models for network analysis, making a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model timely and valuable. The work successfully bridges these concerns.

The key insight—that restricting partitions to tree structures enables efficient marginal likelihood computation—effectively circumvents the exponential partition search required by IRM. However, I remain unclear on how the greedy tree learning procedure aligns with the Bayesian model's generative story. Since the approach is greedy, it cannot be interpreted as performing true posterior inference over tree distributions, nor as finding a MAP or ML estimate. The authors should clarify this relationship in Section 4.

The results are compelling: the proposed method achieves comparable or slightly superior performance to IRM across multiple metrics, with runtime improvements orders of magnitude over IRM inference. While absolute gains in performance metrics are modest, the computational efficiency is substantial.

The paper's clarity and writing require significant improvement. Numerous spelling and grammar errors persist (e.g., line 126). Vague phrasing appears in the introduction (lines 47–50: "local changes" and "iterations" are undefined—likely referring to MCMC iterations, but this should be explicit). The datasets (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are insufficiently described for unfamiliar readers. Some mathematical exposition is unnecessarily obtuse (e.g., Equation 5). Figure 3 is confusing without context for the Monastery dataset, making it unclear which method performs best; a more familiar dataset might aid interpretation. Section 4, containing the core contribution, requires multiple readings for comprehension.

If these issues are addressed, the approach demonstrates sufficient novelty and strong results to warrant publication. The proposed model—a Bayesian variant of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM—is insightful, and the experimental results are robust. However, the writing quality must be substantially improved to convey these contributions effectively.
