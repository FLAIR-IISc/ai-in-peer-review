

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, extending the stochastic blockmodel to a tree-structured mixture of blockmodels. The authors propose a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm (BHCD) that efficiently learns hierarchical community models with worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), where n is the number of vertices. This represents a significant computational improvement over existing Bayesian hierarchical methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which scales poorly with network size.

The paper's main technical contribution lies in restricting the set of allowable partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, enabling exact computation of the marginal likelihood through dynamic programming. This clever approach avoids the exponential complexity of considering all possible partitions, as required by the IRM. The authors demonstrate that BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster in practice, with runtimes independent of the number of nested communities.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with well-justified theoretical analysis of the algorithm's complexity. The experimental results are comprehensive, including both qualitative analysis of Sampson's monastery network and quantitative comparisons on the NIPS co-authorship network. The paper provides a thorough comparison with relevant baselines, including the IRM, LFRM, and ILA. However, the greedy nature of the algorithm means it does not perform true posterior inference over tree structures, which could be better clarified in the presentation. The authors acknowledge this limitation by noting that BHCD may not find the globally optimal tree but provides a computationally efficient alternative.

**Clarity**: The paper would benefit from improved exposition. Several sections contain grammatical errors and unclear phrasing, particularly in the introduction (lines 47-50) where "local changes" and "iterations" are used without sufficient context. The description of the dataset (Sampson's monastery network) is insufficient for readers unfamiliar with this domain, making Figure 3 confusing. The mathematical presentation, especially Equation 5 and the tree-consistent partitions, could be more accessible. Section 4, which presents the core algorithm, requires multiple readings to fully understand, suggesting the authors could improve the explanation of their model selection procedure.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data and providing an efficient inference procedure for hierarchical community detection. While hierarchical community detection has been explored before, the authors' specific approach of using tree-structured partitions to enable efficient marginal likelihood computation represents a novel technical contribution distinct from previous work. The method differs significantly from [8, 9, 10, 11] which either limit hierarchy depth or use computationally expensive inference procedures.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a meaningful problem in network analysis with clear practical implications. Hierarchical community structure is common in social networks, and the proposed method enables efficient analysis of large networks where existing Bayesian approaches become computationally prohibitive. The demonstrated speedup (two orders of magnitude) over IRM is substantial for real-world applications, while maintaining comparable accuracy on standard metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy). The NIPS co-authorship network experiments provide strong evidence of the method's practical utility.

The authors could strengthen the paper by:
1. Clarifying the relationship between the generative model and the greedy learning procedure
2. Providing more context about the datasets used (especially Sampson's monastery network)
3. Improving the presentation of mathematical concepts and the algorithm description
4. Adding a brief discussion of limitations beyond computational speed

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant technical contributions. The method is both novel and practically valuable, addressing a real limitation in existing Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection. While the writing requires improvement, the core ideas are sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the computational advantages are substantial. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of network analysis and Bayesian nonparametric methods, and its practical significance for analyzing large social networks is evident.

The paper would be suitable for NIPS given its technical soundness, clear contribution to the literature, and significant computational advantages over existing methods. With minor revisions to improve clarity and presentation, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the conference.
