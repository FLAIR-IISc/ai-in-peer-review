This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric approach for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The model is formulated as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). This formulation leverages the theory of Bayesian Rose Trees to define a prior over partitions that are consistent with a hierarchical tree structure, replacing the Chinese Restaurant Process prior used in related models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). This choice allows the marginal likelihood to factorize over the tree, which in turn enables an efficient, greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm for model selection. This factorization approach is analogous to methods used for structure learning in Bayesian networks.

**Strengths**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The theoretical exposition is both succinct and elegant, for instance in its use of generalized `f()` and `g()` functions for on- and off-diagonal block likelihoods. The proposed algorithm is clearly described.
*   **Significance & Efficiency:** The main contribution is the development of a fast, greedy agglomerative algorithm for this model class. As highlighted in Table 1, the method's O(n^2 log n) time complexity, which is independent of the number of communities, presents a significant practical advantage over iterative inference methods common in Bayesian nonparametrics. The experimental results convincingly show that BHCD can produce reasonable results orders of magnitude faster than a comparable method like the IRM.
*   **Quality:** The underlying theory is sound, building upon the well-established foundations of SBMs and Bayesian hierarchical clustering. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood and the efficient caching of sufficient statistics for the agglomerative search are technically solid.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the experimental evaluation, which is not as comprehensive as it could be.

*   **Evaluation Protocol and Metrics:** The evaluation is based on link prediction tasks (measured by AUC and predictive probability), which is an indirect way of assessing the quality of discovered communities. For community detection, it is standard practice to evaluate performance on benchmark datasets with known ground-truth partitions using clustering-specific metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI). Adding such experiments would provide a more direct and compelling assessment of the model's ability to recover meaningful structure. The current link prediction protocol should also be explained more explicitly for clarity.
*   **Scope of Comparison:** The empirical comparison is largely restricted to the IRM. To convincingly position BHCD within the field, it should be benchmarked against a wider array of state-of-the-art methods. This should include other scalable probabilistic models as well as the fast, semi-parametric, or non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering methods mentioned in the introduction.
*   **Dataset Scale:** While the method's motivation is scalability, the main quantitative comparison in Figure 4 is performed on a relatively small network of 234 nodes. The claims of efficiency and scalability would be much stronger if demonstrated on larger networks, where the computational advantages over MCMC-based samplers would be even more pronounced. Overall, the experiments feel more illustrative of the method's properties rather than a rigorous comparative analysis against the state of the art.
*   **Originality:** The work presents a novel and effective combination of existing techniques (SBMs, agglomerative clustering, tree-based priors). The contribution is more in this clever synthesis and its efficient implementation rather than in a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.

**Minor Point:**

*   In the final version, the authors might consider rephrasing sentences that begin with a citation number to improve readability, as this is unconventional in the NIPS style.

In summary, this paper introduces a fast and theoretically sound algorithm for hierarchical community detection. The clarity of the presentation and the efficiency of the proposed method are major strengths. The primary weakness is the limited scope of the experimental evaluation, which should be expanded with more standard clustering metrics, a broader range of baseline comparisons, and larger datasets to fully substantiate the method's contributions.
