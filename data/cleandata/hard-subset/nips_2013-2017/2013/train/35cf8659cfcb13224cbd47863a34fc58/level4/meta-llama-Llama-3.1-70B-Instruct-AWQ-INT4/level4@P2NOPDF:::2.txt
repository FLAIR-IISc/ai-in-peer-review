The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling the inference of a hierarchical structure rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. They introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid possible partitions of the entities. The authors then present empirical results from two corpora of social connectivity data, demonstrating that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for tasks of this kind.

This paper addresses an interesting issue that will likely appeal to a significant fraction of the NIPS audience. On the technical side, Bayesian nonparametric methods, while conceptually attractive, are often slow in practice. On the problem domain side, social scientists increasingly use sophisticated Bayesian models for analyzing data, and a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be well-received. This paper touches on both of these issues.

The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. In contrast, the IRM must consider an exponential number of possible partitions of the data. However, the relationship between the learning procedure of the trees and the generative story behind the Bayesian model is unclear. The greedy nature of the algorithm raises questions about whether it can be seen as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or finding a point MAP or ML estimate. Clarification of Section 4 would be beneficial to address this concern.

The results are impressive, with the proposed method performing comparably or slightly better than the IRM, the closest competitor, in terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive llh, accuracy). Although the asymptotic improvements in these scores may not be substantial in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

To improve the overall clarity and writing quality, several issues need to be addressed. First, distracting spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126) should be corrected. Second, the prose is sometimes hard to understand, such as the vague phrasing in lines 47-50 of the introduction (e.g., what are 'local changes' in this setting? What are the 'iterations' referring to?). The authors should clearly describe the content of the datasets they train on (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), which could confuse readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical expressions, such as Eq. 5, seem obtuse and unnecessary. Additionally, Figure 3 is confusing, and using a more familiar dataset might be helpful. Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, requires multiple readings to understand.

If these issues are addressed, the proposed approach has enough novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. The core learning algorithm is insightful, and the experimental results are strong, but the quality of writing needs improvement.
