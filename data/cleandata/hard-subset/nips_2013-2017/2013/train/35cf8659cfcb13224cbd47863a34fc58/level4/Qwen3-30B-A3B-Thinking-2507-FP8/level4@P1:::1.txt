

The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network community discovery, leveraging key insights from Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT) to define a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Their method, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), learns hierarchical community structure via a greedy agglomerative procedure that scales as O(n² log n) in the number of vertices. The paper demonstrates BHCD’s efficiency (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) and comparable predictive accuracy on Sampson’s monastery network, NIPS-234, and the full NIPS co-authorship network.  

**Quality/Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of technical contributions. The model definition, algorithm, and experimental design are logically structured and thoroughly explained. The derivation of the marginal likelihood via dynamic programming (Equation 10) and the efficient sufficient statistic caching (Equation 13) are particularly well-justified. Minor grammatical errors in the draft (e.g., "whilst... but" on p.1, line 48) should be corrected, but these do not impede understanding.  

**Originality.** While BHCD builds directly on BRT’s hierarchical mixture framework, the adaptation to relational data—particularly the maintenance of computational efficiency (O(n² log n) vs. IRM’s O(n²K²))—represents a non-trivial contribution. The paper properly situates its work against prior Bayesian hierarchical methods (e.g., [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]), emphasizing BHCD’s scalability advantage over existing approaches. The extension to sparse networks via BHCD sparse further differentiates it from prior work.  

**Significance.** Scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods to large networks is a critical challenge for practical adoption. BHCD’s O(n² log n) complexity (independent of the number of communities) and 200× speedup over IRM address this need effectively. However, the paper lacks empirical evidence on scalability to networks beyond NIPS-234 (234 nodes) or the full NIPS dataset (2,864 nodes). The authors report NIPS-234 completes in "tens of seconds" but omit the full NIPS runtime. For context, gene networks (10k+ nodes) and large social networks (e.g., Facebook, 1B+ nodes) are common benchmarks; the paper should clarify whether BHCD sparse remains feasible at such scales. Additionally, the greedy approach’s optimality gap (e.g., how close the learned tree is to the global optimum) is unaddressed. Theoretical guarantees (e.g., submodularity of the score function) would strengthen the claim of efficiency.  

**Strengths:**  
- Clear, efficient algorithm with explicit time complexity analysis (Table 1).  
- Strong empirical validation across qualitative (Sampson) and quantitative (NIPS-234) benchmarks.  
- Practical sparse variant (BHCD sparse) that improves speed on real-world networks.  
- Well-motivated hyperparameter choices and thorough comparison to IRM.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- Limited scalability analysis beyond NIPS-234 (full NIPS runtime missing).  
- No theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality or approximation guarantees.  
- Overlooks scalability challenges for very large networks (e.g., gene networks).  

**Recommendation:** The contribution is significant for practitioners seeking scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection. The paper’s technical soundness, clarity, and empirical results outweigh the scalability gaps, which are common in early-stage methodological work. The minor theoretical and scalability questions can be addressed in future work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS.  

**Pro:** Technical contribution is clear, well-executed, and addresses a meaningful scalability bottleneck.  
**Con:** Scalability to networks >10k nodes untested; no theoretical justification for greedy approach.  

The authors should report inference times for the full NIPS network (2,864 nodes) and discuss feasibility for larger networks (e.g., 10k+ nodes) in the revision. Including a brief analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality gap (e.g., via small-scale exhaustive search) would also strengthen the significance. As-is, the work is a valuable addition to the literature.
