This is a well-written paper with strong spelling, grammar, and correct citations—well above average. Please avoid beginning a sentence with a citation in the NIPS style. The theory is concise and consistent (e.g., the use of f() and g() throughout).

The paper studies stochastic block models (SBMs) and extends the simple single-table instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. This lets the CRP formulation of the IRM be replaced by the simpler Eq. (7). The factorization over subtrees follows prior work on learning Bayesian network structures (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Sec. 3), where the posterior factorizes neatly given a constrained class of structures. As a result, greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and related operations can all be carried out cleanly.

Evaluation: For SBMs, it is common to evaluate against gold-standard data where the partition structure is known, using NMI and other clustering metrics to compare against the known clustering. You report AUC, and I had to think through how this applies, since I am more familiar with NMI-based evaluations for SBMs. I presume you delete a subset of links and then predict whether they should be added back. Is that correct? In any case, the evaluation protocol needs to be clearly described (e.g., how held-out edges are selected, how negatives are constructed, the train/test split, and how AUC is computed).

Scale and baselines: Recent SBM evaluations run on larger datasets, larger than your full NIPS corpus. The small evaluation in Figure 4 is a good illustration but should be complemented with results on a larger dataset. It is also not sufficient to compare only against Bayesian nonparametric methods; there are many published semi-parametric methods (some of which you mention) that should be included as baselines.

Contribution: The main algorithmic contribution is the agglomerative method; Table 1 summarizes it well. The extension of the single-table IRM with Bayesian Rose trees yields clear theory and algorithms, though the core ideas and procedures are relatively simple. The experimental work, however, is illustrative rather than genuinely comparative, and therefore not yet adequate.
