This paper introduces a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors propose a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and an efficient, greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this structure directly from data. They demonstrate that their Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method achieves comparable or better accuracy than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) with significantly improved runtime – two orders of magnitude faster in some cases.

This work addresses an important and timely problem. The inference of community structure is central to network analysis, and the ability to model hierarchical relationships within communities is crucial for many applications. Bayesian nonparametric methods offer a principled approach to this problem, but are often computationally expensive. This paper directly tackles that challenge, proposing a method that offers both scalability and a strong theoretical foundation. The potential audience at NIPS would likely include researchers in machine learning, Bayesian statistics, and network science. The paper builds upon existing work in stochastic blockmodels [1], the infinite relational model [5, 6, 7], and Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14], framing it within a novel and compelling approach.

The key contribution is recognizing that restricting potential community structures to those consistent with a tree allows for efficient computation of the marginal likelihood. This circumvents the exponential complexity inherent in considering all possible partitions, as required by methods like the IRM. However, the connection between the greedy learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model could be clarified. Specifically, it’s not immediately obvious if the algorithm performs approximate posterior inference or is a heuristic for finding a good solution. A more detailed discussion of this point would strengthen the paper. 

The experimental results are promising. BHCD demonstrates competitive performance against the IRM on both synthetic and real-world datasets (Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network). The reported runtime improvements are substantial and represent a significant advance over existing methods. The authors also present results on the full NIPS dataset, showcasing the method’s ability to uncover meaningful hierarchical structure.

While the results are encouraging, the clarity of the presentation could be improved. There are instances of awkward phrasing and minor grammatical errors (e.g., line 126). Sections like the introduction (lines 47-50) and the description of the learning procedure (Section 4) could benefit from more precise language and further elaboration. Expanding the description of the datasets used (Sampson’s monastery and NIPS authorship) would enhance readability for a broader audience. Some equations, like Eq. 5, may be overly concise and could benefit from additional explanation. Figure 3, illustrating the monastery network results, is difficult to interpret without prior knowledge of the dataset; a clearer visualization or comparison to known ground truth would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed model and algorithm represent a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric community detection.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm offers substantial runtime improvements over existing methods like the IRM.
*   **Performance:**  Experimental results demonstrate competitive accuracy compared to state-of-the-art approaches.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The approach is grounded in a well-defined Bayesian framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The writing quality and organization could be improved. Some sections are dense and require multiple readings to fully understand.
*   **Justification of Algorithm:** A more thorough discussion of the theoretical properties and limitations of the greedy learning procedure would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to NIPS. The core idea is insightful, the experimental results are promising, and the proposed method addresses a significant challenge in network analysis. However, addressing the identified issues related to clarity and justification of the algorithm would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing these points in a revised version.
