The authors present a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of entity hierarchies rather than flat clusterings based on observed links. They introduce a learning algorithm utilizing agglomerative clustering to identify a tree structure representing potential partitions of the entities. Empirical evaluation on two social connectivity datasets demonstrates that their method achieves comparable or improved accuracy to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) – a widely-used model for this type of task – with significantly faster runtime.

This paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem for a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. Bayesian nonparametric methods, while conceptually appealing, often suffer from computational inefficiency. Simultaneously, the application of sophisticated Bayesian models is growing within the social sciences for network analysis, making a hierarchical extension to a popular model like the IRM a valuable contribution. This work bridges both of these aspects.

The key insight lies in restricting possible partitions to a tree structure, which allows for efficient computation of the marginal likelihood – a challenge for the IRM, which must consider an exponentially large number of partitions. However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model was not entirely clear. The greedy nature of the learning algorithm raises questions about whether it constitutes true posterior inference or a point estimate (MAP or ML). Clarification in Section 4 would be beneficial.

The reported results are promising. The proposed method achieves performance comparable to, or slightly exceeding, the IRM across several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy). While the absolute improvements in these scores may be modest, the reported runtime improvement over IRM inference is substantial. 

The overall clarity and writing quality could be enhanced. Numerous spelling and grammatical errors detract from readability (e.g., line 126). Certain passages are difficult to follow, such as lines 47-50 of the introduction, which contain vague terms like “local changes” and “iterations” (iterations of what?). The datasets used for evaluation (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not adequately described, potentially hindering understanding for unfamiliar readers. Some mathematical expressions (e.g., Eq. 5) appear unnecessarily complex, and Figure 3 is confusing, especially without prior knowledge of the Monastery dataset. A more widely known dataset might improve interpretability. Section 4, detailing the core contribution, required multiple readings to fully grasp. 

Addressing these issues would strengthen the paper considerably, as the proposed approach exhibits sufficient novelty and its results are strong enough to warrant publication. 

In essence, the authors propose a novel Bayesian model for graph edge structure, drawing inspiration from the IRM and resembling a Bayesian approach to agglomerative clustering. I found the core learning algorithm insightful and the experimental results convincing, though the writing requires refinement.
