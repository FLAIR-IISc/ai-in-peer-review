The authors, Bezean, present a hierarchical extension of IRM for network modeling using key ideas derived from the Bayes' tree paper: 1) that the hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over a consistent partition of nodes, 2) that this hierarchy can be learned using an efficient greedy consolidated process. Qualitative results on the Samson's monks dataset, and the full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset are presented. The proposed estimate is computationally much cheaper than IRM, while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity: The paper is very well written and the explanation of the key ideas is clear.

Originality: The main ideas are derived from the Bayes' tree task, but some work was required to adapt these ideas to a relational data setting, especially in maintaining the same computational complexity.

Significance: Scaling BNP models, especially network models, is a significant challenge if these methods are to be used by practitioners. While the n^2 scaling of the basic algorithm can prove prohibitive for large networks, the sparse version should probably remain viable. It would be good to see some time on large real-world networks. NIPS-234 only takes tens of seconds: how long does the full NIPS take? Gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are still very large: how will the estimate grow?

Furthermore, it would be good to know how close the greedy method is to the optimal tree. This can be tested on the largest scale to see if you can still find the optimal tree by tedious search. Can any theoretical support be given to the method? For example, in terms of submodularity?
