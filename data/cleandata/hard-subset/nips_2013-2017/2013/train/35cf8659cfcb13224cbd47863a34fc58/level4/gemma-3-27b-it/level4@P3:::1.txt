The authors present a Bayesian hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, building on ideas from the Bayesian rose tree literature. Their approach leverages a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels to represent hierarchical community structure, and introduces an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure for learning this hierarchy. The paper presents qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset, along with quantitative comparisons to the IRM on a subset of the NIPS data. A key contribution is the significantly reduced computational cost compared to the IRM, while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

This paper builds upon existing work in Bayesian nonparametric modeling of networks, particularly the IRM [5, 6, 7] and Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14]. It addresses a critical limitation of previous approaches – scalability – by proposing a greedy algorithm that avoids the intensive iterative processes common in IRM inference. The connection to the Bayesian rose tree model [14] is clearly articulated, and the adaptation to relational data, while not trivial, is well-motivated. Related work in hierarchical community detection, such as [8, 9, 10, 11, 12], is appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear and well-written:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the core ideas are clearly explained. The mathematical notation is presented logically, and the exposition flows smoothly.
*   **Significant contribution to scalability:** The proposed method offers a substantial improvement in computational efficiency over existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches for hierarchical community discovery. This addresses a major bottleneck in applying these methods to large-scale networks.
*   **Sound methodology:** The approach of using a tree-structured prior on partitions and a greedy agglomerative algorithm is a sensible and effective way to balance model flexibility and computational feasibility.
*   **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The comparison with the IRM on NIPS-234 is particularly convincing.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scaling to very large networks:** While the sparse version of the algorithm is promising, the O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity may still be prohibitive for extremely large networks (e.g., gene regulatory networks or massive social networks with tens of thousands or millions of nodes). Further investigation of scalability is needed.  It would be valuable to report the runtime on the full NIPS dataset.
*   **Suboptimality of greedy approach:** The greedy nature of the algorithm raises questions about its optimality. While the authors acknowledge this, providing some empirical evidence of the gap between the solution found by the greedy algorithm and the true optimal solution (at least for smaller networks where exhaustive search is feasible) would strengthen the paper. Investigating theoretical properties like submodularity could also provide insights.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameters but does not thoroughly explore their impact on performance. A more detailed sensitivity analysis could be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem – scalable Bayesian hierarchical community discovery – with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The method demonstrably outperforms existing techniques in terms of computational efficiency without sacrificing predictive accuracy.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the complex methodology.
*   The experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the algorithm to extremely large networks remains a concern.
*   The lack of a theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm’s optimality and the sensitivity to hyperparameters are minor weaknesses.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric modeling of networks. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While the scalability to very large networks and the optimality of the greedy algorithm are areas for future research, they do not detract significantly from the paper's overall value. I recommend acceptance.
