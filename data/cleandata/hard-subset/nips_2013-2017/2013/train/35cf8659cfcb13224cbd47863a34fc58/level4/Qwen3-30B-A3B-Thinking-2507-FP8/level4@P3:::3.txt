

This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods and extend them to relational data, introducing a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key innovation lies in their efficient greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that operates in O(n² log n) time, significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which scales poorly with network size. The authors correctly position their work relative to previous literature, noting that while hierarchical clustering has been applied to community detection, most approaches lack full probabilistic modeling, and existing Bayesian methods either require excessive computation or are limited to shallow hierarchies.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, with clear derivations of the marginal likelihood for tree-structured partitions (Equations 6-8) and a well-designed algorithm (Algorithm 1) that efficiently computes sufficient statistics for model selection. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of hierarchical community detection by introducing a novel caching scheme that enables O(1) computation per inner loop. The experimental evaluation, while somewhat limited, shows that BHCD achieves comparable accuracy to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster, as claimed. The ablation studies (Section 4.1) and analysis of different merger types provide useful insights into the algorithm's behavior.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors use concise notation (e.g., f(·) and g(·) for on-diagonal and off-diagonal likelihoods) to simplify complex derivations without sacrificing mathematical precision. The paper is well-organized, with logical progression from problem statement through theoretical development to experimental validation. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing how trees define consistent partitions) effectively illustrate key concepts. The writing is professional and free of grammatical errors, with appropriate citations to relevant literature.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the authors correctly note that their approach builds upon Bayesian Rose trees (Blundell et al., UAI 2006) and extends them to relational data, the core algorithmic idea of greedy agglomerative model selection is not entirely novel. However, the specific application to hierarchical community detection with the novel caching scheme represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper adequately references previous work on hierarchical community detection and Bayesian nonparametric models, though it could more explicitly differentiate itself from related work like the Mondrian process (Roy and Teh, NIPS 2008).

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the scope of the experimental evaluation. While the authors correctly compare BHCD to IRM (the fastest Bayesian nonparametric method for this task), the evaluation relies primarily on small datasets (Sampson's monastery network and a subset of NIPS co-authorship data). The full NIPS dataset analysis (2864 vertices) is promising but lacks comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods for hierarchical community detection. The paper claims "comparable or better accuracy" but does not provide standard clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) that would allow direct comparison with gold-standard community structures. The use of AUC for link prediction is reasonable but requires clearer explanation of how it's computed for hierarchical structures. The authors should clarify how they handle missing data in the Sampson network (which has negative affiliations as observed absent edges) and why they use different evaluation metrics for different experiments.

The paper's main strengths are its elegant theoretical development, efficient algorithm design, and clear presentation. The main weaknesses are the limited empirical validation against recent methods and the lack of comprehensive evaluation on larger datasets. While the speed advantage is compelling, the paper would benefit from demonstrating BHCD's performance on networks with more than 1000 vertices to show scalability beyond the NIPS-234 dataset. The claim that BHCD "achieves comparable or better accuracy" would be more convincing with direct comparisons to non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Presents a novel, efficient algorithm for hierarchical community detection with clear theoretical foundation
- Demonstrates significant speed improvements over state-of-the-art Bayesian methods
- Well-written with excellent mathematical clarity
- Addresses an important problem in network analysis

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental evaluation on smaller datasets without comparison to recent methods
- Lacks standard clustering metrics (NMI, etc.) for hierarchical structure validation
- Insufficient explanation of AUC-based evaluation for hierarchical community detection
- Does not demonstrate scalability to very large networks

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to the efficient discovery of hierarchical community structure. While the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited, the theoretical and algorithmic contributions are solid, and the paper makes a meaningful step toward scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection. I recommend acceptance, though the authors should address the evaluation limitations in a revised version. The work would be strengthened by expanding the experimental section to include comparisons with more recent methods and larger datasets, as well as clearer explanations of evaluation metrics.
