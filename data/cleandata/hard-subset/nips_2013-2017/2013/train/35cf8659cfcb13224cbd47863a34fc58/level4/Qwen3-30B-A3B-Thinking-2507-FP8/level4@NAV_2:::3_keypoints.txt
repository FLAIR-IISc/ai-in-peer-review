- Introduces BHCD: a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community discovery.  
- Replaces IRM’s CRP prior with a tree-based prior using Bayesian Rose tree theory to factorize marginal likelihood.  
- Achieves O(n² log n) time complexity and reports ~100× speedup over IRM while maintaining comparable accuracy.  
- Uses a greedy agglomerative inference algorithm derived and justified theoretically (Algorithm 1, eq. 7–8, eq. 10).  
- Employs conjugate priors (Beta-Bernoulli) for computational efficiency, but sensitivity to hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ) is not fully addressed.  
- Demonstrated on Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data (NIPS-234), with additional supplementary hierarchical results.  
- Experimental validation is adequate for the shown datasets but lacks scalability tests on networks with thousands of nodes.  
- AUC-based link prediction evaluation is used but the method for selecting held-out edges and the suitability of AUC versus clustering metrics (e.g., NMI) is unclear.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with precise notation, clear algorithms, and effective figures.  
- Minor clarity improvements suggested: avoid sentence-starting citations and expand the AUC explanation in Sec. 5.2.  
- Core contribution is novel: applying Bayesian Rose tree theory to hierarchical community detection and avoiding IRM’s computational bottlenecks.  
- Does not sufficiently contrast with recent hierarchical SBM or semi-parametric methods (e.g., [11], [18], [21]) in experiments or discussion.  
- Sparse variant (BHCD sparse) improves efficiency on real-world sparse networks.  
- Hierarchical visualization on full NIPS is illustrative but lacks rigorous validation against domain ground truth.  
- Pros: strong theory, clear algorithmic innovation, excellent writing, practical speedups, and a sparse variant for sparse graphs.  
- Cons: insufficient large-scale experiments (>1,000 nodes), unclear AUC methodology, limited comparisons to recent state-of-the-art, and descriptive rather than validated hierarchical results.  
- Overall judgment: technically sound and promising but borderline for acceptance without expanded large-scale evaluation and clearer methodological details for link prediction.
