- Introduces BHCD: a greedy agglomerative algorithm that learns tree-structured mixtures of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community discovery.

- Main technical contribution: a caching scheme for blockmodel sufficient statistics enabling O(1) updates and worst-case O(n² log n) runtime independent of community count.

- Strength — Efficiency & scalability: achieves comparable or better predictive accuracy than IRM while being ~100x faster in experiments.

- Strength — Clarity of presentation: paper is well-written, well-motivated, and connects clearly to prior work on blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering.

- Strength — Probabilistic framework: provides a full probabilistic model enabling Bayes-factor-driven model selection and principled predictive distributions for link prediction.

- Weakness — Limited experimental comparison: evaluation only against the flat IRM and lacks direct comparisons to other hierarchical network models (e.g., Mondrian Process, multiscale community blockmodel).

- Weakness — Technical clarity gaps: claims of an O(n+m) dynamic programming likelihood computation are not detailed; derivations/justifications for the sufficient-statistic updates (Eq. 13) are sparse.

- Weakness — Originality concerns: conceptually a direct application of Bayesian Rose Trees to SBMs, with novelty mainly in efficient implementation rather than a new modeling or inference paradigm.

- Weakness — Terminology imprecision: labeling the method "Bayesian nonparametric" is debatable—it's better seen as model selection over tree structures than a classical nonparametric model.

- Arguments for acceptance: proposes a fast, practical, well-motivated algorithm with a sound caching scheme, strong speedups over a baseline, and clear exposition.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental benchmarking against other hierarchical methods, limited conceptual novelty, and omitted technical details that hinder reproducibility.
