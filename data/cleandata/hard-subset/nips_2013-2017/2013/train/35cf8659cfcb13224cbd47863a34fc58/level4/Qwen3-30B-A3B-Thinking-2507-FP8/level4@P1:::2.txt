

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors extend Bayesian nonparametric methods to the hierarchical setting by modeling community structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Unlike previous approaches that either require a fixed number of communities or struggle with computational complexity, BHCD efficiently learns hierarchical community structure through a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm. The authors build upon the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) but overcome its computational limitations by restricting partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, enabling exact marginal likelihood calculation through dynamic programming.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on community detection. Previous work on hierarchical community structure has either been computationally expensive (e.g., [8, 9, 10]) or lacked a full probabilistic framework (e.g., [3, 4]). BHCD addresses both limitations: it provides a complete probabilistic model while achieving significant computational improvements over existing Bayesian approaches. The authors carefully relate their work to prior contributions, noting how their tree-structured approach differs from methods that require fixed hierarchy depth [11] or limit to depth-two hierarchies [9, 10].

The key technical insight is that restricting partitions to be consistent with a tree structure allows the marginal likelihood to be computed efficiently (in O(n+m) time) rather than requiring exponential computation over all possible partitions. The authors then develop a greedy agglomerative algorithm (Algorithm 1) that builds the tree in a bottom-up fashion, merging trees that maximize the Bayes factor. This approach achieves O(n² log n) time complexity while maintaining comparable accuracy to the IRM, with empirical results showing BHCD runs two orders of magnitude faster.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate BHCD's performance on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS co-authorship network. For the NIPS-234 dataset, BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to IRM but in a fraction of the time (approximately 10 seconds vs. 1000 seconds). The sparse variant of BHCD shows particular promise for large sparse networks, as it avoids unnecessary computations on disconnected components.

The paper has several strengths. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear connections to previous work on stochastic blockmodels [1, 2] and hierarchical clustering [13, 14]. The computational complexity analysis is thorough and well-motivated, with the authors carefully comparing BHCD to other approaches in Table 1. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate both the speed and accuracy advantages of their method.

However, the paper has some weaknesses that need addressing. The most significant issue is the lack of clarity regarding the relationship between the greedy learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian model. The authors state BHCD is a "greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method," but the greedy algorithm does not correspond to true posterior inference or MAP estimation over tree structures. This disconnect between the generative model and the learning procedure requires clearer explanation. Section 4, which describes the core algorithm, is particularly difficult to follow without multiple readings.

The writing quality requires substantial improvement. The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that distract from the technical content. For instance, the draft's description of "local changes" (line 47-50) is vague and could be clarified by specifying that these refer to small updates in MCMC algorithms. The description of the monastery dataset (Figure 3) would benefit from more context for readers unfamiliar with this classic social network. The paper also includes unnecessary mathematical details (e.g., Equation 5) that obscure the main contribution.

I recommend the authors revise the manuscript to:
1) Clearly explain the relationship between the greedy learning procedure and the Bayesian model
2) Improve the organization and clarity of Section 4
3) Provide more context for the datasets used in experiments
4) Correct grammatical errors and improve overall writing quality

Despite these issues, the technical contribution is valuable and well-supported by the experiments. The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis with a solution that offers both theoretical and practical advantages over existing approaches. The computational efficiency of BHCD makes it particularly appealing for large-scale network analysis, which is increasingly important in social network studies.

To summarize the key points for the program committee:

Pros:
- Novel extension of Bayesian community detection to hierarchical structure
- Significant computational improvements over existing Bayesian methods
- Strong empirical results demonstrating comparable accuracy with much faster runtime
- Clear theoretical foundation with connections to prior work
- Well-designed experiments on relevant real-world datasets

Cons:
- Unclear relationship between greedy learning procedure and Bayesian model
- Section 4 (core algorithm) requires substantial clarification
- Writing quality needs significant improvement (grammar, clarity)
- Some dataset descriptions lack sufficient context

The paper's contribution represents a meaningful advance in the field, and with the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS. The technical content is sound, the results are compelling, and the approach addresses a genuine limitation in current methods. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues.
