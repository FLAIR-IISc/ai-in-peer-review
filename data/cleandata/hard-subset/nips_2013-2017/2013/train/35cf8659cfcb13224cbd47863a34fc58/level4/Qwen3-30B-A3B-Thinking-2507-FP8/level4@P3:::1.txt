

The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical clustering (specifically, the Bayesian Rose Tree framework) and adapt these ideas to relational data, creating a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. BHCD efficiently learns hierarchical community structure through a greedy agglomerative procedure that operates in O(n² log n) time, where n is the number of vertices. The authors demonstrate that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being approximately two orders of magnitude faster, with worst-case scaling independent of the number of nested communities.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-supported claims. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation, including marginal likelihood calculations and dynamic programming for efficient inference. The experimental design is appropriate, with comparisons to IRM on multiple datasets including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness through both qualitative (hierarchical structure visualization) and quantitative (predictive performance) evaluations. The paper carefully addresses strengths and limitations, particularly noting that the greedy approach may not always find the globally optimal tree. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed theoretical analysis of the greedy procedure's optimality guarantees.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide clear definitions of key concepts (e.g., tree-consistent partitions, sufficient statistics) and effectively motivate each component of their approach. The algorithm description is precise and accompanied by helpful figures. The presentation of results is logical and easy to follow, with the supplementary materials providing additional context. The paper adheres to the standard of clarity expected in NIPS publications, enabling experts to reproduce the results.

Originality: The core idea of using a tree-structured mixture over consistent partitions is adapted from Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al., UAI 2006), but the authors make a significant contribution by extending this framework to relational data. The adaptation required careful consideration of how to maintain computational efficiency while handling the relational structure. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by avoiding the computational bottlenecks of existing Bayesian hierarchical approaches (e.g., [8, 9, 10, 11]) while still providing a full probabilistic model. While the main ideas are not entirely novel, the specific adaptation to network data represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

Significance: The paper addresses an important practical challenge in network analysis: scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods to larger networks. The authors convincingly demonstrate that BHCD can make reasonable predictions in tens of seconds on the NIPS-234 dataset, whereas IRM requires approximately 1000 seconds. This computational advantage makes the method more accessible to practitioners. While the paper acknowledges that the n² scaling might be prohibitive for extremely large networks, the sparse variant (BHCD sparse) should be feasible for many real-world applications. The paper would be strengthened by additional scaling analysis on larger networks (e.g., full NIPS dataset timing, gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes) and more detailed exploration of how close the greedy method comes to the optimal solution.

Pro and Con for Acceptance:
- PRO: Significant computational improvement over existing Bayesian hierarchical methods while maintaining comparable predictive performance; well-written and technically sound; clear contribution to scaling Bayesian nonparametric network models
- CON: Limited scaling analysis on extremely large networks; lack of theoretical guarantees for the greedy optimization; need for more detailed comparison of hierarchical structure quality

Specific suggestions for improvement:
1. Include timing results for the full NIPS dataset (2864 vertices, 9466 edges) to demonstrate scalability beyond the NIPS-234 subset.
2. Conduct a small-scale study comparing BHCD's hierarchical structure to optimal solutions (via exhaustive search) to quantify the quality loss from the greedy approach.
3. Consider adding theoretical analysis of the greedy procedure's optimality (e.g., whether the objective function exhibits submodularity properties).
4. Clarify the relationship between the hyperparameter γ and the resulting tree structure in the main text.
5. In line 48, "whilst" should be followed by "but" (not "but" alone).

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by adapting Bayesian hierarchical clustering to network data with significant computational advantages. While the paper could be strengthened with additional scaling analysis and theoretical insights, it presents a technically sound, well-written approach that addresses an important practical challenge in network analysis. The contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS, particularly given the demonstrated computational advantages over existing methods.
