

Reviewer 1: The paper is well-written with correct spelling, grammar, and citations, placing it well above average. Please avoid beginning sentences with citations in NIPS style. The theoretical presentation is succinct in key parts, particularly through consistent use of f() and g() notation.  

The paper extends the single-table instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose tree theory, enabling replacement of the CRP formulation with the simpler Equation (7). The factorization over subtrees aligns with established work on Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3), where posterior factorization under constrained structures permits efficient implementation of greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, sufficient statistic handling, and link prediction.  

Evaluation of SBM typically employs "gold standard" data with known partition structures, using metrics like NMI. The paper reports AUC, but the methodology for computing it requires explicit justification (e.g., whether links are deleted and predicted for reconstruction), as this is non-standard for SBM evaluation. The experimental section relies on a small dataset (Figure 4), which is insufficient; recent SBM benchmarks use substantially larger datasets, so results must be validated on larger-scale data. Comparative evaluation against semi-parametric methods (beyond Bayesian non-parametric baselines) is also inadequate, given the existence of multiple published alternatives.  

The core contribution is the agglomerative algorithm, clearly summarized in Table 1. While the theory and algorithms are well-explained and conceptually straightforward, the experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, lacking the rigor required for a strong contribution.
