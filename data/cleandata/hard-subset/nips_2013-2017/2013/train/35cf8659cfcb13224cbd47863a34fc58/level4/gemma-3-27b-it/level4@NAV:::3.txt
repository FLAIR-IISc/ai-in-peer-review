The paper is exceptionally well-written, demonstrating strong command of spelling, grammar, and citation practices – significantly above average in these aspects. A minor stylistic note: avoid beginning sentences with citations, adhering to NIPS guidelines. The theoretical presentation is concise and effective, particularly through the consistent use of notation like f() and g().

This work builds upon stochastic block models (SBM) by extending the standard single-table instance of an Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework. This extension leverages the theory of Bayesian Rose trees, enabling a simplification of the IRM formulation from a Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) to the more direct equation (7). This factorization across subtrees mirrors established techniques in Bayesian network structure learning, such as the approach detailed in Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3).  This parallel allows for clean posterior factorization based on constrained structure classes, facilitating efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction.

Evaluation of SBM typically relies on “gold standard” datasets with known partition structures, utilizing metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) to compare results against these known clusterings. The authors report employing Area Under the Curve (AUC), which initially lacked clarity given my familiarity with NMI-based SBM evaluations. I infer that the approach involves link deletion followed by prediction of link re-addition – confirmation of this would be beneficial.  Regardless, the evaluation methodology requires a more thorough explanation. While the current evaluation (Figure 4) is well-executed, it is performed on datasets smaller than those commonly used in recent SBM literature (including larger than the NIPS dataset). Therefore, presenting results on a larger dataset would strengthen the empirical validation.  Furthermore, comparisons should extend beyond Bayesian non-parametric methods to include relevant semi-parametric approaches, some of which are acknowledged within the paper. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1.

In summary, this paper presents a clear and theoretically sound extension of IRM using Bayesian Rose trees, featuring well-defined algorithms. However, the experimental evaluation is more illustrative than comparative and, as such, remains insufficient to fully demonstrate the method's capabilities.
