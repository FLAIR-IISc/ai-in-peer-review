This paper presents an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical community detection in networks. The core methodology builds heavily upon existing work on Bayesian rose trees [12], retaining the prior over trees and overall algorithmic structure. The key contribution lies in adapting the likelihood function to network data and developing efficient methods for caching and updating sufficient statistics during the agglomerative clustering process. The paper is generally well-written and technically sound, with a clear presentation; however, Section 3 is particularly dense, and the notation surrounding equation (9) and its subsequent derivations could benefit from simplification for improved clarity.

The significance of this work rests on demonstrating the benefits of incorporating hierarchical structure into relational data modeling. However, the experimental evaluation is limited by a lack of comparison to other existing hierarchical network models. While the results favorably compare against a baseline with a flat community structure, they do not establish whether this approach outperforms other methods specifically designed for hierarchical community detection. For instance, relevant related work such as Ho et al. (2012) – utilizing a nested Chinese Restaurant Process – and Roy et al. (2009) – employing the Mondrian Process to generate hierarchical structures over K-d trees – are not discussed. These Bayesian nonparametric models offer alternative approaches to discovering refined latent structures, and their omission represents a significant gap in the related work section.

The originality of the work stems from the adaptation of the Bayesian rose tree framework to a stochastic block model likelihood and the resulting efficiency gains in agglomerative clustering. Specifically, the likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, a noteworthy achievement. However, the description of this computational efficiency, particularly the dynamic programming aspect alluded to before equation (10), is unclear and lacks sufficient detail. The justification for the recursive updates of sufficient statistics also needs further explanation; while equations are provided, the underlying reasoning isn’t fully articulated. 

The use of the term “nonparametric” is questionable. Following Blundell et al. [12], this model appears to be framed as a model selection problem, contrasting it with true nonparametric approaches as they discuss. This interpretation aligns with the original rose tree work.

The experiments, including the qualitative analysis on the Sampson network and the comparison to the IRM, effectively illustrate the advantages of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. However, the most significant limitation remains the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. While scalability is mentioned, the networks used in the evaluation are not sufficiently large to thoroughly assess this aspect.

In summary, the paper successfully integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering for hierarchical community discovery. Despite demonstrating effectiveness in experiments, the lack of comparison to alternative hierarchical network models limits the assessment of its relative performance and broader impact.
