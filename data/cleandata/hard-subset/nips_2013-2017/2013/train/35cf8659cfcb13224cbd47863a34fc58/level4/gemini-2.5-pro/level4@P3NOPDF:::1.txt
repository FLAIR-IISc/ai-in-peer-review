This paper introduces a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling. The authors leverage key ideas from the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) framework, proposing a model where a hierarchy over nodes is used to specify a mixture over consistent partitions. A central contribution is an efficient, greedy agglomerative procedure for learning this hierarchy. The method is evaluated through qualitative results on the Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, and quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed inference scheme is significantly more computationally efficient than the standard IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and effective exposition of the core concepts. The motivation is well-established, and the connection to both the IRM and the BRT is explained logically, making the paper easy to follow.

### Originality

While the core ideas are adapted from the Bayesian Rose Tree framework, their application to relational data is non-trivial. The authors have successfully translated these concepts to the network modeling domain, and the work required to do so—particularly in maintaining the computational complexity of the greedy learning algorithm—represents a solid contribution.

### Significance

The work addresses a critical challenge in the field: scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially for network analysis. Making these powerful models more practical for real-world applications is an important and impactful research direction. The proposed method is a valuable step in this direction.

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points regarding scalability and the optimality of the inference procedure:

1.  **Scalability Analysis:** A key claim of the paper is improved computational efficiency. While the results on the NIPS-234 dataset are promising (inference in tens of seconds), the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of its scalability. The O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may prove prohibitive for larger networks.
    *   What is the runtime on the full NIPS dataset? Providing this timing would offer a more complete picture.
    *   How would the proposed method, particularly the sparse variant mentioned, scale to larger, real-world networks such as gene interaction networks (tens of thousands of nodes) or large social networks? Including experiments or even a detailed projection of performance on such networks would provide practitioners with a clearer understanding of the method's practical limits.

2.  **Optimality of the Greedy Approach:** The paper relies on a greedy agglomerative procedure for learning the hierarchy. It would be beneficial to provide the reader with a better sense of how close this greedy approach comes to finding the optimal tree.
    *   An empirical investigation could be valuable. For instance, on a dataset small enough for an exhaustive search to be feasible, how does the greedy solution compare to the optimal one?
    *   Is it possible to provide any theoretical justification for the greedy method's performance? For example, does the objective function exhibit properties like submodularity that would provide guarantees for the greedy algorithm? Exploring this could lend further theoretical weight to the proposed method.

### Conclusion

While the ideas presented are familiar from the BRT literature, their adaptation to network modeling is a significant and valuable contribution. The paper is well-executed and addresses an important problem. Despite the suggestions for a more comprehensive scalability analysis and investigation into optimality, the work is a solid step forward for probabilistic network modeling. Therefore, I recommend that this paper be accepted.
