The authors present a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modelling, building upon key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree literature: 1) leveraging a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and 2) employing an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure for learning this hierarchy. Qualitative results are demonstrated on Sampson’s monastery network and the full NIPS dataset, complemented by quantitative results on a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network (NIPS-234). The proposed inference method exhibits significantly lower computational cost than the IRM, while achieving comparable predictive performance.

Quality and Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, and the exposition of the core concepts is clear and concise. The authors effectively convey the technical details of their model and algorithm.

Originality: The foundational ideas draw from the Bayesian rose tree work. However, substantial effort was required to adapt these concepts to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining favorable computational complexity. This adaptation represents a valuable contribution.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, particularly for network data, remains a crucial challenge for broader adoption by practitioners. While the O(n<sup>2</sup>) scaling of the basic algorithm may limit its application to very large networks, the sparse version holds promise for improved feasibility. It would be valuable to see timing results on larger, real-world networks. For example, NIPS-234 takes tens of seconds – how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Gene networks often comprise tens of thousands of nodes, and real-world social networks are significantly larger; understanding how inference scales in these contexts is essential. Furthermore, assessing the quality of the greedy algorithm’s solution compared to the optimal tree (determined via exhaustive search, when feasible) would be insightful. Providing theoretical support for the method, such as through arguments relating to submodularity, could further strengthen the contribution.

Despite the familiarity of the underlying ideas from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is significant. This paper should be accepted.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in scaling BNP models to network data.
*   The proposed method achieves a substantial speedup compared to existing approaches like the IRM, with comparable predictive performance.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on multiple datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The O(n<sup>2</sup>) scaling may limit applicability to very large networks, though the sparse version mitigates this.
*   The theoretical justification for the greedy algorithm could be strengthened.
*   Scaling to even larger networks and presenting performance results would be beneficial.
