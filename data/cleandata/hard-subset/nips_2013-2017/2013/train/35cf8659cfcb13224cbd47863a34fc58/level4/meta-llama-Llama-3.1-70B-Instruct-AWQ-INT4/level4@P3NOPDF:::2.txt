The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to the hierarchical setting, enabling the inference of a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. They develop a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree representing valid possible partitions of the entities. The authors then present empirical results from two corpora of social connectivity data, demonstrating that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for tasks of this kind.

This paper tackles an interesting issue that a significant fraction of the NIPS audience will be interested in. From a technical perspective, Bayesian nonparametric methods, while attractive conceptually, are often slow in practice. From a problem domain perspective, social scientists are increasingly using sophisticated Bayesian models for analyzing data, so a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be appreciated. This paper addresses both issues.

The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. In contrast, the IRM must consider the exponential number of possible partitions of the data. However, I was left somewhat confused about how the learning procedure of the trees relates to the generative story behind the Bayesian model. Since the algorithm is greedy, I do not think it can be seen as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or finding some kind of point MAP or ML estimate. I would recommend that the authors improve the clarity of Section 4 to address this concern.

I found the results to be impressive. In terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performed comparably or slightly better than the IRM, the closest competitor. While the asymptotic improvements in these scores may not be impressive in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

However, I think the overall clarity and writing quality could be improved. There are several distracting spelling and grammar errors, such as the one on line 126. The prose is sometimes hard to understand, for instance, the vague phrasing in lines 47-50 of the introduction (what are 'local changes' in this setting? What are the 'iterations' referring to? Iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The authors do not clearly describe the content of the datasets they train on (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship), which could confuse readers unfamiliar with them. I occasionally found the math somewhat obtuse and unnecessary, such as Eq. 5. I also found Figure 3 confusing, as it is visually unclear which method is performing best, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the monastery dataset. Using a more familiar dataset might be helpful. Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, required several readings to understand.

If these issues could be remedied, I believe the proposed approach has enough novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning algorithm to be insightful and the experimental results to be strong, but the quality of writing needs improvement.
