

The paper is well-written with excellent grammar, spelling, and citation accuracy. While the theoretical presentation is concise in parts (e.g., consistent use of f() and g()), avoid beginning sentences with citations in NIPS style. The work extends the single-table IRM by integrating Bayesian Rose tree theory, enabling the replacement of the CRP formulation with the simpler Eq. (7). The subtree factorization aligns with Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, Sec. 3), where posterior factorization over constrained structures facilitates efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, sufficient statistics handling, and link prediction.  

Evaluation of SBM typically employs "gold standard" partition data with metrics like NMI. Your use of AUC requires explicit justification—presumably by link deletion/reconstruction—but this is unclear and needs elaboration. The current evaluation (Figure 4) uses a small dataset, whereas recent SBM work scales to larger networks; thus, results should be validated on bigger data. Comparisons must extend beyond Bayesian non-parametric methods to include established semi-parametric alternatives cited in the paper.  

The algorithm’s contribution lies in its agglomerative approach, well-summarized in Table 1. While the theory and algorithms are clear and conceptually simple, the experimental section is illustrative rather than comparative, rendering the empirical evaluation insufficient.
