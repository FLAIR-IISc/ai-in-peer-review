The paper proposes a scalable agglomerative approach to clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over trees and algorithm structure are largely unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees, but the likelihood has been modified to accommodate networks. The authors have also made adjustments to enable efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear, and the experiments demonstrate favorable results compared to a baseline model with a flat community structure. However, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are noticeably absent.

The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. Section 3, in particular, is dense and could potentially benefit from simpler notation and explanations, especially for equation (9) and the concepts that build upon it.

The significance of the paper is somewhat diminished by the limited comparisons to other models. The experiments primarily demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets, but do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against existing hierarchical approaches. For instance, the "multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009) are two Bayesian nonparametric models that also model latent community structure in a hierarchical fashion. The paper fails to acknowledge and compare against these models, which may be capable of discovering more refined latent structures.

The originality of the paper lies in the adaptation of the Bayesian rose tree model to accommodate network data and the development of an efficient greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm. The likelihood computation in O(Nodes + Edges) time is a notable feature, but its description could be improved. The use of dynamic programming and recursive updating of sufficient statistics is not thoroughly explained, making it challenging to understand the underlying mechanics.

The terminology used in the paper is also questionable. The authors describe their approach as "nonparametric," whereas Blundell et al. (2012) frame the rose tree approach as a model selection method, distinct from nonparametric methods. This interpretation seems more accurate.

The experiments, including the qualitative analysis on Sampson's data and the comparison to the IRM, are well-executed and effectively demonstrate the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a significant limitation. The discussion on scalability is also limited, as the tested networks are not particularly large.

In conclusion, the paper successfully integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. While the experiments show promising results, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a notable omission.
