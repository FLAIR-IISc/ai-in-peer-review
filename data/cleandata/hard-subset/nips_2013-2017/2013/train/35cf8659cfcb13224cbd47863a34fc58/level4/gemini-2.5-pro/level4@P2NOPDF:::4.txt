This paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical community detection in networks. The method adapts the Bayesian rose tree framework, which provides a prior over hierarchical partitions, by incorporating a stochastic block model likelihood suitable for relational data. The main technical contribution lies in generalizing the efficient, greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to this network setting, enabling the recursive updating of sufficient statistics. While the presentation is generally clear and experiments demonstrate the benefits of a hierarchical model over a flat baseline, the paper's primary limitation is the absence of comparisons to other contemporary hierarchical network models.

**Significance and Originality**

The core model is a direct application of the Bayesian rose tree [12] to network data, using a standard stochastic block model likelihood. The primary novelty is therefore not in the probabilistic model itself, but in the derivation of an efficient inference scheme. The authors show how the sufficient statistics for the network likelihood can be recursively updated during the greedy agglomerative clustering procedure, which is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper's assessment of its contribution is hampered by a limited discussion of related work. Comparisons are restricted to a non-hierarchical baseline (the Infinite Relational Model), which effectively demonstrates the general utility of modeling hierarchy, but fails to establish the superiority of this specific approach over other hierarchical methods. Notably, the paper does not cite or compare against other relevant Bayesian nonparametric models that also induce hierarchical latent structures for networks, such as the nested CRP-based model of Ho et al. (2012) or the Mondrian Process of Roy et al. (2009). Including such comparisons would be essential for properly contextualizing the proposed method's performance and originality.

**Technical Soundness and Clarity**

The paper appears to be technically sound, and for the most part, the presentation is clear. However, certain key sections could be significantly improved. Section 3, particularly the notation and explanation surrounding equation (9) and its subsequent development, is very dense and difficult to parse. A simpler or more intuitive explanation would benefit the reader.

Furthermore, the description of the algorithm for computing the likelihood in O(|V| + |E|) time is underdeveloped. The text alludes to dynamic programming before equation (10), but the details of this procedure are not provided, making it difficult to understand and verify. Similarly, while the equations for recursively updating the sufficient statistics are given, the paper would be stronger if it included more intuition or a brief derivation to help the reader understand why these updates are correct.

**Experiments**

The experimental evaluation provides some useful insights but is ultimately limited. The qualitative results on the Sampson monastery dataset are illustrative, and the quantitative comparison against the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively highlights the predictive advantages of a hierarchical approach that averages over multiple structures. However, as noted previously, the most significant limitation is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models. Without these baselines, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers a state-of-the-art solution. Additionally, while the paper discusses scalability as a motivation, the networks used in the experiments are not particularly large. Testing the method on larger-scale networks would be necessary to fully substantiate the claims regarding its scalability.

**Terminology**

A minor point on terminology: The use of the term "nonparametric" to describe this method is questionable. The original Bayesian rose tree paper [12] explicitly frames the approach in terms of Bayesian model selection over a discrete set of tree structures, contrasting it with traditional nonparametric methods. Adopting this more precise terminology would improve the paper's framing.
