- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model that constrains partitions by a tree so marginal likelihoods are computed efficiently via dynamic programming.  
- Proposes a greedy agglomerative algorithm that merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor, returning a single hierarchical tree.  
- Evaluated on Sampson's Monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks; achieves comparable or better predictive performance than IRM while being ~100x faster.  
- Strength: addresses a significant scalability challenge, providing a practical method likely to be widely used.  
- Strength: original combination of ideas—extends hierarchical clustering to relational data and leverages tree-consistency for tractable likelihoods.  
- Strength: empirical results convincingly demonstrate large speed-ups with competitive accuracy against IRM.  
- Weakness: clarity and presentation are poor, with numerous grammatical errors and dense, unclear prose.  
- Weakness: inference procedure justification is weak—greedy search is not posterior inference and its limitations (e.g., local optima) are not discussed.  
- Needs clearer algorithm description: intuitive explanation and examples for tree-consistent partitions, merge-score computation, and merge types.  
- Needs better dataset descriptions: clarify what nodes and edges represent for the Sampson and NIPS networks.  
- Needs improved figures and visualization: Figure 3 is hard to interpret—annotate results or add quantitative comparisons to relate discovered blocks to ground truth.  
- Summary: promising, efficient approach but requires substantial rewriting and clearer methodological justification before publication.
