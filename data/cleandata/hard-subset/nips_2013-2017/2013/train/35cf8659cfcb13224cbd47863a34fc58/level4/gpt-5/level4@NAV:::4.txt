Review- Presents an agglomerative method for scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (potentially non-binary) trees and the overall algorithmic framework closely mirror earlier work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted in a straightforward way for network data, and with some additional care the necessary sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently. The exposition is generally clear and the experiments compare favorably to a flat community baseline, but comparisons against other hierarchical network models are missing.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. Section 3 is quite dense; there may be a clearer way to notate and explain equation (9) and the subsequent derivations that rely on it. Because the comparisons are confined to models without hierarchical latent structure, the experiments mainly demonstrate that incorporating hierarchy benefits relational data, but do not establish superiority over existing hierarchical approaches. For instance, the “multiscale community blockmodel for network exploration” (Ho et al., 2012) uses a nested CRP, and the “Mondrian Process” (Roy et al., 2009) defines a stochastic process yielding hierarchical structures over k-d trees as a prior. The paper does not discuss these two Bayesian nonparametric models, which could uncover more granular latent structures.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The model is essentially a Bayesian rose tree paired with a standard stochastic block-model likelihood. The main novelty lies in how the new likelihood alters the recursive updating of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative procedure. The likelihood can be evaluated in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a strong practical advantage. Unfortunately, the explanation is not very clear—there is a reference to dynamic programming before equation (10), but the mechanics are hard to follow. Likewise, for the recursive sufficient-statistics updates, formulas are given but with limited intuition or justification.

TERMINOLOGY:
It is debatable whether “nonparametric” is the right descriptor—Blundell et al. [12] frame rose trees as model selection and explicitly contrast them with nonparametric methods in their discussion. I share that view.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative analysis on the Sampson data is solid, and the comparison to the IRM nicely illustrates the benefit of averaging over many hypotheses for prediction. The main shortcoming remains the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network methods. While scalability is discussed, the evaluated networks are not especially large.

Overall, the paper effectively combines prior work on Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic block-model likelihood for networks, extending efficient agglomerative clustering to this setting. It yields effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but lacks comparisons to alternative hierarchical network models.
