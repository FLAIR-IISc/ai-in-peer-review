This paper presents a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling. The approach leverages key ideas from the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) paper: firstly, that a hierarchy can specify a mixture over consistent node partitions, and secondly, that this hierarchy can be learned using an efficient, greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors present qualitative results on the Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, along with quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference method is computationally much cheaper than the standard IRM, yet it achieves similar predictive performance.

**Quality and Clarity**
The paper is well-written, and the exposition of the core ideas is clear.

**Originality**
While the main ideas are adapted from the BRT framework, the authors have made a non-trivial contribution in applying them to the relational data setting. A key challenge, which the authors address, was maintaining the same computational complexity as the original BRT method.

**Significance**
Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models, particularly for network data, is an important challenge for their practical adoption. While the O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version described should remain feasible for many applications. However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

*   To better assess the method's scalability, it would be beneficial to see timing results on larger, real-world networks. For instance, given that the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process, how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Furthermore, how would the inference time scale to networks with tens of thousands of nodes (e.g., gene networks) or even larger social networks?

*   It would be valuable to understand how close the greedy method's solution is to the optimal tree. This could be investigated empirically on a small-scale problem where the optimal tree can still be found through exhaustive search.

*   The authors could also strengthen the paper by providing theoretical support for the greedy approach. For example, are there any connections to concepts like submodularity that might offer performance guarantees?

**Minor Corrections**
*   Line 48, page 1: The phrase "whilst... but" is redundant.
*   There are minor typos such as "pair of treeS" and "A fruitful next steps".

**Recommendation**
Despite the conceptual lineage from the BRT work, the adaptation to network modeling is a significant contribution. I recommend that this paper be accepted.
