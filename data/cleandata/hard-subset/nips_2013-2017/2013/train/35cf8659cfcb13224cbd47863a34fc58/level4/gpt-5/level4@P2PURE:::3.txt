Well-written paper with strong spelling, grammar, and correct citations—well above average on these points. However, per NIPS style, avoid starting sentences with citations. The theory is concise in places, for example through the consistent use of f() and g().

The paper studies stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the simple single-table instance of the IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose Trees. This enables replacing the CRP formulation of the IRM with the simpler Eq. (7). The factorization over subtrees parallels work on Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Section 3), where the posterior factorizes cleanly under constrained structure classes, thereby allowing neat handling of greedy search, marginal likelihoods, sufficient statistics, and link prediction.

SBM evaluation is often performed against gold-standard datasets with known partitions, using metrics like NMI and related clustering measures. You report AUC, and I had to think about how this was computed, since I am more familiar with NMI-based evaluations for SBM. I presume you remove a subset of links and evaluate whether the model predicts them (i.e., link prediction). If so, please make this protocol explicit. Also, recent SBM evaluations run on datasets larger than your full NIPS dataset; the small evaluation in Figure 4 is useful but should be complemented with results on a larger dataset. It is also not sufficient to compare only against Bayesian nonparametric methods; include comparisons to established semiparametric methods, several of which you cite.

The primary contribution appears to be the agglomerative procedure, and Table 1 summarizes it well. Overall, the paper extends the simple single-table IRM with Bayesian Rose Trees, presenting clear theory and algorithms, albeit based on relatively simple ideas and procedures. The experimental section is illustrative rather than strongly comparative, and thus is not yet adequate.
