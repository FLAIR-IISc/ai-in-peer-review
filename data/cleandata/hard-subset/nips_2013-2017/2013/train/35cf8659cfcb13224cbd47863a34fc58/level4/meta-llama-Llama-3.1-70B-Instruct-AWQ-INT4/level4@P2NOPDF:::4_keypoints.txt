- Proposes a scalable agglomerative method for hierarchical community detection by adapting Bayesian rose trees with a network-specific likelihood and efficient sufficient-statistics caching.
- Presentation is clear overall, and experiments show improved results over a flat community baseline.
- Missing comparisons to other hierarchical network models, notably Ho et al. (2012) multiscale community blockmodel and Roy et al. (2009) Mondrian Process.
- Section 3 is dense and would benefit from simpler notation and clearer explanations, especially around equation (9).
- Significance is weakened by the limited empirical comparisons to existing hierarchical approaches.
- Original contribution is the adaptation of rose trees to networks and a greedy agglomerative algorithm; O(Nodes + Edges) likelihood computation is notable but under-explained.
- The dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistics updates are not thoroughly explained, making implementation details unclear.
- Terminology is questionable: framing the approach as “nonparametric” may be misleading compared to Blundell et al.’s model-selection framing of rose trees.
- Experiments (including Sampson’s data and IRM comparison) are well-executed and show benefits of averaging hypotheses, but scalability claims are undertested on small networks.
- Conclusion: successfully integrates prior rose-tree work with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods and generalizes agglomerative clustering, but lack of comparisons to other hierarchical models is a key omission.
