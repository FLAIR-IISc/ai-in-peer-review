- Contribution: Proposes a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) using ideas from Bayesian rose trees to model mixtures over consistent node partitions and learn the hierarchy via greedy agglomerative inference.

- Empirical results: Presents qualitative results on Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets and quantitative results on NIPS-234.

- Computational benefit: Inference is much cheaper than standard IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

- Clarity: Paper is well-written and exposition is clear.

- Originality: Adapts known Bayesian rose tree ideas to relational data, offering a valuable and efficient extension.

- Scalability concern: Basic algorithm scales O(n^2), which may be prohibitive for large networks; sparse version could remain feasible.

- Missing runtime evaluation: Lacks runtime results on larger, real-world networks (e.g., full NIPS dataset) to demonstrate practical scalability.

- Large-scale applicability: Unclear how inference would scale to very large networks such as gene or social networks with tens of thousands of nodes.

- Greedy-optimality question: Needs analysis of how close the greedy agglomerative solution is to the optimal tree, e.g., via exhaustive search on small instances.

- Theoretical support: Would be strengthened by theoretical analysis, e.g., connections to submodularity.

- Minor corrections: Fix redundant phrasing ("whilst... but"), ensure consistent pluralization (e.g., "pair of trees"), and correct grammar ("A fruitful next steps" → "A fruitful next step" or "Fruitful next steps").

- Recommendation: Despite familiar core ideas, contribution is significant and the reviewer recommends acceptance.
