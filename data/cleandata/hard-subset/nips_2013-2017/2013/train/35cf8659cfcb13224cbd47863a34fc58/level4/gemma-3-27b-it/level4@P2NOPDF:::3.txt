This paper is exceptionally well-written, demonstrating strong command of language, grammar, and citation style – significantly above average for submissions. A minor stylistic point: in accordance with NIPS guidelines, avoid beginning sentences with citations. The theoretical development is concise and elegant, notably through the consistent use of notation like f() and g().

The paper investigates stochastic block models (SBM) and enhances the standard inference of relational models (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. This allows for a simplification of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of IRM, as evidenced by the more compact equation (7). This factorization over subtrees builds upon established work in Bayesian network structure learning, such as the approach outlined by Koivisto and Sood (JMLR, 2004, Section 3). That work demonstrates how posterior factorization, driven by the chosen class of constrained structures, enables efficient techniques like greedy search, marginal likelihood calculation, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction.

Evaluation of SBM commonly relies on "gold standard" datasets, where the true partition structure is known, allowing for comparison against ground truth using metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering measures. The authors report using Area Under the Curve (AUC), which initially required clarification. Given my familiarity with NMI-based SBM evaluations, I inferred that the AUC calculation involves removing links and then assessing the algorithm’s ability to predict their reinstatement. While this seems correct, the paper would benefit from a clear explanation of this evaluation procedure. Furthermore, recent SBM evaluations increasingly employ larger datasets than those presented here (even exceeding the scale of the NIPS workshop dataset). While the evaluation in Figure 4 is a positive initial demonstration, extending it to a larger dataset would substantially strengthen the findings. Comparing performance solely against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient; a broader comparison including relevant semi-parametric approaches – some of which the authors acknowledge – is necessary.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. 

In essence, the paper successfully extends the foundational single-table instance of an IRM by integrating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. The theoretical and algorithmic presentation is clear and well-structured, although the core concepts and algorithms are relatively straightforward. The experimental results, however, are more illustrative than rigorously comparative, and therefore do not provide a fully convincing assessment of the method's performance.
