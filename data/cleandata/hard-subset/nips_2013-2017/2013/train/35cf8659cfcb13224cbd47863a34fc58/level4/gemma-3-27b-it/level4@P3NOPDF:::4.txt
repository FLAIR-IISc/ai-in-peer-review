This paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical community detection in networks. The core model leverages a prior over (potentially non-binary) trees, largely unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12], and adapts the likelihood function to accommodate network data. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that sufficient statistics can still be efficiently cached within this framework. The presentation is generally clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable performance compared to a baseline model employing a flat community structure. However, a significant weakness lies in the lack of comparison to existing hierarchical network models.

**Significance & Related Work:**

The paper is well-written and appears technically sound. Section 3, however, is particularly dense. The notation and explanation surrounding equation (9) and its subsequent derivations could benefit from simplification to improve accessibility. 

While the experiments effectively demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchical structure in relational datasets—showing an improvement over models lacking latent hierarchical structure—they do not establish whether this approach outperforms other existing hierarchical methods. Notably, the paper fails to engage with relevant Bayesian nonparametric models that explicitly model hierarchical community structure. For example, Ho et al. (2012) utilize a nested Chinese Restaurant Process ("multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration"), and Roy et al. (2009) introduce the Mondrian Process, a stochastic process generating hierarchical structures over K-d trees. These models potentially offer more refined latent structure discovery capabilities and should be considered in a comparative evaluation.

**Originality & Clarity:**

The model can be viewed as a Bayesian rose tree coupled with a standard stochastic block model likelihood. Therefore, the primary innovations reside in adapting the model to network data and developing efficient recursive updates for sufficient statistics during greedy agglomerative clustering. The reported O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood computation is a valuable contribution. However, the description of this efficiency is unclear. The mention of dynamic programming before equation (10) hints at the underlying mechanism, but lacks sufficient detail for full comprehension. Similarly, while equations are provided for the recursive updating of sufficient statistics, the justification for their correctness is not adequately explained. A more intuitive derivation or explanation would substantially improve clarity.

**Terminology:**

The use of "nonparametric" terminology is questionable. As highlighted by Blundell et al. [12], the rose tree approach is fundamentally a model selection technique, contrasting with traditional nonparametric methods. I concur with their interpretation and suggest reconsidering this characterization.

**Experiments:**

The qualitative experiment on the Sampson network is adequate, and the comparison to the IRM effectively illustrates the advantages of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. As previously noted, the primary limitation remains the absence of comparison to other hierarchical network models. While scalability is discussed, the networks used in the experiments are not exceptionally large, limiting the conclusions that can be drawn regarding performance on truly massive datasets.

**Overall:**

This paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this setting. This results in effective hierarchical community discovery, as demonstrated in the experiments. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models significantly weakens the paper's contribution. Addressing this gap, along with clarifying the explanations surrounding the efficient computation and sufficient statistics updates, would substantially strengthen the work.
