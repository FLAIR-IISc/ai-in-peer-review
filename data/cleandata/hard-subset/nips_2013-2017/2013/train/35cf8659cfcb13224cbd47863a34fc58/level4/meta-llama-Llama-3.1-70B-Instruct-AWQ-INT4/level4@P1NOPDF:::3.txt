Well-written paper with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations, exceeding the average standard. One minor suggestion is to avoid starting sentences with citations, as per the NIPS style. The theoretical presentation is succinct in some parts, effectively utilizing notations like f() and g() throughout the paper.

This paper explores stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the simple single-table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This integration allows for the replacement of the CRP formulation of IRM with the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees is reminiscent of work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood's JMLR 2004 paper, section 3. In that work, the posterior neatly factorizes based on the class of constrained structures chosen, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and more.

Evaluation of SBM typically involves comparison against "gold standard" data with known partition structures. Standard metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering metrics are often employed for this purpose. While the authors report using Area Under the Curve (AUC), it was not immediately clear how this was applied, as NMI evaluations are more commonly associated with SBM. Presumably, the authors deleted a subset of links and then attempted to recover them. However, a clearer explanation of this process is necessary. Furthermore, recent evaluations of SBM have utilized larger datasets than the one presented in Figure 4. Therefore, it would be beneficial to include results for a larger dataset as well. The comparison to Bayesian non-parametric methods is not sufficient, as there are numerous published semi-parametric methods, some of which are mentioned in the paper.

The primary contribution of this algorithm lies in its agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper extends the simple single-table instance of an IRM by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees, presenting good, clear theory and algorithms, albeit based on a simple concept. However, the experimental work is primarily illustrative rather than comparative, rendering it somewhat inadequate.
