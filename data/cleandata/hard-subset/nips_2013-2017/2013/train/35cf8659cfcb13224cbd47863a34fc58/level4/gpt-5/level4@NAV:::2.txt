The authors introduce a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to infer a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities from observed links. They propose an agglomerative-clustering-based learning procedure to select a tree that encodes valid partitions of the entities. They then present empirical results on two social connectivity corpora and conclude that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely used model for this task.

I believe this paper addresses an interesting problem that will appeal to a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. On the technical side, Bayesian nonparametric methods, while conceptually attractive, are often slow in practice. On the application side, social scientists are increasingly employing sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis, so a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model should be well received. This paper engages with both aspects.

The key idea is that by restricting allowable partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, the marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently. The IRM, by contrast, effectively considers an exponential number of possible partitions. However, I am somewhat uncertain about how the tree-learning procedure relates to the Bayesian generative story. Because it is greedy, I do not see it as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or as yielding a point MAP or ML estimate—but I may be mistaken. I recommend improving the clarity of Section 4.

I found the results impressive. Across several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performed comparably to or slightly better than the IRM, the most relevant competitor. While the improvements in these scores may not be dramatic in absolute terms, the reported runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

The overall clarity and writing could be improved. There are several distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126). Some prose is hard to follow; for instance, lines 47–50 in the introduction are vague (what are “local changes” in this context? what do the “iterations” refer to—iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The datasets used (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not clearly described, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with them. Some of the mathematics felt opaque or unnecessary, such as Eq. 5. I also found Figure 3 confusing; being unfamiliar with the monastery dataset, it is not visually clear which method performs best. Using a dataset more familiar to readers might help. Section 4, which presents the main contribution, required several readings to understand.

If these issues are addressed, I believe the approach is sufficiently novel and the results are strong enough to warrant publication.

The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be interpreted as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. I found the core learning algorithm insightful and the experimental results strong, but the writing quality needs improvement.
