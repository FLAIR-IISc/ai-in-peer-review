The authors propose a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling, building on core ideas from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) framework: 1) the hierarchy defines a mixture over consistent node partitions; 2) the hierarchy can be learned via a fast greedy agglomerative procedure. They present qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is substantially cheaper computationally than the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.

Quality/clarity. The paper is very well written, and the key ideas are communicated clearly.

Originality. While the main concepts derive from the BRT literature, adapting them to relational data required nontrivial work, especially to maintain the same computational complexity.

Significance. Scaling BNP models—particularly for networks—is an important challenge for practical use. Although the O(n^2) scaling of the base algorithm may be prohibitive for very large graphs, the sparse variant should be viable. It would be helpful to report timings on larger real-world networks. NIPS-234 takes only tens of seconds: how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Gene networks commonly have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are larger still; how will inference scale?

In addition, it would be useful to assess how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be explored at the largest scale where exhaustive search remains feasible. Can any theoretical support be provided, e.g., via submodularity?
