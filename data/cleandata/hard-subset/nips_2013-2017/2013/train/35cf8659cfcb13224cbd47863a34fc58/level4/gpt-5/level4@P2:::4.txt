Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a fast Bayesian model for hierarchical community discovery in networks. The model places a prior over hierarchical (possibly non-binary) trees and uses a blockmodel likelihood to explain within- and between-community edges. The resulting marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions can be computed exactly via a dynamic-programming recursion, and a greedy agglomerative procedure (with join/absorb operations) selects the tree by local Bayes factors with cached sufficient statistics. The approach closely follows Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell, Teh & Heller), but replaces i.i.d. likelihoods with a stochastic blockmodel tailored to relational data. Experiments show strong speed-ups and competitive predictive performance relative to the (flat) Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The model and inference scheme are well-motivated. Using conjugate Beta-Bernoulli blocks ensures tractable marginal likelihoods; the prior over partitions tied to a tree leads to an exact recursion for p(D|T). The greedy merge scores are local and efficiently computed with cached sufficient statistics.
- Exposition of key derivations: Section 3 is dense. The recursion (Eq. 10) is central, but the dynamic-programming idea is only alluded to; a more explicit derivation (e.g., showing how Eq. 6 factorizes into Eq. 10 by conditioning on stop/continue decisions) would help. Likewise, the sufficient-statistics updates in Eq. 13 would benefit from a brief justification (showing how σ¬ch accumulates inter-child counts under join vs. absorb). Providing pseudo-code for the DP likelihood evaluation and a small worked example would greatly improve accessibility.
- Claims and evaluation: The complexity claims (O(n + m) for likelihood, O(n^2 log n) for inference with caching) appear plausible and consistent with the algorithmic structure. Empirical speed-ups over IRM are convincing. However, the experimental section does not evaluate against other hierarchical network models, so it is hard to judge the benefits of this specific hierarchy prior/inference relative to alternatives.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Section 3’s notation around σSS and σ¬chSS (Eq. 9) could be simplified or illustrated with a diagram mapping tree nodes to adjacency blocks. A brief recap of assumptions (undirected, no self-edges, handling of missing edges) upfront would help. Hyperparameter choices are stated, but their role could be explained (e.g., α > β and δ < λ encode within- vs. between-community densities).

Originality:
The core contribution is an integration of Bayesian rose-tree–style model selection with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for relational data, plus an efficient caching scheme for greedy agglomeration. Conceptually, the prior and agglomerative search strategy are not new; the novelty lies in adapting these ideas to networks with a conjugate blockmodel and showing that the recursion and merges remain efficient. This is a solid engineering contribution rather than a fundamentally new probabilistic model.

Significance:
The method is appealing for practitioners needing fast, interpretable hierarchical community discovery with predictive ability. The ability to compute p(D|T) in O(n + m) and to perform greedy search in O(n^2 log n) is valuable, and the empirical speed-ups over IRM are substantial. That said, the paper’s significance would be strengthened by comparisons to existing hierarchical network models, such as:
- Hierarchical IRM variants (e.g., Herlau et al., 2012)
- Multiscale community blockmodel with nested CRP (Ho et al., 2011/2012)
- Mondrian-process-based relational models (Roy & Teh, 2009)
These baselines are designed for hierarchical structures and would provide a more direct test of the proposed approach.

Terminology:
The paper describes the method as Bayesian nonparametric. Since inference proceeds via greedy model selection over trees rather than posterior inference over an unbounded object (e.g., CRP/NRM-based processes), it may be more precise to present it as Bayesian model selection over an unbounded model class (as in Bayesian rose trees), rather than a fully nonparametric Bayesian approach. Clarifying this would avoid confusion.

Experiments:
- Sampson’s monastery: The qualitative structure is sensible and broadly aligns with known groupings; the discussion is informative.
- NIPS-234: The predictive comparisons against IRM (MCMC and greedy) show BHCD achieves competitive or better accuracy/AUC much faster. This convincingly demonstrates the practical speed advantages.
- Full NIPS: The large inferred hierarchy is interesting; the rule used to cut/flatten the tree is reasonable. However, none of the datasets are extremely large by modern standards; a larger sparse graph (e.g., >50K nodes) would better validate the scalability claims, especially for the sparse variant.

Suggestions:
- Provide a clearer derivation of the DP recursion and the sufficient-statistics updates, ideally with a small illustrative example.
- Compare against hierarchical network baselines (Herlau et al.; Ho et al.; Roy & Teh) and discuss differences in modeling assumptions and runtime.
- Explore sensitivity to hyperparameters and, if possible, perform simple hyperparameter inference.
- Clarify the “nonparametric” terminology and emphasize the model-selection perspective.
- Include directed/weighted variants or discuss how the framework would extend.

Pros:
- Simple, fast, and practical hierarchical community model with exact marginal likelihood via DP.
- Efficient greedy agglomeration with cached statistics; clear runtime advantages.
- Competitive predictive performance on real data; interpretable hierarchies.

Cons:
- Limited comparison to other hierarchical network models.
- Dense presentation of key recursions and updates; derivations need more guidance.
- Scalability shown on modest-sized networks; larger sparse benchmarks would strengthen claims.
- “Nonparametric” label may be misleading given the inference strategy.
