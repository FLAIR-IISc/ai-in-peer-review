This paper proposes a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling, borrowing key ideas from Bayesian rose trees: (1) using a hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent partitions of nodes, and (2) learning that hierarchy via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors present qualitative results on Sampson’s Monks and the full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality/clarity. The paper is well written, and the core ideas are explained clearly.

Originality. While the central concepts originate from the Bayesian rose tree literature, adapting them to relational data is nontrivial, particularly while preserving computational complexity. The paper makes a meaningful contribution in this adaptation.

Significance. Scaling BNP models, especially for networks, is crucial for practical use. Although the base algorithm’s n^2 scaling may be prohibitive for very large graphs, the sparse variant should remain feasible. It would strengthen the paper to include timing results on larger real-world networks. NIPS-234 runs in tens of seconds; how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Gene networks often have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are much larger—how does inference scale in these regimes in both time and memory?

It would also help to gauge how close the greedy agglomerative search gets to the optimal tree. This could be studied on datasets small enough for exhaustive search. Is any theoretical support available, for example via submodularity or approximation guarantees?

Minor corrections:
- Line 48, page 1: “whilst … but” — avoid redundant conjunctions (use “although …” or drop “but”).
- “pair of treeS” → “pair of trees”.
- “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps include …”.

Overall assessment. Although the main ideas are familiar from BRT, the adaptation to relational modeling and the demonstrated efficiency gains represent a significant and useful contribution. I recommend acceptance.
