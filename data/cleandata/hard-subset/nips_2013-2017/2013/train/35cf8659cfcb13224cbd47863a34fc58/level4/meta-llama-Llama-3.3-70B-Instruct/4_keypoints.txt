- Proposes a scalable agglomerative method for hierarchical community detection in networks by adapting Bayesian rose trees.  
- Retains prior over trees and algorithmic structure from prior work, modifying only the likelihood for network data.  
- Introduces efficient caching and recursive updates of sufficient statistics to speed computations.  
- Likelihood computation runs in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a notable efficiency claim.  
- Presentation is generally clear, but Section 3 is dense and the notation/explanation around equation (9) needs simplification.  
- Experimental results show favorable performance versus a flat community baseline (IRM), and qualitative analysis on Sampson’s data is informative.  
- Demonstrates benefits of averaging over multiple hierarchical hypotheses for prediction.  
- Lacks comparisons to other hierarchical network models (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel, Mondrian Process), which is a major limitation.  
- Tested networks are not particularly large, so scalability claims are not fully validated empirically.  
- Originality is limited: the work is mainly an adaptation of Bayesian rose trees with a standard block-model likelihood.  
- The description of the recursive update mechanism for sufficient statistics could be clearer for better understanding.  
- Use of the term "nonparametric" is questionable and may contradict previous interpretations of rose trees as model selection rather than nonparametric methods.
