The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, drawing on key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper. Specifically, they utilize the hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and propose an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure for learning this hierarchy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Notably, the proposed inference method is computationally cheaper than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.

In terms of quality and clarity, the paper is well-written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The authors have done an excellent job of presenting their work in a concise and understandable manner.

The originality of the paper lies in the adaptation of the Bayesian rose tree ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity. While the main ideas are not entirely new, the authors have made a significant effort to tailor these concepts to the specific challenges of network modeling.

The significance of this work cannot be overstated, as scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models to larger networks is a crucial challenge for practitioners. The proposed approach offers a promising solution to this problem, with the sparse version of the algorithm potentially remaining feasible even for larger networks. However, it would be beneficial to see some timings on larger real-world networks to fully appreciate the scalability of the method. For instance, the authors mention that the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process, but it would be informative to know how long the full NIPS dataset takes. Furthermore, exploring the scalability of the method on even larger networks, such as gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes or real social networks with millions of nodes, would provide valuable insights.

Additionally, it would be interesting to investigate how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be explored by comparing the results of the greedy method to the optimal tree found through exhaustive search on a smaller scale. Providing any theoretical support for the method, such as submodularity, would further strengthen the paper.

In conclusion, while the ideas presented in this paper are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is still significant, and the paper should be accepted. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of network modeling, and their work has the potential to make a substantial impact on the scalability of BNP models.
