This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for identifying hierarchical community structures in networks. The proposed model conceptualizes the hierarchy as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key contribution is an efficient greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that learns this hierarchical structure in a single bottom-up pass. The authors demonstrate that this approach is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than inference in the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving comparable or superior predictive accuracy on benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods to large network datasets. The proposed method, which is substantially faster than MCMC-based inference for related models like the IRM, represents a significant step towards making sophisticated hierarchical modeling practical for real-world network analysis.
*   **Quality:** The technical work is solid. The adaptation of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational data is well-executed, and the development of the efficient agglomerative algorithm with its caching scheme is clever. The empirical evaluation is sound, providing both qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network and quantitative comparisons on the NIPS co-authorship dataset that convincingly support the authors' claims of speed and accuracy.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model, the likelihood calculations, and the inference algorithm are explained with impressive clarity, making a potentially complex topic accessible.
*   **Originality:** While the approach builds upon existing ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering (specifically, Bayesian rose trees), its application and novel adaptation to the problem of community discovery in relational data are significant. The work required to translate the framework to the network setting and maintain computational efficiency represents a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability Analysis:** The O(n^2 log n) complexity of the dense algorithm could be prohibitive for very large networks. While the experiments on the NIPS-234 dataset are compelling, the paper's impact could be strengthened by a more thorough empirical analysis of scalability. It would be beneficial to report the runtime for the full NIPS dataset analysis. A discussion on the practical performance of the `BHCD sparse` variant on even larger, sparser networks (e.g., those with tens of thousands of nodes, common in biological or social network analysis) would provide a clearer picture of the method's applicability.
*   **Optimality of Greedy Search:** The greedy nature of the inference algorithm is central to its efficiency, but this comes with the standard caveat of potentially finding locally optimal solutions. The paper would be more complete with some discussion on the quality of the hierarchies found by this greedy procedure relative to a potential global optimum. While exhaustive search is intractable, an experiment on a small, synthetic network where the optimal tree is known or can be computed could provide valuable insight. Furthermore, is it possible to provide any theoretical justification for the greedy merge criterion, perhaps by relating the objective function to concepts like submodularity?

**Minor Corrections:**

*   Page 1, line 48: The construction "whilst... but" is redundant. Suggest rephrasing.
*   Page 4, Algorithm 1 section: "pair of tree" should be "pair of trees".
*   Page 6, Section 6: "A fruitful next steps" should be "Fruitful next steps" or "A fruitful next step".

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed method is an elegant and highly efficient approach for discovering hierarchical community structure. Despite the minor points raised for improvement, the novelty of the adaptation, the clarity of the presentation, and the convincing empirical results make a strong case for acceptance.
