Well written paper with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations, well above average in these respects. Though, it's best to avoid beginning sentences with citations, following the NIPS style. The theory is succinct in parts, for instance, through the consistent use of f() and g() throughout the paper. 

The paper explores stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the simple, flat-community instance of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of the IRM to be replaced with the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees follows the work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior factorizes neatly based on the class of constrained structures chosen, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, and handling of sufficient statistics for tasks like link prediction.

Evaluation of SBM is frequently performed against “gold standard” data where the partition structure is known. Scores like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering metrics can be used for this, comparing against the known clusterings. The paper reports using Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUC), and requires further explanation as I initially found the application to this context unclear. Given familiarity with NMI evaluations for SBM, I presume the method involves deleting a few links and then predicting whether they should be added back. Clarification on this process is necessary. Recent evaluations of SBM have been conducted on larger datasets than the one used here; thus, while the small-scale evaluation in Figure 4 is useful, it should be presented alongside results for a larger dataset. It's insufficient to compare solely against Bayesian nonparametric methods, as many published semi-parametric methods exist, some of which are already mentioned in the paper.

The primary contribution of this algorithm lies in its agglomerative approach, which is well summarized in Table 1.

The paper extends the simple, flat-community instance of the IRM by adding the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. The theory and algorithms are presented clearly, although the core concept and algorithms themselves are relatively straightforward. The experimental work is primarily illustrative rather than comprehensively comparative, rendering it somewhat inadequate.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Sound Technical Contribution:** The paper presents a technically sound and efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for hierarchical community discovery.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The methodology is clearly explained, building upon existing concepts in Bayesian networks and stochastic block models.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm offers significant computational advantages over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, as highlighted in Table 1 and the experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and scale. A comparison to semi-parametric methods and evaluation on a larger dataset would strengthen the claims.
*   **Lack of Clarification:** The description of the AUC-based evaluation requires further clarification.
*   **Incremental Advance:** While technically sound, the core idea is relatively straightforward and represents an incremental advance rather than a breakthrough.
