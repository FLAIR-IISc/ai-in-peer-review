

Review: Proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical community detection in networks. The prior over (potentially non-binary) trees and algorithm structure remain largely unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted in a direct manner for networks, with minimal additional effort enabling efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable performance against a flat-community baseline, though comparisons to other hierarchical network models are absent.  

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:  
The paper is well-written and technically sound. Section 3’s exposition is notably dense; a simpler notation or explanation for Equation (9) and related components would be beneficial. Limited comparisons to hierarchical models (e.g., Ho et al. 2012’s multiscale community blockmodel using a nested CRP, and Roy et al. 2009’s Mondrian Process for hierarchical K-d tree structures) overlook existing Bayesian nonparametric methods capable of refining latent structure. The experiments primarily establish hierarchy’s utility for relational data but fail to position the method against state-of-the-art hierarchical alternatives.  

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:  
The core model is a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood, with innovations confined to likelihood-driven recursive updates of sufficient statistics. While the O(Nodes + Edges) computational efficiency is advantageous, the description of this mechanism is inadequate—dynamic programming is mentioned before Equation (10), but details remain opaque. The recursive update equations lack sufficient justification for their correctness.  

TERMINOLOGY:  
The use of "nonparametric" is questionable; Blundell et al. [12] explicitly contrast rose trees with nonparametric methods in their discussion, a perspective this work should acknowledge.  

EXPERIMENTS:  
The Sampson qualitative results and IRM comparison effectively illustrate hierarchy’s benefits for predictive averaging. However, scalability claims lack substantial validation (tested networks are not exceptionally large), and the absence of hierarchical model comparisons remains the most significant limitation.  

The work effectively integrates Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for hierarchical network clustering, enabling efficient community discovery. However, the omission of comparisons to established hierarchical network models undermines the demonstration of relative superiority.
