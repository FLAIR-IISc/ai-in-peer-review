Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes an agglomerative, Bayesian approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model is effectively a Bayesian rose tree prior over (potentially non-binary) hierarchies combined with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The inference procedure mirrors greedy agglomerative clustering for rose trees, adapted so that the likelihood and all necessary sufficient statistics for blocks can be cached and updated efficiently. The authors show that, in the worst case, the algorithm scales quadratically in the number of vertices and is independent of the number of nested communities; the marginal likelihood for a fixed tree can be computed in O(n + m). Experiments compare to the IRM (flat communities) and demonstrate substantially faster runtimes with competitive predictive performance.

This work is closely related to Bayesian rose trees (Blundell et al.), on which the prior and the greedy algorithmic structure are largely based, and to Bayesian blockmodeling for flat communities (e.g., IRM and MMSB). It also connects to hierarchical network models such as the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., 2012) and the Mondrian process (Roy et al., 2009), though direct comparisons to these are not provided.

Quality:
- The model and algorithm appear technically sound. The use of conjugate Beta–Bernoulli likelihoods with distinct within- and between-community hyperparameters is sensible for community detection and enables closed-form marginal likelihoods.
- The dynamic programming recursion for the tree marginal likelihood (eq. 10) is a key strength, leveraging the factorization induced by the tree-consistent partitions. However, the exposition around eqs. (8)–(10) is terse. There is only a brief allusion to dynamic programming before eq. (10); a small worked example or pseudocode tracing how σSS and σ¬chSS are accumulated along the tree would markedly improve clarity.
- Similarly, the recursive updates for the sufficient statistics in the agglomerative step (eq. 13) are correct but under-explained. A derivation or intuitive justification for each case (join vs. absorption) would help readers verify correctness.
- The experimental methodology is reasonable. The Sampson case study is informative, and the NIPS-234 evaluation demonstrates that the method reaches useful predictive performance far faster than the IRM. However, the datasets are not especially large, and there is no comparison to other hierarchical network models, so it remains unclear how this method fares against prior hierarchical approaches.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and clearly organized. Section 3 is dense, especially the notation around eq. (9). The definition σ¬chSS = σSS − ΣC∈ch(S) σCC could be explained more intuitively (e.g., “edges among leaves(S) that cross child boundaries”) and illustrated with a small tree and adjacency block diagram.
- The description of the O(n + m) dynamic programming for p(D|T) would benefit from a short derivation and a complexity argument stated explicitly (what is cached at each node, and how a post-order traversal accumulates terms).
- The agglomerative search is clearly presented, and the use of a heap keyed by Bayes factors is straightforward.

Originality:
- Conceptually, the contribution is a rose-tree prior combined with a standard blockmodel likelihood; the primary novelty lies in tailoring the greedy agglomerative framework to relational data, including the design of local merge scores and efficient caching of sufficient statistics.
- While the model is not entirely new in spirit—extending rose trees to a different likelihood family—the work is a useful and practical specialization with careful engineering for scalability.
- The paper challenges the use of “nonparametric” terminology. As in Blundell et al., the approach is best characterized as Bayesian model selection over trees (with a prior favoring certain branching structures), rather than a fully Bayesian nonparametric process over partitions. I share that interpretation.

Significance:
- The main strength is computational: a simple, effective method that scales as O(n^2 log n) for inference, with a tree marginal likelihood computable in O(n + m). The experimental results indicate two orders of magnitude speedups over the IRM while achieving comparable or better predictive performance on NIPS-234.
- The method’s ability to produce interpretable, non-binary hierarchies is also valuable in exploratory analysis.
- The significance would be stronger with head-to-head comparisons against hierarchical baselines (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel, Mondrian-based models), even on smaller subsets, to clarify accuracy/interpretability trade-offs relative to other hierarchical approaches.

Experiments:
- Sampson’s monastery: The qualitative results are sensible and align with known groups, with reasonable deviations explained by the observed affinities. The comparison between binary and non-binary hierarchies is helpful for interpretability.
- NIPS-234: The predictive curves convincingly show faster convergence to competitive performance than the IRM. It is also clear why the sparse variants are advantageous on this dataset.
- Full NIPS: The illustration of the top-level hierarchy is interesting, though the lack of quantitative metrics on the full graph limits conclusions about overall accuracy at scale.

Pros:
- Clear, practical method with strong computational efficiency.
- Exact tree marginal likelihood via a clean dynamic program.
- Competitive predictive performance vs. IRM with much lower runtime.
- Produces interpretable, non-binary hierarchies.

Cons:
- Limited comparisons to existing hierarchical network models (e.g., Ho et al., Roy et al.).
- Dense exposition around key recursions; derivations/examples are needed for clarity.
- “Nonparametric” terminology is debatable; better to frame as Bayesian model selection over trees.
- Empirical evaluation does not include truly large-scale networks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a worked example or pseudocode for computing p(D|T) with cached σSS and σ¬chSS, and brief derivations for the update rules in eq. (13).
- Include empirical comparisons to at least one hierarchical baseline (multiscale community blockmodel or Mondrian-based approach), even on smaller graphs.
- Clarify the positioning relative to Bayesian nonparametrics; adopting the rose-tree “model selection” framing would avoid confusion.

Overall:
This paper capably integrates rose-tree priors with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods and extends efficient agglomerative clustering to relational data. It delivers a fast, interpretable approach to hierarchical community discovery with promising predictive results. Strengthening the exposition of the dynamic program and including comparisons to other hierarchical network models would significantly increase its impact.
