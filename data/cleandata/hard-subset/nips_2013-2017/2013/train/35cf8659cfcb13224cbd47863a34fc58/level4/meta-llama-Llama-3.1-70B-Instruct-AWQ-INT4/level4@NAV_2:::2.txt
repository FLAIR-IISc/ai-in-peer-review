The authors propose a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to the hierarchical setting, in order to infer a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering of entities when conditioned on observed links between entities. They propose a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree which represents valid possible partitions of the entities. They then present empirical results from two corpuses of social connectivity data and conclude that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used model for tasks of this kind.

The paper addresses an interesting issue that a significant fraction of the NIPS audience will be interested in, as it combines the strengths of Bayesian nonparametric methods with the practical needs of social scientists analyzing complex network data. The key insight is that by restricting allowed partitions to conform to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. This is a significant improvement over the IRM, which must essentially consider the exponential number of possible partitions of the data.

However, the relationship between the learning procedure of the trees and the generative story behind the Bayesian model is not entirely clear. Since the learning procedure is greedy, it is not obvious whether it can be seen as performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees or finding some kind of point MAP or ML estimate. The authors should consider clarifying this point in Section 4.

The results presented in the paper are impressive, with the proposed method performing comparably or slightly better than the IRM in terms of several performance measures (AUC, predictive llh, accuracy). While the asymptotic improvements in these scores may not be dramatic in absolute terms, the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. First, there are a series of distracting spelling and grammar errors that should be corrected. Second, the prose is sometimes hard to understand, with vague phrasing and unclear references to technical concepts. For example, the introduction could be clarified to better explain the context and relevance of the research. Additionally, the authors should consider providing more detail about the datasets used in the experiments, as well as more intuitive visualizations to help illustrate the results.

The math in the paper is sometimes obtuse and unnecessary, such as Eq. 5, which could be simplified or removed. Furthermore, Section 4, which presents the core novel contribution of the paper, requires several readings to understand and could benefit from additional explanation and context.

Despite these issues, the proposed approach has enough novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication. With some revisions to address the clarity and writing quality, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric methods for network analysis.

Strengths:

* Novel model of graph edge structure that can be seen as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM
* Insightful core learning algorithm
* Strong experimental results

Weaknesses:

* Clarity and writing quality need improvement
* Relationship between learning procedure and generative story behind Bayesian model is not entirely clear
* Some math is obtuse and unnecessary
* Section 4 requires additional explanation and context
