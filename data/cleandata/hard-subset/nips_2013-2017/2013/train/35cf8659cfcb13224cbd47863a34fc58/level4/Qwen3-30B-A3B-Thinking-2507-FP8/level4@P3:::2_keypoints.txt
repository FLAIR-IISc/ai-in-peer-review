- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery model using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels.  
- Extends Bayesian nonparametrics to hierarchical community detection, unlike flat models (e.g., IRM) or limited prior hierarchical methods.  
- Key technical contribution: greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm computing marginal likelihood in O(n² log n).  
- Time complexity is a significant improvement over IRM’s O(n²K²), enabling scalability to larger networks.  
- Principled Bayesian approach that avoids combinatorial explosion by restricting to tree-consistent partitions.  
- Shows marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently via dynamic programming.  
- Empirical results: BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM and is ~100× faster in experiments.  
- Sparse BHCD variant performs particularly well on sparse networks.  
- Well-situated in literature: builds on IRM and hierarchical clustering work and cites relevant prior methods.  
- The generative model and marginal likelihood derivation are theoretically sound and well-motivated.  
- Dynamic programming inference is technically solid and practically significant.  
- Experiments (Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship) demonstrate qualitative and quantitative advantages and a strong speed–accuracy tradeoff.  
- Algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly specified and complexity analysis is thorough.  
- Section 4 is unclear about the connection between the generative model and the greedy learning procedure; needs explicit statement of objective (posterior vs MAP).  
- Writing quality issues: grammatical errors and insufficient dataset context for readers unfamiliar with the data.  
- Figure 3 presentation is confusing—labels and explanation of which method is “best” are missing.  
- Mathematical exposition could be more accessible: Equation (5), tree-consistent partition formalism, and sufficient statistics need examples/simplification.  
- Comparison claim vs IRM is unclear (one Gibbs step vs full MCMC); empirical comparisons should be precisely stated.  
- Suggested fixes: explicitly state whether algorithm approximates posterior or finds MAP; add dataset descriptions (e.g., Sampson, NIPS co-authorship stats).  
- Suggested fixes: revise Figure 3 for clear method labeling and interpretability; correct grammar and simplify technical exposition.  
- Overall assessment: strong, original, and significant contribution with practical impact; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
