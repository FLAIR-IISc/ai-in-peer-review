The paper introduces a Bayesian nonparametric approach to uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model can be viewed as a tree-structured mixture over (potentially exponentially many) stochastic blockmodels, borrowing two core ideas from Bayesian Rose Trees: (1) the hierarchy induces a mixture over partitions consistent with the tree, and (2) the hierarchy is learned via a fast, greedy agglomerative model-selection procedure. The authors report qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery data and the full NIPS co-authorship graph, and quantitative link-prediction results on the NIPS-234 subset. The proposed inference is dramatically cheaper than the IRM while achieving comparable predictive accuracy.

Quality/clarity. The manuscript is clearly written, and the exposition of the main concepts and algorithmic choices is crisp and easy to follow.

Originality. While the central framework builds directly on Bayesian Rose Trees, the extension to relational data and the maintenance of favorable computational complexity required nontrivial adaptations.

Significance. Scaling BNP models—especially for networks—is crucial for practical impact. Although the baseline O(n^2) complexity may be limiting for very large graphs, the sparse variant should remain practical in many real-world settings. It would strengthen the paper to include timings on larger real datasets. NIPS-234 runs in tens of seconds; how long does full NIPS take end-to-end? Gene interaction networks often have O(10^4) nodes and many social networks are orders of magnitude larger—how would the method behave there?

It would also be valuable to assess how close the greedy search gets to the optimal tree. This could be studied on the largest instances where exhaustive search is still feasible. Is there any theoretical support for the heuristic (e.g., approximation guarantees or connections to submodularity) that could be provided?

Despite the overlap with BRT ideas, the contribution is meaningful, and I recommend acceptance.
