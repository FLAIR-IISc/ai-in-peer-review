- Proposes an agglomerative method for hierarchical network clustering built on Bayesian rose trees with a network-adapted likelihood and cached sufficient statistics.  
- Prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and algorithmic framework largely unchanged from Bayesian rose trees [12].  
- Likelihood adaptation to networks is straightforward and can be computed in O(nodes + edges) time, enabling scalability.  
- Main algorithmic contribution is how the new likelihood modifies recursive updates of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative procedure.  
- Presentation is generally clear but Sec. 3 is dense; Eq. (9) and subsequent derivations need simpler notation and explanation.  
- Description of computational methods (dynamic programming before Eq. (10) and recursive updates) is hard to follow and lacks intuitive justification.  
- Experiments show benefits over a flat community baseline (e.g., Sampson dataset, comparison to IRM), but evaluated networks are not particularly large.  
- Major empirical shortcoming: no comparisons to other hierarchical network models, so superiority over existing hierarchical methods is unestablished.  
- Relevant missing related work includes Ho et al. (2012, nested CRP multiscale blockmodel) and Roy et al. (2009, Mondrian process), which could discover richer hierarchies.  
- Terminology concern: calling the method “nonparametric” is questionable given Blundell et al. [12] frame rose trees as model selection rather than nonparametric.  
- Overall: effective integration of prior rose-tree work with stochastic block-model likelihoods yielding practical hierarchical discovery, but limited novelty and insufficient empirical comparisons to hierarchical baselines.
