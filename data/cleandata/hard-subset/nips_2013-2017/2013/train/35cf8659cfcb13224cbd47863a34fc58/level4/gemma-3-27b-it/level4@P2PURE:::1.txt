The authors introduce a hierarchical extension to the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modeling, leveraging concepts from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) literature. Specifically, they employ a hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions and utilize an efficient, greedy agglomerative procedure to learn this hierarchy. The paper presents qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, alongside quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference method is significantly more computationally efficient than standard IRM, while achieving comparable predictive performance.

Regarding quality and clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

In terms of originality, while the foundational ideas originate from the BRT work, substantial effort was required to adapt them to the relational data setting, notably in preserving computational complexity. 

The significance of this work lies in addressing the critical challenge of scaling Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) models – and particularly network models – to practical sizes. Although the n^2 scaling of the core algorithm may limit its applicability to very large networks, the sparse version offers a promising path toward feasibility. It would be beneficial to see timing results on larger, real-world networks; for instance, while NIPS-234 requires only tens of seconds, the runtime for the full NIPS dataset is not reported. Given that gene networks often contain tens of thousands of nodes and real-world social networks are substantially larger, understanding the scalability of inference is crucial. 

Furthermore, assessing the quality of the tree structure obtained via the greedy method would be valuable. This could be explored by comparing its performance to exhaustive search at the largest scale where finding the optimal tree remains tractable. Exploring potential theoretical guarantees for the method, such as leveraging concepts like submodularity, could also strengthen the work.
