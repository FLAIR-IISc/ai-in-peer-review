- Proposes a scalable agglomerative method for hierarchical community detection by adapting Bayesian rose trees to network data.  
- Main technical contribution: modify the likelihood for networks and efficiently cache sufficient statistics, enabling O(Nodes + Edges) likelihood computation.  
- Algorithmic innovation: adapts the greedy agglomerative Bayesian rose tree algorithm with recursive sufficient-statistic updates.  
- Presentation is generally clear, but Section 3 is dense and notation around equation (9) needs simplification.  
- The dynamic-programming-based efficient likelihood computation (before eq. 10) is under-explained.  
- Recursive update equations for sufficient statistics are presented without enough justification or intuition.  
- Experiments demonstrate advantages over a flat baseline (Infinite Relational Model) and give reasonable qualitative analysis on the Sampson dataset.  
- Major weakness: no comparisons to other hierarchical network models (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al., 2012], Mondrian Process [Roy et al., 2009]).  
- Scalability claims are not fully substantiated because experimental networks are relatively small.  
- Terminology concern: labeling the approach “nonparametric” is misleading given Bayesian rose trees are framed as model selection rather than nonparametric.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound integration of rose trees with a block-model likelihood, but impact is weakened by missing baselines and limited experimental validation.
