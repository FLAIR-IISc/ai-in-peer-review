

The paper presents a well-written, grammatically sound contribution with precise citations and strong technical exposition. The writing quality exceeds typical standards for the venue. A minor stylistic note: avoid beginning sentences with citations (e.g., "As shown in [X],..."), as this is unconventional in NIPS style.  

The core contribution extends the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian rose tree theory, replacing its CRP-based formulation with a simpler, factorized approach (Equation 7). This factorization leverages structural constraints from Bayesian network learning (Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004), enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, and sufficient statistics handling—key advantages for scalability. The exposition of this theoretical extension is clear and well-motivated, with the agglomerative algorithm and Table 1 effectively summarizing the methodological innovation.  

However, the experimental evaluation requires significant strengthening. The authors use AUC for SBM evaluation, which is unconventional compared to standard metrics like NMI (Normalized Mutual Information) that compare inferred partitions against known "gold standard" clusterings. The AUC methodology is not adequately explained: it appears the approach involves deleting edges and predicting their reconnection, but this must be explicitly clarified to avoid confusion. For instance, specify whether edges are removed uniformly, based on community structure, or via a specific holdout strategy.  

More critically, the evaluation relies solely on the relatively small NIPS dataset (Figure 4), which is insufficient for robust SBM assessment. Recent SBM literature (e.g., recent work by Peixoto, 2021; Zhang et al., 2020) evaluates on significantly larger networks (thousands to millions of nodes), demonstrating scalability and generalization. The current results, while illustrative, lack context against these larger-scale benchmarks. Furthermore, the comparative analysis is inadequate: merely contrasting against Bayesian non-parametric methods (e.g., DP-MCMC) overlooks a broad range of state-of-the-art semi-parametric and parametric SBM approaches (e.g., variational inference, modularity maximization, or recent deep learning methods). The paper cites some of these but does not engage with their performance, weakening the claim of empirical novelty.  

The algorithmic contribution—while conceptually straightforward—is presented with sufficient rigor. The agglomerative method (Table 1) is well-defined, and the theoretical grounding via rose trees is a valid extension. However, the experimental section falls short of establishing the method’s practical value. The evaluation is "illustrative rather than comparative," as noted in the draft, and fails to demonstrate competitive performance against relevant baselines or address scalability on larger datasets. Without this, the empirical justification for the theoretical extension remains tentative.  

To elevate this work, the authors should:  
1. **Clarify the AUC methodology** in the evaluation section, specifying edge deletion and prediction protocols.  
2. **Expand experiments** to include larger benchmark datasets (e.g., from the SBM literature) and compare against ≥3 additional state-of-the-art methods beyond Bayesian non-parametric approaches.  
3. **Reframe the contribution** to emphasize how the rose tree factorization *enables* practical improvements (e.g., reduced runtime, better scalability) rather than merely rephrasing IRM.  

The paper’s theoretical insight is sound and well-executed, but the empirical validation is currently underdeveloped. With the proposed revisions, it would make a stronger contribution to the SBM literature. As submitted, the experimental limitations prevent a definitive assessment of the method’s real-world utility, though the theoretical foundation is promising.  

Overall, the paper is technically competent and well-written but requires substantial expansion of the empirical evaluation to meet NIPS standards. The core idea is valuable, but the evidence supporting its superiority over existing methods is insufficient.
