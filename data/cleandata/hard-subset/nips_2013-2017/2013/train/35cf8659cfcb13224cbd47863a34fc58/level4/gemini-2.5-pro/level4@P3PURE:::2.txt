The authors propose a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, designed to infer a hierarchy of entities from observed link data, rather than a flat clustering. Their approach uses an agglomerative clustering algorithm to select a tree representing the space of valid partitions. The authors present empirical results on two social connectivity corpora, concluding that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely-used baseline for this task.

This paper addresses an important problem that will be of interest to a significant fraction of the NIPS audience. From a technical perspective, it tackles the practical issue of scalability in Bayesian nonparametric methods, which are often conceptually attractive but computationally slow. From a problem domain perspective, the work is timely, as social scientists are increasingly using sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis. A hierarchical extension to a popular network discovery model, as proposed here, is therefore a welcome contribution that touches upon both these areas.

The paper's key technical insight is that by restricting partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently, avoiding the need to consider the exponential number of partitions faced by the standard IRM. However, the connection between the proposed tree learning procedure and the generative model is unclear. Specifically, since the agglomerative algorithm is greedy, it does not appear to be performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees, nor is it explicitly finding a MAP or ML point estimate. I may have misunderstood this point, but I strongly recommend that the authors improve the clarity of this connection in Section 4.

The empirical results are impressive. Across several performance measures (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy), the proposed method performs comparably to or slightly better than the IRM. While the improvements in these metrics are modest in absolute terms, the key advantage is computational: the claimed runtime is orders of magnitude faster than that of IRM inference.

The primary weakness of the paper is its overall clarity and quality of presentation. I recommend a thorough revision to address the following points:
*   **Prose and Grammar:** The paper contains several distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., on line 126). Furthermore, the prose is occasionally difficult to parse. For instance, the phrasing in lines 47-50 of the introduction is vague: it is unclear what 'local changes' and 'iterations' (presumably of an MCMC algorithm?) refer to in this context.
*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The core technical contribution in Section 4 required several readings to understand. The mathematical presentation is also sometimes obtuse; Equation 5, for example, could be simplified or better explained.
*   **Datasets and Figures:** The datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) are not described in sufficient detail, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with this prior work. Similarly, Figure 3 is difficult to interpret without prior knowledge of the monastery dataset, making it hard to visually assess which method performs best. The authors might consider using a dataset that is more widely recognizable to the community.

In summary, the authors propose a novel model for graph structure that can be viewed as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering, inspired by the IRM. The core learning algorithm is insightful and the experimental results are strong. However, the paper is hindered by significant issues in its writing and presentation. If these clarity issues are thoroughly addressed, I believe the proposed approach has sufficient novelty and impact to warrant publication.
