The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, building on key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree literature: 1) using a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and 2) learning this hierarchy through an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results are shown on Sampson’s monastery network and the full NIPS co-authorship dataset, with quantitative results on a subset of the NIPS data (NIPS-234). The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is very well written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The notation is generally well-handled, and the authors provide sufficient detail to understand the approach.

**Originality:** The core ideas are adapted from the Bayesian rose tree work. However, there was substantial work involved in adapting these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining computational efficiency. The combination of a Bayesian nonparametric approach with a scalable greedy inference procedure is a noteworthy contribution.

**Significance:** Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models, especially to network data, is an important challenge if these methods are to be useful to practitioners. While the O(n<sup>2</sup>) scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for extremely large networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. It would have been helpful to see timings on larger real-world networks. The NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process—how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Gene regulatory networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real-world social networks are much larger still: how would inference scale to these sizes? 

Furthermore, it would be valuable to understand how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree. This could be investigated by comparing the results to exhaustive search on the largest scale still computationally tractable. Is it possible to provide any theoretical guarantees for the method, for example, in terms of submodularity or approximation bounds?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to hierarchical community detection.
*   The proposed method is computationally efficient compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on benchmark datasets.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The scalability to truly massive networks (e.g., millions of nodes) is not fully demonstrated.
*   Theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm’s performance is lacking.
*   A more thorough comparison to other scalable community detection algorithms (beyond IRM) would be beneficial.

**Overall:** While the ideas presented here are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is still significant. This paper should be accepted.
