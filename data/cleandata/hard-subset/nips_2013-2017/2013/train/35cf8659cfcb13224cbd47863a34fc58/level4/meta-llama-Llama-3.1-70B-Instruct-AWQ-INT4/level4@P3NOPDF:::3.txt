This paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations, making it well above average in terms of presentation. However, it would be beneficial to avoid starting sentences with citations, as per the NIPS style.

The paper explores stochastic block models (SBM) and extends the simple single table instance of an Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This integration enables the replacement of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of IRM with the simpler equation (7). The factoring over subtrees is reminiscent of the work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood's 2004 JMLR paper, section 3. In this context, the posterior factorizes neatly based on the class of constrained structures chosen, allowing for efficient greedy search, marginal likelihoods, handling of sufficient statistics, link prediction, and other tasks.

The evaluation of SBM typically involves comparing against "gold standard" data with known partition structures. Common metrics used for this purpose include Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and other clustering metrics. The authors report using the Area Under the Curve (AUC) metric, which may be unfamiliar to some readers, particularly those accustomed to NMI evaluations for SBM. It appears that the authors delete a few links and then attempt to guess whether they should be added back, but this approach requires further explanation. Additionally, recent evaluations of SBM have been conducted on larger datasets, exceeding the size of the full NIPS dataset used in this paper. Therefore, it would be beneficial to present the evaluation on a larger dataset as well. The comparison against Bayesian non-parametric methods is a good starting point, but it is not sufficient, as there are many published semi-parametric methods that could be included in the comparison, some of which are mentioned in the paper.

The primary contribution of this algorithm is the agglomerative method, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper provides a clear and well-structured theory, as well as a simple yet effective algorithm. However, the experimental work is more illustrative than comparative, and therefore, it is not entirely adequate. The extension of the simple single table instance of an IRM by adding the theory of Bayesian Rose trees is a valuable contribution, but the paper could benefit from more comprehensive evaluations and comparisons to establish its significance.
