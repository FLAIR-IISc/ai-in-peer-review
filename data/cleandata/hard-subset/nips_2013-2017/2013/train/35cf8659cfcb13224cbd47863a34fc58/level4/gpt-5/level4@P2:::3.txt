Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model treats a hierarchy as a prior over tree-consistent partitions and defines a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Using conjugate Beta–Bernoulli likelihoods with different hyperparameters for within- and between-community edges, the authors derive a dynamic-programming recursion for the marginal likelihood over a given tree. A greedy agglomerative model-selection procedure then builds the hierarchy bottom-up, considering three merge operations (join and two absorptions), with cached sufficient statistics yielding O(1) inner updates and overall O(n^2 log n) complexity; a sparse variant prohibits merges between disconnected subtrees. Link prediction is performed by a recursive mixture down the tree.

The approach extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees from i.i.d. data to relational data, replacing the IRM’s CRP prior with a tree-induced prior over partitions that factorizes over subtrees. This mirrors factorization strategies familiar from structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004), enabling exact marginalization over partitions consistent with a tree and efficient greedy search. The paper situates BHCD among flat and hierarchical SBMs and nonparametric relational models (IRM and its hierarchical extensions, LFRM, ILA, Mondrian process), and compares primarily to IRM.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized exposition with correct notation and clean use of f(·) and g(·) to abstract the on/off-block likelihoods. Minor style suggestion: avoid beginning sentences with citations in NIPS format.
- Sound probabilistic construction: a principled prior over tree-consistent partitions with πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|, and a dynamic program that yields an exact marginal likelihood over the tree in O(n + m).
- Simple, efficient inference: a local Bayes-factor score that depends only on data among candidate subtrees, three intuitive merge operations, and careful caching to achieve O(n^2 log n) overall complexity, independent of the number of communities.
- Practical impact: in link prediction on NIPS-234, BHCD achieves reasonable accuracy orders of magnitude faster than IRM (per reported runs), and produces interpretable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery data and the full NIPS graph.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation protocol needs clarification. Link prediction using AUC is reasonable, but please specify: fraction of edges held out, treatment of non-edges (full complement vs negative subsampling), fold construction, whether hyperparameters are fixed or tuned on training folds, and how predictions are averaged over restarts. Reporting these details would make results reproducible.
- Limited breadth of baselines. Beyond IRM, please include comparisons to widely used non-Bayesian/semi-parametric methods for community detection and hierarchical structure: degree-corrected SBM, Louvain/Leiden modularity, spectral clustering, and hierarchical SBMs (where feasible). Even if these methods do not produce full hierarchies or probabilistic predictions, link-prediction and NMI/VI metrics on comparable datasets would strengthen claims.
- Scale of experiments. The NIPS-234 study is helpful but small relative to recent evaluations; consider adding larger benchmarks (e.g., SNAP networks) to substantiate scalability claims and to compare runtimes and memory usage on sparse graphs.
- Ground-truth clustering metrics. On datasets with known (flat or hierarchical) communities or on synthetic data with planted hierarchies, report NMI/VI or hierarchical clustering metrics in addition to AUC to assess structural recovery.
- Algorithmic details. The “global” variant appears affected by stale heap scores; clarify how heap entries are invalidated/updated, or remove this variant if it is not robust. Provide an ablation on merge operations (join-only vs allowing absorptions) and on the sparse constraint (disallowing merges with zero inter-edges), as these design choices materially affect outcomes.
- Sensitivity and robustness. Explore sensitivity to hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) and the number of restarts R. Since tie-breaking injects randomness, quantify variability across runs for both structure and predictions.
- Theoretical guarantees. While not required, a brief discussion of consistency or bounds for the greedy procedure (or counterexamples) would help position the method relative to other greedy hierarchical clustering approaches.

Quality
The model and derivations appear technically sound and internally consistent. The dynamic-programming likelihood and local Bayes-factor scoring are well motivated. The main gaps are in evaluation detail and breadth of baselines.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and generally easy to follow. Suggestions: avoid sentences starting with citations; ensure Algorithm 1 is fully self-contained (notation defined at first use); standardize capitalization and minor grammar; report hardware and runtime measurement protocol for fair speed comparisons.

Originality
Adapting Bayesian Rose Trees/BHC to relational data with a tree-induced prior on partitions, coupled with a tailored agglomerative procedure for SBMs, is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel combination distinct from IRM-based hierarchical approaches and the Mondrian process.

Significance
If validated on larger datasets with stronger baselines, the proposed method could become a practical tool for fast hierarchical community discovery and link prediction. The independence from the number of communities and strong runtimes are particularly appealing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled, and efficient approach to hierarchical communities with exact tree-marginal likelihood.
- Simple, scalable inference with strong practical runtimes and reasonable predictive performance.
- Clear exposition and interpretable results on canonical datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks key baselines and ground-truth clustering metrics.
- Important experimental details (link-prediction protocol, hyperparameter handling, restart aggregation) are missing.
- Limited analysis of robustness and sensitivity; some algorithmic variants appear fragile.

Overall
A well-written and technically solid paper with a simple and efficient contribution. With a more comprehensive and transparent empirical evaluation and a few clarifications, it would be a strong submission. In its current form, the experiments are illustrative rather than definitive.
