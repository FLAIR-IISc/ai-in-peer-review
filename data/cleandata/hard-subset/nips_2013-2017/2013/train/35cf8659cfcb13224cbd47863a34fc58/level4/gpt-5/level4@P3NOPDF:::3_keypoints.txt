- Paper extends single-table IRM by replacing the CRP prior with Bayesian Rose Trees, enabling factorization over subtrees for SBMs.
- Factorization mirrors Bayesian network structure learning, yielding efficient greedy agglomerative search and tractable marginal likelihoods via sufficient statistics.
- Central algorithmic contribution is a concise agglomerative procedure (Table 1) that exploits subtree factoring for link prediction and scoring.
- Writing and presentation are generally strong, compact, and technically sound.
- The move from CRP to rose-tree prior is conceptually simple and elegant, improving computational attractiveness.
- Use of sufficient statistics and subtree factoring is clearly and cleanly explained.
- Table 1 and derivation around Eq. 7 aid understanding of workflow and computational benefits.
- Link-prediction evaluation protocol is underspecified: need details on held-out fraction, negative sampling ratio, number of trials, and confidence intervals for AUC.
- Missing clustering-quality benchmarks (e.g., NMI, ARI, VI) when ground-truth partitions are available.
- Experiments are small-scale (NIPS subset); larger graphs and reported runtime/memory are needed to substantiate scalability claims.
- Baselines are narrow (Bayesian nonparametrics); should include degree-corrected SBM, MMSB, hierarchical SBM, spectral clustering, and modularity methods.
- Ablation and sensitivity analyses are lacking: compare rose-tree vs CRP under similar inference, hyperparameter sensitivity, merge tie-breaking, and heuristics handling.
- Notational terseness—unspecified f() and g()—obscures technical detail and hurts readability.
- Presentation fixes: avoid sentence-start citations, ensure consistent notation and equation numbering, and keep symbols local to sections.
- Provide a brief intuitive explanation of Bayesian Rose Trees and state modeling assumptions (exchangeability, likelihood family, priors) and limits (e.g., degree heterogeneity).
- Suggested improvements: fully describe link-prediction setup, report AUC with standard errors across folds, add clustering metrics, evaluate larger datasets, and include log-predictive comparisons.
- Suggested experiments: broaden baselines, conduct ablations (rose-tree vs CRP, merge heuristics, hyperparameters), and add complexity/runtime analysis and reproducibility details.
- Overall: neat, practical idea with sound theory and algorithms, but empirical evaluation is illustrative rather than comprehensive and needs strengthening for a strong contribution.
