- Proposes an agglomerative method for scalable hierarchical network clustering built on the Bayesian rose tree framework, mainly adapting the likelihood for network data to enable cached sufficient statistics.
- Relies heavily on prior work (Bayesian rose tree) with minimal algorithmic changes; primary novelty is adapting the likelihood and recursive sufficient-statistic updates.
- Paper is clearly written overall but Section 3 is dense; notation and explanation around Equation (9) should be simplified for clarity.
- Claims superior performance versus a flat community baseline, but omits comparisons to alternative hierarchical network models.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to non-hierarchical baselines, failing to position the method relative to existing hierarchical techniques (e.g., nested CRP / multiscale community blockmodel and Mondrian Process).
- The model’s description treats it as “nonparametric,” which is debatable given the original Bayesian rose tree framing as model selection rather than nonparametric inference.
- Technical strength: O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood computation; however, the paper lacks sufficient detail on this computation.
- The dynamic programming argument before Equation (10) and the recursive update equations lack intuition and justification, making correctness hard to verify.
- Qualitative experiments (Sampson monastery) and IRM comparison are informative, but datasets are too small to convincingly support scalability claims.
- Overall: effectively combines rose trees with a stochastic block-model likelihood and extends an efficient agglomerative algorithm, but the work is significantly limited by missing comparisons to other hierarchical network models.
