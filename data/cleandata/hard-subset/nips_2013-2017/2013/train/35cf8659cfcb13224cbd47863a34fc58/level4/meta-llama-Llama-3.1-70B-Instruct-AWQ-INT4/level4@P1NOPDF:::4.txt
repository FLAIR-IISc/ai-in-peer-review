This paper proposes an agglomerative approach to scalably clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (possibly non-binary) trees and the algorithm structure are essentially unchanged from prior work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is modified in a straightforward way to handle networks, and a bit more work allows sufficient statistics to still be cached efficiently. The presentation is very clear, and experiments compare favorably to a baseline model with a flat community structure. However, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are lacking.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:

The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. The presentation in Section 3 is particularly dense, and I wonder if there might be a simpler way of notating and explaining Equation (9) and the subsequent parts that build upon it.

Since comparisons are limited to models that do not incorporate hierarchy in their latent structure, experiments primarily demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy for relational datasets, but do not establish whether the proposed method is superior to other hierarchical methods that currently exist. For example, there are at least two papers that model the underlying latent community structure in a hierarchical fashion. The "multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) utilizes a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009) defines a novel stochastic process that results in hierarchical structures over K-d trees as its prior. Unfortunately, the paper fails to mention these two Bayesian nonparametric models, which potentially are capable of discovering a more refined latent structure.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:

The model considered here appears to be a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. Thus, the innovations lie in how this new likelihood changes the way sufficient statistics are recursively updated in the greedy agglomerative clustering. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, which is a desirable feature. However, the description of this process is not well-explained - there is an allusion to dynamic programming before Equation (10), but the details are difficult to understand. Furthermore, regarding the recursive updating of sufficient statistics, there are some equations provided, but not much explanation is given to help understand why they are correct.

TERMINOLOGY:

It is debatable whether the "nonparametric" terminology is appropriate in this context. Blundell et al. [12] phrase the rose tree approach in terms of model selection and contrast it with nonparametric methods in their discussion. I agree with their interpretation.

EXPERIMENTS:

The qualitative experiment on Sampson's data is fine, and the comparison to the IRM is useful in demonstrating the benefits of averaging over many hypotheses when making predictions. Again, the biggest limitation is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models. There is some discussion about scalability, but none of the networks tested here are particularly large.

Overall, the paper effectively integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This enables effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but comparisons to other hierarchical network models are lacking.
