This paper is well written, with strong spelling, grammar, and correct citations – well above average in these respects. A minor stylistic note: in the NIPS style, it’s best to avoid beginning sentences with citations. The theory presented is succinct in parts, notably the consistent use of f() and g() throughout the paper.

The paper extends the simple single-table instance of an infinite relational model (IRM) by incorporating the theory of Bayesian Rose trees. This allows for the replacement of the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) formulation of IRM with the simpler equation (7). This factoring over subtrees is reminiscent of work on learning Bayesian network structures, such as Koivisto and Sood (JMLR 2004, section 3), where the posterior factorizes cleanly based on the chosen constrained structure, enabling efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood calculations, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction. 

Evaluation of SBM is frequently performed against “gold standard” data, where the true partition structure is known, and clustering metrics like NMI are used to compare results against these known clusterings. The authors report using AUC, which initially required clarification given my familiarity with NMI evaluations for SBM. I understand the approach involves deleting links and then predicting whether they should be re-added; however, this procedure requires a more detailed explanation within the paper. Furthermore, recent SBM evaluations often utilize larger datasets than those presented here, exceeding the size of the full NIPS dataset. While the evaluation in Figure 4 is a good starting point, results on a larger dataset would strengthen the findings. Comparing solely against Bayesian non-parametric methods is insufficient, as numerous published semi-parametric methods exist, including some already referenced by the authors.

The primary contribution of this algorithm lies in its agglomerative approach, which is effectively summarized in Table 1. The paper presents a good, clear theory and algorithms, though the underlying concepts and algorithms themselves are relatively simple. The experimental work is primarily illustrative rather than comprehensively comparative, and is therefore not entirely adequate to fully validate the proposed approach.
