This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric model for efficiently inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to model the network data as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). This approach builds upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, specifically Bayesian Rose Trees, adapting it to relational data. Unlike the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which uses a Chinese Restaurant Process prior over flat partitions, BHCD defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a hierarchical tree structure. This structural constraint allows the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree to be calculated exactly and efficiently using dynamic programming.

For inference, the paper introduces a greedy agglomerative algorithm that constructs the hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion. Starting with each node as its own community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of subtrees based on the merge that yields the greatest improvement in the local Bayes factor. The authors present an efficient implementation that uses caching of sufficient statistics, resulting in a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), which is notably independent of the number of communities.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Elegance:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The theoretical development is clear, and the use of general functions `f(·)` and `g(·)` for on- and off-diagonal block likelihoods provides a succinct and generalizable framework. The connection between the tree-structured prior and the resulting factorization of the marginal likelihood is elegant and well-explained.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** The main contribution is the novel combination of Bayesian Rose Trees with SBMs for relational data, along with the development of an efficient, greedy model selection algorithm. While the components (agglomerative clustering, SBMs, tree-based priors) are familiar, their synthesis into a scalable Bayesian framework for hierarchical community discovery is a valuable and original contribution.

3.  **Significance and Scalability:** The proposed algorithm's primary strength is its computational efficiency. As highlighted in Table 1, its O(n² log n) complexity, which does not depend on the number of inferred communities, compares favorably to iterative methods like MCMC for the IRM, whose runtime often depends on the number of clusters (K) and iterations (I). The experimental results, showing a two-order-of-magnitude speedup over IRM for comparable accuracy on the NIPS-234 dataset, provide a compelling demonstration of the method's practical significance for analyzing medium-to-large networks.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The paper's main weakness is the scope and rigor of its experimental evaluation. While the experiments are illustrative, they are not comprehensive enough to firmly establish the method's standing relative to the state of the art.
    *   **Missing Standard Metrics:** Evaluations of community detection algorithms, particularly those with a known ground truth, are typically performed using clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) or Adjusted Rand Index (ARI). The paper lacks any evaluation on synthetic or real-world benchmark datasets where such ground-truth partitions are available. This makes it difficult to assess the quality of the discovered hierarchies beyond visual inspection and predictive performance.
    *   **Narrow Baseline Comparison:** The comparison is almost exclusively against the IRM. While this is a relevant baseline, the field of community detection is vast. The paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons to other widely used and scalable hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., Clauset, Newman, and Moore, 2004) or other SBM inference techniques, even if they are not fully Bayesian nonparametric.
    *   **Limited Scale of Quantitative Comparison:** The key claim is scalability, yet the detailed time-vs-performance comparison in Figure 4 is conducted on a small network of only 234 nodes. To substantiate the claim of scalability, a similar quantitative comparison on a larger network (like the full NIPS dataset) is necessary. The current results demonstrate speed on a small problem but do not conclusively show how the speed/accuracy trade-off behaves at a larger scale.

2.  **Unclear Experimental Protocol:** The link prediction evaluation is not clearly described. The paper reports AUC but does not specify how the training/test sets were constructed (e.g., which links were held out and how). For reproducibility and clear interpretation of the results, this protocol needs to be explicitly detailed.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The experimental section should be significantly expanded. Including results on benchmark datasets with known community structures (e.g., Zachary's Karate Club, LFR benchmarks) and reporting NMI scores would provide a more direct assessment of the algorithm's ability to recover true hierarchical structures.
*   The set of baseline methods for comparison should be broadened to include other scalable and state-of-the-art hierarchical community detection algorithms.
*   The link prediction evaluation protocol should be clearly explained.
*   The quantitative performance comparison from Figure 4 should be replicated on a larger dataset to better demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method's scalability.
*   A minor stylistic point: The paper occasionally begins sentences with a citation (e.g., "[9] and [10] propose..."). In the NIPS style, it is preferable to rephrase this (e.g., "Methods have been proposed by [9, 10] that are...").

**Overall Recommendation:**

This paper presents a clear, elegant, and computationally efficient method for a well-motivated problem. The theoretical contribution is sound, and the proposed algorithm offers a significant speed advantage over existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM. However, the experimental evaluation is not yet at the level expected for a top-tier conference paper. It lacks standard evaluation metrics and a sufficiently broad comparison to alternative methods, making it difficult to fully assess the quality and significance of the results. The paper shows great promise but needs a more thorough empirical validation.
