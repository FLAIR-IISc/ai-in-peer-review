This paper introduces a hierarchical extension to the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modeling, drawing inspiration from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) literature. Specifically, the authors leverage two key concepts from the BRT work: utilizing a hierarchy to represent a mixture over consistent node partitions, and employing an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure to learn this hierarchy. The method is evaluated on the Sampson’s monks dataset, the full NIPS dataset, and quantitatively on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference procedure is significantly more computationally efficient than standard IRM, while achieving comparable predictive performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas. The exposition is accessible and logically structured.

While the foundational ideas originate from the Bayesian rose tree work, the authors demonstrate a valuable contribution by adapting these concepts to the relational data setting. A key achievement is maintaining comparable computational complexity during this adaptation.

Scaling Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) models, particularly to network data, is a critical challenge for practical application. The presented work addresses this challenge directly. Although the n<sup>2</sup> scaling of the base algorithm may limit its applicability to very large networks, the sparse version offers potential feasibility. It would be beneficial to see timing results on larger, real-world networks to better assess scalability. For instance, while inference on NIPS-234 completes in tens of seconds, the runtime for the full NIPS dataset is not reported. Networks commonly encountered in practice, such as gene regulatory networks (tens of thousands of nodes) and large-scale social networks, pose a more significant scaling challenge; understanding how inference scales with these network sizes is important.

Furthermore, it would be insightful to understand how closely the greedy hierarchical construction approximates the optimal tree structure. This could be investigated by comparing the greedy solution to the exhaustive search solution at the largest scale where the latter remains computationally tractable. Exploring potential theoretical guarantees for the method, such as those derived from submodularity principles, could also strengthen the work.

Despite the familiarity of the underlying ideas from the BRT literature, the contribution of this work is significant. The authors successfully adapt and extend a powerful technique to a new domain, improving computational efficiency without sacrificing predictive performance. Therefore, I recommend this paper for acceptance.
