This paper proposes an efficient agglomerative approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea builds upon Bayesian rose trees [14], adapting them to relational data by employing a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The resulting model and algorithm efficiently update sufficient statistics recursively during greedy agglomerative clustering. The presentation is generally clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable comparisons to a baseline model with flat community structure; however, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are lacking.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:**

The paper is well-written and appears technically sound. The presentation in Section 3 is particularly dense, and might benefit from a simpler notation and explanation of equation (9) and subsequent derivations. 

The experiments primarily demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy for relational datasets, but do not evaluate performance against other hierarchical methods. Several relevant Bayesian nonparametric models capable of discovering refined latent structure are not discussed. For example, the “multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration” [18] utilizes a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the “Mondrian Process” [12] defines a stochastic process resulting in hierarchical structures over K-d trees as its prior. Acknowledging and contrasting with these existing approaches would strengthen the paper’s positioning within the field.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:**

The model presented is essentially a Bayesian rose tree with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. Consequently, the key innovations lie in how this new likelihood modifies the recursive updating of sufficient statistics during the greedy agglomerative clustering process. The ability to compute the likelihood in O(Nodes + Edges) time is a notable feature. However, the description of this computation lacks detail; the allusion to dynamic programming before equation (10) is insufficient for full understanding. Furthermore, while equations are provided for the recursive updating of sufficient statistics, the rationale behind their correctness could be further elucidated.

**TERMINOLOGY:**

The use of the term “nonparametric” might be debatable. Blundell et al. [14] frame the rose tree approach as model selection and contrast it with truly nonparametric methods. This prior interpretation aligns with the current work, and acknowledging this distinction would improve clarity.

**EXPERIMENTS:**

The qualitative experiment on Sampson’s monastery network is well-executed, and the comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively demonstrates the benefits of hierarchical modeling and efficient computation for predictive performance. Again, the primary limitation is the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. While the paper discusses scalability, the tested networks are not exceptionally large, and the performance gains on larger datasets would be valuable to demonstrate.

**Summary:**

This paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods for networks, and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This allows for effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models limits the assessment of the work’s significance and novelty.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-written.
*   Presents an efficient algorithm for hierarchical community discovery.
*   Demonstrates favorable performance compared to a flat-community baseline (IRM).
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks comparison to other hierarchical network models.
*   The explanation of the efficient likelihood computation could be more detailed.
*   The terminology “nonparametric” might be misleading.
*   Experiments are limited to relatively small networks.
