This paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable clustering of networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over trees – potentially non-binary – and the overall algorithmic structure are largely consistent with previous work on Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood function is adapted to network data in a straightforward manner, and further development enables efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate favorable performance compared to a baseline model with a flat community structure; however, comparisons to other hierarchical network models are absent.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well-written and appears technically sound. Section 3’s presentation is particularly dense, and a simpler notation and explanation of equation (9) and its subsequent derivations would be beneficial. 
As the comparisons are limited to models without hierarchical latent structures, the experiments primarily demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets, but not whether this approach outperforms existing hierarchical methods. For instance, two relevant papers – "Multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012), which utilizes a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a stochastic process resulting in hierarchical structures over K-d trees – are not discussed. These Bayesian nonparametric models have the potential to discover more refined latent structures.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The model can be viewed as a Bayesian rose tree with a standard stochastic block model likelihood. Therefore, the innovations lie in how this new likelihood affects the recursive updating of sufficient statistics during the greedy agglomerative clustering. The likelihood can be computed in O(Nodes + Edges) time, a notable advantage. Unfortunately, the description of this computation is unclear; while dynamic programming is alluded to before equation (10), the details are difficult to follow. Similarly, while equations are provided for the recursive updating of sufficient statistics, the reasoning behind their correctness lacks sufficient explanation.

TERMINOLOGY:
The use of “nonparametric” terminology is questionable. Blundell et al. [12] frame the rose tree approach as model selection, contrasting it with nonparametric methods – a distinction with which I concur.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative experiment on the Sampson network is satisfactory, and the comparison to the IRM effectively illustrates the benefits of averaging over multiple hypotheses for prediction. However, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models remains a key limitation. While scalability is discussed, the tested networks are not particularly large. 

In summary, this work capably integrates prior research on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods for networks, and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This facilitates effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but lacks comparisons to other hierarchical network models.
