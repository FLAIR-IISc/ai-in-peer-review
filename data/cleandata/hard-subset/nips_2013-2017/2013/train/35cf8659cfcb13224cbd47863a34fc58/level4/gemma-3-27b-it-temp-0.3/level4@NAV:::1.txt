The authors introduce a hierarchical extension to the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) framework for network modeling, leveraging concepts from the Bayesian rose tree literature. Specifically, they employ a hierarchical structure to define a mixture over coherent node partitions and utilize an efficient, greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this hierarchy. The paper demonstrates the method’s performance through qualitative analysis on the Sampson’s monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, alongside quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Notably, the proposed inference procedure offers substantial computational advantages over standard IRM, while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and accessible explanation of the core concepts. 

While the foundational ideas originate from the Bayesian rose tree work, adapting these concepts to the relational data context, and preserving computational efficiency, represents a valuable contribution.

Scaling Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) models – and network models in particular – is a crucial step towards practical applicability. Although the quadratic scaling of the base algorithm may limit its use with very large networks, the sparse variant should remain viable. Providing timing results for larger, real-world networks would strengthen the analysis. For example, given that NIPS-234 requires only seconds to process, what is the runtime for the full NIPS dataset? Furthermore, considering the scale of typical gene networks (tens of thousands of nodes) and large social networks, investigating the inference scaling behavior is important. 

It would also be beneficial to assess the quality of the tree structure obtained via the greedy method, perhaps by comparing it to the optimal tree found through exhaustive search at a manageable scale. Exploring potential theoretical guarantees for the method, such as those based on submodularity, would further enhance its robustness.

Despite the familiarity of the underlying ideas from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution is significant, and I recommend acceptance of this paper.
