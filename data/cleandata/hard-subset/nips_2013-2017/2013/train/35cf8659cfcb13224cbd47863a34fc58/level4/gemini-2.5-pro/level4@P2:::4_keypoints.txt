- Efficiency & scalability: BHCD uses a greedy agglomerative algorithm with O(n^2 log n) worst-case time and is experimentally ~100x faster than the IRM while retaining comparable predictive performance.  
- Model integration: The method coherently combines the Bayesian rose tree prior with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood; extending Heller & Ghahramani’s agglomerative approach to blockmodels is technically sound.  
- Experimental validation: Thorough experiments on the NIPS co-authorship dataset (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC over time) and qualitative results on Sampson’s network demonstrate speed and interpretable hierarchies.  
- Limited baseline comparisons: The paper only compares to the flat IRM and omits empirical comparisons to other hierarchical network models (e.g., Mondrian Process, Multiscale Community Blockmodel, Herlau et al.), leaving its standing among hierarchical methods unclear.  
- Incremental novelty: Much of the approach reuses prior components (rose tree prior, greedy agglomeration, standard blockmodel); the main technical contribution is an efficient caching scheme for O(1) statistic updates, which is more engineering than conceptual.  
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: Key claims (O(n+m) marginal likelihood computation, recursive sufficient-statistic updates) are presented without derivations, pseudocode, or intuition, making verification and reproduction difficult.  
- Terminology concern: Labeling the approach as “Bayesian nonparametric” is debatable; the framing should align with the original rose tree view as model selection over tree structures.  
- Scalability testing gap: Claims of scalability would be stronger if evaluated on substantially larger or denser graphs than the 2,864-node NIPS dataset.  
- Suggested improvements: Add derivations/pseudocode in an appendix and include experimental comparisons to at least one other Bayesian hierarchical community detection model to better contextualize performance.  
- Overall conclusion: BHCD is a fast, practical integration of rose trees and blockmodels with compelling speedups, but its significance among hierarchical methods and some technical clarity remain unestablished.
