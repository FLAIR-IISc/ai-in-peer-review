This paper generalizes the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering from observed links. The authors introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering that selects a tree representing valid partitions of the entities, and they evaluate the approach on two corpora of social connectivity data, claiming it is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

Overall assessment and significance:
- The problem is of clear interest to the NIPS community. Conceptually appealing Bayesian nonparametric methods are often slow in practice, and social scientists are increasingly adopting sophisticated Bayesian models for network analysis; a hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model is therefore timely and valuable. This paper addresses both concerns.

Technical contribution:
- The key insight is that restricting the space of partitions to those consistent with a tree allows efficient computation of the data’s marginal likelihood, whereas the IRM must effectively consider an exponential number of possible partitions.
- However, I am confused about how the tree-learning procedure relates to the model’s generative story. Because the algorithm is greedy, it does not appear to perform true posterior inference over trees, nor is it clearly a point MAP or ML estimate. I may be misunderstanding, but Section 4 should be clarified to explain precisely what objective is being optimized, how it connects to the Bayesian model, and in what sense (if any) the procedure approximates posterior inference.

Empirical results:
- The results are impressive. Across AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, the proposed method performs comparably to or slightly better than the IRM, its closest competitor.
- While the absolute gains in these scores are modest, the reported runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference, which is a compelling advantage.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper’s clarity and writing should be improved.
  - There are several spelling and grammar errors (e.g., line 126).
  - Parts of the prose are vague; for example, lines 47–50 in the introduction should define what “local changes” means in this context and what “iterations” refer to (are these iterations of an MCMC algorithm?).
  - The datasets (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not described clearly enough for readers unfamiliar with them; more context would help.
  - Some of the mathematics feels unnecessarily opaque (e.g., Eq. 5).
  - Figure 3 is confusing, especially for readers unfamiliar with the Monastery dataset; it is visually unclear which method performs best. Using a more familiar dataset or improving the figure’s explanation would help.
  - Section 4, which presents the core contribution, required several readings to understand and should be rewritten for clarity.

Conclusion:
- If these issues are addressed, the paper’s novelty and strong empirical results would warrant publication.
- The paper proposes a novel model of graph edge structure that can be viewed as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. The core learning algorithm is insightful, and the experiments are strong, but the writing quality needs significant improvement, especially in Section 4 and in the description of datasets, figures, and key terms.
