- Proposes a hierarchical extension to Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) for network modelling, inspired by Bayesian rose trees (BRT).  
- Leverages hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions and a greedy agglomerative procedure to learn the hierarchy.  
- Evaluated on Sampson’s monks, full NIPS, and quantitatively on NIPS-234 datasets.  
- Inference procedure reduces computational cost relative to standard IRM while maintaining comparable predictive performance.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.  
- Originality: adapts BRT ideas to relational data with substantial effort, retaining computational efficiency.  
- Significance: advancing scalable Bayesian nonparametrics for networks is important; sparse variant looks promising but quadratic scaling may limit very large networks.  
- Requests more investigation of scaling behavior—report inference time on full NIPS and discuss applicability to networks with tens of thousands to millions of nodes.  
- Recommends assessing the quality of the greedy hierarchical construction versus optimal trees (exhaustive search where tractable) and seeking theoretical guarantees (e.g., submodularity).  
- Minor corrections: wording edits (“whilst… but” → “although”; “treeS” → “trees”; “A fruitful next steps” → “Fruitful next steps”).  
- Overall recommendation: despite familiar underlying ideas, the adaptation to relational data and focus on efficiency is a valuable contribution; recommends acceptance.
