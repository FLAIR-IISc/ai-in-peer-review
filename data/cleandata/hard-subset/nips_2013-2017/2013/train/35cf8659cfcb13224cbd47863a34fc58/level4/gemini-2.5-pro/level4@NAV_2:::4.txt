This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for identifying hierarchical community structures in networks. The model is framed as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The core technical contribution is a greedy agglomerative algorithm for model selection, which learns a probabilistic hierarchy in a single bottom-up pass. This work directly extends the Bayesian Rose Trees framework [14] by adapting it to relational data with a blockmodel likelihood and developing an efficient caching scheme for the sufficient statistics, enabling the algorithm to scale quadratically in the number of vertices.

**Strengths**
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed agglomerative algorithm is a key strength. With a worst-case time complexity of O(n^2 log n) that is independent of the number of communities and a fixed number of merge steps, the method is significantly faster than iterative inference schemes like MCMC used for comparable models (e.g., IRM). The experiments clearly demonstrate that BHCD provides good results orders of magnitude faster than the IRM baseline.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically sound. It capably integrates the Bayesian Rose Trees model selection framework with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood appropriate for network data. The generalization of the efficient agglomerative clustering algorithm to this new scenario is well-executed.
*   **Clarity:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the overall approach easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The primary weakness is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models. The experiments compare BHCD exclusively to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which discovers a flat community structure. While these results effectively show the benefits of modeling hierarchy, they do not demonstrate whether BHCD is superior to or different from other existing *hierarchical* methods. The paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons to relevant prior work.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to cite or discuss several highly relevant Bayesian nonparametric models for hierarchical community detection. For instance, the "multiscale community blockmodel" (Ho et al., 2011) uses a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy & Teh, 2009) defines a prior over hierarchical partitions. A discussion of how BHCD relates to these methods is necessary to properly contextualize its contribution.
*   **Clarity of Technical Details:** While generally clear, certain technical sections could be improved.
    *   The description of the O(n+m) likelihood computation in Section 3 is brief. The text alludes to dynamic programming, but the details of this calculation are not provided, making it difficult to fully appreciate this result.
    *   Similarly, Section 4 presents the recursive update equations for the sufficient statistics (Equation 13) without much intuition or justification. A clearer explanation of why these updates are correct would improve the reader's understanding.
*   **Originality:** The conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, as the work is a direct application of the Bayesian Rose Trees algorithm [14] to a new likelihood. The main innovation lies in the engineering required to efficiently cache and update the sufficient statistics for the blockmodel.
*   **Questionable Terminology:** The use of "nonparametric" to describe the method is debatable. The original Rose Tree paper [14] frames the approach as greedy model selection over a discrete space of tree structures, and contrasts it with nonparametric methods. Adopting this more precise framing would be more appropriate.
*   **Scalability Claims:** The paper discusses scalability, but the networks used in the experiments are not exceptionally large. While the complexity analysis is valuable, demonstrating the method's performance on larger networks would better substantiate the claims of its suitability for large-scale analysis.

In summary, the paper presents a capable integration of existing methods to produce an efficient algorithm for hierarchical community discovery. The speed and quality of results compared to a flat-structure baseline are impressive. However, the significance of the contribution is diminished by the omission of comparisons to other, more directly related hierarchical models.
