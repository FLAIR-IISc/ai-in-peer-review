The authors propose a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, drawing on key concepts from the Bayesian rose tree paper. Specifically, they utilize a hierarchy to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes and develop an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure for learning this hierarchy. The paper presents qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Notably, the proposed inference method achieves significantly reduced computational costs compared to the IRM while maintaining similar predictive performance.

Quality and clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and concise exposition of the key ideas.

Originality: Although the main ideas are adapted from the Bayesian rose tree work, the authors have made a notable effort to tailor these concepts to the relational data setting, particularly in preserving the same computational complexity.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially network models, is a crucial challenge for their practical application. While the basic algorithm's n^2 scaling may be limiting for larger networks, the sparse version is expected to remain feasible. It would be beneficial to include timing results for larger real-world networks, such as the full NIPS dataset, which could provide insight into the method's scalability. Furthermore, exploring the performance of the greedy method on larger networks, such as gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes, would be valuable. Investigating the proximity of the greedy method to the optimal tree, potentially through exhaustive search at a smaller scale, could also provide useful insights. Additionally, it would be desirable to provide theoretical support for the method, such as submodularity analysis.

In conclusion, while the ideas presented in this paper are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the contribution remains significant, and the paper deserves acceptance.
