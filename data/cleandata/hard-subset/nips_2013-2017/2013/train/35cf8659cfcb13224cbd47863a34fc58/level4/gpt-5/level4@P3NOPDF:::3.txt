Summary
The paper studies stochastic block models (SBMs) and extends the single-table IRM setting by incorporating Bayesian Rose Trees. This replaces the CRP prior with a rose-tree prior (Eq. 7), enabling a clean factorization over subtrees. The factorization mirrors ideas from Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, JMLR 2004, Sec. 3), yielding efficient greedy search, tractable marginal likelihood computations via sufficient statistics, and straightforward link prediction. The central algorithmic contribution is an agglomerative procedure summarized concisely in Table 1.

Strengths
- Writing quality: The paper is generally well written, with proper spelling, grammar, and citations. The presentation is compact and technically sound.
- Conceptual clarity: The move from a CRP to a rose-tree prior is simple yet elegant. The induced factorization over subtrees makes the search and scoring computationally attractive.
- Algorithmic contribution: The agglomerative method is well motivated and appears to be effective in practice. The use of sufficient statistics and subtree factoring is cleanly explained.
- Presentation aids: Table 1 and the derivation around Eq. 7 clarify the workflow and the computational benefits.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation protocol: The paper reports AUC for link prediction, but the protocol is not described. For SBMs, common practices include holding out a stratified subset of edges and non-edges, specifying the negative sampling ratio, and averaging over multiple random splits with uncertainty estimates. Please clarify precisely: what fraction of edges was held out, how were negative examples constructed, how many trials were run, and are confidence intervals reported?
- Missing clustering benchmarks: When “gold standard” partitions are available, standard metrics such as NMI, ARI, and VI are typically reported. Since the model induces clusterings, these metrics should complement AUC to assess the quality of the recovered partition structure.
- Limited scale: Recent SBM evaluations use larger graphs than the NIPS subset shown in Figure 4. To substantiate claims of scalability and practical utility, include larger datasets and report runtime, memory use, and predictive performance.
- Baselines: The current comparisons focus on Bayesian nonparametrics. To position the contribution more broadly, include strong semi-parametric and parametric baselines, such as degree-corrected SBMs, MMSB variants, hierarchical SBMs, spectral clustering methods, and modularity-based approaches. Without these, it is difficult to judge the practical gains of the rose-tree prior and agglomerative search.
- Ablations and sensitivity: It would help to dissect the contribution of the rose-tree constraint versus a CRP prior using comparable inference (e.g., agglomerative vs. Gibbs or split-merge moves), and to report sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization. Details on merge tie-breaking, handling of local optima, and any pruning or heuristics would also improve reproducibility.
- Notational terseness: While the theory is succinct, the persistent use of unspecified f() and g() obscures some technical detail. Expanding their definitions where they first appear would improve readability.

Clarity and presentation
- Avoid beginning sentences with citations per NIPS style; instead, integrate citations into the sentence.
- Ensure consistent notation and equation numbering. Where possible, keep symbols self-contained within sections.
- Provide a brief intuitive explanation of Bayesian Rose Trees and their implications for partition complexity and hierarchy depth.
- Clearly state assumptions (e.g., exchangeability, likelihood family, prior choices) and discuss when they may be violated in real networks (e.g., degree heterogeneity).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a comprehensive description of the link-prediction setup, including train/test splits, negative sampling, and the scoring function. Report AUC with standard errors across multiple random folds.
- Include clustering-quality metrics on datasets with known partitions; consider synthetic SBMs and real benchmarks with ground truth communities.
- Evaluate on larger graphs and report computational metrics. A log-predictive probability or per-edge held-out likelihood comparison would complement AUC.
- Broaden baselines to include degree-corrected SBM, MMSB, hierarchical SBM, spectral and modularity approaches; discuss where the proposed method is expected to excel or trade off accuracy for speed.
- Provide ablation studies: (i) rose-tree prior vs. CRP with similar search, (ii) effect of different merge heuristics, and (iii) sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Add a brief complexity analysis of the agglomerative procedure (per-merge and overall), along with implementation details sufficient to reproduce results.

Overall assessment
This is a clearly written paper with a neat and practical idea: replacing the CRP with a rose-tree prior to enable subtree factorization and efficient agglomerative search. The theory and algorithms are sound and well presented, albeit somewhat minimalistic in notation. However, the experimental section is largely illustrative, not yet comparative or comprehensive enough for a strong empirical claim. With a fully specified link-prediction protocol, clustering metrics against known partitions, larger datasets, and broader baselines, the contribution would be considerably strengthened. As it stands, the work is promising but requires a more thorough empirical evaluation to meet the bar.
