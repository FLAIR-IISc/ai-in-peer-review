- Introduces a Bayesian hierarchical model extending the IRM by using Bayesian rose trees to form a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative learning algorithm.  
- Demonstrates results on Sampson’s monastery, NIPS-234, and full NIPS co-authorship networks, reporting large speedups while retaining comparable predictive performance.  
- Exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to relational data.  
- Originality lies in successfully adapting Bayesian rose trees to relational modeling, producing a practical and efficient hierarchical community detection method.  
- Significant because it addresses scalability of Bayesian nonparametric models for networks, making them more computationally tractable.  
- Scalability concern: lacks thorough evaluation on very large networks (timings for full NIPS and tens-of-thousands-node graphs, and sparse-version performance, are needed).  
- Algorithmic-quality concern: greedy algorithm’s solution quality not assessed—recommend comparisons to optimal trees for small instances and theoretical guarantees (e.g., submodularity).  
- Minor edits: change “whilst… but” → “while… but”; “pair of treeS” → “pair of trees”; “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step”.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear presentation, successful methodological adaptation, major speed improvements, comparable predictive accuracy, addresses scalability challenge.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited large-scale scalability analysis, no theoretical analysis of greedy optimality, and no exhaustive-search validation of solutions.
