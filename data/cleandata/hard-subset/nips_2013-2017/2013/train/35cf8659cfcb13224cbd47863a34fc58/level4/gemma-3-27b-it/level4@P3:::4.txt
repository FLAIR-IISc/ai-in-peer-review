This paper proposes an efficient agglomerative approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to adapt Bayesian rose trees [12] by incorporating a stochastic blockmodel likelihood, enabling efficient recursive updates of sufficient statistics during greedy agglomerative clustering. The presentation is generally clear, and the experiments demonstrate favorable comparisons to a flat community structure baseline, highlighting the benefits of modeling hierarchical relationships. However, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons to other existing hierarchical network models.

**Significance & Related Work:**

The paper is technically sound and well-written. Section 3’s notation, while dense, ultimately conveys the model effectively. However, the paper’s positioning within the broader literature on hierarchical community detection could be strengthened. While comparisons are primarily made against models focusing on flat community structure, several Bayesian nonparametric methods specifically address hierarchical structures. Notably, the “multiscale community blockmodel for network exploration” [Ho et al., 2012] utilizes a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, and the "Mondrian Process" [Roy et al., 2009] employs a novel stochastic process that generates hierarchical structures over K-d trees. Failing to acknowledge and compare against these related approaches limits the assessment of the proposed method’s unique contributions and relative performance. The work would benefit from a discussion of how it differs from, and potentially complements, these alternative hierarchical models.

**Originality & Clarity:**

The proposed model can be viewed as a Bayesian rose tree with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. Therefore, the primary innovations lie in adapting the recursive updates of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative clustering framework to this new likelihood. The reported O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood computation is a significant advantage. However, the explanation of this efficiency is somewhat lacking. While dynamic programming is mentioned before equation (10), the details are not clearly elucidated. Furthermore, the correctness of the recursive updating equations for sufficient statistics, while presented, isn’t thoroughly explained, hindering complete understanding.

**Terminology:**

The use of “nonparametric” terminology is debatable. As Blundell et al. [12] point out, rose trees are fundamentally about model selection rather than being strictly nonparametric. This paper’s approach aligns with that interpretation, and acknowledging this nuance would enhance precision.

**Experiments:**

The qualitative experiment on Sampson’s monastery network is reasonable, and the comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively demonstrates the performance benefits of incorporating hierarchical structure. The key limitation remains the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. While scalability is discussed, the networks used in the experiments aren't exceptionally large, and the demonstrated speedup, while valuable, doesn’t represent a challenge in current computational resources. Adding experiments with larger datasets would further showcase the scalability benefits.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Efficient Algorithm:** The proposed greedy agglomerative algorithm with cached sufficient statistics offers computational efficiency, particularly for dense networks.
*   **Principled Bayesian Framework:**  The model provides a well-defined Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and presents the core ideas in a reasonably accessible manner.
*   **Demonstrated Benefits of Hierarchy:** Experiments showcase the advantages of modeling hierarchical community structure.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The lack of comparison to other Bayesian nonparametric models for hierarchical community detection hinders the assessment of the method’s novelty and performance.
*   **Insufficient Explanation of Efficiency:**  The explanation of the O(Nodes + Edges) likelihood computation could be more detailed and transparent.
*   **Debatable Terminology:** The use of “nonparametric” is questionable and could be clarified.
*   **Scalability Experiments:** Experiments primarily use moderately-sized networks, and scalability claims could be more rigorously supported with larger datasets.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of community detection.
*   The proposed algorithm is efficient and offers a practical solution for discovering hierarchical structures in networks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models weakens the claim of novelty and makes it difficult to assess the method's relative performance.
*   The explanation of the algorithm’s efficiency could be improved.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the area of hierarchical community detection. The algorithm is well-motivated, efficiently implemented, and demonstrates promising results. However, the lack of comparison to existing hierarchical models is a significant limitation. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting the authors to: 1) Thoroughly discuss and compare their method to relevant hierarchical network models such as [Ho et al., 2012] and [Roy et al., 2009]. 2) Provide a more detailed explanation of the efficient likelihood computation, clarifying the dynamic programming aspects. 3) Reconsider the use of the term "nonparametric."
