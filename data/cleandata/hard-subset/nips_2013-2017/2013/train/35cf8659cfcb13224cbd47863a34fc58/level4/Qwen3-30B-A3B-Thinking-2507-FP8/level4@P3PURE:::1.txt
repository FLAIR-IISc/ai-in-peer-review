

Quality/Clarity. The paper is exceptionally well written, with clear and precise exposition of the core ideas.

Originality. While the foundational concepts are drawn from the Bayesian rose tree framework, the adaptation to relational data—particularly the maintenance of computational efficiency—represents non-trivial work. The key contribution lies in this effective transfer to network modeling.

Significance. Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially for network data, is a critical challenge for practical adoption. The proposed method achieves computational efficiency (significantly cheaper than IRM) while maintaining predictive performance. However, the n² scaling of the base algorithm could limit applicability to very large networks. The sparse variant appears promising, but the paper lacks timing data for larger real-world networks. Specifically: NIPS-234 runs in tens of seconds; how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Gene networks typically involve tens of thousands of nodes, and social networks are substantially larger—how does inference scale in such regimes? Additionally, it would be valuable to assess the quality of the greedy solution relative to the optimal tree. Could the gap be quantified via exhaustive search on the largest feasible scale? Further, is there theoretical justification for the greedy approach (e.g., via submodularity)?

While the core ideas are familiar from the Bayesian rose tree work, the adaptation to relational data and the demonstrated computational gains constitute a significant contribution. This paper should be accepted.
