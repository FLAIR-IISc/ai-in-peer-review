- Proposes a scalable agglomerative method combining the Bayesian rose tree prior with a stochastic block model likelihood for hierarchical community detection.  
- Main contribution: adapts a greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to networks via recursive updates of sufficient statistics.  
- Develops an efficient likelihood computation with O(Nodes + Edges) complexity to support scalability.  
- Shows qualitative results (Sampson’s monastery) and quantitative gains over a flat model (IRM), demonstrating benefits of hierarchical modeling.  
- Major weakness: experimental evaluation omits comparisons to other hierarchical network models and relevant Bayesian nonparametric approaches (e.g., Multiscale Community Blockmodel, Mondrian Process).  
- Section 3 and related technical exposition (notably Equation (9), the dynamic programming before Eq. (10), and recursive-update derivations) are dense and lack intuitive explanation.  
- Originality concern: key components (rose tree prior and agglomerative search) are adopted from prior work; novelty is chiefly the SBM integration and adapted update rules.  
- Terminology concern: calling the method “nonparametric” is potentially misleading given the original framing of Bayesian rose trees.  
- Scalability claims are under-supported because experiments are limited to moderate-size networks; larger real-world graphs are needed.  
- Overall: a principled, useful engineering contribution that would be much stronger with clearer technical exposition and thorough comparisons to hierarchical baselines.
