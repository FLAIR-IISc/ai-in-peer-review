The authors present a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modelling using the key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper: 1) that the hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and 2) that this hierarchy can be learnt using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results on Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset, are presented. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the IRM, whilst obtaining similar predictive performance.

Quality and Clarity: The paper is very well written, and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. The authors have done an excellent job of explaining the technical details of their approach, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian nonparametric methods.

Originality: While the main ideas are taken from the Bayesian rose tree work, there was some work to be done in adapting these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity. The authors have successfully extended the Bayesian rose tree approach to network data, which is a notable achievement.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models to larger networks is an important challenge if these methods are to be used by practitioners. The proposed approach addresses this challenge by providing a computationally efficient method for hierarchical community discovery. While the n^2 scaling of the basic algorithm may prove prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should presumably remain feasible. However, it would have been nice to see some timings on larger real-world networks. For instance, how long does the full NIPS dataset take to process? Gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are much larger still; it would be useful to know how inference scales in these cases.

Additionally, it would be interesting to have some feel for how close to the optimal tree the greedy method gets. This could be investigated at the largest scale that allows for exhaustive search to find the optimal tree. Furthermore, is it possible to provide any theoretical support for the method, e.g., in terms of submodularity?

While the ideas presented here are familiar from the BRT work, the contribution is still significant, and this paper should be accepted.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel extension of the IRM to hierarchical community discovery in networks.
* The proposed approach is computationally efficient and scales to larger networks.
* The authors provide qualitative and quantitative results on several datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The n^2 scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for very large networks.
* The paper could benefit from additional experiments on larger real-world networks.
* Theoretical support for the method, such as submodularity, would strengthen the paper.
