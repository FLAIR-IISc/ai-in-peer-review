The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Information Retrieval Model (IRM) for network modelling, leveraging key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper: 1) the hierarchy specifies a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and 2) this hierarchy can be learned using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results are shown on the Sampson’s monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, with quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed inference is computationally much cheaper than the standard IRM, while achieving similar predictive performance.

Quality and clarity: The paper is very well written and the exposition of the key ideas is clear. 

Originality: The core ideas are drawn from the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) work, but adapting these to the relational data setting required non-trivial effort, particularly in maintaining comparable computational complexity. 

Significance: Scaling Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) models, especially to network models, is an important challenge for practical application. While the n<sup>2</sup> scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should remain feasible. It would be beneficial to see timings on larger, real-world networks. The NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process; how long does inference take on the full NIPS dataset? Gene networks typically have tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are considerably larger still: how would inference scale with these datasets? 

Additionally, it would be helpful to understand how closely the greedy method approximates the optimal tree structure. This could be investigated by comparing its performance to exhaustive search at the largest scale where finding the optimal tree remains tractable. Is there any theoretical justification for the method, perhaps in terms of submodularity?
