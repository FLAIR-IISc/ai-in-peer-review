This paper introduces a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, adapting key concepts from the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) framework. The core ideas are twofold: first, a hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and second, this hierarchy can be learned efficiently via a greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors demonstrate their model with qualitative results on the Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, and quantitative results on the NIPS-234 subset. A key finding is that the proposed inference method is significantly more computationally efficient than standard IRM inference while achieving comparable predictive performance.

**Quality and Clarity**
The paper is well-written, and the exposition of the core ideas is clear. The motivation and the proposed method are easy to follow.

**Originality and Significance**
While the foundational concepts are adapted from the BRT literature, the contribution is significant. The authors have successfully translated these ideas to the relational data setting, a non-trivial task, particularly in maintaining the computational efficiency of the greedy inference algorithm. The work addresses the important challenge of scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) methods for network analysis, a crucial step for their practical adoption by practitioners.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
The primary concern is the scalability of the proposed method and the experimental evaluation of this aspect.

1.  **Scaling and Timings:** The O(n^2) scaling of the basic algorithm may prove prohibitive for larger networks. While the authors suggest that a sparse version should remain feasible, the evaluation could be strengthened by providing empirical evidence. The experiments on the NIPS-234 dataset report timings of only tens of seconds. It would be very informative to see timings for the full NIPS dataset. Furthermore, to understand the practical limits of the model, how would inference time scale on much larger, real-world networks, such as gene networks (tens of thousands of nodes) or large social networks?

2.  **Optimality of the Greedy Method:** It would be beneficial to have a better understanding of how close the greedy agglomerative procedure gets to the optimal tree. This could be investigated empirically by comparing the greedy solution to the optimal tree found via exhaustive search on a network small enough for this to be tractable.

3.  **Theoretical Justification:** Could the authors provide any theoretical support for the greedy method? For instance, does the objective function exhibit properties like submodularity that would provide some performance guarantees for a greedy approach?

**Minor Corrections**
*   Line 48, page 1: The phrase "whilst... but" is redundant. Please rephrase.
*   The phrase "pair of trees" should likely be singular, "pair of tree," depending on the context.
*   "A fruitful next steps" should be corrected to "A fruitful next step" or "Fruitful next steps."

**Conclusion**
Despite the suggestions for a more thorough evaluation of scalability, the paper makes a valuable contribution. The adaptation of the BRT framework to network modeling is a significant step forward for scalable Bayesian nonparametric methods. The ideas are clearly presented, and the initial results are promising. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
