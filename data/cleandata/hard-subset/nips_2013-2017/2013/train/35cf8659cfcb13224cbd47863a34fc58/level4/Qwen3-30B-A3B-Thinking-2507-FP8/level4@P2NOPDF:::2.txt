

The authors introduce a hierarchical extension of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, enabling inference of hierarchical cluster structures rather than flat partitions from observed network data. Their approach employs an agglomerative learning algorithm to construct a valid tree representing nested partitions, contrasting with the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which must consider exponentially many flat partitions. Empirical results on two social network datasets demonstrate that their method achieves comparable or slightly better performance (in AUC, predictive likelihood, and accuracy) than IRM while offering orders-of-magnitude faster inference.

This work addresses a timely problem relevant to a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. Bayesian nonparametric methods, though conceptually appealing, often suffer from computational inefficiency in practice—this paper directly tackles that limitation. Additionally, the growing adoption of sophisticated Bayesian models by social scientists makes this hierarchical extension of a prominent network-discovery framework highly valuable.

The key technical insight lies in efficiently computing the marginal likelihood by restricting partitions to tree-structured hierarchies. However, the relationship between the greedy tree-learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative model remains unclear. As a greedy algorithm, it cannot perform true posterior inference over trees or yield a MAP/ML estimate. The authors should clarify this conceptual gap in Section 4, as the current presentation risks misrepresenting the method’s theoretical foundation.

The empirical results are compelling. While absolute performance gains over IRM are modest, the dramatic speed advantage is a significant practical contribution. The runtime improvements justify the method’s applicability to larger-scale network analysis tasks.

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from multiple clarity issues that hinder comprehension. Spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126) distract from the content. The introduction’s phrasing is vague: Line 47–50 refers to "local changes" and "iterations" without defining them (e.g., are these MCMC iterations? Algorithm steps?). The datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) lack sufficient description, confusing readers unfamiliar with these sources. Mathematical exposition is occasionally unnecessarily dense (e.g., Equation 5), and Figure 3 is difficult to interpret without domain familiarity—indicating which method performs best visually is unclear. Section 4, the core contribution, requires multiple readings to grasp due to its density and lack of intuitive scaffolding.

The writing quality needs substantial improvement. The authors should refine prose for precision, define all technical terms upon first use, and provide clearer context for datasets and figures. A more accessible dataset (e.g., a well-known social network) would strengthen Figure 3’s interpretability. The repeated assertion that the model is a "Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by IRM" is redundant and should be streamlined.

In summary, the paper presents a novel, computationally efficient hierarchical model with strong empirical results. The core idea—leveraging tree-structured partitions to enable tractable inference—is insightful and addresses a genuine limitation of existing methods. However, the current presentation obscures the method’s contributions and rationale. With revisions to clarify the theoretical connection between the greedy algorithm and the Bayesian model, improve writing quality, and enhance figure/dataset explanations, this work would make a strong contribution. Given the novelty, computational benefits, and robust results, the paper merits publication upon addressing these presentation issues.
