Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. A tree defines a mixture over all partitions consistent with that tree, enabling exact marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming. The likelihood uses conjugate beta-Bernoulli blockmodels with distinct priors for within- and between-cluster edges. Inference proceeds by a greedy agglomerative procedure that considers join and absorption merges, scores them via local Bayes factors, and leverages cached sufficient statistics for efficiency. A “sparse” variant forbids merging completely disconnected subtrees, improving speed and results on sparse graphs. Prediction for unobserved links is computed by recursively traversing the hierarchy.

The approach builds directly on Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT), which use trees to define mixtures over tree-consistent partitions, and adapts these ideas to relational data by combining them with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods. The method is compared to IRM (both MCMC and greedy search), and related to other Bayesian nonparametric network models such as LFRM and ILA. Qualitative results are shown on Sampson’s monastery network and the full NIPS co-authorship graph; quantitative results on NIPS-234 demonstrate comparable or better predictive performance than IRM at much lower computational cost (often orders of magnitude faster), with worst-case O(n^2 log n) inference independent of the number of discovered communities.

Strengths:
- Clear, coherent exposition and well-motivated model construction.
- Elegant reuse of BRT to define a tractable mixture over partitions for relational data.
- Practical, scalable inference using local Bayes factors and careful caching; the sparse variant is well motivated.
- Good empirical performance versus IRM in both speed and predictive quality; qualitative hierarchies appear interpretable.
- Exact marginal likelihood on a fixed tree via dynamic programming is a nice technical point, and prediction is efficient.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Originality is moderate: the core hierarchical-mixture idea and agglomerative search are taken from BRT; the main novelty lies in adapting them to the blockmodel setting and preserving computational complexity.
- Empirical scope is limited. NIPS-234 is relatively small, and while full NIPS is shown qualitatively, there are no comprehensive timing/scale reports for larger real-world or synthetic networks.
- No analysis of how close the greedy search gets to the global optimum. The method depends on tie-breaking and restarts; variance across runs is not reported.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees (e.g., approximation bounds, submodularity-like properties) or conditions under which greedy merges are consistent.
- Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and of non-conjugate likelihoods (which prior work suggests can improve predictions).

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound. The generative process, the factorization over tree-consistent partitions, and the DP recursion are well specified. The local Bayes factor scoring and caching strategy are plausible and consistent with the claimed complexity. More detail on memory usage and update costs would further strengthen the quality assessment.

Clarity:
The paper is very well written. The model and algorithms are clearly described, and the distinction between join and absorption merges is helpful. To improve clarity, consider:
- Providing more implementation details on the caching scheme and any data structures needed to achieve O(1) inner-loop updates.
- Reporting the computational complexity of prediction explicitly (linear in depth).
- Explaining tie-breaking and restart strategies early, and reporting variability across restarts.

Originality:
While the core ideas follow BRT, adapting them to relational data with blockmodel likelihoods and preserving O(n^2 log n) behavior is nontrivial. The sparse variant is a practical addition. Overall, the paper offers a meaningful engineering and modeling contribution rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.

Significance:
Scaling Bayesian nonparametric network models is important for practical use. BHCD’s independence from the number of communities and its speed relative to IRM are compelling. However, the base O(n^2) scaling could still be prohibitive for very large graphs; demonstrating performance on larger datasets would strengthen the impact.

Questions and suggestions:
- Please report end-to-end run times and memory usage on the full NIPS graph and, if possible, larger real networks. How does runtime scale with m versus n for the sparse variant?
- For small graphs where exhaustive search is feasible, quantify how close the greedy solution is to the optimal tree (e.g., likelihood gap).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) and for the choice to prohibit merges of disconnected subtrees.
- Report variability across restarts and any heuristics used to stabilize results.
- Discuss whether any submodularity or other properties might justify greedy merging, even partially.
- Consider including comparisons to non-conjugate likelihoods or alternative priors, given prior reports of improved accuracy.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed adaptation of BRT to relational data with strong empirical speedups.
- Simple, scalable inference with exact marginal likelihood on fixed trees.
- Useful in practice; results are interpretable and competitive with IRM.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited novelty beyond adapting BRT ideas.
- Empirical evaluation lacks large-scale timing studies and analysis of search optimality/variance.
- No theoretical guarantees for the greedy procedure.

Minor corrections:
- “computational much cheaper” → “computationally much cheaper”
- “practioners” → “practitioners”
- “pair of treeS” → “pair of trees”
- “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps”
- Avoid “whilst … but”; prefer “whilst … and” or “although …”

Overall recommendation:
While the core ideas are familiar from BRT, the adaptation to network data with efficient inference is useful and well presented. I recommend acceptance.
