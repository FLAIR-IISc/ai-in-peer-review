

The paper proposes an agglomerative approach for scalable hierarchical clustering of networks into communities. The core model—based on a prior over (possibly non-binary) trees—and algorithm structure are largely derived from prior work on Bayesian rose trees (Blundell et al. [12]), with modifications to the likelihood for network data and efficient caching of sufficient statistics. The presentation is clear, and experiments demonstrate advantages over a flat-community baseline. However, the absence of comparisons to established hierarchical network models significantly limits the evaluation.

**Significance & Related Work:** The paper is generally well-written and technically sound, though Section 3’s notation for Equation (9) and its extensions is dense and could be simplified for better accessibility. Crucially, the experiments fail to compare against hierarchical methods that explicitly model nested community structures. Two relevant Bayesian nonparametric approaches are omitted: Ho et al. (2012)’s multiscale community blockmodel (using a nested Chinese Restaurant Process) and Roy et al. (2009)’s Mondrian Process (which induces hierarchical structures via K-d trees). These models directly address hierarchical latent structure and could provide a more meaningful benchmark than flat-community baselines. The paper’s claim that modeling hierarchy benefits relational data is supported, but not its superiority over existing hierarchical methods.

**Originality & Clarity:** The primary technical contribution lies in adapting sufficient statistics for recursive updates within greedy agglomerative clustering under the new likelihood. While the O(Nodes + Edges) runtime is a clear strength, the description of this mechanism is inadequate. The allusion to dynamic programming before Equation (10) lacks concrete explanation, and the recursive update equations (e.g., Equations 11–12) are presented without sufficient justification for their correctness. A clearer derivation or intuitive explanation would strengthen the methodological contribution.

**Terminology:** The use of "nonparametric" is misleading. Blundell et al. [12] explicitly contrast rose trees with nonparametric methods in their discussion, framing the approach as model selection rather than nonparametric inference. The paper should align with this characterization to avoid confusion.

**Experiments:** The Sampson network analysis is appropriately qualitative, and the comparison with the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively illustrates the benefits of averaging over multiple hierarchical hypotheses. However, scalability claims lack substantial support: the networks tested (e.g., Karate, Dolphins) are modest in size, and no large-scale experiments are provided. The absence of comparisons to hierarchical models like Ho et al. (2012) and Roy et al. (2009) remains the most significant gap. Without these, the paper cannot establish whether its hierarchical structure yields superior performance or more refined latent representations than existing methods.

**Overall:** The work successfully integrates Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block models for efficient hierarchical community detection, demonstrating clear benefits over flat models. However, the lack of comparative evaluation against established hierarchical network models undermines the novelty claim. The technical details around sufficient statistics require greater clarity, and the "nonparametric" label should be revised to reflect Blundell et al.’s distinction. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the contribution.
