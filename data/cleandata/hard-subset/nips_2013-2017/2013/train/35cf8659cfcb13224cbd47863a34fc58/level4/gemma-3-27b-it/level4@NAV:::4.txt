This work introduces an agglomerative approach for scalable network clustering into hierarchical communities. The model builds directly upon existing Bayesian rose tree methodology [12], retaining the prior structure and algorithmic framework, while adapting the likelihood function for network data and maintaining efficient sufficient statistic caching. The paper is clearly presented, and experimental results demonstrate improved performance compared to a baseline flat community model; however, it lacks comparison to other hierarchical network models.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:**

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. While Sec. 3 is particularly dense, and a more accessible notation and explanation of equation (9) and its subsequent derivations might be beneficial, the core ideas are present. The experimental evaluation primarily demonstrates the advantages of incorporating hierarchical structure into relational data modeling, as comparisons are limited to non-hierarchical latent models. Notably, the work does not engage with relevant Bayesian nonparametric approaches for hierarchical community detection, such as the nested Chinese Restaurant Process utilized in Ho et al. (2012) or the Mondrian Process proposed by Roy et al. (2009), which may be capable of identifying more refined latent structures.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:** 

The proposed model effectively combines a Bayesian rose tree prior with a standard stochastic block model likelihood. The primary innovations lie in adapting the sufficient statistics update rules for the new likelihood within a greedy agglomerative clustering scheme. The resulting O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood computation is a positive feature. However, the description of this optimization lacks clarity – the alluded-to dynamic programming approach before equation (10) is difficult to follow, and the justification for the correctness of the recursive sufficient statistic updates is not fully explained.

**TERMINOLOGY:** 

The use of “nonparametric” terminology is questionable, aligning with the perspective in Blundell et al. [12] which frames the rose tree approach as model selection and contrasts it with truly nonparametric methods. 

**EXPERIMENTS:** 

The qualitative Sampson example is adequate, and the comparison to the IRM highlights the benefits of model averaging for prediction. The lack of comparison to alternative hierarchical network models remains a significant limitation. While scalability is discussed, the networks used in the experiments are not particularly large-scale.

In summary, this paper successfully integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic block model likelihoods, enabling efficient hierarchical community discovery. However, the absence of comparisons to existing hierarchical network models weakens the assessment of its relative merits.
