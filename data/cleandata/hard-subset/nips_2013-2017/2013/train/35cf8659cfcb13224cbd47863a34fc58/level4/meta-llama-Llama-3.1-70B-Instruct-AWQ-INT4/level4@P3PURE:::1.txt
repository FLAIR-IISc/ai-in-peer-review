The authors present a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) for network modeling, incorporating key ideas from the Bayesian rose tree paper. Specifically, the hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over consistent partitions of the nodes, and this hierarchy can be learned using an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors demonstrate qualitative results on the Sampson's monks dataset and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on the NIPS-234 dataset. Notably, the proposed inference method is computationally much cheaper than the IRM, while achieving similar predictive performance.

The quality and clarity of the paper are excellent, with a clear exposition of the key ideas. The main ideas are adapted from the Bayesian rose tree work, but the authors have made a significant effort to apply these ideas to the relational data setting, particularly in maintaining the same computational complexity.

The significance of this work lies in its contribution to scaling Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) models, especially network models, to larger datasets. This is an important challenge, as these methods need to be scalable for practical applications. While the n^2 scaling of the basic algorithm may be prohibitive for larger networks, the sparse version should presumably remain feasible. However, it would be beneficial to see some timings on larger real-world networks. For instance, the authors mention that the NIPS-234 dataset takes only tens of seconds to process, but they do not provide information on the processing time for the full NIPS dataset. Furthermore, it would be interesting to investigate how inference scales for larger networks, such as gene networks with tens of thousands of nodes or real social networks with even larger numbers of nodes.

Additionally, it would be useful to have a sense of how close to the optimal tree the greedy method gets. This could be investigated at the largest scale that still allows for the optimal tree to be found by exhaustive search. Providing theoretical support for the method, such as in terms of submodularity, would also be beneficial.
