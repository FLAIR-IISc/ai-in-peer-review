This paper proposes a scalable, agglomerative approach for clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The method adapts the Bayesian rose tree framework [12], retaining its prior over non-binary trees and its algorithmic structure. The primary contribution is the modification of the likelihood function to handle network data, along with an efficient method for caching sufficient statistics. While the presentation is clear and experiments demonstrate benefits over a flat baseline model, a significant limitation is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models.

**Significance and Originality**

The paper is generally well-written and appears to be technically sound. The model is effectively a Bayesian rose tree with a standard block-model likelihood. The main technical innovation lies in adapting the greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to this new likelihood, specifically in how sufficient statistics are recursively updated. The ability to compute the likelihood in O(Nodes + Edges) time is a valuable feature.

A major weakness of this work is its limited comparison to existing literature. The experiments effectively demonstrate that modeling hierarchy is beneficial for relational datasets when compared to a flat model (the Infinite Relational Model). However, they fail to establish the proposed method's superiority over other hierarchical approaches. The paper omits any mention or comparison to relevant Bayesian nonparametric models, such as the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., 2012), which uses a nested Chinese Restaurant Process, or the Mondrian Process (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a prior over hierarchical structures. These methods are also capable of discovering refined latent community structures and represent critical baselines for comparison.

**Clarity**

The clarity of the presentation could be improved in several key areas. The explanation in Section 3 is particularly dense, and the notation surrounding equation (9) might benefit from a simpler exposition. Furthermore, the description of the efficient likelihood computation is lacking in detail. The text alludes to dynamic programming before equation (10) but does not provide enough information for the reader to fully grasp the procedure. Similarly, the equations for the recursive updating of sufficient statistics are presented without sufficient justification or intuition for their correctness.

**Terminology**

The use of the term "nonparametric" is questionable. The original work on Bayesian rose trees [12] explicitly frames the approach as a form of model selection and contrasts it with nonparametric methods. This reviewer agrees with that interpretation, suggesting the terminology in this paper may be misleading.

**Experiments**

The experimental results are insightful but limited. The qualitative analysis of the Sampson monastery dataset is reasonable, and the quantitative comparison against the IRM effectively highlights the predictive advantages of averaging over multiple hierarchical structures. However, as noted previously, the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models is a significant omission. Additionally, while the paper discusses scalability, the networks used in the experiments are not particularly large, leaving the claims of scalability not fully substantiated.

**Summary**

This work capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic block model likelihood for networks and generalizes efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This allows for effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments. However, the paper's contributions are weakened by a lack of comparison to other relevant hierarchical network models.
