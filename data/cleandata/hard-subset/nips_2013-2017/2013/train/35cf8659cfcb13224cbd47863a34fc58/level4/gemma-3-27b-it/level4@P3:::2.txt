This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, building upon stochastic blockmodels and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors introduce a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and an efficient greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this hierarchical structure directly from data. They demonstrate that their Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method achieves comparable or better accuracy than the IRM, with significantly improved runtime – two orders of magnitude faster in some cases.

This work addresses an important problem in network analysis. Inferring hierarchical community structure is crucial for understanding complex systems, and Bayesian nonparametric approaches offer a principled framework for this task. The NIPS community will likely find this work relevant, as it combines Bayesian methods, graph modeling, and efficient algorithms – all active areas of research. The issue of computational scalability in Bayesian nonparametric models is particularly pertinent, and the authors’ proposed solution is a valuable contribution. The problem is well-motivated, and the connection to previous work (Stochastic Blockmodels, IRM, hierarchical clustering) is clearly established.

The key insight – leveraging a tree structure to constrain the space of possible partitions and enable efficient marginal likelihood computation – is a significant one. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck in existing methods (e.g., IRM) and offer a clever workaround. The experimental results on both Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data are promising, demonstrating both speed and accuracy gains over the IRM. The ablation study with different BHCD variations (sparse vs. non-sparse, binary vs. non-binary trees) provides useful insights into the algorithm's behavior.

However, the paper suffers from issues in clarity and presentation. While the core idea is sound, the explanation of the learning procedure (Section 4) is dense and difficult to follow. The connection between the greedy tree learning and the underlying Bayesian model is not fully articulated – it’s unclear how the greedy approach relates to posterior inference. The authors mention it is not true posterior inference, but a more detailed discussion of its approximations or limitations would be beneficial. Several minor grammatical errors and instances of awkward phrasing detract from the readability (e.g., line 47-50, Eq. 5, Figure 3 caption). The descriptions of the datasets are brief and assume familiarity. A more thorough description of the NIPS co-authorship dataset would be helpful. The mathematical notation, while generally correct, could be streamlined in places for better clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and the associated greedy learning algorithm represent a novel approach to hierarchical community discovery.
*   **Efficiency:** The algorithm’s scalability (O(n<sup>2</sup> log n)) is a significant improvement over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate competitive accuracy and substantial speedups compared to the IRM.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The grounding in Bayesian nonparametric principles and the efficient marginal likelihood computation provide a solid theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The explanation of the learning procedure (Section 4) is difficult to follow.
*   **Writing Quality:** The paper contains several grammatical errors and instances of awkward phrasing.
*   **Connection to Bayesian Inference:** The relationship between the greedy learning algorithm and the underlying Bayesian model is not fully clarified.
*   **Dataset Description:** Insufficient detail is provided about the datasets used in the experiments.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in efficiency and competitive accuracy.
*   The work addresses a key limitation of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods – scalability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of clarity in the presentation may hinder understanding and reproducibility.
*   The connection to full Bayesian inference is not adequately discussed.
*   The writing quality needs improvement to enhance readability.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The core idea is strong, and the results are promising. However, the authors need to address the issues of clarity and writing quality to fully realize this potential. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly improving the clarity of Section 4 and providing a more detailed discussion of the algorithm’s relationship to Bayesian inference. A careful proofread to address the grammatical errors is also essential.
