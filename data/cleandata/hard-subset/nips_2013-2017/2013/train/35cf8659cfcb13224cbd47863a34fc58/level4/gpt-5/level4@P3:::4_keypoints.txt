- Proposes BHCD: a greedy, agglomerative Bayesian method combining a tree-structured prior (rose trees) with a conjugate stochastic blockmodel likelihood for hierarchical community discovery.
- Exact marginal likelihood on a fixed tree is computed by a recursion using conjugacy and cached sufficient statistics; inference merges subtrees by local Bayes factors (join and two absorptions).
- Introduces a sparse variant that forbids merging subtrees with no interconnecting edges and supports efficient link prediction via tree traversal.
- Situates BHCD relative to stochastic blockmodels, IRM/MMSB, Bayesian hierarchical clustering, multiscale blockmodels, and Mondrian-based relational models, but evaluates mainly against IRM.
- Strength: principled, fully probabilistic hierarchical model integrating a tree prior with a blockmodel likelihood.
- Strength: elegant dynamic program for exact marginal likelihood computation and good use of sufficient statistics and conjugacy.
- Strength: scalable greedy inference with O(1) inner-loop updates via caching and overall O(n^2 log n) complexity; sparse variant is practical for large sparse graphs.
- Strength: empirical results show favorable speed–accuracy trade-offs (e.g., faster useful performance than IRM on NIPS-234) and sensible qualitative hierarchies.
- Writing is generally clear and compact in Sections 2–4.
- Weakness: key technical derivations (DP leading to Eq. 10 and sufficient-statistic updates Eq. 13) are terse and need worked examples or derivations for reproducibility.
- Weakness: complexity claims need empirical scaling plots and clarification of memory costs for cached statistics and the heap.
- Weakness: experimental comparisons omit other hierarchical network models (hierarchical IRM variants, multiscale blockmodel, Mondrian relational models); either add comparisons or justify their omission.
- Clarity issues: Section 3 is notation-dense; add a small running example, a figure mapping merges to operations, and DP pseudocode to aid understanding.
- Clarity issue: define rS intuitively (posterior responsibility) and illustrate predictive path traversal with a short example; fix minor wording/grammar errors.
- Originality: incremental but useful—extends Bayesian rose trees to relational data and adapts merge operations and caching for blockmodels.
- Terminology: clarify “nonparametric” claim—method is effectively greedy model selection over finite trees despite allowing nested communities in principle.
- Significance: addresses a practical need for fast probabilistic hierarchical community discovery, but current experiments don’t decisively establish superiority over other hierarchical Bayesian models.
- Suggestion: include sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters, tie-breaking/restarts, and the benefit of absorptions vs binary-only merges to guide practitioners.
- Pros: principled model, fast inference, practical sparse variant, competitive link prediction at much lower runtime, clear qualitative structures.
- Cons: limited empirical baselines against other hierarchical models, dense presentation of key derivations, potentially misleading “nonparametric” terminology, and datasets modest relative to modern large-scale networks.
- Overall recommendation: solid, timely, and practical contribution; recommend acceptance conditional on expanded exposition of the DP and cached updates and stronger empirical context or justification for omitted baselines.
