This work presents an agglomerative method for scalable hierarchical clustering of networks. The approach heavily relies on the Bayesian rose tree framework [12], adopting its prior over tree structures and algorithmic design with minimal changes. The primary contribution lies in adapting the likelihood function for network data in a straightforward manner, which preserves the ability to efficiently cache sufficient statistics. While the paper is presented with notable clarity and demonstrates superior performance against a flat community baseline, it critically omits comparisons to alternative hierarchical network models.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well-written and appears technically sound. A point of concern is the density of the presentation in Section 3; the notation and explanation surrounding Equation (9) and related components could be simplified for greater clarity. The experimental evaluation is limited by its comparison to only non-hierarchical models. Consequently, the results demonstrate the benefit of modeling hierarchy but fail to position the proposed method relative to other existing hierarchical techniques. The authors overlook key related work in Bayesian nonparametrics, such as the nested CRP-based "multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration" (Ho et al., 2012) and the "Mondrian Process" (Roy et al., 2009), both of which are designed to discover refined hierarchical latent structures.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
The proposed model is fundamentally an application of the Bayesian rose tree framework using a standard stochastic block-model likelihood. Therefore, the main technical innovation is the adaptation of the recursive update for sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm to accommodate this new likelihood. A significant strength is the O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity for likelihood computation. However, the description of this computation is lacking in detail. The text alludes to a dynamic programming solution preceding Equation (10), but the specifics are difficult to grasp. Similarly, while equations for the recursive updating of sufficient statistics are provided, the paper offers little intuition or justification for their correctness, making them hard to verify.

TERMINOLOGY:
The use of the term "nonparametric" to describe this model is debatable. The original work on Bayesian rose trees by Blundell et al. [12] presents the method as a form of model selection and explicitly contrasts it with nonparametric approaches. I find this original interpretation to be more accurate.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative analysis on the Sampson monastery dataset is adequate, and the comparison against the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) effectively illustrates the predictive advantages of averaging over multiple structural hypotheses. Nevertheless, the primary experimental weakness remains the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. Furthermore, while scalability is discussed as a benefit, the datasets used in the experiments are not large enough to substantiate these claims convincingly.

This paper successfully combines the Bayesian rose tree framework with a stochastic block-model likelihood, effectively extending an efficient agglomerative clustering algorithm to the network domain. While the experimental results demonstrate competent hierarchical community detection, the work is significantly limited by the omission of comparisons to alternative hierarchical network modeling techniques.
