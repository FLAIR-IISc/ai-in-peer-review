Presents a scalable agglomerative method for clustering networks into hierarchical communities. The prior over (potentially non-binary) trees and the overall algorithmic framework closely follow Bayesian rose trees [12]. The likelihood is adapted in a straightforward way to relational data, and with some additional bookkeeping the sufficient statistics can still be cached efficiently. The writing is largely clear and the experiments compare well to a flat community baseline; however, there are no comparisons to existing hierarchical network models.

SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:
The paper is generally well written and appears technically sound. Section 3 is quite dense; the notation and explanation around Eq. (9) and the downstream components that rely on it could be simplified or clarified. Because the empirical comparisons focus on methods without hierarchical latent structure, the results primarily demonstrate that modeling hierarchy helps on relational data, rather than whether this approach outperforms other hierarchical models. In particular, at least two Bayesian nonparametric approaches explicitly model hierarchical community structure: the Multiscale Community Blockmodel for network exploration (Ho et al., 2012) based on a nested CRP, and the Mondrian Process (Roy et al., 2009), which defines a stochastic process over kd-tree–like hierarchies. These methods are not discussed and could discover more granular latent structure.

ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:
Conceptually, the model is a Bayesian rose tree coupled with a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood. Thus, the primary contributions concern how the new likelihood affects the recursive updates and caching of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative procedure. The claim that the marginal likelihood can be evaluated in O(Nodes + Edges) is attractive, but the exposition is brief—the reference to dynamic programming before Eq. (10) is hard to parse without more detail. Likewise, while the recursive sufficient-statistic updates are stated, additional intuition or justification would improve readability and confidence in their correctness.

TERMINOLOGY:
The use of “nonparametric” may be debatable here. Blundell et al. [12] frame rose trees as model selection and contrast them with nonparametric methods; I share that interpretation.

EXPERIMENTS:
The qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery data is appropriate, and the comparison to the IRM nicely illustrates the benefits of averaging over many hypotheses for prediction. The main limitation is the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. While the paper discusses scalability, the datasets used are not especially large.

Effectively combines prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods for networks and extends efficient greedy agglomerative clustering to this setting, yielding practical hierarchical community discovery; nonetheless, the lack of comparisons to other hierarchical network models is a notable gap.
