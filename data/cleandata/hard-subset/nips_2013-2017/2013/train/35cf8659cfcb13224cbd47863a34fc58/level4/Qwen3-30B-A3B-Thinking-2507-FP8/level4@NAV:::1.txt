

The authors present a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling, leveraging key concepts from the Bayesian rose tree framework: 1) utilizing the hierarchy to define a mixture over consistent node partitions, and 2) learning this hierarchy via an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure. Qualitative results are shown on Sampson's monks and full NIPS datasets, with quantitative results on NIPS-234. The proposed inference is computationally more efficient than IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.  
Quality/Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear exposition of core concepts.  
Originality: While the foundational ideas derive from the Bayesian rose tree work, adapting them to relational data—particularly preserving computational complexity—required significant effort.  
Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models, especially for networks, is crucial for practitioner adoption. Although the base algorithm's n² scaling may limit larger networks, the sparse variant should remain feasible. Timings for larger real-world networks would be valuable: NIPS-234 runs in tens of seconds—how long does the full NIPS dataset take? Gene networks typically involve tens of thousands of nodes, and real social networks are substantially larger; how does inference scale? Additionally, it would be beneficial to assess how close the greedy method approximates the optimal tree (e.g., via exhaustive search on the largest tractable scale) and whether theoretical guarantees (e.g., via submodularity) can be established.
