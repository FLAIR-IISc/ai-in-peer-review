The authors have presented a hierarchical extension of the IRM for network modeling, using some key concepts from the Bayesian Rose Tree research paper: 1) the hierarchy is used to specify a mixture over a continuous partition of nodes. 2) this hierarchy can be learned through an efficient greedy collective approach. Qualitative results have been presented on the Sampson's Monks dataset, the complete NIPS dataset, and the NIPS-234 dataset. The proposed approach is much less expensive than the IRM, achieving the same predictive performance.

Quality/Clarity: The paper is very well written and the explanation of the key concepts is clear.

Originality: The main concepts are taken from the work of the Bayesian Rose Tree, but some work has been done to adapt these concepts to relational data sets, especially to maintain the same computational complexity.

Significance: Scaling BNP models, especially network models, is a significant challenge if these methods are used by users. Although scaling the n2 of the basic algorithm may be prohibited for larger networks, the sparse version will likely be effective. It would be good to see some time scaling in larger real-world networks. It takes a few seconds for NIPS-234: how long does it take for the complete NIPS? Gene networks usually have thousands of nodes and real social networks are much larger: how will the scale be estimated?

Also, it is good to know how close the greedy approach is to an optimal tree. It can be tested on the largest scale to see if you can still find an optimal tree by a complete search. Is it possible to provide any theoretical support for the method? For example, in the case of Subramodularity?
