The authors propose a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, framing it as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. They introduce a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm designed for efficient inference, achieving significant speedups compared to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable or better accuracy.

This paper addresses an important problem with relevance to a broad NIPS audience. Bayesian nonparametric methods offer a principled approach to community discovery, but often suffer from computational limitations. This work directly tackles that issue by proposing an efficient algorithm for hierarchical community detection, a task with growing importance in social network analysis and related fields. The method builds upon established Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, offering a novel extension to relational data.

The core idea – leveraging a tree structure to constrain the partition space and enable efficient marginal likelihood calculation – is insightful. The authors effectively highlight the computational advantages over methods like IRM, which must grapple with the exponential number of possible partitions. However, the connection between the greedy learning procedure and the underlying Bayesian generative story could be clarified. While the algorithm is presented as a model selection approach, its relationship to posterior inference (or a MAP/ML estimate) is not fully articulated. Greater clarity in Section 4 would be beneficial.

The experimental results are compelling. The proposed method demonstrates competitive performance, achieving comparable or slightly better predictive accuracy than IRM, alongside substantial runtime improvements – reported as two orders of magnitude faster. While the absolute gains in predictive performance may be modest, the speed advantage is significant. The inclusion of results on both the Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset strengthens the evaluation.

The clarity and presentation could be enhanced. Several minor grammatical and typographical errors are present (e.g., line 126). The prose occasionally lacks precision, hindering comprehension. For instance, the discussion on lines 47-50 regarding “local changes” and “iterations” would benefit from more specific detail. Providing more context for the datasets (the monastery network and the NIPS authorship network) would also improve accessibility for readers unfamiliar with these benchmarks. Certain mathematical formulations, such as Equation 5, might be simplified or better motivated. Figure 3 is visually dense and, without prior knowledge of the monastery dataset, it is difficult to assess the relative performance of the different methods. Section 4, detailing the algorithm, requires careful reading for full understanding.

Despite these presentation issues, the proposed approach exhibits sufficient novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication, particularly with revisions addressing the clarity concerns.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel Bayesian nonparametric approach to hierarchical community discovery.
*   Significant computational speedup compared to existing methods (IRM).
*   Competitive predictive performance on benchmark datasets.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity of the connection between the greedy learning algorithm and Bayesian inference is unclear.
*   Writing quality and presentation could be improved (minor errors, occasionally opaque prose).
*   Some mathematical formulations and figures could be simplified.
