The authors present a generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to a hierarchical setting, enabling inference of a hierarchy rather than a flat clustering from observed links between entities. They introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to select a tree that encodes valid partitions of the entities. They then report empirical results on two corpora of social connectivity data and conclude that their method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a widely used baseline for these tasks.

This paper tackles an interesting problem that should appeal to a substantial portion of the NIPS audience. Conceptually attractive Bayesian nonparametric methods are often slow in practice, and on the application side, social scientists are increasingly adopting sophisticated Bayesian models for data analysis. A hierarchical extension to a popular network-discovery model is therefore timely and relevant. The paper speaks to both concerns.

The key insight is that by restricting admissible partitions to those consistent with a tree, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. In contrast, the IRM must effectively consider an exponential number of possible partitions. However, I am unclear about how the tree-learning procedure connects to the generative story of the Bayesian model. Because the algorithm is greedy, it does not appear to perform posterior inference over a distribution of trees, nor does it clearly yield a point MAP or ML estimate—though I may be misunderstanding. I recommend clarifying Section 4.

The empirical results are strong. Across several metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), the proposed method performs comparably to or slightly better than the IRM, its closest competitor. While the gains in these scores are modest in absolute terms, the reported runtime improvements are orders of magnitude over IRM inference.

The clarity and writing could be improved. There are several distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126). Some prose is difficult to follow; lines 47–50 in the introduction are vague (what are “local changes” in this context? what “iterations” are being referenced—iterations of an MCMC algorithm?). The datasets (Monastery networks and NIPS authorship) are not clearly described, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematics feels opaque or unnecessary (e.g., Eq. 5). I also found Figure 3 confusing; given unfamiliarity with the Monastery dataset, it is visually unclear which method performs best. Including a dataset more familiar to readers could help. Section 4, which contains the core contribution, required multiple readings to understand.

If these issues are addressed, the paper’s novelty and empirical strength would warrant publication. The authors propose a novel model of graph edge structure that can be viewed as a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering inspired by the IRM. The core learning algorithm is insightful and the experiments are strong, but the writing needs improvement.
