Summary
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model views a hierarchy (a “rose tree”) as a prior over a restricted set of partitions; given a fixed tree, the marginal likelihood can be computed exactly via dynamic programming using conjugate beta–Bernoulli blockmodels with different hyperparameters for within- and between-cluster edges. Learning proceeds via a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that merges subtrees using one of three local operations (join or absorb), choosing the merge that maximizes a local Bayes factor computed from cached sufficient statistics. The authors also propose a “sparse” variant that forbids merges between disconnected subgraphs. Link prediction is done by recursively mixing on- and off-diagonal posteriors along a path in the tree.

Empirically, BHCD is demonstrated on Sampson’s monastery network, a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset, and the full NIPS co-authorship graph. The method attains predictive performance comparable to or better than IRM while being substantially faster, with worst-case time O(n^2 log n) independent of the number of discovered communities.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on stochastic blockmodels and the IRM, and relates closely to Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees, extending these ideas from i.i.d. vectors to relational data via a blockmodel likelihood. It also connects to hierarchical blockmodels and Mondrian process priors for networks, but differs by restricting inference to a greedy, single-pass agglomeration with exact tree-marginal likelihoods and local merge decisions.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear and practical: exact marginal likelihood given a tree, combined with a simple and fast greedy search with locally computed Bayes factors.
- Scalability: O(n^2 log n) with O(1) inner-loop updates from cached sufficient statistics; a sparse variant further accelerates inference on sparse graphs.
- Interpretability: discovers multi-resolution community structure; the dynamic programming formulation and predictive recursion are well structured.
- Empirical performance: two orders of magnitude faster than IRM in the reported settings while matching or improving AUC/predictive log-likelihood on NIPS-234; sensible hierarchies on Sampson and the full NIPS graph.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Inference vs. generative story: The algorithm performs greedy model selection rather than posterior inference over trees. Please clarify the objective (e.g., greedy maximization of the forest/tree marginal likelihood) and state explicitly that this is not MAP over a well-defined tree prior nor approximate posterior sampling. Discuss implications for uncertainty quantification.
- Claims of speed: At several points the text suggests “two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM” and at another “faster than a single iteration of IRM.” Please reconcile and report wall-clock comparisons under matched settings (same hardware, languages, convergence criteria), and clarify whether IRM results are from a strong baseline implementation with optimized sparse updates.
- Baselines and scope: Comparisons focus largely on IRM. Given the stated relationship to hierarchical IRM variants, Mondrian process models, and recent latent feature/attribute models (LFRM, ILA), it would strengthen the case to include direct head-to-head runtime and predictive comparisons where feasible, or at least ablation-studies and more detailed discussion of differences in complexity and modeling capacity.
- Hyperparameters and robustness: The model fixes α, β, δ, λ, γ. Please justify these choices, report sensitivity analyses, and/or include simple empirical Bayes or weakly informative defaults. Non-conjugate variants and hyperparameter inference are mentioned as future work; a brief experiment would be helpful.
- Random restarts and tie-breaking: The method relies on R restarts and random tie-breaking when many merges have identical scores early on. Please quantify the variability across runs, report the distribution of tree likelihoods, and clarify the computational overhead of R restarts in runtime plots.
- BHCD vs. BHCD-sparse: The sparse constraint (forbidding merges of disconnected components) appears to improve both speed and results. Consider making this the default, and provide guidance on when the unconstrained version is advantageous.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper would benefit from proofreading and tightening. There are multiple grammatical errors and inconsistent capitalization (e.g., in the abstract: “Our algorithms…”; subject-verb agreement; typos). Some equations (e.g., Eq. 5) are hard to parse without an accompanying example; Figure 3 is difficult to interpret for readers unfamiliar with the Sampson data.

Quality
The modeling assumptions are standard for blockmodels, and the dynamic programming decomposition over tree-consistent partitions is sound. The agglomerative search is reasonable and well engineered. However, as a greedy procedure, it does not provide posterior uncertainty or consistency guarantees, and the empirical evaluation could be broadened. Overall, technically solid and practically compelling.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable but needs revisions for grammar, organization, and exposition. Section 4 (the main contribution) should be made more self-contained, with clearer pseudo-code, examples of the three merge types, and a small running example that tracks sufficient statistics and scores through a few merges. Define all notation upon first use and add brief dataset descriptions for readers outside social network analysis.

Originality
The key idea—using a tree to restrict partitions and enable exact marginalization—extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering/Rose Trees to blockmodels. While conceptually incremental, the adaptation to relational data with an efficient agglomerative search and predictive recursion is a useful contribution.

Significance
Given the need for scalable hierarchical community discovery and link prediction, this work is likely to be used by practitioners. The speed–accuracy trade-off is attractive, and the approach is simple to implement.

Pros
- Fast, scalable, and simple algorithm with exact tree-marginal likelihoods
- Competitive predictive performance on real networks
- Clear connection to established Bayesian clustering ideas
- Interpretable hierarchical outputs

Cons
- Greedy model selection without uncertainty quantification
- Limited baselines and some ambiguity in runtime claims
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters and random restarts not fully explored
- Presentation and figures need polishing; some notation heavy sections

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the learning objective; position the algorithm as greedy model selection, not posterior inference, and discuss uncertainty (e.g., averaging over restarts).
- Provide more details on datasets and evaluation protocol (train/validation/test splits, treatment of missing vs. negative edges, undirected/symmetric assumptions).
- Reconcile and standardize runtime claims; include wall-clock vs. quality curves with error bars across restarts.
- Add ablations (with/without sparse constraint; binary vs. non-binary trees; effect of R).
- Include a small synthetic experiment where the ground-truth hierarchy is known to illustrate correctness and limitations.
- Proofread thoroughly to fix grammar and consistency; improve figure captions and legends; add an illustrative example for Eq. 5 and the predictive recursion.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a practical and scalable approach with solid empirical results and a clear connection to prior work. With improvements to clarity, expanded baselines, and clarified claims, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
