The authors propose a hierarchical generalization of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model. The goal is to infer a hierarchy of entities, rather than a flat clustering, from observed relational data. Their approach uses an agglomerative clustering algorithm to construct a tree that defines a restricted set of valid partitions. Empirical results on two social network corpora suggest the method is both faster and more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a popular baseline for this task.

This paper addresses an important problem of interest to the NIPS community. Bayesian nonparametric methods are conceptually elegant but often suffer from slow inference. At the same time, there is growing interest in the social sciences for sophisticated Bayesian models to analyze network data. This work contributes to both areas by proposing a hierarchical extension to a widely-used network model that is also computationally efficient.

The key technical insight is that by restricting the space of possible partitions to those consistent with a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently. This contrasts with the standard IRM, which must consider an exponentially large space of partitions.

However, the connection between the tree learning procedure and the generative model is unclear. The paper describes a greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning the tree. As this procedure is greedy, it does not appear to be performing true posterior inference over a distribution of trees, nor is it clear if it corresponds to finding a MAP or ML point estimate. Clarifying the theoretical justification for this learning algorithm in Section 4 would significantly strengthen the paper.

The empirical results are impressive. On several metrics, including AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, the proposed method performs comparably to or slightly better than the IRM. While the improvements in these scores are modest in absolute terms, the reported speedup is significant, with the new method being orders of magnitude faster than standard IRM inference.

The primary weakness of the paper is its presentation. The overall clarity and writing quality need improvement.
First, there are several distracting spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126). Second, the prose can be difficult to follow; for instance, the introduction's reference to "local changes" and "iterations" (lines 47-50) is ambiguous without more context (e.g., are these MCMC iterations?). Furthermore, the datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS co-authorship) are not described in sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical notation, such as Equation 5, seems unnecessarily complex. The visualization in Figure 3 is also confusing; without prior knowledge of the monastery dataset, it is difficult to visually assess which method performs best. Finally, and most importantly, Section 4, which contains the paper's core contribution, is particularly dense and required multiple readings to understand.

In summary, the authors introduce a novel model for hierarchical community detection that can be viewed as a Bayesian extension of agglomerative clustering. The core learning algorithm is insightful and the experimental results, particularly the computational speedup, are compelling. However, the paper is currently hindered by significant issues in its presentation. If the authors can address these issues—especially by clarifying the learning algorithm in Section 4 and improving the overall quality of the writing—the paper's novelty and strong results would warrant publication.
