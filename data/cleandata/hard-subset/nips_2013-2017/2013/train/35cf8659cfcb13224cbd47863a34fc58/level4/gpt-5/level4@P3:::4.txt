Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy, agglomerative method for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model uses a tree-structured prior over partitions, closely related to Bayesian rose trees, and a stochastic blockmodel likelihood with conjugate priors to distinguish within- versus between-community edges. Given a fixed tree, the marginal likelihood can be computed exactly by dynamic programming using a recursion over the tree nodes; the prior over partitions is induced by a stopping probability πS at each internal node. Inference proceeds bottom-up by merging subtrees using a local Bayes factor that considers three operations (join and two absorptions), while caching sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) complexity. A “sparse” variant forbids merging subtrees with no interconnecting edges. The method supports efficient link prediction by traversing the inferred tree.

BHCD relates to classic approaches for community detection, including stochastic blockmodels and MMSB for flat communities, and Bayesian nonparametric models such as the IRM and its hierarchical extensions. It also builds directly on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees, adapting those ideas to relational data. The paper cites relevant hierarchical network models such as the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., AISTATS 2011/2012) and the Mondrian process (Roy & Teh, NIPS 2009), as well as hierarchical IRM variants; however, empirical comparisons are focused on IRM.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a principled, fully probabilistic hierarchical model that integrates a tree prior with a blockmodel likelihood.
- Elegant recursion for exact marginal likelihood computation on a fixed tree; good use of conjugacy and sufficient statistics.
- Scalable greedy inference with careful caching; the sparse variant is practical for large, sparse graphs.
- Experiments demonstrate strong speed-accuracy trade-offs: on NIPS-234, BHCD reaches useful predictive performance much faster than IRM; qualitative results on Sampson are sensible; a large-scale hierarchy on full NIPS is showcased.
- The paper is generally well written; Section 2–4 describe the model and algorithm compactly.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- The core ideas appear sound. However, key technical pieces—especially the dynamic program leading to Eq. (10) and the correctness of the sufficient-statistic updates in Eq. (13)—are described tersely. A brief derivation or worked example would improve confidence and reproducibility. For instance, explicitly show how combining the prior over partitions (Eq. 7) and the likelihood (Eq. 8) yields the recursion in Eq. (10), and why σ¬ch updates satisfy the necessary inclusion–exclusion.
- Complexity claims would benefit from empirical scaling plots versus n and m, and from clarifying memory costs of the cached statistics and the heap.
- The experimental evaluation omits head-to-head comparisons with other hierarchical network models (e.g., hierarchical IRM extensions, multiscale community blockmodel, Mondrian-based relational models). Even a restricted comparison on small graphs, or a discussion explaining why those methods are impractical at this scale, would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- Section 3 is notation-dense. Consider: (a) adding a small running example (e.g., a 5-node toy tree) to illustrate σSS and σ¬chSS and the flow of Eq. (10); (b) a figure mapping a specific merge to the three possible operations and the corresponding updated statistics; (c) pseudocode for the dynamic program on a fixed tree (complementing Algorithm 1).
- Define rS (Eq. 14) intuitively in the text (as a posterior “responsibility” for the cluster at S) and explain the predictive path traversal with a 2–3 step example.
- Minor wording issues (e.g., plural agreement in the abstract) can be cleaned; a careful proofread will help.

Originality
- Conceptually, BHCD is a direct adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to blockmodel likelihoods. The primary novelty lies in extending the rose-tree framework to relational data, devising merge operations compatible with blockmodels, and engineering efficient sufficient-statistic caching. This is an incremental but valuable contribution that bridges two well-established lines of work.
- Please clarify the “nonparametric” terminology. While the model allows an unbounded number of nested communities in principle, the approach is effectively greedy model selection over finite trees. Framing this as scalable Bayesian model selection over hierarchical partitions may avoid confusion.

Significance
- The method addresses a practical need: fast, probabilistic hierarchical community discovery with useful predictive performance. The empirical speedups over IRM are compelling.
- That said, the current experiments do not decisively establish superiority over other hierarchical Bayesian models; the full NIPS hierarchy is informative but primarily qualitative. Additional baselines and larger-scale predictive evaluations would increase impact.
- Sensitivity analyses (e.g., hyperparameters α,β,δ,λ,γ; effect of tie-breaking and number of restarts R; benefit of absorptions vs binary-only merges) would provide guidance to practitioners.

Pros
- Principled, probabilistic hierarchical model with exact marginal likelihood on a fixed tree.
- Very fast greedy inference with effective caching; sparse variant is practical.
- Competitive link prediction at a fraction of IRM’s runtime.
- Clear qualitative structure on benchmark networks.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparison to other hierarchical network models.
- Dense presentation of key derivations; more exposition needed for DP and updates.
- Terminology (“nonparametric”) may be misleading without clarification.
- Evaluation datasets are modest in size relative to modern large-scale networks; scalability is argued more than demonstrated empirically.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed integration of rose-tree priors with blockmodel likelihoods that yields a scalable, useful algorithm for hierarchical community discovery. While the conceptual novelty is moderate, the contribution is timely and practical. I recommend acceptance if the authors can (i) expand the exposition around the dynamic program and cached updates, and (ii) provide stronger empirical context—either by adding comparisons to at least one hierarchical network model or by carefully justifying their absence and broadening the analysis of BHCD variants and sensitivities.
