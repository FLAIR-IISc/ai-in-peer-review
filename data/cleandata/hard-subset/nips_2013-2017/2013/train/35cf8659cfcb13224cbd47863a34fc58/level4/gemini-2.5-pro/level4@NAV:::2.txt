This paper introduces a hierarchical extension to the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model, designed to infer a hierarchical structure rather than a flat partition of entities from their observed links. The authors present an agglomerative clustering-based learning algorithm that identifies a tree structure to define the space of valid partitions. Through empirical evaluation on two social connectivity datasets, they conclude that their proposed method surpasses the widely-used Infinite Relational Model (IRM) in both computational speed and accuracy.

I believe this work addresses a topic of significant interest to the NeurIPS community. From a technical perspective, it confronts the common challenge of computational inefficiency in Bayesian nonparametric methods, which are otherwise conceptually powerful. From an application standpoint, the growing adoption of sophisticated Bayesian models by social scientists means that a hierarchical extension to a well-known network discovery model is a timely and valuable contribution. This paper successfully operates at the intersection of these two domains.

The central technical insight is that by constraining the possible partitions to a tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the data can be computed efficiently, avoiding the need to consider the exponential number of partitions faced by models like the IRM. However, the connection between the proposed tree-learning procedure and the generative process of the Bayesian model was not entirely clear. Given its greedy nature, it is uncertain whether this algorithm performs formal posterior inference over the space of trees or seeks a maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood (ML) point estimate. I would recommend the authors revise Section 4 to make this relationship more explicit.

The empirical results presented are compelling. Across multiple metrics, including AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, the proposed approach achieves performance that is comparable to or marginally exceeds that of the IRM. While the absolute gains in these scores are modest, the reported runtime is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference, which represents a significant practical advancement.

The manuscript would benefit significantly from improvements to its overall clarity and writing quality. First, there are several distracting typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126) that require correction. Second, the prose can be difficult to follow at times; for instance, the phrasing in lines 47-50 of the introduction is ambiguous regarding the meaning of "local changes" and "iterations." The datasets (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) are not sufficiently described, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with them. Some mathematical formulations, such as Equation 5, appear unnecessarily obtuse. Furthermore, Figure 3 is confusing; for a reader unfamiliar with the monastery dataset, it is difficult to visually assess which method is performing best. Finally, Section 4, which contains the paper's core contribution, required several readings to be fully understood.

In summary, the authors introduce a novel model for graph structure that can be viewed as a Bayesian analogue to agglomerative clustering. The core learning algorithm is insightful and the experimental results are strong. However, the paper's potential is currently hindered by significant issues in its presentation. If these clarity and writing issues can be thoroughly addressed, I believe the proposed approach has sufficient novelty and the results are strong enough to warrant publication.
