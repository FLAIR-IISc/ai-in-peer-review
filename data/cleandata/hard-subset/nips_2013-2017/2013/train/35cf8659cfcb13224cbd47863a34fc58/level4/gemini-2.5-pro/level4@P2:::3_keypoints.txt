- BHCD combines stochastic blockmodels with Bayesian Rose Trees to model hierarchical community structure and compute exact marginal likelihoods.
- Uses a greedy agglomerative algorithm that merges subtree pairs via a local Bayes factor starting from singleton communities.
- Introduces a caching scheme for sufficient statistics to achieve O(n² log n) runtime.
- Evaluated on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship data, with primary quantitative comparison against the IRM.
- Theoretical derivations (recursive marginal likelihood and merge score) are clear, correct, and well-motivated.
- Likelihood factorization over the tree and the caching implementation are strong practical contributions.
- Experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to substantiate claims of practical superiority.
- Limited baselines: mainly compared to the older IRM; comparisons to other modern/non-probabilistic methods are lacking and BHCD is not clearly superior to LFRM/ILA except in speed.
- Scale of evaluation is weak: main quantitative results use a small NIPS subset (234 authors); full NIPS analysis is qualitative only.
- No ground-truth (e.g., synthetic or real partitions with NMI) evaluations to directly assess clustering quality; relies on link-prediction AUC.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized; notation abstractions (f(·), g(·)) simplify presentation.
- Experimental procedure details for link-prediction (how test sets were constructed) are insufficiently described; minor stylistic citation issues.
- The method’s novelty lies in combining existing techniques (Rose Trees + SBMs) and framing agglomerative search as Bayesian model selection.
- Contribution is more incremental/implementation-focused rather than a fundamental theoretical breakthrough.
- BHCD addresses scalable inference for network models and achieves ~100× speedup over IRM while keeping comparable predictive performance.
- Significance is limited by the lack of larger-scale empirical validation and broader baseline comparisons.
- Reasons to accept: elegant model synthesis, fast greedy algorithm with formal scoring, substantial speed improvements, and very clear writing.
- Reasons to reject: limited experimental breadth and scale, absence of ground-truth clustering comparisons, no demonstrated predictive superiority, and incomplete experimental details.
