

The authors propose Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a scalable method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. Building on Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT), BHCD models communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where each partition corresponds to a consistent tree cut. The key innovation is a greedy agglomerative inference procedure that learns the hierarchy in O(n² log n) time—two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM)—while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy. The paper demonstrates the method on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network (NIPS-234), and the full NIPS dataset (2864 nodes), showing clear hierarchical structure and efficient scalability.

**Quality & Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The connection between hierarchical partitions and tree structures is elegantly explained, and the inference algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly motivated and justified. The exposition of the marginal likelihood recursion (Equation 10) and the efficient sufficient statistic caching mechanism are particularly strong. The experiments are well-designed, with thorough comparisons to IRM and relevant baselines. Minor grammatical fixes are needed (e.g., "treeS" → "trees" on page 1, line 48; "A fruitful next steps" → "A fruitful next step").

**Originality**  
The core ideas adapt the BRT framework to relational data, requiring non-trivial modifications to maintain computational efficiency. While the greedy agglomerative approach draws from BRT, the adaptation to network data—particularly the careful handling of edge statistics and the tree-consistent partition prior—is novel. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this adaptation avoids the O(K²) scaling pitfalls of previous hierarchical Bayesian methods (e.g., [8, 9, 10]), where K is the number of communities. The method is distinct from prior work on hierarchical clustering (e.g., [3, 4]), which lacks a full probabilistic framework.

**Significance**  
The work addresses a critical gap: scaling Bayesian nonparametric network models to larger datasets. BHCD’s O(n² log n) complexity (with sparse variants O(n + m)) enables practical application to networks where IRM becomes intractable. However, the significance claim requires stronger empirical support. The paper reports NIPS-234 (234 nodes) inference in ~10 seconds, but the full NIPS dataset (2864 nodes) is cited as having "646 internal nodes." The timing for this larger network is unreported—this is a significant omission. For real-world networks (e.g., gene networks with 10k+ nodes or social networks with millions), the O(n²) scaling could remain prohibitive without further optimization. The authors should clarify whether the sparse variant (BHCD sparse) achieves linear scaling in practice for large sparse networks.  

Additionally, the greedy approach’s suboptimality is not assessed. While the paper mentions tie-breaking via restarts (R=50), it does not quantify how close the greedy solution is to the optimal tree. For instance, on a small dataset (e.g., NIPS-234), exhaustive search could determine the optimality gap. Theoretical guarantees (e.g., submodularity of the score function) would further strengthen the contribution.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a valuable contribution to scalable Bayesian network modeling. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically effective. The minor weaknesses—lack of scalability analysis on larger networks and no optimality guarantees—do not undermine the core technical advance. The authors have addressed the main challenge of scaling hierarchical Bayesian models while preserving probabilistic rigor.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pro*:  
- First method to combine greedy agglomerative inference with a full Bayesian hierarchical model for networks.  
- Clear computational advantage (2x faster than IRM per iteration) with comparable accuracy.  
- Well-executed experiments on multiple real-world datasets.  

*Con*:  
- Limited scalability analysis for networks beyond NIPS-234 (2864 nodes).  
- No theoretical justification for greedy optimality or empirical gap analysis.  

The paper’s contribution is significant for practitioners seeking scalable hierarchical community detection. While the scalability claims would be stronger with larger-network timings, the current results are compelling for networks of moderate size (up to ~10k nodes). The adaptation of BRT to relational data is non-trivial and well-executed. The minor gaps are easily addressable in a revision. Given the high quality of writing, technical soundness, and clear significance, this paper merits acceptance.
