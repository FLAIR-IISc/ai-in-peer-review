

Review:  
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a scalable method for learning hierarchical community structure in social networks via greedy agglomerative model selection. The approach extends Bayesian rose trees [12] to relational data by adapting the likelihood to a stochastic blockmodel framework, enabling efficient inference through dynamic programming and cached sufficient statistics. The model represents communities as a tree-structured hierarchy, with each internal node corresponding to a community and leaves to vertices. BHCD achieves O(n² log n) runtime complexity, two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while maintaining comparable accuracy.  

**Quality**: The technical contributions are sound. The adaptation of the rose tree prior to network data via the hierarchical stochastic blockmodel is well-motivated, and the dynamic programming approach for marginal likelihood computation (Eq. 10) is correctly derived. However, the paper omits critical comparisons to existing hierarchical Bayesian models (e.g., the Mondrian Process [12] and Ho et al.’s multiscale community blockmodel [11]), which directly address hierarchical community discovery. The experimental section focuses solely on flat-model baselines (IRM), failing to demonstrate superiority over hierarchical alternatives. The scalability claims are supported by runtime comparisons (Table 1), but the largest dataset tested (NIPS-234, 234 vertices) is not sufficiently large to validate "suitability for large dense networks" as claimed in the discussion.  

**Clarity**: The exposition is generally clear, though Section 3 (especially Eqs. 9–10) is dense. The recursive sufficient statistics update (Eq. 13) is well-defined but could benefit from a concrete example. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and correctly implements the dynamic programming scheme. The qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery (Figure 3) and predictive performance (Figure 4) are well-presented, but the log-likelihood values for different BHCD variants (e.g., −22.80 for BHCD sparse) lack context for interpretation.  

**Originality**: The core innovation lies in efficiently integrating hierarchical tree priors with stochastic blockmodels for network data, particularly through the caching mechanism enabling O(1) inner-loop operations. While the rose tree framework [12] is reused, the adaptation to relational data with scalable inference is novel. However, the paper understates the similarity to prior work: the Mondrian Process [12] already defines a Bayesian nonparametric prior over hierarchical structures, and Ho et al. [11] propose a hierarchical blockmodel. The claim that "current Bayesian nonparametric methods do not scale well" overlooks these approaches, weakening the novelty argument. The terminology "Bayesian nonparametric" is also misleading, as the method relies on greedy model selection (not infinite-dimensional priors) [12].  

**Significance**: The method’s computational efficiency (O(n² log n) vs. IRM’s O(n²K²)) is a tangible contribution for large networks, and the predictive performance on NIPS-234 (Figure 4) demonstrates practical utility. However, significance is limited by the lack of comparison to hierarchical baselines: if Mondrian Process or Ho et al. [11] already achieve similar scalability and accuracy, BHCD’s incremental contribution diminishes. The hierarchical structure discovered in the full NIPS dataset (Figure 5) is interesting but not rigorously evaluated against alternative hierarchical models. The suggestion to use triangle-based sufficient statistics (Section 6) is a promising direction but remains speculative without empirical validation.  

**Strengths**:  
- Clear adaptation of rose trees to network data with efficient inference.  
- Strong runtime claims (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) backed by experiments.  
- Well-structured algorithm (Algorithm 1) and thorough experimental validation on real data.  
- Effective handling of sparse networks via BHCD sparse.  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Critical omission of comparisons to hierarchical Bayesian models (Mondrian Process [12], Ho et al. [11]).  
- Misleading "nonparametric" labeling and insufficient differentiation from prior tree-based approaches.  
- Limited dataset scale (max 234 vertices) for scalability claims.  
- Underdeveloped discussion of how BHCD’s hierarchical structure improves upon flat models beyond qualitative examples.  

**Recommendation**: The paper makes a technically sound contribution to scalable hierarchical community discovery but falls short in demonstrating its novelty and significance relative to existing hierarchical Bayesian methods. With additional comparisons to Mondrian Process [12] and Ho et al. [11], and a clearer distinction from nonparametric methods, this work would be significantly stronger. As-is, it is borderline for acceptance due to the lack of contextualized significance.
