Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to define a tree-structured prior over partitions and to compute the marginal likelihood over all partitions consistent with a given tree, following the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) framework. The model uses conjugate blockmodel likelihoods (e.g., Beta–Bernoulli) and a prior that favors non-binary trees when the data exhibits weak hierarchy. Inference proceeds via a greedy agglomerative model-selection procedure that merges subtrees using local Bayes factors; three merge types (join and two absorptions) are considered to preserve BRT’s computational profile. The resulting algorithm has worst-case O(n^2 log n) time (independent of the number of communities), with dynamic-programming evaluation of tree likelihoods in O(n + m). Experiments include qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery network, predictive performance on the NIPS-234 coauthorship subset (compared to IRM), and a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS coauthorship network.

Relation to prior work: BHCD extends IRM-like relational modeling to hierarchical structure via BRT-style mixtures over tree-consistent partitions and greedy agglomeration. It contrasts with IRM variants and other Bayesian hierarchical network models that are either flat, depth-limited, or more computationally demanding.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model and inference are clearly specified. The dynamic-programming recursion for tree likelihood is well motivated, and the local Bayes factor scoring aligns with BRT’s efficient caching. The sparse variant is a sensible modification that avoids merging disconnected components.
- Empirical support: On NIPS-234, BHCD achieves predictive performance comparable to LFRM and better than IRM while being far faster; on Sampson’s network it recovers interpretable structure; on full NIPS it yields a large hierarchical decomposition. The claims about runtime and scalability are plausible and supported by timing plots on NIPS-234.
- Suggestions: It would strengthen the empirical case to report absolute timings for the full NIPS run and to include at least one larger real-world sparse network to illustrate scalability in practice.

Clarity
The paper is well written and easy to follow. The exposition of the generative process, the recursion, and the merge operators is clear. The experimental setup and comparisons are generally transparent. A few minor typos are listed below.

Originality
The central ideas—mixtures over tree-consistent partitions and greedy agglomeration—come from BRT. However, adapting these ideas to relational data with blockmodel likelihoods, preserving the computational guarantees, and demonstrating practical speedups over IRM constitute a meaningful and useful contribution.

Significance
Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models for networks is important for practical use. While the O(n^2 log n) worst-case scaling may be limiting for very large graphs, the sparse variant should remain feasible on large sparse networks. Demonstrating strong speed–accuracy trade-offs relative to IRM is valuable. Providing results on larger datasets would further increase impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Scalability: NIPS-234 completes in tens of seconds. How long does full NIPS take end-to-end (including R restarts)? Can you report wall-clock times and memory usage? Any results on datasets with >10^4 nodes?
- Approximation quality: How close does the greedy procedure get to the optimal tree? On small graphs where exhaustive search is possible, can you quantify suboptimality (likelihood gaps) or recovery of planted structure?
- Guarantees: Is there any theoretical support for the greedy objective (e.g., submodularity-like properties or consistency under certain generative conditions)?
- Stability: Since tie-breaking and restarts can affect outcomes, can you report variability across runs (e.g., variance in log likelihoods or predictive metrics)?
- Practical guidance: How sensitive is performance to hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ)? Any heuristics for setting γ or choosing between binary vs non-binary merges?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-executed adaptation of BRT to relational data with an efficient, practical algorithm.
- Strong speed improvements over IRM with competitive predictive performance.
- Interpretable hierarchical outputs on real datasets.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited large-scale empirical evaluation; no results beyond full NIPS and no very large sparse networks.
- No theoretical guarantees on the greedy search quality.
- Heavy reliance on prior BRT ideas; novelty is primarily in the adaptation and engineering.

- “the run time of our algorithms are” → “the runtime of our algorithms is.”

- “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps.”

Recommendation
Accept. While the core ideas build on BRT, the relational adaptation is well executed, the method is fast and useful, and the empirical results are convincing. Additional large-scale experiments and some analysis of the greedy search’s optimality would further strengthen the paper.
