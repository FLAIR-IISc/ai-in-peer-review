

This paper proposes a hierarchical extension of the Bayesian nonparametric stochastic block model to infer tree-structured partitions from network data, rather than flat clusterings. The authors introduce a learning algorithm based on agglomerative clustering to construct valid partition trees, and demonstrate empirical improvements over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) in both speed and accuracy on two social network datasets. The work addresses a meaningful problem for the NIPS community, as it combines the conceptual appeal of Bayesian nonparametrics with practical scalability concerns—particularly relevant for social scientists increasingly adopting sophisticated network models.

The key technical insight lies in efficiently computing marginal likelihood by restricting partitions to tree structures, avoiding the exponential search space of the IRM. However, I remain unclear how the greedy tree-learning procedure aligns with the Bayesian generative model. Since the method is greedy, it cannot perform true posterior inference over trees, nor does it clearly correspond to a MAP or ML estimate. The authors should clarify this relationship in Section 4 to strengthen the methodological foundation.

The results are compelling: the proposed method achieves comparable or slightly better performance than IRM across multiple metrics (AUC, predictive log-likelihood, accuracy), with runtime improvements of orders of magnitude. While the absolute gains in accuracy may appear modest, the dramatic speedup represents a significant practical advantage.

The paper’s main weakness is its writing quality. Frequent spelling and grammatical errors (e.g., line 126) distract from the technical content. The introduction contains vague phrasing (lines 47–50), such as "local changes" and "iterations," without clarifying whether these refer to MCMC steps or model updates. Dataset descriptions (Monastery networks, NIPS authorship) lack sufficient context for unfamiliar readers. Some mathematical notation (e.g., Equation 5) appears unnecessarily complex, and Figure 3 is unclear without prior knowledge of the monastery dataset—readers cannot discern which method performs best visually. Section 4, presenting the core contribution, requires multiple readings for comprehension due to dense prose and insufficient explanation of the algorithm’s novelty.

If these issues are addressed, the paper’s novelty—bridging agglomerative clustering with Bayesian network modeling—and strong empirical results would make it suitable for publication. The core learning algorithm is insightful, and the experimental validation is robust. However, the current writing undermines the technical merits. The authors should revise for clarity, precision, and grammatical accuracy, particularly in defining key terms, contextualizing datasets, and streamlining mathematical exposition. A clearer presentation of the tree-learning procedure’s theoretical grounding would also resolve the primary methodological concern. With these improvements, the work would effectively communicate its significant contribution to hierarchical network modeling.
