### Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the concept of a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) by defining a prior over network partitions using a tree structure, specifically drawing on the theory of Bayesian Rose Trees. This approach replaces the Chinese Restaurant Process prior used in models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), allowing for an exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree structure.

The authors propose a greedy agglomerative algorithm for model selection, which learns the hierarchical tree structure in a bottom-up fashion. Starting with each vertex as a separate community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of subtrees that yield the greatest increase in the model's marginal likelihood, calculated via a local Bayes factor. A key contribution is an efficient implementation with a novel caching scheme for sufficient statistics, resulting in a time complexity of O(n² log n), where n is the number of vertices. The paper evaluates BHCD on several datasets, including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network, comparing its speed and predictive accuracy primarily against the IRM.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
*   **Strengths:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical formulation, which combines SBMs with the Bayesian Rose Tree framework, is elegant and well-motivated. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) and the local Bayes factor score for merging (Eq. 12) is clear and correct. The factorization of the likelihood over the tree structure is reminiscent of efficient inference in graphical models and represents a solid theoretical contribution. The algorithm's design, including the caching of sufficient statistics to achieve the O(n² log n) complexity, is a strong practical contribution.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation, while illustrative, is not comprehensive enough to fully support the paper's claims of practical superiority.
    1.  **Limited Baselines:** The primary comparison is against the IRM, which is a relevant but somewhat dated baseline. The field of community detection includes many methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics (e.g., semi-parametric and non-probabilistic approaches) that would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art. The comparison to LFRM and ILA is noted, but BHCD's performance is not superior, positioning its main advantage as speed.
    2.  **Scale of Evaluation:** The claim of scalability to large networks is a central theme, yet the main quantitative evaluation is performed on a small subset of the NIPS data (234 authors). While the analysis of the full NIPS network is interesting, it is purely qualitative. A stronger case for the method's utility would involve demonstrating its speed and accuracy on larger graphs where O(n²) methods become computationally challenging.
    3.  **Lack of "Gold Standard" Evaluation:** Evaluation for SBM-based methods is often performed on synthetic or real-world networks where ground-truth community structures are known. Using metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) against these known partitions would provide a more direct assessment of clustering quality than the reported link-prediction metrics.

**Clarity:**
*   **Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors use abstractions like `f(·)` and `g(·)` for on- and off-diagonal block likelihoods, which simplifies the presentation without loss of rigor. The algorithm is described clearly, and the figures effectively illustrate the model and results.
*   **Weaknesses:** The evaluation methodology for link prediction could be described in more detail. The use of AUC is mentioned, but the paper does not explicitly state how the test set was constructed (e.g., by holding out a random set of existing and non-existing edges). A brief explanation of this setup would improve reproducibility. Additionally, as a minor stylistic point, several sentences in the introduction begin with a citation number, which is unconventional for the NIPS format.

**Originality:**
*   **Strengths:** The primary novelty lies in the synthesis of two existing lines of work: Bayesian hierarchical clustering (specifically, Bayesian Rose Trees) and relational modeling with SBMs. While the components are not new, their combination to create a fast, fully Bayesian procedure for hierarchical community detection is original. Framing the greedy agglomerative search as a Bayesian model selection procedure with a well-defined scoring function is a valuable contribution.
*   **Weaknesses:** The conceptual framework is an incremental advance, building directly upon well-established techniques. The contribution is more in the effective combination and efficient implementation of these ideas rather than a fundamental theoretical breakthrough.

**Significance:**
*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses an important problem: scalable inference for probabilistic models of network structure. The proposed BHCD method is significantly faster than MCMC-based alternatives like the IRM, achieving a speedup of two orders of magnitude while maintaining comparable predictive performance. This makes it a potentially valuable tool for exploratory analysis of moderate-to-large networks where iterative methods are prohibitively slow.
*   **Weaknesses:** The overall significance is tempered by the limitations of the experimental section. Without stronger evidence of its performance on larger networks and against a broader set of modern baselines, it is difficult to assess how impactful BHCD will be in practice. Its primary selling point is speed, but its predictive accuracy is not shown to surpass existing methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and elegant combination of Bayesian Rose Trees and stochastic blockmodels to create a fully probabilistic model for hierarchical community detection.
2.  It introduces a fast, greedy inference algorithm with a clever caching scheme that achieves O(n² log n) complexity, a significant practical contribution.
3.  The method demonstrates a substantial speed improvement (two orders of magnitude) over a widely recognized baseline (IRM).
4.  The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental evaluation is limited. It relies on relatively small datasets and a narrow set of baselines, which does not fully validate the method's claimed scalability and performance relative to the state of the art.
2.  The evaluation lacks comparison against ground-truth community structures, which is a standard practice for assessing community detection algorithms.
3.  The method's predictive accuracy is not superior to existing models, making its primary contribution speed, which itself requires more thorough empirical validation on larger networks.
4.  Important details of the experimental setup, such as the procedure for link prediction evaluation, are not fully described.
