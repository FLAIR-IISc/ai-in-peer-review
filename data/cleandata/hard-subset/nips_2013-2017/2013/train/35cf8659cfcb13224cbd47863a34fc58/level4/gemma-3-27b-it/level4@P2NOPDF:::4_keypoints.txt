- Proposes an agglomerative method for scalable hierarchical community detection building on Bayesian rose trees (retains prior and algorithmic structure).  
- Main contribution is adapting the likelihood to network data and devising efficient caching/updating of sufficient statistics during agglomeration.  
- Paper is generally well-written and technically sound, but Section 3 is dense and the notation around equation (9) and its derivations needs simplification.  
- Demonstrates the value of incorporating hierarchical structure into relational data modeling.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited by the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models.  
- Omits relevant related work such as Ho et al. (2012) (nested CRP) and Roy et al. (2009) (Mondrian Process/K-d tree).  
- Originality lies in adapting Bayesian rose trees to a stochastic block model likelihood and achieving efficiency gains.  
- Claims of O(nodes + edges) likelihood computation are noteworthy, but the description of this efficiency—especially the dynamic programming before equation (10)—is unclear.  
- Recursive updates of sufficient statistics are presented with equations but lack clear justification and explanation.  
- The use of the term “nonparametric” is questionable; the model appears to be framed as model selection per Blundell et al., not a true nonparametric approach.  
- Experiments (Sampson network, IRM comparison) show benefits of averaging over hypotheses, but do not establish superiority over other hierarchical methods.  
- Scalability claims are weakly supported because evaluated networks are not large enough to thoroughly assess scale.  
- Overall, integrates rose trees with SBM for efficient hierarchical community discovery but the lack of comparisons to alternative hierarchical models limits assessment of relative performance and impact.
