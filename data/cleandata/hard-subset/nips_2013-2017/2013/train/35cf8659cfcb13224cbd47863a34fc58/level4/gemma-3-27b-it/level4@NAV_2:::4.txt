This paper proposes an efficient agglomerative approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model leverages a Bayesian nonparametric framework, specifically adapting Bayesian rose trees [12] to relational data by employing a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The key innovation lies in maintaining efficient updates of sufficient statistics during greedy agglomerative clustering, enabling scalable inference. The presentation is clear, and the experiments demonstrate favorable performance compared to a flat community structure baseline. However, the paper lacks comparisons to other existing hierarchical network models.

**SIGNIFICANCE & RELATED WORK:** The paper is technically sound and well-written. The notation in Section 3 is dense, and simplifying it could improve clarity. The experiments primarily demonstrate the benefits of modeling hierarchy in relational datasets, but a more comprehensive evaluation against other hierarchical methods is needed. Specifically, the paper omits discussion of relevant Bayesian nonparametric models such as the “multiscale community blockmodel for Network exploration” [18] which utilizes a nested Chinese restaurant process, and the “Mondrian Process” [19], which employs a novel stochastic process resulting in hierarchical structures over K-d trees. These models potentially offer more refined latent structure discovery capabilities.

**ORIGINALITY & CLARITY:** The core model resembles a Bayesian rose tree with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. Therefore, the main contributions reside in adapting the inference procedure – specifically, the efficient recursive updating of sufficient statistics within the greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm. The likelihood calculation’s O(Nodes + Edges) time complexity is a valuable feature. However, the explanation of this efficiency lacks detail; the allusion to dynamic programming before equation (10) is insufficient. Furthermore, the justification for the correctness of the recursive sufficient statistic updates requires further elaboration. 

**TERMINOLOGY:** The use of “nonparametric” terminology is questionable. Following Blundell et al. [12], the rose tree approach is better characterized as model selection rather than a genuinely nonparametric method. 

**EXPERIMENTS:** The qualitative experiment on Sampson’s monastery network is adequate, and the comparison to the IRM effectively highlights the advantages of hierarchical modeling. The primary limitation remains the absence of comparisons to other hierarchical network models. While scalability is discussed, the tested networks are not exceptionally large.

**Overall:** This paper capably integrates prior work on Bayesian rose trees with stochastic blockmodel likelihoods for networks, generalizing efficient agglomerative clustering methods to this scenario. This enables effective hierarchical community discovery in experiments, but a stronger comparison to existing hierarchical network models would significantly strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-written.
*   Efficient inference procedure with favorable time complexity.
*   Demonstrates improvements over flat community structure models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks comparison to relevant existing hierarchical network models.
*   Explanation of dynamic programming and sufficient statistic updates could be improved.
*   Questionable use of “nonparametric” terminology.
