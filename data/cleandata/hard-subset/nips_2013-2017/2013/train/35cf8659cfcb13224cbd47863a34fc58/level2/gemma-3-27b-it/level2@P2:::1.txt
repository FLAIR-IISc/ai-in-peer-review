This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method based on a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The core idea is to efficiently learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model via a greedy agglomerative approach, offering a significant speedup compared to existing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors claim their algorithm scales well, particularly being independent of the number of nested communities.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation behind the work, building on existing Bayesian and greedy hierarchical clustering approaches. The problem of discovering hierarchical community structure is important, and the limitations of existing methods (computational cost, parametric assumptions) are well highlighted. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the existing literature (Stochastic Blockmodels, IRM, greedy hierarchical clustering, Mondrian process) with relevant citations. The mathematical formulation of the model and the greedy algorithm are presented in a reasonably clear and concise manner. The experiments demonstrate competitive performance against the IRM in terms of speed and accuracy, and showcase the method’s ability to uncover meaningful hierarchical structures in real-world datasets (Sampson’s monastery network, NIPS co-authorship network).

A major strength of the paper is the emphasis on computational efficiency. The authors explicitly address the scalability issues of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods and propose a greedy algorithm that avoids costly iterative updates. The claimed O(n^2 log n) time complexity (and even faster for sparse networks with the ‘BHCD sparse’ variant) is a significant advantage. The caching scheme to achieve efficient updates is a clever addition. The comparison with IRM highlights this benefit effectively, demonstrating two orders of magnitude faster performance. Another strength is the clarity of presentation – the mathematical notation, while dense, is generally well-explained, and the algorithm is described in a step-by-step fashion. The inclusion of experiments on different datasets adds to the credibility of the proposed method.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the paper claims comparable or better accuracy than IRM, the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The comparisons are primarily focused on predictive performance (link prediction). It would be valuable to include other evaluation metrics relevant to community discovery, such as Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) or adjusted Rand index, comparing the discovered communities to ground truth (where available) or to communities identified by other established methods.  The hyperparameters α, β, δ, and λ are set empirically and the justification for these values is somewhat limited. A more thorough sensitivity analysis regarding these parameters would be beneficial. The explanation of the choice of γ could also be expanded, as it impacts tree structure.

The discussion of the full NIPS dataset is limited. The statement “We cut the tree and retained the top portion of the hierarchy” feels a bit arbitrary. A more principled approach to selecting the level of the hierarchy to display, or a visualization of the entire tree (perhaps using a zoomable interface), would be helpful. The algorithm description relies heavily on the reader's familiarity with hierarchical clustering and Bayesian inference. Providing more intuitive explanations of some key steps, especially regarding the Bayes factor calculation (SCORE function) and the sufficient statistics updates, could enhance clarity.  The notation feels slightly inconsistent at times, (e.g., the use of both S and T to represent tree structures) which causes minor readability issues.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant improvement in computational efficiency compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community discovery.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the model and algorithm.
*   Demonstrated performance on real-world datasets, showing competitive accuracy and substantial speedups.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation; more comprehensive metrics for community discovery are needed.
*   Hyperparameter selection is largely empirical and lacks justification.
*   Explanation of some algorithmic details could be improved for better clarity.
*   Presentation of the results on the full NIPS dataset lacks sufficient detail.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. The focus on computational efficiency is particularly commendable. Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer comments regarding experimental evaluation and hyperparameter sensitivity.
