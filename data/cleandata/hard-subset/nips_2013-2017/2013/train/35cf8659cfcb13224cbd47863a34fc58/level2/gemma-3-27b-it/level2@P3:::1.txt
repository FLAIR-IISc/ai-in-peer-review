This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is to utilize a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, coupled with a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm for efficient inference. The approach aims to balance probabilistic rigor with computational scalability, addressing limitations of existing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

The strengths of the paper are severalfold. Firstly, the proposed model builds upon well-established concepts – stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering – offering a natural and interpretable framework. Secondly, the authors clearly identify a gap in existing literature: scalable Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection. Existing methods either require pre-defined hierarchy depth or suffer from high computational costs. BHCD attempts to address this gap. Thirdly, the experimental evaluation, including comparisons to the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship network, demonstrates a significant speedup (two orders of magnitude) with comparable or better accuracy. The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network provide a qualitative demonstration of the model’s ability to recover meaningful community structures. The algorithm's scalability analysis (O(n^2 log n)) is a notable contribution. Finally, the inclusion of variations (sparse BHCD) and considerations for predictive performance strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

However, there are several areas for improvement. The clarity of the presentation could be enhanced. The mathematical notation, while generally correct, is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. For example, the connection between equation (7) and the intuitive explanation of the prior on partitions is not immediately obvious. A more detailed explanation of the merging criteria in Algorithm 1 would also be beneficial. The paper briefly mentions parameter selection (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4) without justification, and a sensitivity analysis regarding these hyperparameters would strengthen the results. While the comparisons to the IRM are positive, it’s not entirely clear *why* BHCD outperforms it beyond computational efficiency. A more in-depth discussion of the model assumptions and their impact on performance would be valuable. The experimental setup on the NIPS dataset could be expanded to include comparisons with other state-of-the-art community detection algorithms, beyond just the IRM. The claim that the predictive performance is similar to LFRM and ILA, as referenced from [10], needs to be clearly stated with supporting numbers. A figure illustrating the full tree structure found for the NIPS dataset would give a better sense of the hierarchical organization.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis with a novel and scalable approach. The experimental results demonstrate significant speed improvements over existing methods while maintaining competitive accuracy. The theoretical analysis provides insights into the algorithm’s complexity.

Arguments against acceptance: The clarity of the presentation could be improved. The justification for parameter choices is lacking. A more comprehensive comparison with other community detection algorithms would be beneficial. A more detailed analysis of the model's limitations is needed.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. While the presentation could be refined and the experimental evaluation expanded, the strengths of the work – its novelty, scalability, and promising experimental results – outweigh the weaknesses. Addressing the concerns raised above would further enhance the quality of the paper and its impact on the field.
