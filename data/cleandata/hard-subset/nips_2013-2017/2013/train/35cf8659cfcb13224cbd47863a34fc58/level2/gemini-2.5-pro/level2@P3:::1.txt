### Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structures in networks. The model is conceptualized as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). A key contribution is that for any given tree structure, the marginal likelihood of the network data can be computed exactly and efficiently using a dynamic programming recursion.

For inference, the work proposes a greedy agglomerative algorithm that constructs the hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion. Starting with each node as a singleton, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of subtrees that yield the largest increase in the model's evidence, as measured by a local Bayes factor. The paper presents an efficient implementation with a worst-case time complexity of O(n^2 log n), which is notably independent of the number of communities.

This work directly extends the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering, previously applied to item-feature data, to the domain of relational data. It positions itself as a computationally efficient alternative to more flexible but slower MCMC-based methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). Experimental results on both a classic social network and a larger co-authorship network demonstrate that BHCD achieves comparable predictive accuracy to the IRM while being approximately two orders of magnitude faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the derivation of the marginal likelihood calculation via recursion (Equation 10) is sound. The proposed agglomerative inference algorithm is a principled greedy search in the space of tree models, guided by the Bayes factor. The claims of computational efficiency and predictive performance are strongly supported by experimental evidence comparing BHCD to the widely-used IRM baseline. The analysis is thorough, including ablations such as sparse and binary-only versions of the algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and review of related work, then logically builds the proposed model from the standard stochastic blockmodel to the full hierarchical formulation. The inference procedure is described clearly in Algorithm 1, and the mechanisms for efficient computation are explained. The figures and tables effectively support the paper's claims, particularly Figure 4, which provides a compelling visualization of the speed-accuracy trade-off compared to the IRM.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and valuable combination of existing ideas. While the agglomerative Bayesian clustering framework builds upon prior work (e.g., Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2010), its application to relational data via a stochastic blockmodel likelihood is a significant and non-trivial extension. The development of the efficient caching scheme for the sufficient statistics required for the O(n^2 log n) merge-scoring is a solid algorithmic contribution. This approach provides a new point in the trade-off space between computational cost and model flexibility for network analysis.

**Significance:** The primary significance of this work lies in its practicality and scalability. Many Bayesian nonparametric methods for network modeling rely on MCMC, which can be prohibitively slow for large networks. By providing a greedy, single-pass algorithm that is orders of magnitude faster, this work makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling accessible for larger-scale network exploration. The ability to produce a full probabilistic model, rather than just a point estimate of a hierarchy, is a distinct advantage over non-probabilistic clustering methods. These features make the proposed method likely to be adopted by practitioners and to serve as a strong baseline for future research in scalable network modeling.

### Suggestions for Improvement

The main limitation of the proposed method is its greedy nature, which makes it susceptible to local optima. While using multiple restarts is a sensible practical solution, the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this issue.
*   **Essential:** A minor addition could be to report the variance of the final model likelihoods across the R=50 restarts in the NIPS-234 experiment. This would give readers a better sense of the stability of the greedy search.

The O(n^2 log n) complexity is a great result for dense graphs, but it is suboptimal for the sparse graphs common in real-world applications. The paper proposes a `BHCD sparse` variant as a heuristic, which performs well empirically.
*   **Optional:** The paper could be improved by a more detailed discussion of the `BHCD sparse` variant. For example, does this heuristic alter the algorithm's worst-case complexity, or does it primarily offer practical speed-ups? Exploring algorithmic modifications that avoid the initial O(n^2) cost of populating the heap for sparse graphs could be mentioned as a concrete direction for future work.

The model hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed in the experiments.
*   **Optional:** While a full hyperparameter inference scheme is beyond the scope of this work, a brief discussion of the role of these parameters would be helpful. For instance, explaining how the concentration parameter γ influences the resulting tree's shape (e.g., preference for flatter vs. deeper hierarchies) would provide valuable intuition for users of the model.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses the critical need for scalable methods for Bayesian network analysis. The demonstrated two-orders-of-magnitude speedup over a standard baseline (IRM) is a major practical contribution.
2.  **Novel Contribution:** It successfully adapts and extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational data, resulting in a novel and principled algorithm for community discovery.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-formulated, and the inference algorithm is derived correctly from probabilistic principles (maximization of model evidence).
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by solid experiments on real-world datasets, showing clear advantages in speed with competitive accuracy.

### Arguments against Acceptance

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The inference method is inherently greedy and provides no guarantee of finding a globally optimal hierarchy. However, this is a well-understood trade-off for computational efficiency, and the use of restarts partially mitigates the issue.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete, well-executed, and significant piece of research that advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian modeling of networks.
