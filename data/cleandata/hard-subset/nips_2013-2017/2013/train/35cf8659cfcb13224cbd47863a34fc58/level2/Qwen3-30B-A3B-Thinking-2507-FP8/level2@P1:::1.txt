

This paper proposes BHCD (Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: existing Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods (like extensions of the Infinite Relational Model) scale poorly with network size, while non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering lacks probabilistic guarantees. BHCD models hierarchical communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, and introduces a greedy agglomerative inference algorithm that achieves O(n² log n) time complexity—two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining comparable accuracy. The key innovation is a dynamic programming approach with efficient sufficient statistic caching that enables local computations during the agglomerative process.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear derivations of the marginal likelihood (equation 10) and predictive distribution (equation 14). The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-specified, and the complexity analysis is thorough. The experimental section provides compelling evidence: on Sampson's monastery network, BHCD recovers meaningful hierarchical structure consistent with domain knowledge; on NIPS co-authorship data, BHCD achieves comparable predictive accuracy to IRM but with drastically reduced runtime (10 seconds vs. 1000 seconds). The ablation study (Section 5.1) effectively demonstrates the value of the sparse variant for real-world sparse networks.

The work makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It directly addresses a limitation of prior Bayesian hierarchical methods (e.g., [8, 11]) by providing a scalable solution without sacrificing probabilistic interpretation. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work: unlike non-Bayesian methods [3, 4], BHCD provides full probabilistic structure; unlike IRM-based hierarchical extensions [8], it avoids expensive MCMC. The authors appropriately cite recent NIPS work (e.g., [12, 14, 18, 21]) and position their contribution within the literature.

Several aspects could be strengthened. First, while the paper shows BHCD outperforms IRM in speed and accuracy, it lacks a thorough comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical methods (e.g., [3, 4]), which might serve as a more direct baseline for the agglomerative approach. Second, the predictive distribution derivation (equation 14) would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the responsibility term r_S is computed and why it weights the on-diagonal vs. off-diagonal components. Third, the paper does not explore how the model's hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) impact the learned hierarchy—this is a critical practical consideration for users. Finally, while the speed advantage is well-demonstrated, the authors could more explicitly discuss the trade-offs between computational efficiency and the quality of the hierarchical structure (e.g., does the greedy approach miss globally optimal hierarchies?).

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The figures (particularly Figure 3 showing Sampson's network) effectively illustrate the hierarchical results. The code and experimental details are sufficiently described for reproducibility. The writing avoids unnecessary jargon, though a few technical terms (e.g., "r_S" in equation 14) could be clarified with a sentence of explanation.

In terms of significance, BHCD fills an important practical gap: it enables Bayesian hierarchical community discovery on networks that were previously intractable for full Bayesian methods. The scalability claim is well-supported by both theory and experiments. The application to the full NIPS co-authorship network (Figure 5) demonstrates real-world utility, showing how the method can uncover interpretable hierarchical structures in large-scale academic collaboration data.

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend:
1. Adding a brief comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., [3]) in the experimental section to contextualize the accuracy gains.
2. Including a small sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (γ, α, β) in the supplementary material.
3. Clarifying the interpretation of the predictive distribution in equation 14 with a concrete example.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of network analysis. The technical innovation (efficient greedy inference for Bayesian hierarchical models) is significant, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its practical value. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, novel (with clear differentiation from prior work), and addresses an important problem with meaningful impact. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
