Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model and associated greedy agglomerative procedure for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a tree over vertices and uses that tree to induce a prior over a (potentially exponential) family of blockmodel partitions consistent with the tree. With conjugate Beta–Bernoulli likelihoods, the marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions is computed exactly via a dynamic programming recursion, enabling efficient scoring of trees. Inference proceeds by agglomeratively merging trees using a local Bayes factor computed from cached sufficient statistics, considering three merge types (join and two absorptions). The worst-case runtime is O(n^2 log n), independent of the number of communities; the marginal likelihood over a fixed tree is O(n + m). Variants include a “sparse” version that forbids merging disconnected subtrees and a binary-only version.

Experiments include qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery network and link prediction on a NIPS co-authorship subset (NIPS-234), comparing against the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) with both MCMC and greedy search. BHCD achieves comparable or better predictive performance than IRM with significantly lower runtime and is reported to be similar to LFRM and below ILA (using published results on the same folds). A large-scale run on the full NIPS co-authorship network illustrates interpretability of the learned hierarchy.

Relation to prior work
The approach extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) and Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al., 2010) from i.i.d. data to relational data by using a tree to restrict partitions in a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) and by integrating out edge-probability parameters. It contrasts with IRM (Kemp et al., 2006) and its hierarchical extensions (e.g., Herlau et al., 2012) that rely on MCMC or more expensive search, and with nonparametric latent feature models such as LFRM (Miller et al., NIPS 2009) and ILA (Palla et al., ICML 2012). The general goal of hierarchical community detection also relates to the hierarchical random graph (HRG; Clauset, Moore, Newman, PNAS 2008), though BHCD’s tree-conditioned mixture of SBMs and exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions are distinct.

Strengths
- Efficient exact marginalization given a tree: The recursion for p(D|T) is elegant and computationally attractive; caching delivers O(1) inner-loop updates during agglomeration.
- Practical scalability: O(n^2 log n) inference independent of the number of communities and O(n + m) likelihood; the sparse variant is well-motivated for real networks.
- Clear modeling choices: Conjugate on-/off-diagonal models with separate hyperparameters embody a straightforward community assumption (dense within, sparse between).
- Empirical utility: On NIPS-234, BHCD reaches strong predictive performance far faster than IRM; qualitative structure on Sampson and full NIPS appears sensible.
- Simple, implementable algorithm: Greedy merges with local Bayes factors and three merge types are easy to reproduce and extend.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited comparisons: Only IRM is run as a baseline, with LFRM and ILA referenced indirectly via published numbers. Including HRG (Clauset et al., 2008), degree-corrected SBMs, or directly running LFRM/ILA (when feasible) would strengthen claims of significance.
- Modeling assumptions: The Beta–Bernoulli SBM lacks degree correction and cannot capture heavy-tailed degree distributions common in real networks. A degree-corrected SBM likelihood or a simple node-activity correction would likely improve both fit and link prediction.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: Fixed α, β, δ, λ, γ are used across datasets. Reporting sensitivity analyses and/or empirical Bayes or MCMC updates for these hyperparameters would increase robustness and fairness of the comparisons.
- Greedy inference and local optima: Although restarts are used, the method may be sensitive to tie-breaking and early decisions. Diagnostics (variance across restarts) and a brief post-processing local search (e.g., subtree reattachments) would help.
- Evaluation depth: The Sampson analysis is largely qualitative. Reporting quantitative clustering metrics (e.g., NMI/ARI versus known groups) and confidence (e.g., via responsibilities rS) would be informative. For NIPS-234, provide identical hardware, wall-clock comparisons, and calibration of predictive probabilities, not just AUC/accuracy.
- Clarity/details: Equation (14) states “linear in depth” and later “Θ(n)”; clarifying that complexity is linear in tree depth would avoid confusion. A brief discussion of memory footprint of cached statistics and tie-breaking effects would improve clarity. Some minor typographical inconsistencies could be corrected.

Quality
The probabilistic model and inference are technically sound. The dynamic programming derivations and Bayes factor scoring are well reasoned. Empirical methodology is generally careful but could be broadened to include stronger baselines and quantitative clustering metrics.

Clarity
The exposition is clear and well structured. The description of sufficient-statistic caching and the three merge types is helpful. Minor clarifications as noted above would improve readability.

Originality
Adapting rose-tree priors and exact partition marginalization to relational block models with an efficient, local, tree-building algorithm is a novel and useful combination relative to IRM-style MCMC and HRG-style inference.

Significance
Given its speed and acceptable accuracy, BHCD is likely to be useful as a scalable exploratory tool for hierarchical community discovery and for fast link prediction baselines. Incorporating degree correction and richer likelihoods would increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled, and efficient framework for hierarchical community discovery with exact marginalization conditioned on a tree.
- Strong scalability and practical performance; substantial speedup over IRM with comparable accuracy.
- Clear algorithmic contributions (local Bayes factor with caching) and useful variants (sparse, binary).

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation lacks some key baselines (HRG, degree-corrected SBMs) and quantitative clustering metrics on labeled data.
- Model’s simplicity (no degree correction, fixed hyperparameters) limits predictive performance compared to more expressive methods (e.g., ILA).
- Greedy search risks local optima; robustness analysis is limited.

Recommendation and actionable feedback
- Essential: Add at least one stronger baseline beyond IRM (e.g., HRG or degree-corrected SBM); include quantitative clustering metrics on Sampson; clarify runtime measurement protocols and complexity statement for prediction; report sensitivity to γ and to α,β,δ,λ.
- Nice-to-have: Incorporate degree correction; perform a lightweight local refinement after agglomeration; release code and scripts for reproducibility.
