Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a prior over partitions constrained by a tree and integrates out block parameters with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli likelihoods. A greedy agglomerative procedure builds a rose tree by local Bayes factor merges; dynamic programming yields exact marginal likelihoods at each node. Empirically, BHCD delivers two orders of magnitude speedup over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or better link prediction on a NIPS co-authorship subset; it also produces interpretable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery network and scales to the full NIPS graph. This work builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees, adapts them to relational data (blockmodels), and contrasts with IRM and its hierarchical extensions, the Mondrian process, LFRM, and ILA.

Quality: The modeling assumptions are clear and the inference scheme is well specified, with a principled local Bayes factor objective and caching for O(1) inner-loop updates. Claims about speed and accuracy are supported by experiments on real datasets; however, statistical significance and broader comparisons (e.g., to hierarchical IRM, multiscale community blockmodels) are limited. Hyperparameters are fixed without sensitivity analysis, and the impact of multiple random restarts is not quantified.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The recursion (Eq. 10) and merge scoring (Eq. 12) are explained crisply. Some complexity statements are slightly inconsistent: the text claims worst-case quadratic, yet the algorithmic analysis is O(n^2 log n).

Originality: The key novelty is marrying rose-tree-style Bayesian hierarchical clustering with stochastic blockmodels and exploiting tree-consistent partitions for exact marginalization. While related in spirit to prior hierarchical network models, the combination and the efficient greedy selection for relational data are original.

Significance: The method is practically useful: it scales to thousands of nodes, yields interpretable hierarchies, and offers competitive prediction with substantial speedups. This is valuable for practitioners working with large sparse networks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled model with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Scalable greedy algorithm with strong empirical speedups.
- Produces interpretable hierarchical structures and competitive link prediction.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited breadth of baselines and no statistical testing.
- No hyperparameter inference or sensitivity study.
- Greedy search may be unstable; robustness across restarts not analyzed.
- Minor complexity inconsistency and limited discussion of directed/weighted graphs.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add comparisons to hierarchical IRM and multiscale community blockmodels on shared splits; report confidence intervals.
- Provide sensitivity to α,β,δ,λ,γ and analysis of variance across restarts.
- Clarify complexity claims and characterize BHCD-sparse complexity in terms of m.
- Consider hyperparameter learning and non-conjugate likelihoods; extend to directed/weighted ties.
- Release code for reproducibility.

Overall: Weak accept. The paper offers a timely, useful contribution with solid technical grounding and strong empirical efficiency, despite evaluation and analysis gaps.
