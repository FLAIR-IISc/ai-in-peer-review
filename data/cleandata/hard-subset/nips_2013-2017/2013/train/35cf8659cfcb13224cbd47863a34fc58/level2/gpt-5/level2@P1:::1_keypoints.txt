- Paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model that exactly marginalizes over tree-consistent partitions via dynamic programming.  
- Likelihood is a mixture of stochastic blockmodels with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli components distinguishing within- and between-community links.  
- Learning uses greedy agglomerative model selection with local Bayes factors, cached sufficient statistics, join/absorption merges, O(n^2 log n) per run, and a practical sparse variant.  
- Builds on BHC/Bayesian Rose Trees and is compared to IRM, LFRM, ILA, Mondrian, and other hierarchical blockmodels.  
- Empirically much faster than IRM with comparable predictive performance on a NIPS co-authorship subset and yields interpretable hierarchies on Sampson and full NIPS graphs.  

Strengths:
- Conceptually clear mixture-over-partitions view anchored to a tree enabling exact marginalization.  
- Efficient inference via local Bayes factors and cached statistics; sensible sparse variant for real-world graphs.  
- Practical prediction by top-down recursion and substantial wall-clock speed improvements over IRM.  
- Clear relation to prior work and interpretable non-binary hierarchies with meaningful qualitative results.  

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Global tying of Beta priors for all within- and between-community blocks may be too restrictive for heterogeneous networks.  
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed with no data-driven inference or sensitivity analysis.  
- Greedy agglomerative search can get trapped by early merges; limited analysis of stability across restarts.  
- Merge scoring is local to candidate leaves and may ignore informative global structure; no comparison to globally rescored variants.  
- Experimental comparisons focus mainly on IRM; lacks direct side-by-side baselines, more datasets, and error bars.  
- Minor clarity issues: undefined “BHCD global” and ambiguous statement about predictive complexity being Θ(n).  

Quality:
- Modeling and dynamic program are technically sound; merge scoring and caching appear correct and complexity claims plausible, with caveats on global hyperparameter tying and greedy myopia.  

Clarity:
- Generally well written with clear equations and high-level picture; would benefit from explicit pseudocode for Algorithm 1 and clear definitions of all variants.  

Originality:
- Novel adaptation of BHC/BRT to relational blockmodels with exact partition marginalization and efficient sufficient-statistics caching.  

Significance:
- Compelling speed-accuracy trade-off for large networks and produces interpretable hierarchical outputs, making it a practical tool despite not always matching richer models in prediction.  

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add empirical Bayes or basic hyperparameter inference/sensitivity analysis for α,β,δ,λ,γ.  
- Compare directly to at least one other hierarchical relational baseline and report uncertainty (std/CI).  
- Evaluate stability across restarts (e.g., tree distance metrics) and the impact of the sparse constraint.  
- Consider a degree-corrected conjugate variant to handle heterogeneity.  
- Clarify “BHCD global,” define all variants in the main text, and state predictive complexity as a function of tree depth.  
- Cite related work on hierarchical random graphs (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman 2008).  

Pros (acceptance arguments):
- Elegant principled formulation with exact marginalization, significant empirical speed gains, clear extension of Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and interpretable results.  

Cons (rejection arguments):
- Limited expressiveness from globally shared priors, lack of hyperparameter learning, greedy local scoring brittleness, and limited experimental rigor.  

Overall assessment:
- Solid and practical contribution marrying Bayesian hierarchical clustering with stochastic blockmodels; leans toward acceptance pending clarifications, broader comparisons, and basic hyperparameter sensitivity.
