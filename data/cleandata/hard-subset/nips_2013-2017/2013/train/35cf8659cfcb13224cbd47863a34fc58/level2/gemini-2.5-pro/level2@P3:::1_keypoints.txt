- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric, tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with an exact, efficient marginal likelihood computed by dynamic programming.  
- Proposes a greedy bottom-up agglomerative inference algorithm that merges subtrees by maximizing local Bayes factors.  
- Presents an implementation with worst-case time complexity O(n^2 log n), independent of the number of communities.  
- Extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods to relational (network) data, offering a scalable alternative to MCMC-based models like the IRM.  
- Empirically achieves comparable predictive accuracy to the IRM while being roughly two orders of magnitude faster on tested datasets.  
- Model and marginal likelihood derivation are technically sound and well-formulated (Equation 10 recursion).  
- The greedy agglomerative search is principled and guided by Bayesian model evidence.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough and includes useful ablations (sparse and binary-only variants).  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and supported by informative figures and tables (e.g., speed-accuracy trade-off).  
- The combination of hierarchical clustering and SBM likelihood is a non-trivial, novel extension to relational modeling.  
- The caching scheme for sufficient statistics enabling O(n^2 log n) merge scoring is a solid algorithmic contribution.  
- Main significance is practicality and scalability: makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling feasible for larger networks.  
- Produces a full probabilistic model rather than only point-estimate hierarchies, increasing utility over non-probabilistic methods.  
- Primary limitation: greedy inference can get stuck in local optima; restarts mitigate but do not eliminate this.  
- Essential suggested improvement: report variance of final model likelihoods across the R=50 restarts to assess stability.  
- Complexity concern: O(n^2 log n) is suboptimal for sparse real-world graphs; BHCD sparse is a heuristic but needs more discussion.  
- Optional improvement: clarify whether BHCD sparse changes worst-case complexity or mainly offers practical speed-ups, and explore ways to avoid initial O(n^2) heap costs.  
- Optional improvement: discuss roles of fixed hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ), especially how γ affects tree depth/flatness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: high practical impact (large speedups), novel principled adaptation to networks, technical correctness, and strong empirical support.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on greedy inference with no global-optimum guarantees, though this is a known trade-off for efficiency.  
- Overall assessment: strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; the work is a significant, usable contribution and a strong baseline for scalable Bayesian network modeling.
