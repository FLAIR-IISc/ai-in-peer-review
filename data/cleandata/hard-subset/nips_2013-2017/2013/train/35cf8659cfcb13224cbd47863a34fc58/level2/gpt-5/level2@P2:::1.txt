Summary
The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model and greedy agglomerative inference procedure for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model can be interpreted as a tree-structured mixture over exponentially many stochastic blockmodels: given a fixed tree T over the vertices, the likelihood marginalizes over all tree-consistent partitions using a recursive decomposition, with on-diagonal blocks drawn from f (dense, within-community) and off-diagonal blocks from g (sparse, between-community). The key computational contribution is a dynamic-programming recursion for the marginal likelihood of a tree, and a merge-scoring scheme based on local Bayes factors that supports an O(n^2 log n) greedy bottom-up agglomeration with careful caching. Variants include a “sparse” constraint forbidding merges between disconnected subgraphs and binary-only merges. Empirically, BHCD yields interpretable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery network, competitive link-prediction on a NIPS coauthorship subgraph (vs. IRM; comparable to LFRM, worse than ILA), and scales to the full NIPS 1–17 graph, with substantial speedups over IRM.

Relation to prior work
The model extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering/rose trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010) from i.i.d. data to relational data via blockmodel marginals, and contrasts with IRM and its hierarchical variants, LFRM, and ILA. Compared to deterministic hierarchical clustering (e.g., Clauset-Newman-Moore; Girvan-Newman), BHCD provides a probabilistic model with marginalization over partitions consistent with a learned tree. The paper cites hierarchical IRM [8], Mondrian process [12], and multiscale community blockmodels [11], noting their higher computational cost or depth constraints.

Quality
- Modeling: The generative construction on a fixed tree is clear and coherent. The recursion p(DTT|T) = πT f(σTT) + (1−πT) g(σ¬chTT) ∏C p(DCC|C) appears correct, ensuring each edge is explained once at the unique level where its endpoints are separated.
- Inference: The Bayes-factor merge score depends only on local subgraph statistics, enabling efficiency; this is a heuristic approximation to global structure but standard in greedy agglomeration.
- Complexity: The O(n+m) evaluation of a fixed tree via DP and O(n^2 log n) greedy search with cached sufficient statistics are plausible. The sparse constraint is a practical acceleration on real networks.
- Experiments: Qualitative recovery on Sampson is convincing. Predictive comparison on NIPS-234 shows BHCD is much faster than IRM for comparable AUC/log-probability; comparison to LFRM/ILA is via reported numbers and suggests BHCD is competitive with LFRM and below ILA, as expected for a simpler model. The full NIPS hierarchy demonstrates scalability and interpretability.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Empirical breadth: Evaluation is limited to one small qualitative dataset and one predictive benchmark; no synthetic experiments assessing recovery of known hierarchical structure or robustness to noise; no broader real-world benchmarks.
- Baselines: No direct comparison to hierarchical network models (e.g., hierarchical IRM [8], multiscale/community blockmodels [11], or hierarchical random graphs) due to cost; at least one hierarchical baseline (even if approximate) would strengthen claims about hierarchy quality.
- Hyperparameters: All hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; no sensitivity analysis or empirical Bayes/posterior inference. The choice πS = 1−(1−γ)|ch(S)| is justified qualitatively, but γ is not learned.
- “Sparse” variant: Forbidding merges between disconnected subgraphs is a pragmatic constraint but changes the prior/model class; potential failure modes (e.g., disassortative/bipartite structures) are not discussed.
- Stability and restarts: The method relies on random tie-breaking and uses R=50 restarts; runtime reporting should clarify whether times include restarts. No analysis of variability across runs or uncertainty over T beyond simple averaging.
- Clarity/consistency: The Sampson section mentions “BHCD global” without prior definition; clarify variants. Memory complexity and practical constants for caching σIJ and σ¬ch are not discussed.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The model and recursion are clear, and the merge operations (join/absorb) are explained. Algorithm 1 description is sufficient for reproduction given the statistics updates. Minor issues: define all variants consistently; provide a brief derivation for the DP recursion; specify memory requirements; clarify runtime accounting with restarts; and ensure all figures/tables are legible and self-contained.

Originality
Moderate. The key novelty is adapting Bayesian rose-tree ideas to relational blockmodel likelihoods with an efficient DP and an agglomerative, locally scored merge scheme that supports generalized (non-binary) trees. Prior hierarchical models for networks exist but are typically more expensive or restricted; the proposed combination yields a notably fast, fully probabilistic approach.

Significance
Practical significance is good: a simple, scalable probabilistic method for hierarchical community discovery with competitive predictive performance and clear interpretability is valuable. The theoretical novelty is incremental but well executed. With stronger empirical validation and comparisons, impact would be higher.

Constructive suggestions
- Add synthetic experiments with known hierarchical SBMs to quantify structure recovery (e.g., ARI/NMI for flat cuts and tree edit distances).
- Include at least one hierarchical baseline (even if approximate or limited scale), or compare to hierarchical random graphs on smaller subsets.
- Provide hyperparameter sensitivity and, if feasible, empirical Bayes updates or simple grid-search for γ and Beta priors; discuss how γ affects tree branching.
- Analyze the impact of the sparse constraint; show cases where it helps/hurts (e.g., assortative vs disassortative networks).
- Report runtime and memory scaling, including the effect of R restarts; consider strategies to reduce restarts (e.g., informed initialization).
- Clarify “BHCD global” and remove ambiguous statements (e.g., “two orders faster than a single IRM iteration” vs. later runtime plots).
- Release code and scripts to enhance reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled model with an elegant DP marginal likelihood on trees.
- Very fast inference with competitive predictive accuracy versus IRM; interpretable hierarchies.
- Extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data in a useful way.
- Practical variants (sparse, binary) and link prediction procedure.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and lack of hierarchical baselines.
- No hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis.
- Heuristic “sparse” constraint alters the model without theoretical justification.
- Reliance on multiple restarts and potential instability not thoroughly analyzed.

Overall
A solid, well-motivated contribution emphasizing scalability and practicality for hierarchical community discovery. With a stronger empirical section and clearer discussion of limitations, this would be a compelling NeurIPS paper. As is, I view it as borderline accept with revisions focused on evaluation and clarity.
