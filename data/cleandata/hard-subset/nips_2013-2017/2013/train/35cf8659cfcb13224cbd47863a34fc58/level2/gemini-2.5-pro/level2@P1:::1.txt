This paper presents a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to model the network as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). The model builds upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2012), extending it from standard data clustering to relational data. The prior over partitions is restricted to those consistent with a hierarchical tree structure, which allows for the exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood via a dynamic programming recursion. To learn the tree structure itself, the authors propose a greedy agglomerative algorithm that starts with each node as its own community and iteratively merges pairs of subtrees that yield the greatest improvement in the Bayes factor. The primary contribution is a fully probabilistic framework for hierarchical community detection that is computationally very efficient, scaling as O(n^2 log n) in the worst case, and is demonstrated to be two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or better predictive performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Efficiency:** The most significant contribution of this paper is its computational efficiency. Many Bayesian nonparametric models for network analysis, such as the IRM, rely on MCMC sampling, which can be prohibitively slow to converge on large networks. By using a greedy agglomerative search, BHCD offers a practical and scalable alternative. The experimental results in Figure 4 compellingly demonstrate that BHCD provides good predictive results in seconds, whereas the IRM takes thousands of seconds to reach a similar level of performance. This speed-up makes principled Bayesian hierarchical analysis feasible for much larger networks than previously possible.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper presents a novel and clever combination of existing ideas. The extension of the Bayesian Rose Tree framework to relational data using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood is a natural but non-trivial step. The model is technically sound, and the derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) and the local Bayes factor score for merging (Eq. 12) is solid. The caching scheme described to achieve O(1) computation in the inner loop of the algorithm is a key detail that enables the overall O(n^2 log n) complexity.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, builds up the model from the basic SBM, explains the tree-structured prior and likelihood, and details the inference algorithm. The figures are informative, particularly Figure 1 which clearly illustrates the concept of tree-consistent partitions. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

4.  **Practicality:** The introduction of the `BHCD sparse` variant, which restricts merges to connected components, is a thoughtful addition that improves performance on sparse real-world networks, as shown in the experiments. This makes the method even more practical.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The primary limitation of the proposed method is its greedy nature. Agglomerative clustering is known to be susceptible to local optima, and decisions made early in the process cannot be undone. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this approach compared to MCMC methods which can, in principle, explore the full posterior. For example, it would be interesting to know how much variance exists in the final trees across different random restarts.

2.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The main experimental comparison is against the IRM, which discovers a flat, not hierarchical, community structure. While this is a very relevant baseline for speed and flat predictive accuracy, the paper would be stronger if it included comparisons against other Bayesian *hierarchical* community detection methods (e.g., Herlau et al., 2011; Roy & Teh, 2009). While these methods are likely much slower, a qualitative or small-scale quantitative comparison would help to better situate the quality of the hierarchies discovered by BHCD.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values. There is no discussion of how these were chosen or how sensitive the model's performance is to their settings. A sensitivity analysis or a discussion of principled ways to set these hyperparameters would improve the paper's completeness. For example, γ controls the preference for binary vs. non-binary branching, which seems like an important structural parameter.

4.  **Local Merge Assumption:** The merge score is based on a local Bayes factor, which assumes that the decision to merge two communities depends only on the connectivity within and between them. While this locality is crucial for efficiency, it is a strong assumption. A brief discussion of the potential implications or failure modes of this assumption would be welcome.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   A novel and principled Bayesian framework for hierarchical community detection.
*   Extremely efficient inference algorithm, offering a massive speed-up over MCMC-based baselines like the IRM.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating speed and competitive predictive accuracy.
*   The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

**Cons:**
*   The greedy inference approach is susceptible to local optima.
*   Experimental comparisons are primarily against a flat clustering model, not other hierarchical models.
*   Lack of discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on network analysis. The trade-off of a greedy search for a massive gain in computational speed is well-motivated and is likely to be very attractive to practitioners. The proposed BHCD method is elegant, effective, and addresses a clear need for scalable Bayesian network modeling. The weaknesses are largely related to the inherent limitations of the greedy approach, but they do not undermine the paper's core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
