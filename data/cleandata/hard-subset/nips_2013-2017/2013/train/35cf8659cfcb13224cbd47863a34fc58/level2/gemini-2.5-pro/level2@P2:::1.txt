This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The model represents the hierarchy as a tree over the network's vertices, which defines a mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). The paper's core contribution is a fast, greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning this tree structure from data. Starting with each vertex as an individual community (a leaf in a forest), the algorithm iteratively merges the pair of trees that yields the largest increase in the marginal likelihood, calculated via a local Bayes factor. This approach is positioned as a practical and scalable alternative to more computationally intensive MCMC-based methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), building upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and adapting it to relational data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Efficiency and Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is the proposed inference algorithm. By using a greedy agglomerative scheme with careful caching of sufficient statistics, the authors achieve a worst-case time complexity of O(n^2 log n), which is independent of the number of communities. The experimental results compellingly demonstrate this advantage: BHCD is shown to be roughly two orders of magnitude faster than a comparable MCMC-based IRM, achieving similar or better predictive performance in a fraction of the time. This is a significant practical contribution that makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling of networks feasible for much larger datasets than previously possible.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a clear motivation and review of related work, introduces the SBM, and then logically builds up to the hierarchical model in Section 3. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant and the inference procedure (Algorithm 1) is described clearly. The figures effectively illustrate the model structure, the merge operations, and the experimental results.

3.  **Solid Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network show that the method finds sensible and interpretable hierarchies. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS co-authorship data provides strong evidence for the central claim regarding the speed-accuracy trade-off against the IRM. Finally, applying the method to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates its scalability to networks with thousands of nodes.

4.  **Probabilistic Foundation:** Unlike many fast hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., [3, 4]), BHCD is grounded in a fully probabilistic model. This allows for a principled, Bayesian approach to model selection (via marginal likelihood) and provides a clear mechanism for making predictions on held-out data (Section 4.2), which is a valuable feature.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Greedy Inference Limitations:** The main weakness of the proposed method is its greedy nature, which makes it susceptible to local optima. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this approach. For example, it would be interesting to know how the quality of the final hierarchy compares to what an MCMC sampler might find given a much larger computational budget. The current experiments show an advantage in a fixed time window, but not the asymptotic quality of the solution.

2.  **Contextualizing Originality:** The paper builds very heavily on the Bayesian Rose Trees framework of Blundell et al. [14]. The agglomerative algorithm, the three types of merges (join, and two absorptions), and the Bayes factor scoring are adapted directly from that work. The main novelty here is the application of this machinery to relational data using an SBM likelihood. While this is a valuable and non-trivial contribution, the paper could be more explicit about the extent to which it is adapting the framework from [14] to provide a clearer picture of its specific contributions.

3.  **Comparison to Other Methods:** The experimental comparison is focused almost exclusively on the IRM. While the IRM is a key baseline, the introduction mentions other relevant models like LFRM [18] and ILA [10]. The text states that "BHCD performs similarly to LFRM, worse than ILA", but providing a table with the quantitative results (e.g., AUC, Log Predictive Probability) from those papers alongside BHCD's results would provide a more complete and direct comparison, strengthening the evaluation of the method's performance relative to the state of the art.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values. A brief analysis or discussion of the model's sensitivity to these settings would be valuable. For a Bayesian model, understanding the influence of the prior is important for practitioners seeking to apply the method.

5.  **Motivation for the "Sparse" Variant:** The BHCD-sparse variant, which prohibits merges between disconnected components, is shown to work better empirically. However, its justification is heuristic ("does not seem to be a reasonable behaviour"). A more principled motivation for this constraint, perhaps by framing it as an additional prior belief, would make this useful variant feel less ad-hoc.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. It addresses the important problem of hierarchical community detection with a novel method that is both principled and remarkably efficient. The impressive speedup over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods is a major selling point and is well-supported by the experiments. While the greedy nature of the algorithm has its limitations and the relationship to prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering could be clarified, the strengths of the paper—particularly its practical utility and scalability—far outweigh these weaknesses. This work is likely to be influential and widely used by researchers and practitioners working with network data. I recommend acceptance.
