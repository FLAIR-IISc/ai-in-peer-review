

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors develop a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that efficiently learns hierarchical community structure without requiring the number of communities to be known in advance. The key contributions include a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach to hierarchical community detection, a dynamic programming algorithm for efficient inference, and empirical evidence demonstrating that BHCD runs two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or better accuracy.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical notation and consistent presentation of the model. The authors effectively place their work in context by discussing related approaches (IRM, greedy hierarchical clustering, Mondrian process) and highlighting how BHCD differs from these methods. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the experimental section provides appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. The technical soundness of the model and inference procedure, with careful mathematical development of the marginal likelihood computation.
2. The empirical demonstration of significant speed improvements (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) while maintaining competitive accuracy on real-world networks.
3. The clear explanation of how the algorithm avoids the computational bottlenecks of existing Bayesian approaches.
4. The practical implementation details, including the sparse variant that improves performance on sparse networks.

The paper has some notable weaknesses that should be addressed:
1. The paper claims to be "Bayesian nonparametric" but uses fixed hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) rather than placing a prior over these parameters. This is inconsistent with the standard definition of Bayesian nonparametric models and could be clarified.
2. The comparison to IRM is limited - while the paper states BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM," it doesn't provide sufficient context about the time required for IRM to converge to a reasonable solution. The paper should clarify whether this comparison is fair (e.g., is IRM's single iteration equivalent to BHCD's full run?).
3. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations. For example, the stochastic blockmodel inherently assumes non-overlapping communities, which limits the applicability to networks where overlapping communities are expected. The authors should acknowledge this constraint.
4. The experimental section on NIPS-234 shows BHCD outperforms IRM after 10 seconds, but doesn't provide enough detail about how many iterations IRM needed to reach comparable performance. The paper should include more information about the convergence behavior of IRM for fair comparison.
5. The paper claims the algorithm scales "quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but independent of the number of nested communities," but doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for the independence from the number of communities.

The significance of the work is moderate to high. Hierarchical community detection is an important problem in social network analysis, and the proposed method offers a practical solution that could enable analysis of larger networks than previously possible. The speedup over existing Bayesian methods is substantial and well-demonstrated. However, the paper doesn't clearly establish whether the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD provides insights that are fundamentally different from those obtained through other methods, which would strengthen the significance claim.

The paper's main contribution appears to be the efficient inference algorithm rather than a novel model structure. While the algorithm is well-designed and the speed improvements are significant, the model itself builds directly on existing stochastic blockmodel and hierarchical clustering work. The authors should more clearly distinguish between the novelty of the algorithm versus the novelty of the model.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper with a technically sound approach that makes an important contribution to the practical application of hierarchical community detection. The empirical results are compelling, and the algorithm is likely to be useful for researchers working with large social networks. The paper would benefit from clarifying the "Bayesian nonparametric" claim, providing a more complete comparison to IRM, and more thoroughly discussing the limitations of the approach. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
