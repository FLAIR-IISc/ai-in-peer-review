

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) for identifying hierarchical community structure in social networks. The main claims are that BHCD efficiently learns hierarchical community structure through greedy agglomerative model selection, scales quadratically in network size (independent of community count), and runs two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable accuracy.

The paper makes a strong contribution by extending Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data, addressing a gap in the literature where existing Bayesian approaches either scale poorly or don't yield full probabilistic accounts. The theoretical foundation builds appropriately on stochastic blockmodels and tree-structured partitions, with the marginal likelihood computation using dynamic programming to achieve O(n+m) time complexity. The authors provide a clear algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates the method's effectiveness on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data. The runtime comparisons with IRM are compelling, showing BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance in seconds compared to IRM's minutes. The sparse variant is particularly well-motivated for real-world networks. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the potential to merge disconnected components (addressed by the sparse variant) and the binary vs. non-binary tree tradeoff.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical development, clear experimental comparison to relevant baselines, and practical runtime improvements. The paper effectively connects to prior work on stochastic blockmodels, IRM, and hierarchical clustering while demonstrating meaningful improvements.

A few areas for improvement: The paper could better clarify why the tree structure is better suited than flat community models for their application domains. The predictive performance comparison with other state-of-the-art methods (beyond IRM) could be more extensive. The theoretical justification for the specific hyperparameter choices (α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, γ=0.4) is limited.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in network analysis with a technically sound and practically useful approach. The method's efficiency makes it particularly valuable for large-scale applications where existing Bayesian hierarchical models become computationally prohibitive. The paper meets the quality, clarity, and significance criteria for NIPS, and I recommend acceptance.
