This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The model is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, and the authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that can learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model in a single pass through the data.

The main ideas of the paper are well-motivated and clearly related to previous work on community discovery in social networks. The authors provide a thorough review of existing methods, including stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership stochastic blockmodels, and Bayesian approaches to inferring community structure. They also highlight the limitations of these methods, such as the need to know the number of flat communities in advance, and the computational cost of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.

The technical soundness of the paper is good. The authors provide a clear and concise description of their model, including the prior on partitions, the likelihood of the adjacency matrix, and the recursive formula for the marginal likelihood of the generative process. They also provide a detailed description of their agglomerative model selection algorithm, including the Bayes factor score and the caching scheme used to improve computational efficiency.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a good balance of technical detail and high-level intuition, making the paper accessible to a wide range of readers. The figures and tables are also well-organized and help to illustrate the key concepts and results.

The originality of the paper is high. The authors propose a novel model and algorithm for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, which is distinct from existing methods. The use of a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels is a new and interesting approach, and the authors provide a clear motivation for this choice.

The significance of the paper is also high. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on several real-world data sets, including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network. They also show that their method is two orders of magnitude faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, while achieving comparable or better accuracy. This makes the method a useful tool for practitioners and researchers working with large social networks.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original model and algorithm for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks.
* The method is shown to be effective on several real-world data sets, and is two orders of magnitude faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.
* The technical soundness of the paper is good, with a clear and concise description of the model and algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper may benefit from additional experiments on larger data sets, to further demonstrate the scalability of the method.
* The authors may want to consider additional evaluation metrics, such as precision and recall, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their method.
* The paper assumes a specific prior on partitions, which may not be suitable for all data sets. The authors may want to consider alternative priors or a more robust approach to model selection.
