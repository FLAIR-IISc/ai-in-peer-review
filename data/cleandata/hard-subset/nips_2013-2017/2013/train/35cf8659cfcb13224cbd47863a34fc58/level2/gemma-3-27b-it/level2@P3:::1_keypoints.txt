- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric, tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm.  
- Aims to balance probabilistic rigor and computational scalability, addressing limitations of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).  
- Builds on established concepts (stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering), yielding an interpretable framework.  
- Clearly identifies a gap: lack of scalable Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection.  
- Shows significant speedup (≈ two orders of magnitude) over IRM on the NIPS co-authorship network with comparable or better accuracy.  
- Qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network demonstrate recovery of meaningful community structures.  
- Provides algorithmic scalability analysis (O(n^2 log n)), a notable contribution.  
- Introduces practical variations (e.g., sparse BHCD) and considers predictive performance, increasing practical relevance.  
- Presentation clarity is weak: dense mathematical notation and insufficient intuitive explanations.  
- The link between equation (7) and the prior on partitions is unclear and needs elaboration.  
- Merging criteria in Algorithm 1 are underspecified and require more detailed explanation.  
- Hyperparameter choices (α, δ, β, λ, γ) are given without justification; a sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- It’s unclear why BHCD outperforms IRM beyond computational efficiency; discussion of model assumptions and performance impact is needed.  
- Experimental comparisons are limited (primarily to IRM); should include other state-of-the-art community detection methods.  
- Claim of similar predictive performance to LFRM and ILA lacks presented supporting numbers.  
- Visualization of the full hierarchical tree for the NIPS dataset is absent and would aid interpretation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, scalable approach addressing an important problem with strong speedups and theoretical insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: presentation issues, unjustified parameters, limited experimental comparisons, and insufficient analysis of limitations.
