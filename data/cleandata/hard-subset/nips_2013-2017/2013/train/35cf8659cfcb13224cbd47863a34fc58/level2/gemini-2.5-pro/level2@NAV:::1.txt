This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for efficiently inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to model the data as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and learn this structure using a greedy agglomerative algorithm. This work builds upon previous research in Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) and extends it to relational data, positioning itself as a practical alternative to computationally intensive MCMC-based methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and more principled than non-probabilistic greedy methods.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its successful combination of a principled probabilistic framework with a highly efficient inference algorithm. This addresses a significant gap in the literature between slow but rigorous Bayesian models and fast but heuristic clustering approaches. The claims of efficiency and accuracy are convincingly supported by strong experimental results. The comparison against the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset is particularly effective, demonstrating a speedup of two orders of magnitude for comparable predictive performance. This is a significant practical contribution. The qualitative results on the Sampson's Monastery network and the full NIPS dataset effectively illustrate the model's ability to uncover interpretable, non-binary hierarchical structures. The paper is clearly written, the model is well-motivated, and the recursive formulation for the marginal likelihood is elegant.

**Weaknesses:**
The main limitation is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. While the authors pragmatically address this with multiple random restarts, the algorithm is susceptible to local optima. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between this greedy approach and global inference methods (like MCMC) in terms of solution quality, beyond just wall-clock time, would strengthen the paper. Secondly, while the proposed `BHCD sparse` variant is shown to be empirically faster on sparse networks, the worst-case time complexity remains O(n² log n). Given that the likelihood can be computed in O(n+m), this suggests a bottleneck in the inference algorithm for the large, sparse networks common in many real-world applications. A brief discussion on the challenges or potential avenues for developing a near-linear time inference algorithm for sparse graphs would be a valuable addition.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution. The method is elegant, the results are strong, and it provides a valuable tool for network analysis. The weaknesses are inherent to the greedy approach chosen but do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The work is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
