- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model that models networks as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community discovery.  
- Uses a tree-consistent prior enabling exact, efficient marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming (Eq. 10).  
- Learns tree structure with a greedy agglomerative algorithm that merges subtree pairs by maximizing a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12).  
- Achieves worst-case O(n^2 log n) complexity with an O(1) cached inner loop, making inference very efficient.  
- Demonstrates empirical speed-ups of ~100x over the IRM while matching or improving predictive performance.  
- Significance: makes principled Bayesian hierarchical analysis practical for much larger networks than MCMC-based methods.  
- Originality/technical quality: novel extension of Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data with sound derivations and a key caching optimization.  
- Clarity: paper is well written, organized, and accompanied by informative figures (e.g., tree-consistent partitions).  
- Practicality: proposes a BHCD-sparse variant restricting merges to connected components, improving performance on sparse networks.  
- Limitation—greedy inference: agglomerative approach is prone to local optima and early irreversible decisions; multiple restarts are used but variance across restarts is not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Limitation—experimental comparisons: main baseline is the flat IRM; lacks comparisons to other hierarchical Bayesian community methods to situate hierarchy quality.  
- Limitation—hyperparameters: several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed with no sensitivity analysis or principled selection guidance.  
- Limitation—local merge assumption: merge decisions rely on local Bayes factors that ignore broader tree context, a strong assumption with potential failure modes.  
- Overall assessment: elegant, effective, and highly efficient method that trades greedy search for scalability; reviewer recommends acceptance.
