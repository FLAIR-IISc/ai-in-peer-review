

The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for learning hierarchical community structure in social networks via a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. It extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data, leveraging dynamic programming and caching to achieve O(n² log n) time complexity—significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which scales as O(n²K²) where K is the number of communities. The work claims to be the first to provide a fully probabilistic hierarchical model with computational efficiency independent of community count, validated on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data.

Strengths include a technically sound Bayesian framework that properly integrates prior knowledge (e.g., Beta priors with hyperparameters tuned for within-community edge density). The dynamic programming approach for marginal likelihood computation (Equation 10) is well-justified and enables the claimed scalability. Empirical results demonstrate BHCD’s speed advantage: on the NIPS-234 dataset, sparse BHCD achieves predictive accuracy comparable to IRM after 10 seconds versus IRM’s 1000 seconds. The hierarchical structure discovered on the full NIPS network (Figure 5) aligns with domain expectations, and the predictive model (Equation 14) is elegantly derived. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work: unlike IRM extensions [8–11] that require iterative inference or limit hierarchy depth, BHCD uses a one-pass greedy algorithm. The comparison to IRM and non-Bayesian hierarchical methods [3,4] in Table 1 is appropriate, though the latter are not directly benchmarked against in experiments.

Key weaknesses require addressing. First, the claim that BHCD’s complexity is "independent of the number of nested communities" is misleading. While the algorithm avoids explicit K-dependence in its worst-case complexity, the tree structure itself may contain O(n) communities (e.g., a chain), and the caching scheme’s O(n² log n) complexity scales with vertices, not communities. The paper should clarify this distinction. Second, the experimental section omits comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical methods (e.g., [3,4]), which are computationally efficient and could provide context for BHCD’s speed claims. For instance, [4] scales linearly in edges for sparse networks—how does BHCD’s sparse variant compare? Third, the tie-breaking strategy in Algorithm 1 (Section 4.1) is inadequately justified; random tie-breaking risks inconsistent results, and the paper does not analyze its impact on hierarchy quality or stability. The use of 50 restarts to select the highest-likelihood tree is pragmatic but should be evaluated against deterministic alternatives (e.g., merging smallest clusters first).

The significance is strong for practitioners needing scalable hierarchical community discovery, particularly for dense networks where IRM’s O(n²K²) cost becomes prohibitive. However, the predictive accuracy (Figure 4) is comparable to IRM but not superior to non-Bayesian methods like LFRM [18] or ILA [10], which is not discussed in the context of the speed-accuracy tradeoff. The paper should clarify whether BHCD’s speed advantage makes it preferable despite slightly lower accuracy. The supplementary material reference is insufficient; a brief mention of key supplementary results (e.g., stability analysis of tie-breaking) would strengthen reproducibility.

Recommended revisions: 
1. Clarify the community-count independence claim (e.g., "complexity scales with vertices, not with the number of communities in the hierarchy, though the hierarchy depth may grow with vertices").
2. Add a comparative experiment against [3,4] on speed/accuracy for hierarchical community detection, focusing on sparse networks where BHCD sparse is claimed to excel.
3. Justify the tie-breaking strategy or replace it with a deterministic rule (e.g., merge smallest clusters first), and report stability metrics across restarts.
4. Discuss the tradeoff between BHCD’s speed and slightly lower predictive accuracy versus non-Bayesian methods, emphasizing when BHCD’s probabilistic output justifies the tradeoff.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to scalable Bayesian hierarchical modeling, with clear technical merit. Addressing the clarity gaps and experimental omissions would significantly strengthen its impact. The core innovation—greedy agglomeration with caching for exact marginal likelihood—effectively bridges the gap between computational efficiency and probabilistic rigor, offering a practical tool for large-scale network analysis.
