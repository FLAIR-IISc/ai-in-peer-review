Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The key idea is to define a prior over partitions that are consistent with a given tree and to compute exactly the marginal likelihood of the data given that tree by summing over all tree-consistent partitions via dynamic programming. The likelihood is a mixture of stochastic blockmodels, with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli components that distinguish within- and between-community blocks via different hyperparameters. Learning proceeds by greedy agglomerative model selection (in the spirit of Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees) using local Bayes factors and carefully cached sufficient statistics; the algorithm considers join and absorption merges and runs in O(n^2 log n) per run, independent of the number of communities, with a practical “sparse” variant that forbids merges between completely unconnected subtrees.

The work builds on BHC/Bayesian Rose Trees [Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010] by adapting the dynamic program and merge scoring to relational data via blockmodel marginals. It is positioned against IRM and its hierarchical extensions, LFRM, ILA, the Mondrian process, and multiscale/hierarchical blockmodels. Empirically, BHCD is much faster than IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on a NIPS co-authorship subset; it also yields interpretable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery network and the full NIPS graph.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: a clean mixture-over-partitions view anchored to a tree, enabling exact marginalization over partitions consistent with that tree.
- Efficient inference: careful use of local Bayes factors and cached sufficient statistics makes the agglomerative search practical; the “sparse” variant is sensible for real-world sparse graphs.
- Practicality: predictions are derived by a simple top-down recursion along the tree; wall-clock improvements over IRM are substantial.
- Relation to prior work: clear connections to BHC/Rose Trees and IRM, and discussion of other hierarchical relational models.
- Interpretability: the resulting non-binary hierarchies are easier to read than binary trees, and the paper shows meaningful qualitative results.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Model expressiveness: all within-community blocks share the same Beta prior (α,β) and all between-community blocks share (δ,λ). This global tying may be too restrictive for heterogeneous networks (e.g., communities of varying densities or degree-heterogeneity). Discussion acknowledges non-conjugate/triangular sufficient statistics as future work, but even a conjugate degree-corrected variant would be informative.
- Hyperparameters: α,β,δ,λ and γ are fixed without data-driven inference or sensitivity analysis. Empirical robustness or simple empirical Bayes would strengthen the story.
- Greedy search: like other agglomerative methods, BHCD can get trapped by early merges. The paper uses R restarts, but quantitative analysis of stability across restarts, or diagnostics on variability of trees, would be valuable.
- Locality assumption in scoring: the merge score only uses data among leaves of the candidate merge, potentially ignoring informative structure with the rest of the graph. Some ablation or comparison to a (more expensive) globally rescored variant would clarify the trade-off.
- Experimental breadth: predictive comparisons are primarily to IRM, with references to LFRM and ILA via prior folds. Including direct side-by-side results (or at least a reproduced baseline from the authors’ code) and additional datasets would strengthen claims. Reporting variance/error bars would help assess significance.
- Minor clarity: “BHCD global” is mentioned in the Sampson discussion without prior definition. Also, Section 4.2 states predictive complexity as Θ(n); clarifying that it is linear in tree depth (worst-case O(n)) would avoid confusion.

Quality:
- The modeling and dynamic program are technically sound and well-motivated by prior BHC/BRT work. The merge scoring and caching scheme appear correct; the complexity claims are plausible. The main caveat is the global hyperparameter tying and the greedy search’s potential myopia, both acknowledged.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, with clear equations and a helpful high-level picture of how the tree induces partitions. Including explicit pseudocode for Algorithm 1 (even compactly) would improve reproducibility. Define all variants used in figures.

Originality:
- While rooted in BHC/BRT, the adaptation to relational blockmodels with exact partition marginalization over a tree is novel and useful. The merge designs (join/absorption) follow BRT; the contribution here is the relational likelihood and efficient sufficient-statistics caching.

Significance:
- The speed-accuracy trade-off is compelling for large networks, and the hierarchical outputs are interpretable. This is a practical addition to the toolbox, even if not always state-of-the-art in prediction versus richer models like ILA.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add empirical Bayes or simple hyperparameter inference for α,β,δ,λ and γ, or at least a sensitivity study.
- Compare directly to at least one hierarchical relational baseline beyond IRM (e.g., Hierarchical IRM, multiscale community blockmodel) and report uncertainty (std/CI).
- Evaluate stability across restarts (e.g., tree distance metrics) and the effect of the sparse constraint.
- Consider a degree-corrected conjugate variant to mitigate heterogeneity.
- Clarify “BHCD global,” define all variants in the main text, and state predictive complexity as a function of depth.
- Cite additional related work on hierarchical random graphs (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman 2008).

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Elegant, principled formulation with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Significant empirical speed gains with competitive accuracy.
- Clear connection to and extension of established Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods.
- Interpretable hierarchical structures on real data.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited expressiveness due to globally shared priors; no hyperparameter learning.
- Greedy search with local scoring may be brittle; limited analysis of stability.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader and more rigorous.

Overall assessment:
A solid and practical contribution that marries Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas with stochastic blockmodels for scalable hierarchical community discovery. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying variants, expanding comparisons, and adding basic hyperparameter sensitivity; the method’s simplicity and efficiency are valuable.
