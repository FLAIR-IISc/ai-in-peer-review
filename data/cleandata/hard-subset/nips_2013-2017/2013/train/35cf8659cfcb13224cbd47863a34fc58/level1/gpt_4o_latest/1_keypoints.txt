- Presents BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric, tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community detection.
- Introduces a greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm with worst-case O(n² log n) inference complexity, independent of the number of nested communities.
- Proposes a sparse BHCD variant to improve performance on sparse networks.
- Empirically, BHCD is ~100x faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or superior accuracy on real datasets.
- Technically sound: clear probabilistic model, conjugate priors, and detailed inference derivations.
- Efficient: notable computational advantages and scalable likelihood computation (O(n + m)).
- Empirically validated on real-world networks (e.g., Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship) showing meaningful hierarchical discovery and accurate predictions.
- Clear and well-organized presentation, including useful model variations (sparse, binary-tree).
- Significant: advances scalable Bayesian nonparametric modeling with broad applicability in network science.
- Limited originality: builds heavily on stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering; main novelty is the inference algorithm.
- Narrow comparisons: primarily compared to IRM, lacking evaluation against other state-of-the-art methods like LFRM or ILA.
- Interpretability concern: binary-only variants produce less interpretable hierarchies, limiting practical utility in some applications.
- Scalability caveat: sparse variant helps but worst-case inference complexity remains O(n² log n), posing limits for extremely large sparse graphs.
- Arguments for acceptance: substantial computational improvement, strong empirical results, and generalizable method.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited methodological originality and insufficient comparative baselines.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to include broader comparisons and address interpretability issues.
