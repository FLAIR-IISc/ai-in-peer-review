### **Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"**

**Summary**
This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to model the network as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). The model defines a marginal likelihood for the observed network data given a specific hierarchical tree structure. This marginal likelihood is computed efficiently via a dynamic programming recursion (Equation 10) by summing over all partitions consistent with the given tree. This approach builds directly on prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering for non-relational data, specifically the Bayesian Rose Trees model [14], and extends it to the domain of network analysis.

For inference, the paper eschews computationally expensive MCMC or variational methods in favor of a fast, greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm. Starting with each node in its own tree, the algorithm iteratively merges the pair of trees that yields the largest improvement in the Bayes factor. The paper introduces a novel caching scheme for the SBM sufficient statistics, allowing the algorithm to achieve a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), which is independent of the number of communities. The method is evaluated on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset, demonstrating that it is orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable predictive accuracy.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood appears sound, representing a clever application of the sum-product algorithm over a tree structure. The claims of computational complexity are well-supported by the algorithmic description and the novel caching scheme for sufficient statistics.

The experimental evaluation is solid. The qualitative results on the Sampson monastery network are convincing and align well with established sociological interpretations. The quantitative comparison against the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship data is particularly strong, clearly demonstrating the primary advantage of the proposed method: speed. The authors are honest about the trade-offs, noting that their simpler model and greedy inference are designed for speed rather than necessarily outperforming more complex models like the Infinite Latent Attributes (ILA) model in terms of absolute predictive accuracy.

One weakness is the inherent limitation of the greedy search procedure, which is prone to finding local optima. The authors mitigate this by performing multiple restarts, but the dependence on the sequence of local greedy choices remains. Another point for consideration is the selection of hyperparameters, which are set to fixed values that "work well." An analysis of the model's sensitivity to these hyperparameters would have strengthened the paper.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition, starting from the basic SBM (Section 2) before introducing their hierarchical extension (Section 3). The core recursion for the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) is presented concisely. The use of figures to illustrate the tree-consistent partitions (Figure 1) and merge operations (Figure 2) is effective. Algorithm 1 is presented clearly, and the explanation of the sufficient statistic updates helps the reader understand how the O(n² log n) complexity is achieved. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly and are easy to interpret. A minor point of confusion could be the details of the caching mechanism, which are crucial for the runtime claims but are described somewhat briefly. A more detailed exposition, perhaps in an appendix, would be beneficial for reproducibility.

**Originality**
The work is original in its synthesis of ideas. While greedy agglomerative clustering for networks is not new, and the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework has been explored before [13, 14], this paper is the first to combine them in this specific way for network community detection. It successfully translates the "Bayesian Rose Tree" model to relational data, providing a principled, fully Bayesian justification for a fast, greedy algorithm. This stands in contrast to many heuristic hierarchical clustering methods that lack a clear objective function, and it provides a significant speed advantage over more traditional Bayesian nonparametric approaches like hierarchical extensions of the IRM that rely on MCMC. The development of the O(1) update for SBM sufficient statistics within the greedy search framework is also a novel and important technical contribution.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is high, primarily due to its practical implications. The paper addresses a critical need in network analysis: scalable algorithms for discovering hierarchical structure. Many existing Bayesian methods are too slow for even moderately sized networks. By demonstrating a method that is two orders ofmagnitude faster than a widely-used baseline (IRM) while maintaining competitive accuracy, the authors provide a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. The fact that the runtime is independent of the number of inferred communities is a major advantage over methods whose complexity scales with K. This work is likely to be influential and built upon, particularly in exploring the trade-offs between inference speed and optimality in Bayesian modeling of network structures. It opens up avenues for applying similar greedy model selection approaches to other complex probabilistic models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant application of Bayesian hierarchical clustering models to network data.
*   The proposed inference algorithm is exceptionally fast and scalable (O(n² log n)), addressing a major bottleneck in the field.
*   Provides a full probabilistic account of the hierarchical structure, unlike many ad-hoc methods.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrate a compelling speed-accuracy trade-off compared to the state-of-the-art IRM.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly positions its contribution relative to prior work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The inference is based on a greedy search, which is not guaranteed to find the optimal hierarchy.
*   The core modeling ideas are an extension of existing work [14] to a new data type, which could be viewed as an incremental contribution.
*   The empirically superior "sparse" variant introduces a heuristic rule (no merges between disconnected components) that slightly detracts from the fully Bayesian framing.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a principled, highly efficient, and effective method for a significant and challenging problem. The contribution is both technically novel and practically very useful.
