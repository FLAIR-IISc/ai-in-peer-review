Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a tree over vertices; each internal node corresponds to a candidate community. Conditioned on a tree, the marginal likelihood integrates over a mixture of all partitions consistent with that tree, with a prior p(φ|T) induced by a top-down stopping process with node-dependent stopping probability πS and a likelihood that uses f for within-cluster edges and g for inter-cluster edges. With conjugate Beta–Bernoulli models, the tree marginal likelihood is computed exactly via a dynamic programming recursion in time linear in the size of the tree/adjacency (O(n + m)). Learning T is done by a greedy agglomerative procedure that considers three local merge operations (join and two absorptions) and scores candidate merges by a local Bayes factor; with appropriate caching the algorithm runs in O(n^2 log n) per restart. A sparse variant disallows merging subtrees with zero inter-edges. Predictive distributions for missing edges are obtained by a simple top-down recursion along the learned tree.

BHCD is positioned between flat Bayesian models such as the IRM and hierarchical models that explicitly parameterize edge probabilities at internal tree nodes. It builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees, extending them to relational data with blockmodel likelihoods. Relative to the IRM and its hierarchical variants, it targets scalability via greedily constructing the tree and exact marginalization given a fixed tree. Compared to existing hierarchical network models (e.g., hierarchical random graphs of Clauset, Moore, and Newman, 2008; and hierarchical IRM-like models), BHCD uses a tree-induced mixture over partitions rather than per-node parameters at internal nodes; this is an interesting modeling distinction.

Strengths:
- Clear modeling idea: a tree-constrained mixture over blockmodel partitions, with an exact, efficient dynamic program for the marginal likelihood given T.
- Practical, scalable inference: local Bayes factor–based merges with O(1) updates via cached sufficient statistics; overall O(n^2 log n) per run and good wall-clock results.
- Sensible predictive mechanism and a sparse variant that better respects connectivity and accelerates inference on sparse graphs.
- Empirical results suggest favorable speed–accuracy trade-offs versus IRM, and interpretable hierarchies on Sampson and NIPS coauthorship data.

Weaknesses and technical concerns:
- Modeling limitations: The likelihood uses a standard (assortative) SBM. This ignores degree heterogeneity, which is known to bias community inference (degree-corrected SBM, Karrer & Newman, 2011). It also cannot capture disassortative structure or overlaps (mixed membership), and the prior favors assortative communities through α > β and δ < λ. The impact on datasets with hubs or bipartite structure is not assessed.
- Greedy search and locality: The merge score is strictly local to the candidate merged subtree. While this enables efficiency, it can lock in suboptimal early merges and precludes global corrections. The paper uses restarts to mitigate this, but there is no post-hoc refinement (e.g., subtree reattachments or split–merge moves) nor a theoretical guarantee. Tie-breaking randomness can induce variability; the variance across runs and the benefit of R = 50 restarts are not quantified.
- Evaluation and baselines: The comparison is mostly to IRM (MCMC and greedy); results are contextualized with LFRM and ILA via shared folds, but no direct head-to-head on identical hardware/code is shown. Hierarchical baselines such as hierarchical random graphs (Clauset et al., 2008) are not compared. Synthetic experiments with known hierarchical ground-truth would strengthen the case for structural recovery.
- Claims of speed: The abstract claims “two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model,” and the text later states “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM,” which is confusing. A “single iteration” is a low bar; please clarify what is meant (e.g., a full Gibbs sweep) and report absolute times and hardware. Memory costs for the O(n^2) heap and sufficient statistics should be reported.
- Hyperparameters: α, β, δ, λ, γ are fixed a priori. No sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference is provided. Because γ controls the preference for bushy vs. deep trees (via πS), and α/β, δ/λ encode assortativity strength, predictive performance and recovered structure could change markedly.
- Scope: Although the framework mentions general exponential-family f and g, experiments are only on undirected binary graphs without self-edges. Directed, weighted, or signed networks would expand applicability; the Sampson handling of negative ties as absent edges discards information.

Quality:
The methodology is sound and carefully constructed. The recursion for p(D|T) is correct and efficiently implemented; the agglomerative search is well-specified with appropriate caching. However, the empirical evaluation is limited in baselines and ablations, and the impact of modeling choices (no degree correction, fixed hyperparameters) is not explored. Some claims about speed need clearer, quantitative support.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The exposition of the prior over tree-consistent partitions and the dynamic program is clear. It would help to:
- Explicitly derive the posterior predictive forms f(·|σ), g(·|σ) used in Eq. (14).
- Provide pseudocode for computing σ¬chSS and for the dynamic program.
- Clarify the “two orders of magnitude” claim and define an “iteration” for IRM.

Originality:
The combination of a tree-induced mixture over partitions with an exact marginal likelihood and a fast, local Bayes factor–driven agglomerative search for relational data is novel and practical. The work builds upon Bayesian Rose Trees and blockmodels in a nontrivial way.

Significance:
For practitioners needing fast, interpretable hierarchical summaries and reasonable link predictions, BHCD is useful. The scalability and simplicity are compelling. However, omitting degree correction and broader baselines limits the immediate impact, especially given known pitfalls of vanilla SBMs on real networks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, efficient model and inference; exact marginalization given a tree.
- Strong speed–accuracy trade-offs; practical algorithm with open-source-friendly implementation.
- Clear, interpretable hierarchies on real datasets.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited evaluation: few baselines, no synthetic recovery tests, no hierarchical baselines.
- Modeling limitations (no degree correction, assortativity bias) not addressed empirically.
- Greedy search without refinement; variability across restarts unquantified.
- Ambiguity in speed claims and lack of absolute runtime/memory reporting.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add degree-correction (e.g., node propensities) to f and g, or at least perform sensitivity analysis to degree heterogeneity.
- Include hierarchical baselines (e.g., hierarchical random graphs) and synthetic benchmarks with known hierarchical ground truth.
- Report absolute timings, memory, and variability across restarts; clarify IRM iteration comparisons.
- Provide hyperparameter sensitivity and, if possible, simple hyperparameter inference (e.g., empirical Bayes for γ, α/β, δ/λ).
- Add a lightweight post-agglomerative refinement (subtree reattachments) to reduce greediness.
- Explore directed/weighted extensions to demonstrate generality.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The methodological contribution is neat and potentially impactful due to its scalability and interpretability, but the evaluation should be strengthened and some modeling limitations discussed or mitigated.
