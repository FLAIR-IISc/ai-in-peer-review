- Summary: Proposes BHCD, a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy O(n² log n) agglomerative algorithm that is much faster than IRM while retaining comparable accuracy on tested datasets.

- Strength: Computational efficiency — dynamic programming and cached sufficient statistics enable O(1) inner-loop computation and practical runtime improvements.

- Strength: Clear technical presentation — model, likelihood, recursive formulation, and algorithm are well-defined and accessible.

- Strength: Empirical validation — shows two orders of magnitude speedup over IRM and recovers meaningful structures on Sampson’s network and NIPS co-authorship subset.

- Strength: Practical implementation details — includes a clear computational complexity comparison positioning BHCD relative to other methods.

- Weakness: Novelty claim overstated — method closely builds on Heller & Ghahramani [14], with main novelties being application to relational data and caching; differentiation from [14] is insufficient.

- Weakness: Limited experimental scope — evaluation on only two datasets (small/medium) limits claims of generalizability to diverse or large sparse/directed networks.

- Weakness: Theoretical limitations — lacks rigorous guarantees on solution quality; unclear how greedy approach compares to optimal hierarchical clustering.

- Weakness: Incomplete comparisons — misses several relevant recent hierarchical community-detection works (e.g., Zhou et al. NIPS 2012, Wang et al.).

- Overall assessment: Valuable, scalable contribution with solid technical and empirical support, but novelty and evaluation breadth need strengthening.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses scalability, provides a clear and efficient algorithm with strong empirical speed gains, and is well-written.

- Arguments against acceptance: Novelty concerns, narrow experiments, lack of theoretical guarantees, and unclear handling of directed networks.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to better articulate novelty, compare with recent methods, expand experiments to more network types, and discuss limitations (e.g., directed networks).
