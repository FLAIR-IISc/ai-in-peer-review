

# Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. Building upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering [14] and extending it to relational data, BHCD models communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key innovation is an efficient greedy agglomerative algorithm that learns the hierarchical community structure in O(n² log n) time, where n is the number of vertices. This represents a significant improvement over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which requires many iterations to converge. The authors demonstrate BHCD's effectiveness on Sampson's monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, showing it achieves comparable accuracy to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster.

## Strengths
1. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper's main contribution is a well-motivated, efficient inference algorithm. The dynamic programming approach with caching of sufficient statistics enables O(1) computation per inner loop, resulting in practical runtime improvements. This addresses a critical limitation in existing Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods.

2. **Clear Technical Presentation**: The paper provides a thorough mathematical foundation, clearly defining the model, likelihood, and inference algorithm. The recursive formulation (equation 10) and algorithm description (Algorithm 1) are well-structured and accessible.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments demonstrate BHCD's speed advantage over IRM (two orders of magnitude faster) while maintaining competitive accuracy. The qualitative analysis on Sampson's monastery network shows the method recovers meaningful community structures.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity (Table 1) that clearly positions BHCD against other methods, helping practitioners understand its advantages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty Claim**: The paper's novelty is somewhat overstated. BHCD builds directly on Heller and Ghahramani's Bayesian hierarchical clustering [14], with the key innovation being the application to relational data and the caching scheme. The authors should more clearly differentiate their contribution from [14] and acknowledge that the algorithm structure is similar.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to two datasets (Sampson's monastery and a subset of NIPS co-authorship), which may not sufficiently demonstrate generalizability. More comprehensive testing on diverse network types (e.g., directed networks, larger sparse networks) would strengthen the claims.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: While the paper provides asymptotic complexity bounds, it lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees about the quality of the hierarchical structure discovered. It's unclear how the greedy approach compares to optimal hierarchical clustering in terms of solution quality.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare with several relevant recent works on hierarchical community detection, such as Zhou et al.'s "Hierarchical Bayesian Models for Community Detection" (NIPS 2012) or the recent work by Wang et al. on scalable hierarchical clustering.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection by addressing the computational scalability of Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. The BHCD algorithm is well-designed, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The computational efficiency claims are well-supported by both complexity analysis and experimental results.

However, the novelty is somewhat diminished by the close relationship to previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [14]. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with recent hierarchical community detection methods and a broader experimental evaluation.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Addresses a significant practical limitation in Bayesian hierarchical community detection (computational scalability)
- Provides a clear, efficient algorithm with strong empirical validation
- Well-written with clear technical exposition
- Demonstrates a substantial speed advantage over the current state-of-the-art

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Novelty is somewhat overstated relative to prior work
- Experimental evaluation is limited to two datasets
- Lacks theoretical guarantees about the quality of the hierarchical structure
- Doesn't address how the method handles directed networks, which are common in social network analysis

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to scalable hierarchical community detection, and the technical merits outweigh the limitations. The authors should:
1. More clearly articulate the novelty relative to [14]
2. Add a comparison with recent hierarchical community detection methods
3. Expand the experimental evaluation to include additional network types
4. Address the limitations of the approach (e.g., handling directed networks)

This paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS, particularly given the increasing importance of scalable network analysis methods. The authors have made a significant step toward making Bayesian hierarchical community detection practical for large networks.
