- Proposes BHCD: a tree-structured Bayesian nonparametric mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm that learns hierarchies in a single pass and scales O(n² log n).  
- Achieves significant speedups: two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining comparable or better accuracy; sparse BHCD variants reach IRM-like performance quickly.  
- Extends prior work: generalizes IRM to hierarchical structure, avoids costly Bayesian inference like Mondrian, and gives a full probabilistic treatment for relational data.  
- Technical soundness: clear model equations, a justified Algorithm 1, caching scheme, and complexity analysis with empirical validation on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS coauthorship datasets.  
- Clear presentation: well-organized writing, consistent notation, and effective illustrative figures.  
- Limited baseline comparison: lacks evaluation against more recent post-2013 nonparametric methods (e.g., later IRM extensions, LFRM).  
- Disconnected components risk: BHCD can merge disconnected communities and BHCD sparse is only a partial mitigation; needs a more robust solution or discussion.  
- Missing theoretical guarantees: no proofs or detailed analysis of greedy algorithm optimality or convergence; should discuss when it may fail.  
- Pro acceptance arguments: practical, scalable, well-executed approach that makes Bayesian hierarchical community detection feasible for larger networks.  
- Con acceptance arguments: incomplete comparison to recent methods, unresolved handling of disconnected components, and limited theoretical understanding of the greedy method.  
- Overall: strong, significant contribution with solid empirical/theoretical support; recommendation to accept.
