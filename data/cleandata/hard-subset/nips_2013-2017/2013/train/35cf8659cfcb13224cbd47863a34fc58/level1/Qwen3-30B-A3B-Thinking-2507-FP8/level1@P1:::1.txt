

# Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary of Main Ideas

The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors model communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where each internal node represents a community containing the vertices of its subtree. The key innovation is a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that learns the hierarchical structure in a single pass through the data. The authors demonstrate that their method scales quadratically in the number of vertices (O(n² log n)) and independently of the number of nested communities, making it significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The paper builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches but extends them to relational data, addressing a gap in the literature where previous Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection either scale poorly or require known hierarchy depth.

The work relates to several key NIPS papers: the stochastic blockmodel (Holland et al., 1983), the Infinite Relational Model (Kemp et al., 2006), and Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005). Unlike previous hierarchical extensions of the IRM (e.g., [8]), which require expensive MCMC inference, BHCD uses a greedy approach with a novel caching scheme for efficient computation. The paper also distinguishes itself from flat community detection methods (e.g., [1, 2]) by explicitly modeling hierarchical structure.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound model with rigorous theoretical foundations. The authors derive the marginal likelihood for the tree-structured model using dynamic programming, which enables efficient computation. The algorithmic details are well-specified, and the theoretical complexity analysis (O(n² log n)) is clear and well-supported. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's efficiency and accuracy advantages over IRM.

2. **Novelty and Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing a Bayesian nonparametric method for hierarchical community detection that scales efficiently. Unlike previous methods that either require known hierarchy depth ([9, 10]) or have poor computational scaling ([8]), BHCD offers a practical solution for large networks. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant prior art, demonstrating a clear understanding of the field.

3. **Significance**: The speed advantage (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) is highly significant for real-world applications involving large social networks. The method achieves comparable or better accuracy than IRM while being much faster, making it valuable for practitioners. The paper also provides a clear demonstration of the hierarchical structure discovered in the NIPS co-authorship network, which could inform future social network analysis.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and good explanations of the algorithm. The figures (particularly Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the model and results. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including parameter settings and comparison methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods**: While the paper compares BHCD to IRM, it does not compare to other recent hierarchical community detection methods, such as those based on deep learning or more recent Bayesian approaches. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Potential for Merging Disconnected Components**: The paper acknowledges that BHCD may merge disconnected communities if the score is high enough (Section 4.1), which is problematic for real-world networks. While the sparse variant addresses this, it would be helpful to have more detailed analysis of when this issue occurs and how often it affects results.

3. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides empirical evidence of performance but lacks theoretical guarantees about the convergence or optimality of the greedy algorithm. It would be valuable to include some discussion of when the greedy approach might fail to find the optimal tree structure.

4. **Reproducibility Concerns**: The paper mentions using R=50 restarts for tie-breaking, but doesn't provide details on how the random seeds were chosen or whether the results are sensitive to the choice of seeds. Including this information would improve reproducibility.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel approach to hierarchical community detection with significant computational advantages
- Clear theoretical foundations and rigorous experimental validation
- Practical significance for large-scale network analysis
- Well-written paper with thorough comparison to relevant prior work

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Limited comparison with other recent hierarchical community detection methods
- Some concerns about merging disconnected components
- Lack of theoretical guarantees for the greedy algorithm
- Reproducibility details could be improved

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of community detection in social networks. The proposed BHCD algorithm addresses a real problem (computational scalability of hierarchical community detection) with a clear, novel, and well-executed solution. The paper demonstrates impressive speedups over existing methods while maintaining comparable accuracy, which is highly valuable for practitioners working with large networks.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with other state-of-the-art methods and the lack of theoretical guarantees for the greedy algorithm. However, these do not substantially undermine the paper's contributions. The authors have made a clear, well-supported case for the utility of their method, and the technical quality of the work is excellent.

The paper meets all the NIPS criteria: it's high quality, clear, original, and significant. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing a practical tool for hierarchical community detection that scales well to large networks. The authors have properly contextualized their work against prior art and addressed the limitations of their approach.

I recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS. It represents a solid contribution that would be of interest to the broader machine learning community working on network analysis.
