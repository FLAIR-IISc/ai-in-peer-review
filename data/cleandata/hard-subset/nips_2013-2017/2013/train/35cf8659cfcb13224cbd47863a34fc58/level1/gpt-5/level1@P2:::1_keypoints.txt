- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric tree model for hierarchical community detection using conjugate within- and between-child edge likelihoods and a tree-induced prior enabling a DP recursion for marginal likelihood.
- Greedy agglomerative learning merges trees using local Bayes factors with cached sufficient statistics for O(1) updates, yielding O(n^2 log n) runtime and a sparse variant for edge-sparse graphs.
- Builds on BHC/Rose trees and blockmodels; trades expressiveness for scalability by replacing a full pairwise block interaction matrix with a single between-children likelihood per internal node.
- Strength — conceptual clarity: clean integration of hierarchical clustering machinery with blockmodel likelihood and an elegant recursion (Eq.10).
- Strength — algorithmic efficiency: well-motivated greedy merges, caching, and complexity bounds independent of community count.
- Strength — practical impact: orders-of-magnitude faster than IRM, scales to full NIPS coauthorship graph, and yields interpretable hierarchies.
- Strength — clear exposition: generative model, prior, DP, and merge ops given with reproducible mathematical detail.
- Weakness — modeling: pooling all between-child edges into one parameter per node prevents modeling heterogeneous inter-community affinities (limits handling core-periphery or disassortative structure).
- Weakness — inference and uncertainty: purely greedy procedure with restarts and heuristic sparse constraint; posterior uncertainty over trees/partitions is not quantified and sparse heuristic contradicts the prior.
- Weakness — empirical evaluation: limited baselines (mainly IRM), no re-runs of ILA/LFRM or comparisons to hierarchical IRM/Mondrian models, no synthetic planted-hierarchy recovery experiments, and limited ablations.
- Weakness — hyperparameters: α,β,δ,λ,γ are fixed with no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter learning; assortativity bias is hard-coded.
- Weakness — scope/generality: experiments and likelihoods focus on undirected, unweighted, no-self-edge graphs despite claims of generality.
- Quality assessment: technical derivations and complexity claims are sound, but experimental breadth and fairness of comparisons could be improved.
- Clarity assessment: paper is well-organized; suggested improvements include discussing pooled-between-child consequences, adding Algorithm 1 pseudocode, complexity sketch, and hyperparameter roles.
- Originality assessment: novel combination of rose-tree/BHC machinery with blockmodel likelihood and O(1) cached statistics for relational data, occupying a distinct speed–expressiveness point.
- Significance assessment: fast Bayesian hierarchical community discovery is useful, but modeling simplifications and limited experiments reduce demonstrated applicability.
- Pros for acceptance: elegant, principled, fast method with clear derivations, implementable algorithm, and strong runtime benefits producing interpretable hierarchies.
- Cons for acceptance: limited expressiveness from pooled parameterization, insufficient comparisons and synthetic tests, and reliance on heuristics and greedy search without uncertainty quantification.
- Recommendations: add synthetic hierarchical SBM experiments (assortative/non-assortative), compare to hierarchical IRM and re-run ILA/LFRM, perform hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, clarify speed vs. expressiveness trade-offs and possible extensions (e.g., limited between-child groups), and release code for reproducibility.
