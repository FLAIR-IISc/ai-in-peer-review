### Summary

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The model is presented as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs), where the data's marginal likelihood given a specific tree hierarchy can be calculated exactly and efficiently. The core contribution is a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that learns this tree hierarchy. Starting with each network vertex as a singleton tree, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of trees in a bottom-up fashion. The decision to merge is guided by a local Bayes factor, selecting the merge that provides the greatest increase in marginal likelihood for the combined sub-graph.

The proposed method builds directly upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering for non-relational data, specifically [13, 14], by adapting the framework to handle relational data as modeled by SBMs. It positions itself as a computationally efficient alternative to more flexible but slower Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5, 6] and its hierarchical extensions [8]. Unlike MCMC-based inference for these models, which can be computationally intensive and slow to converge, BHCD is a deterministic, single-pass algorithm with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), where n is the number of vertices.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model, defined as a tree-structured mixture of SBMs, is well-formulated. The key technical element is the recursive formula (Equation 10) for computing the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree, p(D|T), via dynamic programming. This allows for an exact evaluation of the model evidence for any given hierarchy, which is a strong foundation for the model selection procedure.

The greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning the tree structure is clearly described in Algorithm 1. The use of a Bayes factor (Equation 12) as the merge score provides a principled, Bayesian criterion for model selection at each step. A crucial technical detail for achieving the claimed time complexity is the caching of sufficient statistics, allowing the inner loop of the algorithm to operate in O(1) time. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical description of the algorithm and the empirical results. The experiments directly compare BHCD to the IRM, a relevant and strong baseline, demonstrating a significant speed advantage (two orders of magnitude) while achieving comparable or superior predictive performance on the NIPS co-authorship dataset. The authors are honest about the limitations, such as the greedy nature of the search, and address this by suggesting multiple random restarts.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. It follows a logical progression from the basic SBM (Section 2), to the hierarchical probabilistic model (Section 3), and finally to the inference algorithm (Section 4). The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of how a tree defines a set of consistent partitions, which is central to the model. Figure 2 clearly depicts the three types of merges (join, absorb left, absorb right) considered by the algorithm. The experimental section is clear, with well-designed comparisons and visualizations that effectively communicate the method's performance both qualitatively (Sampson's monastery) and quantitatively (NIPS co-authorship). The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method's core mechanics.

### Originality

The paper's originality lies in the novel synthesis of several existing ideas to address the problem of scalable hierarchical community detection. While greedy agglomerative clustering is a standard technique, and Bayesian approaches to it have been explored for non-relational data [13, 14], this work is the first to successfully extend this fast, greedy Bayesian framework to relational data using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. This provides a new point in the trade-off space between computational cost and model flexibility/inference quality, distinct from MCMC or variational approaches for hierarchical relational models. The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related work in both flat (IRM) and hierarchical community detection, clearly articulating its contribution in terms of computational efficiency.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant. The problem of discovering hierarchical structure in networks is important across many domains, but existing Bayesian nonparametric methods are often too slow for practical use on medium-to-large networks. BHCD provides a practical, scalable, and principled alternative. The demonstrated two-orders-of-magnitude speedup over the IRM for comparable predictive accuracy is a major practical advance. This makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling accessible for network analysis tasks where previously only non-probabilistic methods or simpler flat models were computationally feasible. The work is likely to be used by practitioners analyzing network data and to inspire further research into fast, approximate inference methods for complex Bayesian relational models.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **High Practical Impact:** The method offers a massive speed improvement (two orders of magnitude in experiments) over a key Bayesian nonparametric baseline (IRM), making hierarchical community detection much more practical for real-world networks.
2.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** It successfully adapts the fast Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework from [14] to the important domain of relational data, which is a novel and valuable contribution.
3.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and presents a well-defined model and inference algorithm.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are convincing and directly support the central claims of speed and accuracy against a strong, relevant baseline.

### Arguments against Acceptance

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The core algorithm is a greedy heuristic. It is not guaranteed to find a globally optimal hierarchy and is sensitive to tie-breaking, requiring multiple restarts. This is a significant limitation compared to MCMC methods that can, in principle, explore the full posterior.
2.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The Bayes factor score used for merging is local, considering only the subgraph induced by the nodes being merged. This may lead to suboptimal decisions from a global perspective.
3.  **Suboptimal Complexity for Sparse Networks:** The O(n² log n) complexity is not ideal for large, sparse networks (where m << n²). While the authors propose a `BHCD sparse` variant that performs well empirically, the worst-case complexity remains the same.
