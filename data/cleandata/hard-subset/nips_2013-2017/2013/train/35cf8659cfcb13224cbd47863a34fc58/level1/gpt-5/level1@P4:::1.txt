Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model and greedy agglomerative inference procedure to discover hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a tree-structured prior over partitions: a hierarchy T over vertices restricts the set of admissible partitions to those consistent with T, enabling exact marginalization over partitions via dynamic programming. Each partition corresponds to a stochastic block model (SBM), yielding a tree-structured mixture of SBMs. Using conjugate Beta-Bernoulli likelihoods with different hyperparameters for within- vs between-community edges, the paper derives a recursion for the marginal likelihood p(D|T). Inference proceeds by bottom-up agglomerative merging of subtrees using a local Bayes factor (comparing three merge types: join, absorb-left, absorb-right), with cached sufficient statistics enabling O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variation disallows merges between disconnected components; tie-breaking is handled by randomization with multiple restarts. The method supports link prediction by hierarchical posterior predictive computation along a path from root to leaves.

The approach builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering/rose trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010) by extending them to relational data via blockmodel likelihoods. It contrasts with IRM (Kemp et al.) and its hierarchical variants (e.g., Herlau et al.), which use CRP-based priors and costly MCMC/greedy search, and with models like LFRM/ILA that capture more flexible latent structures at higher computational cost. The paper also situates itself relative to divisive/greedy hierarchical clustering (e.g., Girvan–Newman; Clauset et al.), which lack full probabilistic treatments.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Model: The definition of a tree-induced prior over partitions and the factorization of p(D|φ,T) are sound, and the dynamic program for p(D|T) (Eq. 10) follows from the generative description. The locality of the Bayes factor for merges is a reasonable approximation for greedy model selection and aligns with rose-tree agglomeration.
- Inference and complexity: The use of cached sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) per inner step and O(n^2 log n) overall is plausible for dense graphs, with a sparse variant reducing practical costs. The likelihood computation in O(n+m) for a fixed tree is correct under the stated conjugate model.
- Predictive computation is described as linear in tree depth, though the text later states Θ(n); this appears to be a typo and should be “Θ(depth).”
- Experiments: The Sampson and NIPS datasets are appropriate. Predictive evaluation on NIPS-234 uses 10-fold CV and compares to IRM; comparisons to LFRM and ILA are indirect via previously reported numbers. Results indicate BHCD attains similar AUC/accuracy to IRM much faster; LFRM/ILA can outperform BHCD in accuracy but at greater cost.
- A notable inconsistency: Section 2 sets the intent α>β (within edges denser) and δ<λ (between edges sparser), but experiments use α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, which implies both within and between edge priors have mean 1/(1+0.2)=0.833, contradicting δ<λ and the intended sparsity between communities. This should be clarified or corrected, and sensitivity to hyperparameters reported.
- The greedy search lacks global optimality guarantees; restarts partially mitigate local optima.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with clear notation, a concrete Beta-Bernoulli instantiation, and an intuitive description of the agglomerative procedure and merge types. Minor issues:
- The predictive complexity statement (Θ(n)) conflicts with “linear in depth.”
- Some references are incomplete, and figures are crucial for interpretation but the text descriptions are serviceable.
- Runtime comparisons sometimes refer ambiguously to “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of IRM,” which is confusing; precise wall-clock comparisons to convergence or to specific predictive targets would be clearer.

Originality
The main novelty is the tree-restricted exact marginalization over partitions in a relational model combined with an efficient agglomerative search with three merge types and caching. While it draws heavily on Bayesian rose trees, applying this framework to SBMs and deriving an exact dynamic program for relational likelihoods is a meaningful and nontrivial extension. Relative to hierarchical IRM variants, BHCD offers a simpler, more scalable alternative. The “sparse” constraint is a practical heuristic.

Significance
The work addresses an important need: scalable probabilistic discovery of hierarchical communities. Its main strength is computational efficiency with reasonable predictive performance, making it attractive for large networks. The demonstration on the full NIPS graph highlights practicality. However, in predictive accuracy it is not state of the art compared to richer latent feature/attribute models, and the evaluation breadth is limited. Nonetheless, as a fast baseline and interpretable hierarchical model, the contribution is useful.

Strengths
- Clear, principled model: tree-structured prior over partitions; exact marginalization via DP.
- Efficient agglomerative inference with O(n^2 log n) worst-case time and practical speedups.
- Simple conjugate likelihood enabling fast evaluation and prediction.
- Empirical evidence of substantial speed gains over IRM with competitive accuracy.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies; supports link prediction.

Weaknesses
- Hyperparameter inconsistency (δ<λ stated but δ=1, λ=0.2 used), and no sensitivity/hyperparameter inference.
- Greedy, local merge score may miss global structure; stability relies on multiple restarts.
- Sparse variant’s prohibition of merging disconnected components is heuristic.
- Limited baselines and datasets; direct comparisons to LFRM/ILA are absent.
- Minor clarity issues (predictive complexity typo; ambiguous runtime claims).

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct or clarify the between-community prior hyperparameters; add sensitivity analyses and/or learn α,β,δ,λ,γ.
- Provide direct experimental comparison to at least one latent feature/attribute model (e.g., LFRM or ILA) on NIPS-234 with matched splits.
- Report stability across restarts (e.g., tree similarity metrics) and the effect of the three merge types.
- Clarify predictive complexity and runtime statements; include wall-clock comparisons to target AUC/log-likelihood.
- Discuss extensions to directed, weighted, or signed networks (beyond the brief note), and to non-conjugate likelihoods as suggested.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, well-motivated probabilistic framework with exact computations conditioned on a tree.
- Significant practical speedups with comparable predictive performance to IRM.
- Extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas to relational data in a novel, efficient way.
- Produces interpretable hierarchical structures at scale.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow and lacks direct comparisons to stronger contemporary baselines.
- Hyperparameter inconsistency raises concerns about calibration and reported results.
- Method trades accuracy for speed relative to richer models; limited evidence of advancing state of the art in predictive performance.

Overall, the paper offers a technically sound and efficient approach to hierarchical community discovery with clear contributions in scalability and model design, though the empirical section and hyperparameter treatment would benefit from strengthening.
