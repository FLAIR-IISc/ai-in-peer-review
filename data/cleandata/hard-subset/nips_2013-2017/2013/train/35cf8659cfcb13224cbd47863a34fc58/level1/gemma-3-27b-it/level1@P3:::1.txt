This paper presents a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, coupled with a greedy agglomerative algorithm for efficient inference. The authors demonstrate good performance in terms of speed and accuracy, comparing favorably to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

The paper tackles an important problem – scalable hierarchical community detection – and builds on relevant prior work. The motivation is clearly articulated, acknowledging the limitations of existing methods like the IRM (computational cost) and previous hierarchical approaches (lack of full probabilistic accounting or limited depth). The authors correctly position their work within the context of Bayesian nonparametric modeling and greedy hierarchical clustering. Related work is appropriately cited, including key papers on stochastic blockmodels [1], the IRM [5,6,7], and hierarchical clustering [3,8,13,14].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative algorithm is a novel approach to hierarchical community detection. While tree priors have been used before [12, 8], the efficient inference procedure proposed here, specifically designed for relational data and leveraging sufficient statistics caching, is a significant contribution.
* **Efficiency:** The reported speed improvements over the IRM (two orders of magnitude faster) are substantial and practically important. The theoretical analysis of the algorithm’s complexity (O(n^2 log n)) is a strength, and the practical observations of independence from the number of communities are valuable. The introduction of BHCD sparse further improves performance on sparse networks.
* **Sound Methodology:** The Bayesian framework provides a principled approach to model selection and uncertainty quantification. The use of conjugate priors simplifies inference and the hierarchical structure is well-defined.  The dynamic programming approach to computing the marginal likelihood is clever and allows for efficient calculation.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network demonstrate the qualitative validity of the approach. The quantitative comparison to the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness. The visualizations of the inferred hierarchy in the full NIPS dataset are insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and the algorithm is explained in a straightforward manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) seems somewhat arbitrary.  While the authors state that the chosen values worked well, a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and justification for the selection would strengthen the paper.  How do different hyperparameter choices affect the resulting community structure?
* **Tie-breaking:**  The random tie-breaking strategy during the greedy merging process could introduce instability. While the authors use restarts to mitigate this, a more deterministic or informed tie-breaking rule (e.g., based on predictive performance or other criteria) might be beneficial.
* **Scalability on Extremely Large Sparse Graphs:** While the complexity analysis is favorable, the O(n^2 log n) complexity might still be limiting for very large, sparse graphs (millions of nodes). The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation into exploiting sparsity beyond the BHCD sparse variant could be valuable. The claimed O(n+m) complexity for likelihood calculation is excellent, but its integration into the overall algorithm and its practical impact deserve more discussion.
* **Comparison to More Recent Methods:**  While the comparison to the IRM is relevant, the field has advanced since many of the cited works.  Comparing to more recent and scalable community detection algorithms, particularly those employing spectral methods or graph embeddings, could provide a more comprehensive benchmark.  For example, methods based on graph convolutional networks might be relevant.
* **Limited Discussion of Predictive Performance:** The predictive performance metrics are reported, but the discussion of *why* BHCD performs well (or not as well as some alternatives like ILA) is limited. A more in-depth analysis of the types of links that are correctly or incorrectly predicted could provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and efficient algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
* The speed improvements over existing methods are significant and practical.
* The theoretical analysis and empirical validation are convincing.
* The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated.
* The random tie-breaking strategy could introduce instability.
* The scalability to extremely large sparse graphs could be a limitation.
* Comparison to more recent methods would strengthen the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The proposed method is novel, efficient, and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the hyperparameter sensitivity and providing a more detailed discussion of the predictive performance and comparison to more recent methods.  The authors should also clarify the interplay between the O(n+m) likelihood computation and the O(n^2 log n) overall complexity.
