This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method based on a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The core idea is to efficiently learn a probabilistic, hierarchical community model using a greedy agglomerative approach. The work builds upon existing Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering and addresses the computational limitations of methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

The paper is well-structured and clearly introduces the problem, related work, and the proposed method. The motivation for developing a more efficient Bayesian approach to hierarchical community detection is strong, given the computational cost of existing methods like the IRM. The authors correctly identify the key challenges and position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers such as those on stochastic blockmodels [1], IRM [5,6,7], and Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14]. The description of the model (Section 3) is reasonably clear, though requires careful reading. The use of a tree to define a prior on partitions is a clever way to enable efficient computation. The explanation of the greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm (Section 4) is detailed and explains how the computational complexity is reduced. The experiments (Section 5) demonstrate the speed and accuracy of the proposed method on Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and also show the kind of hierarchical structure that is discovered on the full NIPS dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative inference scheme is novel. It provides a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate a significant speedup (two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration) with comparable or better accuracy. This is a major strength of the paper. The claimed quadratic time complexity (in the worst case) is also a positive aspect, as it scales better than some other Bayesian methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the model and the inference algorithm. The derivation of the likelihood and the explanation of the greedy search are well-presented.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both small (Sampson's Monastery) and large (NIPS co-authorship) datasets show the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison with the IRM is particularly insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the performance would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with the IRM is valuable, comparing against other relevant methods (e.g., LFRM [18], ILA [10]) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
*   **Tie-breaking:** The discussion of tie-breaking during the greedy search seems somewhat ad hoc. A more principled approach to tie-breaking or an analysis of its impact on the results would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions**: The model assumes that the edge probabilities within and between communities are well-modeled by Beta distributions. The justification for this assumption could be strengthened.
*   **Scalability in Sparse Graphs**: Although the authors mention the potential for further improvement in scalability on sparse graphs, a more detailed discussion of the challenges and possible solutions would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and efficient algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a significant speedup compared to existing methods without sacrificing accuracy.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
*   The problem addressed is relevant and important in the field of network analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough hyperparameter analysis is a concern.
*   The limited comparison with other relevant methods weakens the claim of superiority.
*   The ad hoc tie-breaking strategy could potentially affect the reproducibility of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed method addresses a challenging problem with a novel and efficient solution. The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, comparison with other methods, and tie-breaking. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
2.  Add a more comprehensive comparison with other relevant methods, such as LFRM and ILA.
3.  Provide a more principled approach to tie-breaking or analyze its impact on the results.
