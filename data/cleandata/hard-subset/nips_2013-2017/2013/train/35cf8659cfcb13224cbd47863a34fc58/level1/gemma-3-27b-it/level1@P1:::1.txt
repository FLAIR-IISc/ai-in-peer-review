This paper proposes a Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, coupled with a greedy agglomerative algorithm for efficient model selection. The authors demonstrate competitive accuracy with the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) but with significantly improved speed, particularly for larger networks.

The work builds upon existing Bayesian approaches to community detection like the IRM [5, 6, 7] and extensions for hierarchies [8], acknowledging their computational limitations. It also draws from Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering [13, 14], adapting them for relational data. The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of existing methods, citing relevant work on stochastic blockmodels [1, 2], hierarchical clustering [3, 4], and Bayesian nonparametric models [12]. The connection to prior work is well-established, and the motivation for addressing the scalability issues of existing Bayesian hierarchical models is clear.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The primary strength is the algorithmic efficiency. The authors convincingly demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster than the IRM, with worst-case complexity independent of the number of communities – a crucial advantage for large networks. The caching scheme described is a clever optimization.
* **Probabilistic Framework:** The Bayesian nonparametric approach provides a principled and probabilistic account of the discovered community structure, going beyond purely deterministic hierarchical clustering methods.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the marginal likelihood and the recursive computation (eq. 10) provide a strong theoretical foundation for the method.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data demonstrate the practical utility of the method, showing competitive performance and substantial speedups.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) is not discussed extensively. The authors state they found values that "work well," but a more thorough sensitivity analysis or justification for these choices would strengthen the work.
* **Binary Tree Restriction:** While the introduction of sparse variants of BHCD is valuable, the comparison between binary and non-binary tree structures feels somewhat incomplete. The authors suggest binary trees produce better predictions but harder-to-interpret hierarchies, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison to Recent Methods:** While the comparison to the IRM is valuable, the field has advanced since many of the cited works. Comparing against more recent methods specifically designed for scalability in hierarchical community detection (beyond LFRM [18]) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.  For example, methods leveraging graph embeddings or approximate inference techniques.
* **Link Prediction Details:** The description of the link prediction process is somewhat brief. While the formula (14) is provided, more detail on how the predictive distribution is used for making actual predictions (e.g., thresholding, ranking) would be beneficial.
* **Assumption of Undirected Graphs:** The paper mentions handling undirected graphs but doesn’t fully explore the implications or limitations for directed networks.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Significant improvement in scalability over existing Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods.
* Sound theoretical foundation and a well-defined algorithmic approach.
* Promising empirical results on benchmark datasets.
* Addresses an important problem in network analysis with a novel and practical solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Relatively narrow comparison to existing methods, missing recent advancements.
* Lack of detailed analysis of the trade-offs between binary and non-binary tree structures.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The primary strength lies in its scalability, addressing a significant limitation of existing Bayesian methods. While there are areas for improvement, particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and comparison to more recent work, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
