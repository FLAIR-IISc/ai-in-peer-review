- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric, tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a greedy agglomerative inference algorithm.  
- Novelty: combines tree priors with an efficient, relational-data-specific inference using sufficient-statistic caching.  
- Efficiency: reports ~100x speedup over IRM, theoretical complexity O(n^2 log n), and a BHCD-sparse variant for sparse graphs.  
- Methodology: principled Bayesian framework with conjugate priors and dynamic programming for marginal likelihood computation.  
- Empirical validation: qualitative and quantitative results on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship datasets with insightful hierarchy visualizations.  
- Clarity: well-written, clear notation and algorithm description.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: choice of (α, β, δ, λ, γ) appears arbitrary and lacks thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- Tie-breaking instability: random tie-breaking in greedy merges may introduce instability; restarts mitigate but a more informed rule is desirable.  
- Scalability limits: O(n^2 log n) may be prohibitive for very large (millions-node) sparse graphs despite BHCD-sparse and O(n+m) likelihood claims.  
- Limited benchmarks: comparison mainly to IRM; lacks evaluation against more recent scalable methods (e.g., spectral, embeddings, GCN-based).  
- Predictive analysis gap: reported predictive metrics lack deeper analysis of which link types are well or poorly predicted compared to alternatives.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, efficient, theoretically and empirically supported method addressing an important problem.  
- Rejection concerns: unresolved hyperparameter issues, tie-breaking instability, scalability to extreme sizes, and limited modern baselines.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to analyze hyperparameter sensitivity, clarify O(n+m) vs overall complexity, expand predictive analysis, and compare to newer methods.
