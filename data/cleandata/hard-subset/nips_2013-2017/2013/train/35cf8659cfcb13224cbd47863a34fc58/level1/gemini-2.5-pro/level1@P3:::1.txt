This paper presents a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to model the network as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. By defining a prior over partitions that are consistent with a given hierarchy (a tree), the authors derive a recursive formula to compute the exact marginal likelihood of the data given the tree, `p(D|T)`. This approach builds directly upon previous work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, notably Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and the Bayesian Rose Trees of Blundell et al. (2010), by adapting the framework to relational data with a Beta-Bernoulli likelihood appropriate for stochastic blockmodels.

For inference, the paper proposes a greedy agglomerative algorithm that constructs the hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion. Starting with each node as a singleton cluster, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of subtrees that yield the largest increase in the marginal likelihood (i.e., the highest Bayes factor). The authors present a novel caching scheme for the sufficient statistics, enabling the algorithm to run in O(n^2 log n) time, which is a significant improvement over iterative MCMC-based competitors like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), whose per-iteration cost often depends on the number of inferred clusters.

The method is evaluated on several datasets, including the classic Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network. The results demonstrate that BHCD can uncover meaningful hierarchical structures and achieves predictive performance comparable to the IRM while being approximately two orders of magnitude faster.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its computational efficiency. By moving from sampling-based inference to a fast, deterministic, greedy search, the authors make Bayesian hierarchical modeling of networks practical for much larger datasets. The O(n^2 log n) worst-case complexity, independent of the number of communities, and the dramatic speed-up demonstrated empirically against the IRM are highly significant contributions. This work effectively bridges the gap between fast but non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering methods and principled but slow Bayesian nonparametric models.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed model is well-founded in Bayesian principles. The use of the marginal likelihood `p(D|T)` provides a principled score for entire hierarchies, and the use of Bayes factors offers a solid criterion for the greedy merge decisions. The model is fully generative, allowing for straightforward link prediction, which is elegantly formulated as a recursive traversal of the inferred tree.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The model derivation is clear and logical, starting from the basic stochastic blockmodel and building up to the hierarchical mixture. The inference algorithm is described precisely, and the connection to its predecessors in Bayesian hierarchical clustering is made explicit and appropriately credited.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality:** While the application is novel and the engineering for efficiency is impressive, the core methodological framework is a direct extension of prior work [13, 14]. The main conceptual contribution is adapting the Bayesian hierarchical clustering machinery to relational data using an SBM-style likelihood. While this is a valuable and non-trivial step, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new modeling paradigm or inference technique.

2.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The inference algorithm is greedy and finds a single point estimate for the hierarchy `T`. As with all greedy approaches, it is susceptible to finding local optima. The authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, but this does not capture the posterior uncertainty over hierarchies in the way that an MCMC sampler would. A more explicit discussion of the potential pitfalls of this local, deterministic search would strengthen the paper. For example, a merge that appears optimal locally might preclude a more globally optimal structure later on. This limitation is a direct trade-off for the impressive speed gains, and it would be beneficial to frame it as such more directly.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model relies on several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are fixed across all experiments. The performance of the model, especially the structure of the inferred hierarchy, could be sensitive to these settings. For instance, the choice of `γ` directly influences the model's preference for flatter versus deeper hierarchies. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen values, leaving the robustness of the method as an open question. While hyperparameter inference is noted as future work, a brief discussion on their influence would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of network analysis. The authors have successfully developed a method that makes Bayesian hierarchical community detection significantly more scalable. The trade-off—sacrificing full posterior inference for a massive gain in speed via a principled greedy search—is well-motivated and will likely be very attractive to practitioners working with medium-to-large networks. The experimental results are convincing and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

Despite the reliance on prior methodological frameworks, the novel application to relational data and the careful engineering to ensure efficiency represent a solid and significant advance. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and the results are important. I strongly recommend acceptance.
