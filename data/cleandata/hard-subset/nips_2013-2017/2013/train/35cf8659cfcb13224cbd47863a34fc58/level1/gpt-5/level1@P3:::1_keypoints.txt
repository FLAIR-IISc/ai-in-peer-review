- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model that defines a tree over vertices and integrates over all partitions consistent with that tree.  
- Uses a top-down stopping prior p(φ|T) with node-dependent stopping probability and Beta–Bernoulli conjugacy for an exact tree marginal likelihood via dynamic programming (O(n + m)).  
- Learns the tree with a greedy agglomerative search using three local merge operations scored by local Bayes factors; with caching runs in O(n^2 log n) per restart.  
- Introduces a sparse variant that forbids merging subtrees with zero inter-edges and speeds inference on sparse graphs.  
- Obtains predictive distributions for missing edges by a top-down recursion on the learned tree.  
- Modeling novelty: a tree-induced mixture over blockmodel partitions (distinct from IRM and per-node-parameter hierarchical models).  
- Strength: clear modeling idea and exact, efficient marginalization given a tree.  
- Strength: practical, scalable inference with O(1) cached updates and good empirical wall-clock performance.  
- Strength: sensible predictive mechanism and interpretable hierarchies on real datasets (Sampson, NIPS coauthorship).  
- Weakness: uses assortative SBM likelihood—ignores degree heterogeneity, disassortative structure, and mixed membership; prior also biases assortativity.  
- Weakness: greedy, local merge scores can lock in suboptimal merges; no post-hoc refinement (e.g., subtree reattachments or split–merge) or theoretical guarantees.  
- Weakness: variability from random tie-breaking and unclear benefit/variance of R = 50 restarts not quantified.  
- Weakness: limited empirical baselines—mostly IRM comparisons; missing hierarchical baselines (e.g., hierarchical random graphs) and no synthetic hierarchical recovery experiments.  
- Weakness: ambiguous speed claims (“two orders of magnitude faster” vs “faster than a single iteration of the IRM”); absolute runtimes, hardware, and memory costs (O(n^2) heap) not reported.  
- Weakness: hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) fixed a priori with no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference; γ affects tree shape and α/β, δ/λ affect assortativity.  
- Weakness: experiments only on undirected binary graphs; directed, weighted, signed cases not evaluated and negative ties were treated as absent in Sampson.  
- Quality: methodology and dynamic program are sound and efficiently implemented, but empirical evaluation and ablations are insufficient.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written but should derive posterior predictive forms, provide pseudocode for key computations, and clarify the “iteration” and speed comparisons.  
- Originality: novel and practical combination of tree-induced partition mixtures with exact marginalization and fast agglomerative search for relational data.  
- Significance: useful for practitioners needing fast, interpretable hierarchies and link prediction, but impact limited by lack of degree correction and broader baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant, efficient model; exact marginalization given a tree; strong speed–accuracy trade-offs and interpretable outputs.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited evaluation, modeling limitations (no degree correction), greedy search without refinement, and unclear speed/memory reporting.  
- Suggested improvements: add degree correction or sensitivity analysis to degree heterogeneity.  
- Suggested improvements: include hierarchical baselines and synthetic benchmarks with known hierarchical ground truth.  
- Suggested improvements: report absolute runtimes, memory usage, and variability across restarts; clarify IRM comparison.  
- Suggested improvements: perform hyperparameter sensitivity or empirical Bayes for key priors, and add lightweight post-agglomerative refinements (e.g., subtree reattachments).  
- Suggested improvements: explore directed/weighted extensions to demonstrate broader applicability.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept — neat, scalable contribution but evaluation and some modeling choices need strengthening.
