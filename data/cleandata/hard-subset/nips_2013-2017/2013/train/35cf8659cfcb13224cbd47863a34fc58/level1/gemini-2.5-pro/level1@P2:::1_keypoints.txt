- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD): a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community detection.  
- Computes marginal likelihood for a given tree via a dynamic programming recursion (Equation 10) summing over all tree-consistent partitions.  
- Builds on Bayesian Rose Trees [14], extending Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational network data.  
- Uses a fast greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm instead of MCMC/variational inference, merging trees by maximizing Bayes factor.  
- Introduces a caching scheme for SBM sufficient statistics enabling O(1) updates and overall worst-case O(n² log n) runtime independent of the number of communities.  
- Evaluated on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks, showing orders-of-magnitude speed gains over the IRM with comparable predictive accuracy.  
- Technical derivations and the recursive marginal likelihood are sound and represent a clever sum-product application on trees.  
- Experimental evaluation is solid; qualitative and quantitative results support the method’s speed-accuracy trade-off.  
- Main weakness: greedy search can get stuck in local optima; multiple restarts mitigate but do not eliminate this risk.  
- Secondary weakness: hyperparameters are fixed without sensitivity analysis, weakening understanding of robustness.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, equations and algorithms are clear, and figures effectively illustrate concepts.  
- Minor clarity concern: caching mechanism details are brief and could use a fuller exposition for reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel synthesis—first to translate Bayesian Rose Tree ideas to relational SBMs and to develop O(1) SBM sufficient-statistic updates within greedy search.  
- Significance: high practical impact by providing scalable hierarchical community discovery; runtime independence from K is a major advantage.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled Bayesian framing, exceptional scalability (O(n² log n)), competitive accuracy, clear technical contribution, and strong empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on greedy inference (local optima), perceived incremental nature from prior work, and a heuristic “sparse” variant that weakens the pure Bayesian claim.
