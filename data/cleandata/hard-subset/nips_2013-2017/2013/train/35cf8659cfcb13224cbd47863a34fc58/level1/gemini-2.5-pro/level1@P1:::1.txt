### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel and efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to model the network's adjacency matrix as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Unlike the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which in principle sums over an intractable space of all possible flat partitions, BHCD defines a marginal likelihood for the data given a specific tree structure. This likelihood is computed by summing over the much smaller, structured set of partitions that are consistent with the tree (i.e., partitions formed by cutting the tree at some level). This formulation, which builds directly upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering for i.i.d. data (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2014), allows for an efficient marginal likelihood calculation via dynamic programming.

For inference, the authors eschew slow MCMC methods in favor of a fast, greedy agglomerative algorithm to find the optimal tree structure. Starting with each node as a singleton community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of sub-trees. The decision of which pair to merge is based on a local Bayes factor score, which maximizes the marginal likelihood of the data within the newly formed sub-tree. The paper describes an efficient implementation with a worst-case time complexity of O(n^2 log n), which is notably independent of the number of communities. The authors demonstrate experimentally that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than IRM while achieving comparable or superior predictive performance on benchmark datasets.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is a well-founded probabilistic extension of greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood for a given tree (Eq. 10) is sound and elegant. The greedy model selection procedure, based on maximizing a local Bayes factor, is a principled and pragmatic choice for achieving scalability.

The claims are well-supported by the experiments. The qualitative results on the Sampson's Monastery dataset show that the method finds interpretable and meaningful hierarchies that align with known social structures. The quantitative experiments on the NIPS co-authorship data provide compelling evidence for the central claim of the paper: BHCD offers a significant speed-up over standard Bayesian nonparametric methods like IRM. The plots in Figure 4 clearly show BHCD reaching a good performance level in seconds, whereas IRM takes thousands of seconds. The authors are also honest about the performance trade-offs, noting that their method performs worse than the more complex Infinite Latent Attributes (ILA) model but better than IRM on the chosen task. This is a complete and well-executed piece of work.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition, starting from the familiar stochastic blockmodel, then introducing the hierarchical extension and the prior over tree-consistent partitions, and finally describing the agglomerative inference algorithm. The notation is consistent, and key concepts are explained clearly. Figures 1 and 2 are particularly effective at illustrating the set of tree-consistent partitions and the different types of merge operations, respectively. The paper provides enough detail, including the recursive formulas and the high-level algorithm, for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While it builds on existing ideas—namely, Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani) and stochastic blockmodels—its application and synthesis for relational data are novel. It successfully bridges the gap between fast, heuristic-based hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., Newman-Girvan), which lack a probabilistic interpretation, and more formal but computationally prohibitive Bayesian methods for hierarchical network analysis. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to propose a viable, fast, and fully probabilistic greedy approach for this problem. The introduction of different merge types (join vs. absorb) to allow for non-binary trees is also a valuable contribution inherited from Blundell et al. (2014) and well-adapted here.

### Significance

The significance of this work is high. Scalable inference for structured models of network data is a major challenge in machine learning. Existing fully Bayesian methods often do not scale to the network sizes encountered in practice. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster than a key baseline (IRM) while maintaining a probabilistic foundation and achieving strong predictive performance, this paper makes a significant practical contribution. Researchers and practitioners looking to explore hierarchical structure in medium-to-large networks would likely find BHCD to be a very attractive tool. The ideas presented are likely to inspire further research into fast, approximate inference methods for other complex Bayesian nonparametric models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to the important problem of hierarchical community detection, filling a clear gap in the literature.
2.  **Impressive Performance:** The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating a massive speed advantage (two orders of magnitude) over a standard Bayesian baseline (IRM) with comparable accuracy.
3.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The model is probabilistically well-founded, the inference algorithm is principled, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity.
4.  **Scalability:** The O(n^2 log n) complexity makes the method applicable to networks that are challenging for MCMC-based approaches.

**Arguments against Acceptance (and suggestions for improvement):**

1.  **Greedy Inference and Point Estimates:** The primary weakness is the greedy nature of the inference, which finds a single point estimate of the hierarchy and is susceptible to local optima. This forgoes a key benefit of Bayesian modeling: quantifying posterior uncertainty over structures. While the use of restarts partially addresses this, a more explicit discussion of this limitation and the trade-off being made (speed vs. full posterior characterization) would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The efficiency of the algorithm relies on a local merge score. While pragmatic, this can lead to globally suboptimal decisions. A brief discussion of potential failure modes resulting from this locality assumption would be helpful. For example, are there network structures where this greedy local choice is known to perform poorly?
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters that are fixed in the experiments. A sensitivity analysis or at least a more detailed justification for their chosen values would improve the paper's practical utility. For instance, how does the choice of γ influence the resulting tree's structure (e.g., its "bushiness")?

Despite these minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper are overwhelming. The contribution is substantial, well-executed, and clearly communicated. This is an excellent paper that represents a significant advance in scalable probabilistic modeling of networks.
