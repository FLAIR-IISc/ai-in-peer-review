

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks through a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors extend Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data, proposing a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that learns hierarchical community structure in O(n² log n) time, where n is the number of vertices. Unlike previous Bayesian methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy while being two orders of magnitude faster. The paper demonstrates BHCD's effectiveness on Sampson's monastery network, NIPS co-authorship data, and the full NIPS dataset, showing significant computational advantages over existing methods.

The work builds upon key prior contributions: it extends the stochastic blockmodel framework [1], improves upon greedy hierarchical clustering [3,4], and advances beyond the IRM [5-7] by avoiding expensive inference procedures. The authors clearly position their method within the literature, particularly highlighting how it differs from previous hierarchical Bayesian approaches [8-11] that either limit hierarchy depth or require excessive computation.

Strengths: The paper excels in technical quality, with a sound theoretical foundation and well-supported experimental results. The model's scalability (quadratic in vertices, independent of community count) is a significant practical contribution. The clarity of the exposition is excellent, with well-organized sections and sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility. The originality lies in combining greedy model selection with Bayesian hierarchical community detection, a novel approach that avoids the computational bottlenecks of existing methods. The significance is clear—BHCD enables hierarchical community analysis on larger networks than previously possible.

Weaknesses: The paper could better address how BHCD handles extremely large sparse networks (though the authors note this as future work). The random tie-breaking strategy for merges with equal scores may produce inconsistent results across runs, and the justification for this approach is limited. The interpretability of the discovered hierarchies, particularly for complex real-world networks, could be explored more deeply.

Pro: The method's speed-accuracy tradeoff is compelling, making hierarchical community analysis feasible for larger networks. Con: The paper doesn't fully demonstrate how the method scales on networks with millions of vertices, which is increasingly common in practice.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in community detection. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem with a novel, practical solution. I recommend acceptance.
