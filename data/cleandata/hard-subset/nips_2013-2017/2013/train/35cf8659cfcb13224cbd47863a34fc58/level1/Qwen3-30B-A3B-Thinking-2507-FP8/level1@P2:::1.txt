

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors introduce a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and develop an efficient greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm (BHCD) that learns hierarchical community structure in a single pass through the data. The approach builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods [13,14] and extends them to relational data, addressing a key limitation of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (which typically scale poorly with network size). The paper demonstrates that BHCD scales quadratically in the number of vertices (n) and independently of the number of communities, achieving results two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable or better accuracy.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work: it extends the IRM [5,6,7] to handle hierarchical structure more efficiently, improves upon the Mondrian process [12] by avoiding the computational cost of Bayesian inference, and builds on recent work in hierarchical clustering [3,4] by providing a full probabilistic account of the data. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, particularly noting that previous hierarchical Bayesian methods either had limited depth [9,10], required known depth [11], or were computationally prohibitive [8].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach with clear model specifications (equations 2, 6-10) and a well-justified algorithm (Algorithm 1). The authors provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity (O(n² log n)) and demonstrate how their caching scheme enables efficient computation. The experimental results on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset provide strong empirical evidence for the method's effectiveness.

2. **Significant Speedup:** The paper makes a compelling case for the practical significance of their approach. The demonstrated two orders of magnitude speedup over IRM while maintaining comparable accuracy addresses a critical bottleneck in applying Bayesian nonparametric methods to real-world social networks. The authors effectively compare their method to relevant baselines (Table 1) and show that BHCD sparse variants achieve comparable predictive performance to IRM after just 10 seconds of computation versus IRM's 1000 seconds.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized with logical flow from problem statement to model, algorithm, and experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. Figures 1-5 effectively illustrate the hierarchical structure and the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Methods:** While the paper compares well to IRM and related Bayesian methods, it does not compare to more recent nonparametric methods that have emerged since 2013, such as the Infinite Relational Model extensions in [18,10] or the Latent Factor Relational Model (LFRM). This weakens the significance claim regarding state-of-the-art performance.

2. **Handling of Disconnected Components:** The paper acknowledges that BHCD may merge disconnected communities (Section 4.1), but the proposed solution (BHCD sparse) is only a partial fix. The authors should better address this limitation and potentially discuss whether their approach can be extended to handle disconnected networks more robustly.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** While the paper provides strong empirical results, it lacks theoretical guarantees about the optimality or convergence properties of the greedy algorithm. A brief discussion of when the greedy approach might fail to find the optimal hierarchy would strengthen the paper.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a clear, well-executed solution to an important problem in network analysis.
- The computational efficiency claim is strongly supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The approach is a significant step forward in making Bayesian hierarchical community detection practical for larger networks.
- The paper is well-written with clear explanations and good experimental validation.

*Con:*
- The comparison to the most recent methods (post-2013) is limited.
- The paper does not fully address the issue of merging disconnected communities.
- The theoretical understanding of the greedy algorithm's behavior is incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of community detection in networks. The method addresses a clear and important problem (scalability of Bayesian hierarchical community detection) with a technically sound approach that delivers substantial practical benefits. The quality of the work is excellent, with thorough theoretical analysis, clear presentation, and strong experimental validation. While the paper could be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to recent methods and a deeper discussion of the limitations regarding disconnected components, these are relatively minor issues for a paper of this quality.

The significance of the work is high, as it enables the application of Bayesian hierarchical community detection to larger networks than previously possible, potentially opening up new applications in social network analysis, recommendation systems, and other domains where hierarchical structure is important. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is of high quality, clear, original (in the specific combination of techniques and efficiency improvements), and significant for the field.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a solid, well-executed contribution that advances the state of the art in community detection with practical implications for network analysis.
