- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model that discovers hierarchical communities by placing a prior over tree-constrained partitions and using a Beta-Bernoulli blockmodel likelihood.  
- Computes exact marginal likelihood p(D|T) via a dynamic program over a rose tree (Eq. 10).  
- Learns trees with a greedy agglomerative search using three merge types and a local Bayes factor to choose merges (Eq. 12).  
- Implements caching of sufficient statistics for O(1) inner-loop updates and reports overall O(n^2 log n) complexity.  
- Offers a “sparse” heuristic that forbids merging disconnected subtrees to speed inference.  
- Produces predictive probabilities by a coherent top-down recursion along the tree (Eq. 14).  
- Extends Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering / Rose Trees to relational (edge) data via stochastic blockmodels.  
- Contrasts with IRM/ILA/LFRM approaches that use MCMC or flat/limited-depth search and are typically slower.  
- Compared to hierarchical random graphs, BHCD uses a mixture over tree-consistent partitions and conjugate marginalization.  
- Modeling is well-defined and yields a clean DP due to the chosen prior πS and conjugacy.  
- Inference algorithm is well specified and plausibly efficient with its caching and merge operations.  
- Empirically, BHCD recovers interpretable structure on Sampson’s data and matches or exceeds IRM speed and predictive performance on NIPS-234.  
- On full NIPS the method produces a large, interpretable hierarchy.  
- Main concern: greedy local Bayes-factor merges ignore global interactions and may produce suboptimal trees; failure modes need discussion.  
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed with no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference presented.  
- Uses vanilla SBM without degree correction, potentially hurting performance on degree-heterogeneous networks.  
- Memory/time scaling: storing pairwise statistics is O(n^2); practical limits and sparsity-aware structures need discussion.  
- Clarity is generally good, but pseudocode, heap-consistency details, and tie/restart handling should be provided.  
- Clarify whether experiments maintain exact heap scores after merges and how predictive plots aggregate over restarts.  
- Originality: adapting rose-tree machinery to SBMs and the three-way merge/sparse constraint are novel and practical.  
- Significance: provides a fast, fully probabilistic hierarchical community method with exact marginal likelihoods for fixed trees.  
- Empirical gaps: lacks synthetic recovery experiments, comparisons to hierarchical random graphs and modularity-based methods, sensitivity analyses, directed/weighted graphs, and scaling curves.  
- Pros: conceptually clean Bayesian formulation, exact DP for p(D|T), scalable greedy inference, interpretable hierarchies, practical variants.  
- Cons: no degree correction or hyperparameter learning, greedy search may miss global optima, missing baseline comparisons, worst-case quadratic memory/time.  
- Suggestions: compare to Clauset–Moore–Newman hierarchical random graph, test hyperparameter sensitivity or empirical Bayes, consider degree-corrected likelihoods, report memory limits and sparse data structures, clarify prediction and IRM comparison protocols, include synthetic planted-hierarchy recovery experiments.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — valuable and fast baseline but needs stronger empirical comparisons and discussion of limitations.
