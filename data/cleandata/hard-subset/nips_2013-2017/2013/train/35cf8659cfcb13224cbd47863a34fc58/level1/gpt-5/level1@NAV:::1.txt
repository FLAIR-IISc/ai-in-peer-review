This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model that represents a network’s hierarchical community structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). A key technical contribution is an exact dynamic-programming evaluation of the marginal likelihood over all partitions consistent with a given tree, yielding a recursion that composes on- and off-diagonal marginal likelihoods (beta–Bernoulli instantiation). Learning proceeds via a greedy, agglomerative, rose-tree model selection with local Bayes factors, considering join and absorption merges; complexity is O(n^2 log n) with cached sufficient statistics and a sparse variant that forbids merging disconnected components. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship graphs show substantial speedups over IRM (MCMC and greedy search) with comparable or better predictive performance, and interpretable hierarchies on the full NIPS graph. This work connects to SBMs and mixed-membership SBMs, IRM and hierarchical IRM, Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees, LFRM/ILA, and Mondrian process–based hierarchical relational models.

Quality: The model is sound and carefully derived; the recursion and locality of merge scores are well motivated. Empirical evaluation is credible and uses established benchmarks, though fairness of runtime comparisons to IRM (single Gibbs scans vs complete BHCD runs) should be clarified. Hyperparameters are fixed; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference is provided.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The description of join vs absorption merges and caching could be streamlined; pseudo-code and a complexity proof sketch would help. Some grammar/typos persist.

Originality: The combination of rose-tree priors with SBM marginals for relational data and the resulting exact tree-marginal recursion is novel and elegant. Related work is mostly covered; citing hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008) and degree-corrected/nested SBMs would strengthen positioning.

Significance: The scalability (independent of the number of communities) and interpretability of hierarchical structure are valuable. The method is likely to be used for exploratory analysis and fast link prediction on large networks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, principled extension of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to networks with exact tree-marginal computation.
- Strong empirical speedups with competitive accuracy; scalable to thousands of nodes.
- Interpretable hierarchical outputs; practical sparse variant.

Cons against acceptance:
- Greedy search with tie-breaking and restarts offers limited posterior uncertainty; no consistency guarantees.
- Limited comparisons to modern hierarchical/degree-corrected SBMs; scope of benchmarks modest.
- Fixed conjugate priors and hyperparameters; no learning of γ or robustness analysis.

Overall: In scope for NeurIPS; a solid and useful contribution. I lean toward acceptance with minor revisions on comparisons and hyperparameter treatment.
