Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model and greedy learning algorithm for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a tree over vertices; each internal node corresponds to a potential community. Conditioned on a tree T, the data likelihood is a mixture over the partitions consistent with T, where within-community edges are modeled with an “on-diagonal” marginal likelihood f and between-child edges with an “off-diagonal” marginal likelihood g. With a specific prior over stopping probabilities at each node (πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|), the marginal likelihood of the data given T admits a dynamic-programming recursion, enabling efficient evaluation.

Learning is posed as greedy agglomerative model selection over a forest: at each step, two trees are merged via one of three operations (join or absorb), chosen by the Bayes factor computed from local sufficient statistics, which are cached to permit O(1) updates. The overall complexity is O(n^2 log n), independent of the number of discovered communities; a “sparse” variant disallows merges between unconnected subgraphs. Predictions are obtained by a simple top-down mixture recursion along the tree path.

The work builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering (BHC) and Bayesian Rose Trees [13,14], extending them from i.i.d. vector data to relational data via a blockmodel likelihood. It is related to the IRM [5,6] and hierarchical variants [8], as well as to nonparametric latent feature approaches (LFRM [18], ILA [10]) and Mondrian-process-based trees [12]. Compared to flat IRM, BHCD imposes a tree-constrained mixture over partitions and replaces the full pairwise block interaction matrix with a single “between-children” likelihood per internal node, trading expressiveness for scalability.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean integration of BHC/Rose tree ideas with stochastic blockmodels via conjugate f/g likelihoods and a tree-induced prior over partitions. The recursion (Eq. 10) is elegant and practically useful.
- Algorithmic efficiency: well-motivated greedy agglomeration with local Bayes factors and careful caching yields O(n^2 log n) time, independent of the number of communities; the sparse variant exploits edge sparsity in practice.
- Practical impact: on NIPS co-authorship, BHCD achieves reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than IRM, and scales to the full graph (2864 nodes), producing interpretable hierarchies.
- Clear exposition: the generative story, prior over partitions, dynamic program, and merge operations are described with sufficient mathematical detail to reproduce.

Weaknesses
- Modeling limitations: At each internal node, all between-child edges are pooled into a single g(σ¬chSS). This collapses the pairwise block interaction matrix to one parameter per node, precluding modeling heterogeneous between-community affinities (e.g., some pairs of child communities are closer than others). In contrast, SBMs/IRM allow θpq per pair. This may limit fidelity on networks with core-periphery or disassortative structure.
- Greedy inference and uncertainty: The approach is purely greedy with tie-breaking and multiple restarts; posterior uncertainty over trees/partitions is not quantified beyond averaging over restarts for prediction. The “sparse” prohibition of unconnected merges is a heuristic that breaks coherence with the specified prior.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Comparisons are mainly to IRM (MCMC and greedy). Results versus ILA/LFRM are cited from prior papers rather than re-run under the same protocol. No comparison to hierarchical IRM [8] or Mondrian relational models [12]. No synthetic experiments with planted hierarchies to assess recovery or robustness, and no ablation on join vs absorb or binary vs non-binary beyond brief qualitative notes.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: The model fixes α,β,δ,λ,γ without sensitivity analysis or learning; the assortativity bias (α>β, δ<λ) is hard-coded, and limitations for non-assortative networks are not discussed.
- Scope and generality: Although the text notes the method can handle directed/weighted graphs “in general,” the likelihood and experiments are for undirected, unweighted graphs with no self-edges, and extensions are not detailed.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The technical core appears sound. The dynamic programming recursion and Bayes factor scoring are carefully derived, and complexity claims are plausible given the cached sufficient statistics. Experimental evidence supports the speed claims and shows competitive predictive accuracy versus IRM. However, the evaluation breadth is modest and does not fully test the model’s limitations; fairness of comparison (hyperparameters, convergence) for IRM could be better documented.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. Equations (2), (7)–(10), and (12) make the model and algorithm precise. Suggestions: (i) explicitly discuss the modeling consequence of pooling all between-child edges into σ¬ch; (ii) provide pseudocode for Algorithm 1 in the main text; (iii) add a brief complexity proof sketch; (iv) discuss hyperparameter roles and sensitivity.
- Originality: The combination of rose-tree/BHC machinery with a blockmodel likelihood for networks, including the specific prior over tree-consistent partitions and the O(1) cached statistics for relational data, is novel and non-trivial. Related hierarchical network models exist, but this formulation occupies a distinct simplicity/speed–expressiveness point.
- Significance: Hierarchical community discovery is important, and a fast Bayesian approach could be widely useful. The method’s speed is a clear practical contribution. That said, the modeling simplifications likely limit state-of-the-art accuracy on complex graphs, and the empirical study does not yet establish broad superiority or comprehensive applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, principled, and fast Bayesian method for hierarchical communities.
- Clear derivations and implementable algorithm with strong runtime benefits.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies and competitive predictions at a fraction of the cost of IRM.

Cons for acceptance
- Expressiveness limited by pooled between-community parameterization.
- Evaluation lacks comparisons to the most relevant hierarchical baselines and lacks synthetic recovery tests and sensitivity analyses.
- Heuristic “sparse” constraint and reliance on greedy search/restraints without uncertainty quantification.

Recommendations and suggestions
- Add experiments on synthetic hierarchical SBMs (both assortative and non-assortative) to test recovery and stress the pooled-between-block assumption.
- Compare empirically to hierarchical IRM [8] and run ILA/LFRM under the same splits; report sensitivity to α,β,δ,λ,γ.
- Clarify the trade-off between speed and expressiveness; discuss possible extensions (e.g., limited number of between-child groups at a node, or a small-rank parameterization) that preserve tractability.
- If possible, release code to support reproducibility.

Overall: I lean to a weak accept. The paper offers a clean, scalable Bayesian approach to an important problem with solid technical content and compelling speedups, despite a somewhat limited experimental scope and modeling simplifications.
