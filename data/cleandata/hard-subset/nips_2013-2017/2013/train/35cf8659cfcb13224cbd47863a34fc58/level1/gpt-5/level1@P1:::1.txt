Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical communities in networks. The model defines a prior over partitions constrained by a given tree T (a rose tree), and uses a blockmodel likelihood with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors for within- and between-cluster edges. The tree induces a set of tree-consistent partitions; the marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed exactly via a dynamic program that recurses over the tree (Eq. 10). Learning proceeds via a greedy, agglomerative model selection algorithm over trees: at each step, two current subtrees are merged by either joining under a new parent or by absorbing one as a child of the other. The merge chosen maximizes a local Bayes factor based on data among the leaves of the merged node. Variants include a “sparse” heuristic that forbids merging disconnected subtrees. Predictive probabilities are obtained by a top-down recursion along the tree.

This work builds on Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (BHC; Heller & Ghahramani 2005) and Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al. 2010) by adapting their tree priors and dynamic programs to relational (edge) data via stochastic blockmodels. It contrasts with the IRM (Kemp et al.) and its hierarchical and latent-attribute extensions (e.g., Herlau et al.; Palla et al.) that use MCMC or iterative search over flat or limited-depth structures, often with higher computational cost. It is also related to hierarchical random graph models (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman 2008), but differs in using a mixture over tree-consistent partitions with conjugate blockmodel marginalization and a greedy Bayesian model selection procedure.

Quality
- Modeling: The tree-constrained mixture over blockmodels is clearly defined. The prior over partitions given a tree (πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|) is well-motivated from rose trees and, combined with conjugate f/g terms, yields a clean dynamic program for exact marginal likelihoods (Eq. 10).
- Inference: The agglomerative algorithm is well specified, with three merge types and a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12). The caching scheme for sufficient statistics (Eq. 13) supports O(1) inner-loop updates; overall complexity O(n^2 log n) is plausible.
- Predictive recursion (Eq. 14) is coherent and efficient.
- Empirics: On Sampson’s monastery data, BHCD recovers interpretable structure; on NIPS-234, BHCD achieves comparable or better predictive performance than IRM much faster, and competitive with LFRM while worse than ILA as expected; on full NIPS, it yields a large hierarchy.

Concerns:
- The greedy, locally computed Bayes factor ignores global interactions; while typical for agglomerative schemes, a discussion of failure modes and search suboptimality (beyond using restarts) would help.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference is provided.
- The model uses the vanilla SBM without degree correction; performance on networks with strong degree heterogeneity may suffer.
- Memory/time scaling: storing σIJ for all pairs is O(n^2) memory; practical limits and sparsity-aware data structures could be discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The generative story, prior/lkhd factorization, and dynamic program are described crisply. The algorithm is easy to follow; however:
- Provide explicit pseudocode (Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown in full here), including how the heap is kept consistent and how ties/restarts are handled for predictions versus tree selection.
- Clarify the “BHCD global” comment (out-of-date heap scores) and whether all experiments maintain exact scores after each merge.
- Specify whether predictive plots on NIPS-234 average over R restarts or use a single best run; ensure a fair comparison protocol versus IRM (e.g., wall-clock budget, hyperparameters, burn-in).

Originality
Adapting Bayesian rose tree machinery to relational data with an SBM likelihood, and showing a dynamic program that integrates over tree-consistent partitions for edges, appears novel. The three-way merge operator and the “sparse” constraint are practical contributions. The work is distinct from IRM and ILA/LFRM in inference style and objective. A more explicit comparison to hierarchical random graphs and nested SBMs would strengthen positioning.

Significance
Efficient hierarchical community discovery is important. BHCD provides:
- A fully probabilistic hierarchy (not just a dendrogram from a linkage criterion),
- Exact marginal likelihoods for a fixed tree,
- A very fast agglomerative procedure with strong empirical speedups over IRM,
- Reasonable predictive performance and interpretable hierarchies on real data.

The approach could be a useful baseline/tool for large networks. Its limitations (no degree correction, greedy search, fixed hyperparameters) temper the impact but do not negate its utility.

Empirical evaluation
- Strengths: Real-data case studies; time-vs-performance curves; multiple BHCD variants; full-scale NIPS hierarchy visualization.
- Missing: Synthetic experiments to test recovery of known hierarchies; comparisons to hierarchical random graph methods and modularity-based hierarchical clustering; sensitivity to γ and Beta priors; results on directed/weighted graphs; memory/runtime scaling curves for increasing n under sparsity.

Pros
- Conceptually clean Bayesian formulation; exact DP for p(D|T).
- Simple, scalable greedy inference; strong speed advantages.
- Produces interpretable hierarchical communities and usable link predictions.
- Practical variants (binary-only, sparse).

Cons
- No degree correction or hyperparameter learning; fixed priors may bias results.
- Greedy, local merge criterion may miss better global structures; limited analysis of robustness.
- Evaluation lacks synthetic recovery and comparisons to key hierarchical baselines (e.g., hierarchical random graph).
- Quadratic memory/time in n in worst case; sparsity benefits are heuristic.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Compare to Clauset–Moore–Newman hierarchical random graph; discuss similarities/differences and empirical comparison.
- Assess sensitivity to hyperparameters; consider empirical Bayes or simple updates for γ and Beta parameters.
- Consider degree-corrected likelihoods (e.g., Karrer & Newman 2011) or mixed-membership variants; discuss feasibility within your DP and greedy framework.
- Provide details on memory usage and the largest feasible n; consider sparse data structures to reduce O(n^2) storage.
- Clarify the prediction protocol and IRM setup to ensure time-budgeted fairness; report variance over restarts.
- Include synthetic experiments with planted hierarchical SBMs to quantify recovery as a function of n, m, and noise.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a neat and practical Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery with clear speed advantages and reasonable accuracy. With stronger empirical comparisons and discussion of limitations (degree heterogeneity, hyperparameters), it would be an easy accept.
