- Proposes a hierarchical extension of the IRM using Bayesian rose tree ideas: hierarchy defines a mixture over consistent partitions and is learned by a greedy agglomerative procedure.
- Presents qualitative results on Sampson's monks and full NIPS, and quantitative results on NIPS-234.
- Inference is much cheaper computationally than the IRM while achieving similar predictive performance.
- Paper is very well written with clear exposition of key ideas.
- Main ideas are drawn from Bayesian rose tree work, but nontrivial adaptation was required for relational data while maintaining computational complexity.
- Scaling Bayesian nonparametric network models is important; the O(n^2) algorithm may be prohibitive for large networks, though a sparse version could be feasible.
- Reviewer requests timings on larger real-world networks (how long does full NIPS take compared to NIPS-234?).
- Questions about scalability to very large networks (gene or social networks with tens of thousands+ nodes) and how inference would scale.
- Suggests evaluating how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree (e.g., by exhaustive search at the largest feasible scale) and seeking theoretical support (e.g., submodularity).
- Minor corrections: wording issues ("whilst... but" on line 48), "pair of trees", "A fruitful next steps".
- Overall verdict: despite familiarity with BRT ideas, the contribution is significant and the paper should be accepted.
