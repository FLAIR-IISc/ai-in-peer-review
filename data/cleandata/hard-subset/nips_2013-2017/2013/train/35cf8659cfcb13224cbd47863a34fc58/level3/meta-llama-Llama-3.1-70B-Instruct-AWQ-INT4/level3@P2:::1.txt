This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, building upon previous work on Bayesian rose trees. The model is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where the hierarchy defines a mixture over consistent partitions and is learned by a greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on several datasets, including Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition of key ideas. The main ideas are drawn from Bayesian rose tree work, but nontrivial adaptation was required for relational data while maintaining computational complexity. The authors present qualitative results on Sampson's monks and full NIPS, and quantitative results on NIPS-234, showing that their model achieves similar predictive performance to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being much cheaper computationally.

The paper's strengths include its ability to scale to larger networks, with a worst-case time complexity of O(n^2 log n), making it suitable for large dense networks. The authors also propose a sparse version of the algorithm, which could be feasible for larger networks. The paper's contribution is significant, and it should be accepted.

However, there are some areas that could be improved. The authors do not provide timings on larger real-world networks, such as how long the full NIPS dataset takes to run compared to NIPS-234. Additionally, the authors do not discuss the scalability of their model to very large networks, such as gene or social networks with tens of thousands+ nodes, and how inference would scale in such cases.

Furthermore, the authors do not evaluate how close their greedy method gets to the optimal tree, for example, by exhaustive search at the largest feasible scale. They also do not provide theoretical support for their greedy method, such as submodularity. These are important considerations for evaluating the effectiveness of their model.

In terms of minor corrections, there are a few wording issues, such as "whilst... but" on line 48, and the phrase "pair of trees" could be rephrased for clarity. Additionally, the phrase "A fruitful next steps" could be rephrased to "A fruitful next step".

Overall, the paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric network models. With some minor revisions to address the above concerns, it should be accepted.
