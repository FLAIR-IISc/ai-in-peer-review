- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model using Bayesian rose tree ideas and a greedy bottom-up agglomerative merge procedure.
- Represents communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels where the tree defines a mixture over consistent partitions.
- Key algorithmic advantage: inference scales O(n² log n) and is independent of the number of communities, making it far more efficient than IRM's O(n²K²).
- Adapts Bayesian rose tree methodology nontrivially to relational data while preserving computational complexity.
- Empirical evaluation on Sampson’s monks, NIPS-234, and full NIPS co-authorship shows BHCD matches or outperforms IRM predictive performance and is roughly two orders of magnitude faster.
- Introduces a sparse variant (BHCD sparse) that performs well on sparse real-world networks like NIPS co-authorship.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, with precise algorithm description, solid theoretical foundation, and good connections to prior literature.
- Strengths summarized: computational efficiency, strong empirical results, practical sparse implementation, and clear exposition.
- Main weakness: scalability concerns for very large networks (tens of thousands of nodes); need more timing/scale experiments (e.g., NIPS-234 vs full NIPS).
- Main weakness: lack of theoretical guarantees about how close the greedy solution is to the optimal tree; suggest exhaustive checks at feasible scales and exploring properties like submodularity.
- Minor issues: a few wording and grammatical errors to correct.
- Overall judgment: significant and well-executed contribution; recommend acceptance.
