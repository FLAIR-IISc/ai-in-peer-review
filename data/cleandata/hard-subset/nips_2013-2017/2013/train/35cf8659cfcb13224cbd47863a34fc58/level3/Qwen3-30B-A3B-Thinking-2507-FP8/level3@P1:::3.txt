

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The work extends the stochastic blockmodel (SBM) and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees as a hierarchical structure, replacing the CRP formulation of IRM with a more efficient tree-based prior (equation 7). The key theoretical contribution lies in the posterior factorization over subtrees, which follows Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004) and enables efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, sufficient statistic handling, and link prediction. The main algorithmic contribution is a greedy agglomerative method (summarized in Algorithm 1) that scales quadratically in the number of vertices while being independent of the number of nested communities.

The paper is well-written with excellent spelling, grammar, and citation practices. The theoretical exposition is succinct and consistent, particularly in the use of f() and g() functions for on-diagonal and off-diagonal blocks. The authors successfully connect their work to previous NIPS contributions on community detection, including the IRM (Kemp et al., 2006), Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005), and the Mondrian process (Roy & Teh, 2008).

The main strengths of the paper include:
- Clear theoretical development of the hierarchical mixture model
- Efficient inference algorithm with O(n² log n) time complexity (better than IRM's O(n²K²) and other Bayesian nonparametric methods)
- Demonstrated speedup (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) with comparable accuracy
- Proper handling of hierarchical structure with the tree-based prior
- Well-organized presentation with clear connections to prior work

The primary weaknesses are:
- The evaluation methodology is inadequate for a NIPS paper. The authors use AUC for link prediction without explaining how it was computed (presumably by deleting links), while NMI (Normalized Mutual Information) is the standard metric for comparing community detection results. The paper should have included NMI scores for a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets (Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship network subset), which does not demonstrate scalability or generalization to larger networks. Recent SBM evaluations use much larger datasets (e.g., Facebook, Twitter), which would better establish the method's practical utility.
- The experimental comparisons are insufficient. The authors only compare BHCD to IRM (a Bayesian nonparametric method), but should have included comparisons to semi-parametric methods (e.g., LFRM, ILA) and other hierarchical community detection approaches. The paper mentions these in Table 1 but doesn't provide sufficient comparative results.
- The paper claims "comparable or better accuracy" than IRM but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these claims.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the experimental shortcomings. While the algorithmic efficiency is impressive and the theoretical contribution is sound, the lack of comprehensive evaluation on larger datasets and against a broader range of baselines prevents a strong claim of advancing the state of the art. The paper would have been significantly stronger with:
1) A clear explanation of the AUC computation method
2) Inclusion of NMI scores for community structure evaluation
3) Experiments on larger, more challenging datasets
4) Comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods

The quality of the paper is high in terms of theoretical contribution and algorithmic design, but the experimental evaluation lacks the depth required for a NIPS paper. The clarity is excellent, and the originality is strong in the algorithmic approach (the greedy agglomerative method with tree-based prior), though the theoretical foundation builds on existing work.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Novel algorithmic approach with strong theoretical justification
- Clear, well-written presentation
- Significant speedup (two orders of magnitude) over IRM
- Proper handling of hierarchical structure with tree-based prior
- Good connections to previous NIPS work

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Inadequate evaluation methodology (lacks NMI, unclear AUC computation)
- Limited to small-scale datasets without demonstrating scalability
- Insufficient comparative analysis against relevant baselines
- Experimental results are illustrative rather than conclusive

While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of community detection, the experimental evaluation fails to adequately demonstrate the significance of the proposed method. The authors should expand their evaluation to include standard metrics (NMI), larger datasets, and more comprehensive comparisons with relevant baselines before the work can be considered a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical framework is strong and the algorithm is efficient, but the lack of robust empirical validation limits the paper's impact. I recommend rejection due to insufficient experimental evaluation, though the core algorithmic idea is promising and would be strengthened by the additional experiments.
