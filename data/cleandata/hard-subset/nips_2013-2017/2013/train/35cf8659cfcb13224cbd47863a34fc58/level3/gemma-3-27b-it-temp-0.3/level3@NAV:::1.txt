## Review of “Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery”

This paper presents a novel and efficient Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, learned via a greedy agglomerative procedure, building upon Bayesian rose tree concepts. This approach offers a compelling alternative to existing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), particularly regarding computational cost. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, model details, and algorithmic procedure, making the paper exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

The strength of this work lies in its pragmatic approach to scaling Bayesian nonparametric models for relational data. While drawing inspiration from Bayesian rose trees, the authors demonstrate nontrivial adaptation to maintain computational efficiency – a crucial aspect for real-world network analysis. The reported results on Sampson’s monks, full NIPS, and NIPS-234 are promising. The quantitative comparison to the IRM on NIPS-234 highlights a significant speedup (two orders of magnitude) with comparable predictive performance. 

However, the paper would benefit from further discussion on scalability. While the O(n^2) complexity is acknowledged, timings on the full NIPS dataset compared to NIPS-234 would be valuable for assessing practical limitations. The reviewer questions how inference would scale to very large networks (tens of thousands of nodes) common in domains like gene or social networks. Investigating a sparse version of the algorithm, as hinted at, is a logical next step. 

Furthermore, exploring the quality of the greedy approach would strengthen the work. Evaluating how closely the greedy method approximates the optimal tree (perhaps via exhaustive search on smaller scales) and seeking theoretical justification (e.g., demonstrating submodularity of the objective function) would be beneficial. Minor textual corrections are noted ("whilst... but", "pair of trees", "A fruitful next steps").

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant contribution in scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods for relational data.
*   Clear exposition of key ideas and a well-written manuscript.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating speed and accuracy.
*   Nontrivial adaptation of existing Bayesian rose tree ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of scalability to very large networks.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis or empirical validation of the greedy approach's optimality.

**Overall:** Despite my familiarity with the underlying Bayesian rose tree concepts, I believe this paper presents a significant contribution. The authors have successfully adapted and extended these ideas to a challenging problem, achieving a compelling balance between computational efficiency and predictive performance. I recommend acceptance.
