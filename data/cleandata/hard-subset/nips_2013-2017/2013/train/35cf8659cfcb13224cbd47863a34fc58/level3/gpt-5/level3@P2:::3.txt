Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a given tree (a “rose tree”), resulting in a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). The likelihood integrates out block probabilities with conjugate Beta priors, using different hyperparameters for within- and between-cluster edges. A key modeling choice is to replace the CRP-based partition prior of the IRM with a tree-consistent prior (Eq. 7) parameterized by πS at each internal node, encouraging non-binary trees when structure is weak. This yields a factorization of the marginal likelihood over subtrees (Eq. 10), enabling exact dynamic programming across all partitions consistent with the tree and efficient computation of link-prediction posteriors (Eq. 14).

The main algorithmic contribution is a greedy agglomerative model-selection procedure (Algorithm 1) that merges subtrees using a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12). The method maintains and updates sufficient statistics to make per-iteration costs O(1), yielding overall O(n^2 log n) time, independent of the number of communities; a sparse variant forbids merging completely disconnected subtrees. Empirically, the method produces reasonable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery data and achieves link-prediction performance on the NIPS co-authorship network comparable to or better than IRM, while being substantially faster; complexity comparisons are summarized in Table 1.

The work sits at the intersection of SBMs [Holland et al.], IRM [Kemp et al.], and Bayesian hierarchical clustering using rose trees [Blundell et al.]. It relates to hierarchical IRM-style models [e.g., Herlau et al.] and the Mondrian process for tree priors [Roy & Teh], but emphasizes computational efficiency via subtree factorization akin to ideas used in exact decompositions for hierarchical models (cf. dynamic-programming factorizations in Koivisto & Sood, 2004).

Quality
- Soundness: The probabilistic construction and subtree-wise factorization (Eqs. 7–10) are clear and appear correct. The three-way merge operations (join/absorb) are well-motivated and the local scoring with cached sufficient statistics is technically solid.
- Assumptions/limitations: The greedy local score optimizes a myopic objective and can be sensitive to tie-breaking; the authors mitigate this with restarts. The “sparse” prohibition on merging disconnected subtrees is practically sensible but deviates from the stated generative story; a short discussion of its Bayesian justification or its effect on model evidence would help.
- Empirics: Results nicely illustrate behavior, but the experimental section is largely illustrative. The comparison to IRM is helpful, but broader controlled comparisons and clearer evaluation protocols are needed (details below). Hyperparameters are fixed; no sensitivity analysis is shown.

Clarity
- The paper is well-written and well-organized. Notation is consistent (f and g distinguish on/off-diagonal blocks). Figures effectively convey the hierarchy/partition relationship.
- Minor style: In NIPS style, avoid starting sentences with citations; rephrase to keep citations parenthetical.
- Please detail the evaluation protocol for AUC: fraction of held-out positives, how negatives are sampled, per-fold class balance, whether ROC is computed on all dyads or a subsample, and whether cross-validation is edge- or node-pair-stratified. If links are deleted, specify whether training preserves graph connectivity.
- Consider reporting additional metrics common in SBM comparisons, e.g., NMI/ARI for flat partitions (from an appropriate tree cut), and calibration (Brier/log-loss) for link prediction. For Sampson, report NMI/ARI against the known groups.
- A brief hyperparameter sensitivity study (α,β,δ,λ,γ) would increase reproducibility.

Originality
- Combining SBMs with rose-tree priors to induce a distribution over tree-consistent partitions and deriving an exact subtree-factorized marginal likelihood for fast greedy search is a neat synthesis. The three-way merge proposal with cached sufficient statistics for O(1) inner-loop updates is a practical contribution.
- Relative to hierarchical IRM variants and Mondrian priors, the novelty is in the specific factorization and scalable greedy inference. The paper could more explicitly contrast its prior and inference to those works, and to multiscale/hierarchical blockmodels (e.g., Ho et al., AISTATS 2011), clarifying when BHCD’s assumptions are advantageous.

Significance
- If broadly validated, BHCD offers a useful point on the accuracy–speed trade-off: substantially faster than IRM while retaining a principled Bayesian treatment and producing interpretable hierarchies.
- Current evidence is promising but limited. To strengthen significance, include: (i) larger benchmark networks commonly used in SBM evaluations; (ii) comparisons beyond Bayesian nonparametrics, e.g., degree-corrected SBMs or spectral/flow-based methods; (iii) ablations (binary vs. non-binary trees; with/without the sparse constraint).

Pros
- Clear, principled model with exact subtree marginalization and efficient greedy search.
- Scales as O(n^2 log n), independent of number of communities; sparse variant practical.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies; link prediction is straightforward and fast.
- Empirical speedups over IRM are substantial; accuracy is competitive.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks clarity on AUC protocol.
- No comparisons to strong semi-parametric SBMs (e.g., degree-corrected) or other hierarchical blockmodels.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters, tie-breaking, and restarts not thoroughly explored.
- The sparse variant’s constraint is not fully reconciled with the generative model.

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify link-prediction/AUC protocol and add NMI/ARI (and possibly VI) for partition quality at selected cuts.
- Add larger datasets and broader baselines (e.g., degree-corrected SBM; hierarchical random graphs; multiscale blockmodel) to position BHCD among fast community discovery methods.
- Provide sensitivity analyses (γ and Beta priors), runtime vs. sparsity, and stability across restarts.
- Discuss theoretical or empirical guidance for choosing the sparse prohibition, and quantify its effect on evidence and predictive performance.

Overall assessment
Technically solid and clearly presented, with an elegant factorization enabling fast hierarchical community discovery. However, the empirical evaluation is not yet commensurate with the claims. With a strengthened experimental section and clearer evaluation details, this could be a strong contribution. As is, I lean toward a weak reject, primarily due to the limited and somewhat underspecified empirical validation.
