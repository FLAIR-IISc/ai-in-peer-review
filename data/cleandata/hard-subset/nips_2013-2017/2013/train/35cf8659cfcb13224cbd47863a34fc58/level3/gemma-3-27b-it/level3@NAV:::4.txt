## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The approach leverages Bayesian rose trees combined with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood, aiming for efficient agglomerative clustering. The core novelty lies in adapting the likelihood computation and updating sufficient statistics to enable scalable hierarchical clustering, achieving a claimed O(nodes + edges) complexity. 

The paper builds upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical modeling (IRM, Mondrian process) and greedy hierarchical clustering, offering a pragmatic trade-off between expressiveness and computational efficiency. The authors clearly position their work within the existing landscape of community detection algorithms, referencing relevant literature. The idea of reusing prior tree structure and agglomerative updates is a sensible direction to improve scalability. 

A key strength is the reported speedup compared to the IRM – achieving comparable or better accuracy two orders of magnitude faster. The experimental results on Sampson’s monastery network demonstrate qualitative success, and the NIPS co-authorship dataset showcases competitive predictive performance. The averaging over hypotheses through multiple tree restarts appears to improve robustness. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the presentation is generally clear, Section 3 is dense and the derivations following equation (9) are difficult to follow. The explanations regarding the dynamic programming and recursive sufficient statistic updates lack sufficient detail; a more thorough walkthrough of these key computations is needed. Most critically, the experimental evaluation lacks comparison to established hierarchical network models like the multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al. 2012] and the Mondrian process [Roy et al. 2009]. This omission severely limits the ability to assess the true contribution of BHCD. Furthermore, the use of “nonparametric” terminology is questionable given the reliance on a prior framing of rose trees. While the authors discuss scalability, the tested networks are relatively small, and empirical scalability on larger datasets remains undemonstrated. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   Integrates existing work effectively.
*   Demonstrates a practical speedup over IRM.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited originality; incremental improvement over existing methods.
*   Insufficient explanation of key algorithmic components.
*   Lack of comparison to relevant hierarchical network models.
*   Scalability claims not fully supported by experiments.



**Overall:** This paper presents a solid technical contribution but falls short of being truly impactful due to its limited novelty and incomplete evaluation. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses, particularly a more comprehensive comparative evaluation against existing hierarchical models and a clearer exposition of the algorithmic details.
