This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method, extending the stochastic blockmodel to a tree-structured mixture, enabling inference of hierarchical network structures. The approach introduces a greedy agglomerative learning algorithm for efficient tree selection, demonstrating speed improvements over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) with comparable accuracy on social connectivity data.

The work addresses a relevant problem in network analysis – the need for scalable and interpretable methods for discovering hierarchical community structures, particularly useful in social science applications. Prior work, such as the IRM and its extensions, often suffer from computational limitations when dealing with larger networks. The key technical insight – restricting the partition space to tree structures – is a clever way to achieve efficient marginal likelihood computation, circumventing the exponential complexity of the IRM’s flat partition space.  The authors correctly position their work within the landscape of Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, citing relevant literature (e.g., Holland et al., Airoldi et al., Kemp et al., Xu et al.). The comparison to existing methods like LFRM and ILA in the NIPS dataset context is also valuable.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The empirical results are compelling – BHCD achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to the IRM while maintaining comparable or even better performance in terms of AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy. The application to Sampson’s monastery network provides a qualitative illustration of the model’s ability to uncover meaningful hierarchical structures. The scalability demonstrated on the NIPS co-authorship network is a strong point. The introduction of BHCD sparse is a pragmatic improvement for sparse network analysis.

However, the paper suffers from clarity and presentation issues that hinder a full appreciation of the technical contributions. The most significant weakness is the lack of a clear connection between the greedy tree-learning procedure (Algorithm 1) and the underlying Bayesian generative model. It’s unclear whether the algorithm performs valid posterior inference or simply finds a Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) or Maximum Likelihood (ML) estimate.  Section 4, in particular, needs substantial clarification to resolve this conceptual confusion. The authors state the score is a "Bayes factor improvement," but don't fully explain how this relates to a posterior probability or if approximations are involved.

Further issues detract from clarity. There are numerous spelling and grammatical errors throughout the paper. The writing style is often vague (“additional care is needed…”), and some descriptions are unclear (e.g., the explanation of how ties are broken in the greedy search). The dataset descriptions are insufficient – more detail on the NIPS co-authorship network would be helpful. Some mathematical notation seems unnecessarily obscure, and the figures are confusing, particularly Figure 2, which requires careful study to understand the merging options. The introduction of notation like σ¬chSS without sufficient context also hinders readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The hierarchical extension of the stochastic blockmodel and the efficient tree-learning algorithm represent a novel contribution.
*   **Strong Results:** The empirical results demonstrate significant speed improvements and comparable or better accuracy compared to existing methods.
*   **Relevance:** The problem addressed is relevant to the NIPS audience and has potential applications in social network analysis and related fields.
*   **Scalability:**  The algorithm's scalability is a significant advantage over existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, hindering a full understanding of the technical contributions.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The connection between the greedy algorithm and the Bayesian generative model is unclear, raising concerns about the validity of the inference procedure.
*   **Presentation:** The writing quality and presentation of results need substantial improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, the core ideas and empirical results are promising. If the authors address the clarity and presentation issues, particularly by providing a more rigorous justification for the greedy algorithm and improving the writing quality, this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance contingent upon major revisions addressing the points outlined above. A clearer explanation of Section 4 is paramount.
