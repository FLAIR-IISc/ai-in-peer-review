This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a probabilistic, tree-structured model for hierarchical community detection in networks. The model views a hierarchy T as inducing a prior over a set of partitions Φ(T); conditioned on T, it exactly marginalizes over all tree-consistent partitions via a dynamic program. The likelihood is a blockmodel: on-diagonal blocks use f (within-community density), off-diagonal blocks use g (between-community density), instantiated as conjugate Beta-Bernoulli with different hyperparameters to encourage dense within-cluster and sparse across-cluster edges. Learning proceeds by greedy agglomeration over trees with three merge types (join and two “absorptions”) as in Bayesian rose trees, selecting merges by a local Bayes factor. Efficient caching of sufficient statistics yields O(1) per local update and O(n^2 log n) overall time (worst case), while the marginal likelihood p(D|T) can be computed in O(n+m) by recursion. A sparse variant forbids merging disconnected subtrees. Predictions are computed along a single path in the tree as a mixture of on/off-diagonal posteriors.

Relation to prior work: The stochastic blockmodel and mixed-membership SBM require a fixed number of flat communities; IRM marginalizes over partitions via a CRP but is flat and computationally heavier. BHCD adopts the Bayesian rose-tree prior and agglomerative structure (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.) and adapts it to relational data with a blockmodel likelihood. Compared to hierarchical extensions of IRM and related BNP network models (e.g., Herlau et al.; Ho et al.’s multiscale community blockmodel; Mondrian process with relational likelihood), BHCD trades some modeling richness for much faster, one-pass agglomerative inference.

Quality: The model is technically sound, with a clear probabilistic construction and a sensible merge score grounded in Bayes factors. The dynamic program for p(D|T) and the caching strategy for sufficient statistics are compelling and appear to justify the stated complexity. Experiments demonstrate good link-prediction performance relative to IRM and qualitative recovery of structure on Sampson’s monastery data. However, key derivations (especially the definition and use of σ¬chSS in eq. (9) and the recursion in eq. (10)) are terse; the O(n+m) claim for likelihood and O(1) update cost deserve fuller exposition. The experimental section focuses primarily on IRM baselines; comparisons to existing hierarchical network models are missing. Hyperparameters are fixed without sensitivity analysis, and the impact of tie-breaking/restar ts (R=50) on variability is not quantified.

Clarity: The paper is generally readable, but Section 3 is dense. Notation swaps between S and T, and the blocks DTT, σSS, σ¬chSS could be explained more thoroughly with a worked example and diagrams. Algorithm 1 would benefit from explicit pseudocode detailing cached quantities, memory complexity, and how eq. (13) supports O(1) updates. The predictive recursion (eq. (14)) states complexity as linear in depth but also writes Θ(n); please clarify that it is O(depth(T)). Minor editorial issues (e.g., capitalization, subject-verb agreement) can be fixed easily.

Originality: The main novelty lies in coupling rose-tree model selection with a blockmodel likelihood for networks and engineering an efficient caching scheme. Conceptually, the framework is a natural extension of Bayesian rose trees to relational data rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm. The paper positions itself appropriately but should better differentiate from hierarchical network models such as hierarchical random graphs (Clauset et al., 2008), the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., AISTATS 2011/2012), and Mondrian-process-based relational models (Roy & Teh, NIPS 2009).

Significance: A fast, probabilistic method for hierarchical community discovery is valuable. The ability to exactly marginalize over tree-consistent partitions and to produce calibrated predictions with orders-of-magnitude speedup over IRM is practically important. That said, empirical validation is limited to relatively modest networks (NIPS-234 and NIPS 1–17). Demonstrating performance and runtime on larger graphs (e.g., >10k–100k nodes) or synthetic scaling experiments would substantially strengthen the impact.

Specific suggestions:
- Expand Section 3: provide a full derivation of eqs. (9)–(10), a schematic example illustrating σSS and σ¬chSS, and a proof sketch of the O(n+m) dynamic program.
- Detail Algorithm 1’s caching: list maintained statistics, update rules, and memory cost; clarify handling of directed/self-looped/missing edges.
- Compare to hierarchical models: include HRG (Clauset et al., 2008), the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., 2011/2012), and Mondrian-based hierarchical relational models; discuss trade-offs in accuracy vs. speed.
- Clarify “nonparametric”: the method adaptively selects tree size via model selection; explain in what sense it is nonparametric and consider tempering the claim or connecting to infinite-tree priors.
- Report sensitivity to α,β,δ,λ,γ; consider simple hyperparameter inference or empirical Bayes for robustness.
- Quantify variability across restarts and tie-breaks; report variance bands and how often the top-scoring tree recurs.
- Strengthen scalability evidence: add wall-clock/run-time scaling plots vs. m and n; include sparse graphs with m ≪ n^2; compare to optimized IRM and to fast hierarchical heuristics.

Pros:
- Clear probabilistic formulation with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Efficient agglomerative inference with well-engineered sufficient-statistic caching.
- Strong practical speedups; competitive link prediction vs. IRM.
- Intuitive hierarchies and qualitative success on classic datasets; sparse variant is sensible.

Cons:
- Limited originality relative to rose trees; main novelty is adaptation/engineering.
- Missing comparisons to key hierarchical network models (HRG, multiscale community blockmodel, Mondrian relational).
- Dense and under-explained derivations in Section 3; algorithmic details insufficient for easy reproduction.
- Empirical scalability not fully demonstrated; small set of datasets; fixed hyperparameters; no variance analysis across restarts.
- “Nonparametric” terminology potentially misleading given the model-selection framing.

Overall: A solid, well-motivated contribution that is technically sound and practically useful, but it would benefit from clearer derivations and broader empirical comparisons, especially to contemporary hierarchical models, and from stronger evidence of scalability. With these improvements, the work would make a valuable addition to the literature on hierarchical community discovery.
