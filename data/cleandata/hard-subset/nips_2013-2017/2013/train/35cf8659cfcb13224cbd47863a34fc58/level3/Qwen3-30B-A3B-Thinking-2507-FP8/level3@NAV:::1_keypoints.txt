- Proposes BHCD: extends the Infinite Relational Model by incorporating Bayesian rose tree ideas to define a mixture over consistent partitions for hierarchical community discovery.
- Key innovation is a greedy agglomerative procedure that efficiently learns hierarchical community structure while remaining computationally tractable.
- BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to IRM while offering significantly faster inference (≈ two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration).
- Worst-case time complexity is O(n² log n), and inference complexity is independent of the number of communities.
- Adapts Bayesian rose tree methodology to relational data, requiring nontrivial modifications to retain computational efficiency.
- Technical approach is well-motivated with clear exposition of the hierarchical mixture model and greedy inference algorithm.
- Experiments on Sampson’s monks and NIPS co-authorship networks provide convincing qualitative and quantitative results.
- BHCD sparse variant performs particularly well on sparse networks.
- Strengths: clarity of exposition, strong empirical validation, solid theoretical grounding, and a well-executed adaptation of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
- Weaknesses: limited scalability analysis for very large networks (tens of thousands of nodes); O(n²) complexity may be prohibitive in practice.
- Recommend providing timing comparisons for full NIPS versus NIPS-234 to better characterize computational tradeoffs.
- Suggest investigating the greedy method’s optimality (e.g., exhaustive search on small datasets) and providing theoretical support (e.g., submodularity analysis).
- Minor grammatical issues noted but easily fixable.
- Overall assessment: significant, technically sound contribution with compelling results and clear practical value; recommend acceptance.
