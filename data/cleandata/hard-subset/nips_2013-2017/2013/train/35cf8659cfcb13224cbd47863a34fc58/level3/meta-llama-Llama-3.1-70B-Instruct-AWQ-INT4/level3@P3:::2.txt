The paper "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, and the authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms for inferring the hierarchical community structure. The algorithm scales quadratically in the number of vertices of the network and is independent of the number of nested communities.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of community discovery in social networks, discussing the limitations of previous approaches, including the stochastic blockmodel and the mixed membership stochastic blockmodel. They also review existing Bayesian approaches to inferring community structure, including the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and its extensions.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure. The model is based on a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where each internal node of the tree corresponds to a community, and the leaves of the tree are the vertices of the adjacency matrix. The authors derive a recursive formula for the marginal likelihood of the tree, which can be computed efficiently using dynamic programming.

The authors also propose a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm for inferring the hierarchical community structure. The algorithm starts with a forest of trivial trees, each corresponding to a single vertex, and iteratively merges trees based on a Bayes factor score. The algorithm terminates when only one tree remains, which represents the inferred hierarchical community structure.

The paper presents experimental results on three datasets: Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS co-authorship network. The results show that the proposed algorithm, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), is able to discover reasonable hierarchies and make predictions about two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM, while achieving comparable performance.
