This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to define a model as a mixture over a tree-structured set of stochastic blockmodels. The hierarchical tree structure constrains the space of possible partitions, allowing the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree to be calculated exactly and efficiently via a dynamic programming recursion. The paper then proposes a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm to learn this tree structure. Starting with each node as its own community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of subtrees that provide the largest increase in the model's evidence, as measured by a local Bayes factor.

This work builds directly upon previous research in Bayesian agglomerative clustering, particularly the Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT) of Blundell et al. (2011) and the earlier work of Heller & Ghahramani (2005). The main contribution is the non-trivial adaptation of this framework from i.i.d. data to relational data, where the likelihood is based on a stochastic blockmodel. This contrasts with other Bayesian nonparametric approaches for network data, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which typically infers a flat partition using MCMC methods. While hierarchical extensions to the IRM exist, they are often computationally expensive. BHCD provides a much faster, deterministic alternative.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the marginal likelihood of a tree (Eq. 10) as a recursive sum over tree-consistent partitions is elegant and appears correct. The use of a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12) for the greedy merge criterion is well-justified within the Bayesian model selection framework. The claims of computational efficiency and predictive performance are strongly supported by the experimental results. The comparison with the IRM on the NIPS-234 dataset is particularly compelling, demonstrating a significant speed advantage for comparable accuracy. The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network show that the method produces interpretable hierarchies.

One potential weakness is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. While clearly necessary for computational tractability, it provides no guarantee of finding the optimal hierarchy. The quality of the final solution may depend on the order of merges, and the paper notes that tie-breaking is done randomly, suggesting multiple runs are needed for a robust result.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the stochastic blockmodel, building up to the hierarchical model, and then describing the agglomerative inference procedure. The model's generative process is described intuitively, and the recursive formula for the marginal likelihood is presented clearly. Figures 1 and 2 are very helpful for visualizing the model structure and the merge operations. The algorithm is presented with sufficient detail.

**Originality:** While the algorithmic framework (greedy agglomerative clustering guided by Bayes factors) is borrowed from the Bayesian Rose Tree literature, its application to relational data is novel and constitutes a significant contribution. The authors have successfully adapted the likelihood calculations for the stochastic blockmodel setting and developed a caching scheme for the sufficient statistics that maintains the algorithm's O(n^2 log n) time complexity. This is a non-trivial engineering and modeling achievement that clearly distinguishes this work from prior art. The paper correctly positions itself relative to both the IRM and Bayesian hierarchical clustering literature.

**Significance:** This work addresses the crucial problem of scalability in Bayesian network modeling. MCMC methods for models like the IRM can be prohibitively slow on even moderately sized networks. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster while achieving similar predictive performance, this paper makes a valuable contribution. The proposed algorithm makes hierarchical Bayesian community detection practical for a wider range of networks. Researchers and practitioners looking for a fast and principled way to explore hierarchical structure in networks are likely to use or build upon these ideas. The O(n^2) complexity is still a bottleneck for very large, dense networks, but the "sparse" variant shows promise for the sparse networks common in many real-world applications.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

1.  The speedup on the NIPS-234 dataset is impressive. To better assess the practical scalability of the method, could the authors report the wall-clock time for the experiment on the full NIPS dataset (n=2864)? This would provide a valuable data point for understanding how the runtime scales in practice.

2.  The paper's discussion mentions that the O(n^2 log n) inference complexity is a potential limitation for scaling to very large networks. How do the authors envision this method performing on networks with tens or hundreds of thousands of nodes? Does the empirical performance of the `BHCD sparse` variant suggest a path toward near-linear scaling in the number of edges for sparse graphs?

3.  Regarding the greedy nature of the algorithm: Have the authors considered evaluating its performance against an optimal solution? For a very small toy network (e.g., n=8-10), it might be feasible to enumerate all possible binary trees and find the one with the highest marginal likelihood, providing a gold standard against which the greedy result could be compared. Furthermore, is there any connection to submodularity that might provide some theoretical justification for the greedy approach's performance?

4.  Minor wording suggestions:
    *   Line 48: "whilst each iteration often scales unfavourably... but comes at considerable computational cost" (in reference to another paper) feels slightly redundant. Perhaps simplify.
    *   Line 223: "a pair of tree to merge" should be "a pair of trees to merge".
    *   Line 318: "a fruitful next steps" should be "a fruitful next step" or "fruitful next steps".

**Summary of Recommendation**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important problem of scalable inference for Bayesian network models.
*   Presents a novel and non-trivial adaptation of the Bayesian Rose Tree framework to relational data.
*   Demonstrates a dramatic speedup (two orders of magnitude) over a key baseline (IRM) with comparable predictive accuracy.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and technically sound.
*   The method produces interpretable hierarchical structures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The greedy inference procedure lacks theoretical guarantees of optimality.
*   The O(n^2) worst-case complexity may still be prohibitive for very large-scale networks.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The contribution is solid, the experimental results are compelling, and the work represents a clear advance in making sophisticated Bayesian network analysis more practical. I strongly recommend acceptance.
