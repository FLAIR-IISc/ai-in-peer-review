## Review of Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, framing it as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors leverage a greedy agglomerative approach for model selection, claiming efficiency and scalability, particularly in comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The core technical contribution lies in adapting the likelihood and efficiently updating sufficient statistics to facilitate this clustering process for network data.

The work builds upon a strong foundation in Bayesian community detection and hierarchical clustering. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers such as the stochastic blockmodel [Holland et al., 1971], the mixed membership stochastic blockmodel [Airoldi et al., 2008], and the IRM [Kemp et al., 2006; Xu et al., 2006; Mørup & Schmidt, 2008]. The proposed approach addresses a known limitation of many existing Bayesian methods – their computational cost – by offering a greedy algorithm that aims for a favorable trade-off between accuracy and speed.  The paper also clearly connects to prior work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [Heller & Ghahramani, 2009; Blundell et al., 2013], extending these ideas to relational data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The model appears technically sound, integrating a well-established likelihood (stochastic blockmodel) with a hierarchical prior (rose trees). The proposed algorithm, while complex, is presented with a clear attempt at theoretical justification, particularly in the discussion of computational complexity.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate a significant speed advantage over a single iteration of the IRM in their experiments, which is a major benefit. The claimed O(n^2 log n) worst-case complexity, though not fully verified on very large networks, is promising.
*   **Clear Presentation (Overall):** The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the paper's contribution. The experimental setup is well-defined.
*   **Practical Application:** The inclusion of link prediction as an application demonstrates the potential utility of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (Section 3):** The derivations in Section 3, particularly equation (9) and the subsequent manipulations, are dense and difficult to follow. More detailed explanations and intermediate steps are needed to aid understanding.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Algorithm Explanation:** The descriptions of the dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates are lacking in detail. It’s difficult to fully grasp how these computations are optimized without more explicit explanations.
*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:**  A major weakness is the lack of comparison to established hierarchical network models. The paper fails to benchmark against methods like the multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al., 2012] or the Mondrian process [Roy et al., 2009].  Without such comparisons, it’s difficult to assess the true novelty and performance of the proposed approach.
*   **"Nonparametric" Terminology:**  The use of the term "nonparametric" feels somewhat misleading. While the model avoids specifying the number of communities *a priori*, the rose tree prior is a parametric structure. It would be more accurate to characterize the work as a Bayesian model selection approach.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The scalability claims, while theoretically appealing, are not rigorously validated through experiments on genuinely large-scale networks. The datasets used, while valuable, are relatively modest in size.
*    **Algorithm Variations:** The many different variations of the algorithm (BHCD, BHCD sparse, etc.) muddy the experimental results and make it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the impact of different design choices.

**Originality:**

The originality of the work is somewhat limited. The core ideas of combining Bayesian methods with hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels are not entirely new. However, the specific adaptation of rose trees to relational data, coupled with the efficient algorithm for model selection, does represent a contribution.  The efficiency gains are valuable, but the core innovation doesn’t represent a paradigm shift.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis – identifying hierarchical community structure – and offers a potentially useful tool for tackling this problem.  The efficiency improvements could make Bayesian hierarchical community detection more accessible for larger networks. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the lack of compelling comparisons to existing methods. If the method proves robust and scalable on very large datasets, it could become a valuable addition to the toolkit of network analysts.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Presents a technically sound and well-motivated Bayesian model for hierarchical community detection.
*   Demonstrates significant speed improvements over existing methods (IRM).
*   Provides a clear and generally well-written presentation of the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks a thorough comparative evaluation against state-of-the-art hierarchical network models.
*   The explanation of key algorithmic details is insufficient.
*   Scalability claims need further validation on larger datasets.
*   The use of “nonparametric” terminology is debatable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of network analysis.  However, the weaknesses related to comparative evaluation and algorithmic clarity prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance at this time. I believe the paper would significantly benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to existing hierarchical network models and a more detailed explanation of the algorithm's implementation. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address these concerns and resubmit to a future conference.
