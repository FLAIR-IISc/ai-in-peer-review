- Paper introduces BHCD, a greedy agglomerative method for hierarchical community discovery using tree-structured mixtures of stochastic blockmodels, claiming O(n² log n) runtime.
- Key technical contributions: dynamic programming for marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) and efficient sufficient-statistic caching (Eq. 13).
- Main empirical claim: two orders of magnitude faster inference than IRM while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy on co-authorship networks.
- Demonstrated speed/accuracy on NIPS-234: BHCD sparse ≈10s vs IRM ≈1000s with similar AUC/log-likelihood.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies (e.g., Sampson’s monastery) and supports ensemble predictions via multiple restarts.
- Algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and ablation of BHCD variants strengthen the empirical analysis.
- Primary weakness: missing comparisons to existing hierarchical models (e.g., Ho et al. 2012, Roy et al. Mondrian), undermining the hierarchical contribution.
- Terminology issue: labeling BHCD as "nonparametric" is misleading because it performs greedy model selection over finite trees, not Bayesian nonparametric inference.
- Method exposition is insufficiently clear (especially Eq. 9, recursion in Eq. 10 and forest likelihood Eq. 11); needs concrete examples to clarify sufficient-statistic updates.
- Scalability claims are under-validated: large networks (full NIPS 2864) used only qualitatively and no timings on networks >5k nodes are provided.
- Originality is limited: adaptation of Heller et al. (2005) to networks and computational optimizations are incremental relative to prior hierarchical blockmodels.
- Recommendation: technically sound but requires major revisions—add hierarchical baseline comparisons, correct terminology, clarify Section 3 with examples, and validate scalability; current verdict: Weak Accept pending revisions.
