Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), which adapts Bayesian rose trees to network data by replacing iid likelihoods with a block-model likelihood. A given rose tree T induces a prior over partitions φ consistent with T and a factorized marginal likelihood over blocks via on-diagonal f(·) and off-diagonal g(·) terms. The key recursion (Eq. 10) allows dynamic programming to evaluate p(D|T) using sufficient statistics computed over the tree, with claimed O(n + m) time for likelihood evaluation. Learning proceeds via a greedy agglomerative procedure (as in Bayesian rose trees) that merges subtrees using Bayes factors computed from local sufficient statistics; caching yields O(1) per inner-loop update and overall O(n^2 log n) time. Variants include a “sparse” version that forbids merges between disconnected subgraphs and binary-only merges. For prediction, the authors propose a recursive mixture along a path in the tree.

The method builds directly on Bayesian rose trees (Blundell et al. 2010) and Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani 2005), replacing the iid likelihood with a stochastic blockmodel marginal (closely related to the IRM’s marginal likelihood). Compared to flat models such as the IRM and LFRM, BHCD trades modeling flexibility for speed. Several earlier works infer hierarchical structure in networks (e.g., hierarchical IRM variants, Mondrian process for relational data (Roy & Teh, 2009), and multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., 2012)), which provide natural points of comparison.

Quality
- The technical core—adapting the rose-tree dynamic program to block-structured sufficient statistics and enabling O(1) merge updates—appears sound and is the main novelty.
- The O(n + m) likelihood recursion (Eq. 10) is plausible given cached σ statistics, but the paper does not fully spell out how σSS and σ¬chSS are computed and maintained without hidden quadratic factors or heavy memory usage.
- Empirically, BHCD is substantially faster than IRM and achieves similar or better predictive performance on NIPS-234 and qualitatively reasonable structure on Sampson; averaging over multiple greedy runs improves prediction.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-organized, but Section 3 is dense. Equation (9) and the transition to the recursion in (10), as well as the responsibilities used in prediction (Eq. 14), need more elaboration and a worked example.
- The caching scheme and memory complexity during agglomeration (Eq. 13) require fuller explanation.

Originality
- The contribution is an adaptation and integration of existing components: rose-tree priors, block-model marginal likelihoods, and greedy agglomeration. The novelty lies in the likelihood design for networks and the efficient sufficient-statistic updates. Conceptual originality is moderate.

Significance
- A practical, scalable hierarchical community detector that is two orders of magnitude faster than IRM on tested data is useful. However, the lack of comparisons to other hierarchical network models, and the relatively modest scale of experiments, limit the demonstrated impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled reuse of rose-tree machinery for hierarchical communities.
- Efficient dynamic program and local Bayes-factor scoring enable scalable agglomeration.
- Solid empirical speedups over IRM; averaging over greedy solutions improves prediction.
- Qualitative structures (Sampson, full NIPS hierarchy) are interpretable.

Weaknesses
- Comparative evaluation omits key hierarchical baselines (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel; Mondrian process; hierarchical IRM variants), making it hard to contextualize performance.
- Section 3’s derivations are hard to follow; Eq. (9) and the DP recursion are under-explained.
- The “nonparametric” framing is questionable: the algorithm performs greedy model selection over finite trees rather than posterior inference in an infinite model; no posterior marginalization over trees beyond simple restarts.
- Scalability claims are stronger than the evidence presented; largest graph (2,864 nodes) is not large by today’s standards; memory/runtime scaling with m and n not thoroughly profiled.
- No sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference; handling of degree heterogeneity is limited (no degree correction).

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify σ definitions and updates:
  - Provide a small worked example with a node S having three children to show how σSS, each σCC, and σ¬chSS in Eq. (9) are computed from edge counts, and how these feed into Eq. (10).
  - Specify base cases and the exact DP order for computing p(DSS|S) in O(n + m), including how many σ terms must be computed and cached, and their memory footprint.
- Agglomeration complexity:
  - Detail what statistics are stored per tree (σII, σ¬chII) and per pair (σIJ), and the memory complexity across iterations (likely O(n^2) in the worst case). Discuss implications for larger graphs and any sparsity-aware storage.
  - For BHCD-sparse, provide empirical complexity scaling with m (e.g., near O(m log n)) and a plot of runtime vs m at fixed n.
- Prediction and averaging:
  - Explain whether trees from multiple restarts are weighted by p(D|T) or averaged uniformly; consider likelihood-weighted averaging.
  - Discuss limitations of forbidding zero-edge merges (BHCD-sparse) when anti-communities or bipartite substructures are present.
- Comparisons:
  - Include quantitative comparisons to hierarchical models such as Ho et al. (2012), Roy & Teh (2009), and hierarchical IRM extensions (e.g., Herlau et al.). Even if reimplementations are hard, compare against reported numbers or provide ablations that approximate these models.
  - Consider adding hierarchical random graph baselines (e.g., Clauset–Moore–Newman).
- Terminology:
  - Reconsider “Bayesian nonparametric” in the title/abstract; “greedy Bayesian model selection over hierarchical blockmodels” may be more accurate.
- Robustness:
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for α, β, δ, λ, γ; consider empirical Bayes or hyperparameter inference.
  - Discuss extensions to degree-corrected likelihoods, directed/weighted graphs, and missing data patterns.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the exact memory complexity of the cached σ tensors during agglomeration? How large can n be in practice on commodity hardware?
2. Can you provide a step-by-step pseudocode for computing σ¬chSS and p(DSS|S) bottom-up in O(n + m)?
3. How are multiple trees averaged for prediction—uniformly or weighted by p(D|T)? How sensitive are results to the number of restarts R?
4. How does BHCD perform on graphs with strong degree heterogeneity or bipartite structure?
5. Could you report runtimes and AUC on at least one larger (>10k nodes) sparse graph?

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, efficient hierarchical clustering method for networks with clear speed advantages.
- Technically sound adaptation of rose trees to blockmodel likelihoods.
- Useful predictive performance and interpretable hierarchies.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited originality; primarily an adaptation of prior methods.
- Exposition gaps in key derivations and computational details.
- Missing comparisons to closely related hierarchical network models.
- Scalability validated only on modest-sized graphs.

Overall
A technically solid, well-integrated piece with practical value, but originality and empirical evaluation (comparisons and scale) need strengthening, and Section 3 should be clarified substantially.
