- Proposes BHCD: a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community discovery in networks.
- Novel restriction to tree-consistent partitions enables efficient marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming.
- Introduces a greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithm with O(n² log n) runtime, much faster than IRM’s O(n²K²).
- Empirically achieves comparable or better predictive accuracy (AUC, log-likelihood, accuracy) while running ~100× faster than IRM.
- Sparse BHCD variant is especially efficient for sparse networks (e.g., NIPS co-authorship).
- Successfully recovers meaningful hierarchical structures (e.g., Sampson’s Young Turks and Loyal Opposition).
- Theoretical foundation for dynamic programming is sound and the scalability-focused implementation is well-motivated.
- Bridges Bayesian nonparametrics and efficient agglomerative clustering, filling a gap in hierarchical community detection.
- Significant potential impact for network analysis and social science due to computational feasibility for larger networks.
- Major conceptual confusion: unclear connection between the Bayesian generative model and the greedy inference procedure.
- Ambiguity whether the greedy algorithm performs valid Bayesian inference or merely finds a MAP estimate undermines technical claims.
- Writing quality is poor with many spelling/grammar errors and vague or unnecessary notation.
- Dataset descriptions are insufficient (e.g., unexplained “NIPS-234” subset) and experimental details are lacking.
- Some figures and variant descriptions (e.g., “BHCD global”, Figure 3) are confusing or opaque.
- Overall: promising algorithm and results but presentation and clarification of the inference vs. MAP issue are required before publication.
