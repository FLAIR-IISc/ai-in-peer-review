- Introduces BHCD: tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with greedy agglomerative inference, O(n² log n) time, two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while matching accuracy on NIPS-234.  
- Builds on Bayesian Rose Trees and extends the Infinite Relational Model; internal nodes = communities, leaves = vertices.  
- Key innovation: dynamic programming plus sufficient-statistic caching to avoid costly iterative Bayesian procedures.  
- Empirical validation: qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery and quantitative speed-accuracy comparisons (NIPS-234); sparse variant (BHCD sparse) for real networks.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous marginal likelihood derivation and a clear algorithm with complexity analysis.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written, well-structured sections and effective figures; only minor wording issues.  
- Originality: non-trivial adaptation of BRT to relational data, offering a full probabilistic model unlike greedy hierarchical clustering.  
- Significance: addresses a critical scalability gap in hierarchical community detection and demonstrates practical speedups (e.g., 10s vs. 1000s).  
- Scalability concern: O(n² log n) may be prohibitive for very large graphs (>10⁴ nodes); runtimes for full NIPS (2864 nodes) are missing.  
- Theoretical guarantees: greedy selection lacks formal justification; no evaluation of approximation quality or distance to optimal tree.  
- Sparse-variant uncertainty: BHCD sparse is promising but the paper doesn’t clarify how runtime scales with edge count m in sparse graphs.  
- Pro acceptance: first scalable Bayesian hierarchical community method with strong empirical results, sound theory, and clear exposition.  
- Con acceptance: limited large-scale runtime analysis and missing theoretical support for the greedy approximation.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add larger-scale runtimes (e.g., full NIPS) and a brief theoretical/empirical analysis of greedy approximation quality.
