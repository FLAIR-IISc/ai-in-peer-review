This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical structure in networks. The model treats a hierarchy as a tree of communities whose leaves are vertices, and defines a family of tree-consistent partitions; the marginal likelihood p(D|T) integrates out block parameters and sums exactly over all partitions consistent with T via dynamic programming. Likelihood terms use conjugate Beta-Bernoulli blockmodels with different hyperparameters for within- vs between-community blocks. Learning proceeds by greedy agglomerative model selection: at each step, a pair of subtrees is merged using the best of three local operations (join, absorb-left, absorb-right), chosen by a local Bayes factor that reuses cached sufficient statistics. Variants include BHCD-sparse (disallow merges between subtrees with no connecting edges) and binary-only merges. The method also provides efficient link prediction via a recursive mixture along a single path in the tree.

Relation to prior work: The approach is positioned between stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian nonparametric models such as the IRM. Unlike IRM (flat partitions via a CRP prior), BHCD restricts the partition space to those consistent with a tree and thus can evaluate the marginal likelihood exactly per tree. It is conceptually closest to Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees, but applies those ideas to relational data through a blockmodel likelihood with distinct on-/off-diagonal terms. Compared to prior hierarchical extensions of IRM and Mondrian-process–based models, BHCD trades full posterior inference for greedy model selection, gaining substantial speed. It also contrasts with modularity-based hierarchical clustering by providing a fully probabilistic marginal likelihood and predictive distribution.

Empirical results: On Sampson’s monastery network, BHCD variants recover meaningful groups that align with reported factions. On a NIPS co-authorship subset (NIPS-234), BHCD variants achieve better predictive log-likelihood/AUC than IRM and are competitive with LFRM, though below ILA; importantly, they reach useful accuracy in seconds rather than hundreds or thousands of seconds. On the full NIPS graph (2864 nodes), BHCD yields a large, interpretable hierarchy and demonstrates scalability. The paper reports hyperparameters (α=δ=1, β=λ=0.2, γ=0.4), R=50 restarts with tie-breaking, and O(n^2 log n) worst-case time for learning versus O(n+m) for evaluating p(D|T).

Strengths
- Clear modeling insight: restricting to tree-consistent partitions enables exact marginalization over partitions and fast dynamic programming, avoiding IRM’s exponential partition space.
- Efficient, local agglomerative learning with a principled Bayes factor objective; careful caching enables O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n).
- Practical variants (sparse, binary-only) and a simple, linear-in-depth prediction routine.
- Strong empirical speedups (orders of magnitude) with competitive or better accuracy versus IRM on real datasets; qualitative interpretability on Sampson and full NIPS.
- Relevance: hierarchical community discovery is valuable to social network analysis, and speed is a real bottleneck for Bayesian nonparametrics.

Weaknesses and questions
- Inferential status is underspecified. Is the algorithm maximizing p(D|T) (i.e., ML over trees) or approximating a MAP w.r.t. a prior p(T)? The paper defines a prior over partitions conditional on T via πS(γ), but not a prior over T. Please clarify the precise optimization objective and whether the greedy local Bayes factor is a consistent local approximation to Δ log p(D|T).
- Role and sensitivity of γ. πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| shapes the prior over partitions; without p(T), this acts as a regularizer on branching. Provide guidance on γ’s effect, sensitivity analysis, and whether it is tuned or fixed across datasets.
- Merge locality assumption. The score compares p(DMM|M) to p(DII|I)p(DJJ|J)g(σIJ), assuming local independence. Discuss scenarios where long-range dependencies could mislead greedy merges and whether backtracking or limited lookahead helps.
- Theoretical properties. Are there any consistency or approximation guarantees (e.g., w.r.t. oracle merges, or bounds on regret vs. optimal tree)? At minimum, discuss known failure modes.
- Prediction complexity: Section 4.2 states “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n)”; it is actually linear in tree depth, which can be much smaller than n. Please correct and discuss typical depths.
- Experimental details need tightening. Specify hardware, IRM implementation settings (burn-in, iterations/steps for both MCMC and greedy), wall-clock comparisons normalized over R restarts, and whether hyperparameters were matched. The abstract/text alternately compare to “IRM” vs “a single iteration of IRM”; unify this claim. Clarify the cross-validation protocol (edge holdout strategy for undirected graphs, negative sampling, and variance across folds/runs).
- Modeling limitations. The conjugate Beta-Bernoulli SBM does not account for degree heterogeneity; contrast with degree-corrected SBMs and discuss implications for co-authorship data. Consider testing on synthetic hierarchical SBMs to quantify structure recovery and robustness to degree heterogeneity/noise.
- Clarity and presentation. There are several typos and passages with ambiguous notation (e.g., DTT notation, σ¬chSS definition, and rS responsibilities). Some figures are hard to read and the description of binary vs non-binary trees could be streamlined.

Quality
- The model and dynamic program are technically sound, and integrating out block parameters is appropriate. The greedy inference is reasonable but would benefit from a clearer probabilistic objective statement and discussion of limitations. Experiments support claims of speed and competitive accuracy.

Clarity
- Organization is good, but the prose has grammatical errors, some notation is dense or introduced late, and experimental details are incomplete. Improving exposition of Section 4 (objective, merge scoring, caching) and prediction derivation would significantly help.

Originality
- The combination of rose-tree style hierarchical priors over partitions with a blockmodel likelihood, and the specific agglomerative scoring/caching for relational data, is a meaningful contribution. It is incremental relative to prior hierarchical Bayesian clustering and IRM variants, but novel in this formulation and execution.

Significance
- The method is fast, scalable, and useful in practice; the ability to produce hierarchical communities with predictive capabilities is valuable. The work is likely to be adopted and extended, especially if code is released.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the inference objective (ML vs MAP over trees), define or justify p(T), and explain the role of γ. Add sensitivity analyses for γ and the Beta hyperparameters.
- Provide more experimental detail, including wall-clock vs accuracy curves normalized by restarts, and ablations (BHCD vs BHCD-sparse vs binary; effect of R).
- Add a synthetic study with known hierarchical ground truth and with degree heterogeneity.
- Improve notation (define all symbols on first use), fix typos, and make figures self-contained and legible. Consider including pseudocode for Algorithm 1 in the appendix.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong speed-accuracy tradeoff with clean, principled modeling.
- Addresses an important and timely problem; yields interpretable hierarchies.
- Novel and practically impactful inference scheme with clear scalability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Inference objective and prior assumptions over trees are not clearly connected to Bayesian posterior inference.
- Clarity and experimental specification issues reduce reproducibility and interpretability.
- Limited theoretical analysis and robustness studies.

Overall: I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the inference objective, cleaning up the exposition in Section 4, and tightening the experimental details. The contribution is practical, technically sound, and likely to be influential.
