

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" (NIPS 2013)

This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical clustering approach for discovering community structure in social networks, framed as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors introduce "Bayesian rose trees" with a block-model likelihood to hierarchically cluster networks, reusing prior tree structure and agglomerative clustering principles. The work builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches [13, 14] and extends them to relational data, addressing limitations of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection.

The main technical contribution is an efficient algorithm that computes the likelihood in O(n + m) time (n vertices, m edges), enabling scalable agglomerative clustering. This is achieved through clever dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates that avoid the quadratic complexity typical of hierarchical Bayesian methods. The authors demonstrate that their approach, called BHCD, achieves two orders of magnitude faster inference than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-motivated and integrates prior work on hierarchical clustering and Bayesian network modeling. The likelihood computation and dynamic programming approach are technically sound and represent a meaningful efficiency improvement over previous methods.
2. **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-organized, with clear explanations of the stochastic blockmodel foundation and the hierarchical extension. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is straightforward.
3. **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate clear benefits over the flat IRM baseline, with qualitative success on Sampson's monastery network and quantitative results showing BHCD's superior speed on NIPS co-authorship data. The use of multiple restarts and averaging over hypotheses for prediction is a thoughtful touch.
4. **Computational Complexity Claim:** The O(n + m) likelihood computation claim is significant and well-supported by the algorithm description.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient Technical Details:** Section 3 (particularly equation 9 and subsequent derivations) is dense and hard to follow. The dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates are inadequately explained, making it difficult to verify the O(n + m) complexity claim. More detailed derivations or pseudocode for these steps would significantly strengthen the paper.
2. **Inadequate Comparative Evaluation:** The paper lacks meaningful comparison to existing hierarchical network models (e.g., Ho et al. 2012's multiscale community blockmodel, Roy et al. 2009's Mondrian process). Without these comparisons, it's unclear whether the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over specialized hierarchical models.
3. **Misuse of "Nonparametric" Terminology:** The paper frames rose trees as model selection rather than nonparametric modeling, yet repeatedly uses "nonparametric" terminology. This is confusing and inaccurate—the method is a greedy model selection approach, not a nonparametric Bayesian method.
4. **Unsubstantiated Scalability Claims:** While the paper claims O(n + m) complexity, the experiments use relatively small networks (NIPS-234 has 234 vertices), failing to demonstrate scalability on larger datasets where this would be most valuable.
5. **Limited Originality:** The core approach (greedy agglomerative Bayesian hierarchical clustering) builds directly on prior work [13, 14], and the extension to relational data doesn't represent a significant conceptual leap. The paper would benefit from more clearly articulating how it differs from previous hierarchical Bayesian network models.

**Overall Assessment:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound and well-executed, though the lack of detailed explanations for key technical contributions and the questionable use of "nonparametric" terminology detract from its quality. The experimental evaluation is adequate but lacks comparative benchmarks.

*Clarity:* The presentation is generally clear, but the dense mathematical treatment in Section 3 undermines the otherwise accessible exposition. More detailed explanations of the dynamic programming approach would greatly improve clarity.

*Originality:* The work is not highly original; it extends prior hierarchical clustering methods to relational data with an efficient implementation. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to existing hierarchical network models to better establish novelty.

*Significance:* The computational efficiency improvements are valuable for practitioners, but the limited comparative evaluation and lack of demonstration on large-scale networks reduce the significance of the contribution. The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of network analysis.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound and efficient approach to hierarchical community discovery in networks with clear computational advantages over the IRM. However, the insufficient explanation of key technical contributions, lack of comparison to relevant hierarchical network models, and questionable use of "nonparametric" terminology significantly weaken the paper. The authors should strengthen their technical explanations, provide more rigorous comparative evaluation against existing hierarchical models, and clarify their terminology before the paper can be accepted. As currently written, the paper makes a modest contribution that would be better suited for a more specialized venue. I recommend rejection.
