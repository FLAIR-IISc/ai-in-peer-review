### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to model the network as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). This work extends existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, specifically the Bayesian Rose Trees framework, to relational data. Unlike the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which uses a Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) prior over partitions, BHCD employs a tree-structured prior that only considers partitions consistent with a given hierarchy.

A key contribution is that this specific model structure allows for the exact calculation of the marginal likelihood of a tree via dynamic programming, leveraging a factorization over subtrees that is reminiscent of work by Koivisto & Sood (2004). This analytical tractability enables the main algorithmic contribution: a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm for model selection. This algorithm builds the community hierarchy in a single pass over the data, with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n). The authors demonstrate the model's performance on several datasets, showing it is significantly faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The exposition is clear, logical, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing literature on SBMs, IRM, and hierarchical clustering. The use of generalized notation for the marginal likelihood of on-diagonal (`f(·)`) and off-diagonal (`g(·)`) blocks is succinct and effectively conveys the generality of the framework beyond the specific beta-Bernoulli case.

2.  **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The primary contribution—the application and extension of Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data for community discovery—is novel and well-motivated. Replacing the flexible but computationally demanding CRP prior of the IRM with a more structured, tree-based prior is a clever trade-off. This choice directly facilitates the paper's main technical achievement: a fast, greedy agglomerative search algorithm (Algorithm 1) for learning the hierarchy. The ability to compute the marginal likelihood exactly and efficiently is a significant advantage over sampling-based methods that often struggle with mixing and convergence.

3.  **Significance and Efficiency:** The proposed method addresses a critical challenge in network modeling: scalability. The O(n² log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, makes this approach potentially applicable to much larger networks than traditional Bayesian nonparametric methods like the IRM. The experimental results, showing a two-order-of-magnitude speed-up over IRM for comparable performance on the NIPS co-authorship data, strongly support this claim of significance. This work provides a valuable new point in the trade-off space between model flexibility and computational feasibility for network analysis.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

Despite the strengths of the proposed model, the experimental evaluation could be significantly improved to better substantiate the paper's claims.

1.  **Evaluation Methodology:** The paper relies on link prediction metrics (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC) to evaluate performance. While these are valid metrics, the methodology for computing them is not explicitly described. The reader is left to assume that links were randomly held out from the training set, but this should be clearly stated. More importantly, for a paper focused on "community discovery," the evaluation lacks any metrics that directly assess the quality of the discovered partition or hierarchy. Standard metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) against ground-truth communities (where available) or comparisons of the partition structure against other methods are essential for this task. The current evaluation only shows that the model is good at link prediction, which is a related but distinct goal from finding meaningful community structure.

2.  **Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are illustrative but not sufficiently comparative. The primary baseline is the IRM. While this is a crucial comparison, the field of community detection is vast. To demonstrate the practical utility of BHCD, it should be benchmarked against a wider array of methods, especially those known for their scalability. This includes:
    *   Other scalable SBM variants (e.g., those using variational inference or belief propagation).
    *   Fast, non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004), which the introduction mentions but does not compare against.
    *   Other relevant semi-parametric or parametric models that offer a different set of trade-offs.
    The current comparison makes it difficult to assess where BHCD stands in the broader landscape of community detection algorithms.

3.  **Scale of Datasets:** While the efficiency claims are central to the paper's contribution, the experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale networks (Sampson's has 18 nodes, NIPS-234 has 234). The analysis of the "full NIPS" dataset (2864 nodes) is purely qualitative, showing only a small, curated portion of the discovered hierarchy. To compellingly demonstrate the method's scalability, it should be applied to larger, more challenging benchmark datasets common in recent SBM and community detection literature, which often contain tens of thousands of nodes or more.

4.  **Minor Stylistic Point:** As a minor point of style conforming to NIPS guidelines, the authors should avoid beginning sentences with citations (e.g., "[9] and [10] propose..."). This can be easily remedied by rephrasing (e.g., "Koutsourelakis and Eliassi-Rad [9] and Palla et al. [10] propose...").

In summary, this paper presents a well-formulated, original, and computationally efficient model for hierarchical community discovery. The theoretical and algorithmic contributions are solid. However, the experimental evaluation is currently the weakest part of the paper and does not provide sufficient evidence to fully support the claims of its practical superiority in community detection. Strengthening the evaluation with more appropriate metrics, broader comparisons, and larger datasets would make this a much stronger contribution.
