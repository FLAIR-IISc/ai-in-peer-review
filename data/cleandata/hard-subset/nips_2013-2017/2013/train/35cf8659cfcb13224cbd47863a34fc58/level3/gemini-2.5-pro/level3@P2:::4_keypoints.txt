- Proposes BHCD: adapts Bayesian rose tree framework to networks using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood and greedy agglomerative merges to maximize marginal likelihood.  
- Main technical contribution: recursive marginal likelihood computation with cached network sufficient statistics, yielding O(n^2 log n) inference.  

Strengths:
- Technically sound and well-motivated integration of Bayesian rose trees with network likelihoods.  
- Highly efficient inference: shown to be orders of magnitude faster than MCMC for the flat IRM while matching or improving predictive accuracy.  
- Empirical validation: recovers interpretable hierarchy on Sampson’s monastery and shows good predictive performance on NIPS co-authorship data.  
- Elegant probabilistic model that supports averaging over restarts to mitigate greedy-search local optima.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited originality: core algorithmic and prior structure largely inherited from prior rose-tree work.  
- Inadequate comparison to related hierarchical network models (e.g., Mondrian process, Multiscale Community Blockmodel), restricting assessment of novelty.  
- Clarity issues in Section 3: marginal likelihood recursion and off-diagonal sufficient-statistics updates are dense and under-explained, hurting reproducibility.  
- Scalability claims not fully demonstrated: experiments only up to ~2800 nodes, so O(n^2 log n) may be prohibitive for much larger networks.  
- Terminology: calling the approach “nonparametric” is potentially misleading—it's better seen as Bayesian model selection over a discrete tree space.  

Summary / Recommendation:
- Useful, efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection, but diminished by limited novelty and lack of comparison to key hierarchical baselines; clarity should be improved.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a fast, practical, fully Bayesian hierarchical community detection algorithm.  
- Demonstrates substantial speed gains over MCMC-based IRM while maintaining strong predictive results.  
- Elegant combination of ideas with positive empirical outcomes on chosen baselines.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Fails to cite or experimentally compare to important hierarchical network models, a major omission.  
- Methodological novelty is limited given direct adaptation from prior work.  
- Key technical details are insufficiently explained, impeding reproducibility.  

Overall:
- Borderline paper; would be more compelling if authors add comparisons/discussion of hierarchical baselines and clarify Section 3.
