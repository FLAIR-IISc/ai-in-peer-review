This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, which the authors call Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to define a model as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. A given tree defines a prior over a set of tree-consistent partitions, allowing for an exact marginal likelihood calculation via dynamic programming. This approach builds directly on prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, specifically the Bayesian Rose Tree model [14], and adapts it to relational data. Inference is performed using a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor score. This greedy approach allows the method to be computationally efficient, scaling as O(n^2 log n) in the number of vertices, which is a significant improvement over MCMC-based inference for related models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5, 6].

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of discovering hierarchical structure in network data. While many methods exist for flat community detection, scalable and principled methods for hierarchical discovery are less common. The proposed method provides a significant contribution by adapting the elegant Bayesian Rose Tree framework to relational data. While the core algorithmic idea is from [13, 14], the adaptation to the relational domain, including the likelihood formulation and the caching scheme for the sufficient statistics, is non-trivial and novel. The resulting model is both probabilistic and highly efficient.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood and the agglomerative model selection algorithm are clear and appear correct. The claims of efficiency and accuracy are well-supported by a solid set of experiments. The qualitative results on the classic Sampson's monastery dataset are convincing and provide good intuition for what the model discovers. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 co-authorship network is particularly strong, demonstrating a speed-up of two orders of magnitude over the IRM while achieving comparable or superior predictive performance. The ability to run on the full NIPS dataset further demonstrates the method's practical utility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of building up the model from the basic stochastic blockmodel to the full hierarchical formulation. The inference algorithm is explained clearly, and Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary. The figures are illustrative and support the text effectively. The clarity of the exposition makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

*   **Scalability to Very Large Networks:** The main selling point of the paper is its efficiency. The O(n^2 log n) complexity is a great improvement, but it can still be prohibitive for networks with tens of thousands of nodes or more, which are common in social or biological domains. The authors propose a "BHCD sparse" variant which only considers merging trees that are connected by an edge. While the paper notes its worst-case complexity is the same, its practical performance is likely much better on sparse graphs. It would greatly strengthen the paper to provide more analysis of this. For instance, what was the actual runtime for the full NIPS dataset (n=2864)? Comparing this to the NIPS-234 runtime would give readers a much better sense of the method's practical scaling behavior on sparse real-world data.
*   **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The efficiency of BHCD comes from its greedy nature. As with any greedy algorithm, it is prone to finding local optima. The authors mitigate this by using multiple restarts, but the fundamental limitation remains. It would be valuable to have some characterization of how far the greedy solution is from the optimal one.
    *   Could the authors run an experiment on a toy problem small enough to permit an exhaustive search for the optimal tree? This would allow for a direct quantification of the quality of the greedy solution.
    *   Is there any theoretical justification for the greedy approach? For example, is the objective function related to a submodular function? If so, this could provide some performance guarantees for the greedy algorithm and would be a very strong addition to the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments are run with a fixed set of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these settings would be helpful. How were these values chosen, and how much do the discovered hierarchies and predictive performance change if they are varied?

**Minor Comments:**

*   There are a few minor grammatical points that could be polished. For example:
    *   Abstract, line 4: "In the worst case, Our algorithms scale..." (Should be "our"). The phrasing "whilst... but" is also slightly awkward.
    *   Section 4, paragraph 2: "...which pair of tree to merge..." should be "trees".
    *   Section 6, final sentence: "...a fruitful next steps." should be "fruitful next steps".

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a very nice paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. It combines ideas from Bayesian nonparametrics and hierarchical clustering in a novel way to produce a fast and effective algorithm for discovering hierarchical communities. The experimental results are strong, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. Despite the reliance on a greedy inference scheme, the massive gains in computational efficiency over competing Bayesian methods make this a valuable and important contribution. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
