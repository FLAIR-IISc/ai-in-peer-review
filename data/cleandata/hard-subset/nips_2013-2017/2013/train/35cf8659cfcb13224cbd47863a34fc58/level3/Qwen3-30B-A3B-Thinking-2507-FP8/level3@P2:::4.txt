

**Review for NIPS 2013-2017/2013**

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for learning hierarchical community structure in networks using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The approach extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, ICML 2005) to relational data, leveraging a tree prior (rose trees) and efficient dynamic programming for likelihood computation. The authors claim BHCD scales quadratically in vertices (O(n² log n)) but avoids dependence on community count, and achieves two orders of magnitude speedup over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while matching its predictive accuracy.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical grounding in stochastic blockmodels and rose trees. The dynamic programming recursion (Equation 10) and sufficient statistic updates (Equation 13) are well-motivated. Experiments demonstrate practical utility: BHCD identifies interpretable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery network (Figure 3) and achieves comparable predictive performance to IRM on NIPS co-authorship data (Figure 4), with significant speed gains (10s vs. 1000s seconds).  
*Clarity:* The paper is generally well-structured, with clear problem definition and algorithm description (Algorithm 1). However, Section 3’s derivation of the likelihood (Equation 9) and recursive updates is dense and lacks sufficient intuition, making it difficult to verify the O(n+m) likelihood claim. A concrete example of the sufficient statistic update would greatly aid reproducibility.  
*Significance:* The speed advantage over IRM is compelling for large networks, and the method’s flexibility for link prediction (Section 4.2) is practically valuable. The approach addresses a clear gap in scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection, particularly for dense networks.  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Originality:* The core technical novelty—adapting rose trees to network data via efficient sufficient statistics—is incremental. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work:  
- It fails to compare against established hierarchical methods (e.g., Ho et al.’s *Multiscale Community Blockmodel* (AISTATS 2011) and Roy et al.’s *Mondrian Process* (NIPS 2009)), which explicitly model hierarchical relational structure.  
- The term "nonparametric" is misleading; BHCD performs model selection over a finite set of trees (O(2ⁿ) partitions), unlike truly nonparametric methods like the IRM.  
*Experimental Rigor:*  
- Scalability claims are not empirically validated on large networks (NIPS-234 has only 234 nodes). The paper lacks benchmarks on networks with >1,000 vertices to confirm O(n² log n) behavior.  
- No comparison to depth-limited hierarchical methods (e.g., ILA (AAAI 2010) or LFRM (ICML 2010)) is provided, weakening the significance argument.  
*Clarity:* The explanation of "why" the likelihood is O(n+m) (Section 4) is insufficient; the text states it without demonstrating how the dynamic programming avoids redundant computation.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:*  
- Practical speed advantage over IRM (empirically validated).  
- Clear presentation of the algorithm and link prediction framework.  
- Good qualitative results on Sampson’s network.  
*Con:*  
- Critical omission of comparisons to hierarchical models (Ho et al., Roy et al.).  
- Overstated "nonparametric" claim without justification.  
- Insufficient empirical scalability testing on large networks.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to scalable hierarchical community detection but falls short on originality and evaluation. The core idea is a useful adaptation of existing methods, yet the lack of comparison to key hierarchical approaches (e.g., Mondrian process) and the questionable use of "nonparametric" terminology significantly weaken its claim to novelty. The experimental section would benefit from adding benchmarks against Ho et al. (2011) and Roy et al. (2009) and testing scalability on larger networks (e.g., >1,000 nodes).  

While the method is practical and well-executed, its novelty is limited relative to NIPS standards. The authors should clarify the "nonparametric" claim (e.g., by noting it is a *model selection* approach) and add missing comparisons before the paper can be considered for acceptance. As-is, it is a solid but incremental contribution.  

**Decision:** Weak Reject (or Borderline Accept if revisions are made to address comparisons and terminology).
