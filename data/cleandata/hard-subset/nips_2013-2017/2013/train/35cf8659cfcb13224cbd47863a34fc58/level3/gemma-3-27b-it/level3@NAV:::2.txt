## Review of “Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery”

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a significant problem – the computational expense of existing Bayesian methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) – by proposing a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and an efficient, greedy agglomerative learning algorithm. This is a relevant contribution, as hierarchical network discovery is a crucial tool for social scientists and understanding complex relationships.

The core insight—restricting the partition space to tree structures—is clever and demonstrably effective. The empirical results on two social network corpora are compelling, showing BHCD achieves comparable or better accuracy (AUC, predictive log-likelihood) than the IRM while being orders of magnitude faster. The proposed algorithm addresses a clear limitation of previous work, offering a scalable solution for large networks. The connection to existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods ([13, 14]) is also well-established. 

However, the paper suffers from a crucial conceptual weakness. The relationship between the greedy tree-learning procedure (Section 4) and the underlying Bayesian generative model remains unclear. It’s not immediately obvious whether the algorithm performs valid posterior inference or is merely a MAP/ML estimate.  Clarification of this point, as requested by my supervisor, is essential. A more thorough discussion of the assumptions made during the greedy search would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the writing quality needs substantial improvement. Numerous spelling and grammatical errors, vague phrasing, and unclear descriptions of the datasets detract from the paper’s impact. Some mathematical notation feels unnecessarily obscure, and the figures could be more effectively designed to convey key information. The description of the experiments lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novelty of the tree-structured prior, strong empirical performance, significant speed improvement over existing methods, addresses a relevant problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Conceptual ambiguity regarding the inference procedure, poor writing quality hindering clarity and reproducibility.

**Overall:** Despite the clarity issues, the technical contribution is significant. If the authors address the conceptual confusion regarding the inference method and substantially improve the writing quality, this paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. Currently, I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The core ideas are insightful and the results promising, but the current presentation falls short of NIPS standards.
