

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks using Bayesian rose trees with a block-model likelihood. The authors build on previous work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14] and extend it to relational data, claiming efficient computation through dynamic programming with O(n+m) likelihood computation (n=nodes, m=edges) and agglomerative model selection. The approach represents a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where the tree defines hierarchical community structure, and uses efficient sufficient statistic updates for clustering.

The paper's strengths include a technically sound approach that integrates prior work well, clear presentation of the core algorithm, and promising experimental results showing BHCD achieves comparable accuracy to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster. The qualitative success on Sampson's monastery network demonstrates the method's ability to recover meaningful hierarchical structures.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The paper's "nonparametric" claim is questionable given the prior framing of rose trees as model selection rather than true nonparametric modeling. Most critically, the experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to existing hierarchical network models (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al. 2012], Mondrian process [Roy et al. 2009]), making it difficult to assess the method's relative contribution. The scalability claims are not fully validated empirically, as the tested networks are not especially large. The presentation of Section 3 is dense and difficult to follow, particularly equations (9) and subsequent derivations, with insufficient detail on the dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates.

The paper addresses an important problem (hierarchical community detection) with a technically competent approach, but the limited originality (novelty is primarily in adaptation rather than new concepts) and inadequate comparative evaluation weaken its contribution. While the method appears useful for practitioners, the lack of comparison to established hierarchical models prevents clear assessment of its advancement over the state of the art.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and well-written but requires significant strengthening in comparative evaluation and explanation to justify its contribution to the field. The authors should address the missing comparisons, clarify the "nonparametric" terminology, and provide more detailed explanations of the computational aspects.
