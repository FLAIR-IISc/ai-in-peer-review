This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. It extends stochastic block models (SBM) and the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees, offering a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods. The core contribution lies in a greedy agglomerative algorithm for learning the hierarchy, summarized in Table 1.

The paper is well-written, with excellent grammar and spelling, and citations are correctly formatted. However, the authors occasionally begin sentences with citations (e.g., "Similar approaches have been taken… [7]"), which is not standard NIPS style. The theoretical presentation, while succinct, is clear, with consistent use of f() and g() to denote the marginal likelihoods for on- and off-diagonal blocks respectively. The connection to previous work in Bayesian nonparametric community detection, particularly IRM and Mondrian processes, is well-established. The authors appropriately position their work as addressing the computational limitations of existing hierarchical Bayesian approaches.

A key strength is the proposed formulation, replacing the CRP formulation of IRM with the simpler expression in equation (7). This simplification, coupled with the posterior factorization following Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004), enables efficient greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, and handling of sufficient statistics – all crucial for scalability. The described agglomerative method (Algorithm 1) appears well-designed and its claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) time complexity is a significant advantage.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact. The evaluation methodology is a major concern. While AUC is reported, the method of computing it is unclear. The paper states it's "presumably by deleting links," but lacks explicit explanation, hindering reproducibility. NMI is a more common and informative metric for comparing SBM-based approaches. Furthermore, the experiments are limited in scale, utilizing only a subset of the NIPS dataset initially and then the full dataset. Recent SBM evaluations frequently employ larger datasets, and including such benchmarks would strengthen the claims of scalability and performance.

The experimental work primarily serves as an illustration rather than a rigorous comparison. While BHCD is shown to be faster than IRM, a comprehensive comparison to other semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics is missing. Assessing performance against, for example, spectral clustering or label propagation algorithms would provide a more complete picture of the method's strengths and weaknesses. The ablation study, examining the impact of the “sparse” variants, is useful but doesn’t fully compensate for the lack of broader comparative analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The combination of Bayesian Rose trees with SBMs and a greedy agglomerative algorithm offers a novel approach to hierarchical community discovery.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is a significant improvement over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundations and algorithmic details.
*   **Potential Impact:** The method has the potential to be a valuable tool for analyzing large-scale social networks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation is inadequate due to the lack of clear explanation of the AUC calculation and the small scale of the datasets used.
*   **Insufficient Comparison:** The lack of comparison to non-Bayesian methods limits the assessment of the method's relative performance.
*   **Illustrative Nature of Experiments:** The experimental work primarily serves to demonstrate the method’s functionality rather than to provide a rigorous evaluation of its effectiveness.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of community detection, the limitations in the evaluation and comparison prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection**, but with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the identified weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Provide a detailed explanation of the AUC calculation method.**
2.  **Evaluate the method on larger, more established benchmark datasets.**
3.  **Compare the performance of BHCD to a wider range of algorithms, including both Bayesian and non-Bayesian methods.**
4.  **Expand the experimental analysis to demonstrate the method’s robustness and generalizability.**
