This paper presents a Bayesian method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The approach adapts the Bayesian rose tree framework by incorporating a stochastic block-model likelihood suitable for relational data. This work builds on prior research in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [Heller & Ghahramani, 2005] and Bayesian nonparametric models for networks like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The primary contribution is a greedy agglomerative inference algorithm that learns a hierarchical structure in a single pass, which is presented as a computationally efficient alternative to MCMC-based methods.

**Strengths:**
The main strength of this paper is its novel combination of existing, powerful techniques to create a computationally efficient model for a challenging problem. The integration of Bayesian rose trees with a block-model likelihood is elegant and well-motivated. The proposed inference procedure, which leverages dynamic programming and efficient caching of sufficient statistics to achieve a claimed O(n+m) likelihood computation, represents a significant practical advantage over iterative methods like the IRM. The experimental results, though preliminary, effectively demonstrate this speed advantage and show that the model can find qualitatively sensible hierarchies and achieve competitive predictive performance against a flat IRM baseline. The overall presentation is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper’s primary weakness is its insufficient experimental evaluation, which limits the assessment of its significance. The authors fail to compare their method against any existing *hierarchical* network models, such as the multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al., AISTATS 2011] or methods based on the Mondrian process [Roy & Teh, NIPS 2009]. Without these crucial comparisons, it is difficult to judge whether the proposed model advances the state of the art. Secondly, while scalability is a key claim, the networks used in the experiments are not large enough to fully validate the model's performance on large-scale data.

From a clarity standpoint, Section 3 is overly dense and difficult to follow. The derivations for the marginal likelihood recursion (Equation 10) and the sufficient statistic updates (Equation 9) are not explained in enough detail, which would hinder reproducibility. Finally, the use of the term "nonparametric" is debatable, as the rose tree framework has previously been positioned as a form of Bayesian model selection over a discrete space of tree structures rather than a truly nonparametric prior.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound paper with a good core idea. However, the limited originality, insufficient experimental comparisons, and lack of clarity in a key technical section prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.
