- Proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for hierarchical community discovery in social networks, building on Bayesian rose trees.  
- Model is a tree-structured mixture of many stochastic blockmodels with greedy agglomerative model-selection algorithms.  
- Inference algorithms scale O(n^2) in number of vertices and are independent of number of nested communities.  
- Claims two orders of magnitude speedup over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or better accuracy.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly explains key ideas; adaptation to relational data required nontrivial changes while keeping complexity.  
- Provides qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and full NIPS dataset, and quantitative results on NIPS-234 demonstrating effectiveness.  
- Main strengths: scalable Bayesian nonparametric modeling, efficient inference algorithm, and strong predictive performance.  
- Clear exposition makes the model and algorithm easy to understand and implement.  
- Limitation: O(n^2) complexity may be prohibitive for very large networks; a sparse variant might be needed.  
- Should report timings on larger real-world networks to better demonstrate scalability.  
- Recommend evaluating how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree (e.g., exhaustive search at feasible scales).  
- Suggest seeking theoretical support for the greedy approach (e.g., submodularity).  
- Minor fixes needed: rephrase some sentences and correct wording issues.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel adaptation, significant contribution — recommendation to accept.  
- Quality: technically sound with well-designed experiments.  
- Clarity: well-written and easy to follow.  
- Originality: builds on rose trees but the relational adaptation and efficient inference are novel.  
- Significance: addresses an important network-analysis problem with a scalable solution.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, efficient model; clear motivation and exposition; effective experiments and scalability claims.  
- Arguments against acceptance: O(n^2) cost for very large networks, missing large-scale timing evidence, and lack of evaluation/theoretical guarantees for the greedy method.
