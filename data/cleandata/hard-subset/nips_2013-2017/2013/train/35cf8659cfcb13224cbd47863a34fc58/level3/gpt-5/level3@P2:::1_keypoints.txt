- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric tree-structured mixture over stochastic blockmodels with exact marginal likelihood via dynamic programming.
- Uses Bayesian rose trees to define priors over tree-consistent partitions and Beta–Bernoulli conjugacy for closed-form block marginals.
- Learning via a greedy agglomerative algorithm with three local merge types scored by Bayes factors and O(1) cached updates, overall O(n^2 log n) time.
- Introduces a sparse variant that forbids merging disconnected subtrees to address an edge case.
- Builds on IRM, hierarchical IRM, Bayesian rose trees, and related work but avoids expensive MCMC/variational inference by using greedy model selection.
- Modeling is well defined with a correct recursion and straightforward predictive recursion using conjugacy.
- Inference is technically sound and efficiently implemented with cached sufficient statistics.
- Empirically, BHCD matches IRM predictive performance on NIPS-234 while being orders of magnitude faster and produces interpretable hierarchies (including full NIPS graph).
- Limitations: fixed hyperparameters with no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference.
- Limitations: comparisons focus mainly on IRM; other scalable block/factor model baselines are not quantitatively compared.
- Scalability concern: O(n^2 log n) time and O(n^2) memory for pairwise stats may be prohibitive for very large graphs; wall-clock timings on large graphs are needed.
- Greedy search has no optimality guarantees; an analysis of optimality gaps on small graphs would strengthen the work.
- Paper is clearly written, technically careful, and well organized with helpful notation and figures.
- Minor editorial and typographical issues noted (capitalization, grammar, typos, phrasing).
- Originality: adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to relational/blockmodel data and the combination of ideas (on/off-diagonal marginals, sufficient statistics, local Bayes-factor search) is novel.
- Significance: provides a practical accuracy–speed tradeoff and a likely useful fast baseline for exploratory hierarchical community discovery.
- Suggest reporting wall-clock time and memory for full NIPS vs NIPS-234 and characterizing scaling with n and m.
- Suggest clarifying practical memory usage for cached statistics and exploring pruning or neighbor-restricted heaps to reduce storage.
- Suggest providing sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters and considering empirical-Bayes updates or sampling.
- Suggest evaluating optimality gap via exhaustive search on small graphs and investigating conditions for greedy near-optimality.
- Suggest adding fast non-Bayesian hierarchical baselines (e.g., modularity/Louvain dendrograms) and a Mondrian-process relational comparison.
- Request inference details: number of restarts, tie-breaking, pseudocode for Algorithm 1, and code release for reproducibility.
- Pros: principled model with exact marginals, efficient agglomerative inference, strong empirical speedups, and interpretability.
- Cons: lack of theoretical guarantees for greedy search, limited baseline breadth and sensitivity analysis, and quadratic worst-case resource demands.
- Verdict: Accept, pending suggested clarifications and additional scalability/sensitivity results.
