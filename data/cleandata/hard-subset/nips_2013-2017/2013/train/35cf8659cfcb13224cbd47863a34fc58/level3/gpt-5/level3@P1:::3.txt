This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model and a fast agglomerative inference scheme for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model uses a tree prior to constrain the set of admissible partitions and treats the observed graph as generated by a mixture over tree-consistent stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). Concretely, the likelihood integrates out Bernoulli-Beta block parameters with different hyperparameters for within- and between-community edges, and the prior over partitions factorizes along the tree via stopping probabilities at internal nodes (Eq. 7). This yields an exact marginal likelihood for a given tree computed by dynamic programming (Eq. 10). Inference is cast as greedy, bottom-up agglomerative model selection over three merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right), scored by local Bayes factors (Eq. 12) with cached sufficient statistics, giving O(1) per inner loop and overall O(n^2 log n) time; a “sparse” variant prohibits merging fully disconnected subtrees. Predictive link probabilities are computed by a recursive mixture along the path from root to leaves (Eq. 14). Empirically, on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship data (NIPS-234 subset and full NIPS 1–17), the method is shown to recover interpretable structure and achieves comparable or better link prediction than IRM while being orders of magnitude faster. The approach connects to Bayesian Rose Trees and Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and can be viewed as replacing the CRP prior of IRM with a tree-constrained prior over partitions (Eq. 7); the subtree factorization underpinning the DP recursion is closely related to earlier dynamic programming decompositions over trees/subsets (cf. Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004).

Quality
- Soundness: The probabilistic model, conjugate integration, and tree-constrained partition prior are clearly defined. The DP recursion (Eq. 10) and local Bayes-factor scoring (Eq. 12) are consistent with the factorized generative process. The caching scheme and update formulas (Eq. 13) make the claimed complexity plausible. Assumptions (locality of merge scoring; conjugate priors with fixed hyperparameters) are explicit.
- Empirical support: The experiments illustrate behavior on a classic small network and on NIPS co-authorships, with 10-fold CV following prior work. However, the evaluation is largely illustrative and limited in scale; baselines emphasize BNP methods (IRM, LFRM, ILA) and omit strong semi-/parametric comparators (e.g., degree-corrected SBM).
- Reproducibility: Most algorithmic details are provided. The procedure for AUC computation is not fully specified (e.g., edge removal protocol, negative sampling, thresholding); making this explicit would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is well written, well organized, and uses consistent notation (f and g for within/between blocks; σ for sufficient statistics). Minor style issue: in NIPS style avoid starting sentences with citations.
- The relationship to SBM/IRM and to Bayesian Rose Trees is clearly articulated. A brief intuitive explanation of how Eq. 7 induces a mixture over tree-consistent partitions, and a schematic of the three merge types, help readability.

Originality
- Conceptually, the tree-constrained mixture over SBMs combined with an exact DP evaluation of the marginal likelihood is a neat synthesis of ideas from hierarchical clustering and relational modeling. The agglomerative search with join/absorb moves is a pragmatic contribution enabling speedups. Relative to IRM variants that include trees, this work trades full posterior exploration for a fast, greedy model selection scheme with a different prior, which is novel in this context.

Significance
- The main impact is practical: a scalable, simple method that produces reasonable hierarchical communities and competitive link prediction at a fraction of the cost of MCMC/variational BNP baselines. If validated on larger and more varied datasets, this could be a useful tool for exploratory network analysis.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation; exact marginal likelihood via DP on trees.
- Simple, well-engineered agglomerative algorithm with O(n^2 log n) runtime, independent of the number of communities; sparse variant aligns with practice.
- Strong runtime advantage with competitive predictive performance vs IRM; interpretable hierarchies on real data.
- Consistent and compact notation and exposition; good citations to SBM/IRM, BHC, and rose trees.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope: limited datasets; lacks comparison to non-BNP, strong SBM baselines (e.g., degree-corrected SBM, spectral or modularity-based hierarchical methods), and lacks hierarchical evaluation metrics (e.g., dendrogram purity, cophenetic correlation).
- Predictive protocol under-specified (AUC/accuracy computation, held-out strategy). NMI or ARI on benchmarks with accepted community labels would aid comparability to SBM literature.
- Greedy search may be myopic; no uncertainty quantification beyond restarts; sensitivity to hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) not studied.
- The “locality” assumption in merge scoring could bias structure in presence of long-range dependencies; a brief discussion or ablation would help.
- Worst-case scaling remains O(n^2 log n); while the likelihood is O(n+m), the search is not fully edge-sparse in the worst case.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly describe the link-prediction protocol: fraction of edges held out, whether non-edges are downsampled, how thresholds are chosen, and how AUC is computed. Report calibration (e.g., log loss/Brier score) in addition to AUC.
- Add comparisons to degree-corrected SBM and modularity/spectral hierarchical baselines; include NMI/ARI where ground-truth communities exist.
- Evaluate on larger/public benchmarks used in recent SBM studies; show scaling curves (runtime vs n, m) and memory usage.
- Provide ablations: join-only (binary) vs join+absorb; with/without BHCD-sparse; effect of γ; sensitivity to within/between priors.
- Consider simple hyperparameter estimation (empirical Bayes) and discuss non-conjugate likelihoods as future work (as noted).
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled model with exact tree-marginal likelihood and a fast, practical inference scheme.
- Strong speedups over IRM with competitive predictive performance; interpretable hierarchies on real data.
- Clear writing and solid connections to related work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and missing important baselines/metrics; evidence for general utility across domains is incomplete.
- Some methodological choices (AUC protocol, hyperparameters, tie-breaking and restarts) are under-specified or unanalyzed.
- Greedy search without uncertainty quantification may be brittle; lack of sensitivity analysis.

Overall, this is a promising and well-presented contribution with clear practical value; strengthening the empirical section and clarifying evaluation protocols would substantially increase its impact.
