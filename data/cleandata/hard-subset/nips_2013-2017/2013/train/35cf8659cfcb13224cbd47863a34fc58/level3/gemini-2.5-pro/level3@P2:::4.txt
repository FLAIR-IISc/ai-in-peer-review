This paper proposes a Bayesian model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to adapt the Bayesian rose tree framework, previously developed for hierarchical clustering of i.i.d. data, to relational data. The model uses a stochastic blockmodel likelihood, where different priors are placed on within-community versus between-community edge probabilities. Inference is performed via a greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees to maximize the marginal likelihood of the data. The authors' main technical contribution lies in adapting the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood and developing an efficient caching scheme for the network-specific sufficient statistics, resulting in an O(n^2 log n) algorithm. This approach aims to bridge the gap between fast but non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering methods and more principled but computationally expensive Bayesian nonparametric models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

**Strengths**

*   **Quality & Efficiency:** The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated combination of the Bayesian rose tree model with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The most significant strength is the computational efficiency of the proposed greedy inference algorithm. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based inference for the related flat IRM, while achieving comparable or better predictive accuracy. This makes the method a practical tool for network analysis where speed is a concern.
*   **Empirical Results:** The qualitative results on the classic Sampson's monastery dataset are compelling, showing that the method recovers a meaningful and interpretable hierarchy that aligns with known social structures. The quantitative results on the NIPS co-authorship data further validate the model's predictive performance relative to a strong baseline.
*   **Model Formulation:** The model is elegant, providing a fully probabilistic treatment of the hierarchical structure. The ability to average predictions over multiple runs (restarts) is a good feature that helps mitigate the risk of getting stuck in poor local optima inherent to greedy search.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Originality and Comparison to Related Work:** The paper's primary weakness is its failure to compare with or adequately discuss other highly relevant Bayesian nonparametric models for hierarchical community detection. The core algorithmic and prior structure is directly inherited from Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2010). While adapting this to network data is a valid contribution, the experimental comparison is limited to the *flat* IRM. This overlooks key contemporary work on *hierarchical* network models, most notably the Mondrian process (Roy & Teh, NIPS 2009) and the Multiscale Community Blockmodel (Ho et al., AISTATS 2011). Without comparing to these methods, it is difficult to assess the significance and novelty of the proposed approach in the context of the existing literature on hierarchical relational modeling.
*   **Clarity:** While generally well-written, the paper's clarity suffers in Section 3. The description of the marginal likelihood calculation is dense, and the transition from the general formulation to the recursive dynamic programming in equation (10) could be explained more thoroughly. Specifically, the update for the off-diagonal sufficient statistics (σ¬chSS in eq. 9 and its use in eq. 10) is not sufficiently intuitive and could benefit from a more detailed derivation or explanation. This lack of detail hinders the reader's ability to fully understand the mechanics of the algorithm and potentially reproduce the work.
*   **Scalability Claims:** The paper emphasizes the scalability of the method, but the empirical validation is on networks of moderate size (the largest having ~2800 nodes). While the O(n^2 log n) complexity is suitable for dense networks, the experiments do not fully demonstrate performance on what would be considered large-scale networks today (e.g., tens or hundreds of thousands of nodes), where the quadratic scaling might become prohibitive.
*   **Terminology:** The use of the term "nonparametric" may be slightly misleading. The Bayesian rose tree framework is more accurately characterized as performing Bayesian model selection over a discrete (though exponentially large) space of possible tree structures, rather than a truly nonparametric model where complexity can grow infinitely with data. This is a minor point but could be clarified for precision.

**Summary and Recommendation**

This paper introduces an efficient and useful method for hierarchical community detection by adapting the Bayesian rose tree framework. Its main strengths are its computational speed and solid performance against the flat IRM. However, its contribution is diminished by a lack of originality in the core methodology and, more critically, a failure to engage with the most relevant literature on hierarchical network models. The clarity of the technical sections could also be improved.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a fast, practical, and fully Bayesian algorithm for hierarchical community detection.
*   Demonstrates significant speed improvements over established MCMC methods for a related problem.
*   The combination of ideas is elegant and the empirical results are positive on the baselines chosen.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Fails to cite or compare against key related work on Bayesian hierarchical network models (e.g., Mondrian process, Multiscale Community Blockmodel), which is a major omission.
*   The methodological novelty is limited, as the core algorithm is a direct adaptation of prior work.
*   Key technical details in Section 3 are not explained clearly enough to ensure reproducibility.

Overall, this is a borderline paper. The work is technically sound and the results are promising, but the lack of comparison to closely related hierarchical models makes it difficult to judge its advance over the state of the art. I would be more inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors could (at a minimum) thoroughly discuss the relationship of their work to other hierarchical models and ideally provide some form of comparison. Addressing the clarity issues in Section 3 would also strengthen the paper considerably.
