This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is to represent the hierarchical structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, allowing for efficient inference via a greedy agglomerative algorithm. This builds upon existing Bayesian approaches to community detection, notably the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), but addresses its computational limitations. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network (NIPS-234), and the full NIPS co-authorship network.

The work is well-motivated, building on a rich history of research in community detection and Bayesian nonparametrics. The authors clearly position their contribution relative to previous work, including stochastic blockmodels [1], the mixed membership stochastic blockmodel [2], hierarchical clustering [3, 4], the IRM [5, 6, 7] and its extensions [8-11], and the Mondrian process [12]. They correctly identify the computational bottlenecks in existing Bayesian nonparametric methods and propose a solution that offers significant speed-ups. The connection to Bayesian rose trees [13, 14] is also clearly articulated. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical contribution is sound. The proposed model is a natural and principled extension of existing approaches, and the greedy agglomerative inference procedure appears well-designed and efficient. The caching scheme used to achieve O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) time complexity is a clever optimization. Second, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The exposition of the model and algorithm is accessible and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the specific details of Bayesian nonparametrics. Third, the experimental results are convincing. The qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network are insightful, and the quantitative results on NIPS-234 demonstrate that BHCD can achieve comparable predictive performance to the IRM with significantly reduced computational cost. The analysis of the full NIPS dataset provides further evidence of the model’s ability to uncover meaningful hierarchical structure. Finally, the authors are careful to acknowledge potential limitations and suggest avenues for future work.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is a significant improvement over the IRM, it may still be prohibitive for very large networks (e.g., gene regulatory networks or large social networks with tens of thousands of nodes). The reviewer notes this as a concern and requests timings on the full NIPS dataset to provide a better sense of the practical scalability of the method. The sparse version of the algorithm addresses this partially, but a more detailed analysis of its performance on sparse networks would be valuable. A theoretical analysis supporting the greedy approach would be also beneficial – understanding how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree would be great. Consideration of submodularity properties might provide such support. 

The writing quality is generally excellent. However, there are minor stylistic issues ("whilst... but" on line 48) and some phrasing could be improved ("pair of trees", "A fruitful next steps"). These are minor points and do not detract significantly from the overall clarity of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant improvement in computational efficiency compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and clear exposition of the model and algorithm.
*   Convincing experimental results demonstrating the efficacy of the approach on multiple datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly organized paper.
*   Addresses an important problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity may still be prohibitive for very large networks.
*   Lack of theoretical guarantees on the optimality of the greedy inference procedure.
*   Minor stylistic issues in the writing.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a few minor weaknesses, this is a strong and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric network modeling. The authors have developed a novel and efficient algorithm for discovering hierarchical community structure, and their experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly motivated. While the scalability to extremely large networks remains a concern, the authors have identified this limitation and suggest potential avenues for future work. 

I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend acceptance. The originality and significance of the work, combined with its technical soundness and clarity of presentation, make it a valuable addition to the conference program.
