Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy Bayesian procedure for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model combines a stochastic blockmodel likelihood with the Bayesian rose tree prior (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2010): a tree T defines a set of tree-consistent partitions Φ(T), and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) sums exactly over these partitions using a recursion with conjugate beta–Bernoulli terms. A greedy agglomerative algorithm builds T bottom-up by maximizing a local Bayes factor over three merge types (join vs. absorptions), with careful caching of sufficient statistics to obtain O(1) inner-loop updates and an overall O(n^2 log n) worst-case runtime (independent of the number of communities). The likelihood for a fixed T is computed by dynamic programming in O(n + m). The authors also describe “sparse BHCD” that prohibits merging disconnected components and a simple predictive scheme for link prediction based on traversing the tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS coauthorship data show qualitative hierarchies and predictive performance competitive with or better than the IRM, with substantially lower runtime; results are compared to prior IRM-based results for LFRM and ILA.

Relation to prior work: The paper directly builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees, and on stochastic blockmodels and their nonparametric extensions (IRM). It contrasts with hierarchical extensions of IRM (e.g., Herlau et al., 2013), Mondrian process priors for trees (Roy & Teh, 2009), and other hierarchical blockmodels (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel, Ho et al., AISTATS). The main contribution is the adaptation of rose-tree mixtures to relational data with an efficient agglomerative search and caching.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of p(D|T) as an exact sum over Φ(T) with the factorized prior p(φ|T) and blockmodel likelihood is sound and consistent with prior rose-tree work. The dynamic-programming recursion (Eq. 10) appears correct given Eqs. 7–9. The merge scoring via local Bayes factors is standard and computationally attractive; the locality assumption is reasonable for hierarchical block structures. Conjugate priors enable closed-form marginal likelihoods and simple predictions.
- Areas needing clarification or validation:
  - Section 3 is dense. Equation (9) and the derivation of the recursion in Eq. (10) would benefit from an explicit step-by-step derivation and a proof sketch of the O(n + m) computation, including how σ¬ch is maintained.
  - The efficient O(1) update rules (Eq. 13) are critical to the claimed O(n^2 log n) runtime; please give pseudocode for data structures, memory complexity, and a formal complexity analysis (including heap operations and cache invalidation).
  - Predictions are described as “Θ(n)”; they are linear in the depth of T, not necessarily in n. Please correct and quantify expected depth in practice.
  - Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; some sensitivity analysis would increase confidence. Are they learned or cross-validated?
  - The “sparse” variant prohibits merging trees with no edges; specify whether this preserves the likelihood principle or induces bias, and how it affects the search space and guarantees.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a clear motivation and algorithm outline. However, Section 3 (Eqs. 6–10) and Section 4’s dynamic programming and caching are hard to follow without more exposition. Suggestions:
- Add a worked toy example tracing σ updates through a few merges.
- Provide an explicit dynamic-programming algorithm for p(D|T) with complexity and memory analysis.
- Clarify treatment of directed graphs and self-edges (briefly mentioned) and missing data (used in Sampson).
- Define BHCD “global” vs. “sparse” more precisely and explain tie-breaking/restarts and their impact on variance.
- Improve figure references and ensure all variants in Figure 3 are clearly labeled.

Originality
The idea of using a rose-tree prior and exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions is known; the novelty here lies in adapting the likelihood to stochastic blockmodels for networks and engineering an efficient agglomerative procedure with cached sufficient statistics. While useful, this is a relatively incremental combination of existing ingredients. The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of how BHCD differs from and complements:
- Multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., AISTATS): hierarchical block structures with scalable inference.
- Mondrian process relational models (Roy & Teh) that induce recursive partitions over the adjacency.
- Hierarchical IRM variants (e.g., Herlau et al., 2013) and depth-limited hierarchical models.

Significance
BHCD is attractive for practitioners: simple, fast, and yields interpretable hierarchies with predictive performance competitive with IRM and comparable to LFRM in prior splits. The independence from the number of communities in worst-case runtime is appealing. However:
- Empirical scalability is not fully demonstrated: the largest network (NIPS) has ~2.8k nodes and ~9.5k edges; larger and sparser graphs (e.g., 10^4–10^5 nodes) would better validate scalability and the O(n + m) likelihood advantage.
- Comparative evaluation omits key hierarchical baselines (MCBM, Mondrian-based, hierarchical IRM). Including at least one would strengthen claims.
- The “nonparametric” characterization may be misleading: the approach is greedy model selection over finite trees rather than a fully Bayesian nonparametric posterior over unbounded structures. Please clarify terminology and positioning.

Pros
- Clear, principled model leveraging exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Very fast greedy inference with careful caching; practical and easy to implement.
- Good qualitative hierarchies and competitive predictive performance vs. IRM, with substantial speedups.
- Sensible “sparse” variant for real-world networks.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to prior rose-tree and hierarchical network literature.
- Missing comparisons to existing hierarchical network models (MCBM, Mondrian, hierarchical IRM).
- Some key derivations and complexity claims are under-explained.
- Scalability not validated on truly large networks; hyperparameter sensitivity not studied.
- “Nonparametric” terminology is debatable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add detailed derivations for Eqs. (9)–(10), dynamic-programming pseudocode, and a formal complexity analysis including memory usage.
- Compare against at least one hierarchical baseline (e.g., Ho et al., Mondrian, Herlau et al.) on NIPS-234 and a larger synthetic/sparse dataset.
- Provide scalability experiments on larger graphs (≥10k nodes), including wall-clock, depth, and heap statistics; include ablations (no absorptions, binary-only).
- Report sensitivity to α,β,δ,λ,γ and discuss empirical guidance or hyperparameter inference.
- Clarify predictive complexity (linear in depth), handling of missing data, directed/self-edges, and the effect of the “sparse” constraint.
- Consider releasing code for reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A solid and practically useful contribution with strong engineering and promising empirical performance, but with limited conceptual novelty and missing comparisons to closely related hierarchical models. With stronger experimental baselines and clearer technical exposition, this could be a compelling paper. As it stands, I lean toward borderline acceptance; it fills a niche for fast hierarchical community discovery and could be valuable to the NIPS community, provided the authors address the clarity and evaluation gaps.
