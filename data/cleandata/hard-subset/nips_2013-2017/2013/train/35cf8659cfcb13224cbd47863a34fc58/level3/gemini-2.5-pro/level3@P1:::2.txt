This paper presents a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, which the authors call Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to define a model as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Unlike the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which considers an exponential space of flat partitions, this work cleverly restricts the space of possible partitions to only those that are consistent with a hierarchical tree structure. This restriction is the key technical insight, as it allows for the exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree via dynamic programming. For inference, the paper proposes a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees to build a final hierarchy, using a local Bayes factor as the merge criterion. The model is evaluated on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset, showing that it is significantly faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable or slightly better predictive accuracy.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a very important and practical problem in machine learning and network science: the scalability of Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection. Existing methods like the IRM often rely on slow MCMC samplers that are prohibitive for large networks. The proposed method provides a pragmatic solution that achieves a speedup of two orders of magnitude, making hierarchical Bayesian network analysis accessible to a wider range of problems and practitioners.

2.  **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The approach of defining a probabilistic model over tree-consistent partitions and combining it with a fast, greedy model selection algorithm is a novel and effective synthesis of ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering and relational modeling. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Eq. 10) and the efficient caching scheme for the agglomerative algorithm (allowing O(1) updates in the inner loop) are solid technical contributions.

3.  **Quality of Results:** The experimental results are compelling. On the NIPS-234 co-authorship network, BHCD achieves predictive performance (in terms of log-likelihood, accuracy, and AUC) comparable to the IRM in a fraction of the time (seconds vs. thousands of seconds). This is a strong demonstration that the proposed modeling assumptions and inference approximations are effective and advance the state-of-the-art in terms of the speed-accuracy trade-off for this class of models. The qualitative results on the Sampson monastery data also appear reasonable, recovering known social structures.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on the Inference Procedure:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear connection between the generative model described in Section 3 and the greedy inference algorithm in Section 4. The model is presented as fully Bayesian, but the inference is a greedy model selection procedure. It is unclear what objective this greedy algorithm is optimizing. Is it attempting to find the maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood tree `T`? The algorithm uses a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12) to make merge decisions. While intuitive, it is not explained how this local optimization relates to finding a tree with a high global marginal likelihood `p(D|T)`. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could clarify this relationship. For instance, is the final tree's likelihood `p(D|T)` guaranteed to be a local maximum under the defined merge operations? A more precise characterization of the algorithm as a search for a high-likelihood model structure, rather than as full posterior inference, would be more accurate and less confusing.

2.  **Writing and Presentation:** The paper's clarity suffers from numerous issues that hinder readability and would benefit from careful revision.
    *   There are several typographical and grammatical errors.
    *   The descriptions of the datasets are sparse. For example, the Sampson monastery network section mentions "time four is the most interesting time," but does not explain why.
    *   Some terminology is used without sufficient introduction for a general NIPS audience (e.g., "rose trees").
    *   Figure captions are not always self-contained. The caption for Figure 3 refers to "BHCD global," a variant not mentioned or defined anywhere in the main text. This makes the figure difficult to interpret.
    *   The mathematical exposition could be improved. For instance, the formal definition of tree-consistent partitions in Equation (5) is dense; a more intuitive explanation alongside the formalism would be helpful.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the critical and timely problem of scaling Bayesian methods for network analysis.
*   Presents a novel and elegant technical approach that combines a tree-structured prior with an efficient greedy search algorithm.
*   The empirical results are very strong, demonstrating a dramatic improvement in runtime over a key baseline (IRM) with no loss in predictive accuracy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The connection between the probabilistic model and the inference algorithm is poorly explained, leaving a significant conceptual gap in the paper's core methodology.
*   The overall quality of the writing and presentation is below the expected standard for NIPS, with clarity issues that obscure the contributions.

In conclusion, this paper contains a strong core idea and impressive results that are of clear interest to the NIPS community. The proposed method is a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian modeling. However, the paper is hampered by significant clarity issues, most critically the justification for the inference algorithm. If the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to clarify the relationship between their model and algorithm and improve the overall presentation, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication.
