- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method using tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels for hierarchical community detection.  
- Extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering (rose trees) to relational data and uses dynamic programming for efficient likelihood computation.  
- Claims scaling roughly O(n² log n) in vertices and two orders of magnitude speedup over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while matching predictive accuracy.  
- Technical approach is sound with clear grounding in stochastic blockmodels and well-motivated recursion and sufficient-statistic updates.  
- Dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) and sufficient-statistic updates (Eq. 13) are strengths that enable practical performance.  
- Experiments show interpretable hierarchies (Sampson’s network) and comparable predictive performance to IRM on NIPS co-authorship data with large speed gains (10s vs. 1000s seconds).  
- Paper is generally well-structured and algorithm presentation (Algorithm 1) is clear.  
- Section 3’s likelihood derivation and recursion are dense and lack intuition; a concrete example of sufficient-statistic updates is needed for reproducibility.  
- Significance: speed advantage over IRM is compelling for large or dense networks and useful for link prediction.  
- Originality is incremental—main novelty is adapting rose trees to network data with sufficient statistics, not a fundamentally new model.  
- Fails to compare against established hierarchical relational models (e.g., Ho et al. AISTATS 2011, Roy et al. NIPS 2009), weakening novelty and baseline evaluation.  
- The label "nonparametric" is misleading since BHCD searches a finite set of trees and behaves more like model selection than a true nonparametric prior.  
- Scalability claims are not empirically validated on large networks (>1,000 nodes); experiments use small datasets (NIPS-234, 234 nodes).  
- Lacks comparisons to depth-limited hierarchical methods (e.g., ILA, LFRM), reducing the strength of empirical claims.  
- Explanation for the O(n+m) likelihood claim is insufficient — the paper asserts it without demonstrating how dynamic programming avoids redundant computation.  
- Recommendations: clarify the "nonparametric" terminology, add comparisons to key hierarchical methods (Ho et al., Roy et al.), include scalability benchmarks on larger networks, and provide a concrete example for sufficient-statistic updates.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and practical but incremental; would be acceptable after added comparisons, clearer terminology, and stronger empirical validation.  
- Decision: Weak Reject (borderline Accept if authors address missing comparisons, terminology, and scalability tests).
