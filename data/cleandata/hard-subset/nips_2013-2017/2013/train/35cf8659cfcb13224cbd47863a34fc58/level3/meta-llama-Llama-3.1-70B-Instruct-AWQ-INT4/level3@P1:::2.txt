**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), extends the stochastic blockmodel to infer hierarchies instead of flat clusters. The authors introduce an agglomerative-clustering-based learning algorithm to select a tree of valid partitions. The empirical evaluation on two social connectivity corpora shows that the method is faster and slightly more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

**Strengths of the Paper**

* The paper addresses a relevant problem in social network analysis, which is to speed up Bayesian nonparametric methods and provide hierarchical network discovery useful to social scientists.
* The key technical insight of restricting partitions to tree-structured ones enables efficient marginal likelihood computation, which is a significant improvement over IRM's exponential partition space.
* The results are strong, with comparable or better AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, and orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

* The writing quality needs improvement, with spelling and grammar errors, vague phrasing, unclear dataset descriptions, and some unnecessary or obscure math.
* The figures are confusing and need to be improved for better clarity.
* The relationship between the greedy tree-learning procedure and the Bayesian generative model is unclear, and it is uncertain whether it performs valid posterior inference or finds a MAP/ML estimate.
* Section 4 needs a clearer explanation to resolve the above conceptual confusion.

**Originality and Significance**

* The paper proposes a novel extension of the stochastic blockmodel to infer hierarchies instead of flat clusters.
* The agglomerative-clustering-based learning algorithm is a new contribution to the field of social network analysis.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on two social connectivity corpora, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Clarity and Presentation**

* The writing quality needs improvement, with clearer and more concise language.
* The figures need to be improved for better clarity and readability.
* The dataset descriptions need to be clearer and more detailed.
* The math needs to be explained in a more intuitive and accessible way.

**Recommendation**

* The paper has sufficient novelty and strong results to merit publication, but the clarity and presentation issues need to be addressed.
* The authors need to improve the writing quality, figures, and dataset descriptions to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* The relationship between the greedy tree-learning procedure and the Bayesian generative model needs to be clarified, and Section 4 needs a clearer explanation.

* Clarity and Presentation: 5/10

Overall, the paper has strong results and a novel contribution to the field of social network analysis, but the writing quality and presentation need improvement. With revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to be a high-quality contribution to the field.
