This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a model for uncovering hierarchical structure in network data. The approach extends the well-known stochastic blockmodel by defining a prior over tree-structured partitions, in contrast to models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) which consider the entire space of flat partitions. The core technical contribution is a method for efficiently calculating the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. The paper then proposes a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm to search for a high-likelihood tree structure. This work relates to previous research in Bayesian nonparametric models for relational data, particularly the IRM, and Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods. The primary goal is to provide a method that retains the probabilistic grounding of a Bayesian model while offering significant computational speedups over traditional inference methods like MCMC.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper addresses two important and relevant problems in machine learning and network science: a) the computational cost of inference in Bayesian nonparametric models, and b) the discovery of hierarchical, rather than flat, community structures, which is often a more realistic representation for social and biological networks. The proposed method offers a pragmatic solution that could be highly useful for practitioners.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The key insight of restricting the universe of partitions to those consistent with a tree structure is elegant and effective. This constraint makes the computation of the marginal likelihood of a given tree tractable via a simple dynamic program (Equation 10), sidestepping the need to sum over the exponentially large space of all partitions that plagues models like the IRM. This is a strong technical contribution.

3.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's primary advantages. BHCD is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than a standard MCMC-based IRM, achieving comparable or slightly better predictive performance (AUC, accuracy, log-likelihood) in a fraction of the time. The runtime improvements are substantial and represent a significant practical advance. The qualitative results on the Sampson monastery network are also compelling and demonstrate the model's ability to find interpretable hierarchical structures.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Conceptual Clarity of the Inference Procedure:** The most significant weakness is the lack of a clear connection between the probabilistic model described in Section 3 and the greedy search algorithm in Section 4. The model in Section 3 defines a marginal likelihood p(D|T) by summing over partitions consistent with a tree T. However, the algorithm in Section 4 performs a greedy search over the space of trees, maximizing a local Bayes factor at each step. It is not clear what this procedure is optimizing with respect to the generative model.
    *   Is the algorithm attempting to find the maximum a posteriori (MAP) tree T? If so, this should be stated explicitly, and the connection between the local score (Equation 12) and the global objective p(D|T) should be justified.
    *   Is this algorithm a form of valid posterior inference, or is it a heuristic search procedure inspired by the Bayesian model? The term "Bayesian" in the paper's title and method name implies the former, but the greedy nature of the algorithm suggests the latter.
    *   Clarifying this point is crucial for understanding the theoretical grounding of the proposed method. A more detailed discussion in Section 4 explaining what objective the agglomerative procedure approximates would greatly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Writing and Presentation:** The paper's quality is unfortunately diminished by numerous issues with clarity and presentation that hinder readability and reproducibility.
    *   **Language:** There are several spelling and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., "Our algorithms scale quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but independent of the number of nested communities" in the abstract). Phrasing is sometimes vague.
    *   **Dataset Descriptions:** The description of the Sampson monastery data is too brief. It is unclear how "positive affiliations," "negative affiliations," and "unknown affiliation" were precisely mapped to the presence of edges, the observed absence of edges, and missing data, respectively. This detail is needed for reproducibility.
    *   **Mathematical Notation:** Some of the mathematical derivations could be explained more intuitively. For instance, the transition from the factorized likelihood and prior (Equations 7 and 8) to the recursive marginal likelihood (Equation 10) is a key step that could benefit from a more detailed walkthrough.
    *   **Figures:** Figure 3 is cluttered and difficult to parse. While it shows the results, a cleaner visualization (perhaps showing the hierarchy next to a single, clearly ordered adjacency matrix) would be more effective. The caption for Figure 5 is also minimal; it is unclear what the labels on the nodes (e.g., "Bayes", "Learning") represent without deeper domain knowledge of the NIPS community.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and computationally efficient method for hierarchical community detection. The core idea of using a tree-structured prior to enable efficient marginal likelihood calculation is clever, and the experimental results demonstrating massive speedups over the IRM with comparable accuracy are very strong.

However, the paper is held back by significant clarity issues, most importantly the ambiguous relationship between the generative model and the greedy inference algorithm. The numerous presentation flaws also make the paper difficult to follow.

I believe the work has sufficient novelty and impact to be published at NIPS, but not in its current state. If the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address the conceptual ambiguity surrounding the inference algorithm and fix the numerous presentation issues, it would be a strong contribution to the conference.

**Recommendation:** Borderline paper, leaning toward acceptance if major revisions for clarity are made.
