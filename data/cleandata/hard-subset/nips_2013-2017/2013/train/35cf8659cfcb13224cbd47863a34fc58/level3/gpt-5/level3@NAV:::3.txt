This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model that discovers hierarchical community structure by placing a tree-structured prior over partitions and mixing over (potentially exponentially many) stochastic block models (SBMs). The key idea is to replace the IRM’s CRP prior with a simpler tree-consistent partition prior (eq. 7) built from Bayesian Rose trees, enabling exact marginalization via a recursion that factorizes over subtrees (following Koivisto & Sood). This factorization supports a fast, greedy agglomerative model selection procedure (Algorithm 1) with O(n^2 log n) time, independent of the number of discovered communities, and efficient link prediction via recursive mixture along a path. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery and the NIPS co-authorship network show large speed gains over IRM with competitive predictive performance.

Quality: The modeling and algorithmic development are technically sound. The succinct use of f(·) and g(·) clarifies the generalization beyond beta–Bernoulli. The posterior factorization and DP recursion are well motivated, and the agglomerative scoring is locally consistent with cached sufficient statistics. However, the evaluation is under-specified and limited: the AUC computation protocol is unclear (e.g., whether links are deleted, how negatives are selected, and whether non-edges are treated as observed zeros or missing), and the comparisons center on IRM only.

Clarity: The paper is well written with good grammar and appropriate citations. Minor style issue: under NIPS style, avoid beginning sentences with citations. Please also explicitly describe the AUC protocol and add a brief sensitivity analysis for α,β,δ,λ,γ.

Originality: The combination of Rose-tree priors with SBM likelihoods, the reformulation of the IRM-style mixture via eq. (7), and the subtree-factorized marginal enabling a single-pass agglomerative search are novel in this context. The contribution is primarily algorithmic and computational.

Significance: The speed/accuracy tradeoff is attractive and could make hierarchical community discovery more practical. That said, the current experiments are illustrative rather than decisive. To strengthen impact, include larger, standard benchmarks used in recent SBM studies and compare against strong semi-parametric baselines (e.g., degree-corrected SBMs, hierarchical SBMs, spectral methods). Also report NMI (and ARI) for cluster quality, which is more common in SBM comparisons.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, elegant theory; exact marginal via subtree factorization.
- Practical, scalable agglomerative algorithm with strong speedups.
- Simple, reusable sufficient-statistics framework and link prediction.

Cons against acceptance:
- Evaluation lacks clarity on AUC protocol and broader metrics (NMI).
- Limited datasets and baselines; largely illustrative comparisons.
- No analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity or robustness.
