This paper presents a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method based on a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors address the problem of discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, a common task with applications in social network analysis and beyond. They propose an efficient agglomerative algorithm for learning this structure, aiming to overcome the computational limitations of existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The work builds on prior Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering and offers a compelling combination of theoretical grounding and practical efficiency.

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. The mathematical notation is concise and consistently applied, facilitating understanding of the model’s components (e.g., the use of *f()* and *g()* for likelihood functions). The authors effectively relate their work to existing literature on stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership models, and Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, acknowledging limitations of these approaches and positioning BHCD as a significant improvement, particularly in terms of computational speed. The connection to previous work by Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004) regarding posterior factorization is highlighted, demonstrating a strong foundation in relevant theoretical frameworks.

A significant strength of this work lies in its algorithmic contribution – the greedy agglomerative approach. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the computational bottlenecks in existing methods and develop a strategy to address them. The reported complexity of O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) represents a considerable improvement over the IRM, which suffers from higher computational costs, especially as the number of communities grows. The caching scheme described for efficient calculation of sufficient statistics is particularly noteworthy.

However, the evaluation section requires substantial improvement. While the authors present results on Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship dataset, the experimental design feels somewhat illustrative rather than rigorously comparative. The use of AUC for evaluation is mentioned, but the precise methodology (e.g., how links are removed for testing) remains unclear.  NMI (Normalized Mutual Information) is a more standard metric for evaluating SBM-based methods, and its inclusion would strengthen the analysis.  Crucially, the experiments lack comparisons to a broader range of semi-parametric methods, limiting the ability to fully assess the benefits of the Bayesian nonparametric approach. Including results on larger datasets commonly used in SBM evaluations would further bolster the claims of scalability and effectiveness. The comparison to IRM is valuable, but more diverse benchmarks would paint a more complete picture.

Regarding originality, the paper offers a novel combination of Bayesian nonparametric modeling with a practical, efficient agglomerative algorithm. The replacement of the CRP formulation in the IRM with the simpler formulation in equation (7) is a significant contribution. However, it’s important to acknowledge that the core building blocks – SBMs and tree-structured models – are established concepts. The originality lies in the specific integration of these elements and the development of the efficient inference procedure.

The significance of this work is considerable. The proposed method provides a practical solution for discovering hierarchical community structure in large networks, a problem with relevance across various disciplines. The potential for faster inference compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric techniques could encourage broader adoption of these methods in real-world applications. The ability to make predictions based on the inferred hierarchy further enhances its practical value.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and efficient algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery.
*   Strong theoretical grounding and clear connections to relevant literature.
*   Addresses a computationally challenging problem with a promising solution.
*   Demonstrates potential for scalability and speed improvements compared to existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation with small datasets and inadequate comparison to alternative methods.
*   Unclear methodology for AUC calculation.
*   Lack of broader benchmarks and comparisons to semi-parametric approaches.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the shortcomings in the experimental evaluation, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The proposed method is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and addresses a practically important problem. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the evaluation section. Specifically, they should provide a more rigorous and comprehensive experimental analysis, including comparisons to a wider range of methods and evaluation on larger, more established datasets, as well as a clear explanation of the AUC calculation procedure.
