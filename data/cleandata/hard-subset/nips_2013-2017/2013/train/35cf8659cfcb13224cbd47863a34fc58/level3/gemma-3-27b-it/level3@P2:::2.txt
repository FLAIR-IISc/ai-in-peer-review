This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is to represent the community structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, allowing for nested communities. A greedy agglomerative algorithm is then developed for efficient inference, aiming to sidestep the computational bottlenecks of existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate that their method achieves comparable or improved accuracy with significantly faster runtimes on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper addresses a significant problem in network analysis: scalable and probabilistic inference of hierarchical community structure. Existing methods often struggle with computational cost or lack a fully probabilistic treatment. BHCD offers a promising solution by cleverly exploiting the tree structure to enable efficient marginal likelihood computation, contrasting with the exponential complexity faced by methods exploring the full partition space. The connection to previous work, particularly the IRM and other Bayesian approaches to community detection, is well-established, and the motivation for the proposed method is clearly articulated.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. The core technical insight – restricting the space of partitions to those consistent with a tree – is a significant contribution enabling the computational efficiency gains. The empirical results are compelling, showcasing substantial speedups (two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM in some cases) with competitive or even superior performance on the tested datasets. The comparison to existing methods, including variations of BHCD itself (sparse vs. non-sparse, binary vs. non-binary trees), is thorough and insightful. The inclusion of Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data adds to the credibility of the evaluation. The demonstration of prediction capabilities is a valuable addition.

However, the paper also suffers from several weaknesses, primarily related to clarity and presentation. While the intuition behind the model is understandable, the precise relationship between the Bayesian generative model and the greedy tree-learning procedure (Section 4) requires further clarification. It is not immediately obvious whether the algorithm performs valid posterior inference or is merely finding a maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood (ML) estimate. A more explicit discussion of this point would significantly strengthen the paper.

The writing quality could be improved. There are instances of awkward phrasing, spelling/grammar errors, and unnecessarily complex mathematical notation. The descriptions of the datasets (e.g., NIPS co-authorship data) are somewhat sparse, and the figures (particularly Figure 1) could be more visually clear and informative.  More descriptive figure captions and labels would assist readers in understanding the model and experimental results. The discussion of the hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) lacks sufficient justification; a sensitivity analysis or a discussion of how these parameters impact the results would be beneficial.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, although the connection between the generative model and the inference algorithm warrants further clarification. The claims are generally well-supported by the experimental results, and the work appears complete, representing a significant contribution. The authors do attempt to address potential weaknesses, such as the trade-offs between sparse and non-sparse versions of the algorithm.
* **Clarity:** The clarity is the primary weakness. The writing is often dense and could benefit from simplification. The explanation of the inference procedure in Section 4 is particularly challenging to follow. Figures could be improved for visual clarity.
* **Originality:** The combination of a tree-structured prior on partitions with a greedy agglomerative algorithm for inference is novel. While building on existing work in Bayesian nonparametric modeling, the paper presents a distinct approach with significant practical advantages. The reference list appears comprehensive, covering relevant prior work.
* **Significance:** The results are important. The demonstrated speedup over existing methods makes the proposed approach more applicable to large-scale networks. The ability to discover hierarchical community structure is valuable for a wide range of applications in social network analysis and beyond. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian nonparametric community detection.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results demonstrating significant speedups with comparable accuracy.
* Novel and technically sound approach to hierarchical community discovery.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in network analysis.
* Clear motivation and connection to prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Clarity issues and writing quality need improvement.
* The relationship between the generative model and the inference algorithm requires further clarification.
* Lack of sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The technical ideas are strong, and the experimental results are compelling. However, the paper’s impact will be significantly enhanced by addressing the clarity and writing quality issues. If the authors can address these concerns, particularly by providing a more thorough explanation of the inference procedure and improving the presentation of their results, I would recommend acceptance. Currently, I rate this paper as a **weak accept**, contingent on significant revisions.
