This paper presents a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method, extending stochastic blockmodels and the infinite relational model (IRM) by leveraging Bayesian Rose trees to model hierarchical community structure. The authors propose an efficient agglomerative algorithm for inference, achieving significant speedups compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and technical details of the proposed method. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are succinct, particularly the use of f() and g() to represent the marginal likelihoods. The connection to prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] is clearly established, and the authors appropriately position their contribution within the broader landscape of community detection techniques. The introduction effectively motivates the need for hierarchical community discovery and highlights the limitations of existing approaches.

A key strength of this work lies in the clever adaptation of existing techniques—specifically, the factorization over subtrees inspired by Koivisto & Sood [2004]—to enable a greedy search and efficient marginal likelihood computation. This allows the method to scale favorably, as demonstrated in Table 1 and the experimental results. The algorithmic contribution, the agglomerative approach, is well-described and its complexity analysis is justified. The inclusion of variations like BHCD sparse, addressing the challenges of sparse networks, further demonstrates the authors’ attention to practical considerations.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while illustrative, is not sufficiently comprehensive. While the speedup compared to IRM is impressive, the experiments are relatively small-scale, and a more rigorous comparison to a wider range of methods—including semi-parametric approaches—is needed. Specifically, comparisons to recent advancements in scalable SBM inference would be valuable. The use of AUC as the primary evaluation metric is somewhat unclear; the method for computing it is not explicitly stated, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation. NMI (Normalized Mutual Information) is a more common metric for evaluating community structure and would provide a more standard basis for comparison.

The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network provide qualitative validation, but are limited in scope. The NIPS co-authorship dataset experiments demonstrate speed advantages, but a deeper analysis of the discovered hierarchies—beyond just runtime—would be beneficial. The full NIPS dataset analysis is promising, but the method for selecting which communities to display (based on the probability threshold rT) requires further clarification. A more thorough quantitative evaluation of the discovered hierarchies using established metrics for hierarchical community structure would strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge limitations of the method and suggest future directions—such as exploring non-conjugate likelihoods and adapting to triangular representations—these points could be elaborated on. The discussion section could also benefit from a more nuanced assessment of the trade-offs between computational efficiency and model expressiveness.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Bayesian Rose trees to hierarchical community discovery.
*   Efficient agglomerative inference algorithm with favorable scalability.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and theoretical foundations.
*   Demonstrated speedups compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of the experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of comprehensive comparison to a wider range of methods.
*   Unclear explanation of the AUC metric and its computation.
*   Insufficient quantitative evaluation of the discovered hierarchical structures.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection, offering a computationally efficient and theoretically sound approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. While the experimental evaluation could be more thorough, the method's novelty, efficiency, and clear presentation justify its consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns regarding the experimental evaluation and the clarification of the AUC metric. The authors should be encouraged to expand the experimental section to include larger datasets and comparisons to a wider range of relevant methods.
