Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a hierarchical extension of the stochastic blockmodel that places a tree prior over partitions and restricts attention to tree-consistent partitions. This restriction enables exact dynamic-programming computation of the marginal likelihood p(D|T) in O(n+m) for a fixed tree and yields an efficient greedy, agglomerative model-selection procedure to learn T in O(n^2 log n). The approach connects to Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) and Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al., 2010) by reusing their tree prior/selections but adapts them to relational data via on- and off-diagonal block likelihoods with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli terms. Empirically, BHCD is compared against the IRM (Kemp et al.) on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks; it is reported to be orders of magnitude faster and slightly more accurate, with additional qualitative insights from the learned hierarchy. Compared to prior hierarchical network models (e.g., [8], [11]), BHCD emphasizes scalability via tree-structured partition restriction and a one-pass agglomerative learning scheme.

Strengths
- Clear technical idea: restricting partitions to those consistent with a tree and combining a simple prior over tree-consistent partitions with conjugate blockmodels allows exact marginal likelihoods and efficient local Bayes factor computations.
- Practical impact: strong runtime improvements (two orders of magnitude vs IRM) with comparable or better AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy on social connectivity corpora.
- Useful contribution for social network analysis: infers interpretable hierarchical community structure, not just flat clusters, and makes fast predictions.
- Algorithmic design: well-engineered caching and local scoring enable O(1) inner-loop updates; sparse variant is sensible for real networks.
- Solid experimental protocol on standard datasets (Sampson; NIPS-234), with restarts and predictive evaluation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Conceptual clarity: it is unclear how the greedy, agglomerative tree-learning relates to the stated Bayesian generative model. Is the algorithm maximizing p(D|T) (model selection/MAP over trees), a lower bound, or an approximation to posterior inference p(T|D)? The use of local Bayes factors and absorptions vs joins needs a clearer derivation and justification.
- Model–algorithm mismatch: the “BHCD sparse” constraint (forbidding merges with zero inter-tree edges) seems to change the implied prior/support and could bias inference; its Bayesian interpretation is not discussed.
- Presentation issues: multiple spelling/grammar errors; some vague phrasing; figures need clearer legends/axes; Section 4’s notation (σ¬ch, caching rules) is hard to follow; dataset descriptions and evaluation splits could be clearer; some math is introduced before motivation.
- Limited ablations/sensitivity: fixed hyperparameters without sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference; no study of the impact of γ or the Beta priors; no memory/space complexity discussion for large graphs; limited discussion of tie-breaking and its effect on stability.
- Comparisons: while IRM is a natural baseline, the relation to other hierarchical network models ([8], [11]) and to flat-but-fast alternatives is underdeveloped; fairness of runtime comparison (C implementations, number of Gibbs sweeps, convergence diagnostics) should be documented.

Quality
- The core modeling and algorithmic ideas are technically sound, and the dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) is a nice insight. Empirical results support speed and accuracy claims on two datasets. However, the training objective optimized by the greedy procedure should be explicitly stated and theoretically tied to the generative model (even if only as a heuristic/MAP). Provide a theorem or proposition showing that each accepted merge increases p(D|F) (Eq. 11), or explain when/why local Bayes factor scoring is only an approximation.

Clarity
- The paper needs editing for grammar and flow. Section 4 is the primary source of confusion: please give a step-by-step derivation of Eq. (12) from Eq. (11), define all sufficient statistics once, and provide a small working example illustrating each merge type (join vs absorption) and how σ¬ch updates. Figures should have self-contained captions, consistent ordering of leaves, and clearer visualization of on/off-diagonal blocks.

Originality
- Novel and useful combination: importing Bayesian rose-tree machinery into relational blockmodeling with a tailored likelihood and efficient learning. The tree-restricted partition idea and exact marginal likelihood computation are well-motivated and meaningfully different from IRM and prior hierarchical extensions.

Significance
- High practical relevance: the method addresses the key obstacle of speed in Bayesian nonparametrics for networks and offers hierarchical structure discovery of direct use to social scientists. If clarity is improved, the work is likely to be adopted and extended.

Requests for clarification (particularly Section 4)
1) Objective: Are you maximizing p(D|T) for a single tree T (i.e., Bayesian model selection/MAP over trees with implicit p(T))? If so, state the objective and the assumed prior on trees. If not, characterize the approximation to posterior inference.
2) Local score derivation: Derive Eq. (12) explicitly from Eq. (11), showing which terms cancel and under what assumptions locality holds. Does every greedy merge guarantee a non-decreasing p(D|F)?
3) Absorption vs join: Provide an intuition and formal justification for allowing “absorb” merges and how they relate to the prior πS and the generative process.
4) BHCD sparse: Give a Bayesian interpretation (e.g., a zero-probability prior on inter-cluster edges) or acknowledge it as a heuristic, and quantify its impact on likelihood vs speed.
5) Hyperparameters: Report sensitivity to α, β, δ, λ, γ; consider simple empirical Bayes updates or sampling.
6) Experimental details: Describe train/test splits, number of restarts R and selection criteria, exact IRM settings (iterations, burn-in), and memory/runtime environment; include stability across random tie-breaking.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, relevant problem with a principled and efficient solution.
- Clear technical insight enabling exact marginal likelihoods and fast learning.
- Empirically compelling speedups with competitive accuracy.
- Provides interpretable hierarchical structures.

Cons against acceptance
- Ambiguity about the inference objective and its Bayesian validity.
- Presentation/writing issues hinder reproducibility and understanding.
- Limited sensitivity analysis and ablations; sparse variant’s status unclear.
- Narrow set of baselines and limited discussion of related hierarchical models.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. The core idea and results are strong and practically valuable, and the contribution is novel relative to IRM-style approaches. However, the paper needs significant improvements in clarity—especially a precise statement of the learning objective and the relationship of the greedy procedure to the Bayesian model—plus tightened exposition and more thorough experimental details. If these are addressed, the paper merits publication.
