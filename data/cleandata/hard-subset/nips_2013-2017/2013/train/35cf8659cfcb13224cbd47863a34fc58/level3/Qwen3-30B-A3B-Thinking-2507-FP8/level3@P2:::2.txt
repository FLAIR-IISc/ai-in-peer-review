

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structure in social networks via a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The key innovation lies in restricting partitions to tree-consistent ones, enabling exact marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming (O(n+m) time) rather than approximating over the exponential space of partitions as in the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). BHCD employs a greedy agglomerative algorithm (Algorithm 1) that merges trees in O(n² log n) time, achieving two orders of magnitude faster inference than IRM while matching or exceeding its predictive accuracy on benchmark datasets. The work builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) and the IRM (Kemp et al., 2006), but addresses a critical gap in scaling Bayesian hierarchical methods to large networks.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Soundness & Significance:** The computational efficiency claim is well-supported by theoretical analysis (Section 4) and empirical results (Table 1, Figure 4). BHCD’s O(n² log n) complexity (vs. IRM’s O(n²K²)) is a substantial advance for large-scale network analysis, with runtime improvements validated on NIPS co-authorship data. The empirical results demonstrate strong predictive performance (AUC, log-likelihood) comparable to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., ILA, LFRM) while being vastly faster.  
2. **Originality:** Restricting partitions to tree-structured sets to enable efficient inference is a novel and clever solution to the scalability bottleneck in Bayesian hierarchical community detection. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work (e.g., [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]) by avoiding expensive MCMC or depth-constrained hierarchies.  
3. **Relevance:** Hierarchical community discovery is a well-motivated problem for social scientists (e.g., Sampson’s monastery analysis), and the method directly addresses the need for scalable Bayesian approaches in this domain.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Conceptual Confusion in Bayesian Interpretation:** The paper claims BHCD provides a "fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model," but Section 4’s greedy algorithm (Algorithm 1) does not compute the posterior over trees. Instead, it selects a single tree via local Bayes factors (Section 4.1), which corresponds to a MAP estimate, not Bayesian inference. This undermines the core "Bayesian" claim. The generative model (Section 3) and inference (Section 4) are disconnected: the algorithm does not sample from the posterior or compute full marginal likelihoods for model comparison. This is a critical flaw for a NIPS paper, as the Bayesian framework is central to the contribution.  
2. **Clarity & Presentation:** The writing contains significant issues:  
   - Ambiguous terminology (e.g., "rose trees" without definition in Section 4.1; "BHCD global" in Section 5.1 lacks explanation).  
   - Vague descriptions (e.g., "the algorithm finishes when just one tree remains" without justifying why this is meaningful).  
   - Figure 3 lacks axis labels and legend, making log-likelihood comparisons (−6.62 vs. −22.80) unclear.  
   - Section 4 is overly technical without sufficient motivation for the greedy selection criteria.  
   - Spelling errors ("naı̈ve," "co-occurrence") and grammatical issues (e.g., "this does not seem to be a reasonable behaviour") reduce professionalism.  
3. **Missing Analysis:** The paper does not address why the greedy approach (which ignores global dependencies) still yields competitive results. A brief comparison of the inferred trees’ interpretability (e.g., via dendrogram quality) versus IRM would strengthen the significance claim.

**Recommendation:**  
This paper addresses a significant problem with a technically sound (if mislabeled) solution that delivers strong empirical results. However, the conceptual misalignment between the claimed Bayesian framework and the MAP-like inference method is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. If the authors clarify the algorithm’s relationship to Bayesian inference (e.g., by explicitly framing it as a fast MAP estimator or modifying the inference to compute posteriors), and substantially improve the writing and presentation, this work would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, the paper’s core contribution is obscured by its own conceptual confusion.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel scaling solution, strong empirical results, relevant problem, clear technical contribution to computational efficiency.  
*Con:* Misleading Bayesian claims, poor writing, conceptual disconnect between model and inference.  

**Decision:** Weak Accept (with major revisions required to clarify the Bayesian interpretation and improve writing). The paper has significant potential but must resolve the fundamental methodological confusion to meet NIPS standards.
