- Proposal: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with an exact marginal likelihood via dynamic programming, adapting Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data.  
- Inference: Greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor; runs in O(n^2 log n) and is much faster than MCMC-based IRM.  
- Novelty/significance: Non-trivial adaptation of Rose Tree ideas to relational data (likelihood formulation and caching), yielding a probabilistic and highly efficient hierarchical community model.  
- Technical quality: Model and recursive marginal likelihood derivations are sound and clearly presented; Algorithm 1 summarizes inference concisely.  
- Empirical evidence: Convincing qualitative results (Sampson’s monastery) and strong quantitative results (NIPS-234) showing ~100× speed-up over IRM with comparable or better predictive performance; method scales to full NIPS dataset.  
- Clarity/presentation: Exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and illustrated; explanations and figures effectively support the contributions.  
- Scalability concern: O(n^2 log n) may be prohibitive for very large networks; the proposed “BHCD sparse” variant needs more analysis and concrete runtime reporting (e.g., full NIPS n=2864 vs NIPS-234).  
- Greedy inference limitation: Greedy merging can get stuck in local optima despite restarts; authors should quantify solution quality (e.g., exhaustive search on small graphs) and seek theoretical justification or guarantees (e.g., submodularity).  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Experiments use fixed hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ); the paper should discuss how these were chosen and how sensitive results are to them.  
- Minor edits: A few grammatical fixes suggested (examples given in abstract, Sec. 4, Sec. 6).  
- Recommendation: Strong, significant contribution with large computational gains; recommend acceptance despite greedy inference limitations.
