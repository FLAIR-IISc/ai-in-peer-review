This paper proposes BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical communities in networks. The key idea is to represent a hierarchy as a tree that induces a mixture over all partitions consistent with that tree; the marginal likelihood can be computed exactly via a dynamic program that recurses over tree nodes. The likelihood uses a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate, different priors for within- and between-cluster densities, enabling closed-form marginalization. The prior over partitions is defined by per-node stopping probabilities (as in Bayesian rose trees), favoring non-binary branching when hierarchy is weak. Inference is a greedy agglomerative procedure with three merge operations (join and two absorptions) scored by a local Bayes factor; careful caching yields O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time, independent of the number of latent communities. A sparse variant forbids merges between disconnected components. The authors provide a recursive predictive distribution for link prediction by traversing the tree path between two vertices. Experiments show qualitative recovery on Sampson’s monastery data, competitive link-prediction on NIPS-234 at orders-of-magnitude lower time than IRM, and a large-scale hierarchy for the full NIPS co-authorship graph.

Relation to prior work: Compared to the IRM and its hierarchical extensions (e.g., Mondrian process and IRM-with-tree variants), BHCD trades MCMC/variational inference for greedy model selection, leveraging the Bayesian rose tree machinery to get an exact marginal likelihood for a given tree. Relative to flat SBMs and mixed-membership SBMs, BHCD discovers nested structure and does not require pre-specifying the number of groups. Prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods operated on i.i.d. data; adapting them to relational blockmodels with on/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods and local merge scoring is a nontrivial and useful contribution.

Strengths
- Elegant modeling: a principled mixture over tree-consistent partitions with exact marginalization and a clear recursion for p(D|T), plus a simple predictive scheme.
- Efficient inference: O(n^2 log n) regardless of the number of communities; the sparse variant accelerates on sparse graphs. The local Bayes factor and caching are well thought out.
- Strong empirical story: on NIPS-234, BHCD reaches useful accuracy and AUC before IRM completes a single full Gibbs pass, and remains competitive with LFRM and below ILA, aligning with expectations given model simplicity. Qualitative results on Sampson and a large NIPS hierarchy are convincing.
- Clarity: the paper is well written, with clear derivations and algorithms.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability at very large n: O(n^2 log n) can still be prohibitive for graphs with 10^4–10^5+ nodes. The sparse variant helps, but there is no systematic scaling study vs. n and m or memory usage. Timings on “full NIPS” are not reported; please include wall-clock time and memory for Sampson, NIPS-234, and full NIPS, and, if possible, a larger real-world network (social or biological) with ≥10k nodes.
- Optimality of greedy search: the algorithm uses local merges and random tie-breaking with R restarts, but lacks theoretical guarantees. It would strengthen the paper to (a) benchmark the greedy solution against exhaustive search on small n (e.g., n ≤ 20) to quantify the gap; and/or (b) provide conditions (e.g., approximate submodularity/consistency) under which the greedy procedure is near-optimal.
- Sensitivity and robustness: hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed. Please report sensitivity analyses for γ and the Beta priors, and an ablation of binary-only vs. rose, and sparse vs. dense variants on predictive performance and runtime. Also discuss the effect of the number of restarts R.
- Scope and generality: the model focuses on undirected, unweighted graphs without self-loops; the text claims broader applicability. A brief experiment or discussion on directed/weighted networks and handling of missing data would be helpful.
- Empirical fairness: clarify IRM implementations (MCMC vs. greedy), number of iterations, and how convergence was judged to ensure fair time/accuracy comparisons.

Quality
- The technical development is sound, with correct marginal likelihood derivation, an appropriate prior, and coherent predictive computations. The greedy inference is a reasonable engineering trade-off. Claims about speed vs. IRM are supported; predictive quality comparisons are consistent with expectations.

Clarity
- Very clear exposition and organization. Minor language/typography issues listed below could be fixed easily.

Originality
- While the prior over partitions and greedy framework build directly on Bayesian rose trees, the adaptation to relational data via blockmodel marginal likelihoods, the local Bayes factor for merges, and the O(1) caching scheme for sufficient statistics constitute a novel and practically meaningful combination.

Significance
- Addresses an important gap: scalable Bayesian discovery of hierarchical community structure. Likely to be used as a fast baseline and as a practical tool when very fast, interpretable hierarchical structure is desired. The O(n^2) barrier limits applicability to extremely large graphs, but the method fills a useful niche between heuristic hierarchical clustering and slow nonparametrics.

Specific suggestions
- Provide wall-clock timings and memory usage for all datasets; add a larger network if feasible.
- Add sensitivity analyses for γ and priors; report impact of R restarts.
- Evaluate optimality gap on small synthetic datasets with known hierarchies, or give theoretical insight into the greedy objective (e.g., conditions implying near-optimality).
- Discuss (or demonstrate briefly) directed/weighted extensions and handling of missing entries.

Minor corrections
- Fix phrasing such as “whilst … but” (line ~48), “pair of trees” (plural), “A fruitful next step” (singular), agreement errors (“run time … is,” “algorithms are”), and typos in references (e.g., Physical Review E; Eric P. Xing). Ensure consistent citation formatting and notation (e.g., subscripts/superscripts in equations).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled model with exact marginal likelihood for a given tree.
- Substantial speedups over IRM while retaining competitive predictive performance.
- Nontrivial and well-executed adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to relational data.
- Clean code-friendly algorithm with practical impact and nice qualitative results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Greedy inference without guarantees; limited analysis of optimality gap.
- Limited scalability evidence beyond a few datasets; O(n^2 log n) may be limiting at larger scales.
- Sparse/parameter sensitivity and robustness not thoroughly explored.

Overall recommendation
- Accept. Despite drawing on known BRT ideas, the contribution is significant for network modeling: a well-motivated, efficient Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery with strong empirical evidence and clear presentation.
