This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to apply the Bayesian Rose Tree framework, previously developed for agglomerative clustering, to relational data. This is achieved by defining a likelihood for the network's adjacency matrix based on a stochastic blockmodel, where different priors are used for within-community (on-diagonal) and between-community (off-diagonal) blocks. The model defines a marginal likelihood over tree structures, which represent the community hierarchy. Inference is performed using a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor, building the hierarchy in a single pass over the data. This work builds directly on the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and the Bayesian Rose Trees of Blundell et al. (2010), adapting their model selection approach to the network analysis domain, specifically as an alternative to methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The primary contribution is the adaptation of the Bayesian Rose Tree's marginal likelihood calculation and greedy search algorithm to network data. The derivation of the recursive sufficient statistic updates (Equation 13), which allows the inner loop of the merge algorithm to be performed in O(1) time after initial caching, is a key component for achieving the claimed O(n^2 log n) overall complexity. The experimental setup is reasonable, demonstrating the method's ability to find meaningful structure in the classic Sampson monastery dataset and showing impressive speed gains over a flat IRM baseline on the NIPS co-authorship network.

However, the experimental evaluation has a major weakness that significantly impacts the assessment of the work's quality and contribution. The paper positions itself as a method for *hierarchical* community discovery, yet it only compares its performance against the IRM, a model for *flat* community discovery. To properly validate the proposed method, it is essential to include comparisons with other prominent Bayesian hierarchical network models. Relevant baselines would include the hierarchical extension of the IRM [8], the multiscale community blockmodel [11], and the Mondrian process [12]. Without these comparisons, it is impossible to determine if BHCD offers a tangible improvement in the speed-accuracy trade-off over existing hierarchical approaches.

Furthermore, while scalability is a central selling point, the experiments are performed on networks that are not particularly large by modern standards (NIPS-234 and the full NIPS network with 2864 nodes). Demonstrating the empirical scaling on larger graphs would lend more weight to the complexity claims.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and the overall structure is logical. The introduction effectively situates the work within the literature, and the description of the agglomerative algorithm in Section 4 is clear.

The main area where clarity could be improved is in Section 3, "Bayesian Hierarchical Communities." This section is dense and the derivation of the model's core recursion is difficult to follow. The crucial step of calculating the marginal likelihood p(D|T) via dynamic programming, culminating in Equation (10), is presented without sufficient explanation. A more detailed walkthrough of how the sum over tree-consistent partitions in Equation (6) simplifies into the recursive form of Equation (10) would be invaluable. Similarly, the definition of the off-diagonal sufficient statistics σ¬chSS in Equation (9) and how it is used in the likelihood factorization would benefit from a more explicit explanation. Providing more detailed derivations, perhaps in a supplementary appendix, would greatly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility for readers not already deeply familiar with the Rose Tree framework.

**Originality**

The work's originality lies in the novel combination and adaptation of existing techniques rather than the development of a fundamentally new model. It takes the well-established Bayesian Rose Tree model and inference procedure [14] and pairs it with a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The main technical novelty is the specific implementation of this combination, particularly the efficient caching and updating of sufficient statistics for the blockmodel likelihood within the agglomerative search framework.

While this is a sensible and useful contribution, the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited since the core components—the tree prior, the inference algorithm, and the blockmodel likelihood—are all pre-existing. The authors are transparent about building on this prior work. A minor point on framing: the paper uses the term "Bayesian nonparametric," which is associated with the underlying Chinese Restaurant Process prior in the IRM. The Rose Tree framework, however, is better described as a form of Bayesian model selection over a discrete (though exponentially large) space of tree structures. A more precise framing would strengthen the paper.

**Significance**

The potential significance of this work is considerable. A scalable, principled Bayesian method for discovering community hierarchies is a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. The empirical results, showing a speedup of two orders of magnitude compared to MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance, are compelling and highlight the practical utility of the proposed greedy approach.

However, the paper's current significance is hampered by the aforementioned lack of comparison to other hierarchical models. To truly advance the state of the art, the paper must demonstrate that BHCD is not just faster than a flat model, but that it also offers a competitive or superior solution compared to existing hierarchical techniques. If BHCD is significantly faster than other hierarchical models while producing hierarchies of similar quality, its impact would be substantial. As it stands, the paper demonstrates a fast greedy algorithm for a hierarchical blockmodel, but does not yet establish its place in the landscape of hierarchical network modeling.

In summary, this is a solid paper describing a useful and efficient adaptation of a powerful hierarchical clustering framework to network data. The main strengths are its technical soundness and impressive computational speed. The primary weaknesses are the lack of crucial experimental comparisons to other hierarchical models and a need for greater clarity in the model's technical description. With a strengthened experimental section and improved exposition, this could be a significant contribution.
