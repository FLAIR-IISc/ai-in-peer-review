## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to represent the network as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, leveraging a greedy agglomerative approach for efficient inference. The authors build upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering and aim to address the scalability limitations of existing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The key technical contribution lies in adapting the likelihood computation and efficiently updating sufficient statistics for network data, achieving a claimed O(nodes + edges) complexity.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivates the problem and the proposed approach. The connection to existing work in stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian nonparametric methods is well-established. The experimental results demonstrate the method’s speed advantage over the IRM and qualitative success on Sampson’s monastery network, with averaging over hypotheses improving prediction. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Section 3, particularly around equation (9) and subsequent derivations, is dense and difficult to follow. The explanations of the dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates lack sufficient detail, hindering a complete understanding of the inference process. A significant drawback is the lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models such as the multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al. 2012] or the Mondrian process [Roy et al. 2009]. This omission makes it difficult to assess the relative merits of BHCD. 

Furthermore, the use of “nonparametric” terminology feels somewhat questionable given the prior framing of rose trees as model selection rather than a truly nonparametric Bayesian approach. While scalability is discussed, the tested networks are not exceptionally large, leaving empirical scalability somewhat undemonstrated. The claim of O(nodes + edges) complexity needs more robust empirical validation on larger datasets.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and integrates prior work effectively.
*   Demonstrates a speed advantage over the IRM.
*   Presents a novel adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to network data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited originality; builds heavily on existing concepts.
*   Insufficient explanation of key technical details.
*   Lack of comparison to relevant hierarchical network models.
*   Questionable use of “nonparametric” terminology.
*   Scalability claims not fully supported by empirical evidence.



**Overall:** This paper presents a solid contribution, but requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses in explanation, comparative evaluation, and justification of terminology. I recommend **rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit** after addressing these concerns. A more thorough comparison to existing hierarchical models and a clearer explanation of the inference process are crucial for elevating the paper’s impact.
