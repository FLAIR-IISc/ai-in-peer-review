### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical community structures in networks. The proposed model can be understood as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The core technical contribution is a method for efficiently calculating the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree structure. Unlike the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which considers an exponentially large space of flat partitions, this work restricts the set of possible partitions to those consistent with a hierarchical tree. This constraint allows the marginal likelihood to be computed exactly and efficiently via dynamic programming.

For inference, the authors propose a greedy agglomerative algorithm that builds the hierarchy in a bottom-up fashion. Starting with each node as its own community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of subtrees that yield the largest increase in the Bayes factor, a local measure of model evidence. The authors demonstrate their method on two social network datasets, showing that it discovers meaningful hierarchies and achieves comparable or better predictive accuracy than the IRM, while being orders of magnitude faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper appears to be technically sound. The derivation of the marginal likelihood under the tree-structured partition prior (Eq. 10) is clever and provides the foundation for the method's efficiency. The agglomerative algorithm is well-defined, and the experimental evaluation is strong, comparing against a relevant and widely-used baseline (IRM).

However, a major point of conceptual confusion arises from the connection between the generative model (Section 3) and the greedy inference procedure (Section 4). The paper presents a fully probabilistic model but then uses a greedy model selection algorithm to find a single tree structure. It is unclear if this greedy procedure is intended to find a maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood tree, or if it should be viewed as a form of approximate posterior inference. This lack of clarity on the theoretical grounding of the inference algorithm in relation to the generative model is a significant weakness. Is the Bayes factor score in Eq. (12) a valid proxy for the global posterior probability of the forest? A more detailed explanation would greatly strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:**
The clarity of the paper needs significant improvement. While the core ideas are understandable, the presentation is hampered by several issues:
*   There are numerous spelling and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript that detract from its readability.
*   The descriptions of the datasets and experimental setup are sometimes vague. For example, in the Sampson's Monastery experiment, the handling of "negative affiliations as observed absent edges" and "unknown affiliation as missing data" could be explained more clearly.
*   Some mathematical notation and derivations could benefit from more intuition. The relationship between the prior on partitions (Eq. 7) and the final recursive likelihood (Eq. 10) could be elaborated on for better understanding.
*   The figures are difficult to interpret without more detailed captions. For instance, Figure 3 presents several trees but does not sufficiently explain the differences between them or why one (BHCD) is considered superior despite another (BHCD sparse) having a better log-likelihood. Figure 5 is interesting but would be more impactful with more context about the discovered communities.

**Originality:**
The paper makes a novel contribution by combining ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) with relational models for community detection. While hierarchical extensions of the IRM exist, this work's focus on a greedy agglomerative search guided by a marginalized likelihood is a new and practical approach. The key insight of restricting partitions to be tree-consistent to achieve computational tractability is a significant and original contribution to scalable Bayesian nonparametric methods for network analysis. The work is well-situated within existing literature and clearly articulates its relationship to prior models like the IRM.

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant. The problem of discovering hierarchical structure in networks is important in many fields, particularly in the social sciences, and scalable methods are in high demand. The proposed method offers a practical solution that is orders of magnitude faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM, while maintaining competitive predictive performance. This speed-up makes Bayesian hierarchical analysis feasible for much larger networks than was previously possible. If the method proves to be robust, it is likely that other researchers and practitioners will use and build upon these ideas. The dramatic runtime improvement combined with strong accuracy represents a demonstrable advance in the state of the art for this problem.

### Conclusion and Suggestions for Improvement

In summary, this paper presents an innovative, efficient, and highly promising method for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. The core algorithmic and experimental contributions are insightful, and the performance improvements over existing methods are impressive. The paper addresses a problem of significant interest to the NIPS community.

However, the paper is currently held back by a lack of clarity in its presentation. I would recommend acceptance if the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address the following points:

1.  **Clarify the Inference Procedure:** The authors must provide a clearer theoretical justification for the greedy agglomerative algorithm in Section 4. Explain its relationship to the probabilistic model in Section 3. Is it finding a MAP estimate? Is it a variational procedure? This is the most critical point that needs to be addressed.
2.  **Improve Writing Quality:** The manuscript requires a thorough proofreading to correct numerous grammatical and spelling errors. Phrasing should be made more precise throughout.
3.  **Enhance Experimental Descriptions and Figures:** Provide clearer descriptions of experimental setups and improve the captions and explanations for all figures to make them more self-contained and interpretable.

With these revisions, this paper would represent a strong and valuable contribution to the conference proceedings.
