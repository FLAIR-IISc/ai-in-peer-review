This paper proposes a Bayesian model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The core idea is to combine the Bayesian rose tree prior, previously used for hierarchical clustering of vector data, with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood suitable for relational data. This extends the greedy agglomerative inference framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2010) to the network domain. The model defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a given tree structure, which allows the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree to be computed exactly and efficiently via dynamic programming. The main technical contribution is the adaptation of the likelihood and the design of an efficient caching scheme for sufficient statistics, enabling a greedy bottom-up algorithm to learn the tree structure. The authors claim the likelihood can be computed in O(n+m) time (nodes + edges), and the overall greedy search has a time complexity of O(n^2 log n).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality & Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed model is a well-motivated and sensible integration of two established probabilistic models: rose trees for hierarchies and blockmodels for networks. The derivation of the agglomerative algorithm, which reuses the structure from prior work on rose trees while adapting the merge scores for network data, is a solid piece of engineering. The use of a fully probabilistic framework allows for principled model selection and prediction.

2.  **Efficiency:** A key strength is the focus on computational efficiency. The greedy, single-pass nature of the algorithm, combined with the efficient updates of sufficient statistics, allows it to be significantly faster than iterative MCMC-based methods like the standard Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that BHCD can produce comparable or better predictions than the IRM in a fraction of the time.

3.  **Clarity (in parts):** The paper is generally well-written, with a clear introduction that situates the work within the literature. The high-level motivation and the overall algorithmic structure are easy to understand. The qualitative results on the Sampson monastery data are presented clearly and show that the method finds interpretable social structures.

4.  **Significance:** The work provides a practical and fast Bayesian method for hierarchical network analysis. Given the prevalence of hierarchical structure in real-world networks, an efficient tool for its discovery is a valuable contribution. The speed advantage over sampling-based methods makes it applicable to larger datasets where MCMC would be intractable.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity (Section 3):** The clarity of the paper breaks down significantly in Section 3, which contains the core technical details. The recursive definition of the marginal likelihood in equation (10) and the update rule for sufficient statistics in equation (9) are presented too densely. The crucial dynamic programming step that enables the efficient likelihood calculation is asserted but not adequately explained. This makes it very difficult for the reader to follow the derivation, verify its correctness, or reproduce the implementation. The paper would be substantially improved by a more detailed, step-by-step explanation or an appendix with pseudo-code for the likelihood calculation.

2.  **Originality:** While the combination of techniques is novel, the conceptual originality is somewhat limited. The framework directly builds upon Bayesian rose trees, and the primary contribution is adapting the likelihood and inference for a new data modality. This is more of an incremental, though useful, extension rather than a fundamentally new approach to hierarchical modeling.

3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** This is a major weakness. The experiments only compare against a flat IRM. To properly assess the contribution, the paper must include comparisons against other *hierarchical* Bayesian network models. The authors cite relevant work such as the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., 2012) and the Mondrian process (Roy et al., 2009) but do not compare against them. Without these comparisons, it is impossible to know if BHCD offers any advantage in terms of model fit or predictive accuracy over the existing state-of-the-art for this specific problem.

4.  **Scalability Claims:** The scalability claims are not fully substantiated by the experiments. While the asymptotic complexity is analyzed, the datasets used (NIPS-234 and the full NIPS network of ~2800 nodes) are not particularly large by modern standards. The O(n^2 log n) complexity of the greedy search may still be prohibitive for networks with tens or hundreds of thousands of nodes. It would be more convincing to demonstrate the method's performance on larger graphs to validate its practical scalability.

5.  **Terminology:** The use of the term "Bayesian nonparametric" is debatable. The rose tree model is more accurately framed as a form of Bayesian model selection over an exponentially large but discrete set of hierarchical partitionings. It does not involve priors over infinite-dimensional objects in the way that Dirichlet process or Pitman-Yor process models do. The authors should be more precise in their terminology to avoid confusion.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** A novel and sound integration of rose trees and blockmodels for a fully probabilistic treatment of hierarchical community detection.
*   **Pro:** The greedy inference algorithm is very fast and empirically outperforms a standard flat baseline (IRM) in terms of speed and predictive accuracy.
*   **Con:** Key technical details in Section 3 are poorly explained, hindering reproducibility and verification.
*   **Con:** The experimental evaluation is weak due to the lack of comparisons with any other hierarchical network models.
*   **Con:** Scalability claims are not fully demonstrated on large-scale networks.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a useful contribution. The proposed method is elegant and practical. However, the weaknesses in the technical explanation and, most importantly, the experimental comparison significantly limit its impact. I would recommend acceptance if the authors can substantially improve the clarity of Section 3 and, crucially, add experimental comparisons to other relevant hierarchical network models.
