This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is to represent the network using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, leveraging a Bayesian approach to hierarchical clustering and a novel efficient agglomerative model selection algorithm. The authors claim their method scales favorably with network size, achieving performance comparable to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) but with significantly improved speed.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in community detection, acknowledging both flat community models like the stochastic blockmodel [Holland et al., 1971] and mixed membership extensions [Airoldi et al., 2008], as well as hierarchical approaches like greedy clustering [Girvan & Newman, 2002] and Bayesian methods including the IRM [Kemp et al., 2006; Xu et al., 2006]. It positions itself as offering an efficient alternative to the computationally expensive hierarchical IRM [Herlau et al., 2008] and methods limited to specific hierarchy depths [Palla et al., 2007; Eliassi-Rad & Koutsourelakis, 2008]. The authors appropriately cite related work, demonstrating an understanding of the existing landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model appears theoretically well-founded, carefully combining Bayesian principles with a tree-based hierarchical structure and a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The claimed efficiency improvements through caching of sufficient statistics are a significant contribution.
* **Scalability:** The authors address a critical limitation of many Bayesian hierarchical models – computational cost. The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is a substantial improvement over existing methods, and the reported empirical speedup over the IRM is compelling.
* **Clarity (Overall):** The paper is generally well-written and organized, providing a clear introduction to the problem and the proposed solution. The algorithm description is relatively easy to follow.
* **Practical Results:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network demonstrate the method’s ability to recover known community structure. The comparison to the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset provides quantitative evidence of the method’s efficiency and predictive performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Section 3):** The mathematical details in Section 3, particularly equation (9) and the following derivations, are dense and lack sufficient explanation. A more step-by-step walkthrough of the likelihood calculation would improve understanding.
* **Insufficient Detail:** The explanation of the dynamic programming approach and the recursive updates for sufficient statistics is inadequate. Providing more details on these critical implementation aspects is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the efficiency gains.
* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** A major weakness is the lack of comparison to other existing hierarchical network models. The paper does not compare against methods like the multiscale community blockmodel [Ho et al., 2012] or the Mondrian process [Roy & Teh, 2009], which are relevant baselines. This limits the assessment of the novelty and significance of the contribution.
* **"Nonparametric" Terminology:** The use of "nonparametric" is somewhat questionable, as the method relies on a pre-defined tree prior (rose trees) and functions more as a model selection approach rather than a truly nonparametric Bayesian procedure.
* **Scalability Demonstration:** While scalability is a key claim, the experiments are performed on networks that are not exceedingly large. Further evaluation on larger datasets would strengthen the empirical evidence of the method's scalability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of community detection.
* The proposed model addresses a key limitation of existing Bayesian hierarchical methods – computational cost.
* The experimental results demonstrate promising performance and efficiency gains.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work and position their contribution appropriately within the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison to other hierarchical network models weakens the assessment of the method’s novelty and significance.
* The clarity of Section 3 and the details of the implementation need improvement.
* The scalability claims require further validation on larger datasets.
* The use of "nonparametric" terminology is potentially misleading.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian hierarchical community detection. The proposed method offers a compelling combination of theoretical soundness, computational efficiency, and practical performance. However, the lack of comprehensive comparative evaluation and the areas for improvement in clarity and detail prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **conditional acceptance** pending significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically: 

1.  **Include comparisons to state-of-the-art hierarchical community detection methods** (e.g., Ho et al., Roy & Teh).
2.  **Expand the explanations in Section 3,** providing a more detailed walkthrough of the likelihood calculation.
3.  **Provide more details on the dynamic programming approach and sufficient statistic updates.**
4.  **Evaluate the method on larger datasets** to further demonstrate its scalability.
