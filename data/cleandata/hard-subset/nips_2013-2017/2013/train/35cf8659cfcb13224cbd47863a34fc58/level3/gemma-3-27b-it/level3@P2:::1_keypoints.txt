- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for hierarchical community detection in networks.  
- Represents hierarchy as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with greedy agglomerative inference.  
- Builds on and compares to prior work (IRM, mixed-membership SBMs, hierarchical clustering, Mondrian process, Bayesian rose trees).  
- Addresses computational bottlenecks of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods and offers significant speed-ups.  
- Introduces a caching scheme achieving O(n^2 log n) time complexity for inference.  
- Demonstrates efficacy on Sampson’s monastery, NIPS-234, and the full NIPS co-authorship network.  
- Qualitative results (Sampson) are insightful and quantitative results (NIPS-234) show comparable predictive performance to IRM with much lower cost.  
- Full NIPS analysis provides evidence BHCD uncovers meaningful hierarchical structure at scale.  
- Technical contribution is sound and a natural/principled extension of existing approaches.  
- Greedy agglomerative inference procedure is well-designed, efficient, and accessible in exposition.  
- Writing is exceptionally clear and paper is well-organized.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and suggest future work directions.  
- Concern: O(n^2 log n) complexity may still be prohibitive for very large networks (tens of thousands of nodes).  
- Request for reported timings on the full NIPS dataset to better assess practical scalability.  
- Sparse-version partially addresses scalability, but needs more detailed performance analysis on sparse networks.  
- Weakness: lack of theoretical guarantees on how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree; analysis (e.g., submodularity) would help.  
- Minor stylistic and phrasing issues (e.g., “whilst... but”, “pair of trees”, “A fruitful next steps”).  
- Arguments for acceptance: computational improvement, strong theoretical foundation, convincing experiments, clear exposition, important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: remaining scalability concerns, lack of greedy-optimality guarantees, minor writing issues.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution; recommended for acceptance despite minor weaknesses.
