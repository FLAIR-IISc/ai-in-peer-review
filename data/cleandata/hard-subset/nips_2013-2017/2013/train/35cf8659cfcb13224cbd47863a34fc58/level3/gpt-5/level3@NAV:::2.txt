This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric extension of the stochastic block model that infers hierarchies rather than flat partitions. The key idea is to restrict the partition space to those consistent with a tree, yielding a tractable dynamic-programming computation of the marginal likelihood for a given tree via a recursion over internal nodes. Learning proceeds via a single-pass, greedy agglomerative model-selection procedure that considers join/absorb merges scored by local Bayes factors. Empirically, on two social connectivity corpora, BHCD attains comparable or slightly better AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while being orders of magnitude faster. The work relates to IRM and its hierarchical extensions, Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees, and agglomerative community detection methods.

Quality: The modeling of tree-consistent partitions and the efficient marginal likelihood recursion are technically sound and well-motivated; the computational advantage over the IRM’s exponential partition space is clear. However, Section 4 needs to clarify the relationship between the greedy tree-building procedure and the Bayesian generative model: Is the algorithm maximizing the marginal likelihood over trees (model selection/MAP over T), or is it intended as an approximate posterior inference scheme? The locality of the Bayes factor (Eq. 12) suggests a heuristic objective; please articulate the global objective being optimized, any monotonicity guarantees, and whether the merge prior over tree topologies is encoded. Report sensitivity to γ and Beta hyperparameters, and justify tie-breaking/restarts and their impact on variance.

Clarity: The paper would benefit from substantial editing. There are multiple grammar/spelling errors, vague or overloaded notation, unclear dataset descriptions (splits, preprocessing, sparsity), and several figures are hard to parse. Streamline ancillary math, enlarge and label figures, and expand Section 4 with a step-by-step derivation of the score and its connection to the model.

Originality: While building on known components (SBM, Bayesian rose trees), the combination for relational data with an efficient tree-restricted marginal likelihood is novel and useful.

Significance: Strong potential impact: fast, interpretable hierarchical structure discovery for networks is valuable to social scientists and ML practitioners.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear technical insight enabling efficient marginal likelihood on trees
- Strong speedups with comparable/better predictive performance
- Practical hierarchical outputs with simple prediction

Cons for acceptance:
- Conceptual gap between model and greedy learning (Section 4)
- Clarity/presentation issues hinder reproducibility
- Limited baselines beyond IRM; ablations and hyperparameter sensitivity missing

Overall: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying Section 4 and improving writing and presentation.
