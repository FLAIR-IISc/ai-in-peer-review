

The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for learning hierarchical community structure in social networks. Building upon Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT) [14] and extending the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5,6], BHCD models hierarchical communities as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where each internal node represents a community and leaves correspond to vertices. The key innovation lies in its efficient greedy agglomerative inference algorithm, which avoids the costly iterative procedures of prior Bayesian hierarchical methods (e.g., [8,9,10]) by leveraging dynamic programming and sufficient statistic caching. This yields O(n² log n) worst-case time complexity—quadratic in vertices but independent of community count—making it two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while matching its predictive accuracy on NIPS-234 co-authorship data.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical contribution is sound. The paper rigorously derives the marginal likelihood for tree-consistent partitions (Eq. 6–10) and provides a clear algorithm (Algorithm 1) with complexity analysis. Empirical validation is thorough: qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery (Fig. 3) align with established community structure, while quantitative results on NIPS-234 (Fig. 4) demonstrate BHCD’s speed-accuracy trade-off versus IRM and LFRM [18]. The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) further optimizes for real-world sparse networks.  
*Clarity*: The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-structured sections explaining the model (Sec. 3), inference (Sec. 4), and experiments (Sec. 5). Figures 1–5 effectively illustrate the tree-structured partitions, algorithmic steps, and results. Minor wording issues (e.g., "whilst... but" on p.4) are easily fixable.  
*Originality*: While BHCD adapts BRT ideas to relational data, the non-trivial adaptation—ensuring computational efficiency for hierarchical community detection without sacrificing probabilistic modeling—distinguishes it from prior work. It avoids the limitations of depth-constrained [9,10] or tree-augmented [8] approaches, and unlike greedy hierarchical clustering [3,4], it delivers a full probabilistic model.  
*Significance*: Scalable hierarchical community detection is a critical gap in network analysis. BHCD’s speed advantage (e.g., 10s vs. 1000s of seconds for NIPS-234) makes it practical for large networks, advancing the state-of-the-art in Bayesian nonparametric network modeling. The method’s applicability to co-authorship networks (Sec. 5.3) demonstrates tangible utility.

**Weaknesses**  
*Scalability*: The O(n² log n) complexity, while theoretically efficient, may still be prohibitive for very large networks (e.g., social graphs with >10⁴ nodes). The paper reports timings for NIPS-234 (234 vertices) but omits runtime for the full NIPS dataset (2864 vertices, Fig. 5). The reviewer requests explicit timing comparisons: *How long does BHCD take on full NIPS versus NIPS-234?* This is critical for assessing real-world viability.  
*Theoretical Guarantees*: The greedy selection lacks theoretical justification (e.g., submodularity of the scoring function). While the paper acknowledges that tie-breaking may yield different trees (Sec. 4.1), it does not evaluate how close BHCD’s solution is to the optimal tree (e.g., via exhaustive search on small networks). A brief analysis of approximation quality would strengthen the argument.  
*Sparseness*: The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) is promising for large networks, but the paper does not discuss how its O(n² log n) worst-case complexity compares to the sparse graph’s edge count (m ≪ n²). For gene networks with m = O(n), the practical scaling remains unclear.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- First scalable Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection with probabilistic guarantees.  
- Clear, well-motivated adaptation of BRT to relational data.  
- Strong empirical results showing 100× speedup without sacrificing accuracy.  
- Well-written with excellent organization and figures.  

*Con*:  
- Limited scalability analysis for very large networks (e.g., >10⁴ nodes).  
- Missing theoretical support for greedy approximation quality.  
- Sparse variant’s practical runtime on massive sparse graphs unquantified.  

**Conclusion**  
BHCD makes a significant contribution to scalable Bayesian network modeling. While the scalability discussion could be strengthened with runtime data on larger networks (e.g., full NIPS) and a brief theoretical analysis of the greedy approach, the paper’s technical soundness, clarity, and empirical impact are compelling. The minor writing issues are trivial to address. This work advances the state-of-the-art in hierarchical community detection and deserves acceptance at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability and theoretical points.
