This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in networks, leveraging Bayesian rose trees and a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The approach aims to efficiently learn a probabilistic, hierarchical community model through greedy agglomerative model selection.

The work builds upon prior research in Bayesian nonparametric modeling of networks, particularly the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), addressing its computational limitations. It’s positioned within a rich landscape of hierarchical network models like the multiscale community blockmodel and the Mondrian process. The authors correctly identify the computational bottlenecks in existing Bayesian approaches and focus on efficient likelihood computation (claimed to be O(nodes + edges)).

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The core idea of adapting Bayesian rose trees for hierarchical community detection is logically sound and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of relevant prior work.
* **Efficiency Focus:** The emphasis on computational efficiency is a significant strength. Achieving (or even approaching) O(nodes + edges) likelihood computation is crucial for scaling to larger networks.  The caching scheme described appears well-reasoned.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide evidence of improved speed compared to the IRM, and qualitative success on Sampson’s monastery network is encouraging. The benefit of averaging over hypotheses (multiple restarts) is also demonstrated.
* **Integration of Prior Work:** The paper effectively connects to and builds upon existing literature in Bayesian nonparametric modeling and hierarchical clustering.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Technical Details:** Section 3, particularly around equation (9) and subsequent derivations, is dense and difficult to follow. The connection between the tree structure, the likelihood, and the sufficient statistics is not presented with sufficient clarity.
* **Insufficient Explanation of Dynamics:** The dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates are inadequately explained. More detailed walkthroughs or pseudocode examples would significantly improve understanding.  The leap from equations to the algorithm is too large.
* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** A major weakness is the lack of comparison to existing hierarchical network models. The authors fail to compare against closely related work such as the multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al. 2012) or the Mondrian process (Roy et al. 2009). This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and relative performance of BHCD.
* **Questionable “Nonparametric” Terminology:** The framing of rose trees as model selection, rather than a truly nonparametric process, raises concerns about the use of the term “nonparametric.” A clearer justification for this terminology is needed.
* **Scalability Concerns:** While scalability claims are made, the tested networks are not exceptionally large. Empirical demonstration of scalability on significantly larger datasets is lacking. The O(n^2 log n) complexity, while potentially better than alternatives, still limits application to very large graphs.
* **Limited Originality:** The originality of the work is somewhat limited. It primarily adapts existing techniques (rose trees, stochastic blockmodels) to a new application. The core innovation lies in the efficient implementation rather than a fundamentally novel modeling approach.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are supported by experimental results, although the scope of those results is limited. It feels like a complete piece of work regarding the implemented algorithm and its demonstration on the chosen datasets, but incomplete in its comparative evaluation. The authors are reasonably careful in presenting their results, acknowledging limitations implicitly through the lack of comparison.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written, but as noted above, Section 3 requires significant improvement. The organization is logical, but the lack of detailed explanation for key steps hinders understanding. Reproducibility is potentially hampered by this lack of detail.

**Originality:** The originality is moderate. The combination of rose trees and stochastic blockmodels is not entirely novel, and the primary contribution is the efficient implementation.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant, particularly if the claimed efficiency gains hold up on larger datasets. The method could be valuable for researchers and practitioners dealing with network data where computational cost is a major constraint. However, the significance is diminished by the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art hierarchical models.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in network analysis.
* Offers a computationally efficient solution compared to existing Bayesian methods.
* Demonstrates promising results on benchmark datasets.
* Well-written and generally clear (except Section 3).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of comparative evaluation against relevant hierarchical network models.
* Insufficient clarity in the technical details (Section 3).
* Questionable use of "nonparametric" terminology.
* Limited demonstration of scalability on very large networks.
* Moderate originality.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents a valuable contribution in terms of efficiency, but the lack of comparative evaluation and clarity in the technical details are significant weaknesses. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact. Specifically, the authors *must* compare to existing hierarchical models, provide a more detailed explanation of the dynamic programming aspects, and clarify the use of the term “nonparametric.”  Further experiments on larger networks would also be beneficial.
