- Proposes BHCD: a Bayesian tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels with an efficient agglomerative model selection algorithm claiming IRM-like performance but much faster.  
- Leverages Bayesian hierarchical clustering and caching of sufficient statistics to improve computational efficiency.  
- Claims asymptotic complexity O(n^2 log n) and reports empirical speedups over the IRM.  
- Builds appropriately on prior work in flat and hierarchical community models and cites relevant literature.  

Strengths:
- The model is theoretically well-founded, combining Bayesian principles with a tree-based SBM likelihood.  
- Caching sufficient statistics and algorithmic design are significant contributions to efficiency.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with a relatively clear algorithm description.  
- Experiments (Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship) show recovery of known structure and competitive predictive performance.  

Weaknesses:
- Section 3 is dense; equation (9) and subsequent derivations lack sufficient step-by-step explanation.  
- Dynamic programming details and recursive updates for sufficient statistics are underspecified, hurting reproducibility.  
- Comparative evaluation is limited—no comparisons to other hierarchical methods like Ho et al. (multiscale blockmodel) or Roy & Teh (Mondrian).  
- The use of "nonparametric" is questionable since the method relies on a pre-defined tree prior and resembles model selection.  
- Scalability claims are under-supported because experiments use relatively small networks.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Technically sound contribution addressing computational costs of Bayesian hierarchical community detection.  
- Promising empirical efficiency and predictive results and appropriate positioning in related work.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art hierarchical baselines weaken claims of novelty and significance.  
- Key mathematical and implementation details need clarification for reproducibility.  
- Scalability needs stronger empirical validation on larger datasets.  
- Terminology (“nonparametric”) may be misleading.  

Overall assessment and required revisions:
- Solid contribution but recommend conditional acceptance pending revisions.  
- Required changes: add comparisons to relevant hierarchical methods (e.g., Ho et al., Roy & Teh); expand Section 3 walkthrough of the likelihood; provide detailed dynamic programming and sufficient-statistic update procedures; evaluate on larger datasets.
