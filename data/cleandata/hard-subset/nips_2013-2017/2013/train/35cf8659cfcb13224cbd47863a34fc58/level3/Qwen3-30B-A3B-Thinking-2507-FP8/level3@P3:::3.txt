

**Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"**

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors extend the stochastic block model (SBM) and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees, which allows for efficient hierarchical community discovery. The key innovation is a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that learns hierarchical community structure in a single pass through the data, replacing the computationally expensive CRP formulation of IRM with a simpler formulation (equation 7).

The paper builds upon previous work in several key areas. It extends the IRM (which was previously used for flat community structure) to handle hierarchical structures, but does so with a much more efficient inference algorithm than previous hierarchical extensions. The authors note that previous Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection either had limited depth (e.g., depth 2 [9,10]) or required expensive inference procedures [8]. The authors also build upon the Bayesian hierarchical clustering work of Heller & Ghahramani [14], but adapt it to relational data and introduce a novel caching scheme for efficient computation.

The main theoretical contribution is the posterior factorization over subtrees, which follows Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004), enabling several computational advantages: greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction. The authors demonstrate that their algorithm, called BHCD (Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery), has a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), where n is the number of vertices, and importantly, this complexity is independent of the number of nested communities. This is a significant improvement over the IRM, which has time complexity O(n²K²) where K is the number of communities.

**Strengths:**
- Well-written paper with excellent spelling, grammar, and correct citations. The main theoretical contributions are clearly explained and well-supported.
- The theory is succinct and consistent in its use of f() and g() functions for on-diagonal and off-diagonal blocks.
- The posterior factorization over subtrees enables several computational advantages, including greedy search and marginal likelihood computation.
- The algorithmic contribution (summarized in Table 1) is well-structured and clearly described.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental evaluation is limited to small-scale datasets (only the NIPS dataset is used, with a subset of 234 nodes and the full dataset of 2864 nodes). The paper should include larger datasets used in recent SBM evaluations.
- The evaluation uses AUC, but the method of computing it is unclear (presumably by deleting links) and needs explicit explanation. NMI (Normalized Mutual Information) is a more standard metric for SBM comparisons.
- The experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, lacking comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics.
- Minor style issue: the paper begins some sentences with citations in NIPS style (e.g., "As shown in [1],..."), which should be avoided.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is complete, and the algorithm is clearly described. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their work (e.g., restricted dataset size).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main ideas are accessible, and the algorithm is well-explained. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by combining Bayesian Rose trees with hierarchical community detection. It extends the SBM and IRM in a meaningful way, and the approach to posterior factorization is novel. The paper properly references related work, including the IRM and previous hierarchical approaches.

**Significance:** The approach offers a significant computational advantage over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, which is valuable for large-scale network analysis. However, the limited experimental scope reduces the perceived significance.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel combination of Bayesian Rose trees with hierarchical community detection
- Significant computational speedup (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM)
- Clear theoretical foundation with well-supported claims
- Well-written with proper citations and organization

*Con:*
- Limited experimental scope (only small NIPS dataset used)
- Lack of comparison to standard SBM metrics like NMI
- Inadequate explanation of evaluation metrics (AUC)
- Missing comparison to recent methods in the field

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that offers a significant computational advantage over existing methods. However, the limited experimental evaluation and lack of comparison to recent methods reduce its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the experimental limitations by including larger datasets and more comprehensive comparisons to recent methods. The authors should clarify the AUC computation method and include NMI as a standard evaluation metric for SBM comparisons.
