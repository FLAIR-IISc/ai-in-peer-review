

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, extending the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) to handle tree-structured hierarchies. The authors introduce a hierarchical mixture of stochastic block models where the partition space is restricted to tree-consistent partitions, enabling efficient marginal likelihood computation through dynamic programming. They develop a greedy agglomerative learning algorithm that constructs a hierarchical community structure in O(n² log n) time, significantly faster than IRM's exponential partition space.

The paper builds upon the IRM [5-7] and Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14], addressing a key limitation of previous Bayesian hierarchical approaches [8-11] that either required known hierarchy depth or incurred high computational costs. By restricting partitions to tree structures, the authors enable exact marginal likelihood computation while maintaining the Bayesian framework's probabilistic interpretability.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The technical contribution is sound, with clear algorithmic development and theoretical justification for the computational efficiency. The empirical evaluation on two social network datasets demonstrates significant speed advantages (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) with comparable or better predictive accuracy (AUC, log-likelihood, and accuracy). The theoretical analysis of the marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming is rigorous and well-motivated.

*Originality:* The paper makes a novel contribution by restricting the partition space to tree-structured ones, which is a clever way to maintain Bayesian nonparametric flexibility while enabling efficient computation. This represents a significant departure from previous hierarchical Bayesian models that either used approximate inference or restricted to known depths.

*Significance:* The work addresses a relevant problem for social scientists who often seek hierarchical network structures rather than flat clusters. The runtime improvements make hierarchical community discovery feasible for larger networks, potentially enabling new applications in social network analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*Clarity:* The writing quality needs substantial improvement. The paper contains several spelling/grammar errors (e.g., "naı̈ve" on page 10), vague phrasing ("the generative process" without sufficient detail), and unclear dataset descriptions (e.g., Sampson's monastery network). Some figures are confusing (e.g., Figure 3 lacks clear labeling of the four groups found by previous work). The mathematical notation is sometimes inconsistent (e.g., σ¬chSS appears without clear definition).

*Quality:* The most critical weakness is the conceptual confusion regarding Section 4. The paper claims to perform Bayesian inference but describes a greedy agglomerative procedure that does not explicitly follow from the Bayesian generative model. It is unclear whether the algorithm performs valid posterior inference, finds a MAP estimate, or computes a marginal likelihood. This fundamental ambiguity undermines the Bayesian claim of the paper. The authors should clarify whether the greedy procedure is computing the marginal likelihood (as implied by the Bayes factor score) or approximating the posterior.

*Clarity:* Section 4 requires a much clearer explanation of how the greedy tree-learning procedure relates to the Bayesian model. The connection between the generative process in Section 3 and the agglomerative algorithm in Section 4 is not adequately established. The paper should explicitly state whether the algorithm computes the marginal likelihood of the tree (which would be Bayesian) or finds the maximum a posteriori tree (which would be MAP).

**Recommendation:**

This paper has strong technical content and compelling empirical results that address a meaningful problem in social network analysis. If the authors address the clarity issues—particularly by clarifying the relationship between the greedy algorithm and the Bayesian model—the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.

The writing needs significant polishing to fix grammatical errors, clarify vague statements, and improve figure quality. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of Section 4 to resolve the conceptual confusion about whether the method performs Bayesian inference or finds a MAP estimate. They should also clarify the dataset descriptions and fix the mathematical notation inconsistencies.

The results are sufficiently strong: comparable or better accuracy with orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements. With the proposed revisions, this paper would have sufficient novelty and strong results to merit publication at NIPS.

**Summary:** The paper presents a technically sound and empirically strong approach to hierarchical community discovery with significant computational advantages over existing Bayesian methods. The core algorithm and experiments are insightful, but the writing quality and conceptual clarity in Section 4 require substantial improvement. If these issues are addressed, the paper would be a strong candidate for publication.
