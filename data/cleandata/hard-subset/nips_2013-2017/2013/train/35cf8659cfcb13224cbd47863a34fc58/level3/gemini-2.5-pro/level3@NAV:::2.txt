This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric model for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The work builds upon the tradition of Bayesian nonparametric relational models, most notably the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), but distinguishes itself by focusing on discovering nested, hierarchical clusters rather than a flat partition. The core technical insight is to restrict the enormous space of possible network partitions to only those consistent with a tree structure. This constraint allows for the exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood for a given tree, a significant departure from the approximate MCMC or variational methods typically required for models like the IRM.

The primary strength of this paper is its successful combination of a principled Bayesian approach with a highly efficient inference algorithm. By framing learning as a greedy agglomerative clustering procedure that merges sub-trees to maximize a local Bayes factor, the authors achieve a significant practical breakthrough. The experimental results are strong, showing that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than the IRM while achieving comparable or even slightly better predictive accuracy, AUC, and log-likelihood on two social network datasets. This directly addresses a critical challenge in the field: scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods to larger networks, making the contribution highly significant.

However, the paper has two main weaknesses. The most critical is a conceptual ambiguity regarding the learning algorithm. While the paper first defines a coherent generative model (Section 3), the greedy agglomerative procedure (Section 4) seems disconnected from it. It is unclear whether this algorithm is performing valid posterior inference over trees, finding a maximum a posteriori (MAP) or maximum likelihood estimate, or is simply a heuristic inspired by the model. A clearer explanation of the relationship between the generative process and the bottom-up inference is needed to establish the full technical soundness of the approach. Secondly, the paper's clarity needs substantial improvement. The manuscript suffers from spelling and grammatical errors, vague phrasing, and figures that are difficult to interpret. The dataset descriptions lack sufficient detail for full reproducibility.

In summary, this work presents an elegant and practically important method for hierarchical network analysis. The core algorithm and empirical results are insightful and address a relevant problem. The paper's novelty and strong performance warrant publication, but acceptance should be conditioned on a major revision to improve the writing quality and, most importantly, to clarify the theoretical connection between the proposed generative model and the inference algorithm.
