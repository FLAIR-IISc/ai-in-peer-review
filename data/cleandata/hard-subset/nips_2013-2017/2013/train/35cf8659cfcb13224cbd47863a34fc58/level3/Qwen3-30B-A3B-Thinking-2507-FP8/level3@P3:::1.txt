

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach to discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors propose a hierarchical extension of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) using Bayesian rose tree (BRT) ideas. The key innovation is a greedy agglomerative procedure that learns the hierarchical structure by merging communities in a bottom-up fashion, while maintaining computational efficiency. The model represents community structure as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where the tree defines a mixture over consistent partitions. Unlike previous hierarchical Bayesian approaches, BHCD's inference scales quadratically with the number of vertices (O(n² log n)) but independently of the number of communities, making it significantly more efficient than existing methods.

The paper builds upon the Bayesian rose tree framework [14] but requires nontrivial adaptation for relational data while maintaining computational complexity. The authors demonstrate their approach on Sampson's monks network (qualitative results), NIPS-234 (quantitative results), and the full NIPS co-authorship network. Their experiments show that BHCD achieves comparable or better predictive performance than IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster—making it feasible for larger networks where traditional Bayesian approaches become prohibitively expensive.

The paper excels in clarity and exposition. The key ideas are well-motivated, with clear explanations of how the tree structure defines consistent partitions, the marginal likelihood computation, and the greedy model selection algorithm. The algorithm description is precise and the theoretical foundation is solid. The authors effectively connect their work to prior literature, distinguishing their approach from flat community models, hierarchical clustering methods, and previous Bayesian hierarchical approaches [8-12].

Strengths of the paper include:
1. Computational efficiency: BHCD's O(n² log n) complexity is superior to IRM's O(n²K²) complexity (where K is the number of communities) and other Bayesian nonparametric methods, as demonstrated in Table 1.
2. Strong empirical results: BHCD achieves similar predictive performance to IRM on NIPS-234 while being much faster.
3. Practical implementation: The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) works particularly well on sparse networks like the NIPS co-authorship network.
4. Clear exposition: The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex concepts accessible to readers.

The main weaknesses and concerns include:
1. Scalability to very large networks: While the paper acknowledges the O(n²) complexity may be prohibitive for extremely large networks (tens of thousands of nodes), it would be valuable to see timing comparisons between NIPS-234 (234 vertices) and the full NIPS network (2864 vertices) to understand how runtime scales in practice.
2. Theoretical guarantees: The paper would benefit from exploring how close the greedy approach gets to the optimal tree. The authors should consider evaluating this via exhaustive search at the largest feasible scale and investigating theoretical support (e.g., submodularity).
3. Minor issues: There are a few wording issues (e.g., "whilst... but" on line 48), "pair of trees" should be "pair of trees" (plural), and "A fruitful next steps" should be "A fruitful next step."

The authors make a compelling case for the significance of their work. While the core ideas draw from Bayesian rose tree literature, the adaptation to relational data represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The ability to discover hierarchical community structure efficiently is valuable for analyzing large-scale social networks, and the paper's focus on scalability addresses an important gap in current Bayesian nonparametric network models.

The paper's contribution is significant despite the familiarity with BRT concepts. The authors have successfully adapted these ideas to relational data while maintaining computational efficiency—a nontrivial achievement that advances the state of the art in community detection. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of network analysis. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented. While the authors should address the scalability concerns through additional experiments and theoretical exploration, these are minor issues that do not detract from the paper's overall strength.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in hierarchical community detection by providing an efficient, scalable method that achieves comparable performance to existing approaches while being significantly faster. The clear writing, solid theoretical foundation, and compelling experimental results make this a strong candidate for NIPS.
