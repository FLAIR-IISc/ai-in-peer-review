This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical structure in networks. The core idea is to extend the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by defining a prior over partitions using a tree structure, drawing inspiration from recent work on Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT). This tree-based formulation defines a mixture over a constrained set of consistent partitions, allowing for exact and efficient calculation of the marginal likelihood. Inference is performed via a greedy agglomerative procedure that builds the hierarchy bottom-up, which is a departure from the more computationally intensive MCMC methods typically used for IRM.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its significant improvement in computational efficiency for a challenging modeling problem. Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models for network data is a critical research area, and this paper makes a valuable contribution. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than a standard IRM implementation while achieving comparable or better predictive accuracy on the NIPS-234 benchmark. The originality of the work is solid; while the main components are inspired by IRM and BRT, their adaptation to relational data in a way that preserves computational efficiency is a non-trivial and successful effort. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and logical exposition that makes the model and inference algorithm easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The main concern is the ultimate scalability of the proposed method. The O(n^2 log n) complexity of the greedy algorithm, while a major advance over alternatives, may still be prohibitive for the very large networks common in social or biological domains (tens of thousands of nodes or more). The authors should discuss this limitation and the potential of the sparse variant more thoroughly. To that end, the experimental section would be strengthened by providing the run time for the full NIPS dataset to directly compare against the NIPS-234 results. This would provide a clearer picture of the method's practical scaling properties.

Additionally, the greedy nature of the inference procedure raises questions about the quality of the learned hierarchy. It would be beneficial to include an evaluation of how close the greedy method gets to an optimal tree, perhaps via an exhaustive search on a small, tractable network. Alternatively, exploring theoretical justifications for the greedy approach, such as potential submodularity properties, could provide stronger guarantees.

**Overall Assessment:**
Despite the reliance on familiar ideas from BRT, the contribution here is significant. The paper presents a novel and practical algorithm that makes hierarchical Bayesian community detection far more accessible. The weaknesses identified are primarily avenues for future research rather than fundamental flaws. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem. The paper should be accepted.
