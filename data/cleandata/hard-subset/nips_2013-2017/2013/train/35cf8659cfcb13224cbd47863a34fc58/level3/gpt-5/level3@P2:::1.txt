Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model that discovers hierarchical community structure in networks by representing a tree-structured mixture over stochastic blockmodels. Given a tree T (a “Bayesian rose tree”), the model defines a prior over partitions consistent with T and an exact marginal likelihood via a dynamic program that exploits on-diagonal and off-diagonal conjugate block marginals (Beta–Bernoulli in the main instantiation). Learning proceeds via a greedy agglomerative procedure that considers three local merge types (join and two absorptions) scored by Bayes factors computed from local sufficient statistics; careful caching yields O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time. A sparse variant disallows merging disconnected subtrees.

The work builds on stochastic blockmodels and mixed-membership variants (Holland et al.; Airoldi et al.), the IRM (Kemp et al.) and its hierarchical extensions, Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.), and the Mondrian process for tree priors. Unlike most hierarchical IRM-style approaches that require expensive MCMC/variational inference, BHCD uses a greedy model-selection view with exact marginal likelihood for a fixed tree and a fast agglomerative search. Experiments include qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery data, predictive comparisons on NIPS-234 against IRM (with MCMC and greedy), and a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS co-authorship graph.

Quality:
- Modeling: The hierarchical mixture over tree-consistent partitions is well defined, with a clear generative story and conjugate Beta–Bernoulli treatment that allows closed-form marginal likelihood. The recursion (Eq. 10) is correct given the construction, and the predictive recursion is straightforward.
- Inference: The agglomerative search and local Bayes-factor scoring are technically sound and efficiently implemented via cached sufficient statistics. The sparse variant addresses an undesirable edge case (merging disconnected subtrees).
- Empirics: On NIPS-234, BHCD reaches predictive performance comparable to IRM orders of magnitude faster; on Sampson, the inferred structure aligns with known groups. The full NIPS demonstration shows interpretability at scale (2.8k nodes).
- Limitations: Hyperparameters are fixed; there is no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference. Comparisons are strongest vs IRM; quantitative comparisons to other scalable nonparametric block/factor models are indirect. For very large graphs, O(n^2 log n) time and O(n^2) memory for pairwise statistics may be prohibitive; concrete wall-clock timings on larger graphs would clarify practical scalability. The greedy search has no optimality guarantees; an analysis of optimality gaps on small graphs would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, technically careful, and well organized. The model description, dynamic program, and inference procedure are presented with helpful notation and figures. The experiments are easy to follow, and the discussion is candid about trade-offs. Minor editorial issues:
- “In the worst case, Our algorithms scale …” → lowercase “our.”
- “run time of our algorithms are” → “is.”
- “pair of trees” (consistent phrasing).
- “A fruitful next steps” → “Fruitful next steps.”
- “wherem n2” → “where m ≪ n^2.”
- Reference typos (e.g., “Erix” → “Eric”), stray periods.
- Occasional “whilst … but” constructions could be simplified.

Originality:
While the hierarchical prior and dynamic program are inspired by Bayesian rose trees, the adaptation to relational data with blockmodels is nontrivial: the mixture over tree-consistent partitions, the separation of on/off-diagonal marginals with different conjugate priors, the σ¬ch sufficient statistic, and the efficient local Bayes-factor search are novel in combination. Relative to hierarchical IRM and Mondrian-process models, BHCD trades modeling generality for major computational gains.

Significance:
Scalable Bayesian hierarchical community discovery is important for social and biological networks. BHCD offers a practical point on the accuracy–speed frontier, achieving competitive link prediction much faster than IRM and yielding interpretable hierarchies. The method is likely to be adopted as a fast baseline and as a tool for exploratory analysis. That said, for very large graphs (tens of thousands of nodes), the quadratic worst-case cost and memory footprint may limit use without additional sparsity-aware engineering.

Questions/suggestions:
- Scalability: Please report wall-clock time and memory for full NIPS versus NIPS-234 (and, if possible, a larger public graph), and characterize scaling with n and m. What are practical limits on n with the current C implementation?
- Memory: What is the asymptotic and practical memory usage for cached σIJ and σ¬ch? Can you prune candidate merges or use neighbor-restricted heaps to reduce O(n^2) storage?
- Hyperparameters: Provide sensitivity analysis for α, β, δ, λ, γ; consider simple empirical-Bayes updates or sampling.
- Optimality: On small graphs where exhaustive search over trees is feasible, quantify the gap between BHCD and the optimal tree. Any conditions (e.g., submodularity-like properties) under which greedy is near-optimal?
- Baselines: Consider adding a fast non-Bayesian hierarchical community method (e.g., modularity-based hierarchical clustering or Louvain dendrograms) for runtime/quality context. A comparison to a Mondrian-process-based relational model would also be informative.
- Inference details: Report number of restarts R and its effect on runtime/predictive performance; clarify tie-breaking; provide pseudocode for Algorithm 1 (even if deferred to appendix). Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, principled model connecting hierarchical trees to mixtures over block partitions with exact marginals.
- Elegant, efficient agglomerative inference with careful engineering and solid empirical speedups.
- Meaningful predictive performance and interpretable hierarchies on real data.
- Well written and easy to understand.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Greedy search lacks theoretical guarantees; no optimality/ablation study on small graphs.
- Limited breadth of quantitative baselines beyond IRM; no sensitivity analysis.
- Quadratic worst-case runtime and memory may hinder very large-scale use; scalability characterization could be stronger.

Verdict:
Accept. Despite leveraging known ideas from Bayesian rose trees, the adaptation to relational data and the resulting practical, fast algorithm are a valuable contribution. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem with convincing empirical evidence. With the suggested clarifications and additional results on scalability and sensitivity, it will make a strong NIPS paper.
