### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for identifying hierarchical community structures in networks. The work builds upon the stochastic blockmodel (SBM) and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The key innovation is replacing the Chinese Restaurant Process prior on partitions, common in models like the IRM, with a tree-structured prior derived from Bayesian Rose Trees. This modeling choice constrains the space of partitions to those consistent with a hierarchy, which in turn allows for the exact computation of the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree via a simple dynamic programming recursion.

Leveraging this property, the authors propose a greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm for model selection, i.e., to learn the tree structure itself. This algorithm is highly efficient, with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), which is notably independent of the number of communities. The paper demonstrates the method's performance on Sampson's monastery network for qualitative analysis and compares its speed and predictive accuracy on a subset of the NIPS co-authorship dataset against the IRM. The results show that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the marginal likelihood under the tree-structured prior (Eq. 10) and the subsequent development of the greedy agglomerative search algorithm are well-founded. The factorization of the posterior over subtrees, which enables the efficient local computation of merge scores, is a key component that follows from the model's structure. The connection of this type of factorization to earlier work on exact inference (e.g., Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004) could be made more explicit to better contextualize the theoretical basis.

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the experiments effectively illustrate the model's behavior and its impressive speed, they are not comprehensive enough to be considered a rigorous comparative study.
-   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is restricted to the IRM. While this is a relevant baseline, the field of community detection is broad. The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by comparisons to other scalable methods, including semi-parametric approaches or fast non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004).
-   **Limited Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale networks. The largest network analyzed, the full NIPS dataset, is used for a qualitative illustration rather than a quantitative comparison. To convincingly demonstrate the method's utility, it should be benchmarked on some of the larger, standard network datasets used in recent SBM and community detection literature.
-   **Evaluation Metrics:** For the link prediction task, the paper reports AUC but does not detail how it was computed (e.g., how the held-out set of links and non-links was constructed). Furthermore, for evaluating the discovered community structure itself, a metric like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) against ground truth (where available) or against the partitions of other methods is standard practice and is missing here.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written, with clear prose and a logical organization. The theoretical concepts are presented succinctly, for instance, through the elegant use of generic functions f(·) and g(·) for the marginal likelihood of on- and off-diagonal blocks. The algorithm is described clearly and supplemented with a helpful pseudocode block.

A minor stylistic point: in several places, sentences begin with citations (e.g., "[9] and [10] propose..."). Following NIPS style guidelines, these should be rephrased (e.g., "The methods proposed in [9, 10]...").

**Originality:**
The work presents a novel and clever synthesis of existing techniques: stochastic blockmodels, the IRM, and Bayesian Rose Trees. The idea of using a tree to define a prior on partitions is not entirely new, but its specific application here to create an efficient, greedy inference scheme for hierarchical SBMs is original. This approach successfully trades the flexibility of a full CRP-based model for a significant gain in computational efficiency, which is a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**
The main significance of this work lies in its practicality and efficiency. The proposed BHCD algorithm offers a fast, fully Bayesian method for exploring hierarchical structures in networks. The O(n² log n) complexity and the demonstration of a two-order-of-magnitude speed-up over a standard Gibbs sampler for the IRM make this a potentially valuable tool for practitioners who need to analyze medium-to-large networks quickly. The ability to produce a full probabilistic model, rather than just a point estimate of the hierarchy, is another significant advantage over many purely algorithmic clustering methods. If the performance holds up on larger datasets and against a wider range of methods, this approach could be widely adopted.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and elegant combination of Bayesian nonparametrics (Rose Trees) and relational modeling (SBMs).
*   The resulting greedy agglomerative algorithm is highly efficient and scalable, representing a significant practical contribution for network analysis.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The model provides a full probabilistic treatment of hierarchical community structure, which is a significant advantage over many ad-hoc methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation is the paper's main weakness. It is more illustrative than comparative, lacking comparisons to a broader set of state-of-the-art methods and evaluations on larger, standard benchmark datasets.
*   The choice of evaluation metrics is not fully justified, and standard metrics for community detection (like NMI) are absent.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel modeling idea and a significant contribution in terms of computational efficiency. The clarity of the presentation is excellent. However, the work is let down by an inadequate experimental evaluation that fails to rigorously situate the method's performance within the wider context of community detection literature. Despite this serious reservation, the novelty of the approach and its computational advantages are compelling. I lean towards acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to strengthen the experimental section for the final version by including more diverse and larger datasets, a broader set of baseline methods, and more standard evaluation metrics.
