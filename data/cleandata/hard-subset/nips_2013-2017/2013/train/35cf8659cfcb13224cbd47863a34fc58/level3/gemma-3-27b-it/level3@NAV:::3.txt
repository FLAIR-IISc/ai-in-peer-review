## Review of “Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery”

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The authors extend stochastic block models (SBMs) and the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees, offering a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods. A key contribution is a greedy agglomerative algorithm (summarized in Table 1) leveraging factorization techniques from Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004) to enable efficient inference. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundation of the proposed model. The notation is consistent (use of f() and g()) aiding readability. It successfully builds upon previous work in Bayesian nonparametrics and hierarchical clustering, addressing a significant limitation of existing methods – computational cost. Replacing the CRP formulation of IRM with a simpler approach (Eq. 7) is a positive step towards practical applicability. The algorithm's scaling properties are also a valuable contribution, particularly its independence from the number of nested communities. 

However, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the authors demonstrate the method’s speed advantage compared to IRM, the evaluation primarily serves as an illustration rather than a rigorous comparison. The use of AUC is mentioned, but the computation method is unclear and needs detailed explanation. More standard metrics for SBM evaluation, such as Normalized Mutual Information (NMI), would strengthen the results. Furthermore, the experiments are limited to a single, relatively small NIPS dataset. Inclusion of larger datasets commonly used in SBM evaluations (e.g., those found in recent SBM benchmarks) is crucial.  The paper lacks comparison to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics, limiting the understanding of the relative performance of BHCD. The conclusion that BHCD gets "good results quickly" is not sufficiently backed by comparative results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of SBMs and IRM to hierarchical structures.
*   Efficient greedy agglomerative algorithm with favorable scaling.
*   Clear theoretical foundation and well-written exposition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited and insufficiently detailed experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of comparison to a broader range of methods.
*   Small-scale experiments hindering generalizability assessment.



**Overall:** The paper presents a promising theoretical framework and an efficient algorithm. However, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation raises concerns about its practical impact. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after a substantial expansion of the experimental section, including comparisons to a wider range of methods on larger, more representative datasets and a clearer explanation of the AUC calculation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate the true value of the proposed method.
