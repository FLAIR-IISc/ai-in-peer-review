- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD): a tree-structured prior over partitions mixing over SBMs to discover hierarchical communities.  
- Replaces IRM’s CRP prior with a tree-consistent partition prior from Bayesian Rose trees (eq. 7), enabling exact marginalization via subtree factorization.  
- Uses recursion that factorizes over subtrees (following Koivisto & Sood) to support efficient computation and marginalization.  
- Introduces a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm (Algorithm 1) with O(n^2 log n) runtime, independent of number of communities.  
- Provides efficient link prediction via recursive mixture along tree paths.  
- Experiments (Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship) show large speedups over IRM with competitive predictive performance.  
- Technical development is sound; use of f(·) and g(·) cleanly generalizes beyond beta–Bernoulli, and posterior factorization/DP recursion are well motivated.  
- Agglomerative scoring is locally consistent and exploits cached sufficient statistics for speed.  
- Evaluation is under-specified and limited: AUC computation protocol is unclear (how links/negatives are selected and whether non-edges are treated as zeros or missing).  
- Comparisons are narrow, centered only on IRM, lacking stronger baselines (degree-corrected/hierarchical SBMs, spectral methods).  
- Paper is well written with good grammar and citations; minor style note to avoid sentence-initial citations and to describe AUC protocol explicitly.  
- Recommends adding sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters α, β, δ, λ, γ.  
- Originality: novel combination of Rose-tree priors with SBM likelihoods and subtree-factorized marginal enabling single-pass agglomerative search.  
- Significance: attractive speed/accuracy tradeoff that could improve practicality, but impact limited by small datasets and illustrative experiments; suggest larger standard benchmarks and reporting NMI/ARI.  
- Pros: elegant theory with exact marginalization, practical scalable algorithm, and reusable sufficient-statistics/link-prediction framework.  
- Cons: unclear AUC and evaluation metrics, limited datasets/baselines, and no hyperparameter sensitivity or robustness analysis.
