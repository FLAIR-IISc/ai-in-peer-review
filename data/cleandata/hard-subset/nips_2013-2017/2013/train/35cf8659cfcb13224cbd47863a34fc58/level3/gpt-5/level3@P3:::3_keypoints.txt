- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric, tree-structured mixture over SBMs with exact marginal likelihood via DP recursion over subtrees.
- Replaces IRM’s CRP marginalization with a tree-conditioned prior (eq.7), enabling exact and efficient computation of p(D|T).
- Uses Bayesian Rose Trees to place a prior on hierarchical refinements and allow non-binary merges.
- Main algorithmic contribution is a greedy agglomerative search (Algorithm 1) evaluating three merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right) with cached sufficient statistics.
- Posterior factorization over subtrees (eqs.6–10) parallels dynamic-programming approaches and enables efficient search, amortized statistics, and fast link prediction (eq.14).
- Relation to prior work is appropriate, building on SBMs, IRM, HRG, and Bayesian hierarchical clustering while leveraging conjugate blocks for assortative structure.
- Model and recursions are coherent; Beta–Bernoulli blocks with different on/off-diagonal hyperparameters are sensible for assortative communities.
- DP derivation, caching scheme, and link prediction recursion are correct, though some derivation steps are terse.
- A short worked example on a tiny tree would improve reproducibility and clarity of derivations.
- Agglomerative search and local Bayes score updates are well-motivated and complexity claims are credible; memory complexity and cache symmetry constraints need clarification.
- Experiments are illustrative and show strong speedups over IRM (e.g., NIPS-234), but evaluation breadth and metrics are limited.
- Writing, grammar, and citations are good; minor style fixes (avoid starting sentences with citations) and notation consistency issues remain.
- Clarify DTT vs. Dleaves(T), directed vs. undirected conventions, and handling of missing edges during training/prediction.
- Explicitly cite dynamic-programming literature (e.g., Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004) that mirrors the recursion.
- Combining rose-tree priors with SBM likelihoods and Bayes-factor-driven agglomerative search is a novel and meaningful methodological contribution.
- Main significance is scalability: exact marginal likelihood on a fixed hierarchy and O(n^2 log n) search with practical speedups over IRM.
- Claims about community discovery quality are under-supported due to small-scale evaluation and unclear AUC protocols.
- Request detailed AUC protocol: sampling of positives/negatives, link deletion for training, symmetry in scoring, and class imbalance handling (suggest PR-AUC).
- Recommend reporting community quality metrics (NMI/ARI) on synthetic benchmarks (e.g., LFR) or comparisons to flat partitions.
- Suggest adding strong baselines: degree-corrected SBM, hierarchical random graphs, spectral/modularity-based hierarchical clustering.
- Recommend expanding datasets to larger, diverse networks (political blogs/books, Enron, SNAP graphs) to support scalability/accuracy claims.
- Discuss model limitations: current conjugate setup favors assortativity and ignores degree heterogeneity; consider degree-corrected or non-conjugate extensions.
- Report robustness: variability across runs (mean±std of likelihood/AUC) and wall-clock costs per restart.
- Clarify Table 1’s role, provide memory usage, and describe sparsity-optimized data structures for BHCD-sparse.
- Pros: principled model with exact marginal likelihood, efficient DP/caching, practical agglomerative search with speedups, and clear writing.
- Cons: limited/unclear evaluation protocols, small dataset set, missing strong non-BNP baselines, and tailoring to assortative, degree-homogeneous networks.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept pending stronger, clearer empirical evaluation, broader baselines/datasets, and clarified evaluation protocols.
