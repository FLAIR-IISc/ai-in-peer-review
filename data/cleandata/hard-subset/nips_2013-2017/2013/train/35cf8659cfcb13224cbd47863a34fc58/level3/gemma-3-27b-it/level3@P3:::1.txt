This paper proposes a Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method, extending the Bayesian Rose Tree (BRT) framework to relational data for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The core idea is to represent the hierarchy as a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, learned via a greedy agglomerative procedure. The authors demonstrate its efficiency—two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration—while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts. The motivation is well-presented, the mathematical formulation is accessible, and the experimental setup is described thoroughly. It effectively situates the work within the existing literature on community detection, Bayesian nonparametrics, and relational modeling, referencing relevant work (e.g., IRM, stochastic blockmodels, BRTs) appropriately. The connection to prior work on BRTs is clearly articulated, and the authors appropriately highlight the nontrivial adaptations required for relational data and computational complexity.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The model is built on a solid theoretical foundation, combining stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering. The use of dynamic programming for likelihood calculation is a clever optimization.
* **Clarity:** The paper is remarkably clear and easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with all the underlying techniques. The organization is logical, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive.
* **Originality:** While building on existing ideas (BRTs), applying them to hierarchical community *discovery* in networks is a novel contribution. The greedy agglomerative procedure and the resulting computational efficiency are significant advancements.
* **Significance:** The method addresses a practically important problem – discovering hierarchical structure in networks – with a computationally efficient solution. The speedup over IRM is substantial, opening up the possibility of applying this approach to larger networks. The focus on predictive performance is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** Although the paper states O(n^2 log n) complexity, and demonstrates speedups on NIPS-234, it doesn’t fully address scalability to truly large networks (tens of thousands of nodes). The authors acknowledge this and propose potential avenues for improvement (triangle representations, sparse implementations).
* **Greedy Method Optimality:** The paper doesn’t provide any theoretical guarantees about the quality of the solution found by the greedy algorithm. An evaluation of how closely the greedy method approximates the optimal tree (e.g., via exhaustive search on smaller instances) would be valuable. Exploring theoretical properties like submodularity could also strengthen the work.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention parameter settings used, a sensitivity analysis examining the impact of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) on the results would be beneficial.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-explained solution to an important problem.
* The computational efficiency gain over existing Bayesian methods is substantial.
* The results on both qualitative (Sampson's monks) and quantitative (NIPS-234) datasets are promising.
* The writing is excellent, making the paper accessible and impactful.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* Scalability to very large networks remains a concern, despite the authors' acknowledgement.
* Lack of theoretical justification for the greedy algorithm’s optimality.

**Specific Comments/Minor Corrections:**

* Line 48: “whilst… but” should be revised for clarity (e.g., "while… and").
* “pair of trees” – slightly awkward phrasing; consider “two trees”.
* “A fruitful next steps” – should be "A fruitful next step."

**Response to Reviewer Questions:**

The reviewer requests timings on larger real-world networks. The paper provides timings for NIPS-234, but doesn’t compare it to a full NIPS run. Addressing this directly—providing the runtime for the full NIPS dataset—would be valuable. The reviewer also raises valid concerns regarding scalability to tens of thousands of nodes and the optimality of the greedy method. The authors acknowledge these concerns and hint at possible solutions, but a more detailed discussion (and ideally, some preliminary investigation) would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Verdict:**

Despite my familiarity with the Bayesian Rose Tree ideas, the adaptation to relational data and the resulting algorithmic efficiency are significant contributions. The paper is exceptionally well-written and makes a compelling case for the proposed method. While some questions remain regarding scalability and the optimality of the greedy algorithm, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
