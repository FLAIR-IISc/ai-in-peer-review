- Paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model adapting Bayesian rose trees to relational data via blockmodel likelihoods and on/off-diagonal marginals.  
- Prior: node-specific stopping probabilities πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| encourage arity; exact tree-marginal likelihood computed by dynamic programming in O(n + m).  
- Inference: greedy agglomerative model selection using local Bayes factors with three merge operators (join + two absorptions); sparse variant forbids merging disconnected components.  
- Related work: builds on Bayesian rose trees and the IRM; offers much cheaper inference than IRM variants (single-pass greedy merges, O(n^2 log n) worst case).  
- Experiments: qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery and full NIPS co-authorship graph; quantitative comparison on NIPS-234 shows IRM-level predictive performance at orders-of-magnitude faster runtime.  

Strengths
- Clear, well-written exposition with clean derivation of tree-marginal likelihood and predictive recursion.  
- Nontrivial, effective adaptation of BRT ideas to relational data (on/off-diagonals, tree-dependent off-diagonal structure, local merge scoring).  
- Fast, practical inference with careful caching: O(n^2 log n) complexity and likelihood computation O(n + m); sparse variant empirically faster on sparse graphs.  
- Strong empirical efficiency story: comparable predictive performance to IRM much faster; compelling qualitative hierarchies.  
- Produces probabilistic hierarchies and mixtures over partitions enabling interpretability and predictive distributions.  

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability: O(n^2 log n) may be prohibitive for very large networks; asks for wall-clock timings and memory usage on larger networks and asymptotic behavior as n grows.  
- Sparse variant: requests empirical scaling (does runtime track O(m log n)?) and discussion of further pruning/parallelization.  
- Greedy search optimality: no guarantees; requests evaluation of suboptimality gap via exhaustive or beam search and theoretical support or counterexamples for the merge heuristic.  
- Empirical scope: needs additional baselines and larger datasets (e.g., 10k–100k node networks) to bolster significance.  
- Hyperparameters: α, β, δ, λ, γ are fixed; asks whether they can be learned and requests sensitivity analysis or empirical Bayes.  
- Reproducibility: unclear whether BHCD code is publicly available.  

Assessment
- Quality: technically sound with well-executed dynamic programming and caching; empirical support strong for medium-scale networks but scalability and greedy guarantees are caveats.  
- Clarity: very well written; algorithm and implementation details are clearly presented.  
- Originality: meaningful contribution by adapting BRT to blockmodel likelihoods, introducing merge operators and efficient caching despite inspiration from prior work.  
- Significance: important scaling contribution with a good speed/accuracy trade-off; greater impact would follow from larger-scale evidence and sparse-scaling guidance.  

Suggestions for improvement
- Add timing and memory plots vs n and m, compare full NIPS to NIPS-234, and include at least one larger sparse network.  
- Report how often absorptions vs joins are selected and ablate restricting to binary trees.  
- Evaluate greedy optimality on small graphs and discuss structural properties (e.g., approximate submodularity) that justify the heuristic.  
- Discuss parallelization strategies and candidate pruning (e.g., k-nearest neighbor merge candidates).  
- Provide sensitivity analysis for γ and on/off priors and consider empirical Bayes updates.  
- Clarify memory complexity and implementation details for the heap and caches.  

Minor corrections
- Fix wording: avoid “whilst … but”; use “whilst …” or “but …” not both.  
- Change “pair trees” → “pair of trees.”  
- Change “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps.”  

Pros
- Fast, simple, and effective inference with exact tree-marginal computation.  
- Interpretable hierarchical structure with predictive mixtures over partitions.  
- Strong qualitative and competitive quantitative results with large speedups vs IRM.  
- Clear writing and solid positioning relative to prior work.  

Cons
- O(n^2 log n) inference may limit applicability to very large graphs.  
- No guarantees on greedy search and limited analysis of suboptimality.  
- Limited large-scale experimental evidence and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.  

Recommendation
- Accept, with the caveat that additional large-network experiments and analysis of the greedy procedure would further strengthen the paper.
