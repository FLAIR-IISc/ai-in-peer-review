This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), is a tree-structured mixture of potentially exponentially many stochastic blockmodels. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that take just one pass through the data to learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model. The worst-case run time of these algorithms scales quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but independent of the number of nested communities.

The paper relates to previous work on social networks, including stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership stochastic blockmodels, and Bayesian approaches to inferring community structure. The authors also compare their model to existing hierarchical network models, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and its extensions.

The technical novelty of the paper lies in adapting the likelihood and updating sufficient statistics efficiently for network data. The authors claim that the likelihood computation can be done in O(nodes + edges) time, enabling scalable agglomerative clustering. However, the presentation of the dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates is dense and hard to follow, and could benefit from more detail.

The experimental results show benefits over a flat IRM baseline and qualitative success on Sampson's monastery network. The authors also demonstrate the speed and accuracy of their method on a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network compared to IRM. However, the experiments lack comparisons to existing hierarchical network models, such as the multiscale community blockmodel and the Mondrian process.

The use of the term "nonparametric" is also questionable, given the prior framing of rose trees as model selection rather than nonparametric. Additionally, the scalability claims are discussed but not fully demonstrated, as the tested networks are not especially large.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and integrates prior work well. However, the originality is limited, and the explanation and comparative evaluation need strengthening.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the presentation of some sections, such as the dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates, is dense and hard to follow. (Score: 8/10)

**Clarity:** The presentation is generally clear, but some sections could benefit from more detail and explanation. The notation is also sometimes confusing, and the reader needs to refer back to previous sections to understand the definitions. (Score: 7/10)

**Originality:** The paper builds upon prior work on Bayesian rose trees and stochastic blockmodels, but the originality is limited. The adaptation of the likelihood and updating sufficient statistics efficiently for network data is a notable contribution, but it is not a major breakthrough. (Score: 6/10)

**Significance:** The paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of network analysis and community detection. The proposed model and algorithm could be useful for large-scale network analysis, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. However, the lack of comparisons to existing hierarchical network models is a significant limitation. (Score: 8/10)
