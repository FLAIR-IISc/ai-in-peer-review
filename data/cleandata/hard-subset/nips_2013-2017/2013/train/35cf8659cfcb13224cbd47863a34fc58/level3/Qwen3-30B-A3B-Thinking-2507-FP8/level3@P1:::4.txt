

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for learning hierarchical community structure in networks via tree-structured mixtures of stochastic blockmodels. BHCD builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller et al., 2005) but adapts it to relational data through a novel likelihood computation using dynamic programming and sufficient statistics caching. The key claim is a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n) for n vertices, achieving two orders of magnitude faster inference than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy on co-authorship networks. The work integrates well with prior Bayesian community detection literature (e.g., IRM, Mondrian process) but mislabels its approach as "nonparametric," as it performs greedy model selection rather than Bayesian nonparametric inference.

**Strengths**  
The technical execution is sound. The dynamic programming approach for marginal likelihood computation (Eq. 10) and efficient sufficient statistic updates (Eq. 13) are well-motivated, enabling the claimed complexity. Empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages: on NIPS-234 (234 nodes), BHCD sparse achieves comparable predictive performance (AUC, log-likelihood) to IRM in ~10 seconds versus IRM’s ~1000 seconds, validating the scalability claim. The method’s ability to produce interpretable hierarchies (e.g., on Sampson’s monastery) and leverage ensemble predictions via multiple restarts (Section 4.1) are notable contributions. The clarity of the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and the thorough ablation of variants (BHCD, sparse, binary) strengthen the empirical analysis.

**Weaknesses**  
The primary weakness is the **lack of comparison to hierarchical models**. The experiments benchmark only against flat-structure baselines (IRM), omitting established hierarchical approaches like Ho et al.’s multiscale community blockmodel (AISTATS 2012) and Roy et al.’s Mondrian process (NIPS 2009). These are direct competitors for hierarchical community discovery; their absence undermines the significance claim. For instance, Ho et al. explicitly handle nested structure with scalable inference, while Mondrian provides a Bayesian nonparametric prior on trees—both directly relevant to BHCD’s framework. Without such comparisons, the "hierarchical" contribution remains unverified.

Second, the **terminology "nonparametric" is misleading**. BHCD is a greedy model selection procedure over a finite set of tree structures (Section 3), not a Bayesian nonparametric method like the IRM (which marginalizes over infinite partitions via CRP). The paper’s framing conflates "nonparametric" with "model selection," which misrepresents the field. This confusion should be corrected to avoid misleading readers.

Third, **methodological exposition is insufficient**. Section 3 (especially Eq. 9) is dense and lacks concrete examples to clarify the sufficient statistic updates (e.g., how σ¬chSS is derived from σSS). The dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) and forest likelihood (Eq. 11) require more intuitive explanation to demonstrate why caching enables O(1) inner-loop operations. A small illustrative example (e.g., merging two subtrees) would resolve this.

Finally, **scalability claims are under-validated**. The full NIPS network (2864 nodes) is used only for qualitative hierarchy visualization, not timing comparisons. The NIPS-234 subset (234 nodes) is too small to demonstrate scalability for large networks. The paper should report runtimes on larger synthetic or real-world networks (e.g., >5,000 nodes) to substantiate the O(n² log n) claim.

**Originality and Significance**  
The core idea—applying Bayesian hierarchical clustering to networks via efficient sufficient statistics—builds on Heller et al. (2005) and is not sufficiently novel for NIPS. The paper’s novelty lies in the adaptation to network data and computational optimizations, but this is incremental given prior work (e.g., Ho et al. 2012, which also uses tree-structured blockmodels with scalable inference). The significance is limited because the method does not advance the state of the art in hierarchical modeling; it merely offers a faster alternative to IRM for flat structures, while omitting direct comparisons to true hierarchical methods.

**Recommendation**  
The paper is technically sound with clear empirical benefits over IRM but suffers from critical omissions in evaluation and terminology. The missing comparisons to hierarchical baselines (Ho et al., Roy et al.) and the misleading "nonparametric" label significantly weaken the contribution. While the speed advantage is practical, the lack of theoretical novelty and unverified scalability on large networks reduce its impact. The work would be acceptable with major revisions: (1) add comparisons to Ho et al. and Roy et al., (2) correct the nonparametric terminology, (3) clarify Section 3 with examples, and (4) validate scalability on larger networks. As-is, it lacks sufficient novelty and completeness for acceptance. **Weak Accept** pending revisions addressing these gaps.
