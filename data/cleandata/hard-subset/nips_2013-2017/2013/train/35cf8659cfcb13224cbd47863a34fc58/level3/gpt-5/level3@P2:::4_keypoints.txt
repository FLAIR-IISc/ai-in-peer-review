- Proposes BHCD: a greedy Bayesian algorithm combining stochastic blockmodel likelihood with Bayesian rose tree prior to discover hierarchical communities.
- Exact marginal likelihood p(D|T) sums over tree-consistent partitions via a recursion with conjugate beta–Bernoulli terms.
- Greedy agglomerative algorithm maximizes local Bayes factors over join/absorption merges with cached sufficient statistics for O(1) inner updates.
- Claims worst-case runtime O(n^2 log n) (independent of number of communities) and likelihood computation O(n + m) via dynamic programming.
- Introduces “sparse BHCD” that forbids merging disconnected components and a simple tree-traversal predictive scheme for link prediction.
- Builds directly on Bayesian rose trees and stochastic blockmodels; adapts rose-tree mixtures to relational data with efficient caching.
- Technical construction of p(D|T) and recursion appears sound and consistent with prior work.
- Local Bayes factor merge scoring and conjugate priors make the method computationally attractive and analytically tractable.
- Section 3 and Eq. (10) need clearer step-by-step derivation and a proof sketch for the O(n + m) computation, including maintenance of σ¬ch.
- Provide pseudocode for data structures, memory complexity, and formal complexity analysis (heap ops, cache invalidation) for the O(1) updates claim.
- Correct the prediction complexity claim: prediction is linear in tree depth, not necessarily in n; quantify expected depth in practice.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; add sensitivity analysis and clarify whether they are learned or cross-validated.
- Clarify whether the sparse variant preserves likelihood principles or induces bias and how it changes the search space and guarantees.
- Paper is generally well written, but Sections 3–4 (recursions, DP, caching) are hard to follow and need worked examples and clearer exposition.
- Clarify handling of directed graphs, self-edges, missing data, and define “global” vs. “sparse” BHCD and tie-breaking/restarts effects.
- Novelty is incremental: main contribution is adapting rose-tree priors to blockmodels and engineering efficient agglomeration rather than new theory.
- Need deeper comparison to related hierarchical models (MCBM, Mondrian relational models, hierarchical IRM variants).
- Attractive for practitioners: simple, fast, interpretable hierarchies with predictive performance competitive with IRM and much faster runtimes.
- Empirical scalability is limited: experiments on NIPS (~2.8k nodes) are small; evaluate on much larger/sparser graphs (≥10k nodes).
- Missing comparisons to key hierarchical baselines weaken empirical claims.
- Pros: principled exact marginalization over partitions, very fast inference with caching, interpretable hierarchies, useful sparse variant.
- Cons: limited novelty, missing hierarchical baselines, under-explained derivations and complexity claims, unvalidated scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity, ambiguous “nonparametric” labeling.
- Suggestions: add detailed derivations, DP pseudocode, formal complexity/memory analysis; compare to hierarchical baselines; run larger scalability/ablation studies; report hyperparameter sensitivity; clarify predictive complexity and data-handling; release code.
- Overall: solid, practical contribution with strong engineering but limited conceptual novelty and missing evaluations—lean borderline accept with revisions to clarity and experiments.
