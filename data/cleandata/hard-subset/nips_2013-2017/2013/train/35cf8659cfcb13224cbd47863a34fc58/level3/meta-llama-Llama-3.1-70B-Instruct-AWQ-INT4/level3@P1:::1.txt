This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, building upon previous work on Bayesian rose trees. The model is a tree-structured mixture of potentially exponentially many stochastic blockmodels, and the authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms for learning a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model. The algorithms scale quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but independent of the number of nested communities, making them two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable or better accuracy.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas, which are drawn from Bayesian rose tree work but require nontrivial adaptation for relational data while maintaining computational complexity. The authors present qualitative results on Sampson's monks and the full NIPS dataset, as well as quantitative results on NIPS-234, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

The main strengths of the paper are its ability to scale Bayesian nonparametric network models to larger networks, its efficient inference algorithm, and its good predictive performance. The authors also provide a clear exposition of their model and algorithm, making it easy to understand and implement.

However, there are some limitations and potential areas for improvement. Firstly, the O(n^2) algorithm may be prohibitive for very large networks, although a sparse version could be feasible. The authors should provide timings on larger real-world networks to demonstrate the scalability of their approach. Additionally, it would be interesting to evaluate how close the greedy method gets to the optimal tree, for example by exhaustive search at the largest feasible scale, and to seek theoretical support for the approach, such as submodularity.

Minor corrections are also needed, such as rephrasing some sentences for clarity and correcting some wording issues.

Overall, despite some familiarity with Bayesian rose tree ideas, the contribution of this paper is significant, and it should be accepted.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of their model and algorithm. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the key ideas and a good structure.

**Originality:** The paper builds upon previous work on Bayesian rose trees, but the adaptation to relational data and the development of an efficient inference algorithm are novel and significant contributions.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis and provides a scalable and efficient solution, making it a significant contribution to the field.
