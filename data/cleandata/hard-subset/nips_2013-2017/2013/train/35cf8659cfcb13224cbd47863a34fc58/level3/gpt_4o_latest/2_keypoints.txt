- Presents BHCD, a Bayesian nonparametric model that infers hierarchical community structures by generalizing stochastic block models to tree-structured partitions.  
- Uses a tree-structured mixture of stochastic block models with a greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm for efficient inference.  
- Claims comparable or superior accuracy to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while running much faster, scaling as O(n^2 log n).  
- Novelty: introduces a Bayesian version of agglomerative clustering and the key insight of restricting partitions to trees for tractable marginal likelihoods.  
- Efficiency: significantly faster than IRM (about two orders of magnitude), suitable for large-scale networks.  
- Empirical results: strong scalability and competitive predictive performance on real datasets (e.g., NIPS co-authorship); sparse BHCD is effective for sparse networks.  
- Significance: hierarchical outputs provide richer community insights than flat clustering and could inspire further work.  
- Clarity issues: writing, grammar, vague phrasing, and unclear dataset descriptions—Section 4 is particularly hard to follow.  
- Mathematical presentation: some equations and figures (e.g., Eq. 5, Fig. 3) are confusing or unnecessary.  
- Limited comparisons: lacks sufficient discussion vs. other hierarchical models (e.g., Mondrian process, shallow-hierarchy methods).  
- Interpretability: learned hierarchies—especially binary-only variants—can be hard to interpret and may limit practical utility.  
- Pro: strong contribution addressing computational inefficiencies of existing Bayesian nonparametric hierarchical methods.  
- Con: unclear link between the generative model and the learning algorithm plus presentation problems hinder accessibility and reproducibility.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, fix Section 4, and polish writing and presentation.
