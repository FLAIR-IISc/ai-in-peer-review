Summary: The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a hierarchical extension of blockmodeling that leverages Bayesian rose tree (BRT) ideas to define a mixture over tree-consistent partitions. The hierarchy is learned via a greedy agglomerative procedure (join/absorb moves), with dynamic programming for exact marginal likelihood evaluation on a fixed tree and careful caching for O(1) local updates. Conceptually, the model can be seen as a hierarchical analogue of the IRM that remains tractable by restricting to partitions consistent with the learned tree. The authors present qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and the full NIPS co-authorship network, and quantitative link-prediction results on NIPS-234 showing predictive performance comparable to IRM at dramatically reduced computational cost. The work relates closely to BRTs (for i.i.d. data), the IRM and hierarchical IRM variants, and nonparametric network models such as LFRM and ILA from prior NIPS work.

Quality: The model and inference are technically sound, with clear derivations (especially the recursion for p(D|T) and the Bayes factor-based merge scoring). Experimental evidence supports the claims of large speedups with similar predictive accuracy on NIPS-234. The discussion is honest about limitations and avenues for scaling.

Clarity: The paper is very well written, with clear exposition, intuitive diagrams, and a precise description of the caching strategy and merge operations. Minor wording issues noted below.

Originality: While the prior over partitions is inspired by BRTs, the adaptation to relational data (on-/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods, forest likelihood, and merge types) is nontrivial and novel. The sparse variant and join/absorb design are pragmatic contributions.

Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric models for networks is important. BHCD provides a practical, interpretable hierarchy with inference dramatically cheaper than IRM, which should make it attractive to practitioners.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The O(n^2 log n) agglomerative phase may be prohibitive for very large graphs. Please provide wall-clock timings and memory usage on larger real-world networks; e.g., how long does full NIPS take vs NIPS-234 on specified hardware? What are the limits (tens of thousands or more nodes)?
- Clarify the complexity and implementation details of BHCD-sparse; is the observed runtime closer to O(m log n)?
- Robustness of greedy search: quantify how close the greedy tree is to optimal via exhaustive search on small graphs; provide theoretical support if possible (e.g., submodularity or approximation bounds).
- Report sensitivity to restarts, tie-breaking, and hyperparameters; consider stability metrics across runs.
- Comparisons to ILA/LFRM on shared splits would strengthen claims.

Minor corrections:
- Line 48: avoid “whilst … but”.
- Use “pair of trees” consistently.
- “A fruitful next steps” → “Fruitful next steps” or “A fruitful next step”.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed idea; strong empirical speedups; solid predictive performance; interpretable hierarchies.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited evidence on extreme-scale graphs; limited analysis of greedy optimality and stability.

Verdict: Accept. Despite familiarity with BRT ideas, the adaptation to relational data and the resulting scalable method are significant and timely.
