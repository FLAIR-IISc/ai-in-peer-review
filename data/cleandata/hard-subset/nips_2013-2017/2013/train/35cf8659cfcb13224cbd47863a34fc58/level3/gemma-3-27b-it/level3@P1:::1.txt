This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for uncovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model extends the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by adopting a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, leveraging ideas from Bayesian rose trees. A key contribution is an efficient, greedy agglomerative algorithm for inference, requiring only a single pass through the data and achieving significantly faster runtimes than the IRM—two orders of magnitude faster on the NIPS-234 dataset—while maintaining comparable or better accuracy. The approach offers a fully probabilistic account of the data, unlike previous greedy hierarchical clustering methods.

The work builds upon a rich history of community detection techniques. Early approaches like the stochastic blockmodel [1] and mixed membership stochastic blockmodel [2] suffered from parametric limitations. While greedy hierarchical clustering [3, 4] offered flexibility, they lacked a probabilistic framework. Bayesian approaches, like the IRM [5, 6, 7] and its extensions to hierarchies [8, 9, 10, 11, 12], often faced computational hurdles. This paper addresses these challenges by adapting Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering [13, 14] specifically for relational data.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical soundness is evident in the clear formulation of the model and the efficient inference algorithm. Claims are well-supported by experimental results on three datasets: Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network (NIPS-234), and the full NIPS co-authorship network.  The paper provides a complete and well-defined piece of work, demonstrating a thoughtful consideration of both the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.  The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the complex ideas accessible. The authors adequately inform the reader, and the level of detail appears sufficient for expert reproduction of the results.  

The originality lies in the successful adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to relational data, coupled with a focus on computational efficiency.  The algorithm’s quadratic time complexity (O(n^2 log n)) is a significant improvement over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods. The significance of the work is clear: efficient hierarchical community detection has broad applications in social network analysis, and the improved speed compared to IRM makes it a practical tool for larger networks. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than previous research, offering a unique and efficient solution.

However, some weaknesses warrant consideration. While the O(n^2) complexity is a significant improvement, it may still be prohibitive for very large networks (tens of thousands of nodes or more). The paper briefly acknowledges this, mentioning a sparse version and potential future work on triangle-based representations.  It would be beneficial to provide timings on the full NIPS network to give a better sense of practical scalability.  Further exploration of the algorithm’s performance on even larger real-world networks would strengthen the conclusions.  

A further line of inquiry could involve assessing the quality of the greedy approximation. How close does the greedy algorithm get to the optimal tree structure (perhaps estimated through exhaustive search on smaller subgraphs)? Establishing theoretical guarantees, such as leveraging submodularity properties, could bolster confidence in the algorithm’s performance.  Minor editorial corrections are suggested—avoiding phrasing like “whilst…but” (line 48), clarifying instances of “pair of trees”, and revising “A fruitful next steps” to “Future work” or similar.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant improvement in computational efficiency compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of the model and algorithm.
*   Strong experimental validation on multiple datasets.
*   Addresses a practically important problem with a novel and effective solution.
*   The probabilistic framework offers advantages over purely greedy approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Quadratic time complexity may limit scalability to extremely large networks.
*   Lack of theoretical guarantees regarding the quality of the greedy approximation.
*   The performance on very large, real-world networks beyond NIPS remains largely unexplored.


**Overall:**

Despite these minor concerns, this is a highly valuable contribution to the field. The authors have successfully adapted Bayesian rose trees to the relational setting, resulting in a practical and efficient algorithm for hierarchical community discovery. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem with demonstrable improvements over existing methods.  I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2013.
