- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model using a tree-structured prior (Bayesian Rose Trees) for hierarchical community discovery in networks.
- Replaces CRP-style priors with a hierarchy-constraining prior, enabling exact marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming.
- Proposes a greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm for tree learning with worst-case O(n² log n) time complexity independent of community count.
- Demonstrates qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network and speed/predictive comparisons to IRM on NIPS co-authorship data.
- Shows orders-of-magnitude speedup over MCMC-based IRM inference while achieving comparable predictive performance.
- Theoretical derivations (marginal likelihood and factorization enabling local merge scores) are sound and well-founded.
- Could better contextualize connections to prior exact inference work (e.g., Koivisto & Sood 2004).
- Experimental evaluation is limited and not a rigorous comparative study.
- Comparisons are restricted to IRM; lacks baselines from other scalable or non-probabilistic hierarchical methods.
- Datasets are relatively small; largest (full NIPS) used qualitatively rather than for quantitative benchmarking.
- Evaluation metrics are under-specified (AUC computation details missing) and lack standard community metrics like NMI.
- Paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and uses helpful abstractions and pseudocode.
- Minor stylistic issues: sentences beginning with citations should be rephrased per NIPS style.
- The methodological synthesis (SBMs + IRM + Rose Trees) is novel and effectively trades CRP flexibility for computational efficiency.
- The main significance is practical: fast, fully Bayesian hierarchical community discovery with attractive complexity and usability.
- Acceptance arguments: novel combination, highly efficient algorithm, clear presentation, full probabilistic modeling of hierarchies.
- Rejection arguments: insufficient experimental breadth, missing comparisons, and incomplete evaluation metrics.
- Overall recommendation: lean towards acceptance conditional on strengthening experiments with larger datasets, more baselines, and standard evaluation metrics.
