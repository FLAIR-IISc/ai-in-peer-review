

**Review**

The paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, extending the stochastic blockmodel (SBM) and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors develop a tree-structured mixture of SBMs where the tree represents hierarchical community structure, and present an efficient greedy agglomerative inference algorithm that operates in O(n² log n) time. This method is significantly faster than existing Bayesian approaches like IRM while achieving comparable accuracy.

The work builds upon key prior contributions: the stochastic blockmodel (Holland et al., 1983), the IRM (Kemp et al., 2006), and Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005). The authors' key innovation replaces the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) prior used in IRM with a tree-based prior that enables efficient marginal likelihood computation through dynamic programming. The model leverages posterior factorization over subtrees (Koivisto & Sood, 2004), enabling their greedy search procedure, marginal likelihood computation, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction.

**Quality**: The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear derivation of the marginal likelihood and posterior factorization. The algorithm design is elegant, with the key insight being the use of sufficient statistics to enable O(1) computation per merge operation. The experimental evaluation demonstrates that the method achieves comparable accuracy to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster. However, the experimental section has limitations: the evaluation metrics (AUC) are not clearly explained (presumably by deleting links), and the comparison to relevant baselines is limited. The authors should clarify how AUC is computed and include standard metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) for community detection. The experiments are limited to small datasets (NIPS-234 with 234 vertices), and the paper does not include comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics, which would strengthen the evaluation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written with excellent organization. The consistent use of f() and g() to denote on-diagonal and off-diagonal block models simplifies the presentation. The algorithm description is clear, and the figures (Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate the hierarchical structure. The only minor issue is occasional use of citations at the beginning of sentences, which is not standard in NIPS style.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending SBM to hierarchical structures using Bayesian Rose trees. The key novelty is the efficient greedy inference algorithm that avoids expensive MCMC procedures used in previous Bayesian hierarchical approaches. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly the IRM (which uses a CRP prior) and other hierarchical community detection methods (which often require knowledge of hierarchy depth or are limited to binary trees). The authors cite relevant work appropriately, including the Mondrian process (Roy & Teh, 2008) and Heller & Ghahramani (2005).

**Significance**: The method addresses an important problem in network analysis—discovering hierarchical community structure—and provides a practical solution that scales better than existing Bayesian approaches. The speed advantage (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) makes it applicable to larger networks. However, the significance would be enhanced by demonstrating the method's utility on larger datasets or showing how hierarchical structure provides additional insights beyond flat community detection. The paper's claim of "comparable or better accuracy" is somewhat vague without clear comparisons to non-Bayesian methods.

**Strengths**: Clear theoretical foundation with sound mathematical derivation; elegant algorithm design with efficient computation through sufficient statistics caching; good experimental results demonstrating significant speed improvements; well-structured paper with clear presentation of concepts; appropriate references to relevant prior work.

**Weaknesses**: Limited experimental evaluation (small datasets only); unclear explanation of AUC computation; lack of comparison to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics; missing use of standard metrics like NMI for community detection; limited discussion of when hierarchical structure is particularly useful compared to flat community models.

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a technically sound contribution that addresses an important problem in network analysis with a novel and efficient algorithm. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional comparisons and larger datasets, the method is clearly a valuable addition to the field of community detection. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the experimental methodology and expand the evaluation.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
- Pro: Novel algorithmic approach with clear computational advantages over existing Bayesian methods
- Pro: Well-written and technically sound
- Con: Limited experimental evaluation with small datasets
- Con: Missing comparison to non-Bayesian methods
- Con: Unclear explanation of evaluation metrics
