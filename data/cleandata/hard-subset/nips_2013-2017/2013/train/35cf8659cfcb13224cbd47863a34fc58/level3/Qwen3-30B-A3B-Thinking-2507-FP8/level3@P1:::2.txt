

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks through a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. It extends Bayesian nonparametric approaches to hierarchical community detection, addressing a gap in the literature where previous Bayesian methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) either produce flat communities or require prohibitively expensive inference for hierarchical structures. The key insight is restricting the partition space to tree-consistent partitions, enabling efficient marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming, which contrasts with IRM's exponential partition space. The authors introduce a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm that builds a hierarchical community structure in O(n² log n) time, significantly faster than IRM's O(n²K²) complexity, while achieving comparable or better predictive accuracy.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network. BHCD achieves two orders of magnitude faster runtime than IRM while maintaining comparable predictive performance (AUC, log-likelihood, accuracy). The sparse variant of BHCD is particularly efficient for sparse networks like the NIPS co-authorship data. The method successfully recovers meaningful hierarchical structures, such as identifying the Young Turks and Loyal Opposition groups in Sampson's network, with BHCD sparse outperforming other variants in log-likelihood.

The paper's strengths include a novel approach to hierarchical community detection that bridges Bayesian nonparametric methods and efficient agglomerative clustering, strong empirical validation showing significant computational advantages without sacrificing predictive accuracy, and clear connections to relevant prior work like the IRM and Mondrian process. The theoretical foundation for the marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming is sound, and the algorithmic implementation is well-motivated for scalability.

However, the paper has significant clarity issues that undermine its impact. The connection between the Bayesian generative model described in Section 3 and the greedy model selection procedure in Section 4 is conceptually confusing. The paper claims to produce "fully probabilistic" hierarchical community models, but the greedy algorithm appears to find a MAP estimate rather than performing proper Bayesian inference, creating a disconnect between the model and the inference procedure. This fundamental ambiguity needs clarification, as it affects the paper's scientific contribution.

The writing quality requires substantial improvement. There are numerous spelling and grammatical errors, vague descriptions of datasets (e.g., "NIPS-234" without explaining how this subset was selected), and unnecessary mathematical notation that obscures the main contributions. Some figures (e.g., Figure 3) are confusing without clear captions, and the explanation of the "BHCD global" variant is particularly opaque. The paper would benefit from more precise language and clearer explanations of key concepts.

The significance of this work is high for network analysis and social network science. Hierarchical community structure is a natural representation of social organization, and the computational efficiency of BHCD makes Bayesian hierarchical community detection feasible for larger networks. The results suggest BHCD could become a practical tool for social scientists studying network structure.

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: Strong empirical results but conceptual confusion about inference procedure weakens technical soundness
- Clarity: Poor writing quality with numerous errors and unclear explanations
- Originality: Novel tree-restricted partition space enables efficient inference, distinct from previous hierarchical community detection methods
- Significance: Addresses an important problem with practical computational benefits

Pro:
- Novel tree-structured partition space enables efficient inference
- Strong empirical results showing 100x speedup over IRM with comparable accuracy
- Clear theoretical justification for dynamic programming approach
- Demonstrated utility on meaningful social network data

Con:
- Conceptual confusion between Bayesian model and greedy inference procedure
- Poor writing quality with numerous grammatical errors and unclear explanations
- Insufficient detail about dataset construction and experimental setup
- Unclear how the greedy algorithm relates to Bayesian inference (MAP vs. posterior)

The paper presents a promising approach to hierarchical community detection with substantial computational advantages, but its current presentation undermines its scientific contribution. If the conceptual confusion between the Bayesian model and inference procedure is clarified and the writing is improved, this work would be a strong candidate for publication. As written, the clarity issues prevent the paper from fully realizing its potential impact. The core algorithm and empirical results are insightful, but the presentation needs significant revision before it can be considered for publication. The authors should explicitly address whether the greedy procedure performs valid Bayesian inference or finds a MAP estimate, and provide clearer explanations of their method and results.
