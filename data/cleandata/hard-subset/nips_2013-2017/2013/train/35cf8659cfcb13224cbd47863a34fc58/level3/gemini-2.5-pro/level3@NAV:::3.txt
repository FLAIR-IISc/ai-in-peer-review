This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical structure in relational data. The work extends the well-known stochastic blockmodel (SBM) and the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). Its main contribution is replacing the Chinese Restaurant Process prior used in the IRM with a more structured prior over partitions defined by a Bayesian Rose Tree. This novel formulation allows for the exact calculation of the marginal likelihood via dynamic programming, a key departure from the approximate MCMC or variational methods typically required for related models. The authors propose a greedy agglomerative algorithm to learn the tree structure, which constitutes the primary practical contribution of the paper.

The paper has several clear strengths. It is well-written and the theoretical development is elegant and easy to follow, building upon previous work by Koivisto & Sood (2004) to enable posterior factorization. The proposed agglomerative algorithm is a significant contribution, offering impressive speed improvements—reportedly two orders of magnitude—over MCMC-based IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on the tested datasets. The succinct formulation using general f() and g() functions for on- and off-diagonal blocks is a nice touch.

However, the experimental evaluation is the paper's primary weakness and is not sufficient for a top-tier conference. The evaluation is illustrative rather than rigorously comparative. The choice of AUC as a metric for link prediction is not clearly explained (e.g., how held-out links were chosen), and the absence of more standard community detection metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) makes it difficult to compare against the broader SBM literature. Furthermore, the experiments are limited to small-scale networks; the "full NIPS dataset" is still modest by modern standards. To substantiate claims of scalability and state-of-the-art performance, the authors must include larger datasets and comparisons to a wider range of methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics, including other semi-parametric or scalable hierarchical clustering techniques. A minor stylistic note: several sentences begin with citations, which should be avoided in NIPS style.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
-   Novel and elegant integration of Bayesian Rose Trees with SBMs for hierarchical community discovery.
-   Enables exact marginal likelihood computation, moving beyond typical approximate inference schemes.
-   The proposed greedy algorithm demonstrates a significant and practical speed advantage over a relevant baseline (IRM).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
-   Experimental evaluation is inadequate, lacking rigor, standard metrics (NMI), and sufficient scale.
-   The comparison is too narrow, failing to benchmark against a broader set of relevant state-of-the-art methods.
-   Claims of scalability and performance are not fully substantiated by the limited experimental results.
