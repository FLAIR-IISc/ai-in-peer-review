- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), adapting Bayesian rose trees to networks by replacing iid likelihoods with a block-model marginal likelihood.
- A rose tree T induces a prior over partitions and a factorized marginal likelihood with on-diagonal f(·) and off-diagonal g(·) terms.
- Key recursion (Eq. 10) enables dynamic programming to evaluate p(D|T) using sufficient statistics cached over the tree, claimed to run in O(n + m) time for likelihood evaluation.
- Learning uses a greedy agglomerative procedure like Bayesian rose trees, merging subtrees via Bayes factors with cached updates yielding O(1) per inner-loop and overall O(n^2 log n) time.
- Variants include a “sparse” version forbidding merges between disconnected subgraphs and a binary-only merge version.
- For prediction the paper proposes a recursive mixture along a path in the tree.
- Builds directly on prior work (rose trees, Bayesian hierarchical clustering) and uses a blockmodel marginal related to the IRM.
- Compared to flat models (IRM, LFRM), BHCD trades modeling flexibility for substantially faster inference.
- The main novelty is adapting rose-tree DP to block-structured sufficient statistics and efficient O(1) merge updates; conceptual originality is moderate.
- The technical core appears sound, and the efficient sufficient-statistic updates are the primary contribution.
- The O(n + m) likelihood recursion is plausible, but the paper does not fully explain how σSS and σ¬chSS are computed and maintained without hidden quadratic costs or heavy memory.
- Empirically BHCD is much faster than IRM and attains similar or better predictive performance on NIPS-234, with interpretable hierarchical structure on Sampson.
- Averaging over multiple greedy runs improves predictive performance.
- Exposition is generally clear but Section 3 is dense; Eq. (9)→(10) transition and prediction responsibilities (Eq. 14) need more elaboration and a worked example.
- The caching scheme and memory complexity during agglomeration (Eq. 13) require fuller explanation.
- Significance: a practical, scalable hierarchical community detector with large speedups, but impact is limited by lack of comparisons to other hierarchical models and modest experiment scale.
- Strengths: principled reuse of rose-tree machinery, efficient DP and Bayes-factor scoring, solid empirical speedups, and interpretable qualitative hierarchies.
- Weaknesses: missing comparisons to key hierarchical baselines, under-explained derivations in Section 3, questionable “nonparametric” framing, limited scalability evidence (largest graph 2,864 nodes), and no sensitivity analysis or degree correction.
- Recommend clarifying σ definitions with a worked example showing σSS, σCC, and σ¬chSS computations feeding into Eq. (10).
- Ask to specify base cases and DP order for bottom-up computation in O(n + m), and list exact σ terms and their memory footprint.
- Request detailing what statistics are stored per tree and per pair (e.g., σII, σ¬chII, σIJ), worst-case memory complexity (likely O(n^2)), and sparsity-aware storage implications.
- Suggest empirical complexity scaling for BHCD-sparse (e.g., runtime vs m) and plotting runtime vs m at fixed n.
- Clarify how trees from multiple restarts are averaged for prediction (uniform vs likelihood-weighted) and sensitivity to number of restarts R.
- Discuss limitations of BHCD-sparse when handling anti-communities or bipartite structures.
- Ask for comparisons to hierarchical models (e.g., Ho et al. 2012, Roy & Teh 2009, hierarchical IRM variants, hierarchical random graphs) or at least reported-number baselines.
- Recommend rewording “Bayesian nonparametric” to avoid misleading claims (e.g., “greedy Bayesian model selection over hierarchical blockmodels”).
- Propose robustness checks: sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ), empirical Bayes or hyperparameter inference, and extensions to degree-corrected, directed/weighted, or missing-data settings.
- Questions for authors: exact memory complexity of cached σ tensors and practical n limits on commodity hardware.
- Questions for authors: provide step-by-step pseudocode for computing σ¬chSS and p(DSS|S) bottom-up in O(n + m).
- Questions for authors: how are multiple trees averaged for prediction and how sensitive are results to R?
- Questions for authors: performance on graphs with strong degree heterogeneity or bipartite structure?
- Questions for authors: can you report runtimes and AUC on at least one larger (>10k nodes) sparse graph?
- Arguments for acceptance: practical, efficient hierarchical clustering with clear speed advantages, technically sound adaptation, and interpretable hierarchies.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited originality, exposition gaps in key derivations and computational details, missing comparisons to related hierarchical models, and scalability validated only on modest graphs.
- Overall: technically solid and practically useful but needs stronger originality/empirical evaluation and substantial clarification of Section 3 and memory/complexity claims.
