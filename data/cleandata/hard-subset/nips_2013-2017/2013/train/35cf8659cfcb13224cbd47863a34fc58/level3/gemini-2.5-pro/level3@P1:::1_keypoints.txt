- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric model that discovers hierarchical community structure via a mixture over tree-structured stochastic blockmodels.  
- Uses a recursive dynamic-programming formula to compute the marginal likelihood of a tree exactly and efficiently.  
- Proposes a greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that merges subtree pairs by largest local Bayes factor increase.  
- Adapts Bayesian Rose Trees and related Bayesian agglomerative clustering ideas to relational (network) data.  
- Provides a deterministic, much faster alternative to MCMC-based IRM and hierarchical IRM approaches.  
- Derivation of the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant and appears correct; the merge criterion (Eq. 12) is well-justified.  
- Experimental results support claims of computational efficiency and competitive predictive performance (notably NIPS-234).  
- Produces interpretable hierarchical structures (e.g., Sampson’s monastery network).  
- Greedy inference is a limitation: no optimality guarantees and sensitivity to merge order and tie-breaking.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and presents the model and algorithm accessibly; figures and details are helpful.  
- Novel contribution: non-trivial adaptation of the BRT framework to stochastic blockmodels with a caching scheme preserving O(n^2 log n) time.  
- Significant practical impact: makes hierarchical Bayesian community detection far more scalable and usable for many networks.  
- Remaining scalability concern: O(n^2) worst-case complexity may still be prohibitive for very large or dense graphs.  
- Sparse BHCD variant shows promise for typical sparse real-world networks but full large-scale behavior is unclear.  
- Request to report wall-clock time on the full NIPS dataset (n=2864) to better assess practical runtime scaling.  
- Ask whether BHCD can scale to tens/hundreds of thousands of nodes and whether sparse variant suggests near-linear edge scaling.  
- Suggest evaluating greedy algorithm against optimal solutions on tiny networks (n≈8–10) and exploring submodularity for theoretical justification.  
- Minor wording corrections suggested (lines 48, 223, 318).  
- Recommendation summary: strengths outweigh weaknesses; strongly recommend acceptance.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses scalable Bayesian network inference, novel adaptation, large speedup vs IRM with comparable accuracy, clarity, and interpretability.  
- Rejection/concern arguments: greedy algorithm lacks theoretical guarantees and O(n^2) complexity may limit very large-scale applicability.
