The paper "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" proposes an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model is a tree-structured mixture of potentially exponentially many stochastic blockmodels. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that take just one pass through the data to learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model.

The main ideas in the paper relate to previous work on stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership stochastic blockmodels, and Bayesian approaches to inferring community structure. The authors extend stochastic block models and the single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. They replace the CRP formulation of IRM with the simpler formulation in equation (7). The posterior factorization over subtrees follows Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004), enabling greedy search, marginal likelihood computation, handling of sufficient statistics, and link prediction.

The paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations. The theory is succinct in parts, with consistent use of f() and g(). However, there is a minor style issue: the authors should avoid beginning sentences with citations in the NIPS style.

The main algorithmic contribution is an agglomerative method, summarized in Table 1. The evaluation uses AUC, but the method of computing it is unclear and needs explicit explanation. NMI is a more common metric for SBM comparisons.

The experiments are small-scale, with the full NIPS dataset being the largest. The authors should include larger datasets used in recent SBM evaluations. The experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative and thus inadequate. The authors need to provide comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics.

The strengths of the paper include its novel combination of stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian Rose trees, which enables the discovery of hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors also provide a fast and efficient algorithm for learning the hierarchical community model. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The weaknesses of the paper include the limited experimental evaluation and the lack of comparisons to other methods. The authors should provide more extensive experiments and comparisons to establish the effectiveness of their approach.
