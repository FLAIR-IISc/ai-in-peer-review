This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric Bayesian model for identifying hierarchical community structures in network data. The work extends the well-known stochastic blockmodel (SBM) and the flat-partitioning Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. This novel combination defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a tree structure, allowing the marginal likelihood of a given hierarchy to be calculated exactly and efficiently via dynamic programming. The core algorithmic contribution is a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative clustering procedure that constructs the hierarchy in a single pass. The authors demonstrate that their method is significantly faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on a small co-authorship network.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very well-written, with clear prose, correct grammar, and proper citations. The theoretical development is presented elegantly and succinctly. The consistent use of generalized functions `f()` and `g()` for on- and off-diagonal block likelihoods is a nice touch that presents the model in a general form beyond the specific beta-Bernoulli case. The overall structure is logical and easy to follow.

*   **Originality and Technical Soundness:** The primary contribution is the novel application of Bayesian hierarchical clustering techniques, specifically Bayesian Rose trees, to relational data. This provides a principled and efficient way to extend SBM-style models to hierarchical structures. Replacing the standard CRP formulation of the IRM with the simpler, tree-consistent partition prior (Equation 7) is a key insight that enables the efficient, recursive computation of the marginal likelihood. The subsequent factorization over subtrees, which follows from established work (Koivisto & Sood, 2004), is correctly leveraged to develop a greedy search algorithm, compute marginal likelihoods, and perform link prediction. The resulting agglomerative algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a solid and well-justified contribution.

*   **Significance and Efficiency:** The proposed method offers a significant practical advantage in terms of computational speed. By moving from iterative MCMC sampling to a deterministic, greedy agglomerative approach, the authors achieve a substantial speedup (reportedly two orders of magnitude) over the IRM. The O(n^2 log n) complexity, which is independent of the number of communities, makes this approach potentially applicable to larger networks than many traditional Bayesian nonparametric methods. This focus on efficiency addresses a major bottleneck in the application of Bayesian relational models.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Inadequate Experimental Evaluation:** The main weakness of the paper lies in its experimental section, which feels more illustrative than conclusive.
    *   **Comparisons:** The evaluation is primarily limited to a comparison with the IRM. While this is a natural baseline, the paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons to a broader range of methods. Given the focus on efficient hierarchical community detection, comparisons to other semi-parametric or algorithmic approaches (e.g., recent scalable SBM variants, spectral methods for hierarchical SBMs, or even popular non-probabilistic methods like the Louvain algorithm) are necessary to properly situate the method's performance in the wider literature. The current comparison only establishes superiority over one specific class of (slow) models.
    *   **Scale:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets. While the Sampson monastery network is a classic example, the NIPS co-authorship network is also modest in size. To convincingly demonstrate the claimed scalability advantages, the method should be evaluated on some of the larger benchmark networks commonly used in recent SBM and community detection literature.
    *   **Metrics & Clarity:** The use of AUC for link prediction is appropriate, but the paper fails to explain how it was computed (e.g., how were test links/non-links sampled from the data?). This detail is crucial for reproducibility. Furthermore, for community discovery tasks, Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) is a standard metric when ground truth or reference partitions are available; its inclusion would facilitate comparison with a large body of existing work.

*   **Minor Stylistic Issue:** Several sentences in the introduction and related work sections begin with citations (e.g., "[9] and [10] propose..."). While minor, this is contrary to NIPS style guidelines and should be revised for better readability (e.g., "Koutsourelakis and Eliassi-Rad [9] and Palla et al. [10] propose...").

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and original contribution to the modeling of hierarchical structures in networks. The proposed method is elegant and its computational efficiency is a significant advantage. However, the work is let down by an experimental evaluation that is too narrow in scope to fully demonstrate the significance and practical utility of the proposed method relative to the state of the art. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive and rigorous experimental section that includes larger datasets and a broader set of baseline comparisons.
