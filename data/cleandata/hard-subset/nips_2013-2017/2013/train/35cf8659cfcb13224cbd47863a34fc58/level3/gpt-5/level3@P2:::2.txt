Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model and greedy agglomerative learning procedure for discovering hierarchical community structure in graphs. The model views a hierarchy as a tree-structured mixture over partitions, where only tree-consistent partitions are considered. For a fixed tree, the marginal likelihood is computed exactly via dynamic programming by combining conjugate blockmodel likelihoods (on-diagonal f vs. off-diagonal g) with a partition prior induced by a stopping probability πS defined as a function of the node’s arity (following Bayesian rose trees). Inference over the tree is performed by bottom-up greedy agglomeration with three merge operations (join, absorb-left, absorb-right), selecting merges by a local Bayes factor. A sparse variant disallows merging disconnected subgraphs. The method targets scalability: likelihood evaluation is O(n + m) and the greedy search is O(n^2 log n) with caching, independent of the number of latent communities.

The paper relates to: stochastic blockmodels and mixed-membership SBMs; the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and its hierarchical extensions; Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees; the Mondrian process for hierarchical priors over partitions; and nonparametric latent feature models for link prediction (LFRM, ILA). Compared to IRM, BHCD restricts the partition space to those consistent with a learned tree, enabling exact marginalization for a given tree and far faster search; compared to hierarchical IRM variants, it trades some modeling flexibility for speed. Empirically, BHCD is reported to be orders of magnitude faster than IRM with comparable or slightly better predictive performance on NIPS co-authorship data and gives interpretable hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery network.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear probabilistic model for tree-consistent mixtures of blockmodels with conjugate priors; exact marginal likelihood on a fixed tree via a well-structured dynamic program.
- A practical, well-engineered agglomerative search with local Bayes factors and caching; thoughtful sparse variant improves speed and accuracy on sparse graphs.
- Solid empirical results: on NIPS-234, BHCD reaches similar or better AUC/log-likelihood than IRM in a fraction of the time; qualitative results on Sampson’s network are convincing.
- Complexity is carefully analyzed; independence from the number of communities is attractive.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- The relationship between the greedy procedure and Bayesian inference is underspecified. It appears to be a MAP model selection over trees with local search, not posterior inference over trees. Please clarify the objective being optimized and any guarantees (e.g., that the Bayes factor is the correct local score for optimizing the model evidence in a greedy agglomerative framework).
- The “locality” assumption in the merge score (only data among leaves of the merged pair) is intuitive but approximates the true evidence change (which would include interactions with the rest of the forest). Discuss its implications and any pathological cases.
- Hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed; no sensitivity analysis is shown. Since α>β and δ<λ encode assortativity, performance on disassortative or bipartite networks is unclear.
- Comparisons are primarily to IRM. Given the strong runtime claims, additional comparisons to other scalable methods (e.g., LFRM, ILA, multiscale community blockmodels, hierarchical IRM variants) would strengthen the case, even if only on small/medium graphs.
- Evaluation details need more precision: data preprocessing (e.g., NIPS co-authorship thresholding, handling of multi-edges), train/test split protocol for link prediction, proportion of held-out edges, and how missing/negative ties are treated.
- The multi-restart strategy (R=50) and tie-breaking could materially affect results; provide variance across restarts and report how often different topologies arise.

Clarity
The paper needs editing for grammar and organization. Some suggestions:
- Clean up typos and formatting (e.g., capitalization, missing spaces, duplicated punctuation, incorrect venue names).
- Improve Section 4 to more explicitly connect the merge score to the generative model and to the objective; outline the exact algorithmic steps, data structures, and memory complexity.
- Figures: ensure legibility, consistent labeling, and that captions fully explain what is plotted. For the NIPS hierarchy, describe the cut criterion more clearly and provide more of the tree in the supplement with readable names.
- Datasets: provide concise, self-contained descriptions of construction, statistics (n, m, density), and splits.
- Notation: keep consistent (e.g., indices and set notation), avoid unnecessary generality where beta-Bernoulli is used in practice.

Originality
The combination of a tree-constrained mixture of SBMs with a greedy, rose-tree-inspired agglomerative search for hierarchical communities is a novel and practical contribution. The dynamic programming for exact marginalization given a tree leverages known ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering but adapts them to relational block models in a useful way. Compared to hierarchical IRM and Mondrian-based models, the main novelty is the restriction to tree-consistent partitions to enable speed, and the specific merge-scoring/caching scheme.

Significance
Hierarchical community discovery is important for network science and practical applications. A method that is two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining competitive accuracy is valuable, especially for medium to large graphs. The work is likely to be used as a baseline or component in scalable hierarchical network models. The main limitation is that the current paper focuses on assortative structure with fixed hyperparameters and lacks broader comparisons; addressing this would increase impact.

Detailed questions and suggestions
- Clarify whether πS is fixed by γ or learned; have you tried optimizing γ or placing a prior?
- Provide ablations: no-absorption vs. full merge set; effect of the “sparse” constraint; impact of R restarts on performance and runtime.
- Report wall-clock time and hardware details; ensure fair comparison settings for IRM (same language/implementation).
- Add synthetic experiments with known hierarchical ground truth to assess recovery and sensitivity to noise, degree heterogeneity, and disassortativity.
- Discuss extensions to directed/weighted graphs and overlapping communities, and whether non-conjugate likelihoods can be supported with approximations.
- Release code and data splits to facilitate reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable algorithm with clear probabilistic underpinnings.
- Strong speedups with competitive predictive performance.
- Interpretable hierarchical outputs; addresses a relevant NIPS topic.
- Builds thoughtfully on prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering work.

Cons against acceptance
- Conceptual gap between the Bayesian model and the greedy inference is not fully addressed.
- Limited comparative evaluation beyond IRM; no sensitivity or synthetic studies.
- Presentation needs significant polishing for clarity and reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on improving clarity in Section 4, providing more precise experimental details, and better explaining the connection between the merge score and Bayesian model selection. The methodological contribution and empirical speed/accuracy tradeoff are compelling and of interest to the NIPS community.
