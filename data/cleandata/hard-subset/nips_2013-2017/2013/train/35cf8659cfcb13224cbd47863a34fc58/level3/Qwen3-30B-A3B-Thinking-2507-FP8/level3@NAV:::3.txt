

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks through a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors extend the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees, replacing the CRP formulation with a simpler one (equation 7), and enabling efficient greedy search through posterior factorization over subtrees following Koivisto & Sood (JMLR 2004).

The paper is well-written with excellent grammar and correct citations (though it occasionally begins sentences with citations, violating NIPS style guidelines). The theoretical development is succinct and well-organized, particularly in the consistent use of f() and g() for on-diagonal and off-diagonal blocks. The main algorithmic contribution (summarized in Table 1) offers a quadratic-time agglomerative method that scales well with network size and is two orders of magnitude faster than IRM.

Strengths include: a clear theoretical foundation building on established SBM and IRM work; novel incorporation of Bayesian Rose trees to enable efficient greedy search; and proper handling of sufficient statistics for marginal likelihood computation.

Weaknesses are significant: the evaluation uses AUC without clear explanation of its computation (presumably by link deletion), while NMI is the standard metric for SBM comparisons; experiments are limited to small datasets (NIPS-234 subset and full NIPS only), lacking larger datasets commonly used in recent SBM evaluations; and the experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, missing essential comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with good clarity, but the experimental evaluation undermines its claims of significance. While the speed advantage is demonstrated, the lack of thorough comparative analysis and standard metrics limits the paper's impact. The quality of the technical content is strong, but the experimental section requires substantial revision to meet NIPS standards.

Arguments for acceptance: Solid theoretical contribution, clear writing, and demonstrated speed advantage over IRM.

Arguments against acceptance: Inadequate experimental evaluation, lack of standard metrics, and insufficient dataset scope.

The paper would benefit from stronger comparative experiments on larger datasets using standard metrics before being suitable for publication at NIPS.
