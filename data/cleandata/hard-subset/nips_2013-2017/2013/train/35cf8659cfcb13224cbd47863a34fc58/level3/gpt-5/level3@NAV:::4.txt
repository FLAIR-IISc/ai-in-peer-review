This paper adapts Bayesian rose trees (Blundell et al., UAI’10) to network data by coupling the rose-tree prior over partitions with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The result is a hierarchical mixture over blockmodels whose marginal likelihood can be evaluated on a fixed tree by dynamic programming in O(n + m), and learned via a greedy agglomerative procedure with three merge types and cached sufficient statistics. Empirically, the method (BHCD) improves over a flat IRM baseline on prediction and yields interpretable hierarchies on Sampson; averaging over multiple runs further helps prediction. The work relates naturally to IRM/HSBM variants and to scalable hierarchical clustering.

Quality: The approach is technically sound and the reuse of the rose-tree framework is appropriate. The O(n + m) recursion (Eq. 10) appears correct but Section 3 is difficult to verify: Eq. (9) and the transition to the DP recursion need a careful derivation to rule out double counting across off-diagonal blocks. The description of cached sufficient-statistic updates (Eq. 13) is too terse; a complete pseudocode/appendix derivation and complexity accounting (including heap operations) would improve reproducibility. Claims of scalability are plausible but not compelling given the modest graph sizes; include wall-clock vs. n,m on substantially larger networks and memory costs.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but Section 3 is dense. Please add a small worked example illustrating σSS, σ¬chSS, and one full DP pass; highlight how responsibilities rS are computed and used for prediction; and clarify when merges with no inter-edges are disallowed (BHCD-sparse).

Originality: The main novelty is the blockmodel likelihood and efficient sufficient-statistic maintenance in the rose-tree setting. This is an incremental contribution; please clarify distinctions from prior hierarchical network models and rose-tree applications.

Significance: A fast, simple hierarchical alternative to IRM is valuable, and the results are promising. However, the absence of comparisons to existing hierarchical network models limits impact.

Terminology: Calling the method “nonparametric” is questionable given the model-selection framing over finite trees. Please justify or soften the claim.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, scalable hierarchical clustering for networks; O(n + m) tree evaluation.
- Solid performance vs. IRM; useful ensemble predictions.
- Clear integration with prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering work.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to multiscale community blockmodel (Ho et al., AISTATS’12) and the Mondrian process (Roy & Teh, NIPS’09), and other hierarchical SBMs.
- Dense, under-explained derivations and update rules.
- Limited empirical scalability demonstration; originality is modest.

Suggested fixes could elevate the paper to a strong accept.
