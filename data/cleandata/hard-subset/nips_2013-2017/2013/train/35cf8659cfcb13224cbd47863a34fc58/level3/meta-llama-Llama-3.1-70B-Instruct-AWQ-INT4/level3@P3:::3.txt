This paper proposes an efficient Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model extends stochastic block models (SBM) and the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that take just one pass through the data to learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model.

The paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations. However, there is a minor style issue: the authors should avoid beginning sentences with citations in the NIPS style. The theory is succinct in parts, with a consistent use of f() and g(). The paper extends SBM and the single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees, which is a novel combination of familiar techniques.

The main algorithmic contribution is an agglomerative method, summarized in Table 1, which has a time complexity of O(n^2 log(n)) through a novel caching scheme. The authors also propose a sparse version of the algorithm, BHCD sparse, which only needs to perform computations on the parts of the network with edges present. This version of the algorithm runs faster for sparse networks and produces better results.

The paper includes experimental results on three datasets: Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS dataset. The results show that BHCD performs similarly to the Latent Factor Relational Model (LFRM) and better than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) in terms of predictive performance. The authors also demonstrate that BHCD can make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The evaluation uses AUC, but the method of computing it is unclear and needs explicit explanation. Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) is a more common metric for SBM comparisons and should be used in addition to AUC. The experiments are small-scale, and larger datasets used in recent SBM evaluations should be included. The experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, and comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics are needed.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. However, the paper is not a complete piece of work, as it does not provide a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the algorithms and the experimental setup.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques, extending SBM and the single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. The authors also propose a new algorithmic contribution, the agglomerative method, which has a time complexity of O(n^2 log(n)) through a novel caching scheme.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results show that BHCD can make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan, and the algorithm is suitable for large dense networks. However, the paper does not provide a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, and the experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques, extending SBM and the single-table IRM by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees.
* The authors propose a new algorithmic contribution, the agglomerative method, which has a time complexity of O(n^2 log(n)) through a novel caching scheme.
* The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.
* The results show that BHCD can make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The evaluation uses AUC, but the method of computing it is unclear and needs explicit explanation.
* The experiments are small-scale, and larger datasets used in recent SBM evaluations should be included.
* The experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, and comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics are needed.
* The paper does not provide a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but the authors should address the limitations mentioned above, including providing a clear explanation of the AUC computation method, including larger datasets in the experiments, and providing a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.
