This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The model extends stochastic block models (SBM) and the single-table Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian Rose trees. The authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that take just one pass through the data to learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model.

The paper is well-written, with good spelling, grammar, and correct citations. The theory is succinct in parts, with a consistent use of notation. The model is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses the limitations of previous methods in handling hierarchical community structure.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The model is a novel combination of familiar techniques, extending SBM and IRM to handle hierarchical community structure.
* The authors provide a clear and concise description of the model and its components.
* The algorithm for learning the hierarchy of communities is efficient, with a time complexity of O(n^2 log n).
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the model in discovering hierarchical community structure and making predictions about unobserved connectivity.

However, there are some weaknesses and limitations:

* The evaluation uses AUC, but the method of computing it is unclear and needs explicit explanation.
* The experiments are small-scale, and the authors should include larger datasets used in recent SBM evaluations.
* The experimental work is illustrative rather than comparative, and the authors should provide comparisons to semi-parametric methods beyond Bayesian nonparametrics.
* The authors should discuss the limitations of the model and potential avenues for future work, such as adapting the scheme to work with non-conjugate likelihoods and doing hyperparameter inference.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and a clear evaluation methodology. However, the authors could improve the clarity of the paper by providing more detailed explanations of the model components and the algorithm.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by proposing a novel model for discovering hierarchical community structure. The model is a novel combination of familiar techniques, and the authors provide a clear and concise description of its components.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in social network analysis, and the results have the potential to impact the field. The model is efficient and scalable, making it suitable for large dense networks.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the weaknesses and limitations mentioned above.
