This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a nonparametric model for uncovering hierarchical structure in networks. The method extends the ideas of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by defining a prior over tree structures, where each tree implicitly defines a mixture model over the set of partitions consistent with it. This builds directly on previous work on Bayesian rose trees (BRT), adapting the framework to handle relational data. The model is learned not through MCMC, but via a fast, greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm that builds the hierarchy bottom-up. The authors demonstrate that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on benchmark datasets.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance & Efficiency:** The paper addresses the critical and difficult problem of scaling Bayesian nonparametric models for network analysis. The proposed BHCD method offers a compelling solution, with an inference algorithm that is demonstrably two orders of magnitude faster than standard MCMC for the IRM. This is a significant practical contribution that could make hierarchical Bayesian analysis feasible for much larger networks than previously possible.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The exposition is clear and logical, starting from the stochastic blockmodel, building up to the tree-structured mixture model, and then detailing the agglomerative inference algorithm. The connection to Bayesian rose trees is made explicit, and the technical details of the model and algorithm are easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** While the core machinery is adapted from the Bayesian rose tree framework, the application and necessary modifications for relational data are non-trivial and novel. The authors have successfully designed a likelihood and a caching scheme for the sufficient statistics that allows the agglomerative algorithm to maintain a manageable O(n^2 log n) complexity, which is a key part of the contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is solid. The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network and the full NIPS co-authorship dataset are compelling and provide good intuition for the model's behavior. The quantitative comparison against the IRM on the NIPS-234 dataset provides strong evidence for the central claims of the paper: BHCD is much faster and achieves similar or better predictive accuracy (in terms of log predictive probability, accuracy, and AUC).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the O(n^2 log n) algorithm is a great improvement over methods that scale with the number of MCMC iterations, it may still be prohibitive for very large networks. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the practical limitations more explicitly. How would inference scale to networks with tens of thousands or hundreds of thousands of nodes, such as large gene-interaction or social networks? The authors mention a sparse variant which is promising, and further exploration of this would strengthen the paper.
*   **Experimental Timings on Larger Data:** The speed comparison to the IRM is a major selling point. However, the quantitative timing results are only shown for the NIPS-234 dataset (n=234). It would be very informative to see how the run times compare on the full NIPS dataset (n=2864). How long does a full run of BHCD take on this larger graph? This would provide a more realistic test of the claimed scalability.
*   **Optimality of Greedy Search:** The use of a greedy algorithm naturally raises questions about the quality of the learned hierarchy compared to the (intractable) posterior over trees. It would be insightful to investigate how close the greedy solution is to the optimal one. Could the authors run an exhaustive search over all possible trees for a very small network (e.g., n=5 or 6) and compare the likelihood of the greedy solution to the true MAP tree? Furthermore, does the objective function (the marginal likelihood) possess any properties like submodularity that might provide theoretical guarantees on the performance of the greedy algorithm? Adding a brief discussion on this would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding.

**Minor Comments**

*   Line 48: The phrasing "whilst each iteration often scales unfavourably... but comes at considerable computational cost" seems to contain a redundant "whilst...but" construction. Consider rephrasing for clarity.
*   Throughout Section 4, the phrase "pair of trees" is used to describe the inputs to a merge. This is clear, but ensure consistency in phrasing.
*   The final sentence of the conclusion, "fruitful next steps," should be corrected to either "a fruitful next step" or "fruitful next steps."

**Overall Verdict**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to discover hierarchical communities is both novel and effective. The primary strength is the development of a fast, greedy inference scheme that makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling practical for networks of a size that would be challenging for traditional MCMC methods. Despite the familiarity of the underlying BRT ideas, the contribution is substantial, well-executed, and clearly presented. The paper should be accepted. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already excellent manuscript.
