This paper proposes a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model is a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels, and the authors describe a family of greedy agglomerative model selection algorithms that take just one pass through the data to learn a fully probabilistic, hierarchical community model.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the existing work in the field, and the authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of their model and algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to scale to large networks. The authors claim that their algorithm scales quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but independent of the number of nested communities. This is a significant improvement over existing methods, which often have a much higher computational complexity.

The experimental results are also impressive, showing that the authors' method can discover reasonable hierarchies and make predictions about two orders of magnitude faster than one of the fastest existing Bayesian nonparametric schemes, whilst attaining comparable performance.

However, there are a few weaknesses in the paper. One of the main limitations is that the authors do not provide a thorough comparison with existing hierarchical network models. They only compare their method with the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which is a flat community model, and not with other hierarchical models such as the multiscale community blockmodel or the Mondrian process.

Another limitation is that the authors' use of the term "nonparametric" is questionable. While their model does not have a fixed number of parameters, it still relies on a set of hyperparameters that need to be tuned. This is not necessarily a problem, but it is not entirely clear how the authors' model differs from other parametric models in terms of its ability to adapt to the data.

Finally, the authors' claim that their method is "Bayesian nonparametric" is not entirely accurate. While their model does use Bayesian inference, it is not entirely nonparametric. The authors' use of a conjugate prior and a specific likelihood function means that their model is not as flexible as some other nonparametric models.

Overall, I would give this paper a score of 7 out of 10. While it has some significant strengths, including its ability to scale to large networks and its impressive experimental results, it also has some limitations, including its lack of comparison with existing hierarchical network models and its questionable use of the term "nonparametric".
