Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to define a mixture over the exponentially many stochastic blockmodels that are consistent with a learned tree (hierarchy), where the tree induces a structured set of partitions. This follows the Bayesian rose tree (BRT) line of work in which a tree provides a mixture over consistent partitions, but here the approach is adapted to relational data via blockmodel likelihoods and separate on-/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods (Beta-Bernoulli in the main exposition). A generative prior over partitions is defined via node-specific stopping probabilities πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|, encouraging arity when appropriate. The marginal likelihood for a fixed tree can be computed exactly by dynamic programming in O(n + m), and the hierarchy is learned by a greedy agglomerative model selection procedure that uses local Bayes factors and considers three merge operators (join and two absorptions). A sparse variant forbids merging disconnected components.

The work relates closely to BRTs [Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.] in both the prior over tree-consistent partitions and the use of greedy agglomerative search, and to the IRM in its use of marginal likelihoods over partitions of vertices. Compared with IRM variants (flat and hierarchical), BHCD offers dramatically cheaper inference by making a single pass of greedy merges with cached sufficient statistics, achieving O(n^2 log n) worst-case complexity independent of the number of communities.

Experiments include qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network and a visualization of the hierarchy on the full NIPS co-authorship graph, as well as quantitative comparisons on NIPS-234 against IRM (MCMC and greedy), showing similar predictive performance at a fraction of the runtime.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written exposition with a clean derivation of the tree-marginal likelihood recursion and an intuitive predictive recursion.
- Elegant reuse of BRT ideas with a nontrivial and effective adaptation to relational data: on/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods, tree-dependent off-diagonal structure, and local merge scoring.
- Inference is fast and practical: O(n^2 log n) with careful caching; likelihood O(n + m); a sparse variant is straightforward and empirically faster on sparse graphs.
- Strong empirical story for efficiency: on NIPS-234 the method reaches IRM-level predictive performance orders of magnitude faster; qualitative results on Sampson and full NIPS are compelling.
- The approach provides a probabilistic hierarchy and a mixture over consistent partitions, supporting interpretable structure and predictive distributions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability limits: O(n^2 log n) can be prohibitive for very large networks (e.g., tens of thousands to millions of nodes), even if the likelihood is O(n + m). Please provide:
  - Wall-clock timings and memory usage on larger real networks. How long does full NIPS (n≈2.9k, m≈9.5k) take end-to-end relative to NIPS-234? What is the asymptotic behavior as n grows?
  - A discussion of how the sparse variant scales in practice (e.g., does runtime track O(m log n) empirically?), and whether further pruning/parallelization is viable.
- Optimality of the greedy search: No guarantees are provided. It would strengthen the paper to:
  - Evaluate the suboptimality gap on small graphs by exhaustive search (or high-quality beam search) to quantify how close the greedy tree is to optimal.
  - Explore theoretical support for the merge heuristic (e.g., approximate submodularity/monotonicity properties) or provide counterexamples/intuition.
- Empirical scope: While NIPS-234 is standard for comparison, additional baselines and larger-scale datasets would bolster significance (e.g., social/gene networks with 10k–100k nodes using the sparse variant).
- Hyperparameter treatment: α,β,δ,λ,γ are fixed; can they be learned? Sensitivity analysis or simple empirical Bayes would be useful. Nonconjugate likelihoods and hyperparameter inference are mentioned as future work; a brief ablation on γ or on/off priors would help.
- Reproducibility: The IRM code is public; is BHCD code available?

Assessment along review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound model and inference. The dynamic programming for exact tree-marginal likelihoods and the local Bayes factor caching are well executed. Empirical support is solid for medium-scale networks. Main caveats are scalability beyond O(n^2) and lack of guarantees for the greedy procedure.
- Clarity: Very well written; the algorithm, sufficient-statistic caching, and predictive recursion are clearly presented. Minor wording issues noted below.
- Originality: While the prior over partitions and greedy framework are inspired by BRTs, the adaptation to blockmodel likelihoods for relational data, the merge operators, and the efficient caching scheme constitute a meaningful and nontrivial contribution.
- Significance: Scaling Bayesian nonparametric network models is important. BHCD offers a compelling speed/accuracy trade-off and practical interpretability. Impact would be higher with evidence on larger networks and clearer guidance on sparse scaling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide timing and memory plots versus n and m, including full NIPS vs NIPS-234, and at least one larger sparse network if possible.
- Quantify how often absorptions vs joins are selected and their effect on likelihood/prediction; include an ablation restricting to binary trees.
- Evaluate greedy optimality on small graphs and discuss any structural properties (e.g., approximate submodularity) that might justify the heuristic.
- Discuss parallelization (e.g., parallel score computation; blockwise candidates) and candidate pruning (e.g., consider merges only among k-nearest neighbors under a quickly computed similarity).
- Add sensitivity analysis for γ and the on/off diagonal priors; consider simple empirical Bayes updates.
- Clarify memory complexity and implementation details of the heap and caches.

Minor corrections
- Line 48: avoid “whilst … but”; use “whilst …” or “but …”, not both.
- “pair of trees” (not “pair trees”).
- “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps”.

Pros
- Fast, simple, and effective inference with exact tree-marginal computation.
- Interpretable hierarchical structure with predictive mixtures over partitions.
- Strong qualitative and competitive quantitative results with large speedups vs IRM.
- Clear writing and solid positioning relative to prior work.

Cons
- O(n^2 log n) inference may limit applicability to very large graphs.
- No guarantees on greedy search; limited analysis of suboptimality.
- Limited large-scale experimental evidence and hyperparameter sensitivity.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite borrowing from the BRT literature, the adaptation to relational data and the resulting speed/accuracy trade-off represent a significant and timely contribution. With additional results on larger networks and a brief analysis of the greedy procedure’s optimality, this paper would be even stronger.
