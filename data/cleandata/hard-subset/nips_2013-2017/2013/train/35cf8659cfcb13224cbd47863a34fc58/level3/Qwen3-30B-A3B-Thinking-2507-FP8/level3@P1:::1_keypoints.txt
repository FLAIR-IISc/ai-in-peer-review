- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian nonparametric method for hierarchical community discovery using a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels and a tree-consistent prior.  
- Adapts Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data, deriving an exact marginal likelihood via dynamic programming and enabling O(n² log n) greedy agglomerative model selection.  
- Avoids iterative inference of IRM-based methods, achieving comparable predictive accuracy to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster on NIPS-234.  
- Presents a sparse BHCD variant to further accelerate performance on sparse networks.  
- Originality/significance: addresses a major scalability bottleneck in hierarchical Bayesian community discovery with a nontrivial adaptation of prior work.  
- Technical quality: rigorous marginal likelihood derivation, correct dynamic-programming recursion, and O(1) merges via cached sufficient statistics.  
- Experimental design: qualitative validation on Sampson’s monastery and quantitative speed–accuracy comparisons (NIPS-234) against IRM and baselines.  
- Clarity: well-written exposition with clear examples, precise algorithm description, and accessible predictive probability derivation; only minor grammatical issues.  
- Scalability concern: algorithmic complexity and experiments are limited to n=234 (and partial n=2864); no timings or analysis for much larger graphs (e.g., n > 10k).  
- Sparse-variant promise: O(n+m) likelihood suggests potential for larger-scale use, but the paper lacks empirical runtime analysis on larger synthetic or real graphs.  
- Theoretical gap: greedy agglomerative selection has no approximation guarantees, no small-scale exhaustive comparison to quantify suboptimality, and no submodularity analysis of the score.  
- Minor presentation issues: small typos and insufficient justification of binary vs. non-binary hierarchies for interpretability.  
- Recommendation: strong accept—significant, novel, and practical contribution—request authors to add larger-scale scalability experiments and theoretical analysis in the final version.
