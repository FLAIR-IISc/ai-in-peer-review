

Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, extending the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) through a tree-structured mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The work builds on Bayesian Rose Trees (BRTs) [14] but makes nontrivial adaptations for relational data: it defines a prior over hierarchical partitions via tree consistency, derives an exact marginal likelihood using dynamic programming, and introduces a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm (BHCD) that operates in O(n² log n) time. This contrasts with IRM and its hierarchical extensions [8,9,10], which suffer from prohibitive computational costs due to iterative inference. The paper demonstrates BHCD achieves comparable predictive accuracy to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster on the NIPS-234 dataset, with a sparse variant further accelerating performance on sparse networks.

**Strengths**  
*Originality and Significance*: The paper makes a significant contribution by solving a critical scalability bottleneck in Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. While hierarchical extensions of IRM exist [8,9,10], they either restrict hierarchy depth or scale poorly (e.g., O(n²K²) for [8]). BHCD’s key innovation—using tree-consistent partitions with dynamic programming to avoid iterative inference—enables efficient greedy model selection. The adaptation of BRTs to relational data is nontrivial, as the authors carefully handle the interplay between tree structure and blockmodel likelihoods (Equations 7–10). This addresses a clear gap in the literature, as prior work struggles with scalability for networks beyond small size. The significance is reinforced by experiments showing BHCD matches IRM’s predictive performance (AUC, log-probability) in 10 seconds versus IRM’s 1000 seconds on NIPS-234, with sparse variants offering further speedups.  
*Quality*: The technical development is sound. The marginal likelihood derivation (Equation 10) and dynamic programming recursion are rigorously derived, and the algorithm (Algorithm 1) correctly leverages cached sufficient statistics for O(1) merge operations. Experiments are well-designed: Sampson’s monastery validates qualitative structure discovery (Figure 3), while NIPS-234 quantifies speed-accuracy tradeoffs against IRM and baselines. The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) is a practical improvement for real-world networks.  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written. Key concepts—tree-consistent partitions, marginal likelihood, and greedy selection—are introduced incrementally with clear examples (Figure 1). The algorithm description is precise, and the exposition of predictive probabilities (Equation 14) is particularly accessible. Minor grammatical issues (e.g., "whilst... but" on p.4) do not impede understanding.

**Weaknesses**  
*Scalability Concerns*: While BHCD’s O(n² log n) complexity is a major advance, the paper does not demonstrate scalability beyond n=234 (NIPS-234) or n=2864 (full NIPS). The reviewer requests timing for the full NIPS network (2864 nodes) versus NIPS-234 to assess whether the 100x speedup holds at scale. For networks with n > 10,000 (e.g., social networks), O(n²) remains prohibitive; the sparse variant’s O(n+m) likelihood computation suggests potential for further optimization, but the paper lacks analysis of runtime on larger synthetic or real-world graphs.  
*Theoretical Justification*: The greedy approach lacks theoretical guarantees. The paper notes that tie-breaking in early merges can yield different trees (Section 4.1), but does not quantify how close BHCD’s solution is to the optimal tree. A small-scale exhaustive search (e.g., on n=50) to measure the greedy approximation ratio would strengthen the claim of "efficient" model selection. The absence of submodularity analysis for the score function (Equation 12) is notable, as this is standard for greedy hierarchical methods [4].  
*Minor Issues*: The paper states "a fruitful next steps" (p.14), which should be "a fruitful next step." The discussion of binary vs. non-binary trees (Section 5) could better justify why non-binary hierarchies are preferred for interpretability (e.g., via Figure 3’s log-likelihoods).

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a strong contribution to scalable Bayesian network modeling. The adaptation of BRTs to relational data is original, the computational efficiency is rigorously demonstrated, and the results are significant for practitioners dealing with medium-scale networks. While scalability to very large graphs (n > 10k) remains an open question, the sparse variant and O(n+m) likelihood computation suggest promising directions. The minor theoretical gaps are common in applied ML work, and the paper’s clarity and empirical validation outweigh these concerns. The reviewer strongly supports acceptance, as the work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way—addressing a critical scalability bottleneck while maintaining predictive accuracy. The authors should address the scalability analysis and theoretical remarks in the final version.
