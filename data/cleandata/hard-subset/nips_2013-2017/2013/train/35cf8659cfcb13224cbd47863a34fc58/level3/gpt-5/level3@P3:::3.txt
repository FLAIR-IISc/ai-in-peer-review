Summary
The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric model for hierarchical community detection in networks. The model is a tree-structured mixture over exponentially many stochastic block models (SBMs): a fixed hierarchy T induces a set of tree-consistent partitions Φ(T), and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed exactly via a DP recursion that factors over subtrees. Compared to the IRM’s CRP-based marginalization over all partitions, the authors replace the CRP with a simpler, tree-conditioned prior over partitions (eq. 7), yielding an exact and efficient computation. They build on Bayesian Rose Trees to place a prior on hierarchical refinements and to allow non-binary merges. The main algorithmic contribution is a greedy agglomerative procedure (Algorithm 1) that evaluates local Bayes factors for three merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right) using cached sufficient statistics; Table 1 usefully summarizes complexity vis-à-vis contemporaneous models. Posterior factorization over subtrees (eqs. 6–10) parallels dynamic-programming ideas for tree-structured models (cf. Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004), which enables efficient search, marginal likelihood evaluation, amortized handling of sufficient statistics, and fast link prediction along a path (eq. 14).

Relation to prior work is appropriate: SBMs and MMSB for flat structure, IRM and its hierarchical variants, HRG-like approaches, and Bayesian hierarchical clustering/Rose trees. The work extends SBM/IRM by using a rose-tree prior to constrain partitions and by leveraging conjugate f(·)/g(·) blocks for assortative structure.

Quality
- Soundness: The model and recursions (eqs. 7–10) are coherent. Using conjugate Beta–Bernoulli blocks with different on/off-diagonal hyperparameters is sensible for assortative communities, and the DP derivation and caching scheme justify the stated complexity. The link prediction recursion (eq. 14) is straightforward and correct under the tree prior.
- Proof/derivation clarity: The theory is concise; the consistent f(·)/g(·) notation and σ-statistics are helpful. Some steps (e.g., the precise derivation of eq. 10 from eqs. 7–8) are terse; a short worked example on a tiny tree would aid reproducibility.
- Algorithm: The agglomerative search with three merge types is well-motivated and practically valuable. Complexity claims are credible given the local Bayes score and O(1) updates via cached σ’s; please also state memory complexity and clarify how symmetry/no-self-edge constraints are enforced in caches.
- Evaluation: The experiments are primarily illustrative. Predictive results on NIPS-234 show BHCD catching up to and surpassing IRM in seconds rather than hundreds of seconds, which is compelling for speed. However, comparative breadth and evaluation metrics are limited (see below).

Clarity
- The paper is well-written with good grammar, spelling, and correct citations. Minor style issue per NIPS guidelines: avoid starting sentences with citations (rephrase to “As shown by [X], ...”).
- Notation is mostly consistent (f/g, σss, σ¬chss). Ensure consistent use of DTT versus Dleaves(T), clarify directed vs. undirected conventions beyond the Beta–Bernoulli instantiation, and explicitly state how missing edges are handled during training and prediction.
- Please explicitly cite the dynamic-programming literature underlying the subtree factorization (e.g., Koivisto & Sood, JMLR 2004), which closely mirrors your recursion and makes the connection clearer.

Originality
- The combination of a rose-tree prior over hierarchical partitions with SBM likelihoods and an agglomerative, Bayes-factor-guided search is novel in this specific form. Replacing the IRM’s CRP marginalization with the tree-conditioned prior (eq. 7) is a clean and practical reformulation. Methodologically, this is a meaningful extension of Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data.

Significance
- The main benefit is scalability: exact marginal likelihood on a fixed hierarchy and O(n^2 log n) search independent of the number of communities, with strong practical speedups over IRM. This is useful for practitioners seeking hierarchical structure quickly.
- That said, claims about community discovery quality are under-supported: the evaluation is small-scale and largely limited to NIPS coauthorship, with limited comparative baselines and unclear AUC protocols. Additional metrics and datasets are needed to fully establish impact.

Specific suggestions and weaknesses
- AUC computation: The method of computing AUC is not specified. Please detail: (i) how positives/negatives are sampled (all non-edges or a balanced sample), (ii) whether links are deleted for training and then predicted (and how many per fold), (iii) whether symmetry is enforced in scoring, and (iv) the handling of class imbalance (consider also reporting average precision/PR-AUC).
- Community quality metrics: For SBM-style comparisons, report NMI or ARI against known ground truth on synthetic benchmarks (e.g., LFR graphs) or at least against competing flat partitions on real data. This would complement link prediction with structure recovery quality.
- Baselines: Beyond BNP methods (IRM, LFRM, ILA), include semi-parametric/parametric baselines: degree-corrected SBM (Karrer & Newman), hierarchical random graphs (Clauset–Moore–Newman), spectral or modularity-based hierarchical clustering. Even if not Bayesian, they are standard comparators for community detection and hierarchy.
- Datasets: Add larger and diverse networks used in recent SBM evaluations (e.g., political blogs/books, Enron email, citation/coauthorship subsets beyond NIPS, SNAP social graphs). The current empirical section (Sampson, NIPS-234, full NIPS) is informative but not sufficient for a general claim of scalability and accuracy.
- Model scope: The current conjugate setup favors assortativity and does not handle degree heterogeneity. Please discuss performance on disassortative or degree-heterogeneous graphs and whether a degree-corrected variant or non-conjugate priors (as noted in the Discussion) could be integrated.
- Robustness: You already note tie-breaking and restarts. Please report variability across runs (mean±std of likelihood/AUC) and the wall-clock costs per restart.
- Minor: Table 1 summarizes complexity; make clear that the algorithmic contribution is Algorithm 1 (agglomerative search) and that Table 1 is a complexity comparison. Provide memory usage and any sparsity-optimized data structures for BHCD-sparse.

Pros
- Clear, principled model with exact marginal likelihood on a fixed hierarchy.
- Elegant subtree factorization enabling efficient DP, caching, and fast link prediction.
- Practical, well-engineered agglomerative search; strong speedups over IRM.
- Good writing and coherent notation; useful qualitative examples.

Cons
- Limited and somewhat unclear evaluation protocols (AUC), and lack of NMI/ARI.
- Small set of datasets; missing standard, larger benchmarks.
- Few comparisons to strong non-BNP baselines (e.g., DC-SBM, HRG).
- Model currently tailored to assortative, degree-homogeneous networks.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The modeling and algorithmic contributions are solid and practically valuable, but the paper needs stronger, clearer empirical evaluation (AUC protocol, NMI/ARI) and broader baselines/datasets. If these are addressed, the work would make a meaningful and timely contribution to hierarchical community discovery.
