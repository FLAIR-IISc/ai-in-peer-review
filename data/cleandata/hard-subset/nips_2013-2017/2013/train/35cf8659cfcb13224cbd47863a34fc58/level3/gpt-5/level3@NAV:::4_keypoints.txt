- Paper adapts Bayesian rose trees to networks by coupling a rose-tree partition prior with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood (BHCD).
- Result is a hierarchical mixture over blockmodels with marginal likelihood evaluable on a fixed tree by DP in O(n + m) and learned via greedy agglomerative merges.
- Empirically BHCD outperforms a flat IRM baseline on prediction, yields interpretable hierarchies (Sampson), and ensembles across runs improve prediction.
- Technical approach is sound and reuse of the rose-tree framework is appropriate.
- Eq. (10) DP recursion appears correct, but the transition from Eq. (9) to the recursion needs a careful derivation to avoid double counting off-diagonal blocks.
- Cached sufficient-statistic updates (Eq. 13) are too terse; provide full pseudocode/appendix derivation and complexity accounting (including heap operations) for reproducibility.
- Scalability claims are plausible but unconvincing given small graphs; include wall-clock vs. n,m on larger networks and report memory costs.
- Section 3 is dense; add a worked example illustrating σSS, σ¬chSS, a full DP pass, how responsibilities rS are computed, and clarify BHCD-sparse merge rules.
- Main novelty is combining blockmodel likelihood with efficient sufficient-statistic maintenance in rose-trees; contribution is incremental—clarify distinctions from prior hierarchical network models.
- Absence of comparisons to existing hierarchical SBMs (e.g., Ho et al. AISTATS’12, Mondrian process Roy & Teh NIPS’09) limits impact.
- Calling the method “nonparametric” is questionable given model-selection over finite trees—justify or soften this terminology.
- Pros: simple, scalable hierarchical clustering for networks; O(n + m) tree evaluation; solid IRM performance; useful ensemble predictions; clear integration with prior Bayesian clustering.
- Cons: missing comparisons to related hierarchical SBMs, dense/under-explained derivations and update rules, limited empirical scalability demonstration, and modest originality.
- Suggested fixes (detailed derivations, pseudocode, larger-scale experiments, additional comparisons, clarity improvements) could raise the paper to strong accept.
