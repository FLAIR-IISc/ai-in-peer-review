- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a hierarchical blockmodel using Bayesian rose tree ideas to define a mixture over tree-consistent partitions.
- Learns hierarchy with a greedy agglomerative procedure (join/absorb moves), uses dynamic programming for exact p(D|T) on a fixed tree and caching for O(1) local updates.
- Conceptual contribution: a hierarchical analogue of the IRM that remains tractable by restricting partitions to those consistent with the learned tree.
- Experiments: qualitative results on Sampson’s monks and full NIPS co-authorship network, quantitative link-prediction on NIPS-234.
- Empirical finding: predictive performance comparable to IRM but with dramatically reduced computational cost.
- Situates work relative to BRTs, IRM/hierarchical IRM, and nonparametric network models (LFRM, ILA).
- Technical quality: sound derivations (p(D|T) recursion, Bayes factor merge scoring) and evidence supporting claimed speedups.
- Clarity: very well written with clear exposition, intuitive diagrams, and precise description of caching and merge operations.
- Originality: nontrivial and novel adaptation of BRT priors to relational data (on/off-diagonal likelihoods, forest likelihood, merge types); sparse variant and join/absorb design are pragmatic contributions.
- Significance: provides a practical, interpretable hierarchy and scalable inference, important for applying Bayesian nonparametrics to networks.
- Scalability concern: O(n^2 log n) agglomerative phase may be prohibitive for very large graphs — request wall-clock timings and memory usage on larger real networks (e.g., full NIPS vs NIPS-234) and limits in nodes.
- BHCD-sparse: ask for clarification of complexity and implementation; is observed runtime closer to O(m log n)?
- Robustness of greedy search: request quantification of how close greedy tree is to optimal via exhaustive search on small graphs and any theoretical support (submodularity/approximation bounds).
- Stability: request sensitivity analysis to restarts, tie-breaking, and hyperparameters and stability metrics across runs.
- Comparisons: recommend adding comparisons to ILA/LFRM on shared data splits to strengthen claims.
- Minor wording corrections: avoid “whilst … but” (Line 48), use “pair of trees” consistently, and change “A fruitful next steps” → “A fruitful next step” or “Fruitful next steps.”
- Pros: clear idea, strong empirical speedups, solid predictive performance, interpretable hierarchies.
- Cons: limited evidence on extreme-scale graphs and limited analysis of greedy optimality and stability.
- Verdict: Accept — adaptation to relational data and resulting scalable method are significant and timely.
