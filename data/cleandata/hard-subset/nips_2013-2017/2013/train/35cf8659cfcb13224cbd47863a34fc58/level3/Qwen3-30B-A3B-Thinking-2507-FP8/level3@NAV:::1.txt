

This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method that extends the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by incorporating Bayesian rose tree ideas to define a mixture over consistent partitions. The key innovation is a greedy agglomerative procedure that efficiently learns hierarchical community structure while maintaining computational tractability. The authors demonstrate that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to IRM but with significantly faster inference (two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration), with worst-case time complexity O(n² log n) that is independent of the number of communities.

The paper makes a significant contribution by adapting Bayesian rose tree methodology to relational data, requiring nontrivial modifications to maintain computational efficiency. The technical approach is well-motivated, with clear exposition of the hierarchical mixture model and the greedy inference algorithm. The experiments on Sampson's monks and NIPS co-authorship networks provide convincing qualitative and quantitative results, with BHCD sparse variant showing particular promise on sparse networks.

Strengths include excellent clarity in exposition, strong empirical validation, and a well-executed adaptation of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods to hierarchical community discovery. The paper is technically sound with appropriate theoretical grounding and thorough experimental evaluation.

Weaknesses include limited scalability analysis for very large networks (tens of thousands of nodes), where the O(n²) complexity may become prohibitive. The authors should provide timing comparisons for full NIPS versus NIPS-234 to better understand computational tradeoffs. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough investigation of the greedy method's optimality (e.g., via exhaustive search on smaller datasets) and theoretical support for the greedy approach (e.g., submodularity analysis).

The paper is well-written with minor grammatical issues that are easily fixable. While the core ideas build upon Bayesian rose tree work, the adaptation to relational data is nontrivial and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

Overall, despite some scalability concerns, the paper presents a significant, well-executed contribution that advances Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. The technical quality is high, the results are compelling, and the method has clear practical value. The minor issues noted do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. I recommend acceptance.
