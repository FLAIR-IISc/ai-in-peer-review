- Proposes Bayesian hierarchical clustering (BHCD) using Bayesian rose trees with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for community detection.
- Aims for efficient greedy agglomerative model selection and claims near O(nodes + edges) likelihood computation via caching.
- Builds on Bayesian nonparametric network models (e.g., IRM) and situates itself among hierarchical models like multiscale community blockmodel and Mondrian process.
- Strength: Technically sound adaptation of rose trees to hierarchical community detection with clear understanding of prior work.
- Strength: Strong emphasis on computational efficiency and a well-reasoned caching scheme to speed likelihood computation.
- Strength: Experiments show improved speed over IRM and qualitative success on Sampson’s monastery network; multiple restarts improve results.
- Strength: Effectively integrates and references relevant prior literature in Bayesian nonparametrics and hierarchical clustering.
- Weakness: Section 3 (esp. eq. (9) and derivations) is dense and hard to follow; connection between tree structure, likelihood, and sufficient statistics is unclear.
- Weakness: Dynamic programming and recursive sufficient-statistic updates are under-explained; algorithmic walkthroughs or pseudocode are needed.
- Weakness: Lacks comparisons to existing hierarchical network models (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel, Mondrian process), limiting assessment of novelty and performance.
- Weakness: Use of the term “nonparametric” is questionable given rose trees are framed as model selection rather than a true nonparametric process.
- Weakness: Scalability claims are not convincingly demonstrated—tested networks are not very large and O(n^2 log n) complexity still restrictive.
- Weakness: Originality is moderate; main contribution is efficient implementation rather than a fundamentally new model.
- Quality: Technically sound with supported experimental claims but limited scope of evaluation and comparative analysis.
- Clarity: Generally clear writing, but key technical sections lack sufficient detail, potentially hindering reproducibility.
- Originality: Moderate—combines existing techniques (rose trees, stochastic blockmodels) with implementation-focused improvements.
- Significance: Potentially significant if efficiency scales to larger datasets, but impact is reduced by lack of state-of-the-art comparisons.
- For acceptance: Addresses an important problem, offers computationally efficient methods, and shows promising benchmark results.
- Against acceptance: Insufficient comparative evaluation, unclear technical exposition, questionable terminology, limited large-scale experiments, and moderate novelty.
- Recommendation: Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit after adding comparisons to hierarchical models, clarifying dynamic programming and “nonparametric” claims, and testing larger networks.
