This paper proposes a novel Bayesian nonparametric model, Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The model extends the stochastic blockmodel to a tree-structured mixture of potentially exponentially many stochastic blockmodels. The authors develop an efficient scheme for inferring hierarchical community structure using a greedy agglomerative model selection algorithm. The algorithm scales quadratically in the number of vertices of the network, but independent of the number of nested communities.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks is relevant and important. The proposed model and algorithm are novel and insightful, and the empirical evaluation on two social connectivity corpora shows that the method is faster and slightly more accurate than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

The key technical insight of the paper is that restricting partitions to tree-structured ones enables efficient marginal likelihood computation, which is a significant improvement over the IRM's exponential partition space. However, it is unclear how the greedy tree-learning procedure relates to the Bayesian generative model, and it is uncertain whether it performs valid posterior inference or finds a MAP/ML estimate. A clearer explanation of Section 4 is needed to resolve this conceptual confusion.

The results presented in the paper are strong, with comparable or better AUC, predictive log-likelihood, and accuracy, and orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements. However, the writing quality needs improvement, with spelling and grammar errors, vague phrasing, unclear dataset descriptions, unnecessary or obscure math, and confusing figures.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper scores well on originality, significance, and quality. The model and algorithm are novel and insightful, and the empirical evaluation is thorough and well-executed. However, the paper could benefit from improvement in terms of clarity and presentation.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and insightful model and algorithm for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks.
* The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-executed, and the results are strong.
* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The writing quality needs improvement, with spelling and grammar errors, vague phrasing, unclear dataset descriptions, unnecessary or obscure math, and confusing figures.
* It is unclear how the greedy tree-learning procedure relates to the Bayesian generative model, and it is uncertain whether it performs valid posterior inference or finds a MAP/ML estimate.
