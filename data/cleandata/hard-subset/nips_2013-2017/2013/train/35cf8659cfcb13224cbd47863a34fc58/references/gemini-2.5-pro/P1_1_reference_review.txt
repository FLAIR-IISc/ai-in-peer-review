### Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework, previously developed for i.i.d. data (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2012), to relational data modeled by a stochastic blockmodel.

The model defines a marginal likelihood for the network data given a specific hierarchical tree structure. This likelihood is computed efficiently via dynamic programming by summing over all flat partitions consistent with the tree. The inference problem of finding the best tree is framed as a greedy model selection task. Starting with each node as a singleton tree, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of trees in a way that maximizes a local Bayes factor. The authors present an efficient implementation with O(n^2 log n) complexity by carefully caching sufficient statistics. The paper evaluates BHCD on several network datasets, demonstrating that it discovers meaningful hierarchies and offers a dramatic speedup (two orders of magnitude) compared to MCMC-based inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving comparable predictive performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Practical Contribution:** The primary strength of this work is its computational efficiency. The experimental results, particularly on the NIPS co-authorship data (Figure 4), are compelling. The ability to obtain comparable or better predictive accuracy than a full MCMC-based IRM in seconds rather than hours makes Bayesian hierarchical community detection practical for medium-to-large networks where previous methods were prohibitively slow. The O(n^2 log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, is a significant advantage over methods whose complexity scales with the number of inferred clusters.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Framework:** The paper does an excellent job of combining two well-established lines of research: stochastic blockmodels for network analysis and Bayesian hierarchical clustering. The resulting framework is principled, grounding the greedy search in the language of Bayesian model selection via Bayes factors. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood of a tree (Eq. 10) is a clean and powerful way to score candidate hierarchies.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It logically builds from the basic stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical extension and then to the agglomerative inference algorithm. The notation is consistent, and the motivations for design choices (e.g., different priors for on- vs. off-diagonal blocks) are clearly explained. The algorithm is described with sufficient detail to understand its mechanics.

4.  **Flexibility of the Model:** The ability of the model to generate non-binary trees (by allowing "absorption" merges) is a valuable feature inherited from the Bayesian Rose Tree model. This allows the model to capture more flexible hierarchical structures where a community might be composed of multiple sub-communities, rather than just being a series of binary splits. The proposed `BHCD sparse` variant is also a sensible and practically useful heuristic for sparse networks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Search:** The main limitation of the proposed method is its greedy nature. The algorithm makes a sequence of locally optimal decisions, which provides no guarantee of finding a globally optimal hierarchy. While the use of multiple random restarts helps mitigate this, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the potential pitfalls. For example, how sensitive are the final results to the random tie-breaking in the initial steps? An analysis of the variance in model likelihoods across different restarts would be informative.

2.  **Locality of the Merge Criterion:** The Bayes factor score used for merging (Eq. 12) is inherently local; it only considers the data within the two sub-trees being merged and the connections between them. The paper notes this relies on an assumption that "local community structure should depend only on the local connectivity structure." This assumption can be problematic. A merge that looks good locally might preclude a better global structure later on. While this is a necessary simplification for efficiency, its potential impact on the quality of the final hierarchy deserves more discussion.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values in the experiments. The quality of the discovered communities and the shape of the hierarchy can be sensitive to these settings. For example, γ controls the prior trade-off between splitting and merging, effectively influencing the depth and bushiness of the resulting tree. A sensitivity analysis or a discussion of potential strategies for setting or inferring these hyperparameters would strengthen the paper's practical utility.

4.  **Scalability on Sparse Networks:** While the paper's title and results emphasize speed, the O(n^2 log n) complexity remains a bottleneck for very large, sparse networks where the number of edges `m` is much smaller than `n^2`. The authors correctly identify this discrepancy between the fast likelihood computation (O(n+m)) and the slower inference procedure in the discussion, but it is a key limitation worth highlighting more prominently.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The claim that BHCD's complexity is independent of the number of communities is a key selling point. However, could it be argued that for a fixed `n`, a more complex community structure (i.e., a deeper tree) might take longer to converge, even if the worst-case bound is the same? The number of iterations is fixed at `n-1`, but perhaps the heap operations could have different practical costs.
*   In the Sampson monastery experiment, the log likelihood for BHCD sparse (-22.80) is substantially lower than for BHCD (-6.62), yet the resulting partition in Figure 3 looks arguably more interpretable and block-diagonal. This suggests a potential disconnect between the model's objective function (marginal likelihood) and the qualitative goal of finding clean communities. Is it possible that the model is overfitting in the non-sparse case by merging disconnected components? A brief comment on this would be insightful.
*   The prediction method in Section 4.2 is elegant. Could you clarify the term `p(Dxy|DCC, C)` in the recursion? I assume this means recursively calling the same predictive function on the subtree `C`.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  The paper addresses a significant problem: scalable inference for hierarchical community detection.
2.  The proposed method, BHCD, offers a massive and empirically verified speedup over established Bayesian nonparametric methods like the IRM.
3.  The approach is a novel and well-executed combination of existing probabilistic modeling ideas, making them practical for a wider range of network analysis problems.
4.  The paper is very clearly written, technically sound, and presents convincing experimental results on standard benchmark datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1.  The core weakness is the greedy search algorithm, which may find suboptimal solutions.
2.  The originality lies more in the combination and application of existing ideas rather than a completely new theoretical foundation.
3.  The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and the limitations of the local merge criterion could be more developed.

Overall, the strengths, particularly the significant practical impact of the method's efficiency, far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper presents a valuable tool that researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon. It is a solid contribution to the field of network analysis and probabilistic modeling.
