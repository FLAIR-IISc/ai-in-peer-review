- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks.
- Extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data with a principled probabilistic framework.
- Key innovation: greedy agglomerative inference that computes marginal likelihood in O(n² log n) time.
- Demonstrates ~100× speedup over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable predictive performance on NIPS co-authorship data.
- Provides clear derivations for marginal likelihood, greedy inference, and sufficient-statistics caching enabling the claimed complexity.
- Empirical results support computational claims and show BHCD yields reasonable predictions much faster than IRM.
- Acknowledges greedy approach may not find the global optimum and discusses trade-offs between binary and non-binary trees.
- Lacks theoretical analysis of the approximation error introduced by the greedy procedure.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear algorithm description and illustrative figures.
- Experimental section is strong, including qualitative analysis of Sampson’s monastery network and relevant baselines.
- Novelty: does not require preset number of communities or known hierarchy depth, and is computationally practical for larger networks.
- Properly situates and cites related work (IRM, Mondrian process, prior hierarchical clustering methods).
- Significant practical impact by making Bayesian hierarchical community discovery scalable and applicable to larger networks.
- Opens promising future directions: non-conjugate likelihoods, triangle-statistic parameterizations, and improved scalability for sparse networks.
- Strengths: clear presentation, strong experiments, major computational efficiency gains, and solid mathematical formulation.
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical guarantees, need for more comparisons with recent hierarchical methods, and potential inconsistency from random tie-breaking (mitigated by restarts).
- Recommendation: strong accept for NeurIPS based on quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
- Suggested improvements: more recent method comparisons, theoretical analysis of greedy error, and exploration of hyperparameter impacts.
