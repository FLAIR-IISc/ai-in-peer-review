### Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core contribution is the adaptation of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework, previously applied to i.i.d. data, to relational data governed by a stochastic blockmodel. The authors define a probabilistic model where the marginal likelihood of the network data, given a specific tree structure, can be calculated exactly and efficiently. This is achieved by summing over only the partitions that are consistent with the given tree, avoiding the intractable sum over all possible partitions that plagues models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). Inference of the tree structure itself is performed via a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm. At each step, the algorithm merges the pair of sub-trees that yields the largest improvement in the local Bayes factor, making it a model selection procedure. The authors demonstrate that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on benchmark datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold and highly effective approach to a notoriously difficult problem. Full Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection are often elegant in theory but computationally prohibitive in practice. This work carves out a new, pragmatic path that cleverly blends the principled nature of Bayesian modeling with the efficiency of greedy algorithms.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The adaptation of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational data is a significant and novel contribution. While the components (stochastic blockmodels, greedy clustering) are not new, their synthesis into a coherent and computationally efficient model for network analysis is. This work provides the community with a much-needed tool that sits between fast but ad-hoc heuristics (e.g., Newman-Girvan) and principled but extremely slow Bayesian methods (e.g., hierarchical IRM). The potential impact is high, as it makes scalable Bayesian hierarchical analysis of networks feasible.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The central claim of the paper—a massive speed-up over existing Bayesian methods—is convincingly demonstrated. The experimental results show that BHCD can produce meaningful results in seconds, while the IRM requires thousands of seconds to converge. The derivation of the O(n² log n) algorithm with an O(1) inner loop via caching of sufficient statistics is a key technical contribution that enables this performance.

3.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The probabilistic model, the recursive definition of the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10), and the greedy search algorithm are all explained with clarity. The model is technically sound within its stated assumptions. The authors are also honest about the performance relative to other models, noting that they do not outperform the state-of-the-art ILA model on predictive accuracy, which reinforces the credibility of their claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The most significant weakness is inherent to the greedy approach. The algorithm is deterministic (up to tie-breaking) and susceptible to local optima. There is no guarantee that the discovered hierarchy is close to the one with the highest marginal likelihood under the full model. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple restarts, it remains a strong approximation compared to MCMC methods that can, in principle, explore the full posterior. However, I view this not as a fatal flaw but as a necessary and well-motivated compromise to achieve scalability.

2.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The merge score is based on a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12), considering only the data within and between the two sub-trees being merged. This is computationally convenient but ignores the global structure of the forest, which could potentially lead to sub-optimal merges from a global perspective.

3.  **Fixed Hyperparameters:** The model's hyperparameters are fixed rather than inferred from data. This is a common practice for speed, but a fully Bayesian treatment would place priors on these parameters. The performance might be sensitive to the choice of these values, and the paper provides little guidance beyond stating the values that "work well".

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant synthesis of existing ideas to create a powerful new tool for network analysis.
*   The computational gains over established Bayesian methods are dramatic (orders of magnitude), representing a significant practical advance.
*   The approach opens a new direction for developing "fast but principled" approximate Bayesian methods for complex structured data problems.
*   The work is a complete package: a well-motivated model, an efficient inference algorithm, and strong empirical validation.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   The core inference algorithm is a greedy search, which is a strong approximation and may not be robust.
*   The methodological contribution could be seen as an application of the existing BHC framework rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.
*   The lack of hyperparameter inference makes the method less self-contained than a full Bayesian model.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The core idea is bold, novel, and addresses a critical bottleneck in the field of network analysis: the scalability of principled hierarchical models. The authors trade the theoretical purity of full posterior inference for a massive, game-changing improvement in speed, without sacrificing the Bayesian framing entirely. This is precisely the kind of work NIPS should encourage—it doesn't just offer an incremental improvement but introduces a new way of thinking about a hard problem. The weaknesses, primarily the greedy nature of the search, are acknowledged and represent a reasonable engineering trade-off for the achieved performance. This paper will likely be widely used and will inspire further research into efficient and scalable Bayesian methods for structured data.
