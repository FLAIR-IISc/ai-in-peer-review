- Proposes BHCD: a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering for networks using a tree-consistent partition prior and conjugate beta–Bernoulli block likelihoods.  
- Computes p(D|T) with a dynamic programming recursion that is linear in edges given T, and builds T by greedy agglomeration using local Bayes factor merge scores.  
- Introduces three merge types (join and two absorptions) and a “sparse” constraint disallowing merges between disconnected components.  
- Claims large runtime advantages over IRM with worst-case inference O(n^2 log n) and likelihood evaluation O(n+m), independent of number of communities.  
- Novelty: adapts Bayesian rose trees to relational data with a conjugate marginal likelihood and tree-consistent prior enabling an exact DP and fast greedy merges.  
- Missing discussion and empirical comparison to hierarchical random graphs (HRG) and nested stochastic blockmodels, which are conceptually very close.  
- Technical setup and recursion (Eq. 10) are plausible and well-specified, with clear factorization and sufficient-statistics caching.  
- Bayes factor merge scoring and O(1) inner-loop updates are reasonable given the forest decomposition, but no proof sketch for the DP or update claims is provided.  
- Complexity claims are argued but lack space-complexity analysis and more rigorous convergence/stability analysis for greedy search.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: main quantitative comparison only to IRM on one dataset and indirect comparisons to LFRM/ILA; no HRG/nested SBM baselines.  
- No hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, no ablation of merge types, and the “sparse” variant is treated ad hoc with only qualitative discussion.  
- Link prediction results are promising but lack statistical significance tests and broader dataset validation.  
- Clarity is generally good with clear equations and notation, but some terminology (e.g., “BHCD global”) and prior-model relationships need clarification.  
- Originality is moderate: useful technical combination but the high-level idea of tree-structured hierarchical communities is not new.  
- Significance is moderate: method is practical and scalable with interpretable hierarchies, but stronger empirical comparisons are needed to claim state-of-the-art modeling advances.  
- Pros: principled model with exact marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions, elegant fast greedy inference, strong runtime advantage, and interpretable/sparse options.  
- Cons: lacks comparison to HRG/nested SBMs, limited experiments and indirect baselines, no hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis, and absent theoretical guarantees for greedy approximation.  
- Suggested improvements: compare empirically to HRG/nested SBMs, add sensitivity and ablation studies for hyperparameters and merge types, provide derivations/proofs and memory usage, clarify predictive complexity, and consider simple hyperparameter priors and extensions.  
- Overall: solid, practically useful contribution with good clarity but moderate originality and significance; recommend stronger empirical positioning and analysis before acceptance (borderline/weak reject as is).
