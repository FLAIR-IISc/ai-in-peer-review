**Summary**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative clustering method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to combine the computational efficiency of bottom-up hierarchical clustering with a principled Bayesian model selection criterion. Starting with each node as a separate community, the algorithm iteratively merges the pair of communities (or sub-trees) that results in the largest increase in marginal likelihood, calculated via a Bayes factor. The underlying generative model defines a probability distribution over the network's adjacency matrix given a tree structure, marginalizing over all partitions consistent with that tree. This marginalization is made tractable through dynamic programming. The authors demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster—by orders of magnitude—than MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving comparable predictive performance on link prediction tasks.

The work builds directly on the framework of Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) and Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al., 2012), adapting it from i.i.d. data to relational data. The key contribution is the formulation of a network-specific likelihood based on the stochastic blockmodel, which allows the BHC framework to be applied to community detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Positioning:** The paper clearly identifies a critical gap in the literature: the trade-off between fast but non-probabilistic heuristic methods (e.g., Newman-Girvan) and principled but computationally expensive Bayesian models (e.g., hierarchical IRM extensions). BHCD presents a compelling solution that occupies a valuable middle ground.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength is its speed. The O(n^2 log n) complexity is a significant improvement over MCMC methods, whose runtime depends on the number of iterations and often scales poorly with the number of communities. The experimental results in Figure 4 provide a dramatic and convincing demonstration of this advantage, showing that BHCD obtains good results before a standard IRM sampler completes a single iteration.

3.  **Principled Greedy Approach:** While the algorithm is greedy, the merge criterion is not an ad-hoc heuristic like modularity. Instead, it is based on maximizing the marginal likelihood of the data, which is a well-founded Bayesian model selection principle. This provides a probabilistic interpretation of the resulting hierarchy.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It logically builds from the stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical model and the agglomerative inference scheme. The recursive definition for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant and clearly explained. Algorithm 1 and the description of the sufficient statistics caching are detailed enough for an expert to understand the implementation.

**Weaknesses and Questions**

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Search:** The most significant weakness is the nature of greedy search. The algorithm is deterministic (up to tie-breaking) and can get stuck in local optima. Once a merge is performed, it cannot be undone. While the authors mitigate this with multiple random-restart runs, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the potential pitfalls of this approach more explicitly. For example, how sensitive is the final hierarchy to the initial (random) tie-breaks? Are there specific network structures where this greedy approach is known to fail?

2.  **Originality in Context:** The algorithmic framework is heavily based on Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012). The main novelty is the adaptation of the marginal likelihood calculation for relational data using a blockmodel. While this is a valuable and non-trivial contribution, the paper could be more explicit about what is directly inherited from prior work and what constitutes the specific contribution for network data.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model relies on several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). The paper states they were set to fixed values that "work well." This raises questions about the model's sensitivity to these settings. A brief sensitivity analysis or discussion on how one might set these parameters in a principled way (e.g., empirical Bayes or cross-validation) would strengthen the work's practical utility.

4.  **Scalability on Sparse Networks:** The paper proposes a "BHCD sparse" variant, but the worst-case time complexity remains O(n^2 log n). The discussion notes a discrepancy between the O(n+m) likelihood computation and the O(n^2 log n) inference time. The reason for this—that the algorithm must still potentially consider O(n^2) pairs of trees to merge—is a crucial point. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why the inference doesn't scale better on sparse graphs and whether this is a fundamental limitation of the agglomerative approach or an area for future algorithmic improvement.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the marginal likelihood appears correct, and the connection to the BHC framework is solid. The experiments are well-designed, using standard datasets and comparing against a strong and relevant baseline (IRM). The results robustly support the central claim of speed with comparable accuracy.
*   **Clarity:** As mentioned, clarity is a major strength. The writing is excellent. A minor suggestion would be to explicitly define `ancestorT(φ)` and `subtreeT(φ)` in the text accompanying Equation 7 for readers less familiar with the BHC literature.
*   **Originality:** The work represents a novel and effective combination of existing ideas (agglomerative clustering, Bayesian model selection, stochastic blockmodels) to solve an important problem. It is a direct and successful application of the BHC paradigm to the network domain, which is a significant contribution in its own right.
*   **Significance:** The significance is high. BHCD provides a practical, scalable, and principled tool for exploratory analysis of network data. For practitioners who find MCMC-based methods too slow for large networks, this work offers a highly attractive alternative. It is likely to be widely used and built upon, particularly for its balance of speed and probabilistic rigor.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution. It addresses a well-defined problem with an elegant and effective solution. The empirical results are compelling, and the work will be of significant interest to researchers and practitioners working on network analysis and community detection. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations inherent to the greedy approach or areas for future refinement, rather than fundamental flaws in the presented work.
