

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for discovering community structure in social networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The authors build upon existing work on stochastic blockmodels and hierarchical clustering, claiming their method achieves a two orders of magnitude speedup over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The paper presents a novel greedy algorithm for hierarchical community detection that uses a Bayesian nonparametric approach.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong mathematical foundation. The algorithm is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the theoretical development of the model is sound. The authors correctly identify the computational limitations of existing Bayesian hierarchical approaches (e.g., [8, 9, 10]) and present a compelling argument for the need for more efficient methods. The time complexity analysis (O(n² log n)) is well-supported, and the caching scheme for efficient computation is a technical contribution worth noting.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses that undermine its claims and scientific contribution:

1. **Insufficient Experimental Validation**: The paper claims BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" but provides minimal evidence for this claim. The speed comparison in Table 1 is limited to a single dataset (NIPS-234), with no analysis of how the speedup scales with network size or sparsity. The authors fail to compare with other hierarchical community detection methods (e.g., [8, 9, 10]) that they cite as limitations of existing work, making the claimed speedup appear unsubstantiated.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper only compares to IRM, which is a flat community detection method, not a hierarchical one. This is a fundamental flaw in the evaluation, as the authors are claiming to solve a hierarchical community detection problem, yet they don't compare with other hierarchical approaches. The paper cites [8, 9, 10] as previous hierarchical methods but doesn't show how BHCD compares to them in terms of both speed and performance.

3. **Lack of Rigorous Validation of Community Quality**: The Sampson's monastery example shows some reasonable results, but the paper doesn't provide any quantitative metrics for community quality (e.g., normalized mutual information, modularity). The claim that "BHCD finds the most likely tree" is unverified without showing how it compares to ground truth or other metrics.

4. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims BHCD "attains comparable performance" to IRM but only provides limited evidence through log predictive probability on one dataset. The statement that "BHCD is able to make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan" is misleading without showing actual predictive performance metrics.

5. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper fails to address key limitations of the greedy approach, such as potential local optima and sensitivity to the initial forest. The paper mentions that BHCD sparse "only needs to perform computations on the parts of the network with edges present" but doesn't discuss how this affects the quality of the discovered communities.

6. **Questionable Bayesian Claims**: While the paper uses Bayesian terminology, it doesn't provide a full Bayesian treatment. The method relies on greedy model selection rather than proper Bayesian inference, and the paper doesn't discuss hyperparameter selection or Bayesian model averaging for predictions. This undermines the "Bayesian" label.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a technically sound algorithm with reasonable computational complexity, the lack of rigorous experimental validation against appropriate baselines and the overstatement of the method's contributions make it difficult to assess the true significance of the work. The paper claims to advance the state of the art in hierarchical community detection but fails to demonstrate how it compares to existing hierarchical methods. The speedup claim is particularly problematic without evidence of how it scales across different network sizes and sparsity levels.

The paper's primary contribution appears to be an efficient implementation of a greedy approach to hierarchical community detection, which is a refinement of existing techniques rather than a fundamental advance. While the time complexity is well-analyzed, the paper doesn't convincingly show that this method solves a problem that existing methods can't address more effectively.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper makes several strong claims that are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The experimental validation is limited to one dataset, lacks comparison with appropriate baselines, and doesn't provide enough detail to assess the quality of the discovered communities. As a skeptical reviewer who values rigorous validation, I find the evidence insufficient to support the paper's claims about speed and performance.

The authors should either provide more comprehensive experimental validation comparing with hierarchical community detection methods (not just flat community methods), or substantially revise their claims to better reflect the actual contribution of the work. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet the quality standards expected at NIPS.
