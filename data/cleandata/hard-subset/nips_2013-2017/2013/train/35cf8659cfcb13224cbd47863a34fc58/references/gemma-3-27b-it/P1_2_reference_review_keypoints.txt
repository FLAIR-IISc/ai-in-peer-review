- Introduces BHCD: a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method that efficiently computes marginal likelihoods for scalable community discovery.  
- Bridges gap between parametric SBMs and nonparametric IRM by offering a Bayesian approach with practical computational cost.  
- Well-positioned relative to prior work with clear extensions to relational data and comprehensive citations.  
- Major strength: computational efficiency (O(n^2 log n), BHCD sparse potentially O(n+m)) and large speedups versus IRM.  
- Bayesian formulation provides principled uncertainty quantification and incorporation of prior knowledge.  
- Recovers interpretable hierarchical community structure via a tree representation.  
- Algorithm is clearly described, using caching of sufficient statistics and practical optimization strategies.  
- Empirical validation on Sampson’s Monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks shows competitive performance and significant speedups.  
- BHCD sparse demonstrates practical trade-offs for sparse networks between accuracy and computational cost.  
- Weakness: insufficient hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (α, β, δ, λ, γ).  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence or optimality of the greedy algorithm.  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison to non-Bayesian methods (e.g., spectral clustering, Louvain).  
- Weakness: link prediction section is brief and lacks implementation/evaluation details for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: potential scalability bottleneck in likelihood computation for sparse networks noted but not fully resolved.  
- Paper is technically sound and claims are well-supported by experiments.  
- Generally clear and well-organized, though some mathematical derivations could be simplified.  
- Originality: novel combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering and relational modeling with an efficient inference scheme.  
- Significance: likely to advance community detection for large networks and be useful to practitioners.  
- For acceptance: significant speedups, principled Bayesian hierarchy, clear algorithmic contributions, strong empirical results.  
- Against acceptance: need for hyperparameter studies, theoretical analysis, and broader method comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: accept.
