- Introduces BHCD: a Bayesian greedy agglomerative method for hierarchical community detection using a tree prior and conjugate blockmodels with exact marginal likelihood via dynamic programming.
- Uses a stopping probability πS (Bayesian Rose Trees) to encourage non-binary splits; predictive link probabilities computed as mixtures along tree paths.
- Inference is a bottom-up greedy merge (join/absorb) using local Bayes factors, cached sufficient statistics, O(n^2 log n) complexity, and a sparse variant that forbids merges with no edges.
- Experiments: qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery, predictive performance on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subgraph (comparable AUC to IRM faster, similar to LFR, below ILA), and a full NIPS hierarchy.
- Strength: clear probabilistic formulation combining BHC/Rose Trees with SBMs and exact DP marginalization.
- Strength: practical, scalable inference with careful caching and an attractive O(n^2 log n) runtime independent of community count.
- Strength: good predictive performance with much lower wall-clock time than IRM; sensible trade-off vs more expressive models.
- Strength: three-way merge (join/absorb) enables interpretable non-binary hierarchies.
- Strength: clean predictive distribution derivation with interpretable responsibilities.
- Strength: situates work relative to IRM, BHC/Rose Trees, and hierarchical IRM approaches.
- Weakness: single global between-block likelihood g(·) encourages assortative structure and may underfit disassortative or core–periphery patterns.
- Weakness: greedy local merges are myopic with no guarantees; restarts mitigate but their timing cost is unclear—need clarification on whether reported timings include R restarts.
- Weakness: fixed hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) without sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference; γ affects non-binarity and branching.
- Weakness: limited evaluation—comparisons mainly to IRM, indirect LFR/ILA comparisons; missing baselines (HRG, hierarchical IRM), synthetic recovery experiments, and explicit structural recovery metrics.
- Weakness: sparse variant may prevent discovering hierarchies connected only via higher-level structure; needs justification and ablation.
- Weakness: confusing descriptions of “BHCD global” and stale heap scores; variants and comparisons need clearer definitions.
- Clarity: overall well-written but needs Algorithm 1 included, explicit heap update policy, and symbol definitions at first use.
- Clarity: figures should be legible and self-contained; explain handling of missing/signed edges and state which variant (binary/non-binary, sparse/dense) results correspond to.
- Originality: novelty is mostly incremental—applying rose-tree ideas to relational SBMs and adding absorb/join moves with efficient caching; comparisons to HRG and hierarchical IRM should be expanded.
- Significance: method is simple, fast, interpretable, and fills a practical niche between heuristics and nonparametric Bayesian models.
- Questions: do timing curves include all R restarts; how are ties/random seeds handled and how sensitive are results to R?
- Questions: performance sensitivity to γ and effect of constraining to binary merges across datasets?
- Questions: can authors report synthetic hierarchical SBM recovery results versus hierarchical IRM/HRG?
- Questions: would allowing per-parent or small sets of between-block parameters improve fit without large speed loss?
- Pros for acceptance: solid probabilistic model, scalable algorithm, useful predictions, and interpretable hierarchies.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical breadth, modeling restrictions (single between-block parameter), greedy inference without guarantees, unclear restart timing, and some clarity/related-work gaps.
- Recommendation: Weak accept; valuable practical contribution but needs stronger evaluation, hyperparameter analysis, and clarity improvements.
