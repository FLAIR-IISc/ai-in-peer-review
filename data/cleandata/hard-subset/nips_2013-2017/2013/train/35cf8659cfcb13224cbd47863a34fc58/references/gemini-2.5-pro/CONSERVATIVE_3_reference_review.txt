**Summary**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach extends previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] to relational data. The core of the method is a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm. At each step, the algorithm merges the pair of clusters (or sub-trees) that provides the largest increase in the marginal likelihood of the data, calculated via a Bayes factor. The model defines a probability distribution over a restricted set of partitions that are consistent with a tree structure, allowing for exact and efficient calculation of the marginal likelihood via dynamic programming. The authors demonstrate that their method is significantly faster—by orders of magnitude—than MCMC-based inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a well-established Bayesian nonparametric model for flat community detection, while achieving comparable predictive performance on a co-authorship network.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound in its derivation. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood of a tree (Eq. 10) is a correct application of the principles from [14] to a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The caching scheme described for the sufficient statistics, enabling an O(1) update within the main loop, is a solid piece of algorithmic engineering that leads to the claimed O(n^2 log n) overall complexity.

However, the primary weakness of the paper lies in its core methodological choice: a greedy search. While the authors frame this as "model selection," it is fundamentally a heuristic search for a single point estimate (one tree) of the posterior distribution over hierarchies. This approach is susceptible to finding poor local optima and provides no characterization of posterior uncertainty. Established Bayesian methods, such as MCMC for the IRM [5, 6] or its hierarchical extensions [8], are designed to approximate the full posterior distribution, which is a much stronger and more principled form of inference. The paper's claim to be a "Bayesian" method is somewhat tenuous; it uses a Bayesian scoring function (the marginal likelihood) to guide a non-Bayesian search procedure.

Furthermore, the experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous. The comparison is made almost exclusively against the standard (flat) IRM. While demonstrating a speed advantage here is valuable, it is an incomplete comparison. For a paper proposing a *hierarchical* model, the most relevant baselines are other *hierarchical* community detection models, particularly probabilistic ones like the hierarchical IRM [8] or the Mondrian process [12]. The authors dismiss these due to computational cost, but without at least a small-scale comparison, it is impossible to assess whether BHCD's speed comes at a significant cost to model quality or the ability to find meaningful hierarchies. Quoting results from other papers (LFRM, ILA) is not a substitute for a direct, controlled comparison.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of building up from the stochastic blockmodel to their hierarchical formulation. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the connection to the prior work of Heller et al. is made explicit. The figures are illustrative and support the text well.

**Originality:**
The originality of this work is limited. The conceptual framework is almost entirely adopted from Heller and Ghahramani [13, 14]. The primary contribution is the application of this framework to relational data, which involves defining the appropriate likelihood for the on- and off-diagonal blocks (a standard SBM likelihood) and deriving the efficient updates for the associated sufficient statistics. While this is a useful and non-trivial extension, it does not represent a significant conceptual advance in probabilistic modeling or inference. The idea of using greedy agglomerative clustering for community detection is also well-established [3, 4]; the novelty here is the use of a specific Bayesian scoring function.

**Significance:**
The main significance of this work lies in its computational efficiency. A method that can produce reasonable hierarchical clusterings for networks on the order of thousands of nodes in seconds is of practical interest. This could be a useful tool for exploratory data analysis.

However, the significance to the machine learning research community is less clear. The paper prioritizes speed over inferential rigor by resorting to a greedy search. This trades the desirable properties of a full posterior approximation for a fast point estimate. In a field that has invested heavily in developing sophisticated inference techniques (MCMC, variational inference) to handle complex Bayesian models, a return to a simple greedy heuristic feels like a step backward, even if the scoring function is Bayesian. Without a more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art hierarchical models, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that it advances the state of the art in a meaningful way beyond speed. The proposed method is more of a fast heuristic with a probabilistic justification than a new contribution to Bayesian inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The method is extremely fast and scalable, showing a significant practical speed-up (two orders of magnitude) over a widely used Bayesian nonparametric baseline (IRM).
2.  The paper is clearly written and the algorithm is well-described, making the work easy to understand and potentially reproduce.
3.  The empirical results, though limited in terms of baselines, show that the method achieves competitive predictive performance against the flat IRM.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Methodological Weakness:** The greedy search is a major limitation. It finds only a point estimate, is prone to local optima, and is a poor approximation of a true Bayesian posterior over structures.
2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The paper fails to compare against the most relevant baselines: other probabilistic hierarchical community detection models. This makes it impossible to judge the quality of the discovered hierarchies or to properly contextualize the speed/accuracy trade-off.
3.  **Limited Originality:** The work is a straightforward application of a previously published method [13, 14] to a standard network model (SBM). The conceptual novelty is low.
4.  The "sparse" variant, while empirically effective, feels ad-hoc and lacks a strong probabilistic justification. It is a heuristic layered on top of another heuristic.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a fast and practically useful algorithm, its methodological contributions are incremental, and its reliance on a greedy heuristic is a significant drawback from a Bayesian inference perspective. The lack of comparison to appropriate hierarchical baselines is a critical flaw in the experimental evaluation.
