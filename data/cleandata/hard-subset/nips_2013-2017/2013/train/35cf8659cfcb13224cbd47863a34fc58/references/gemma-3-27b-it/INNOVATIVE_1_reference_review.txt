This paper presents a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for community discovery in networks, offering a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM. The core idea – leveraging a hierarchical structure defined by a tree to efficiently calculate marginal likelihoods – is genuinely novel and a potentially significant contribution. The authors build upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering but adapt it successfully to relational data, a non-trivial extension. The speed-up reported – two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration – is impressive.

The paper is well-structured, and the mathematical derivations, while dense, appear sound. The authors clearly explain the core concepts and the algorithm. The experiments, including application to Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data, are relevant and provide evidence for the method’s effectiveness. The comparison to the IRM is particularly compelling, showcasing the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The ablation studies with sparse BHCD variants are also valuable.

However, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. The discussion of hyperparameter selection is minimal. While the authors state they found specific values to work well, a more systematic exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial. The connection to existing work on stochastic blockmodels is slightly superficial; a deeper engagement with the literature on blockmodel limitations and extensions might provide a stronger justification for the proposed method. While the authors acknowledge the potential for non-conjugate priors, they don’t delve into why they chose conjugate priors beyond computational efficiency. A brief discussion of the trade-offs involved would be useful. Finally, the explanation of predictive probabilities, while present, feels somewhat tacked on and could be expanded with more illustrative examples. The section detailing model variations (sparse, binary) is also a bit fragmented and could be streamlined.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of using a tree-based prior to enable efficient computation of marginal likelihoods in hierarchical community discovery is highly novel. This is not a minor tweak; it's a different way of framing the problem.
*   **Efficiency:** The reported speed-up compared to IRM is substantial and addresses a significant bottleneck in Bayesian network analysis.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical framework appears solid, and the derivations are clearly presented.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the method's ability to discover meaningful community structure and achieve competitive predictive performance.
*   **Potential Impact:** A fast, scalable Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery could have broad applicability across various domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Hyperparameter Analysis:** The lack of a thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity is a weakness.
*   **Shallow Literature Engagement:** The connection to related work, particularly in stochastic blockmodeling, could be deeper.
*   **Predictive Section Could be Expanded:** The explanation of the predictive probabilities feels a bit cursory.
*   **Some Disjointed Sections:** Model variations section is a bit fragmented.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor shortcomings, this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of community discovery. The proposed method is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated. The potential impact of this work is considerable. I value novelty highly, and this paper delivers on that front. I believe the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and recommend acceptance. While further refinement and more extensive analysis would be beneficial, the core idea and its initial implementation are compelling enough to warrant inclusion in NIPS. I'd encourage the authors to address the hyperparameter sensitivity and literature engagement points in a future version.
