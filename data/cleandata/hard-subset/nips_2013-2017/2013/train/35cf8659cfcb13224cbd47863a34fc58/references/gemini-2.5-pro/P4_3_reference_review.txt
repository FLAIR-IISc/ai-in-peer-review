### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The approach extends previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering, specifically Bayesian Rose Trees [14], to the domain of relational data. The model defines a prior over partitions of network vertices that are consistent with a hierarchical tree structure. This constrained set of partitions allows for the exact and efficient computation of the tree's marginal likelihood, which is derived from a stochastic blockmodel [1] likelihood. The likelihood function distinguishes between intra-community edges (on-diagonal blocks) and inter-community edges (off-diagonal blocks) by using separate Beta-Bernoulli priors, a common technique in Bayesian community detection [7].

Inference is performed via a bottom-up greedy search. The algorithm starts with a forest where each vertex is a singleton tree and iteratively merges the pair of trees that yields the highest Bayes factor, until a single tree encompassing all vertices is formed. A key contribution is an efficient implementation that caches sufficient statistics, allowing the algorithm to achieve a time complexity of O(n^2 log n), which is notably independent of the number of inferred communities. This contrasts with many existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for network analysis, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5, 6], which typically rely on more computationally intensive MCMC or variational inference and whose complexity often scales with the number of communities. The authors evaluate BHCD qualitatively on Sampson's monastery network and quantitatively on the NIPS co-authorship dataset, comparing its speed and predictive accuracy primarily against the IRM.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is a well-founded extension of the framework presented in [13, 14] to relational data using a standard stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Eq. 10) is correct and follows from the model's generative process. The claims regarding computational complexity are well-supported by the description of the algorithm and its caching mechanism. The experimental evaluation is solid, providing both qualitative and quantitative evidence for the method's utility. The comparison against IRM, a prominent Bayesian nonparametric baseline, is appropriate and effectively demonstrates the primary contribution of BHCD: achieving comparable predictive performance at a fraction of the computational cost.

A potential point of confusion lies in the experimental results for the Sampson monastery network. The paper states that the sparse variant of BHCD empirically produces better results, yet for the presented example, its log-likelihood (-22.80) is substantially lower than that of the standard BHCD (-6.62). As the optimization objective is to maximize this likelihood, this result is contradictory and requires clarification. It is unclear if this is a typographical error or if it highlights a subtlety in the model's behavior or evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It begins by situating the work within the existing literature on community detection, clearly articulating the limitations of prior methods that it aims to address. The model is built up logically from the base stochastic blockmodel to the full hierarchical formulation. The inference algorithm is described in sufficient detail, and the recursive equation for the marginal likelihood is presented clearly. The figures and tables effectively support the paper's claims, especially the plots comparing the runtime and performance of BHCD against the IRM. The exposition is sufficient for an expert to understand the method's core mechanics.

**Originality:** The work's primary originality lies in the novel combination and application of existing techniques. While greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and stochastic blockmodels [1] are not new, their synthesis into a fast, scalable algorithm for hierarchical community discovery is a novel contribution. The paper clearly delineates how its approach differs from previous Bayesian methods for network analysis, particularly in its use of greedy model selection over MCMC-based inference, which leads to significant computational advantages. The related work section is comprehensive and correctly positions the contribution within the field.

**Significance:** The proposed method is of high practical significance. Scalability is a major challenge for Bayesian network models, often limiting their application to small or medium-sized graphs. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster than a key competitor like the IRM, this work makes principled Bayesian hierarchical analysis more accessible for larger networks. Researchers and practitioners in fields like social science, biology, and machine learning who analyze network data are likely to find this method useful. The trade-off of optimality for speed is a pragmatic one, and BHCD represents a valuable point in this design space. It advances the state of the art by demonstrating that a greedy Bayesian approach can be competitive with more computationally demanding inference schemes.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Practical Impact:** The method offers a significant speedup (two orders of magnitude in experiments) over standard Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM, addressing a critical need for scalable network analysis tools.
2.  **Novel Combination:** It successfully and non-trivially combines Bayesian hierarchical clustering with stochastic blockmodels, creating a new and useful tool for hierarchical community detection.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-defined, and the inference algorithm is principled within a greedy model selection framework. The complexity analysis is correct.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally clear and easy to follow, making the contribution accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limitations of Greedy Search:** The core inference method is a greedy algorithm, which is not guaranteed to find a globally optimal hierarchy. The paper mitigates this with random restarts but does not deeply analyze the potential sub-optimality of the solutions.
2.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the comparison to the IRM is strong and relevant, the evaluation could be strengthened by including comparisons to other modern scalable community detection methods, including non-Bayesian hierarchical approaches.
3.  **Inconsistency in Results:** The reported log-likelihoods for the Sampson monastery network seem to contradict the claim that the sparse variant of the model performs better, which could indicate an error or requires clarification.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly its contribution to scalability and its practical significance, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete and valuable piece of research.
