- Proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD): greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering for relational data using a tree prior over partitions and exact marginalization via blockmodel marginals.
- Introduces a dynamic programming recursion to compute p(D|T) exactly over tree-consistent partitions using on-/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods (f, g).
- Learns hierarchy with bottom-up agglomerative model selection using three merge types scored by a local Bayes factor computed from cached sufficient statistics.
- Presents a sparse variant that forbids merges between disconnected trees and a top-down mixture scheme for link prediction along root-to-leaf paths.
- Empirical results show sensible hierarchies on Sampson’s monastery and competitive link prediction on NIPS-234 versus IRM, with substantially lower runtime; performance comparable to LFRM and below ILA.
- Builds on Bayesian rose trees and hierarchical clustering literature, replacing i.i.d. likelihoods with stochastic blockmodel marginals and redefining f/g for relational data.
- Trades full posterior inference of IRM/hIRM for greedy model selection and a restricted tree-based prior, yielding large speedups.
- Distinct from classical graph hierarchical clustering (e.g., Girvan-Newman) and flat Bayesian link models (LFRM, ILA); needs clearer positioning versus hierarchical random graphs and Mondrian-process relational models.
- Strength: clear probabilistic formulation with interpretable stopping prior πS and an exact, well-motivated dynamic program.
- Strength: efficient inference with O(1) merge updates via caching and worst-case O(n^2 log n) complexity independent of community count; practical C implementation and restarts.
- Strength: practical predictive distribution and demonstrations on large NIPS datasets supporting runtime claims.
- Weakness: limited expressivity due to Beta-Bernoulli blockmodel without degree correction, making it weak on degree-heterogeneous, assortative/disassortative, or overlapping-membership networks.
- Weakness: single global hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) fixed a priori with no hyperparameter learning or sensitivity analysis; γ influences tree arity but is not learned.
- Weakness: greedy local merges are myopic with no approximation guarantees; restarts mitigate but indicate instability and lack of ablation on restart/merge choices.
- Weakness: sparse variant’s prohibition of merges with zero observed edges can fail under missing-not-at-random data or for bipartite/disassortative structures.
- Weakness: evaluation issues — the “two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration” claim is unclear and comparisons omit hierarchical baselines like hierarchical SBMs/dendrogram models.
- Weakness: missing uncertainty quantification for predictive metrics (no error bars) and limited experimental protocol details (held-out fraction, missingness handling, restarts per fold).
- Clarity issues: Algorithm 1 is referenced but not fully presented; notation is dense and could use worked examples (e.g., σ¬chSS) and clearer pseudocode.
- Quality assessment: technically sound recursion and Bayes factor consistent with generative story; caching for O(1) updates is a useful contribution.
- Originality: moderate — main novelty is adapting Bayesian rose trees to relational data with blockmodel marginals and engineering for speed.
- Significance: practical significance is good (fast, interpretable hierarchical discovery); theoretical significance is modest due to simple likelihood and greedy search.
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to hyperparameters and learning γ; performance on degree-heterogeneous/disassortative graphs and potential for degree-corrected marginals; variability across restarts and confidence intervals; clarification of runtime claim; comparisons to hierarchical SBMs/dendrogram models.
- Pros: elegant Bayesian formulation, very fast inference, competitive predictive performance with lower compute, and meaningful hierarchies on real data.
- Cons: limited model expressivity, greedy search with limited stability analysis, and incomplete comparisons and experimental detail.
- Recommendation: borderline accept — technically sound and practical but would be strengthened by broader comparisons, degree correction, and hyperparameter inference.
