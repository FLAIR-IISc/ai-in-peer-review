

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper introduces BHCD, a novel Bayesian hierarchical clustering approach for community discovery in social networks. The authors address a significant limitation in existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (like the IRM), which suffer from poor scalability due to inefficient inference algorithms. BHCD builds upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, introducing a caching scheme for sufficient statistics that enables O(n² log n) time complexity—two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The key novelty lies in the efficient inference algorithm (Algorithm 1), which leverages dynamic programming and caching to avoid the quadratic dependence on the number of communities that plagues previous methods. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves significant speedups while preserving the Bayesian framework's advantages, such as probabilistic interpretation of community structure. The paper makes a strong case for BHCD's significance by showing it can make reasonable predictions before IRM completes a single Gibbs scan on the NIPS co-authorship dataset.

The paper's strengths are compelling. The problem statement is well-motivated, with clear identification of the scalability bottleneck in current Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods. The technical contribution—particularly the caching scheme for sufficient statistics—is novel and elegantly implemented. The experimental results on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset demonstrate that BHCD discovers meaningful hierarchical structures while offering dramatic speed improvements. The authors also thoughtfully explore variations (e.g., BHCD sparse) and acknowledge limitations (e.g., the beta-Bernoulli restriction).

The paper's main weakness is the limited comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, which might be faster than both BHCD and IRM. While the paper acknowledges that "greedy hierarchical clustering has previously been applied," it doesn't directly compare BHCD's speed to these approaches, which would strengthen the significance claim. Additionally, the paper's speedup claim ("two orders of magnitude faster than one iteration of IRM") would be more convincing with concrete runtime comparisons for large networks rather than just asymptotic complexity.

However, these are minor concerns given the paper's strong novelty and significance. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and suggest promising future directions, including non-conjugate likelihoods and triangle-based sufficient statistics. The paper's technical quality is high, with a clear mathematical formulation and well-structured algorithm. The experimental design is sound, using standard benchmarks and comparison methods.

What makes this paper particularly compelling for NIPS is the bold approach to addressing a fundamental scalability problem in Bayesian network analysis. While many papers focus on incremental improvements to existing methods, BHCD presents a fundamentally different inference strategy that enables practical application of Bayesian hierarchical community detection to larger networks. This represents a significant step toward making Bayesian approaches viable for real-world network analysis at scale.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and adequately references related work. The comparison to prior work (IRM, Mondrian process, greedy hierarchical clustering) is thorough, and the paper clearly articulates how BHCD differs from these approaches.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a scalable Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery. The novel inference algorithm addresses a critical bottleneck in the field, and the experimental results demonstrate both the method's practical utility and theoretical soundness. While some aspects of the comparison could be strengthened, the paper's novelty, significance, and technical quality make it an excellent candidate for NIPS.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**:
- Novel inference algorithm with significant scalability improvements
- Clear demonstration of practical speedup over existing methods
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-motivated Bayesian approach
- Comprehensive experimental validation on real-world datasets
- Thoughtful exploration of method variants and limitations

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods
- Some speedup claims would benefit from more concrete runtime data
- Limited discussion of trade-offs between binary and non-binary hierarchies

The paper's bold approach to a fundamental scalability problem in Bayesian network analysis, combined with its strong experimental validation, makes it a valuable contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in scalable community detection and opens promising directions for future work.
