Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy Bayesian method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model places a tree T over vertices, with each internal node corresponding to a community; the set of partitions consistent with T define a mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Using conjugate Beta–Bernoulli likelihoods with different priors for within- and between-community blocks (f and g), the authors derive a dynamic-programming recursion for the marginal likelihood p(D|T) that sums over all tree-consistent partitions. They then perform bottom-up agglomerative model selection, using local Bayes factors to choose among three merge operations (join and two absorb variants, as in Bayesian Rose Trees), with caching to achieve O(1) per inner-loop update and overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant forbids merging trees with no edges between them. Prediction for missing edges is computed via a recursion down the tree.

Experiments include qualitative structure for Sampson’s monastery, quantitative link prediction on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset versus IRM (both MCMC and greedy search), and a large-scale qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS dataset (2864 nodes). Results show BHCD is much faster than IRM and achieves comparable predictive performance to LFRM and worse than ILA (via numbers from prior work), while being substantially faster.

The work sits at the intersection of stochastic blockmodels (SBM), IRM and its hierarchical variants, and Bayesian hierarchical clustering (BHC) / Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT). The novelty is extending BHC/BRT’s dynamic program to a relational (blockmodel) likelihood and designing a merge-score/caching scheme that makes inference practical at scale.

Quality
- Modeling: The mixture over tree-consistent partitions with a simple, interpretable prior (πS) is well-motivated and yields a clean recursion (Eq. 10). Using separate within/between marginal likelihoods f and g is a reasonable way to bias towards assortative structure.
- Inference: The agglomerative, locally evaluated Bayes factor and caching are sound and clearly explained; the join/absorb options follow prior work on rose trees.
- Predictive computation is straightforward from the recursion, though the stated complexity “Θ(n)” seems to contradict “linear in depth of the tree”; it should be O(depth).
- Empirics: The speedups are compelling and the qualitative structure looks sensible. However, quantitative evaluation is limited (one main dataset, no error bars), and baselines for hierarchical models (e.g., hierarchical IRM, Mondrian-based models) are not compared due to cost; this leaves open how much of the gain is from modeling vs. heuristic speed.

Important technical concerns:
- Hyperparameters: The text states α>β and δ<λ to favor within over between edges, but experiments set α=δ=1.0 and β=λ=0.2. This makes f and g identical, removing the intended assortative prior. Either this is a typo or undermines the core modeling assumption. Please clarify and report sensitivity/learned hyperparameters.
- The “sparse” variant forbids merges with zero inter-tree edges; this is a practical speed heuristic but changes the posterior and could fail on disassortative/bipartite/core–periphery graphs. A discussion of when this is safe and a synthetic stress test would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with precise notation and references to BHC/BRT. Suggestions:
- Fix the hyperparameter inconsistency and explicitly justify πS=1−(1−γ)|ch(S)|.
- Correct the predictive complexity statement and give a brief derivation of Eq. (10) in the appendix.
- Provide memory complexity and exact operations maintained in the cache/heap; describe how stale heap entries are handled.
- Some references lack years and details.

Originality
The main novelty is adapting BHC/BRT to a relational likelihood and designing the merge scoring/caching for SBMs. This is a meaningful extension that enables practical hierarchical community discovery with a Bayesian objective, though conceptually it builds on known ingredients (SBM, BHC/BRT).

Significance
The method is attractive for practitioners who need fast, interpretable hierarchical structure with predictive capability. The speed is a genuine contribution. However, the absence of hyperparameter learning, uncertainty quantification, and broader baselines limits the immediate scientific impact. Adding degree correction or showing robustness to degree heterogeneity would increase impact.

Pros
- Elegant dynamic program for p(D|T) and efficient agglomerative search.
- Very fast inference; practical on thousands of nodes.
- Clear connection to BHC/BRT and SBMs; interpretable hierarchies.
- Simple, effective prediction procedure.

Cons
- Apparent hyperparameter inconsistency; lack of sensitivity analysis/learning.
- Greedy search with tie-breaking and restarts; limited posterior uncertainty.
- Sparse variant is heuristic and may bias results.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and missing comparisons to other hierarchical Bayesian network models; no error bars.
- No tests on disassortative/bipartite or degree-heterogeneous graphs.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the intended α,β,δ,λ; if f=g in experiments, explain why results still show assortative structure. Consider learning hyperparameters (e.g., via Empirical Bayes).
- Report variability across restarts (R), and add error bars over CV folds.
- Add synthetic experiments spanning assortative, disassortative, bipartite, and degree-heterogeneous regimes; discuss failure modes.
- Compare (even on small graphs) to at least one hierarchical Bayesian baseline (e.g., hierarchical IRM or Mondrian-process–based model) to separate modeling from speed effects.
- Consider a split/merge refinement pass after agglomeration, or a limited MCMC over trees, to mitigate greediness.
- Discuss or experiment with degree-corrected blockmodels within your framework.
- Correct the “two orders faster than a single IRM iteration” claim if it is a misstatement; the time plots suggest “than many IRM iterations.”

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a neat and potentially useful method with strong computational advantages and clear ideas. However, the apparent hyperparameter issue, limited quantitative evaluation, and lack of robustness analysis/baselines make it premature for acceptance. Addressing these points would make a strong resubmission.

Confidence: High on methodological assessment; medium on empirical comparisons due to limited details provided.
