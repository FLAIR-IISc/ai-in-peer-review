This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to extend the framework of Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering, previously developed for i.i.d. data, to the relational setting of network analysis. The authors define a probabilistic model over tree-structured hierarchies where the marginal likelihood of the data given a specific tree, p(D|T), is calculated by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. This marginalization is made computationally tractable through a dynamic programming recursion. Inference is performed not via traditional MCMC, but through a fast, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm. At each step, this greedy algorithm merges the pair of sub-trees that provides the largest increase in a local Bayes factor score. The paper positions this work as a practical and scalable alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and its hierarchical extensions, which are often computationally prohibitive due to slow MCMC inference.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its bold and novel approach to a long-standing problem. While Bayesian nonparametric models for network structure offer great flexibility, their practical application has been severely limited by computational cost. This paper directly confronts this issue by making a clever and pragmatic trade-off: it sacrifices the theoretical purity of full posterior inference for a massive gain in speed and scalability.

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The synthesis of Bayesian hierarchical clustering with stochastic blockmodels is a novel contribution to the network analysis literature. Instead of another incremental improvement on MCMC sampling for an existing model, the authors propose a fundamentally different inference paradigm for this problem class. The resulting method is not just an algorithm but a complete probabilistic model that allows for principled model selection and prediction. The claimed "two orders of magnitude" speed-up over even a single IRM iteration is a dramatic and highly significant result that could enable the application of Bayesian hierarchical methods to datasets that were previously out of reach.

2.  **Bold Direction:** The choice to pursue a greedy model selection approach over a more "correct" but intractable MCMC approach is commendable. This work challenges the community's reliance on sampling as the default inference tool for complex Bayesian models and shows that well-designed deterministic approximations can offer a compelling balance of speed and performance. This is a direction that NIPS should encourage.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very clearly written. The authors do an excellent job of motivating their approach by outlining the limitations of prior work. The model, the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10), and the greedy inference algorithm are all explained concisely and effectively. The connection to prior work like Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) is made explicit, which helps situate the contribution.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly support the paper's main claims. The qualitative results on the classic Sampson's monastery dataset show that BHCD finds meaningful and interpretable hierarchies. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS co-authorship data is the most compelling part of the evaluation, clearly demonstrating that BHCD variants can achieve predictive performance comparable or superior to the IRM in a tiny fraction of the time. The ability to scale to the full NIPS dataset further underscores the practical utility of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

While the paper's strengths are considerable, there are some weaknesses, though in my view they are acceptable in light of the contribution.

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The most significant methodological limitation is the greedy nature of the search algorithm. The final hierarchy is a single point estimate, highly dependent on the path of merges taken. The authors acknowledge this and use multiple random restarts to mitigate the issue, but the method does not capture uncertainty over the tree structure itself, which is a key feature of fully Bayesian approaches. However, this is the central trade-off the paper makes for speed, and it is a well-justified one.

2.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experimental comparison is primarily against the IRM. While this is a crucial and well-chosen baseline for a flat clustering method, the paper would have been stronger with a comparison to other Bayesian *hierarchical* models mentioned in the introduction (e.g., [8], [12]). Even a small-scale comparison demonstrating the computational challenges of those methods would have further highlighted the significance of BHCD's efficiency.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model's hyperparameters are set to fixed values that are reported to "work well". A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these settings, or a strategy for setting them, would have been beneficial for practitioners looking to apply this method.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

**Justification:**

This paper presents a significant and exciting contribution. It introduces a novel, principled, and highly scalable method for hierarchical community detection. The core idea is elegant, and the empirical results are compelling. The work's primary value lies in its bold departure from the standard, slow-moving MCMC paradigm, offering a practical path forward for Bayesian network analysis on larger datasets. The weaknesses, primarily the greedy nature of the inference, are an explicit and well-reasoned part of the methodological trade-off for computational efficiency. This paper is likely to be influential, inspiring further work on fast, deterministic approximations for complex Bayesian models. It prioritizes a novel and impactful direction over incremental methodological perfection, making it an excellent fit for NIPS.
