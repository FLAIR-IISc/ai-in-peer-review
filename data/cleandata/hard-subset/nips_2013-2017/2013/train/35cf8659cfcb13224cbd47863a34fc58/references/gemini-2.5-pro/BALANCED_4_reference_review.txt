### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2014) to relational data, which is a key contribution. The model defines a probability distribution over tree structures, where the marginal likelihood of a tree is computed by summing over all tree-consistent partitions. This summation is made tractable via a dynamic programming recursion. Inference is performed using a bottom-up, greedy agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees based on the local Bayes factor, aiming to find the tree structure that best explains the observed network data. The authors propose this method as a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which often rely on slow MCMC inference. Experiments on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network demonstrate that BHCD can discover sensible hierarchies and achieve competitive predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than a standard IRM implementation.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Contribution:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated combination of existing ideas: applying the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to the problem of community detection. Extending this framework from i.i.d. data to relational data using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood is a significant and non-trivial contribution. This provides a principled, model-based justification for a greedy agglomerative algorithm, which is an improvement over purely heuristic methods.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** A major strength of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. The greedy search, combined with an effective caching scheme for sufficient statistics, results in an O(n^2 log n) time complexity. The experimental results powerfully illustrate this advantage, showing that BHCD obtains reasonable results in seconds, whereas MCMC-based inference for the IRM requires thousands of seconds to converge. This makes the method a practical tool for analyzing moderately large networks where MCMC would be prohibitive.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It logically builds from the standard stochastic blockmodel (Section 2) to the hierarchical model (Section 3) and then to the inference algorithm (Section 4). The use of figures, particularly Figure 1, is effective in illustrating the concept of tree-consistent partitions and how the model explains the data. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is presented elegantly.

4.  **Sound Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed to showcase the method's strengths. The qualitative analysis on the classic Sampson's monastery dataset shows that BHCD finds meaningful and interpretable community structures. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 benchmark is strong, demonstrating the speed-accuracy trade-off against a relevant and widely used baseline (IRM). The final experiment on the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's ability to scale. The authors are also honest about the predictive performance relative to other state-of-the-art models (e.g., ILA).

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The primary weakness is the reliance on a greedy search algorithm. While fast, this approach is prone to finding local optima. The authors mitigate this by using multiple restarts, but the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations.
    *   **Suggestion:** Could the authors comment on the variance of the results across different random restarts? How sensitive is the final discovered hierarchy to the random tie-breaking in the initial stages? A brief discussion on potential failure modes of the greedy approach (e.g., situations where a locally optimal merge prevents a globally better structure from being found) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The merge score (Eq. 12) is computed locally, based only on the data within the sub-clusters being merged. This is key to the algorithm's efficiency but rests on the strong assumption that local connectivity is sufficient to determine local community structure. This may not hold in networks with more complex, long-range dependencies.
    *   **Suggestion:** Acknowledging this assumption more explicitly as a limitation and discussing its potential impact would be valuable. For instance, would this local view disadvantage the model on certain types of network structures compared to methods with a more global view, like MCMC?

3.  **Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The main empirical comparison is against the IRM. While this is a crucial baseline, the field of network modeling is broad. A comparison against other modern hierarchical community detection methods would provide a more complete picture of BHCD's place in the literature.
    *   **Suggestion:** While running extensive new experiments may be out of scope, a more thorough discussion of how BHCD compares conceptually to other hierarchical Bayesian models (e.g., hierarchical IRM [8], Mondrian process [12]) would be beneficial. Furthermore, including a comparison to a fast, non-Bayesian but popular hierarchical clustering algorithm (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004) would help contextualize the trade-offs between the principled Bayesian approach and purely algorithmic ones.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values. The performance of Bayesian models can often be sensitive to such choices.
    *   **Suggestion:** A small sensitivity analysis on a subset of the data, showing how the results (e.g., likelihood of the final tree or predictive accuracy) change with different hyperparameter settings, would significantly increase the paper's completeness and practical utility for other researchers wanting to apply the method.

5.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The term "BHCD global" appears in the caption for Figure 3 but is not defined in the main text. It is contrasted with "BHCD sparse". The text should clarify what this variant is and why its performance differs.
    *   The criterion used to prune the tree for visualization in the "Full NIPS" experiment is interesting but could be explained more intuitively for the reader.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, elegant, and computationally efficient method for hierarchical community detection. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a valuable contribution by successfully adapting the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational data. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's primary advantage: achieving competitive performance at a fraction of the computational cost of traditional Bayesian nonparametric approaches. While the greedy nature of the inference introduces limitations, the authors' approach is pragmatic and effective. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the scope of the discussion and experimental comparisons, which can be addressed to further strengthen the paper. Overall, the work is significant and likely to be influential and useful for practitioners and researchers working with network data.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
