Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for community detection in networks. The model defines a hierarchy (tree) whose internal nodes represent communities; each vertex belongs to all communities along the path to its leaf. Conditioned on a tree, the model induces a prior over partitions consistent with the tree and a likelihood that distinguishes intra-community edges from inter-community edges via conjugate Beta–Bernoulli marginal likelihoods. The marginal likelihood of a fixed tree is computed exactly by a dynamic-programming recursion analogous to Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (BHC) and Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT). Learning proceeds via agglomerative model selection: iteratively merge two subtrees using a Bayes factor score, considering three merge types (join and two absorptions as in BRT). A sparse variant forbids merging disconnected subgraphs. The authors provide a recursive predictive distribution for link prediction. They report O(n + m) time to evaluate a fixed tree’s likelihood and O(n^2 log n) time for the greedy search with caching, independent of the number of communities. Experiments include Sampson’s monastery, a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset compared against IRM, and a qualitative hierarchy for the full NIPS co-authorship network.

The work builds on stochastic blockmodels (SBM), mixed-membership SBM, IRM, and Bayesian clustering methods (BHC, BRT). It is close in spirit to hierarchical models for networks such as hierarchical random graphs/dendrogram models (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman, Nature 2008) and hierarchical SBMs (e.g., Herlau et al.), but differs by using a tree to define a mixture over tree-consistent partitions with conjugate intra/inter-block marginal likelihoods and greedy agglomeration for inference. Compared to nonparametric feature/attribute models (LFRM, ILA), BHCD emphasizes speed and simplicity.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity: The generative story (stop/split at each node with πS) and the resulting recursion p(DTT|T) = πT f(σTT) + (1 − πT) g(σ¬chTT) ∏C p(DCC|C) are clearly specified and directly extend BHC/BRT to relational data by separating intra- and inter-block marginal likelihoods.
- Computational efficiency: Exact tree likelihood evaluation by dynamic programming with cached sufficient statistics is well-argued, and the agglomerative search has a transparent O(n^2 log n) complexity. Independence from the number of communities is attractive in practice.
- Practical variants: The “sparse” variant that forbids merging disconnected sets is sensible for graphs and improves speed/accuracy in sparse settings.
- Empirical evidence of speed: On NIPS-234, BHCD reaches reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than an IRM implementation.
- Usable predictive mechanism: The link-prediction recursion is simple and linear in tree depth.

Weaknesses

Quality (technical soundness)
- Inference is greedy model selection rather than full Bayesian posterior inference over trees/partitions. Although restarts and predictive averaging over R trees mitigate this, the approach may get trapped in local optima and provides limited uncertainty quantification.
- The prior p(φ|T) uses πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| from BRT; there is no learning of γ or sensitivity analysis. Similarly, the Beta hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ) are fixed; no hyperparameter inference is provided, despite known impact on partitioning bias (e.g., within vs between edge density).
- The sparse variant introduces a hard constraint (no merges without edges) that is not derived from the prior/likelihood; it is pragmatic but less principled.
- Comparative evaluation: The IRM baseline is provided; for LFRM/ILA, the paper relies on published numbers and matching folds rather than a unified implementation/compute budget, which weakens conclusions about comparative accuracy.
- Claims about speed (“two orders faster than a single iteration of IRM”) need careful framing; the figures show BHCD achieves good accuracy in ~10s versus ~1000s for IRM, but “single iteration” is ambiguous and depends on implementation details.

Clarity
- Overall writing is clear, with concrete equations and an intelligible algorithm. However:
  - Several citations are incomplete; crucial related work on hierarchical random graphs/dendrogram models (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008, Nature; also nested/hierarchical SBMs) should be cited and contrasted explicitly.
  - Some notation is terse (e.g., σ¬chSS) and could benefit from a brief example to aid readers less familiar with BHC/BRT recursions.
  - The algorithm pseudocode is only sketched; memory requirements for cached sufficient statistics and heap maintenance could be more explicit.
  - Minor typos and duplicated punctuation occur.

Originality
- The main novelty is the adaptation of BHC/BRT to relational data via a mixture over tree-consistent partitions with distinct intra/inter marginal likelihoods, combined with an agglomerative search tailored to graphs. This is an incremental but useful contribution relative to prior hierarchical community models (HRG/hSBM) and BHC/BRT. The absorption (non-binary) merges follow BRT.
- Missing comparison to HRG/hSBM makes it harder to position the contribution; those approaches also induce dendrograms/hierarchies for networks and perform link prediction.

Significance
- The approach is fast and simple, and produces reasonable hierarchies and competitive predictive performance versus IRM at a fraction of the time. This can be valuable for exploratory analysis of medium-to-large sparse networks.
- However, the empirical scope is limited (two datasets; qualitative on full NIPS). There is no comprehensive study of scalability beyond ~3k nodes, robustness to hyperparameters, or comparisons to other fast hierarchical baselines (e.g., HRG, degree-corrected variants).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to networks with an exact tree marginal likelihood.
- Strong computational profile; practical algorithm with O(n^2 log n) runtime and O(n + m) tree likelihood.
- Fast and reasonable link prediction; good empirical speed vs IRM.
- Readable paper with actionable algorithmic details.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing hierarchical network models and BHC/BRT; missing key related work/context.
- Greedy inference with restarts, no posterior uncertainty or hyperparameter learning; ad hoc sparse variant.
- Modest experimental breadth and baseline coverage; reliance on literature numbers for some comparisons.
- Some clarity and citation issues.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Cite and compare to hierarchical random graphs/dendrogram models (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008, Nature) and hierarchical SBMs more directly; discuss modeling differences and runtime.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for γ and Beta hyperparameters; consider simple empirical Bayes or hyperparameter inference.
- Evaluate against HRG/hSBM baselines and, if possible, run LFRM/ILA under a unified protocol.
- Report memory/runtime scaling on larger sparse graphs; quantify the benefit of the sparse constraint and of absorption vs binary-only merges.
- Clarify the statement about speed vs “single iteration of IRM” and ensure fair compute budgets.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed adaptation with practical impact, but the novelty and empirical evaluation appear borderline for NIPS without stronger positioning and comparisons.
