## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The approach builds upon Bayesian extensions of greedy hierarchical clustering, leveraging a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The core contribution lies in an efficient inference scheme – utilizing a tree-based prior on partitions – allowing for faster computation than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate the speed and effectiveness of BHCD on both synthetic (Sampson’s monastery network) and real-world (NIPS co-authorship network) datasets.

**Relationship to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of flat community detection methods (stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership models) and highlights the computational challenges associated with fully Bayesian hierarchical approaches (IRM, Mondrian process). BHCD directly addresses these challenges by offering a computationally efficient alternative while retaining a probabilistic framework. The authors correctly point to prior work on Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering ([13, 14]) as a foundation for their approach.  The comparison to LFRM [18] and ILA [10] in the experiments also demonstrates awareness of related techniques for network analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength of BHCD is its speed. The authors convincingly demonstrate a significant speedup compared to IRM, particularly for larger networks, due to the efficient inference scheme and caching mechanism. This makes it practical for analyzing datasets where IRM would be prohibitively slow. The reported complexity of O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) is attractive.
* **Probabilistic Framework:**  Unlike purely greedy hierarchical clustering algorithms, BHCD provides a full probabilistic account of the discovered structure, enabling predictions and uncertainty quantification.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the model and inference procedure. The use of equations and figures aids in understanding the key concepts. The breakdown of the likelihood calculation and recursive nature of the prediction is well described.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network are compelling. The qualitative results on the monastery network demonstrate the ability to recover known community structure, and the quantitative results on the NIPS dataset demonstrate competitive performance in link prediction.
* **Variants & Analysis:**  The inclusion of variations like BHCD sparse and the discussion of tie-breaking strategies show thoughtful consideration of practical implementation and potential improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not delve deeply into the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). While they report values that "worked well", a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter influence would strengthen the work.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** While the authors mention the time complexity, there’s a lack of theoretical guarantees regarding the quality of the discovered community structure – i.e., under what conditions does BHCD recover the true underlying hierarchy?
* **Comparison Depth:** The comparison with IRM in the NIPS experiments is somewhat limited. Showing results alongside other popular community detection algorithms (e.g., Louvain algorithm, Leiden algorithm) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Scalability Discussion:** While the authors highlight the O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity, a discussion of memory requirements and how they scale with network size would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant challenge in network analysis: efficient and scalable hierarchical community detection.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach with clear theoretical justification.
* Demonstrates compelling empirical results that showcase the method’s speed and effectiveness.
*  The work is well-written and clearly presented.
*  The problem is relevant to the NIPS audience, particularly those working on probabilistic modeling and graph learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of extensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
*  Absence of theoretical guarantees on the quality of the recovered community structure.
*  Limited comparison to a broader range of community detection algorithms.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of network analysis. The authors successfully address a critical challenge – efficient hierarchical community detection – by proposing a novel and well-motivated Bayesian approach. While there are some areas for improvement (as outlined above), the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses.  The speedup compared to existing Bayesian methods is particularly noteworthy and could significantly broaden the applicability of Bayesian hierarchical modeling to larger networks.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1.  **Hyperparameter Analysis:** Include a brief analysis of the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the results, perhaps by showcasing results on a small subset of the NIPS dataset with varying parameter values.
2.  **Algorithm Comparison:** Add a comparison to at least one or two non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection algorithms (e.g., Louvain, Leiden) on the NIPS dataset.
3.  **Memory Considerations:** Briefly discuss the memory requirements of the algorithm and how they scale with network size.
