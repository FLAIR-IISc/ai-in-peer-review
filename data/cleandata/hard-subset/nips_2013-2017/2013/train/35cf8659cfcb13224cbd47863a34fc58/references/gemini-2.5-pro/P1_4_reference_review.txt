This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to frame greedy agglomerative clustering as a Bayesian model selection problem. The algorithm starts with each node as its own community and iteratively merges pairs of communities (or sub-trees) that yield the greatest increase in the marginal likelihood of the observed network data.

The main contribution lies in formulating a probabilistic model for which this marginal likelihood, p(D|T), can be calculated efficiently. The model defines the likelihood of a network D given a hierarchy T by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. While this sum is typically intractable, the authors leverage a specific prior on partitions and a factorizing likelihood (based on the stochastic blockmodel) that permits an efficient dynamic programming recursion to compute the marginal likelihood in O(n+m) time. This score is then used to guide a bottom-up greedy search for a good hierarchy T.

This work builds directly on previous research in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, particularly Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012), extending these ideas from i.i.d. data to relational data. In the context of network analysis, it positions itself as a computationally efficient alternative to more flexible but slower MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and its hierarchical extensions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning and network science: the discovery of hierarchical structure in large networks. Existing methods often face a trade-off between computational scalability (e.g., non-probabilistic greedy methods) and probabilistic rigor (e.g., MCMC-based nonparametric models). BHCD offers a compelling middle ground by combining the speed of a greedy algorithm with a principled Bayesian model selection criterion. The application of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation to relational data is a novel and elegant contribution.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is clear and correct, providing a solid probabilistic foundation for the greedy search. The experimental evaluation is strong and directly supports the paper's main claims. The head-to-head comparison with the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship data (Figure 4) is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance in a fraction of the computational time. The qualitative results on the Sampson's Monastery network also show that the method finds interpretable and meaningful community structures. The authors are also honest about performance relative to other models (ILA), which is commendable.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the model is developed in a logical progression from the stochastic blockmodel to the full hierarchical formulation. Algorithm 1 provides a clear overview of the inference procedure. The figures are informative and support the text well.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limitations of Greedy Search:** The primary weakness, inherent to the approach, is its greedy nature. The algorithm is susceptible to finding local optima and is not guaranteed to find the hierarchy with the highest marginal likelihood. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of this limitation. For example, are there particular network structures where this greedy approach is likely to fail significantly? A deeper analysis of the potential for poor local optima would strengthen the paper.

*   **Implementation Details and Reproducibility:** The paper claims an O(n^2 log n) time complexity, which relies on a caching scheme that allows for O(1) computation of sufficient statistics in the inner loop. While the update rules in Eq. 13 are presented, the details of how the heap of potential merges is efficiently managed and updated for the three different merge types (join, absorb left, absorb right) are not fully specified. A more detailed explanation, perhaps in an appendix, would be necessary for a reader to fully reproduce the efficient implementation.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ for the Beta priors, and γ for the tree prior), which are set to fixed values that were "found to work well". The performance of Bayesian models can be sensitive to hyperparameter settings. The paper would be more complete if it included a sensitivity analysis or at least a more detailed discussion on the influence of these parameters and guidance for setting them in practice.

*   **Clarity in Experiments:** In Figure 3's caption, the label "BHCD global" is used but not defined in the main text. It is presumably the standard, non-sparse version of BHCD, but this should be made explicit to avoid confusion.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  **High Significance:** It provides a practical and scalable Bayesian method for a fundamental problem (hierarchical community detection).
    2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The claim of being orders of magnitude faster than a key baseline (IRM) for comparable accuracy is well-supported by experiments.
    3.  **Novelty:** The combination of a recursive marginal likelihood calculation with a greedy search for hierarchical blockmodels is an elegant and effective contribution.
    4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  **Inherent Greediness:** The limitations imposed by the greedy search are a key weakness and could be discussed in more depth.
    2.  **Missing Details:** Key details for an efficient implementation are somewhat sparse, impacting full reproducibility.
    3.  **No Hyperparameter Analysis:** The lack of sensitivity analysis for the model's hyperparameters is a minor weakness.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution. The strengths—particularly the significant practical speedup combined with a principled probabilistic foundation—far outweigh the weaknesses. The method is likely to be used by practitioners and serve as a strong baseline for future research in scalable Bayesian network analysis. I recommend acceptance.
