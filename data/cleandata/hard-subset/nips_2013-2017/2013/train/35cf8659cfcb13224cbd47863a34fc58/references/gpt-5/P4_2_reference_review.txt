Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian method to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. A rooted tree T over the vertices defines a set of tree-consistent partitions Φ(T); a generative process over T yields a prior p(φ|T) over these partitions and a marginal likelihood p(D|T) that can be computed exactly by dynamic programming. Within each tree node, either the model “stops” and explains all within-cluster edges via an on-diagonal marginal f (Beta-Bernoulli for concreteness), or it “continues,” explains inter-child edges via an off-diagonal marginal g, and recurses on children. This leads to a recursion p(DTT|T) = πT f(σTT) + (1−πT) g(σ¬chTT) ∏C p(DCC|C).

Inference uses a bottom-up agglomerative procedure (in the spirit of Bayesian Rose Trees), merging trees greedily to maximize a local Bayes factor that depends only on data among the leaves being merged. Three merge types are considered (join and two absorptions), with sufficient statistics cached to make each inner-loop update O(1). Overall time is O(n^2 log n), independent of the number of communities. A “sparse” variant prohibits merging components with no edges between them.

Experiments on Sampson’s monastery data, a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset, and the full NIPS 1–17 graph show that BHCD recovers qualitatively sensible hierarchies and achieves link-prediction performance comparable to LFRM and better than IRM, while being substantially faster (reported two orders of magnitude speedup over IRM in wall-clock time for comparable accuracy). The method also scales to 2.8k nodes.

Relative to prior work: BHCD sits between flat blockmodels (SBM, IRM) and hierarchical approaches (hierarchical IRM; multiscale community blockmodels; Mondrian process). Its novelty is in transferring Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.) to relational data via a blockmodel likelihood, enabling exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions and efficient greedy inference. Compared with nonparametric relational models (IRM, LFRM, ILA), BHCD emphasizes computational efficiency and scalability over expressivity (e.g., no mixed membership).

Quality (technical soundness)
- The probabilistic construction (equations (6)–(10)) is coherent: each edge is explained exactly once either by an on-diagonal component at a “stop” node or by an off-diagonal component at the lowest ancestor where endpoints diverge. The dynamic programming recursion is standard and appears correct.
- The Bayes factor merge score is local and consistent with the assumed factorization; caching of σ-statistics supports the claimed O(1) per inner-loop.
- Complexity claims match those of Bayesian Rose Trees; the independence from number of communities is a practical advantage.
- Predictive distribution (Eq. 14) is derived via posterior responsibilities rS; this is plausible and computationally efficient.

Points that need clarification/correction:
- Undirected graphs: Equation (2) multiplies over p,q∈φ, p≠q. For symmetric networks, this double-counts off-diagonal blocks unless restricted to unordered pairs (e.g., p<q). The text notes σpq=σqp but does not state that the product is over unordered pairs. This should be clarified to avoid ambiguity and potential over-counting.
- Notation consistency in Section 4 (Eq. 13): variable reuse (I, J, M, X, Y) is confusing. A clean, consistent presentation would improve reproducibility.
- Prediction complexity: the text first states it is linear in tree depth, then “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n).” The latter is only a worst-case bound; please state O(depth(T)).
- “BHCD global” appears in the Sampson discussion but is not previously defined; likely a naming inconsistency.
- Many ties in early merges are broken at random and R restarts are used. Some discussion of variance across runs and sensitivity to R would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a good balance between model, inference, and experiments. The hierarchical prior and dynamic programming recursion are well explained. However:
- Some notation (Section 2 products; Section 4 updates) should be tightened.
- The forest likelihood and merge score are central; adding a compact pseudocode for Algorithm 1 (referenced but not shown in the excerpt) would help.
- Hyperparameters are fixed; a brief rationale or sensitivity analysis would aid readers.

Originality
The core novelty is adapting Bayesian Rose Trees to relational blockmodeling, yielding exact marginalization over a constrained partition space and an efficient agglomerative search for hierarchical communities. While hierarchical network models exist (e.g., hierarchical IRM, multiscale blockmodels, Mondrian-based models), BHCD offers a distinct combination: conjugate blockmodel likelihood with tree-structured prior over partitions, and an efficient greedy algorithm with absorption merges. This appears to be a meaningful, if incremental, contribution, focused on scalability.

Significance
The method addresses an important practical gap: scalable Bayesian discovery of hierarchical communities with explicit predictive capabilities. The demonstrated speedups over IRM and applicability to a few thousand nodes are valuable. However, the empirical comparison is limited to IRM (and indirect comparison to LFRM/ILA via reported numbers), and there is no direct comparison to recent hierarchical network models due to computational cost. The paper’s impact would be higher with broader benchmarks or more detailed ablations (e.g., effect of absorptions, sparse variant, restarts).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled Bayesian model with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Efficient, scalable inference with strong practical runtime advantages; complexity independent of number of communities.
- Competitive link-prediction performance versus established baselines on standard data.
- Meaningful hierarchical structures recovered, with interpretable visualizations.
- Relevance to NeurIPS topics (probabilistic modeling, scalable inference, network analysis).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Some technical and notation ambiguities (off-diagonal counting; prediction complexity; undefined “BHCD global”).
- Limited empirical comparison to other hierarchical relational models; no ablation/sensitivity analyses (e.g., hyperparameters, restarts).
- The model’s expressivity is limited relative to mixed-membership or latent-attribute models; performance lags ILA on NIPS-234.
- Greedy search may get stuck; no guarantees or diagnostics beyond restarts.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the products in Eq. (2) for undirected graphs (use unordered pairs) and tidy notation in Section 4.
- Report variability across restarts and sensitivity to hyperparameters α,β,δ,λ,γ.
- Include ablations: with/without absorption merges; effect of prohibiting merges with zero inter-edges; sparse vs non-sparse variants.
- Compare more broadly if feasible (e.g., multiscale community blockmodel), or provide stronger justification for omitting them.
- Provide more details on fairness of IRM comparisons (initialization, hyperparameters, number of iterations).
- Clarify the “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of IRM” statement to avoid ambiguity; use wall-clock plots as in Figure 4.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and practically useful contribution that extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas to network data with a scalable algorithm. With minor clarifications and expanded empirical analysis, it would make a solid addition to the NeurIPS program.
