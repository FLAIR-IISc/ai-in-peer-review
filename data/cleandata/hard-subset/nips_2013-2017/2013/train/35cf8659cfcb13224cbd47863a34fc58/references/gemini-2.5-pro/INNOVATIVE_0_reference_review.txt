This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea is to combine the speed of greedy agglomerative clustering with the principled framework of Bayesian model selection. The authors adapt the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data by using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The model defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a tree structure, which allows for the exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of the data given a hierarchy. Inference is performed via a bottom-up greedy search, where pairs of subtrees are merged at each step to maximize the Bayes factor. This approach cleverly circumvents the extreme computational cost of MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which attempt to sample from the full posterior over partitions.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and significance. The authors propose a bold and pragmatic solution to a major bottleneck in the field: the scalability of Bayesian network analysis. While most prior work has been split between fast but heuristic methods (like Clauset et al., 2004) and principled but computationally prohibitive Bayesian models (like the IRM), this paper carves out a new and highly compelling middle ground. The synthesis of greedy search with an exact marginal likelihood calculation is elegant. Instead of settling for an incremental improvement on existing MCMC samplers, the authors have developed a fundamentally different inference approach that yields a dramatic practical benefit.

The results are significant. The claim of being "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" is well-supported by the experiments on the NIPS co-authorship data (Figure 4). Achieving comparable or superior predictive performance to a 1000-second IRM run in under 10 seconds is a major advance and could make hierarchical Bayesian analysis practical for a much wider range of problems and practitioners. Furthermore, the worst-case O(n^2 log n) time complexity, which is independent of the number of communities, represents a crucial theoretical advantage over models whose complexity scales with the number of inferred clusters.

The paper is also clearly written and the model is well-motivated. The experiments, though not exhaustive, are well-chosen to demonstrate the method's key advantages: interpretability on a classic dataset (Sampson's Monastery), speed and accuracy on a standard benchmark (NIPS-234), and scalability on a larger network (full NIPS). The introduction of the "BHCD sparse" variant is a thoughtful addition that demonstrates an awareness of the practicalities of real-world network data.

**Weaknesses:**

The most apparent weakness is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. By committing to the best merge at each step, the algorithm is susceptible to finding poor local optima, a fact the authors acknowledge by using multiple random restarts. This is a significant compromise compared to MCMC methods, which can in principle explore the entire posterior. However, I view this not as a fatal flaw, but as a well-justified engineering decision. The enormous gains in speed are a direct result of this trade-off, and for many practical purposes, a "good enough" hierarchy found quickly is far more valuable than a marginally better one found after days of computation.

The experimental validation could be broader. The main comparison is with the IRM, which is the correct Bayesian nonparametric baseline. However, comparing performance and scalability against other modern, highly scalable (though perhaps non-Bayesian) community detection algorithms would have provided more context for the method's practical utility on very large graphs. Additionally, the hyperparameters are set to fixed values that are said to "work well," without a sensitivity analysis or a discussion of how a user might set them. This is a minor point but leaves a question of robustness open.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty & Significance:** The paper introduces a new, scalable paradigm for Bayesian community detection. The performance gains are not incremental but represent a leap of orders of magnitude, which can fundamentally change how researchers approach this class of problems.
*   **Impact:** The method is likely to be widely used and built upon due to its speed, principled foundation, and the availability of code (implied by the specific C implementation details). It addresses a clear and pressing need in the community.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, the model is presented logically, and the contributions are stated clearly.
*   **Boldness:** The authors have prioritized a practical, high-impact solution over methodological purity (i.e., MCMC), a trade-off that is both well-motivated and highly successful. This is exactly the kind of creative, problem-driven research that NIPS should encourage.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Approximation Quality:** The greedy search is a strong approximation, and the paper does not theoretically characterize how far its solutions might be from the true posterior mode.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The experiments focus primarily on a single, albeit important, baseline (IRM).

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses associated with the greedy approximation are far outweighed by the novelty of the approach and the immense practical significance of its computational efficiency. This work presents a creative and elegant solution to a long-standing problem, successfully bridging the gap between heuristic speed and Bayesian rigor. It is a complete and well-executed piece of research that is likely to have a substantial impact on the field.
