- Introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a Bayesian nonparametric method for nested community detection in networks.
- Key innovation: greedy model selection with dynamic programming to compute marginal likelihoods, achieving O(n² log n) time.
- Extends stochastic blockmodel to a hierarchical mixture and uses a greedy agglomerative algorithm with three merge types.
- Evaluated on Sampson’s monastery, NIPS co-authorship subset, and full NIPS dataset, showing comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art Bayesian methods.
- Technical contribution: efficient caching of sufficient statistics enabling O(1) computation per inner loop.
- Computational efficiency: claims of being ~two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration and scalable to large networks.
- Clear exposition and well-structured algorithm presentation (Algorithm 1).
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and relevant Bayesian baselines (IRM, LFRM, ILA).
- Adequate related work discussion situating BHCD relative to prior hierarchical and Bayesian methods.
- Practical implementation concerns addressed: tie-breaking, sparse variant, and binary tree restriction.
- Limited comparison with non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, weakening claims about being the fastest overall.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on hierarchy quality, approximation bounds, or convergence properties.
- Vague speedup claim without concrete timing comparisons on the experimental datasets.
- Insufficient analysis of limitations and failure modes, e.g., performance on overlapping-community networks.
- Sparse vs. non-sparse variant tradeoffs not comprehensively analyzed or recommended.
- Predictive performance results lack detailed metrics (exact log predictive probabilities, AUC scores).
- For acceptance: strong technical advance in efficient sufficient-statistic caching and practical impact for large networks.
- For acceptance: well-executed experiments, clear presentation, and relevance to an important network-analysis problem.
- Against acceptance: limited theoretical analysis and vague empirical speed claims.
- Against acceptance: incomplete comparisons with non-Bayesian methods and limited exploration of failure cases.
