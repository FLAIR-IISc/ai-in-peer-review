**Summary**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative algorithm for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The work extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework, previously applied to i.i.d. data (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2010), to relational data. The core of the model is a tree-based prior over network partitions, where each internal node represents a potential community. The marginal likelihood of the network data given a specific hierarchical tree structure can be computed exactly and efficiently via a dynamic programming recursion. The inference procedure builds this tree bottom-up by iteratively merging subtrees in a greedy fashion. At each step, the pair of subtrees that yields the largest increase in the marginal likelihood (i.e., the highest Bayes factor) is merged. The authors demonstrate that a caching scheme for sufficient statistics allows the algorithm to run in O(n^2 log n) time, which is significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, such as MCMC-based inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The method is evaluated qualitatively on Sampson's monastery network and quantitatively on the NIPS co-authorship dataset, where it is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel and effective adaptation of the greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to the problem of community detection in networks. While the components (stochastic blockmodels, greedy agglomerative clustering, Bayesian model selection) are not new in isolation, their combination provides a principled and, most importantly, computationally efficient approach to a challenging problem. The claimed speed-up of two orders of magnitude over the IRM, a widely recognized baseline, is a significant practical and scientific contribution. This makes Bayesian hierarchical analysis feasible for networks of a size that would be prohibitive for MCMC-based methods.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation and review of related work, progresses from the basic stochastic blockmodel to the full hierarchical model, and then details the inference algorithm. The recursive definition of the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) and the Bayes factor score for merges (Equation 12) are presented clearly. The algorithmic description is sufficient for an expert to understand the procedure.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the derivation of the marginal likelihood is sound. The use of a Bayes factor as the greedy merge criterion is a principled approach to model selection. The description of the caching scheme for sufficient statistics, which is crucial for achieving the O(n^2 log n) complexity, appears correct and is a key technical aspect of the work. The experimental methodology is standard, using well-known datasets and appropriate evaluation metrics (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The main weakness of the proposed method is inherent to its greedy nature. The algorithm is not guaranteed to find the optimal hierarchy and is susceptible to finding local optima. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts and selecting the best result, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this approach. For example, the final structure can be sensitive to the initial tie-breaking, a point the authors mention but do not explore in depth. It is unclear how suboptimal the discovered hierarchies might be compared to the true posterior mode.

2.  **Locality of the Merge Criterion:** The merge score (Equation 12) is computed based only on the data within the leaves of the two trees being considered for a merge. The paper justifies this as an assumption that local structure depends on local connectivity. However, this locality could be a significant limitation. A merge that appears optimal locally might be globally suboptimal when considering the new cluster's connections to the rest of the network. This assumption is strong and its potential impact on the quality of the discovered hierarchy warrants further discussion.

3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental comparison is primarily focused on the IRM. While this is an important baseline, the comparison could be strengthened by including other relevant methods. Specifically:
    *   A comparison to a state-of-the-art non-Bayesian but fast hierarchical clustering method (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004) would help contextualize the trade-offs between a fully Bayesian approach and an algorithmic one in terms of both quality and speed.
    *   The paper mentions other Bayesian hierarchical models for networks but does not compare against them experimentally, citing their computational cost. While understandable, this leaves the performance relative to other hierarchical Bayesian models unsubstantiated.
    *   The comparisons to LFRM and ILA are based on results reported in a previous paper. While this is acceptable, direct comparisons using the same experimental setup would be more robust.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model involves several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values found to "work well". As with many Bayesian models, the results can be sensitive to these settings. A sensitivity analysis or a more detailed justification for the chosen values would improve the paper's completeness and reproducibility. For example, how does the prior belief about within-community versus between-community density (controlled by α, β, δ, λ) affect the final hierarchy?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and highly efficient Bayesian algorithm for a significant problem (hierarchical community detection).
*   The computational speed-up over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods is substantial and well-supported by experiments, representing a significant advance in scalability.
*   The paper is clear, technically sound, and presents a complete piece of work from model definition to inference and evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The greedy inference approach has inherent limitations (local optima) that are not fully explored.
*   The experimental comparison, while demonstrating a clear advantage over the IRM, could be more comprehensive by including other classes of methods.
*   The impact of the local merge criterion and hyperparameter choices is not fully analyzed.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The significant improvement in computational efficiency for Bayesian hierarchical community detection is a strong point that likely outweighs the weaknesses, which are largely limitations inherent to the chosen greedy framework rather than fundamental flaws. The work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working with network data.
