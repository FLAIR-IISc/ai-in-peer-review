### Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative clustering method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The approach extends prior work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering, specifically the Bayesian Rose Tree model [14], by adapting it for relational data using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The model defines a prior over tree-structured partitions and computes the marginal likelihood of a given hierarchy efficiently via dynamic programming. Inference of the hierarchy is performed using a bottom-up greedy search, where pairs of subtrees are merged based on the move that maximizes the Bayes factor. The primary contribution is a computationally efficient (O(n² log n)) alternative to more flexible but slower Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), with experiments demonstrating that BHCD can achieve comparable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster.

### Strengths

1.  **Efficient Probabilistic Framework:** The paper successfully combines the elegant probabilistic formalism of Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] with the stochastic blockmodel, resulting in a method that is both probabilistic and computationally efficient. The recursive formulation for the marginal likelihood of a tree (Eq. 10) is sound and the use of a Bayes factor (Eq. 12) provides a principled criterion for the greedy agglomerative search.

2.  **Impressive Speed and Scalability:** The key selling point of the paper is the method's speed. The experimental results on the NIPS co-authorship dataset convincingly demonstrate that BHCD provides reasonable predictions significantly faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM. The O(n² log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, is a notable advantage over many competing models whose complexity scales with the number of inferred clusters.

3.  **Well-Motivated Variants:** The introduction of the "BHCD sparse" variant, which prohibits merges between disconnected components, is an intuitive and sensible heuristic for community detection that the authors show empirically improves performance on sparse networks.

### Weaknesses

The paper, while presenting a promising direction, is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, technical inaccuracies, and inconsistencies that collectively undermine its quality and polish.

1.  **Technical Inaccuracies and Inconsistencies:**
    *   **Predictive Complexity:** The complexity of making a prediction for a single edge is stated to be Θ(n) in Section 4.2. This is incorrect. The predictive computation involves traversing a single path from the root to a leaf in the hierarchy. Therefore, the complexity is proportional to the depth of the tree, O(depth), which can range from O(log n) for a balanced tree to O(n) in the worst case for a degenerate tree. This is a notable technical error.
    *   **Contradictory Experimental Results:** In the Sampson's Monastery experiment (Section 5), the authors state that BHCD sparse "produces better results than BHCD." However, the reported log marginal likelihoods for the presented trees are -6.62 for BHCD and a dramatically lower -22.80 for BHCD sparse. Since the algorithm seeks to maximize this objective (or a closely related one), this is a stark contradiction. The authors must clarify whether "better" refers to some unstated qualitative criterion or if there is an error in the reported likelihoods. This inconsistency calls the evaluation into question.
    *   **Undefined Terminology:** The term "BHCD global" appears once in the Sampson's Monastery results without any prior definition, leaving the reader to guess its meaning.

2.  **Methodological Details and Evaluation:**
    *   **Lack of Hyperparameter Justification:** The model's hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed to specific values "which we found to work well." For a Bayesian model, this is insufficient. The paper would be much stronger with a sensitivity analysis or a more principled method for setting these parameters, as the results could be highly dependent on these choices.
    *   **Locality Assumption:** The greedy merge decision is based on a Bayes factor that only considers data local to the two subtrees being merged. The authors note this assumes "local community structure should depend only on the local connectivity structure." This is a strong assumption whose limitations are not discussed. An analysis of cases where this assumption might fail would be valuable.
    *   **Limited Novelty:** The core algorithmic machinery—the recursive likelihood calculation on a tree and the greedy agglomerative search using Bayes factors—is directly adopted from previous work [13, 14]. The primary novelty is its application to a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for network data. While this is a valid contribution, the conceptual leap is modest.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Sloppy Referencing:** The reference list is poorly formatted, containing numerous stray commas, missing publication years, and incomplete entries (e.g., References [1], [2], [6]).
    *   **Inconsistent Citation Style:** The in-text citation style is inconsistent, sometimes appearing as `[1]` and other times as `[5, 6, 7]`.
    *   These presentation issues suggest a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation, which detracts from the overall impression of quality.

### Recommendation: Reject

While the paper presents a computationally efficient and useful method for hierarchical community detection, the accumulation of technical errors, unexplained inconsistencies in the results, and a general lack of polish make it fall short of the high standards for NIPS. The contradiction between the stated performance and the reported model evidence for the "sparse" variant is a particularly serious flaw that must be addressed. I would be willing to reconsider a future submission if these issues are thoroughly resolved, as the underlying direction of research is valuable.
