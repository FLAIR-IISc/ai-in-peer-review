This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering, previously developed for i.i.d. data, to the relational data setting of networks. The model defines a probability distribution over tree structures, where the marginal likelihood of a tree is computed efficiently via dynamic programming by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. Inference is performed not via MCMC, but through a fast, bottom-up greedy search that iteratively merges sub-trees based on the move that maximizes the local Bayes factor. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness through qualitative analysis on the classic Sampson's monastery network and quantitative link prediction experiments on the NIPS co-authorship dataset, showing a significant speed advantage over the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is a well-founded extension of the stochastic blockmodel and builds logically on prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2012). The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood for a tree (Equation 10) is elegant and provides a principled scoring function for the hierarchy. The agglomerative search algorithm is well-described, and the claim of `O(n^2 log n)` complexity, enabled by a caching scheme for sufficient statistics, is a key strength.

The experimental evaluation is solid and convincing. The comparison against IRM, a prominent Bayesian nonparametric model for relational data, is highly relevant. The results in Figure 4 are striking, demonstrating that BHCD achieves comparable or superior predictive performance (in terms of log probability, accuracy, and AUC) orders of magnitude faster than IRM. This addresses a major bottleneck of many Bayesian nonparametric methods: slow inference. The qualitative results on Sampson's network are also compelling, showing that the method recovers meaningful and interpretable hierarchical structures.

The main weakness in terms of quality lies in the nature of the greedy inference. The algorithm produces a single point estimate of the tree structure (or a small set via restarts). This is a significant departure from fully Bayesian methods like MCMC-based IRM, which aim to characterize the full posterior distribution over structures. The greedy approach is susceptible to local optima, and the paper only briefly touches upon this by mentioning random tie-breaking and restarts. A more thorough discussion of the trade-off between the speed of this deterministic approach and the richness of a full posterior approximation would strengthen the paper. For instance, the model does not capture uncertainty about the hierarchy itself, which can be a key output of a Bayesian analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, positioning their work relative to prior art, and building up their model from first principles. The progression from the stochastic blockmodel (Section 2) to the hierarchical model (Section 3) is logical and easy to follow. Figure 1 is a helpful illustration of the core concept of tree-consistent partitions. Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the inference procedure. The writing is concise and precise, making a complex topic accessible. A minor suggestion would be to slightly elaborate on the caching of sufficient statistics (Equation 13), as this is central to the claimed efficiency of the algorithm's inner loop.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and effective synthesis of existing ideas. While greedy hierarchical clustering and Bayesian approaches to community detection are not new, the specific combination presented here is. The primary novelty lies in the successful adaptation of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework (specifically the recursive likelihood calculation) to relational data using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. This extension is non-trivial, requiring careful definition of the on-diagonal and off-diagonal likelihood components and how they contribute to the score. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior Bayesian hierarchical models for networks which were either limited in depth or computationally much more expensive. The explicit formulation of the agglomerative search based on Bayes factors for this model is also a key contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is high. Scalability is one of the most pressing challenges for Bayesian methods in network analysis. Many powerful models (like extensions of the IRM) remain confined to small- or medium-sized networks due to the computational cost of MCMC inference. By providing a method that is two orders of magnitude faster than a key baseline while maintaining competitive accuracy, this paper makes Bayesian hierarchical analysis practical for a much wider range of real-world problems. The `O(n^2 log n)` complexity is suitable for dense networks of moderate size, and the `BHCD sparse` variant shows promising performance on sparse graphs. Researchers and practitioners looking for a fast, principled, and off-the-shelf method for exploring hierarchical structure in networks are very likely to use or build upon this work. It advances the state-of-the-art by demonstrating that a carefully constructed greedy search can be a highly effective alternative to traditional sampling-based inference in this domain.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Impact:** Addresses the critical problem of scalability in Bayesian network analysis, offering a massive speed-up over established methods like IRM.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to be not only much faster but also achieves comparable or better predictive performance on a standard benchmark task.
*   **Technically Sound and Novel:** The paper presents a novel combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels, with a clear and principled mathematical formulation.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is extremely well-written, making the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

**Arguments for Rejection (or points for improvement):**
*   **Greedy Inference Limitations:** The primary limitation is the use of a greedy search, which finds a single point estimate and cannot capture posterior uncertainty over the hierarchy. The trade-offs associated with this choice could be discussed more deeply.
*   **Limited Baselines:** While the comparison to IRM is strong and relevant, comparing to other modern, scalable (even non-Bayesian) hierarchical clustering methods could have provided additional context on the performance/quality trade-off.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters are fixed across experiments. A brief analysis of the model's sensitivity to these settings would be beneficial.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—particularly its significant contribution to the practical applicability of Bayesian hierarchical modeling for networks—far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete, well-executed piece of research that is likely to be influential.
