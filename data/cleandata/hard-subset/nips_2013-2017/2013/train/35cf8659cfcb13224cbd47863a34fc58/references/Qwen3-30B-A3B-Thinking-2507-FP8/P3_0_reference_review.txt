

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

## Summary

This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method for inferring hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian approaches to hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and extend them to relational data. BHCD organizes communities into a nested hierarchy and uses a greedy agglomerative algorithm to efficiently learn the hierarchical structure. The key innovation is a dynamic programming approach for computing the marginal likelihood of hierarchical structures, enabling an inference algorithm with O(n² log n) time complexity, where n is the number of vertices. The authors demonstrate that BHCD is approximately two orders of magnitude faster than IRM (Infinite Relational Model) while achieving comparable predictive performance.

The paper is well-organized, with clear descriptions of the stochastic blockmodel foundation, the hierarchical Bayesian model, the efficient inference algorithm, and empirical validation on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network. The authors position their work as addressing a key limitation of previous Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection, which often suffer from poor scalability.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the hierarchical Bayesian model. The derivation of the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) using dynamic programming is well-motivated and carefully presented. The authors correctly identify the key challenge of efficiently computing the marginal likelihood over hierarchical partitions, and their solution using dynamic programming is elegant.

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The complexity analysis is thorough and well-supported. The authors correctly identify that their algorithm's worst-case complexity (O(n² log n)) is significantly better than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (which often have complexity depending on the number of communities). This is a genuine contribution to the field, as scalability is a major barrier to applying Bayesian methods to larger networks.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset provide reasonable evidence of the method's performance. The qualitative results on Sampson's network show that BHCD can recover meaningful hierarchical community structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comprehensive Comparison:** The paper claims BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM," but provides no concrete timing data to substantiate this claim. While Table 1 shows the asymptotic complexity, it doesn't provide actual runtimes for comparable implementations on the same hardware. The comparison with IRM is limited, and the paper fails to compare with other recent scalable methods like the Mondrian process [12] or the approaches in [8, 9, 10, 11].

2. **Limited Evaluation of Community Quality:** The paper focuses primarily on computational efficiency and predictive performance (log likelihood), but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD is qualitatively better or more interpretable than that of competing methods. The Sampson's monastery example shows that BHCD finds similar structure to flat community detection methods, but doesn't demonstrate how the hierarchical structure adds value over flat community detection.

3. **Inadequate Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn't sufficiently address limitations of the approach. For example:
   - How does BHCD handle overlapping communities? The paper acknowledges that their flat model doesn't support overlapping communities, but doesn't discuss how this limitation affects the hierarchical structure.
   - The paper doesn't address how BHCD would handle directed networks or networks with multiple edge types (e.g., positive/negative affiliations), which are common in social network analysis.
   - The paper doesn't provide guidance on choosing hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) or how sensitive the results are to these choices.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The empirical results don't include statistical significance tests to determine if the performance differences between BHCD and IRM are meaningful.

## Clarity Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Overall Organization:** The paper is well-structured and logically flows from problem statement to methodology to experiments.

2. **Mathematical Presentation:** The mathematical derivations (Equations 2-10) are clear and well-explained, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand the model.

3. **Figures:** The figures (particularly Figure 1 and Figure 3) are helpful in illustrating the hierarchical structure and the model's output.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 is somewhat confusing, particularly the explanation of the three merger types (joining, absorption). A clearer explanation of the differences between these merger types, possibly with a small diagram, would improve clarity.

2. **Sufficient Statistics Update:** The update rules for the sufficient statistics (Equation 13) are presented without sufficient explanation. The paper should clarify why these specific update rules are correct.

3. **Relationship Between Tree and Communities:** The paper doesn't adequately explain how the tree structure maps to actual communities at different levels of the hierarchy. For example, it's unclear how to interpret the communities at intermediate levels of the tree.

## Originality Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Efficient Inference Algorithm:** The dynamic programming approach for computing the marginal likelihood is a genuine contribution that builds on previous work [13, 14] but extends it to relational data. The authors correctly position this as addressing a key limitation in the literature.

2. **Greedy Bayesian Approach:** The paper's focus on a greedy Bayesian approach (rather than MCMC) for hierarchical community detection is novel in the context of Bayesian nonparametric methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Nature of Contribution:** The main contribution (the efficient inference algorithm) is somewhat incremental. The approach is very similar to previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14], and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from those works.

2. **Mischaracterization of Related Work:** The paper claims that "Current Bayesian nonparametric methods do not scale well to larger networks because the inference algorithms used make many small changes to the model." This is an oversimplification. The Mondrian process [12] is a Bayesian nonparametric method that does scale better than IRM, and the paper should have compared with this method.

3. **Lack of Novelty in Model Structure:** The hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels is not particularly novel. Previous work has used similar hierarchical structures [8, 11].

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Scalability:** The paper addresses a real and important problem—scalability of Bayesian methods for large networks—which has been a barrier to applying these methods in practice.

2. **Practical Utility:** The computational efficiency of BHCD makes hierarchical community detection more practical for larger networks, which is valuable for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Questionable Performance Claims:** The paper claims BHCD "attains comparable performance" to IRM, but the evidence is limited to a single co-authorship dataset. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that BHCD actually discovers better hierarchical structures than existing methods.

2. **Lack of Broader Impact:** The paper doesn't clearly articulate how BHCD could be applied to other domains beyond social networks, nor does it discuss potential applications in other fields where hierarchical community structure is important (e.g., biology, neuroscience).

3. **Limited Demonstration of Hierarchical Value:** The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD provides additional value compared to flat community structure. For example, on the Sampson's monastery network, the authors don't show how the hierarchical structure helps with interpretation or prediction compared to flat communities.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach to hierarchical community detection. The main contribution—the dynamic programming approach for efficient inference—is valuable and addresses a genuine limitation in the literature. However, the paper's claims about the superiority of BHCD are not fully supported by the empirical evidence provided.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods (including the Mondrian process and other scalable hierarchical community detection methods)
2. More thorough evaluation of the quality of the discovered hierarchical structure
3. Clearer explanation of the relationship between the tree structure and actual communities
4. More detailed discussion of the method's limitations and how they might be addressed

While the paper makes a useful contribution to the field of community detection, the current version is somewhat incremental and lacks sufficient evidence to support the authors' claims about the method's superiority.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a genuine contribution to the scalability of Bayesian hierarchical community detection, and the theoretical foundation is sound. However, the empirical evaluation is limited, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from previous work. With revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above, this paper would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add a direct comparison with the Mondrian process [12]**, which is also a Bayesian nonparametric method for hierarchical community detection. This would better position BHCD's contribution.

2. **Provide concrete timing data** to support the claim that BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM." Include actual runtimes on the NIPS-234 dataset.

3. **Expand the experimental section** to include more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., [8, 9, 10, 11]) and provide statistical significance testing for the results.

4. **Clarify the relationship between the tree structure and actual communities** at different levels of the hierarchy. Provide a concrete example showing how to interpret the communities at intermediate levels.

5. **Add a subsection discussing limitations** and potential extensions of the method, including how it might be adapted for overlapping communities or directed networks.

6. **Provide more guidance on hyperparameter selection** and sensitivity analysis.

7. **Clarify the three merger types** in Algorithm 1 with a small diagram or additional explanation.

## Conclusion

"Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" presents an interesting and potentially useful approach to hierarchical community detection. The paper's main contribution—the efficient inference algorithm—is well-motivated and theoretically sound. However, the empirical evaluation is limited, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from previous work. With the suggested revisions, this paper could make a strong contribution to the field of community detection and Bayesian modeling of networks. As it stands, it's a useful but not groundbreaking contribution that would benefit from more thorough empirical validation and clearer positioning within the existing literature.
