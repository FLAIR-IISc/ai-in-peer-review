Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method tailored to relational data. The model uses a tree to restrict the set of partitions of the vertex set and computes the marginal likelihood by summing exactly over tree-consistent partitions via a dynamic program (an adaptation of the recursion used in Bayesian rose trees). Within each partition, a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors is assumed, with different hyperparameters for within- vs between-cluster edges. Inference proceeds by agglomerative model selection using local Bayes factors and three merge operations (join, and two absorb variants), with careful caching to obtain O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant forbids merging trees with no connecting edges. Predictions are obtained by traversing the tree and mixing on- and off-diagonal posteriors along a path. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset compare against the IRM (MCMC and greedy), with BHCD reported to achieve comparable predictive performance substantially faster; qualitative structure on the full NIPS graph is shown.

Relation to prior work
The model leverages the Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose tree frameworks, extending them to a relational likelihood. It relates to the IRM, mixed-membership/block models, and prior hierarchical network models (e.g., hierarchical IRM extensions, the Mondrian process for relational data, and non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection). Compared to these, the contribution is primarily an efficient greedy selection scheme for a particular hierarchical SBM-like marginal likelihood.

Strengths
- Clear, computationally efficient inference procedure with sensible caching; the local Bayes factor and three-way merge are well motivated and practical.
- Exact marginal likelihood for a fixed tree via dynamic programming is elegant, following prior hierarchical clustering work.
- Empirical results suggest favorable speed-accuracy trade-offs vs. the IRM on a standard co-authorship benchmark and reasonable qualitative hierarchies.
- Code is implemented in C and runtime complexity is analyzed; the independence from the number of (latent) communities is attractive in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: the core idea (tree-restricted sum of partitions with a recursive marginal likelihood) closely follows Bayesian rose trees; the main novelty is the adaptation to a blockmodel likelihood and a tailored agglomerative search. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is fundamentally new vs. straightforward adaptation.
- Bayesian justification vs. heuristics: the “sparse” variant forbids merges with no edges, which breaks the stated generative story and posterior optimality; tie-breaking and multiple restarts further emphasize heuristic aspects. There is no theoretical analysis of when greedy local merges optimize the true marginal likelihood or any consistency guarantees.
- Hyperparameter setting and potential inconsistency: the text states α>β for within-cluster and δ<λ for between-cluster (prioring assortativity), but experiments set α=δ=1.0 and β=λ=0.2, which implies δ>λ and a high prior mean (≈0.83) both within and across clusters. This contradicts the stated modeling assumption and could strongly bias results. No sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference is provided.
- Assortativity built in: by hard-coding different priors for on- vs off-diagonal blocks, the model is biased toward assortative communities and may perform poorly on disassortative/bipartite/core-periphery structures. This limitation is not examined empirically.
- Baselines are thin: the main quantitative comparison is to the IRM; prior hierarchical models (e.g., hierarchical IRM extensions [8], Mondrian-relational methods [12], hierarchical random graphs/nested SBMs) are not compared empirically. The paper cites LFRM/ILA results from prior work but does not run those baselines directly under the same implementation and resource conditions. Without broader baselines, it’s hard to assess significance.
- Evaluation scope: only two datasets with quantitative evaluation (Sampson qualitative; NIPS-234 predictive metrics). More varied domains (e.g., biological networks, signed networks, bipartite graphs) and larger-scale predictions would strengthen claims; the handling of signed edges in Sampson by mapping negatives to “absent” is questionable.
- Clarity and rigor issues: several places need tightening. Examples: claim of “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” conflicts with later phrasing; predictive complexity stated as “linear in depth” but summarized as Θ(n); reference formatting is incomplete; small typos (e.g., “wherem n2”). The reproducibility claim mentions a C implementation but provides no link or detailed settings beyond fixed hyperparameters and R=50 restarts.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the intended hyperparameter settings. If the goal is α>β (intra high) and δ<λ (inter low), why are δ and λ set to 1.0 and 0.2 respectively? Provide a sensitivity analysis over α,β,δ,λ and γ.
2. How does BHCD perform on disassortative or core-periphery networks? Can the model be made neutral (e.g., same prior for on/off) and still recover structure?
3. Can you provide empirical comparisons to hierarchical IRM variants [8], Mondrian-based relational models [12], and/or nested SBMs/hierarchical random graphs, at least on NIPS-234?
4. How stable are the learned trees across restarts? Please report variance in predictive performance and structural similarity metrics.
5. Does the “sparse” constraint degrade marginal likelihood? Can you quantify its effect and justify it probabilistically?

Minor comments
- Improve reference details (years, venues). Fix typos and notation (e.g., σ{x}{x}=0 phrasing).
- Clarify the computational cost of computing all σ statistics initially on sparse graphs.
- Provide code and exact experimental settings (seeds, hardware) for reproducibility.

Pros
- Simple, fast, and practical method; exact marginal likelihood for a fixed tree.
- Clear algorithmic contribution with caching that yields good runtimes.
- Reasonable predictive performance on a standard dataset.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to Bayesian rose trees; largely an adaptation.
- Heuristic departures from the Bayesian model (sparse constraint, tie-breaking, restarts).
- Inconsistent hyperparameter story; no sensitivity/hyperparameter inference.
- Narrow baselines and datasets; limited evidence of significance over established hierarchical network models.

Recommendation
Leaning toward reject in the current form. The efficiency-oriented adaptation is interesting, but the methodological novelty is modest and the empirical validation is not yet sufficient to support strong claims. Addressing the hyperparameter inconsistency, adding rigorous comparisons to stronger hierarchical baselines, providing sensitivity analyses, and clarifying the Bayesian-vs-heuristic choices would substantially strengthen the paper.
