

# Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel Bayesian approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks, termed Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD). The authors address a gap in the literature where existing Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection are computationally expensive, while greedy hierarchical clustering methods lack a full probabilistic account. BHCD extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data by modeling communities as a nested hierarchy, with each internal node representing a community and leaves representing individual vertices. The key innovation is a greedy agglomerative algorithm that builds the hierarchy bottom-up, with a time complexity of O(n² log n), which is significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate BHCD's efficiency and effectiveness on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network, showing it can make predictions two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Technical Contribution**: The paper effectively identifies the limitations of existing methods (parametric flat community models, computationally expensive Bayesian hierarchical approaches, non-probabilistic greedy methods) and positions BHCD as a solution that bridges the gap between computational efficiency and probabilistic modeling.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The mathematical formulation of the model as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels is well-developed. The authors provide a clear derivation of the marginal likelihood and the efficient dynamic programming approach for computing it, which is the core of the method's efficiency.

3. **Thorough Experimental Comparison**: The paper includes a comprehensive comparison with existing methods (IRM, LFRM, ILA) on both qualitative (Sampson's monastery) and quantitative (NIPS co-authorship) datasets. The computational complexity analysis (Table 1) is particularly convincing, showing BHCD's significant advantage in worst-case time complexity.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper addresses important practical considerations, such as the BHCD sparse variant for sparse networks and the handling of tie-breaking in merge scores. The experimental results confirm that BHCD sparse outperforms the standard version on sparse networks like NIPS.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of Hierarchical Structure Quality**: While the paper demonstrates BHCD's ability to discover block diagonal structure in adjacency matrices (Figure 3), it lacks quantitative metrics to assess the quality of the discovered hierarchies. For instance, how does BHCD's hierarchy compare to ground-truth hierarchies when available? What metrics could be used to evaluate the "goodness" of the hierarchical structure beyond visual inspection?

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Non-Bayesian Methods**: The paper focuses primarily on comparing with Bayesian methods but doesn't adequately address how BHCD compares with state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches (e.g., [3, 4]). Given that the authors claim computational efficiency as a key advantage, it's important to show that this efficiency doesn't come at the cost of significantly worse hierarchical structure discovery.

3. **Ambiguity in Performance Claims**: The claim that BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" needs clarification. What is the specific implementation of IRM being compared to? How is "a single iteration" defined for IRM (which typically uses MCMC)? The paper would benefit from providing actual timing comparisons rather than just theoretical complexity.

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses fixed hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) without exploring how sensitive the results are to these choices. It would be valuable to see how varying these hyperparameters affects the discovered hierarchies and predictive performance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a technically sound contribution to the field of community detection in networks. The proposed BHCD algorithm addresses a genuine gap in the literature by providing an efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery. The theoretical foundation is solid, the algorithm is clearly presented, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's computational advantages.

The main limitation is the lack of thorough evaluation of the hierarchical structure quality beyond visual inspection. While the paper shows that BHCD can discover reasonable hierarchies, it doesn't provide metrics to quantify how good these hierarchies are compared to other methods or ground truth. This is particularly important for a paper claiming to discover "hierarchical structure."

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Adding quantitative metrics for evaluating hierarchical structure quality
2. Providing a more detailed comparison with non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods
3. Including a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters
4. Clarifying the performance claims with actual timing comparisons

## Recommendation

**Accept**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a well-motivated, technically sound approach to Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. While there are some limitations in the evaluation of hierarchical structure quality, the method's computational advantages are significant and well-demonstrated. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and addresses an important problem in network analysis. The authors have made a strong case for the practical utility of their method, particularly for large-scale networks where computational efficiency is critical. The method's ability to make reasonable predictions in seconds compared to IRM's minutes or hours represents a meaningful advance in the field.

The paper is a strong candidate for NIPS, as it addresses a significant problem with a novel solution that advances the state of the art in both computational efficiency and probabilistic modeling of hierarchical network structure. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and has clear potential for impact in network analysis.
