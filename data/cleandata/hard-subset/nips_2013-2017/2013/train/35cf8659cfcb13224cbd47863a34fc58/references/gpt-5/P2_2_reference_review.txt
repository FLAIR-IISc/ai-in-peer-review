Summary and relation to prior work
- The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for network community detection. It defines a prior over tree-consistent partitions (via Bayesian rose trees) and a blockmodel likelihood with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli terms that distinguish within- vs between-cluster edges. The marginal likelihood of a fixed tree is computed by a dynamic-programming recursion. A bottom-up agglomerative procedure selects merges by a local Bayes factor score, considering three merge types (join and two absorptions). Variants include a sparse version that forbids merging disconnected components and a binary-tree-restricted version. The authors present qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network, predictive performance on a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset compared to the IRM (both MCMC and greedy), and a large-scale visualization on the full NIPS network.
- Relation to prior work: The model builds directly on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.) by swapping the i.i.d. data likelihood for a relational blockmodel. It contrasts with the IRM (flat) and hierarchical extensions thereof, as well as limited-depth latent attribute models. The paper positions BHCD as a faster, greedy alternative to MCMC/variational nonparametrics. Closely related but un-cited work includes hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman, PNAS 2008) and nested SBMs, which also aim at hierarchical network structure and link prediction; these baselines would strengthen context.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: A clean mapping of rose-tree priors to relational data, yielding an exact marginal likelihood for a fixed tree and a simple recursion.
- Scalability: Inference uses cached sufficient statistics and a heap to achieve O(n^2 log n) worst-case, independent of the number of communities, with per-tree likelihood in O(n + m). The sparse variant is practical for large, sparse graphs.
- Practicality: Greedy agglomeration with local Bayes factors is easy to implement and fast in wall-clock time. The predictive recursion is linear in tree depth.
- Empirical evidence: BHCD reaches reasonable link-prediction quality much faster than IRM; qualitative structure on Sampson is sensible; it scales to full NIPS with interpretable clusters.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Baselines and scope: Comparisons are limited to IRM on NIPS-234. The paper does not compare to hierarchical network models such as hierarchical random graphs or nested/degree-corrected SBMs, which are natural baselines for hierarchy and link prediction. Nor does it compare to strong non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods beyond early modularity work.
- Quantitative claims: The abstract-level claim “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” seems inaccurate; the experiments show two orders of magnitude faster to reach comparable predictive performance than a full IRM run, not a single iteration. This should be clarified and toned down.
- Model expressivity: The likelihood assumes a single pair of Beta priors for all within- and between-blocks, which biases toward assortative structure and cannot capture heterogeneous densities or disassortative patterns without hyperparameter adaptation. While mixtures over tree-consistent partitions help, there are no cluster-specific parameters or degree correction.
- Evaluation breadth: No synthetic experiments to test recovery of known hierarchies; no ablations on γ or sensitivity to hyperparameters; limited discussion of variability across restarts. The sparse variant is motivated but only lightly evaluated.
- Theoretical guarantees: No analysis of when the local Bayes factor objective is consistent with the global posterior/MAP tree, or bounds on suboptimality introduced by locality.
- Clarity of references and reproducibility: Several references are incomplete; code availability is not stated. Algorithm 1 is described textually but full pseudocode and complexity accounting could be clearer.

Quality
- The technical core is sound: the partition prior over rose trees, the factorization of the marginal likelihood, and the dynamic-programming recursion appear correct and are appropriately justified by prior work. The Bayes factor scoring for merges is local but correctly accounts for the replacement of the between-IJ block. Complexity claims are plausible given the cached sufficient statistics.
- Empirical support is adequate for a fast, heuristic Bayesian method, but the evaluation is not comprehensive relative to the current literature on hierarchical network models. Claims about predictive competitiveness should be contextualized (e.g., ILA outperforms BHCD).

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable, with clear notation for σ, f, g, and the recursion. The explanation of merge types and caching is helpful. However, some reference entries are incomplete, and the bold claim about speed is confusing. Including full pseudocode and a clearer description of the heap maintenance would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The work is a thoughtful combination of known components: rose-tree priors + blockmodel likelihood + greedy agglomeration with local Bayes factors. The main novelty is adapting the rose-tree machinery to relational data with an efficient caching strategy and demonstrating its practical speed. Methodological originality is moderate but nontrivial.

Significance
- For practitioners needing fast hierarchical community discovery and link prediction with some Bayesian grounding, BHCD is appealing. Its scalability and simplicity are valuable. However, without comparisons to established hierarchical network models, it is hard to judge advances in state of the art beyond speed. The approach is likely to be built upon (e.g., adding degree correction, non-conjugate likelihoods, or richer priors).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the speed claim relative to “a single iteration” of the IRM, and report wall-clock comparisons under matched implementations/hardware.
- Add baselines against hierarchical random graphs and/or nested/degree-corrected SBMs, and consider modularity-based hierarchical clustering for runtime/quality comparison.
- Provide synthetic experiments with known hierarchies to assess recovery and sensitivity analyses for γ and Beta hyperparameters; report variance across restarts and the effect of the sparse constraint.
- Discuss applicability to directed/bipartite/weighted networks; provide details or experiments if feasible.
- Consider degree correction or cluster-specific parameters to mitigate assortativity bias; at least discuss trade-offs.
- Improve references and provide code or more implementation details.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good core method and sound derivations; evaluation adequate but incomplete.
- Clarity: mostly clear; some claims and references need polishing.
- Originality: moderate; solid adaptation rather than a fundamentally new model.
- Significance: moderate; strong on scalability/practicality, less so on state-of-the-art accuracy.

On balance, I see this as a useful, well-motivated contribution with clear strengths in speed and simplicity, but it would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and clarifications before being fully competitive at NIPS. I would lean toward borderline accept if the additional comparisons and clarifications can be addressed, otherwise borderline reject.
