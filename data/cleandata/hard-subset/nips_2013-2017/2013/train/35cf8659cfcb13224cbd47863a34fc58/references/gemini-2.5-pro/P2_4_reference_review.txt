### Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller and Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data, using a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) as the underlying likelihood model for network edges. The method defines a generative process over partitions that are consistent with a tree structure, which allows for the exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of the data given a specific hierarchy via dynamic programming. Inference of the hierarchy is performed using a greedy agglomerative search algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees to maximize a local Bayes factor. The authors demonstrate that their approach is significantly faster—by orders of magnitude—than comparable MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving competitive predictive performance.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, building logically on established work in both Bayesian hierarchical clustering and network modeling.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Model Derivation:** The derivation of the marginal likelihood for a given tree (Equation 10) is a key strength. By restricting the prior over partitions to only those consistent with a tree, the authors make the intractable sum over all partitions in the standard SBM (Equation 4) tractable. This is a clever and effective modeling choice.
2.  **Inference Algorithm:** The agglomerative search algorithm is clearly described and well-motivated. The use of a local Bayes factor (Equation 12) as the merge criterion is principled. The authors' attention to computational detail, specifically the caching scheme for sufficient statistics that allows for O(1) updates in the inner loop and an overall O(n² log n) complexity, is commendable and crucial for the paper's main contribution.
3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed to support the paper's claims. The qualitative analysis on the Sampson's Monastery network shows that the method finds interpretable structures. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 dataset against IRM is compelling, clearly demonstrating the massive speed advantage of BHCD (Figure 4). The final experiment on the full NIPS dataset showcases the method's ability to scale to larger networks where MCMC would be prohibitively slow.
4.  **Honesty about Limitations:** The authors are transparent about the trade-offs. They acknowledge that the greedy search can get stuck in local optima (addressed via restarts) and that their predictive performance, while good, is surpassed by more complex models like ILA. They also identify potential areas for improvement in the discussion, such as hyperparameter inference and better handling of sparse networks.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Greedy Search:** The primary limitation is the greedy nature of the inference. While fast, it offers no guarantee of finding a globally optimal hierarchy. The final structure is highly dependent on the path of merges, and the use of random tie-breaking means results can vary between runs. While restarts mitigate this, the paper does not analyze the variance of the results across these restarts.
2.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The Bayes factor score is computed locally (Equation 12), considering only the data within the two subtrees being merged. This makes an implicit assumption that the best local merge will lead to a good global structure, which may not always be true.
3.  **"Sparse" Variant:** The BHCD sparse variant, which prohibits merges between disconnected components, appears to be a heuristic modification rather than a direct consequence of the probabilistic model. While it performs well empirically and is intuitively appealing, its connection to the underlying generative model is not formally justified.

### Clarity

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The progression from the standard stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical formulation and finally to the agglomerative inference scheme is logical and easy to follow.

*   **Model Description:** Section 3 explains the tree-consistent partitions and the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood very well. Figure 1 is an effective visual aid.
*   **Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the inference procedure. The explanation of how sufficient statistics are cached and updated is sufficient for an expert to understand the implementation's efficiency.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide enough detail about the model, algorithm, and experimental setup (including hyperparameters) that reproducing the results should be feasible.

One minor point of confusion is the term `rS` in the predictive distribution (Equation 14). While defined mathematically, a more intuitive explanation of its role as the posterior probability of a subtree being the "active" cluster would be helpful.

### Originality

The work presents a novel combination of existing ideas to solve an important problem. While Bayesian hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels are not new, their synthesis in this manner is.

*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contribution relative to prior art. It clearly builds upon [13, 14] by adapting their general framework to the specific domain of relational data. It also clearly differentiates itself from MCMC-based methods like IRM [5] by proposing a much faster, deterministic (per run) inference scheme.
*   **Novel Contribution:** The primary novelty lies in demonstrating that this greedy Bayesian approach can be made highly efficient for network data and offers a practical and powerful alternative to more computationally intensive sampling methods. The specific adaptation of the sufficient statistics caching for SBMs is a key technical contribution that enables this.

### Significance

The significance of this work is high. Scalability is a major challenge for Bayesian nonparametric models, particularly in the network domain. MCMC-based inference for models like the IRM can be too slow for moderately large networks, limiting their practical application.

*   **Practical Impact:** BHCD provides a "good enough, fast enough" solution that is highly valuable for practitioners. The ability to obtain a reasonable hierarchical clustering and competitive link prediction results in seconds, rather than hours, makes Bayesian network analysis accessible for larger datasets and for interactive data exploration.
*   **Advancing the State of the Art:** The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art in terms of the speed-accuracy trade-off for Bayesian community detection. It establishes a strong baseline for fast, greedy inference in this space. Other researchers are likely to build on this work, for example, by exploring more sophisticated search procedures or by extending the framework to other relational data likelihoods.
*   **Problem Difficulty:** Inferring hierarchical structure in networks is a fundamentally hard problem. This paper presents an elegant, efficient, and principled approximate solution that significantly lowers the computational barrier to entry.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The paper's main claim of being orders of magnitude faster than a key competitor (IRM) is well-supported and represents a major practical advance.
2.  **Principled Framework:** The method is grounded in a proper Bayesian model, using a well-defined marginal likelihood and Bayes factors for model selection, which is preferable to purely heuristic agglomerative methods.
3.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** The application of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational SBMs is novel, effective, and clearly presented.
4.  **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, the model is technically sound, and the experiments are convincing.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Greedy Inference Limitations:** The greedy search is a significant theoretical weakness compared to MCMC methods that can, in principle, explore the full posterior. The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the potential pitfalls of this greedy approach.
2.  **Predictive Performance:** The method does not achieve state-of-the-art predictive performance, being outperformed by ILA on the NIPS-234 benchmark. The primary contribution is speed, not accuracy.

### Recommendation

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It addresses a critical problem (scalability of Bayesian network models) with a novel and exceptionally efficient solution. The work is technically solid, clearly communicated, and will be of significant interest to both researchers and practitioners in the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
