Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian method to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model builds on stochastic blockmodels with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors and extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering (BHC; Heller & Ghahramani) and Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al.) from i.i.d. vectors to relational data. A tree T over vertices induces a prior over the set of partitions consistent with T; the marginal likelihood of D under T can be computed exactly via a dynamic program with a recursion analogous to BHC. Inference is performed by a bottom-up agglomerative procedure that considers three merge types (join and two absorptions) and scores local merges with a Bayes factor using cached sufficient statistics, yielding O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant prohibits merges between disconnected components. The method produces link predictions by mixing on-diagonal and off-diagonal posteriors along a path in T.

Experiments qualitatively analyze Sampson’s monastery network, quantitatively compare to IRM (MCMC and greedy) on a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset (showing BHCD’s much faster time-to-reasonable-predictions and predictive performance comparable to LFRM and worse than ILA per prior work), and demonstrate a large hierarchy on the full NIPS coauthorship graph.

Relation to prior work is discussed: SBMs and MMSB for flat communities; IRM and hierarchical variants; Mondrian processes; BHC/Rose Trees for clustering. Compared to hierarchical probabilistic approaches for networks (e.g., IRM with tree augmentation), BHCD trades exact Bayesian inference for a greedy Bayesian model selection, gaining speed. There is related non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection (e.g., Girvan-Newman; Clauset-Newman-Moore), and Bayesian hierarchical network models not cited include the hierarchical random graph of Clauset, Moore, and Newman (Nature 2008) and degree-corrected hierarchical SBMs (later work), which would be relevant points of comparison.

Strengths
- Clear and principled construction: a tree-induced prior on partitions with an exact marginal over tree-consistent partitions, leading to a simple, efficient recursion for p(D|T).
- Efficient inference: careful caching of block sufficient statistics and local Bayes factor scoring yields O(n^2 log n) worst-case complexity, independent of the number of communities; a sparse variant is practical on real graphs.
- Practical performance: on NIPS-234, BHCD attains reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than IRM; qualitative results on Sampson’s network are sensible; large-scale hierarchy on full NIPS is demonstrated.
- Honest positioning: authors acknowledge that greedy inference aims at speed, not SOTA predictive accuracy, and that other models (ILA) can perform better.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- The merge score (Eq. 12) is purely local to the merged set, ignoring the effect on edges from the merged set to the remainder of the forest beyond σIJ. While this mirrors BHC’s locality assumption, in graphs this may lead to suboptimal merges in the presence of degree heterogeneity or long-range dependencies. Discussion of this bias and possible remedies (e.g., incorporating degree-corrected likelihoods or global adjustment terms) would strengthen the work.
- The model uses a standard Bernoulli SBM without degree correction. This is known to be problematic on networks with heavy-tailed degrees; extending f/g to degree-corrected SBMs would likely improve accuracy without losing conjugacy.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed and not inferred; no sensitivity analysis is provided. Given that γ controls tree arity and α,β,δ,λ encode strong within/between priors, robustness should be assessed.
- Experimental comparisons are limited. IRM is a reasonable baseline; however, given the focus on hierarchy, comparison to hierarchical random graphs (Clauset et al., 2008) or Mondrian-based relational models would be informative. The claim that BHCD is two orders faster than a single IRM iteration seems extreme; please clarify measurement methodology.
- Predictive complexity: the text states prediction is linear in tree depth and then claims Θ(n). Please clarify; it should be O(depth), typically much smaller than n.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with a clear exposition of the model and algorithm. Notation occasionally becomes dense (e.g., DTT, DSS, σ¬chSS) and Eq. (13) mixes symbols M/I/J/X/Y; a pass to standardize symbols would help readability.
- Some references are incomplete (years/pages) and contain typos (e.g., “Erix P. Xing”). Consider adding Clauset-Moore-Newman (Nature 2008) for hierarchical random graphs.
- The dynamic programming complexity claim O(n+m) for a fixed tree is plausible but would benefit from a brief derivation or pseudocode explaining how σSS and σ¬chSS are computed in total linear time.

Originality
- The combination of BHC-style tree-consistent partition priors and marginalization with SBMs, plus the specific agglomerative scheme and caching for relational data, is novel to my knowledge. While conceptually related to BHC/Rose Trees, adapting these ideas to networks with an SBM likelihood and deriving a practical O(n^2 log n) algorithm is an original and useful contribution.

Significance
- The approach provides a practical tool for fast hierarchical community detection with a Bayesian objective, filling a gap between expensive nonparametric models (IRM + hierarchy) and heuristic clustering. The speed advantage and simplicity make it attractive for exploratory analysis and as a baseline. The lack of degree correction and limited comparisons reduce immediate impact on SOTA link prediction but do not diminish its utility.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive are results to γ and to the within/between priors? Can you learn γ (e.g., Empirical Bayes) or cross-validate it?
- Could the sparse variant’s complexity be characterized more precisely in terms of m? Are there data structures to achieve o(n^2) behavior provably on sparse graphs?
- Would incorporating degree-corrected SBMs (with conjugate priors) be straightforward within your f/g framework?
- For NIPS-234, did BHCD results reported use a single run or the R=50 restart average? Please report wall-clock time including restarts to ensure fair comparison.
- Consider adding a comparison to hierarchical random graphs or modern hierarchical SBMs on at least one dataset.

Pros
- Elegant Bayesian formulation with exact marginal over tree-consistent partitions.
- Fast, scalable inference with careful engineering.
- Sensible qualitative hierarchies; good time-to-accuracy in prediction tasks.
- Clear, reproducible algorithmic description.

Cons
- Limited baselines for hierarchical models; no degree correction.
- Greedy, local merges may be myopic; tie-breaking randomness introduces variance.
- Fixed hyperparameters; no robustness analysis.
- Minor clarity and reference issues.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a well-motivated, efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery with clear derivations and practical utility. Addressing degree correction, clarifying complexity/experimental details, and broadening comparisons would further strengthen it.
