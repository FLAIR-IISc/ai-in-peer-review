This paper presents a novel Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to leverage a greedy approach within a Bayesian framework, offering a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the IRM. The authors build upon previous work on Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering and adapt it to relational data.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and contextualizes the proposed approach within existing literature, correctly identifying limitations of prior methods (parametric SBMs, computationally expensive IRM-based approaches, and depth-limited hierarchies). The technical details are presented in a logical order, starting with a review of stochastic blockmodels and culminating in the description of the BHCD algorithm.  The presentation of the model, while dense, is reasonably clear. The inclusion of the marginal likelihood equations (2, 3, 6, 10) and the explanation of the score function (12) demonstrates a solid theoretical understanding. 

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work lies in its computational efficiency.  The authors convincingly argue and demonstrate (Table 1, Figure 4) that BHCD significantly outperforms IRM, particularly for larger networks. The claimed O(n^2 log n) complexity, and the further optimization via BHCD sparse, is a major contribution.
* **Bayesian Framework:**  Employing a Bayesian approach provides a principled way to handle uncertainty and incorporate prior knowledge.
* **Hierarchical Structure:** The method naturally discovers hierarchical community structure, a feature often missing in simpler flat community detection algorithms.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the explanations are, for the most part, understandable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The comparison with IRM is particularly strong.
* **Practical Algorithm:**  The detailed description of the algorithm, including the caching scheme for sufficient statistics, indicates a focus on practical implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) is crucial for performance, yet the paper provides limited justification for the specific values used. A more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results. It’s mentioned they "found these to work well", but lacks rigor.
* **Model Assumptions:** The use of Beta priors, while simplifying computation, might be restrictive. The authors acknowledge the potential benefits of non-conjugate priors, but don't explore this direction in detail.
* **Scalability for Extremely Large, Sparse Networks:** While the method scales well compared to IRM, the O(n^2 log n) complexity might still be a bottleneck for extremely large, sparse networks. The authors acknowledge this and propose considering triangle representations, but this is left as future work.  The sparse variant helps, but a more systematic investigation of scaling behavior on very large sparse graphs is needed.
* **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** While the comparison to IRM is strong, the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art hierarchical community detection algorithms (e.g., those based on modularity optimization or label propagation).
* **Visualization of Large Hierarchies:**  The visualization of the full NIPS hierarchy (Figure 5) only shows a partial view.  More sophisticated visualization techniques might be needed to effectively convey the full structure of large hierarchies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in network analysis – hierarchical community detection – with a novel and computationally efficient approach.
* The proposed method (BHCD) demonstrably outperforms existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (IRM) in terms of speed without sacrificing accuracy.
* The technical details are well-presented, and the empirical results are convincing.
* The code is likely to be useful for researchers in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The hyperparameter selection is not thoroughly justified, raising concerns about the robustness of the results.
* The scalability to extremely large, sparse networks remains a potential limitation.
* The comparison to alternative hierarchical community detection algorithms is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. The computational efficiency of BHCD is a significant advantage, and the Bayesian framework provides a principled approach to hierarchical community detection. While there are some weaknesses, they are not severe enough to warrant outright rejection.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Perform a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the performance of the algorithm.
2.  **Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations:** Elaborate on the challenges of applying BHCD to extremely large, sparse networks and discuss potential solutions beyond triangle representations.
3.  **Include a more comprehensive comparison to alternative hierarchical community detection algorithms:** Compare BHCD to at least a few other state-of-the-art methods.
4. **Improve visualization of large hierarchies:** Explore more effective visualization techniques for conveying the full structure of large hierarchies discovered by BHCD.
