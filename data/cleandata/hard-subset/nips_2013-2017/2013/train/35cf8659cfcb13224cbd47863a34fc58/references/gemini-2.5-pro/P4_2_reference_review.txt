This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative clustering method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The method is framed within a Bayesian model selection context. The core of the model is a likelihood for the network's adjacency matrix given a tree structure, p(D|T). This likelihood is computed by marginalizing a stochastic blockmodel likelihood over all partitions of the network's vertices that are consistent with the given tree. The prior over these partitions and the likelihood factorization allow for an efficient O(n+m) dynamic programming algorithm to compute p(D|T). Building on this, the paper proposes a bottom-up greedy search algorithm to find a good hierarchy T. Starting with each vertex as a singleton tree, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of trees that yield the highest local Bayes factor, until a single tree remains. The authors leverage an efficient caching scheme for sufficient statistics, leading to an overall O(n^2 log n) time complexity for the inference procedure.

This work builds directly upon previous research in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, specifically [13] and [14], by adapting that framework to relational data. It positions itself as a practical alternative to other Bayesian nonparametric approaches for community detection, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5, 6] and its hierarchical extensions [8, 12]. While those methods often rely on computationally intensive MCMC sampling, BHCD provides a deterministic, much faster, greedy alternative. It also provides a full probabilistic treatment, unlike non-Bayesian greedy methods such as [3, 4].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Eq. 10) is correct. The agglomerative algorithm for model selection is a standard and reasonable heuristic for navigating the combinatorial space of possible hierarchies. A key technical contribution is the careful caching of sufficient statistics (Eq. 13) that enables O(1) time complexity for the inner loop of the merge algorithm, which is critical for the overall efficiency of the method. The claims are well-supported by the experiments. The authors are honest about the greedy nature of their inference algorithm and address the issue of local optima by performing multiple random restarts. The evaluation is thorough, including a qualitative analysis on a classic social network dataset and a quantitative comparison of runtime and predictive performance against a relevant baseline (IRM).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The progression from the standard stochastic blockmodel in Section 2 to the hierarchical model in Section 3 is logical and easy to follow. The generative process and the resulting recursive formula for the marginal likelihood are explained clearly. Figures 1 and 2 are very effective at illustrating the concepts of tree-consistent partitions and the different types of merges considered by the algorithm. The description of the agglomerative search procedure in Section 4 and Algorithm 1 is clear and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the implementation. The experimental section presents its results clearly, with informative plots and tables.

**Originality:** The primary novelty of this work lies in the successful application and adaptation of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of [13, 14] to the problem of community discovery in relational data. While the core algorithmic structure is not new, its extension to a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, including the formulation of the likelihood and the specific efficient updates for the relational sufficient statistics, is a novel contribution. This work effectively bridges the gap between fast, heuristic methods for hierarchical clustering and slower, more principled Bayesian nonparametric models. The consideration of "absorption" merges in addition to simple "joins" is also a novel feature in this context. The work is well-situated within the literature and adequately references prior contributions.

**Significance:** The main significance of this paper is its practical impact. The problem of scalable inference for network models is of great importance to the NIPS community. The experimental results show that BHCD can achieve predictive performance comparable to that of the IRM but is orders of magnitude faster. This dramatic speed-up makes Bayesian inference for hierarchical community structure feasible on networks that would be computationally prohibitive for existing MCMC-based methods. Researchers and practitioners looking for a fast, probabilistic method to explore the hierarchical structure of their network data are very likely to find this method useful. While the approach is greedy and may not provide the theoretical guarantees of a sampler that explores the full posterior, its speed and performance create a highly compelling trade-off. It represents a significant advance in the practical application of Bayesian methods to network analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important problem of scalable inference for hierarchical community detection.
*   The proposed method is technically sound, combining existing ideas in a novel and effective way.
*   The paper is written with exceptional clarity.
*   The experimental results are strong, demonstrating a significant improvement in computational efficiency over a key baseline (IRM) with comparable predictive accuracy. This makes the method highly significant from a practical standpoint.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The inference algorithm is greedy and therefore heuristic, with no guarantees of finding a globally optimal structure.
*   The core ideas (Bayesian hierarchical clustering, stochastic blockmodels) are not new; the main novelty is in their combination and efficient implementation for a new domain.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its impressive computational efficiency and strong empirical results on a significant problem, far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a complete and well-executed piece of work that is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS audience.
