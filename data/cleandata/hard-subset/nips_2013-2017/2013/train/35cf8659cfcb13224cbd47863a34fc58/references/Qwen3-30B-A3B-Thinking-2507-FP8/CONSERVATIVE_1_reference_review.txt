

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for discovering community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering work [13, 14] and extend it to relational data, claiming significant computational advantages over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The method represents communities as a hierarchical tree structure, with each internal node corresponding to a community and leaves representing vertices. The authors develop a greedy agglomerative model selection procedure for learning the hierarchy, claiming BHCD runs two orders of magnitude faster than IRM with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n).

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a sound mathematical foundation grounded in established Bayesian approaches to community detection. The method appears to be a logical extension of previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and the algorithmic description is sufficiently detailed for reproduction.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and validation, which undermine its scientific contribution. The main weakness is the insufficient empirical validation to support the paper's key claims. The authors claim BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" but provide no runtime comparisons across multiple network sizes. The experimental results only show speed comparisons on a single network (NIPS-234), without demonstrating how the speed advantage scales with network size. The paper lacks a proper complexity analysis showing the actual runtime improvement across different network sizes, which is essential for supporting such a strong claim.

More critically, the paper fails to provide adequate validation of the quality of the discovered communities. While the authors show qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network, they don't provide quantitative metrics for community quality (e.g., normalized mutual information, modularity, or other standard community detection metrics) compared to ground truth or other methods. The claim that "BHCD finds the most likely tree" is not supported with evidence beyond log likelihoods, which don't necessarily correlate with meaningful community structure. The paper doesn't demonstrate that the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD is actually better than what could be achieved with simpler flat community detection methods.

The paper also fails to thoroughly compare BHCD to established flat community detection methods (e.g., stochastic blockmodel, LFRM, ILA) that might be more appropriate for the problem at hand, rather than just comparing to other Bayesian hierarchical methods. This is a significant oversight, as most practical applications of community detection use flat community structures.

Furthermore, the authors acknowledge that BHCD "does not yield a full probabilistic account of the data," which is a fundamental limitation compared to the Bayesian methods it claims to outperform in speed. The paper doesn't adequately address this trade-off between computational efficiency and the quality of the probabilistic model.

The paper's experimental section lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameter selection and sensitivity analysis. While the authors mention using α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, and γ=0.4, they don't explain why these specific values were chosen or how sensitive the results are to these parameters. The lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis weakens the experimental validation.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a computational optimization to an existing approach, rather than a fundamentally new method. While the optimization may be useful, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support the claimed advantages. Without thorough validation against established baselines and clear evidence that the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD is meaningful and useful, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of this work.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting computational optimization, the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and the absence of clear evidence demonstrating superior community quality make it difficult to endorse as a significant contribution to the field. The paper would be substantially strengthened by comprehensive experimental validation across multiple network sizes, quantitative metrics for community quality, and a thorough comparison to both flat community detection methods and other hierarchical approaches.

Given the reviewer's preference for established methods and skepticism of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims about computational advantages to be overstated without sufficient evidence. The paper presents an interesting computational optimization, but the lack of rigorous validation makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work to the field. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence supporting the claims and lack of thorough validation against established baselines.
