### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering, previously developed for i.i.d. data (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2012), to relational data. The method defines a probabilistic model over trees where each tree implies a set of consistent, flat partitions of the network's nodes. The marginal likelihood of the data given a tree is computed efficiently by summing over these partitions using a dynamic programming recursion. Inference for the tree structure is performed using a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm. At each step, the algorithm merges the pair of subtrees that yields the greatest improvement in the marginal likelihood, as measured by a local Bayes factor. The authors present a clever caching scheme that allows the algorithm to run in O(n² log n) time, making it significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which typically rely on slower MCMC inference. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness through qualitative analysis on the Sampson monastery network and quantitative comparisons on the NIPS co-authorship dataset, showing a dramatic speed advantage over IRM with comparable predictive performance.

### Review

This is a well-executed and valuable contribution that addresses a significant bottleneck in the application of Bayesian nonparametric models to network analysis: computational scalability. The paper is clearly written, the method is technically sound, and the experimental results are compelling.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood for a tree (Equation 10) is a key contribution, providing an elegant and efficient way to score a given hierarchy under a principled probabilistic model. The agglomerative search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a sensible and practical choice for inference. The claim of O(n² log n) complexity is well-supported by the description of the caching scheme for sufficient statistics, which is a crucial detail for making this approach viable.

The experimental evaluation is strong. The authors provide both a qualitative analysis on a classic social network dataset (Sampson's monastery) and a rigorous quantitative comparison on a real-world network (NIPS co-authorship). The comparison against IRM is highly relevant, as IRM is a cornerstone model in this area. The results in Figure 4 are striking, demonstrating that BHCD achieves comparable predictive accuracy to IRM in a fraction of the time (seconds vs. thousands of seconds). The authors are also honest about the method's performance relative to other models (ILA), which is commendable. The work is a complete piece, presenting a model, an efficient inference algorithm, and a solid empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of situating their work within the existing literature, clearly distinguishing it from previous approaches like flat blockmodels, non-probabilistic clustering, and more computationally intensive Bayesian methods. The model is introduced logically, building from the standard stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical formulation. The recursive likelihood calculation, which is the technical core of the model, is explained well. The figures are informative, especially Figure 1 which provides a helpful illustration of tree-consistent partitions, and Figure 4 which powerfully visualizes the speed-accuracy trade-off. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics and potential for implementation.

**Originality:** The work's originality lies in the successful synthesis and extension of several powerful ideas. The concept of Bayesian hierarchical clustering using a marginal likelihood score is not new (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005), nor is the idea of non-binary "rose trees" (Blundell et al., 2012). However, the application of this framework to relational data, using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood, is novel and non-trivial. The design of the efficient O(1) update for the sufficient statistics within the greedy search loop is a key technical innovation that enables the method's impressive scalability. While greedy agglomerative clustering is a standard technique, its use here to optimize a well-defined Bayesian model score is a principled departure from purely heuristic methods. The work clearly carves out a new niche between fast but heuristic methods and principled but slow Bayesian sampling methods.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. A major barrier to the widespread adoption of Bayesian nonparametric models for network analysis has been their computational cost, which often limits their use to small- or medium-sized networks. This paper provides a practical and principled alternative that is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based competitors. The demonstrated ability to obtain good results on a network of ~300 nodes in seconds, and to scale to a network of ~3000 nodes, makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling accessible for a much wider range of problems and practitioners. Researchers are very likely to use this method and build upon its ideas. It advances the state of the art by showing that a carefully designed greedy search over model space can be a viable and highly effective alternative to sampling for this class of models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Major Scalability Improvement:** The paper convincingly demonstrates a two-order-of-magnitude speedup over a key baseline (IRM), addressing a critical, long-standing problem in the field.
*   **Principled Approach:** Unlike many fast clustering algorithms, BHCD is based on optimizing a well-defined Bayesian model's marginal likelihood, providing a probabilistic interpretation of the resulting hierarchy.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves predictive performance comparable to or better than IRM, showing that the speedup does not come at the cost of accuracy.
*   **Clarity and Novelty:** The paper is clearly written and presents a novel combination of existing ideas to create a powerful new tool for network analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance (Minor Weaknesses):**
*   **Greedy Inference:** The main limitation is the greedy nature of the inference, which is susceptible to local optima. While the use of multiple restarts mitigates this, the paper could benefit from a short discussion on the potential gap between the found solution and the global optimum.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters are fixed, and there is no discussion of their sensitivity or methods for setting them. While this is a common practice, a brief analysis or justification would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Baselines:** While the comparison to IRM is excellent, comparing against other fast hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004) on predictive tasks could have provided additional context, even if those methods are not Bayesian.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in scalable Bayesian modeling for networks. The strengths—particularly the impressive speedup, principled foundation, and strong empirical results—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
