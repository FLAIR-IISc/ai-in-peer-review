- Introduces BHCD: a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for community detection in networks.
- Extends Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data via a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels.
- Develops dynamic programming for efficient marginal likelihood computation and a caching scheme.
- Claims large speedup (≈100×) over a single IRM iteration and O(n^2 log n) runtime independent of community count.
- Evaluated on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks with qualitative trees and predictive performance.
- Correctly positions itself relative to flat community models (SBM, MMSBM) and fully Bayesian nonparametrics (IRM, Mondrian).
- Distinguishes contribution from prior Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering work and emphasizes scalability.
- Strength: substantial computational efficiency enabling large-network analysis.
- Strength: fully probabilistic framework allowing uncertainty quantification and principled model comparison.
- Strength: scalability due to runtime independence from number of communities.
- Strength: empirical validation showing speed-accuracy tradeoffs and useful qualitative results.
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation with helpful pseudocode and derivations.
- Weakness: hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) fixed in experiments without sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to scalable non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering baselines (e.g., spectral methods).
- Weakness: treatment of binary vs. non-binary tree variants is underdeveloped and rationale is unclear.
- Weakness: emphasis on predictive accuracy overshadows deeper qualitative interpretation of discovered hierarchies.
- Weakness: Bayes factor score (eq. 12) lacks a stronger formal justification or analysis of properties.
- Qualitative: method is technically sound and dynamic programming approach is well-justified.
- Qualitative: notation can be dense but explanations are generally adequate.
- Qualitative: approach is original in combining Bayesian hierarchical clustering with greedy scalable inference for relational data.
- Qualitative: significant potential impact for fast, scalable Bayesian community detection.
- For acceptance: major computational improvement, sound motivation and technique, clear presentation, and comprehensive experiments.
- Against acceptance: insufficient hyperparameter analysis, limited non-Bayesian baselines, and underdeveloped discussion of tree variations.
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Required revisions: add hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, compare to non-Bayesian baselines, elaborate binary vs. non-binary tree tradeoffs, and provide deeper community interpretation for NIPS results.
