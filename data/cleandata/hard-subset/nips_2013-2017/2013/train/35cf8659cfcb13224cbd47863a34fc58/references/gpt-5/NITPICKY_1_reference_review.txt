Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method that learns a tree-structured hierarchy of communities in a graph. The model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs) with conjugate beta-Bernoulli likelihoods. Following Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al., 2010) and Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005), a tree defines a restricted set of partitions over which the marginal likelihood is computed exactly via dynamic programming. Inference constructs the tree bottom-up with three merge types (join and two absorptions), scored by a local Bayes factor. The paper claims O(n^2 log n) time for inference with caching and O(n+m) time for evaluating the marginal likelihood of a fixed tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data show qualitative hierarchies and link prediction performance competitive with IRM at a fraction of the wall-clock time.

This work sits between hierarchical community detection and Bayesian nonparametric network models. Related Bayesian approaches include IRM and its hierarchical extensions; nonparametric latent feature models (LFRM, ILA); multiscale/hierarchical block models; and dendrogram-based hierarchical random graph (HRG) models. The paper builds most directly on Bayesian Rose Trees by transferring the prior over tree-consistent partitions to a relational likelihood.

Strengths
- Clear and computationally efficient greedy framework; careful caching and local scoring make the algorithm practically fast.
- Exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions given a tree; simple conjugate collapsed form.
- Three-way merge choice (join/absorb) is a sensible adaptation of Rose Trees to relational data.
- Empirical speed advantage over IRM is plausible and valuable for large networks; qualitative results on real data are reasonable.
- Predictive distribution derivation (recursion using responsibilities rS) is neat and fast (linear in depth).

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and modeling
- Inconsistent and arguably incorrect prior specification: the text requires α>β (denser within) and δ<λ (sparser between), but experiments set α=δ=1.0 and β=λ=0.2. This makes within- and between-block priors identical (mean 0.83), contradicting the stated inductive bias towards communities and yielding an extremely dense prior for typically sparse networks. This is a substantial flaw that undermines claims and could materially affect results.
- Undirected graph double counting: Eq. (2) multiplies over p,q∈φ, p≠q for off-diagonal blocks. For undirected graphs with σpq=σqp, this product double-counts unless products are restricted to p<q and statistics defined accordingly. The paper notes σpq=σqp but does not adjust the product; this needs clarification/correction.
- Predictive complexity inconsistency: Sec. 4.2 says prediction is linear in tree depth, but ends with “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n).” It should be O(depth) (≤ O(n)).
- Dynamic programming complexity claim: “The marginal likelihood (6) may be calculated… in O(n+m) time.” For a fixed tree, recursion in Eq. (10) is O(#nodes), but computing σSS and σ¬chSS for all internal nodes in general is nontrivial; the O(n+m) bound is asserted without a derivation showing how to compute all needed sufficient statistics in a single pass over edges. Provide a concrete algorithm or cite.
- Memory complexity is not discussed. Maintaining σIJ for all current tree pairs to get O(1) inner-loop updates implies O(|F|^2) memory, i.e., O(n^2), which can be prohibitive for large n.
- “BHCD sparse” disallows merges between trees with no edges. This is a heuristic that can be harmful under missing data or noisy sparse graphs; the generative model can prefer merges even with zero observed edges if within- vs between-block posteriors warrant it. Justify theoretically or provide ablations.
- Sampson data handling: treating negative affiliations as observed absent edges collapses signed relations into binary, potentially conflating antagonism with non-interaction. This modeling choice should be discussed and, ideally, evaluated against alternatives (e.g., two-layer or signed graph models).

Novelty and related work
- The core framework is very close to Bayesian Rose Trees adapted to beta-Bernoulli relational likelihoods. The paper should more carefully delineate what is novel beyond this adaptation (e.g., specific scoring, caching, complexity).
- Missing key related work: hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008) and nested/multilevel SBMs that explicitly model hierarchical community structure; degree-corrected variants; dendrogram priors (coalescent, Dirichlet diffusion tree) applied to networks. The Multiscale Community Blockmodel is cited, but the HRG literature is a notable omission.
- The Mondrian process is mischaracterized as a prior for relational data; it is typically for space partitioning.

Experimental methodology and clarity
- Comparisons appear unfair/incommensurate: BHCD curves show a full run per point while IRM shows single Gibbs steps or search steps; IRM needs many steps to mix. Reporting wall-clock is fine, but then normalize methods by comparable stopping criteria (e.g., until convergence) and report final performance with uncertainty.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is not explored; no hyperparameter inference. Given the prior issue above, this is critical.
- Results lack numerical tables and variance/error bars; many claims are qualitative. Provide exact AUC/accuracy/log-prob means and standard errors, and final converged performance for all methods.
- The number of BHCD variants is inconsistent: text says four variants, later “six variants,” and “BHCD global” appears without prior definition.
- Cutting the full NIPS tree using rT ∏(1−rA)>0.5 is ad hoc; justify or evaluate alternative thresholds.

Clarity, presentation, and formatting
- Numerous typos, grammar issues, and inconsistencies (e.g., “Physics Review E,” “Erix P. Xing,” missing years/pages in references). Several references are incomplete or malformed.
- Some notation is imprecise (e.g., σ{x}{x}=0 “for each vertex x” should mean no self-edges; define how counts are computed for undirected off-diagonals).
- Claims like “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” are unclear and likely hyperbolic; define “iteration.”

Questions for the authors
- Please resolve the contradiction between the stated prior (α>β, δ<λ) and the experimental settings (α=δ=1, β=λ=0.2). Re-run experiments with priors that encode within>between, and/or perform hyperparameter inference.
- Provide a precise algorithm/proof for the O(n+m) likelihood computation over a fixed tree, including memory usage.
- Clarify undirected handling in Eq. (2): are off-diagonal terms over unordered pairs p<q with appropriate counts?
- Define “BHCD global” and reconcile the number of variants used in experiments.
- Report full numerical results with standard errors, and fair final comparisons to IRM (and, ideally, HRG/multiscale SBMs).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, fast hierarchical model with exact marginalization given a tree.
- Practical speedups and reasonable predictive performance on real data.
- Clear link prediction mechanism and scalable greedy inference.

Cons for acceptance
- Major inconsistency in prior specification; potential invalidation of experiments.
- Incremental novelty relative to Bayesian Rose Trees; missing core related work (HRG).
- Methodological and clarity issues (double counting, complexity claims, fairness of comparisons).
- Presentation quality and referencing below NeurIPS standards.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline if major issues are fixed), primarily due to the prior inconsistency, incomplete related work, and experimental/methodological clarity. Confidence: High.
