### **Summary**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core contribution is a fast, greedy, agglomerative clustering algorithm that builds a hierarchy from the bottom up. The model itself can be viewed as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where a tree structure defines a prior over consistent partitions of the network's vertices. The key innovation lies in the inference scheme: rather than employing computationally intensive MCMC or variational methods common to Bayesian nonparametric models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), the authors adapt the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data. At each step, the algorithm greedily merges sub-trees based on the move that provides the largest increase in the marginal likelihood (i.e., the highest local Bayes factor). The authors demonstrate that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on link prediction tasks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Bold and Novel Approach to Inference:** The most significant strength of this paper is its bold departure from the standard inference playbook for complex Bayesian relational models. While most prior work has focused on developing more sophisticated MCMC samplers or variational approximations to characterize a complex posterior, this paper takes a pragmatic and effective model selection approach. Casting the problem as a greedy search through the space of tree structures, guided by a principled Bayesian score, is a novel and refreshing perspective in this domain. This is not merely an incremental tweak to an existing sampler; it is a fundamentally different and much faster way to approach the problem.

2.  **Dramatic Improvement in Scalability:** The practical impact of this novel approach is substantial. The experiments compellingly show that BHCD can produce high-quality results in seconds, while a competitor like the IRM requires thousands of seconds to converge to a similar performance level. This two-order-of-magnitude speed-up is a significant achievement. It pushes the boundary of what is feasible, making Bayesian hierarchical community detection practical for much larger networks than previously possible with related nonparametric methods. The paper's claim of an O(n^2 log n) runtime, independent of the number of communities, is a major advantage over methods whose complexity scales with the number of inferred clusters.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the value of the proposed method. The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network show that BHCD finds interpretable and meaningful hierarchies that align with known sociological structures. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS co-authorship dataset clearly establishes the method's superior speed-accuracy trade-off compared to the IRM. The authors are honest about the performance relative to other models (like ILA), which lends credibility to their evaluation.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Greedy Search and Local Optima:** The primary methodological limitation is the greedy nature of the algorithm. The bottom-up agglomerative procedure is deterministic (given a tie-breaking rule) and can get trapped in local optima. A merge decision, once made, is never revisited. This is a significant approximation compared to MCMC methods which, given enough time, can escape local modes and explore the full posterior distribution. While the authors wisely employ multiple random restarts to mitigate this, the solution is still a point estimate (the best tree found) rather than a posterior distribution over hierarchies. However, I view this not as a fatal flaw but as a deliberate and well-justified engineering choice. The immense gain in speed is a direct result of this compromise, and the strong empirical results suggest it is a highly effective one.

2.  **Dependence on Prior Work:** The core algorithmic machinery is a direct adaptation of the Bayesian Rose Trees framework [14] to the relational data setting. One could argue this reduces the originality of the contribution. However, the adaptation is non-trivial, requiring a new likelihood model for network blocks (Eq. 2) and an efficient caching scheme for the sufficient statistics. More importantly, successfully applying this framework to solve a major scalability bottleneck in a different domain is a significant contribution in its own right.

3.  **Fixed Hyperparameters:** The model's performance relies on a fixed set of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). The paper provides a setting that "worked well" but does not explore sensitivity to these parameters or provide a method for their inference. This is a common limitation in papers focused on inference speed, but it means that practitioners would need to perform a grid search to find optimal settings for new datasets.

### **Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly effective approach to a well-known and challenging problem. While a critic focused purely on methodological purity might object to the greedy nature of the inference, such a view would miss the forest for the trees. The authors have traded theoretical completeness for a massive leap in practical utility, developing a method that is orders of magnitude faster than its Bayesian nonparametric predecessors while maintaining competitive performance. This is precisely the kind of work that can unlock new applications and inspire follow-up research. The paper introduces a significant new direction for inference in relational models, prioritizing speed and scalability without sacrificing Bayesian principles. Its potential impact is high, and it represents a clear and valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
