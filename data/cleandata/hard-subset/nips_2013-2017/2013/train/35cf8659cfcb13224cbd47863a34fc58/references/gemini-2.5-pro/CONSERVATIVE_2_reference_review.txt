This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to adapt the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data. The model defines a marginal likelihood for the network data given a tree structure by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. This marginal likelihood can be calculated efficiently via dynamic programming. The hierarchy itself is then constructed in a bottom-up, greedy agglomerative fashion, where at each step, two subtrees are merged based on the move that maximizes a local Bayes factor. The primary contribution and claimed advantage of this work is its computational efficiency, with the authors demonstrating that it produces reasonable results significantly faster than MCMC-based inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Framework:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the proposed model. The formulation of the marginal likelihood `p(D|T)` as a recursion (Equation 10) is elegant and provides a principled, tractable scoring function for a given hierarchy. The connection to established work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering is clear and logical.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The main strength of the paper is the method's speed. By replacing computationally intensive MCMC sampling with a fast, deterministic greedy search, the authors have developed a tool that can analyze networks on a timescale that is orders of magnitude faster than comparable nonparametric Bayesian models. The `O(n^2 log n)` complexity is favorable for dense graphs, and the experimental results in Figure 4 convincingly demonstrate a substantial speed-up over the IRM.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its core inference strategy. The method is not performing Bayesian inference over hierarchies, but rather a greedy model selection for a single tree structure. This agglomerative approach is well-known to be susceptible to local optima. The decision to merge two trees is based on a *local* Bayes factor (Equation 12), which only considers the data within the sub-graphs of the trees being merged. This makes a strong and unsubstantiated assumption that the optimal global hierarchy can be constructed from a series of locally optimal decisions. This greedy heuristic lacks the theoretical grounding of MCMC or variational methods, which attempt to approximate the full posterior distribution. While the authors use multiple restarts to mitigate this, it remains a fundamental limitation of the approach.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate speed, they are not sufficiently rigorous to support the claims of quality and are lacking in scope.
    *   The comparison to the IRM in Figure 4 is potentially misleading. It compares full runs of BHCD against individual Gibbs steps (or single search steps) of the IRM. A more honest comparison would be time-to-convergence or time-to-best-held-out-likelihood. While the IRM is indeed slow, the presentation exaggerates the performance gap.
    *   The predictive performance is not state-of-the-art. The authors' own results show that BHCD is outperformed by the Infinite Latent Attributes (ILA) model on the NIPS-234 dataset. This undermines the argument that the method achieves "good results quickly," as it appears to trade a significant amount of accuracy for speed. For a NIPS paper, simply being faster than an older baseline (IRM) is not a sufficient contribution if accuracy is sacrificed relative to other contemporary methods.
    *   The set of baselines is too narrow. The introduction mentions several related Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection ([8], [12]), but no experimental comparisons are made. To properly situate the work, a comparison against at least one other dedicated Bayesian hierarchical model would be necessary.
    *   The analysis on the "Full NIPS" dataset is purely qualitative. While it demonstrates scalability, it offers no quantitative assessment of the discovered hierarchy's quality. A link prediction experiment on this larger dataset would have been a much stronger piece of evidence.

3.  **Originality:** The work is a straightforward application of the Bayesian Rose Tree model [14] to relational data using a standard Stochastic Blockmodel likelihood. The technical novelty is therefore limited to the derivation of the specific update rules for the SBM's sufficient statistics within the agglomerative clustering framework. This feels more like a solid workshop paper than a significant conceptual advance for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** The paper proposes a novel and extremely fast heuristic for finding hierarchical community structures.
*   **Pro:** The probabilistic model for scoring a fixed tree is sound and computationally efficient.
*   **Pro:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

*   **Con:** The greedy, local-decision-based inference procedure is methodologically weak and prone to local optima, a significant step back from more principled Bayesian inference methods.
*   **Con:** The experimental comparison is weak. It uses a potentially misleading metric for speed comparison and shows that the method is outperformed in accuracy by existing models. The set of baselines is insufficient.
*   **Con:** The work's originality is limited, as it largely combines two existing lines of research (Bayesian hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels).

In conclusion, while the paper presents a fast and interesting heuristic for an important problem, its methodological shortcomings and the limitations of its experimental validation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The reliance on a greedy search without theoretical guarantees, coupled with experimental results that do not demonstrate state-of-the-art performance in terms of accuracy, makes the contribution fall short of the high standards expected for NIPS. The work prioritizes speed over rigor and performance, a trade-off that is not well-justified by the results presented.
