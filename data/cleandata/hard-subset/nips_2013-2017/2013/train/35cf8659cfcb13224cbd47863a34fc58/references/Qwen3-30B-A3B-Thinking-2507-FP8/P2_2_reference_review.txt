

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

## Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, extending them to relational data while maintaining a principled probabilistic framework. The key innovation is an efficient greedy agglomerative inference procedure that computes the marginal likelihood of hierarchical community structures in O(n² log n) time, making it significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods. The authors demonstrate their approach on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS dataset, showing that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while being two orders of magnitude faster.

The paper situates itself within the context of community detection literature, noting that while flat community structure has been extensively studied (e.g., stochastic blockmodel, mixed membership stochastic blockmodel), hierarchical approaches have been limited by computational constraints. Previous Bayesian methods either restrict to shallow hierarchies or have poor computational scaling. BHCD addresses this by combining a Bayesian nonparametric approach with an efficient greedy inference algorithm.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-motivated mathematical formulations. The authors provide clear derivations of the marginal likelihood for tree-structured partitions, the greedy inference procedure, and the efficient sufficient statistics caching mechanism that enables their O(n² log n) time complexity. The experimental results support their claims about computational efficiency, with BHCD being approximately 100x faster than IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on the NIPS co-authorship network.

The authors demonstrate appropriate care in evaluating their method's strengths and limitations. They acknowledge that the greedy approach might not find the globally optimal solution, discuss the trade-offs between binary and non-binary tree structures, and provide empirical evidence on when sparse variants perform better. The paper also includes a thorough comparison of computational complexity with existing methods (Table 1), clearly showing BHCD's advantage in worst-case complexity.

One minor limitation is the lack of theoretical analysis of the approximation error introduced by the greedy approach. While the empirical results support the method's effectiveness, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the greedy procedure's performance guarantees would strengthen the paper.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and motivates the need for hierarchical community discovery. The mathematical formulations are presented with sufficient detail, and the figures (particularly Figure 1 and Figure 2) effectively illustrate key concepts like the tree-structured partitions and the different merge operations.

The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly clear, with detailed explanations of the sufficient statistics updates and the heap-based greedy selection procedure. The authors provide a good balance between mathematical rigor and intuitive explanations, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian methods and network analysis.

The experimental section is well-structured, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines and clear presentation of results. The qualitative analysis of the Sampson's monastery network provides valuable insight into the method's behavior.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis by addressing a clear gap in the literature. While hierarchical community discovery has been studied, previous Bayesian approaches have been computationally expensive or limited to shallow hierarchies. BHCD's key novelty is the efficient greedy inference procedure that maintains a Bayesian interpretation while scaling to larger networks.

The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous approaches:
- Unlike [5, 6, 7], BHCD does not require the number of communities to be known a priori.
- Unlike [8, 9, 10, 11], BHCD does not require the hierarchy depth to be known.
- Unlike [12], BHCD does not have prohibitive computational costs for larger networks.

The paper properly cites all relevant prior work, including the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), Mondrian process, and previous hierarchical clustering methods. The authors clearly articulate how BHCD improves upon these methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Hierarchical community structure is important in many applications, from social network analysis to biological networks, and the computational bottleneck has limited the applicability of Bayesian approaches. BHCD directly addresses this bottleneck, making Bayesian hierarchical community discovery feasible for larger networks.

The empirical results demonstrate that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to the IRM (one of the fastest Bayesian nonparametric models) while being orders of magnitude faster. The authors show that BHCD can make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan, which is a significant practical advantage.

The paper also opens several promising directions for future work, including adapting the method to non-conjugate likelihoods, exploring parameterizations using triangle statistics, and investigating ways to improve scalability for sparse networks. These suggestions are well-motivated and could lead to meaningful extensions of the current work.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear and well-structured presentation of a novel approach
- Strong experimental validation with real-world networks
- Significant computational efficiency gains over existing methods
- Careful consideration of limitations and trade-offs
- Practical impact demonstrated through real-world applications
- Clear mathematical formulation with well-justified assumptions

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of the greedy approximation
- Could provide more detailed comparison with recent hierarchical community detection methods
- The tie-breaking strategy in the greedy algorithm (random tie-breaking) could potentially lead to inconsistent results, though the authors address this with multiple restarts

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed BHCD method addresses a real problem with a well-motivated solution, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing approaches. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the state of the art.

The main contribution is the efficient inference algorithm that enables Bayesian hierarchical community discovery to scale to larger networks while maintaining a principled probabilistic framework. This is a practical contribution that could have a significant impact on the field, as previous Bayesian methods were limited by computational constraints.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NeurIPS. The paper's quality, clarity, originality, and significance all meet the high standards of the conference.

## Additional Comments

The authors could potentially strengthen the paper by:
1. Providing a more detailed comparison with recent hierarchical community detection methods that have emerged since the cited works (e.g., recent papers on hierarchical stochastic blockmodels)
2. Including a theoretical analysis of the greedy approximation error
3. Exploring the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the discovered hierarchies

However, these are minor suggestions, and the paper is already of high quality and ready for publication as is.
