Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a probabilistic hierarchical clustering approach for graphs. The model defines a prior over partitions constrained by a given tree T (a hierarchy whose leaves are vertices and internal nodes are communities). The likelihood is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels: at each internal node S, with probability πS the leaves of S form a cluster whose within-block edges are generated by an “on-diagonal” model f; otherwise, edges between children of S are generated by an “off-diagonal” model g and the process recurses on children. For the beta-Bernoulli case, f and g are conjugate marginal likelihoods with potentially different hyperparameters for within- and between-community edges. The marginal likelihood p(D|T) admits a dynamic-programming recursion across the tree. Structure learning uses a greedy agglomerative procedure with three merge types (join and two absorptions) scored by a local Bayes factor; careful caching of sufficient statistics leads to O(n^2 log n) time overall. A “sparse” variant forbids merges between subtrees that have no connecting edges. Link prediction is done by a recursive mixture along a root-to-leaf path. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship (234-node subset and full 2864-node graph) show that BHCD recovers interpretable hierarchies and achieves predictive performance comparable to LFRM and better than IRM while being much faster.

Relation to prior work
BHCD builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani) and Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al.), extending them from i.i.d. vectors to relational data via blockmodel likelihoods. Compared to IRM, BHCD constrains the partition sum to those consistent with T, enabling exact marginalization over partitions for a fixed T with DP. Previous hierarchical network models include hierarchical IRM, two-level latent attribute models (ILA, IMMM), and Mondrian process priors. Important related work that should be discussed includes hierarchical/nested stochastic block models (e.g., Clauset et al., PNAS 2008; Peixoto’s nested DCSBM/MDL formulations) which directly infer hierarchical communities and scale well.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea: a tree-indexed mixture over partitions with a conjugate f/g blockmodel yields tractable p(D|T) and efficient prediction.
- Greedy structure learning is computationally attractive; caching local sufficient statistics and using a local Bayes factor leads to O(n^2 log n) with small constants.
- Non-binary merges (absorptions) allow more natural rose trees than strictly binary HAC.
- Empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups over IRM while maintaining competitive predictive performance; qualitative structure on real networks is sensible.
- The sparse variant is a pragmatic improvement for large sparse graphs.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Model expressivity: The likelihood assumes only two rates per split (within vs between), shared across all pairs of children at a node. This prevents modeling heterogeneous inter-community densities (e.g., group A connects strongly to B but weakly to C) and disassortative or core–periphery structures. Compared to general (degree-corrected) SBMs or nested SBMs, this is restrictive and may bias hierarchies.
- Degree heterogeneity: No degree correction; Beta-Bernoulli on edges often overfits hubs or splits by degree instead of community. This likely explains some misplacements and limits predictiveness relative to ILA/DCSBM.
- Hyperparameters: There is an inconsistency. The text states α>β and δ<λ to encode higher within- than between-density, but experiments use α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, which make within and between priors identical and violate δ<λ. This undermines the stated modeling bias and calls into question the results. At minimum, clarify whether this is a typo (e.g., δ=0.2, λ=1.0) and provide sensitivity analyses or hyperparameter inference.
- Greedy search: Purely agglomerative with local Bayes factors can make irreversible early mistakes and is not guaranteed to optimize the global marginal likelihood over T. Restarts only partially mitigate this. No post-merge local refinements (e.g., subtree rotations, split-and-merge moves) are considered.
- BHCD-sparse: Forbidding merges without edges is ad hoc and can preclude identifying meaningful structures (e.g., hierarchical bipartite communities where absence-of-edges is informative). This choice is aligned with an assortative bias but should be discussed as a modeling assumption.
- Evaluation: 
  - Comparisons are limited (IRM and literature-reported LFRM/ILA). Missing strong hierarchical baselines such as nested (degree-corrected) SBMs (and, in modern context, Peixoto’s MDL methods).
  - Predictive metrics are shown over time, but numerical summaries, error bars, and sensitivity to restarts/hyperparameters are missing. Fairness of IRM settings (burn-in, convergence) is unclear.
  - No synthetic experiments with controlled hierarchical ground truth to assess recovery accuracy, robustness to degree heterogeneity, or to quantify when BHCD’s simplifying assumptions help/hurt.
- Clarity/reproducibility: The core recursion is presented clearly, but Algorithm 1 is referenced without full pseudocode and some implementation details are deferred (e.g., precise data structures, memory complexity). Public code availability is not stated.

Quality
Technically sound in the core derivation: the DP for p(D|T) and predictive recursion are correct given the model. The agglomerative score is a proper Bayes factor between forests. However, the hyperparameter inconsistency is serious and must be resolved. The empirical section supports speed claims but is limited in breadth and ablations.

Clarity
Generally well written with helpful notation and explanations. Suggestions: include full pseudocode; add a brief proof sketch of the recursion; detail the caching scheme and its memory footprint; be explicit about directed/symmetric handling and missing data treatment; report exact predictive numbers and variances.

Originality
Extends Bayesian rose-tree ideas to relational data with a tailored blockmodel and efficient scoring. However, relative to existing hierarchical community models (hierarchical IRM, nested SBMs), the contribution is incremental and trades modeling power for speed.

Significance
The main value is a very fast, simple hierarchical community discovery method with competitive predictive accuracy on standard datasets. If hyperparameters are fixed and the assortative bias is acceptable, BHCD is a practical tool. Broader impact is limited by lack of degree correction and restricted inter-group modeling.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify the hyperparameters used in experiments; correct the δ,λ mismatch and report sensitivity analyses or infer hyperparameters.
- Discuss how to extend BHCD to allow heterogeneous between-block rates at each split or degree correction while retaining tractability.
- Add comparisons to hierarchical/nested (degree-corrected) SBMs and top-down or split-merge heuristics.
- Provide synthetic benchmarks with known hierarchies and controlled degree distributions.
- Release code and document data preprocessing (e.g., handling of missing edges).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a neat and efficient adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to networks with strong empirical speed advantages. However, the modeling limitations, missing key related work, limited evaluation, and especially the hyperparameter inconsistency reduce confidence. With corrections, stronger baselines, and more thorough experiments (and ideally degree-corrected/heterogeneous extensions), this would be much stronger.
