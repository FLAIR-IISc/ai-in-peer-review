

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method for inferring hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches and extend them to relational data, using a greedy agglomerative procedure to learn tree-structured community hierarchies. The key contribution is a novel algorithm that claims to be two orders of magnitude faster than existing Bayesian methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n). The paper presents theoretical foundations, an efficient inference algorithm, and experimental results on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship networks.

## Strengths
The paper presents a well-structured and clearly written technical contribution that addresses an important problem in network analysis. The hierarchical Bayesian model is logically developed, and the inference algorithm is described with sufficient detail. The experimental results demonstrate reasonable performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency. The authors provide a clear explanation of the model's advantages over existing approaches, particularly regarding scalability.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies and Errors
1. **Complexity Claim Inconsistency**: The paper claims BHCD has "worst case run-time quadratic in the number of vertices" (Section 1), but the algorithm's complexity is stated as O(n² log n) in Section 4.1. This is a significant technical error—O(n² log n) is not quadratic (which would be O(n²)), and this discrepancy undermines the paper's main scalability claim.

2. **Equation Notation Errors**: 
   - In Equation (10), the notation "p(DCC|C)" should be consistent with "p(DTT|T)" as "p(DCC|C)" would imply a different structure. The correct notation should be "p(DCC|C)" where C represents the subtree.
   - In Equation (12), the score is defined as "SCORE(M; I, J) = p(DMM|F?) / p(DMM|F)", but the notation is inconsistent with the forest notation used earlier.

3. **Algorithm Description Error**: In Algorithm 1, the line "σIJ = σXJ + σY J for J ∈ F, J ≠ I" contains a notational error. It should be "σIJ = σXI + σYI" (or similar) to maintain consistency with the definition of σIJ as the sufficient statistics between sets I and J.

4. **Typos**: "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap" (Section 5) should be "led astray" (not "lead").

### Insufficient Experimental Detail
1. The paper claims BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" but provides no concrete timing measurements to support this claim. Without specific runtime data, this assertion lacks empirical grounding.

2. The comparison with IRM on the NIPS-234 dataset lacks sufficient detail. The paper states "BHCD sparse makes as good predictions as the IRM after about 1000 seconds" but doesn't provide the actual runtime for BHCD sparse or the IRM on the same hardware.

3. The paper mentions "BHCD sparse is faster than the non-sparse variants" but doesn't provide comparative timing results for the sparse vs. non-sparse versions on the NIPS dataset, making it difficult to assess this claim.

### Conceptual Clarity Issues
1. The distinction between "sparse" and "binary" variants (Section 4.1) is not clearly explained. The paper states "BHCD sparse restricted to binary trees" but doesn't clarify what "restricted to binary trees" means in the context of the sparse variant.

2. The paper claims that "the hierarchies found can be more difficult to interpret than the non-binary hierarchies" but doesn't provide specific examples or evidence for this claim, which weakens the argument.

### Formatting and Consistency Issues
1. The paper uses inconsistent notation for the adjacency matrix (sometimes "D", sometimes "D" with subscripts like "DTT" or "DII"), which creates confusion.

2. Several references in the bibliography are incomplete (e.g., "Social Networks, 5:109137, , " has extra commas).

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a methodologically sound approach to hierarchical community detection with promising scalability claims, the technical inconsistencies and lack of concrete experimental validation significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The complexity claim error alone is substantial enough to question the paper's technical rigor. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading to address the numerous notational inconsistencies and typos, and from providing more detailed experimental results to support the speed claims.

The contribution is not sufficiently novel compared to existing work on hierarchical community detection. While the authors build upon previous work by Heller et al. [13, 14], the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from these prior methods in a way that constitutes a significant advancement.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper has some merit, the technical errors, lack of concrete experimental validation for key claims, and formatting inconsistencies are substantial enough to prevent this paper from meeting NIPS standards for technical rigor. A revised version addressing these issues would be necessary before reconsideration.
