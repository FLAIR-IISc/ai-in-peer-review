### Paper Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in network data. The core idea is to extend the Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data, specifically using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The model defines a marginal likelihood for the observed network given a specific tree structure, which is calculated efficiently by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree using dynamic programming. To learn the tree structure itself, the authors propose a greedy agglomerative algorithm. Starting with each node in its own tree, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of trees that yield the largest increase in the Bayes factor. This approach is deterministic and computationally efficient, with a time complexity of O(n² log n). The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness through experiments on Sampson's monastery network for qualitative interpretation, and on the NIPS co-authorship network for quantitative comparison against the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The results show that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to the IRM but is two orders of magnitude faster.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses a well-known and important problem: discovering hierarchical communities in networks. While many Bayesian nonparametric models exist for this (or related) problems, they often rely on slow MCMC inference, limiting their applicability. BHCD provides a principled Bayesian alternative that is significantly faster, making it a highly practical tool for analyzing medium-to-large networks where sampling-based methods would be computationally prohibitive. The speed-accuracy trade-off presented is very compelling.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of building up their model, starting from the standard stochastic blockmodel, introducing the tree-based prior and likelihood, and finally detailing the agglomerative inference algorithm. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) and the Bayes factor score for merging (Eq. 12) are clearly presented. The figures, especially the qualitative results on the monastery network, are illustrative and support the claims effectively.

3.  **Novelty and Soundness:** While the core ideas of Bayesian agglomerative clustering and tree-based marginal likelihoods build directly on previous work ([13, 14]), their application to network community discovery is novel and well-executed. The extension involves defining a relational likelihood suitable for the framework and, crucially, designing an efficient caching scheme for the sufficient statistics (Eq. 13) that allows the greedy search to maintain its O(n² log n) complexity. The model is technically sound and provides a full probabilistic account of the data given the inferred tree.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed to showcase the method's different strengths.
    *   The Sampson's monastery experiment provides a good qualitative validation, showing that BHCD finds interpretable and meaningful community structures that align with known sociological findings.
    *   The NIPS-234 experiment provides a strong quantitative comparison against a relevant baseline (IRM). The plots of performance vs. time clearly demonstrate the primary contribution of the paper: achieving good results very quickly.
    *   The application to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's scalability to a network with thousands of nodes.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The main limitation, inherent to the chosen approach, is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. While fast, this bottom-up procedure is susceptible to local optima and cannot capture uncertainty over the tree structures themselves. The use of random restarts for tie-breaking is a reasonable heuristic, but it only partially mitigates this issue. Competing MCMC-based methods, though slower, have the advantage of exploring the posterior distribution more fully. The authors are honest about this trade-off, but it remains a significant conceptual difference.

2.  **Locality of the Merge Criterion:** The merge score (Eq. 12) is based only on the data within the sub-blocks of the two trees being considered for a merge. This local decision-making process is key to the algorithm's efficiency but is a strong assumption. A merge that looks optimal locally might not be part of the globally optimal hierarchy. It would be valuable to discuss the potential impact of this assumption more explicitly.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The primary comparison is against the IRM. While this is a very relevant baseline in the Bayesian nonparametric space, the comparison could be strengthened. It would be interesting to see how BHCD stacks up against:
    *   Other fast, non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods (e.g., the algorithm by Clauset, Newman, and Moore, 2004) to better situate its performance in the broader community detection literature.
    *   Other scalable Bayesian models for network data, even if they infer flat structure, to provide a more complete picture of the speed/accuracy landscape.

4.  **Hyperparameter Setting:** The hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed across all experiments. The paper states that these values were "found to work well," which lacks rigor. A sensitivity analysis or a brief discussion on the effect of these parameters would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness. For example, how does the choice of γ influence the "bushiness" of the resulting tree?

### Overall Evaluation and Recommendation

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. It successfully bridges the gap between fast, heuristic hierarchical clustering methods and slower, fully Bayesian nonparametric models. The proposed BHCD method is elegant, clearly explained, and empirically demonstrated to be both fast and effective. The main selling point—achieving performance comparable to IRM in a fraction of the time—is a significant practical achievement.

The weaknesses, primarily related to the greedy nature of the inference, are acceptable trade-offs for the massive gains in computational efficiency. The paper is well-written, the work is novel in its application, and the results are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working with network data.

**Recommendation: Accept.**

**Minor suggestions for improvement:**
*   Acknowledge the locality assumption of the merge score more explicitly in the discussion of weaknesses.
*   If space permits, add a short paragraph discussing hyperparameter sensitivity or potential strategies for setting them (e.g., empirical Bayes).
*   In the related work section, briefly mentioning fast non-Bayesian hierarchical methods would provide better context for the reader.
