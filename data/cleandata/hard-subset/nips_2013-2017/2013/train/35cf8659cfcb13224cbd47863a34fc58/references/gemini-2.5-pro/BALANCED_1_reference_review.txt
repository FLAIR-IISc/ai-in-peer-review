**Summary**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach builds upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2013) and adapts it to relational data. The underlying generative model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). The model defines a probability distribution over tree structures, where the marginal likelihood of the network data given a tree is calculated efficiently via dynamic programming by summing over all tree-consistent partitions. Inference of the hierarchy is performed using a bottom-up greedy search, where pairs of subtrees are merged at each step to maximize the local Bayes factor. The authors demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than comparable Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving competitive predictive performance on link prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The primary contribution is a fast and principled Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection. The claimed speed-up of two orders of magnitude over MCMC-based inference for the IRM is substantial and addresses a critical bottleneck for applying Bayesian nonparametric models to larger networks. The method's O(n² log n) worst-case time complexity, which is independent of the number of communities, is a significant advantage over methods whose per-iteration cost scales with the number of inferred clusters.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper presents a novel and elegant combination of existing ideas: the likelihood from stochastic blockmodels, the marginalization over tree-consistent partitions from Bayesian rose trees, and a greedy agglomerative search framework. The adaptation of the caching scheme to handle relational sufficient statistics, enabling O(1) computation in the inner loop of the greedy search, is a key technical contribution that ensures the algorithm's efficiency. The model is technically sound and provides a full probabilistic interpretation of the resulting hierarchy.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building up the model from the basic SBM (Section 2) to the hierarchical extension (Section 3) and finally the inference algorithm (Section 4). The recursion for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) and the Bayes factor score for merges (Eq. 12) are clearly defined. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the inference procedure.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed to showcase the method's strengths.
    *   The qualitative analysis on Sampson's monastery network demonstrates that BHCD finds interpretable and reasonable hierarchies that align with established sociological findings.
    *   The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 dataset against the IRM is highly effective. The performance-vs-time plots (Figure 4) clearly illustrate the primary claim: BHCD provides good results much faster than its MCMC-based competitor.
    *   The application to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's ability to scale to networks with thousands of nodes.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The main weakness of the proposed method is its greedy nature. Greedy search is prone to finding locally optimal solutions that may be far from the global optimum. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of this limitation. For example, are there specific network structures (e.g., with significant long-range dependencies) where the local merge criterion (Eq. 12) is likely to fail? Contrasting this with the ability of MCMC methods to (in theory) escape local modes and explore the full posterior would provide a more balanced perspective.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are fixed across all experiments. The paper states that the chosen values "work well," but provides no sensitivity analysis. This is a crucial point for practitioners. How robust is the model to different settings of these parameters? A brief study in the supplementary material showing performance as these hyperparameters are varied would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of practical utility.

3.  **Comparison to Non-Probabilistic Methods:** The introduction mentions fast, non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004). While the focus on Bayesian competitors is understandable, a comparison against at least one such state-of-the-art method would be very informative. This would help to contextualize BHCD's performance not just against other Bayesian methods, but within the broader landscape of community detection algorithms, clarifying the trade-offs between speed, probabilistic rigor, and the quality of the discovered communities (e.g., measured by modularity).

4.  **Clarity on "BHCD global":** The caption for Figure 3 mentions that "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap". The term "BHCD global" is not defined in the text, and the reason for scores becoming "out of date" is unclear. I assume this refers to the non-sparse variant of BHCD. The phrasing might imply a bug, but it is more likely an inherent property of using a heap for greedy search where a merge that looked promising initially becomes a poor choice after other merges have altered the global structure. The authors should clarify this point in the text to avoid confusion.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. It successfully combines ideas from different areas to create a method that is fast, principled, and effective. The significant speed improvements over established Bayesian nonparametric methods make it a compelling choice for analyzing large-scale networks. While the greedy nature of the inference is a limitation, the authors are transparent about their approach, and the empirical results are convincing. The weaknesses identified above are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
