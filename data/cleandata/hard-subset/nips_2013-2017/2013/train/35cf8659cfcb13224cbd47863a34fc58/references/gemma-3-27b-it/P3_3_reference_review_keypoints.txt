- Proposes BHCD: a greedy Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery using a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels.
- Emphasizes computational efficiency, achieving ~100x speedup over a single IRM iteration via caching and algorithmic choices.
- Claims scalability with O(n^2 log n) time complexity and further improvements for sparse networks.
- Frames the approach in a principled Bayesian way, enabling uncertainty quantification for community structure.
- Provides a clear, detailed mathematical description of the model and likelihood/prior computations.
- Introduces a "sparse" BHCD variant to improve performance on sparse networks.
- Empirically validated on synthetic data, Sampson’s monastery network, and a NIPS co-authorship subset; shows good link-prediction performance.
- Correctly situates the work relative to prior Bayesian clustering and relational models, building naturally on Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering.
- Compares favorably to IRM and competitively to LFRM in experiments, highlighting a favorable speed-accuracy trade-off.
- Falls short of ILA in predictive accuracy; lacks deep analysis of this performance gap.
- Hyperparameter choices (α, δ, β, λ, γ) are insufficiently justified and lack sensitivity analysis.
- Experiments focus on shallow hierarchies; method’s behavior on deeper, more complex hierarchies is not explored.
- Random tie-breaking during merges may introduce variability; alternative strategies were not investigated.
- Lacks a comprehensive comparison to fast non-Bayesian hierarchical or modularity-based methods.
- Reviewer argues for acceptance due to novelty, technical soundness, and practical speed improvements.
- Reviewer notes arguments against acceptance: not state-of-the-art predictive performance, limited hyperparameter analysis, and shallow-hierarchy focus.
- Overall assessment: strong submission recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
- Suggested revisions: add hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and expand NIPS experiments to include a detailed comparison to ILA.
