This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data. The model defines a marginal likelihood over a specific tree structure by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. This is made computationally tractable by using a specialized stochastic blockmodel (SBM) likelihood with different conjugate priors for within-community and between-community edges. Inference is performed not via MCMC, but with a fast, bottom-up agglomerative clustering algorithm. At each step, the algorithm greedily merges the two sub-trees that provide the largest increase in the marginal likelihood (i.e., the best Bayes factor). This approach cleverly circumvents the slow mixing and high per-iteration cost of traditional Bayesian nonparametric models like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), positioning BHCD as a practical and scalable alternative for discovering hierarchical network structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of hierarchical community detection in a scalable, probabilistic framework. While the underlying machinery (Bayesian hierarchical clustering, SBMs) is not new, their combination is novel and effective. Applying the recursive marginal likelihood calculation from the Bayesian Rose Tree model to a relational SBM likelihood is a significant contribution. The resulting method provides a full probabilistic account of the data and the discovered hierarchy, a feature lacking in many fast, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithms.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The main strength of BHCD is its speed. The authors demonstrate that their method is orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of a comparable MCMC-based method (IRM). The O(n^2 log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, makes it applicable to moderately large networks where MCMC methods would be infeasible. The experimental results in Figure 4 provide compelling evidence of this, showing BHCD achieving strong predictive performance in seconds, while the IRM takes over 15 minutes to converge to a similar level.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of building up the model, starting from the standard SBM (Section 2), introducing the hierarchical extension and the key marginal likelihood recursion (Eq. 10 in Section 3), and then detailing the greedy inference algorithm (Section 4). The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and the explanation of the efficient caching of sufficient statistics (Eq. 13) provide sufficient detail for the expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the work.

4.  **Experimental Quality:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. They cover three key aspects:
    *   **Qualitative evaluation:** The analysis on Sampson's monastery network shows that the method finds interpretable and meaningful community structures that align with prior sociological studies.
    *   **Quantitative comparison:** The link prediction task on the NIPS-234 dataset provides a rigorous comparison against a key baseline (IRM), clearly demonstrating BHCD's superior speed-performance trade-off. The authors are also honest about their model's performance relative to other state-of-the-art models like ILA.
    *   **Scalability:** Applying the model to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates its utility on larger real-world networks.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The primary weakness of the method is its greedy nature. The bottom-up agglomerative search is susceptible to local optima and the quality of the final tree can depend heavily on the initial merges, especially when ties are broken randomly (as the authors note). While using multiple restarts is a pragmatic solution, the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the potential pitfalls of this greedy approach compared to MCMC methods that can, in principle, escape local modes.

2.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The merge score (Eq. 12) is based only on the data local to the two trees being merged. The paper justifies this with the assumption that "local community structure should depend only on the local connectivity structure." This is a strong assumption that may not hold in all cases. A brief discussion of network types or scenarios where this locality assumption might be problematic would add nuance.

3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The main baseline is the IRM. While this is an important and relevant model, the field has produced many other scalable community detection methods. Comparing only to IRM (and referencing results for LFRM and ILA) feels slightly limited. A comparison against a state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical method (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004) on the link prediction task could provide a more complete picture of where BHCD stands in the broader landscape.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed across all experiments. While the chosen values seem reasonable, the paper would benefit from a short discussion or supplementary experiment on the sensitivity of the results to these settings. How does the balance between α/β and δ/λ affect the discovered structure?

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant problem with a novel and elegant combination of existing Bayesian techniques.
*   The dramatic improvement in computational efficiency over standard Bayesian nonparametric approaches is a major practical contribution.
*   The paper is extremely clear, well-structured, and presents a complete piece of work.
*   The experimental validation is strong, demonstrating interpretability, quantitative performance, and scalability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The inference method is greedy and may not find the optimal hierarchy, a limitation that could be discussed in more detail.
*   The set of experimental baselines, while reasonable, could be expanded to include more recent or non-Bayesian competitors for a fuller comparison.

Overall, this is an excellent paper. It presents a valuable and practical method that significantly advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian community detection. The strengths, particularly the impressive speedup and clear presentation, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
