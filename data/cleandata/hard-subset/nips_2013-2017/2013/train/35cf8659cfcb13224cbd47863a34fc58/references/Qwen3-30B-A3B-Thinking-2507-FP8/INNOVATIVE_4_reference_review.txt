

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: existing Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection are computationally prohibitive for large networks, while non-Bayesian approaches lack probabilistic guarantees. BHCD builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14] but extends it to relational data with a key innovation: a dynamic programming approach that enables efficient inference with O(n² log n) time complexity.

The paper's main novelty lies in its efficient greedy inference algorithm that scales well to large networks. The authors formulate the problem as a Bayesian nonparametric model where communities are organized into a tree structure, and they develop a dynamic programming approach to compute the marginal likelihood efficiently. This allows BHCD to be two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM (one of the fastest Bayesian nonparametric methods) while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The authors demonstrate this on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network, showing that BHCD discovers meaningful hierarchical structure while significantly outperforming existing Bayesian approaches in computational efficiency.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by bridging the gap between theoretical Bayesian models and practical scalability for large networks. Previous Bayesian hierarchical approaches [8,9,10] suffered from poor scalability due to complex inference algorithms, while non-Bayesian hierarchical methods [3,4] lacked probabilistic guarantees. BHCD provides a scalable Bayesian alternative that maintains the probabilistic interpretation of community structure.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Clear novelty in the efficient inference algorithm that enables practical use of Bayesian hierarchical community detection
- Strong empirical demonstration of the computational advantage (2 orders of magnitude faster than IRM)
- Good theoretical foundation with clear probabilistic model formulation
- Well-organized presentation with clear algorithm description and experimental results
- Appropriate comparison to relevant baselines (IRM, LFRM, ILA)

Weaknesses include:
- The paper doesn't fully explain why the conjugate prior choice is sufficient (non-conjugate priors might yield better predictive performance as noted in the discussion)
- The novelty compared to [14] (which also introduced Bayesian hierarchical clustering) isn't as clearly articulated as it could be
- The theoretical analysis of the algorithm's properties is somewhat limited (e.g., no formal convergence guarantees)

However, these weaknesses are minor in the context of the paper's significant contribution. The authors have made a bold, novel contribution that directly addresses a substantial limitation in the field: the computational infeasibility of Bayesian hierarchical community detection for large networks. The speedup is not just incremental—it's a two-order-of-magnitude improvement that makes hierarchical community detection practical at scale.

The significance of this work is substantial. Hierarchical community structure is common in many real-world networks (social, biological, etc.), and having a scalable Bayesian method to discover it opens up new avenues for network analysis. The authors demonstrate this through compelling experiments on real-world networks, showing that BHCD can discover meaningful hierarchical structure while being practical for large networks.

In terms of originality, while the paper builds on [13,14], the extension to relational data with the efficient inference algorithm represents a significant advance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by focusing on computational efficiency without sacrificing the Bayesian interpretation of community structure.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field of network analysis. The authors have successfully addressed a significant bottleneck in the application of Bayesian hierarchical community detection to large-scale networks. While the theoretical novelty might not be revolutionary, the practical impact is substantial, and the paper demonstrates how theoretical advances can lead to tangible improvements in computational efficiency.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS' focus on impactful, novel machine learning approaches. The authors have taken a bold approach to addressing a real computational bottleneck, and their method has clear practical implications for network analysis at scale. The minor weaknesses noted are outweighed by the paper's significant contribution to making Bayesian hierarchical community detection feasible for large networks.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it presents a novel and impactful approach to an important problem in network analysis. The practical significance of the speedup combined with the Bayesian interpretation of community structure makes this a valuable contribution to the field.
