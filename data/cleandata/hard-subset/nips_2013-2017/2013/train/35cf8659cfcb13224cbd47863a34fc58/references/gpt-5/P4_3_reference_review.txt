Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian agglomerative method for discovering hierarchical communities in networks. The model views observed edges via a stochastic blockmodel whose parameters are integrated out, with different beta-Bernoulli priors for within- and between-community edges. A tree T (a “rose tree”) induces a prior over partitions; the marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed exactly by dynamic programming via a recursion that mixes over all tree-consistent partitions, with a stopping probability at each internal node πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| (following Bayesian rose trees).

Learning proceeds by bottom-up agglomeration starting from singletons. At each step, two current trees are merged using one of three local merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right); the merge chosen maximizes a Bayes factor that depends only on the data local to the merged leaves. Caching of sufficient statistics enables O(1) updates per candidate merge, leading to overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant disallows merges between completely disconnected trees. Predictive link probabilities are computed by recursively mixing on- and off-diagonal posteriors along the path in the learned tree.

Empirically, BHCD is applied to Sampson’s monastery network (qualitative structure recovery) and to NIPS coauthorship data (NIPS-234 subset for predictive performance vs. IRM; full NIPS for qualitative hierarchy). BHCD attains predictive performance comparable to LFRM (via comparison to published results), worse than ILA, and better than IRM, while being orders of magnitude faster than IRM in wall-clock time.

This work sits at the intersection of stochastic blockmodels, Bayesian nonparametrics for networks (IRM and extensions), and Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Bayesian rose trees). The approach is distinct from flat community models (SBM, IRM) and from methods that impose fixed-depth hierarchies. It is related to dendrogram-based hierarchical network models (e.g., hierarchical random graphs), but differs by integrating over tree-consistent partitions under a rose-tree prior with conjugate likelihoods and using a greedy merge objective derived from marginal likelihoods.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic model is clearly specified. The prior over partitions induced by a tree and the dynamic programming recursion for p(D|T) are consistent with prior work on Bayesian rose trees, and the beta-Bernoulli specialization ensures tractable integrals. The Bayes-factor-based local merge scoring is coherent with the model and supports efficient greedy optimization with cached sufficient statistics. The prediction procedure is a straightforward posterior predictive mixture along a path. Assumptions (undirected binary networks, conjugacy) are explicit.
- Limitations: The method is entirely greedy with no global optimality guarantees; reliance on random tie-breaking and restarts is a practical workaround but not theoretically justified. The “sparse” variant’s prohibition on merging disconnected trees is heuristic and could be problematic with missing data. Hyperparameters are fixed and chosen to prefer assortative structure; there is no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference. Experimental comparisons to LFRM/ILA are indirect (via prior papers) and do not include runtime comparisons or confidence intervals. The predictive complexity claim in Section 4.2 states Θ(n) but is effectively O(depth), which is typically much smaller.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The modeling and inference sections are clear, and the recursion is easy to follow. Some notational choices (σ¬chSS and aggregation identities) could benefit from a short proof or figure to reassure readers that edge counts are not double-counted across ancestors. The term “BHCD global” appears in the Sampson discussion without prior definition. The predictive complexity statement should be corrected to O(depth). Providing a short pseudocode for Algorithm 1 (referred to but not shown) would aid reproducibility.

Originality:
Adapting Bayesian rose-tree clustering to relational data via a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels and deriving an efficient greedy agglomerative procedure with exact marginal likelihood evaluation is, to my knowledge, novel relative to flat IRM and mixed-membership models. The three-way merge operations and caching scheme follow prior rose-tree work but are innovatively applied to blockmodel sufficient statistics. Closely related hierarchical network models include hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008), which are not cited; BHCD’s generative semantics and inference differ, but a direct comparison and discussion would strengthen the contribution.

Significance:
The main value is practical: a simple, scalable Bayesian approach that discovers interpretable hierarchical communities and delivers competitive predictive performance at much lower computational cost than common nonparametric baselines. This is useful for exploratory analysis and fast link prediction in large graphs. On the other hand, it does not surpass state-of-the-art predictive models and lacks theoretical guarantees, so its primary impact is likely to be as a fast, interpretable alternative rather than a new SOTA in accuracy.

Pros:
- Clear, principled probabilistic model with exact marginal likelihood via DP.
- Very fast greedy inference with careful caching; worst-case O(n^2 log n).
- Practical sparse variant and restart strategy.
- Competitive predictive performance vs. IRM with two orders of magnitude speedup.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies; qualitative results on real data.

Cons:
- Greedy heuristic with no guarantees; outcome depends on tie-breaking.
- Hyperparameters fixed; no sensitivity analysis or learning.
- Sparse variant is heuristic; may be brittle with missing/noisy data.
- Experimental comparison to LFRM/ILA is indirect; no CIs; limited baselines (e.g., no comparison to hierarchical random graphs or hierarchical SBMs).
- Minor clarity issues (undefined “BHCD global,” predictive complexity, omitted algorithm pseudocode).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Discuss and compare to hierarchical random graph models (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman, Nature 2008) and, if feasible, include empirical comparisons.
- Add confidence intervals and statistical tests for predictive metrics; report hardware and implementation details for fair timing comparisons.
- Provide sensitivity analysis for α,β,δ,λ,γ; consider simple hyperparameter learning or empirical Bayes.
- Clarify “BHCD global,” correct the predictive complexity statement, and include pseudocode for Algorithm 1.
- Release code to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall recommendation:
The paper presents a technically sound and practically useful approach with clear novelty in adapting rose-tree Bayesian clustering to relational data and delivering strong speed-accuracy trade-offs. With improved related-work positioning and stronger empirical comparisons, it would be a solid contribution. Pending those, I view it as a borderline-to-weak accept based on clarity, soundness, and practical significance.
