- Presents BHCD, a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for community detection that models a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels with O(n^2 log n) inference complexity.
- Demonstrates substantial speedups over the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance on datasets like Sampson’s monastery and a NIPS co-authorship subset.
- Primary strength: significant computational efficiency and a clever caching scheme enabling scalability independent of community count.
- Strong theoretical grounding: model builds on stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian inference with rigorous likelihood and marginal likelihood derivations.
- Empirical validation: clear speed advantage and competitive accuracy on real-world networks, notably the NIPS co-authorship data.
- Algorithm presentation is clear (Algorithm 1), with practical variants (sparse BHCD) and tie-breaking strategies discussed.
- Authors acknowledge and mitigate issues such as merging disconnected components via BHCD sparse.
- Weakness: limited comparison to fast non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, hindering assessment of trade-offs.
- Weakness: insufficient exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and unclear justification for chosen hyperparameter values.
- Weakness: predictive performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art (e.g., outperformed by ILA on NIPS).
- Weakness: hierarchy visualization (Figure 5) is hard to interpret and could be improved.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of model assumptions (stochastic blockmodel) and performance degradation when assumptions are violated.
- Overall assessment: strong, technically sound, and well-written paper with major contribution in efficiency and scalability.
- Quality: strong; Clarity: very good with some dense sections; Originality: good for combining Bayesian hierarchical modeling with greedy inference.
- Significance: good — makes Bayesian hierarchical community detection more practical for large networks.
- Arguments for acceptance: major efficiency gains, sound theory, clear algorithm, and competitive empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks broader method comparisons, detailed hyperparameter analysis, and top predictive accuracy.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept — contributions outweigh minor weaknesses and merit inclusion in the proceedings.
