This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to build a hierarchical tree of communities, leveraging a stochastic blockmodel and a Bayesian nonparametric approach to efficiently infer the tree structure. The authors highlight the speed advantage of their method over existing Bayesian approaches like the IRM, particularly for larger networks, with a runtime that scales favorably with network size.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the core concepts. It builds upon existing work in stochastic blockmodels (SBM) [1, 2] and Bayesian approaches to community detection [5, 6, 7, 8], effectively addressing the computational limitations of previous methods like the IRM. The introduction does a good job of setting the stage and motivating the problem, while the subsequent sections provide a detailed explanation of the model and inference procedure. The inclusion of variations like BHCD sparse and the consideration of tie-breaking strategies demonstrate a thoughtful exploration of the method's parameters. The experimental results on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS network are convincing, showing competitive performance and significant speedups compared to the IRM. The detailed comparison with other related models in Table 1 provides a valuable benchmark.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** The main strength of this work is its computational efficiency. The authors convincingly demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster than existing Bayesian approaches, particularly the IRM, while maintaining reasonable performance. The O(n<sup>2</sup> log(n)) runtime is a significant improvement for large networks.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The method is grounded in a solid Bayesian framework, combining the stochastic blockmodel with a hierarchical prior. The derivation of the marginal likelihood and the recursive formulation for efficient computation are well-explained.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with the field. The figures and tables are helpful in illustrating the concepts and results.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets, including a real-world network (Sampson’s monastery) and a large co-authorship network (NIPS), provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The discussion of variations (sparse BHCD) and tie-breaking strategies demonstrates an awareness of practical implementation issues.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions setting hyperparameters α, β, δ, and λ to specific values (1.0, 0.2 for the first two, 0.4 for the last). However, it lacks a discussion of sensitivity to these hyperparameters and justification for the chosen values. A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the robustness of the results.
* **Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** While the comparison to IRM is valuable, it would be beneficial to compare BHCD against faster, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., those based on modularity optimization [3, 4]). This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency.
* **Predictive Performance Discussion:** Although predictive performance is evaluated, the discussion lacks depth. Specifically, the authors mention that BHCD performs "similarly to LFRM, worse than ILA, and better IRM". Quantifying "similarly" and providing insights into *why* BHCD performs differently from other methods (e.g., due to its model assumptions) would be helpful.
* **Limited Scalability Exploration:** The paper focuses primarily on networks with up to 2864 vertices. While the stated complexity is promising, it would be valuable to explore the scalability of BHCD on even larger networks to confirm its practical limitations.
* **Figure 3 Clarity:** The explanation of the qualitative results on Sampson’s Monastery could be more detailed. It’s a bit difficult to immediately understand *how* the identified communities relate to the known groupings of monks.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection.
* The computational advantages over existing Bayesian approaches are significant and potentially impactful.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on multiple datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
* The work addresses a challenging problem in network analysis with a well-motivated approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is lacking.
* The comparison to non-Bayesian methods is missing.
* The discussion of predictive performance could be more in-depth.
* While promising, the scalability to extremely large networks hasn't been thoroughly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The proposed method offers a compelling trade-off between accuracy and efficiency, addressing a key limitation of existing Bayesian approaches. While there are some areas for improvement (primarily related to hyperparameter tuning, comparisons to non-Bayesian methods, and more in-depth discussion of predictive performance), the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, I suggest the authors include a section discussing hyperparameter sensitivity, add a comparison to a relevant non-Bayesian method, and expand the discussion of the predictive performance results.
