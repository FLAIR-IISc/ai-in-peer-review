## Review of Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The approach builds upon existing Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering techniques and aims to offer a computationally efficient alternative to methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), particularly for larger networks. The core idea is to represent the hierarchical structure as a tree, allowing for efficient marginal likelihood calculation and greedy model selection.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear articulation of the problem, building on established concepts like stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian inference. The authors correctly identify the computational bottlenecks of existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches for hierarchical community detection. The proposed method, in principle, addresses these limitations by leveraging a greedy approach and caching sufficient statistics. The empirical evaluation, including a comparison to IRM on the NIPS co-authorship data, provides some support for the claimed speed advantage.  The connection to prior work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering ([13, 14]) is also well-established.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, while the claimed speedup is significant, the justification relies heavily on asymptotic complexity analysis. A more thorough empirical comparison, especially concerning the *quality* of the discovered communities, is needed. Simply stating that BHCD is faster than IRM is insufficient; demonstrating comparable, or even slightly worse, performance *given the substantial speedup* is critical. The experiments on Sampson's monastery network are qualitative and lack a quantitative evaluation against ground truth or other established community detection algorithms.

Secondly, the novelty of the approach feels incremental. Extending Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data, while a valid contribution, doesn’t represent a breakthrough. The authors don't convincingly demonstrate why this specific approach to hierarchical Bayesian community discovery is superior to other potential extensions. The core idea of using a tree-based prior is not entirely new (see [8, 12]), and the authors don’t fully articulate the advantages of their specific tree structure and inference procedure over existing alternatives. 

Thirdly, the parameter settings (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4) are rather arbitrary. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate how the results change with different parameter choices. Justification for these specific values is missing.

Fourthly, the description of the different merge strategies (absorbing vs. joining trees) and the tie-breaking rule feels somewhat underdeveloped. A more detailed explanation of why these choices were made and their potential impact on the results would be beneficial. The improvement offered by “BHCD sparse” is acknowledged but doesn’t justify the initial complexity of the full BHCD model.

Finally, the discussion of predictive performance is limited. While the AUC and accuracy metrics are presented, a deeper analysis of the types of errors made by BHCD compared to IRM would be valuable.  The predictive analysis relies on the same dataset used for structure inference – a more rigorous evaluation would involve a truly held-out test set.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in network analysis.
*   Presents a computationally efficient approach for hierarchical community detection.
*   Demonstrates a significant speedup compared to IRM.
*   Builds upon established concepts in Bayesian inference and stochastic blockmodels.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; lacks a strong justification for why this approach is superior.
*   Limited empirical evaluation, particularly regarding community quality.
*   Arbitrary parameter settings without sensitivity analysis.
*   Underdeveloped discussion of merge strategies and tie-breaking rules.
*   Predictive evaluation could be more rigorous.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a technically sound and reasonably well-written approach, it doesn’t offer sufficient novelty or empirical evidence to justify acceptance at NIPS. The speedup is appreciated, but it’s not enough to compensate for the lack of a compelling advantage in terms of community quality or theoretical insight. The paper feels more like a system implementation than a significant scientific contribution. I encourage the authors to conduct a more thorough empirical evaluation, including a sensitivity analysis of parameters and a comparison to a wider range of baseline methods, before resubmitting. They should also more strongly articulate the unique contributions of their method and its advantages over existing approaches.
