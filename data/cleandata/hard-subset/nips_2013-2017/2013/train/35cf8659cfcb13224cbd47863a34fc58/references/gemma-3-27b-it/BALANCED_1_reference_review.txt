This paper introduces a Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method for uncovering community structure in social networks. The approach extends existing Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering techniques to relational data, aiming for a balance between computational efficiency and probabilistic rigor. The authors position their work within the landscape of stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership models, and Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). They demonstrate BHCD’s speed advantage over a single IRM iteration and provide empirical results on Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Relevance:** The paper addresses an important problem – hierarchical community detection – and proposes a novel approach that combines the benefits of Bayesian methods with the efficiency of greedy algorithms. Extending existing Bayesian greedy clustering to relational data is a valuable contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  A key strength is the claimed computational efficiency, particularly in comparison to methods like IRM. The reported O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity (and O(n+m) for likelihood computation) is attractive for large networks, and the empirical results seem to support these claims, demonstrating significant speedups.  The caching scheme to achieve this complexity is well-motivated.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear probabilistic model building on stochastic blockmodels and incorporates a hierarchical prior based on a tree structure. The mathematical formulation is generally well-presented, and the connection to existing models (IRM) is clearly outlined.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on both Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data provide evidence of the method's effectiveness. The comparison to IRM is particularly valuable, demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Variations and Extensions:** The authors explore variations like BHCD sparse and binary-tree restricted versions, offering insights into the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. The inclusion of a prediction task adds further practical relevance.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors make a good effort to explain the technical details and motivations behind their choices.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameter choices (α, β, δ, λ, γ) is not thoroughly explored. The authors state they “found these to work well,” but a sensitivity analysis or justification for these specific values would strengthen the paper.  How does performance change with different priors?
* **Limited Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** While IRM is a natural comparison point, the paper would benefit from comparisons to established non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection algorithms (e.g., those based on modularity optimization or spectral clustering). This would provide a broader context for evaluating the method’s performance.
* **Scalability to Very Large Networks:** Although the claimed complexity is favorable, the experiments are performed on relatively modest network sizes (up to 234 nodes). The scalability to truly massive networks (millions of nodes) remains an open question.
* **Predictive Performance:** While BHCD shows promising predictive performance, the AUC values presented in Table 1 are not dramatically higher than IRM, considering the significant speedup. More detailed analysis of the predictive capabilities and comparisons to state-of-the-art link prediction methods would be beneficial.
* **Figure Clarity:** The discussion of Figure 3 references clarity of the discovered structure, however it is difficult to immediately discern the structure. A more visually compelling presentation would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to hierarchical community detection with a clear focus on computational efficiency.
* The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness and its speed advantage over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.
* The theoretical framework is sound, and the mathematical formulations are generally clear.
* The paper addresses a significant problem in network analysis with potential applications in various domains.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is lacking, and the choice of hyperparameters seems somewhat arbitrary.
* The comparison to non-Bayesian methods is limited, potentially understating the relative advantages of the proposed approach.
* Scalability to very large networks remains an open question, and the empirical evaluation is limited in network size.
* The predictive performance gains over IRM, while promising, are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the method on its own.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The method is novel, theoretically sound, and demonstrates promising empirical results. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity, a broader comparison to existing methods, and a more compelling demonstration of scalability.

**Recommendation:**

I lean towards **acceptance**, but with a request for minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) and discuss the impact of different choices on the results.
2.  Include comparisons to at least one established non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection algorithm.
3.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations and potential strategies for addressing them.
4.  Clarify the visual representation of the community structure in figures like Figure 3.
