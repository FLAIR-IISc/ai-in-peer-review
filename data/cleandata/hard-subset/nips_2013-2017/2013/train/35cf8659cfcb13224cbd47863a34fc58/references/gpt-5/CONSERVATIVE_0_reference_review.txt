Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for relational data. The model defines a prior over partitions via a tree (as in Bayesian hierarchical clustering/rose trees), uses a conjugate stochastic blockmodel likelihood with different priors for within- vs between-community edges, and computes the marginal likelihood of a given tree by dynamic programming. Inference is an agglomerative merge procedure with three merge types (join and two absorptions), scored by a local Bayes factor. A “sparse” variant disallows merging trees with no inter-tree edges. The authors report O(n^2 log n) time for inference and O(n+m) for likelihood evaluation, and present experiments on Sampson’s monastery network, a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subgraph (compared to IRM), and the full NIPS graph.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.), adapted to blockmodel likelihoods. It is positioned against the IRM and hierarchical IRM extensions, and mentions the Mondrian process. However, the paper omits discussion and comparison to hierarchical stochastic block models and hierarchical random graphs (e.g., Clauset-Moore-Newman, PNAS 2008), which are directly relevant baselines for hierarchical community structure and link prediction. Connections and differences to [8] (hierarchical IRM) are only briefly mentioned; a clearer comparative analysis would help.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and computational efficiency: a clean adaptation of BHC/BRT-style dynamic programming to a blockmodel likelihood.
- Careful caching for local Bayes factor updates and three-way merges is a practical contribution; the algorithmic design is well motivated.
- The dynamic programming recursion is clear, and the link prediction recursion is straightforward.
- Empirical results suggest the method is fast and yields sensible hierarchies and reasonable predictive performance with minimal tuning.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Hyperparameter inconsistency: The paper states priors should satisfy α>β (dense within) and δ<λ (sparse between), but experiments use α=δ=1.0 and β=λ=0.2, which violates δ<λ and implies a very dense prior even for between-block edges. This is a serious issue: either a typo or a problematic prior that conflicts with the stated modeling assumptions. A sensitivity analysis and/or hyperparameter inference is essential.
- Limited validation and baselines: Predictive comparisons are only against IRM (and only run your own IRM); comparisons to hierarchical SBMs/HRGs and strong non-Bayesian hierarchical community methods (e.g., Louvain with hierarchical refinements, Infomap hierarchical) are missing. The claim that BHCD performs “similarly to LFRM, worse than ILA” is based on comparisons to reported numbers in other papers rather than re-running those models on the same splits, which weakens the evidence.
- Ambiguous speed claims: “Two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” is unclear (a single Gibbs scan? a single sweep?). Runtime plots lack precise hardware, implementation, and stopping criteria details. Without a controlled environment and equal-optimization implementations, speedup claims are hard to assess.
- Theoretical novelty is modest: the main novelty is the application of the BHC/BRT framework to a blockmodel; the prior over partitions, the πS choice, and the dynamic program are largely inherited. A more rigorous analysis (e.g., approximation properties or consistency under a planted hierarchy) is absent.
- Locality assumption in scoring: The Bayes factor in Eq. (12) uses only local data. While this enables efficiency, it presumes independence structures that may be violated in practice; there is no analysis of when local merges are justified or of the potential for pathologically suboptimal merges. Restarts partially mitigate but are ad hoc.
- “Sparse” variant changes the objective: Prohibiting merges with no inter-tree edges is a heuristic constraint not derived from the model; it biases the search and should be analyzed (e.g., show it corresponds to a limit of hyperparameters or a constrained prior).
- Clarity and completeness: Some notation could be tightened (avoid double counting for undirected graphs in Eq. (1); clarify whether products are over p≤q). The paper references “BHCD global” in the Sampson section without prior definition. The link prediction complexity alternates between “linear in depth” and “Θ(n)”. Many references lack years and complete bibliographic data.

Quality
Technically competent in adapting BHC/BRT to a blockmodel with conjugate priors, and the dynamic program appears correct. However, key empirical claims are under-supported, and the hyperparameter inconsistency is a red flag. No ablations (e.g., join-only vs absorptions, effect of γ) or sensitivity studies are provided.

Clarity
Generally well written, but with several notational ambiguities, missing details for the algorithm variant(s), and incomplete references. Figures are referenced to make points that should be summarized quantitatively in the text.

Originality
Moderate. The algorithm is an incremental but sensible adaptation of known Bayesian hierarchical clustering machinery to relational data. The merge operators mirror rose trees; the likelihood is a standard conjugate SBM.

Significance
Potentially useful as a fast heuristic for hierarchical community discovery and link prediction, but the current validation is not strong enough to establish clear advantages over established hierarchical SBM/HRG methods or modern scalable community detection techniques.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the intended priors: are α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2 correct? If so, how do you justify δ<λ earlier? Provide sensitivity analyses and/or learn these hyperparameters.
- Provide precise runtime numbers, hardware, and implementation details, and compare to strong hierarchical baselines (e.g., HRG, hierarchical SBM) and non-Bayesian hierarchical methods.
- What is “BHCD global”? How does it differ from BHCD/BHCD sparse?
- Can you formalize the effect of the “sparse” constraint within the Bayesian objective?
- Clarify the product ranges in Eq. (1)-(3) for undirected graphs to avoid double counting.

Pros
- Simple, fast method with a clean dynamic program.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies and competitive link prediction on small benchmarks.
- Implementation details around caching and merge scoring are practical.

Cons
- Hyperparameter inconsistency and no sensitivity/hyperparameter inference.
- Missing key baselines and limited quantitative evidence.
- Modest novelty; heuristic search modifications not grounded in the model.
- Some clarity and referencing issues.

Recommendation
Lean reject. With corrected/justified hyperparameters, stronger and fairer baselines (including hierarchical SBMs/HRGs), quantitative ablations/sensitivity analysis, and clearer runtime evidence, the paper could be much stronger.
