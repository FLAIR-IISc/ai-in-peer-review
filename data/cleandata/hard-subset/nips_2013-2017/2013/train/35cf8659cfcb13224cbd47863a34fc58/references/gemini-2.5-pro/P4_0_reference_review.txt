### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a probabilistic method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The work builds upon the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and applies it to relational data. The core idea is to model the network using a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The model defines a probability distribution over the observed adjacency matrix conditioned on a tree structure representing the community hierarchy. This marginal likelihood, P(D|T), is computed efficiently and exactly using a dynamic programming recursion.

Inference of the hierarchical structure T is framed as a model selection problem. The paper proposes a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm to find a good tree. Starting with each network vertex as a separate leaf node, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of trees in the current forest. At each step, it chooses the merge that maximizes a local Bayes factor score. The algorithm considers three types of merges between two trees: joining them under a new parent or having one tree absorb the other. This greedy procedure has a time complexity of O(n² log n), making it significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, such as MCMC inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which often scale with the number of communities and require many iterations. The authors demonstrate the method's performance on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset, showing it finds sensible hierarchies and achieves competitive predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than IRM.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is a well-defined extension of the stochastic blockmodel to a hierarchical setting, leveraging the tree-based prior on partitions from [14]. The use of conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors allows for the exact and efficient computation of the marginal likelihood of a partition, which is key to the algorithm's speed. The recursive formula for the tree likelihood (Eq. 10) appears correct and enables the greedy search.

The claims of computational efficiency are well-supported by both the complexity analysis and the empirical results in Figure 4, which show a dramatic speed-up over IRM. The authors are also candid about the model's limitations, noting that its predictive performance is comparable to some models (LFRM) but worse than others (ILA), positioning BHCD as a method that prioritizes speed and scalability while maintaining a principled Bayesian formulation. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a model, an efficient inference algorithm, and experimental validation.

A primary weakness is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. Agglomerative clustering is a deterministic local search that is highly sensitive to initial conditions and can easily get stuck in local optima. While the authors mitigate this by performing multiple random-restart runs, it remains a heuristic approach to finding a single point estimate for the tree structure. This contrasts with MCMC-based methods which, given enough time, can characterize the full posterior distribution over structures. Furthermore, the reliance on a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12) for merge decisions is an approximation that assumes local connectivity is sufficient to determine local community structure, which may not always hold true.

**Clarity:**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature. The progression from the standard stochastic blockmodel (Section 2) to the hierarchical model (Section 3) is logical and easy to follow. The description of the generative process and the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood are particularly clear. Figures 1 and 2 are effective aids for understanding the concepts of tree-consistent partitions and merge operations. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the inference procedure. The experimental section is also clear, with well-designed comparisons and interpretable results.

A minor point for improvement would be to provide slightly more detail on the caching scheme that enables O(1) computation per inner loop, which is central to achieving the O(n² log n) overall complexity. While this follows from [14], a brief explanation in the context of relational sufficient statistics would make the paper more self-contained.

**Originality:**

The originality of this work lies in the novel combination of several established techniques to solve an important problem. The core algorithm is adapted directly from previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14], and the likelihood is based on the well-known stochastic blockmodel. However, the application and adaptation of this specific greedy Bayesian model selection framework to relational data for community discovery is new. This approach provides a distinct alternative to the dominant MCMC and variational inference paradigms used in other Bayesian nonparametric models for network analysis (e.g., [7], [8], [10]). The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior art, providing adequate citations and context. The resulting method fills a valuable niche for fast, probabilistic, hierarchical network analysis.

**Significance:**

The primary significance of this paper is practical. It offers a scalable, probabilistic algorithm for a fundamental task in network science. The demonstrated ability to produce meaningful results on a network of 234 nodes in seconds, compared to thousands of seconds for an MCMC-based IRM, is a substantial contribution. This efficiency makes principled Bayesian hierarchical analysis feasible for exploratory data analysis on much larger networks than previously possible with similar models. Researchers and practitioners who need to quickly infer hierarchical structure from network data are likely to find this method useful. While it may not achieve the absolute best predictive accuracy compared to more computationally intensive models, it represents a highly valuable point in the speed-accuracy trade-off space. The problem it addresses is of broad interest, and the proposed solution is a demonstrable advance in terms of computational efficiency for this class of models.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel combination of existing ideas to create a fast and scalable Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection, a problem of significant interest.
2.  **Performance:** The method is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than a key Bayesian nonparametric competitor (IRM) while achieving competitive predictive accuracy. This represents a significant practical contribution.
3.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, the model is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental evidence.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Greedy Inference:** The core inference algorithm is a greedy search, which provides only a point estimate of the community hierarchy and is susceptible to local optima. This is a significant limitation compared to methods that can approximate a full posterior.
2.  **Incremental Originality:** The contribution can be viewed as a straightforward application of the algorithm from [14] to a new data type (relational data) using a standard likelihood model (stochastic blockmodel).
