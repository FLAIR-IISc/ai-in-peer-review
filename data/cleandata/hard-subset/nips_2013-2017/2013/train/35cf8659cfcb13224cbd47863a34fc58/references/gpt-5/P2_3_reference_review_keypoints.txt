- Proposal: BHCD — a greedy Bayesian procedure that infers hierarchical community structure using a blockmodel likelihood with Beta-Bernoulli priors and dynamic programming to sum over tree-consistent partitions.
- Inference: Greedy agglomerative tree search with three merge operators (join, absorb left, absorb right) scored by local Bayes factors and O(1) updates via cached sufficient statistics.
- Complexity: Algorithm achieves overall O(n^2 log n) time and includes a “sparse” variant that forbids merging disconnected components.
- Prediction: Produces a predictive distribution for missing edges via a top-down recursion on the learned tree.
- Empirics: Qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery, predictive comparisons to IRM on NIPS-234, and a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS graph.
- Novelty: Adapts BHC/Rose Tree machinery to relational data with blockmodel likelihoods and engineering (merge ops + caching), but core prior and DP marginalization are reused.
- Strength — probabilistic formulation: Exact marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions with a simple recursive computation.
- Strength — algorithmic engineering: Efficient local scoring and caching enable fast updates and claimed runtime independent of community count.
- Strength — empirical speed: Much faster than IRM on NIPS-234 while achieving reasonable predictive performance.
- Strength — simplicity: Conjugate likelihood yields closed-form scoring and fast predictions.
- Weakness — originality: Contribution is incremental, mainly plugging blockmodels into existing BHC/Rose Tree framework.
- Weakness — limited empirical evaluation: Comparisons focus on IRM only; missing baselines like HRG, nested SBMs, Mondrian-based models, and degree-corrected SBMs.
- Weakness — hyperparameters and robustness: Fixed hyperparameters with no sensitivity analysis, ablation studies, or reporting of variability across restarts.
- Weakness — clarity/consistency issues: Conflicting claims about prediction complexity (O(depth) vs Θ(n)), handling of missing data vs sparse merging, undefined “BHCD global” in figures, and typographical/notation errors.
- Weakness — model restrictions: Supports only undirected, no-self-edge graphs; no degree heterogeneity modeling; greedy search has no theoretical guarantees.
- Technical soundness: Recursion, merge-score derivation, and caching identities are correct; sparse rule is reasonable for fully observed graphs but may fail under missingness.
- Experimental gaps: Speed claims supported but evaluation scope is narrow, hardware/timing details missing, and baselines insufficient.
- Clarity suggestions: Clarify prediction complexity, define/remove “BHCD global,” specify missing-data handling, provide pseudocode/implementation details, and fix typos.
- Originality assessment: Useful but modest extension of existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering to networks; related hierarchical network models are cited but not empirically compared.
- Significance: Practical tool due to speed and interpretability, useful as an initializer or for medium-to-large graphs, but methodological impact is limited by incremental novelty and sparse evaluation.
- Acceptance pros: Simple, efficient Bayesian hierarchical model with exact per-tree marginalization; strong speed-accuracy trade-off vs IRM and clear exposition.
- Acceptance cons: Limited novelty, narrow empirical evaluation, lack of degree-correction and hyperparameter learning, and some clarity issues.
- Suggested improvements: Add quantitative comparisons to hierarchical network baselines and degree-corrected SBMs, perform sensitivity/ablation studies, clarify missing-data handling and complexity, consider empirical Bayes/hyperparameter learning, and release code.
- Recommendation: Weak reject due to moderate novelty and insufficient empirical breadth despite solid engineering and speed benefits.
