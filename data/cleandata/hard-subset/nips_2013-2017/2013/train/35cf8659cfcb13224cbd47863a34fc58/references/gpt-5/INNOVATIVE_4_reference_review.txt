Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy Bayesian procedure for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The model marries stochastic blockmodels with Bayesian rose trees: a tree T induces a restricted family of partitions consistent with T, and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) sums exactly over these partitions via a dynamic program. Within each partition, on-diagonal blocks use one conjugate likelihood (f; Beta-Bernoulli with α>β), and off-diagonal blocks use another (g; Beta-Bernoulli with δ<λ), biasing toward dense within-community and sparse between-community edges. The prior over partitions given T follows the “stop or recurse” construction of rose trees, with πS depending on node arity. Inference is performed by bottom-up agglomerative model selection, evaluating three merge types (join and two absorptions) with a local Bayes factor that only depends on statistics among the merged leaves, enabling O(1) inner-loop updates via cached sufficient statistics. The overall runtime is O(n^2 log n) per restart, independent of the number of communities; a sparse variant prohibits merging disconnected components and is faster on sparse graphs. The paper also provides a predictive scheme for link prediction by recursively mixing on- and off-diagonal posteriors along a path in the tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery data (qualitative), NIPS-234 coauthorship (predictive accuracy vs IRM), and the full NIPS graph (qualitative tree) show that BHCD produces reasonable hierarchies and achieves competitive link prediction much faster than IRM.

Relation to prior work
The approach extends Bayesian rose trees (Blundell et al., 2010) from i.i.d. vector data to relational data via a hierarchical mixture of blockmodels and a DP recursion akin to Heller & Ghahramani (2005). Compared with IRM and its hierarchical variants, BHCD avoids MCMC/variational inference by restricting to tree-consistent partitions and using greedy agglomeration, yielding substantial speedups. It is conceptually related to other hierarchical network priors (e.g., Mondrian process for trees and hierarchical blockmodels), but is simpler and more scalable.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Elegant synthesis of rose-tree-style priors with blockmodel marginals to define an exact sum over tree-consistent partitions with an efficient DP. This strikes me as a bold, clean idea.
- Practical scalability: O(n^2 log n) greedy agglomeration with O(1) local merge updates via cached sufficient statistics; sparse variant is compelling for real networks.
- Usable predictive distribution: The top-down mixture for link prediction is simple, intuitive, and computationally light.
- Empirical evidence: On NIPS-234, BHCD reaches reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than IRM and is competitive with more complex latent feature models (per comparisons to reported numbers).
- Interpretability: Non-binary “rose” trees often yield more interpretable hierarchies than binary trees.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical breadth: Only two datasets with quantitative evaluation (Sampson qualitative; NIPS-234 quantitative; full NIPS qualitative). Stronger claims would benefit from more benchmarks (e.g., SNAP social/biological networks) and ablations.
- Hyperparameters fixed: No sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference (α,β,δ,λ,γ). Given the strong inductive bias (within vs between densities), robustness should be demonstrated.
- “Bayesian” scope: The method is greedy MAP over trees, not fully Bayesian over T; averaging over R restarts is a heuristic. This should be stated more explicitly.
- Merge heuristic in BHCD-sparse: Prohibiting merges with no edges improves results, but breaks the generative story. Some discussion on when this is justified would help.
- Clarity/details: A few places would benefit from clarification (e.g., precise normalization of the partition prior with πS=1−(1−γ)|ch(S)|; exact meaning of “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of IRM”—iteration vs full Gibbs sweep?).

Quality
Technically sound overall. The DP recursion (eq. 10) and local Bayes factor scoring are consistent with the rose-tree prior and blockmodel marginals. The caching scheme and O(1) updates are well motivated. The main caveats are the heuristic choices (BHCD-sparse, tie-breaking with restarts) and fixed hyperparameters. Nonetheless, the central claims (speed, reasonable predictive performance, interpretable hierarchies) are supported.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The exposition of the model, prior over partitions, and DP recursion is concise and informative. Suggestions:
- Explicitly derive or reference the normalization of p(φ|T) under the πS choice, and discuss intuition for the arity-dependent stop probability.
- Clarify computational claims (O(n+m) to evaluate p(D|T); O(n^2 log n R) for inference) and the “two orders of magnitude” comparison (what exactly is counted as an IRM iteration).
- Summarize memory requirements and what is cached.
- Briefly discuss directed/weighted networks and how f/g would change.

Originality
Moderately high. While it builds on rose trees and SBMs, the instantiation to relational data with exact summation over tree-consistent partitions and a practical, local-score agglomerative search is a fresh and useful contribution. The speed/interpretability tradeoff versus IRM-class models is clear.

Significance
High practical significance for scalable hierarchical community discovery. The method is simple, fast, and yields structures that practitioners can interpret, with competitive predictive performance. I expect others to build on this and to use it as a baseline for hierarchical network models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add sensitivity analysis over α,β,δ,λ,γ; optionally learn γ.
- Include one or two additional real datasets and a synthetic study with known hierarchical ground truth (report dendrogram purity/NMI).
- Report direct comparisons to LFRM/ILA on the same splits (if possible) to substantiate the statements of relative performance.
- Provide ablations: join-only vs absorptions; BHCD vs BHCD-sparse; effect of R restarts.
- Release code for reproducibility.

Typos/minor
- Some references incomplete (years/pages). Please standardize.
- A few sentences repeat words (e.g., “The Mondrian process [12] propose…”).

Recommendation
Accept. This paper introduces a neat and novel direction—hierarchical community discovery via rose-tree-style Bayesian aggregation of blockmodels—that is simple, very fast, and practically useful. Despite some experimental and clarity gaps, the conceptual contribution and scalability are compelling. My enthusiasm is driven by the bold idea and the clear potential for impact. Confidence: medium-high.
