**Summary**

This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The method extends the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005) to relational data by employing a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) likelihood. The core idea is to build a hierarchy bottom-up, starting with each node in its own cluster. At each step, the algorithm greedily merges the two sub-trees that provide the largest increase in the marginal likelihood, calculated via a local Bayes factor. The paper's main contribution is this adaptation and an efficient implementation with a novel caching scheme that achieves a time complexity of O(n² log n), which is presented as a significant speed improvement over more traditional MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Grounding:** The proposed method is a sensible and well-motivated combination of two established lines of work: the probabilistic framework for greedy hierarchical clustering and the stochastic blockmodel for network data. This provides a solid foundation for the approach.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary advantage of this work is its computational efficiency. The O(n² log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, is a significant practical benefit over MCMC-based methods whose runtime depends on the number of communities and iterations required for convergence. The experimental results in Figure 4, showing that BHCD obtains reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than a standard IRM implementation, provide compelling evidence for this claim.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The recursive definition of the tree-based marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant, and the agglomerative algorithm is described in sufficient detail. The connection to prior work is also clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Greedy Model Selection vs. Bayesian Inference:** The paper's primary weakness is its reliance on a greedy search heuristic. While computationally attractive, this approach finds only a single point estimate of the hierarchy (or a few, with restarts) and is susceptible to local optima. This is a significant departure from the principles of full Bayesian inference, which aims to characterize the posterior distribution over structures. MCMC methods, while slow, have the theoretical guarantee of eventually exploring this posterior. The "Bayesian" aspect of this model is largely confined to the use of marginal likelihoods as a scoring function for a greedy search, rather than performing inference over the posterior. The authors should be more circumspect about the claims of being a "Bayesian" procedure, as it is more accurately described as greedy model selection using a Bayesian scoring criterion.

2.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The main experimental comparison is against the IRM. While the IRM is a foundational model, it is also over a decade old. The field of network community detection has advanced considerably, with many scalable algorithms, including other SBM variants (e.g., those using variational inference) and fast non-probabilistic hierarchical methods (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004). A more thorough evaluation would compare BHCD against a wider and more modern set of baselines to better contextualize its performance in terms of both speed and accuracy. The comparison to LFRM and ILA is mentioned, but the paper would be stronger if these methods were included directly in the time-vs-performance plots.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of the "Sparse" Variant:** The paper introduces `BHCD sparse`, which empirically performs better. However, the motivation for this variant—prohibiting merges between disconnected components—is presented as a heuristic rather than being derived from first principles. While plausible, this modification lacks a firm theoretical justification within the proposed probabilistic model. It is unclear if this is an ad-hoc fix or a principled constraint that could be formalized as part of the model's prior.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model relies on several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are fixed in the experiments. There is no discussion of the model's sensitivity to these choices or any procedure for setting them from data. A rigorous evaluation would include an analysis of how different hyperparameter settings affect the resulting hierarchies and predictive performance.

**Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound within the scope of what it presents: a greedy algorithm. The derivation of the marginal likelihood and the caching scheme for efficiency are solid. However, the claims are not fully supported by a rigorous experimental comparison against the state of the art, and the evaluation of the model's properties (e.g., hyperparameter sensitivity) is minimal.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model and algorithm are explained with sufficient detail for understanding.

*   **Originality:** The work is an incremental but valuable contribution. It does not propose a fundamentally new probabilistic model but successfully applies an existing clustering framework (Bayesian Rose Trees) to a new and important domain (relational data) with a focus on computational efficiency. The primary novelty lies in this specific application and its efficient implementation.

*   **Significance:** The significance is primarily practical. For practitioners seeking a fast, off-the-shelf method for hierarchical community detection that has a probabilistic interpretation, this work is useful. It provides a strong alternative to slower MCMC methods when speed is a primary concern. However, its significance to the Bayesian machine learning community is tempered by its reliance on a greedy heuristic over a full posterior inference approach.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a computationally efficient method for discovering hierarchical communities. Its main strength is its speed, which is convincingly demonstrated against the IRM. However, this speed comes at the cost of methodological rigor, as the greedy search provides no guarantees of finding a globally optimal structure and does not characterize posterior uncertainty. While the work is a useful contribution to the toolbox of scalable network analysis methods, its claims should be moderated to reflect its nature as a greedy model selection algorithm rather than a full Bayesian inference procedure. The experimental evaluation, while clean, would be significantly strengthened by comparison to a broader set of modern baselines.
