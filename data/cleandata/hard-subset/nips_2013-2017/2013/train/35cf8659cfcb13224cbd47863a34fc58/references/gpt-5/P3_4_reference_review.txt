Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy Bayesian method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a tree T over vertices; internal nodes represent communities, leaves are vertices. A partition prior p(φ|T) is induced by a top-down “stop or split” process with node-specific stopping probabilities πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| (as in Bayesian Rose Trees), and the likelihood is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels with conjugate Beta–Bernoulli marginal likelihoods. A key contribution is a dynamic-programming recursion (Eq. 10) that computes p(D|T) exactly in O(n + m) for a fixed T. Learning T is framed as greedy agglomerative model selection with three merge types (join, absorb, reverse absorb); a local Bayes factor score (Eq. 12) permits O(1) updates via cached sufficient statistics, giving an overall O(n^2 log n) algorithm. A sparse variant forbids merges across components with no edges. A recursive predictive distribution (Eq. 14) yields fast link prediction. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network, a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset, and the full NIPS graph suggest BHCD recovers interpretable hierarchies and attains competitive link prediction much faster than the IRM.

Relation to prior work
- The approach builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees [Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.], adapting them from i.i.d. data to relational block models.
- Compared to IRM [Kemp et al.] and its hierarchical extensions (e.g., Herlau et al.), BHCD limits the partition space to those consistent with a learned tree, enabling exact marginalization and a fast greedy search rather than MCMC/variational updates.
- The modeling choice resembles hierarchical random graphs (HRG; Clauset-Moore-Newman) and later nested SBMs (e.g., Peixoto), which also impose hierarchical structure over blocks/partitions; those provide important baselines for both modeling assumptions and scalability.
- Other nonparametric relational models (MMSB, LFRM, ILA) allow overlapping membership; BHCD instead enforces laminar structure and emphasizes scalability.

Strengths
- Clear, principled generative model: a tree-induced prior over partitions with distinct within/between-block conjugate marginals; exact p(D|T) via DP.
- Elegant and efficient agglomerative procedure with local Bayes factor scoring and careful caching. The O(n^2 log n) complexity is attractive and independent of the number of communities.
- Predictive recursion is simple and leverages the hierarchy; averaging over restarts gives a practical hedge over greedy local optima.
- Empirical evidence that BHCD is dramatically faster than IRM while achieving similar or better link-prediction performance on NIPS-234; qualitative structure on Sampson and the full NIPS graph is sensible.
- The sparse variant is a useful practical improvement for large sparse graphs.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing key baselines. There is no comparison to HRG or nested SBMs, which are highly relevant hierarchical community models with scalable inference and strong empirical performance. Including at least HRG (likelihood-based dendrogram) and a nested SBM would make the empirical story far stronger.
- Limited experimental depth. The NIPS-234 comparison primarily contrasts to IRM, with LFRM/ILA results taken from prior work rather than re-run; the fairness of time comparisons (C implementations, hardware, stopping criteria) is unclear. Claims such as “two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration” need precise definition (one Gibbs sweep? one full pass over nodes?) and controlled conditions. The full NIPS result is qualitative; no quantitative evaluation beyond visually interpretable clusters is presented.
- Heuristic modifications break Bayesian coherence. The “sparse” rule that forbids merges with no connecting edges is sensible but not derived from a prior; it changes the model class. If kept, it should be framed as a prior/penalty (e.g., zero prior mass for cross-component merges) or justified theoretically.
- Hyperparameter and sensitivity analysis are missing. The model fixes α,β,δ,λ and γ; there is no learning of these parameters or sensitivity study. Performance and tree topology may depend strongly on γ and the within/between priors. At least a brief sensitivity sweep and/or empirical Bayes would strengthen the claims.
- Ambiguity about complexity and implementation details. While the per-tree likelihood is O(n + m), inference is O(n^2 log n); a discussion of memory costs and the constants in caching would help. The predictive complexity is described as Θ(n) but is actually linear in tree depth. The tie-breaking and restart scheme can lead to different trees; reporting variability across runs would be informative.
- Treatment of signed/negative edges in Sampson’s data folds negative ties into “absent edges,” which is arguably inappropriate; a signed likelihood would be more faithful.
- Scope limitations: the model enforces a single laminar hierarchy and cannot express overlapping communities; this should be highlighted as a modeling trade-off relative to MMSB/LFRM/ILA.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the recursion (Eq. 10) and merge score (Eq. 12) are plausible and appear correct given the factorization, though a short derivation in the appendix would aid reproducibility. The algorithmic engineering for O(1) updates is a nice contribution. The evaluation is suggestive but not yet comprehensive.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Some notational passages (Eq. 5 set recursion) could be expanded. It would help to:
- Provide a derivation of Eq. 10 from Eqs. 7–9.
- Clarify exactly how the Bayes factor in Eq. 12 corresponds to the ratio p(D|F?)/p(D|F), including the removal of g(σIJ) from between-forest terms.
- State predictive evaluation protocol (heldout fraction, masking strategy) and hardware/runtime details.

Originality
The combination of rose-tree priors with blockmodel likelihoods and a tailored agglomerative search for relational data is a neat, practical idea. That said, hierarchical network models are well-studied; novelty rests on the specific Bayesian scoring and efficient implementation.

Significance
As a fast, interpretable baseline for hierarchical community detection with decent predictive performance, BHCD is useful. Broader impact would be stronger with comparisons to HRG/nested SBMs and scalability to larger sparse graphs.

Pros
- Simple, elegant model with exact marginalization for fixed T.
- Very fast inference; competitive link prediction.
- Clear algorithm and reproducible ingredients.

Cons
- Missing key hierarchical baselines; limited experimental depth and fairness.
- Heuristic “sparse” variant; no hyperparameter learning or sensitivity.
- Modeling restriction to laminar hierarchies; treatment of signed data.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to HRG and nested SBMs; re-run LFRM/ILA where possible.
- Provide derivations, ablations (binary vs non-binary, with/without absorb, sparse vs full), and sensitivity to γ and Beta priors.
- Formalize the sparse constraint as a prior; or at least report its empirical effect systematically.
- Consider hyperparameter learning (empirical Bayes) and handling directed/weighted/signed networks.
- Report variability across restarts and tree depth statistics; quantify interpretability (e.g., purity vs known labels).

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has solid ideas and promising speed/accuracy, but the empirical comparison set and analysis are not yet at the standard needed for NeurIPS. Strengthening the experiments and situating BHCD against the most relevant hierarchical baselines would likely tip this to acceptance. Reviewer confidence: High.
