Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian agglomerative procedure to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model builds on stochastic blockmodels with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli likelihoods, but introduces a tree T that constrains partitions to be tree-consistent. A prior over partitions is induced via a rose-tree-like stopping probability πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|. The marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed exactly by a dynamic program (Eq. 10), and a bottom-up agglomerative search merges trees using a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12), considering three merge types (join/absorb). Caching sufficient statistics yields O(n^2 log n) time per run (O(R n^2 log n) with restarts). A “sparse” variant forbids merges between disconnected components. For prediction, edge probabilities are computed by a recursive mixture along the path in the tree (Eq. 14). Experiments on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship data show qualitative hierarchies; on a 234-author subset BHCD is faster and reportedly competitive with IRM; on the full NIPS graph BHCD yields a large hierarchy.

Relation to prior work
The method combines ideas from stochastic blockmodels, the IRM, and Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT), and is related to hierarchical IRM variants, Mondrian process priors, and other nonparametric relational models (LFRM, ILA). The main contribution is adapting BRT-style dynamic programming and greedy model selection to relational data with an on/off diagonal block likelihood.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: constrain partitions by a tree and exploit exact marginalization via a simple recursion.
- Practicality: O(n^2 log n) agglomerative inference with useful caching, and a sparse variant that exploits sparsity.
- Local Bayes factor scoring enables efficient updates; predictions are straightforward.
- Qualitative results on canonical benchmarks (Sampson) are sensible, and scalability to full NIPS graph is demonstrated.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Major technical issues
- Hyperparameter inconsistency: The text states α > β and δ < λ to encode higher within-community densities than between. However, experiments fix α = δ = 1.0 and β = λ = 0.2, which sets the within and between Beta priors to the same mean (0.833), and violates δ < λ (1.0 < 0.2 is false). This undermines the modeling motivation and invalidates parts of the empirical claims. Please clarify and correct; provide sensitivity analyses.
- Undirected counting/double counting: Eq. (2) multiplies over p,q ∈ φ with p ≠ q, while later assuming undirected networks with σpq = σqp. This appears to double-count off-diagonals unless the product is over unordered pairs {p, q}. Similarly, clarify whether n1pp and n0pp use combinations choose(|p|,2) and exclude self-edges. The current notation mixes ordered and unordered indexing.
- Predictive complexity: Sec. 4.2 says prediction cost is linear in tree depth, but then concludes “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n)”. This is only true in the worst case of depth n; otherwise it should be O(depth(T)). Please correct.
- Claims vs evidence: “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” is unclear/misleading. A single Gibbs sweep for IRM can be O(nK^2) or O(mK^2); a whole BHCD run is O(n^2 log n). Please define “iteration,” include wall-clock comparisons under matched implementations, and avoid hyperbole.

Methodological limitations and missing details
- The Bayes factor in Eq. (12) uses only local data. While standard in greedy BHC/BRT, the paper should discuss the implications (e.g., lack of global optimality, possible myopia) and show robustness via restarts or annealing.
- Heaps and “out-of-date scores”: The Sampson section references “BHCD global” being “led astray by out of date scores on its heap,” but this variant is never defined. Using a buggy or intentionally stale-heap variant in the figure is confusing.
- Tie-breaking/random restarts: R = 50 restarts are used, but no variance across runs or impact of R is reported. The stochasticity could materially alter trees and predictions.
- Comparisons: For NIPS-234, BHCD is compared to IRM (MCMC and greedy) in the authors’ runs, but comparisons to LFRM/ILA are only via “quantitatively comparable” reuse of folds and numbers from prior work. This is not a controlled comparison. Please run LFRM/ILA or at least re-implement comparable baselines under the same evaluation and hardware.
- Hyperparameter inference: γ, α,β,δ,λ are fixed; no model selection, cross-validation, or posterior hyperparameter treatment. Given the role of γ in favoring non-binary trees and of α,β,δ,λ in controlling density contrast, sensitivity analyses are important.
- Scalability claims: The paper emphasizes O(n+m) for likelihood via dynamic programming, but the search remains O(n^2 log n) (and O(R n^2 log n) with restarts). It would be useful to report memory usage and actual wall-clock scaling on sparse vs dense graphs.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation and definitions:
  - Trees as nested sets: T = {{a,b},{c,d,e}, f} mixes sets and atoms; please consistently represent leaves as singleton sets and define n_T = |ch(T)|.
  - Define σ¬chFF formally for a forest. In Eq. (13), several overloaded symbols (M, I, J, X, Y) make it hard to follow.
  - Clarify whether θpq = θqp is enforced and how.
- Missing proofs/derivations: A brief derivation of Eq. (10) from Eqs. (7–8) (or an appendix) would help reproducibility.
- Figures and quantitative reporting: Provide tables with exact AUC/accuracy/log-likelihood with error bars; currently plots and narrative are qualitative.

Formatting and references (numerous)
- Multiple typos and inconsistencies: “physics Review E” → Physical Review E; “Erix P. Xing” → Eric P. Xing; double period “known depth..”; “lead astray” → “led astray”; “n 0 pq” spacing; “σ{x}{x} = 0” awkward.
- Several references lack years/venues/pages; some entries are incomplete or URLs are broken/malformed.
- Use consistent notation for beta functions and sufficient statistics, and ensure all symbols are defined at first use.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, fast, and practically useful hierarchical model with exact marginalization under conjugacy.
- Solid extension of BRT/BHC ideas to relational data with a workable greedy search.
- Qualitative results are sensible; code purportedly efficient.

Cons:
- Critical hyperparameter inconsistency invalidates the stated modeling bias and likely the experiments.
- Weak, partially uncontrolled empirical comparison; no sensitivity analyses.
- Numerous notation/clarity issues and formatting problems.
- Some claims are overstated or imprecise.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising and likely useful, but the current manuscript has significant technical inconsistencies (notably the priors), limited and partially uncontrolled empirical evaluation, and numerous clarity/formatting issues. With corrected hyperparameters, stronger baselines under controlled conditions, sensitivity analyses, and improved presentation, this could be a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
