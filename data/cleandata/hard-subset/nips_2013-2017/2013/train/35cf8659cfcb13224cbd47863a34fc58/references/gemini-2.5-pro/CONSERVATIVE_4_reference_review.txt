**Summary of the paper:**
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach frames hierarchical clustering as a greedy Bayesian model selection problem. It builds upon the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and extends it to relational data by using a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) as the likelihood for network partitions. The model defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a tree structure and calculates the marginal likelihood of the data given a tree via dynamic programming. Inference is performed using a bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges sub-trees based on the largest local Bayes factor, resulting in a computationally efficient procedure with a claimed complexity of O(n^2 log n). The authors demonstrate empirically that their method is significantly faster than MCMC-based inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable link prediction performance.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work is its computational efficiency. The proposed greedy inference scheme is orders of magnitude faster than traditional MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods like the IRM. The fact that its complexity does not depend on the number of communities is a significant practical advantage, making it potentially applicable to larger networks where MCMC methods would be intractable.
2.  **Probabilistic Foundation:** Unlike many fast, algorithmic hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., Girvan-Newman), BHCD is grounded in a coherent Bayesian framework. The greedy merges are guided by the principled criterion of maximizing the marginal likelihood (via Bayes factors), which provides a sounder basis for model selection than heuristic modularity scores.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the model, the inference algorithm, and the connection to previous work. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) and the agglomerative algorithm are explained in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Originality:** The main contribution appears to be the application of an existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework ([13], [14]) to network data using a standard SBM likelihood. While a useful combination, this feels more like an incremental extension than a novel conceptual contribution. The technical novelty seems limited to the efficient caching of sufficient statistics for relational data, which is a valuable engineering step but not a fundamental advance in modeling or inference.
2.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The choice of a greedy agglomerative search is a major methodological weakness. Such algorithms are well-known to be highly sensitive to initial conditions and prone to finding suboptimal local minima. The final hierarchy is entirely determined by a series of local decisions, with no mechanism to revise or undo poor merges made early in the process. While the authors use multiple random restarts to mitigate this, it is a heuristic patch on a fundamentally limited inference strategy. In contrast, MCMC methods, though slow, offer a more principled approach to exploring the posterior distribution over structures. The paper does not adequately discuss the implications of this greedy approximation or characterize how its solutions might differ in quality from those found by more exhaustive search methods.
3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental comparison is not comprehensive enough to substantiate the claims of advancing the state of the art.
    *   The primary baseline is the IRM, which is a model for *flat* partitions. A more rigorous evaluation would require comparison against other established *hierarchical* community detection methods, including the Bayesian approaches mentioned in the introduction (e.g., [8], [12]) and prominent non-Bayesian hierarchical algorithms.
    *   The link prediction results show that BHCD performs "similarly to LFRM, worse than ILA, and better than IRM." This suggests that the proposed method offers a speed-accuracy trade-off, but does not outperform existing state-of-the-art models in terms of predictive quality. Given the paper's focus on a new method, stronger evidence of its superiority in some dimension other than just speed is expected.
    *   The qualitative analysis on the "Full NIPS" dataset relies on a heuristic for cutting the tree to display communities, which detracts from the principled Bayesian framing of the model.

4.  **Model Assumptions:** The model relies on a number of strong assumptions that are not fully justified. The local computation of the Bayes factor score (Eq. 12) assumes that the decision to merge two communities depends only on the connectivity within and between them. This local view may be insufficient for capturing more global network properties. Furthermore, the specific choice of prior on partitions (Eq. 7) is inherited directly from prior work on non-relational data, and its suitability for modeling social network hierarchies is not specifically motivated or tested.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of scalable inference for hierarchical community detection.
*   The proposed method is demonstrably much faster than a standard Bayesian nonparametric baseline (IRM).
*   The work provides a useful bridge between the literature on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and network analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The methodological contribution is incremental, primarily combining two established lines of work.
*   The greedy inference algorithm is a significant theoretical limitation and may produce poor quality solutions.
*   The experimental comparison is narrow, lacking key baselines from the hierarchical clustering literature and failing to show state-of-the-art predictive performance.
*   The quality of the obtained hierarchies is not rigorously evaluated against ground truth or compared to hierarchies produced by other methods.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a fast and potentially useful algorithm for hierarchical community detection, its contributions are undermined by concerns about originality and the significant limitations of its greedy inference approach. The experimental validation is not thorough enough to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed speed-accuracy trade-off is superior to that of other existing methods. The work represents a solid piece of applied machine learning but may lack the novel conceptual insight or rigorous empirical validation expected for a publication at NIPS.
