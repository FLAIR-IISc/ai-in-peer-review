

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper proposes a "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" (BHCD) method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14] and stochastic blockmodels to develop a greedy algorithm that claims to be two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper's main contribution is a greedy agglomerative approach for hierarchical community detection that uses a tree-based representation of community structure. The authors claim their method has O(n² log n) time complexity, which they argue is better than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods that often have higher complexity or require many iterations to converge. The method uses a Bayesian approach with conjugate priors, and the authors introduce a caching scheme to achieve their claimed efficiency.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments. The technical details of the model and algorithm are presented with sufficient rigor. The authors make a reasonable effort to contextualize their work within the existing literature on community detection, particularly discussing the limitations of previous Bayesian approaches. The computational efficiency claim is potentially valuable, as scalability is a significant issue in network analysis.

## Weaknesses

While the paper has some technical merit, I have significant concerns about the evidence supporting its claims, particularly regarding the novelty and superiority of the proposed method.

First, the paper fails to provide sufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The authors compare BHCD to IRM, but omit comparisons to other well-established hierarchical community detection methods (e.g., [3, 8, 9, 10, 11]). The paper's claim that "BHCD produces good results two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" is unsubstantiated. The timing comparisons in Section 5 are limited to a single dataset (NIPS-234) and lack detailed timing data showing the claimed speedup. The paper provides a table of complexity classes but fails to demonstrate actual runtime differences on comparable hardware.

Second, the experimental evaluation is insufficiently thorough. The paper uses Sampson's monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, but fails to demonstrate the method's performance on standard community detection benchmarks (e.g., LFR benchmark networks, which are commonly used for community detection evaluation). The evaluation metrics (log likelihood, predictive performance) are standard, but the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the results, nor does it compare the method's ability to recover ground truth communities when available.

Third, the paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for why their greedy approach should outperform existing methods. While the caching scheme for efficiency is reasonable, the paper doesn't provide analysis of the conditions under which BHCD would be expected to produce better results than alternative approaches. The claim that "BHCD finds the most likely tree" is not substantiated with evidence showing that the greedy approach reliably finds the global optimum.

Fourth, the paper's novelty claim is questionable. The approach builds directly on [13,14], and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contributions from these prior works. The "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" name is somewhat misleading, as the method is a greedy extension of existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches rather than a fundamentally new Bayesian framework.

## Recommendations

I recommend rejection of this paper for the following reasons:

1. The paper's claims about speed and performance are not adequately supported by empirical evidence. The timing comparisons are limited and lack sufficient detail to verify the "two orders of magnitude faster" claim.

2. The experimental evaluation is incomplete and fails to compare against multiple established baselines, making it impossible to assess whether BHCD offers genuine advantages over existing methods.

3. The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis to explain why the proposed method should work better than alternatives.

4. The paper's contribution appears to be a minor extension of existing work rather than a significant advance in the field.

While the caching scheme for efficiency is an interesting technical detail, this paper falls short of NIPS's standards for rigor and thorough validation. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation by comparing against a wider range of baselines on standard benchmark datasets, providing detailed runtime comparisons, and including statistical significance tests for their results before this work could be considered for publication.

The paper's claims about scalability and performance advantages remain unsubstantiated, and the lack of comprehensive validation against established methods makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. Given the field's emphasis on rigorous evaluation of new methods, I believe this paper would benefit from significant additional experimentation and analysis before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
