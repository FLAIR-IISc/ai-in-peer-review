This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in relational data. The core idea is to extend the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering, previously applied to i.i.d. data, to the network setting. The model treats hierarchy inference as a Bayesian model selection problem, where each possible tree represents a model. The paper introduces an elegant recursive formula to compute the marginal likelihood of the data given a specific tree, which sums over all partitions consistent with that tree's structure. To find a good hierarchy, the authors propose a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges sub-trees to maximize the Bayes factor, a principled measure of model evidence. This approach stands in contrast to prevalent Bayesian nonparametric methods for network analysis, such as the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which typically rely on computationally intensive MCMC or variational inference to approximate a posterior over flat partitions.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and the boldness of its approach. The authors have effectively identified a major bottleneck in Bayesian network analysis—the computational cost of inference—and have proposed a compelling and conceptually new solution. Instead of making incremental improvements to existing MCMC samplers for models like the IRM, this work reframes the problem in a way that permits a fast, deterministic inference algorithm. The combination of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework with the stochastic blockmodel likelihood for relational data is both clever and highly effective.

This novel formulation leads to the paper's second major strength: efficiency. The greedy agglomerative search, enabled by a novel caching scheme for the sufficient statistics, runs in O(n^2 log n) time. This is a significant theoretical and practical improvement over MCMC-based methods, whose runtime often scales poorly and depends on the number of latent communities and iterations to convergence. The experiments convincingly demonstrate this advantage, showing that BHCD can produce meaningful results "two orders of magnitude faster" than a single Gibbs scan of an IRM. This kind of performance leap makes Bayesian hierarchical analysis practical for a much wider range of real-world networks and is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is also very clearly written. The probabilistic model is developed logically from the stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical formulation. The recursive calculation of the tree likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant, and the agglomerative search algorithm is described with sufficient detail. The experimental results, though not exhaustive, are well-chosen to highlight the method's qualitative interpretability (Sampson's monastery), its speed-accuracy trade-off (NIPS-234), and its ability to handle larger graphs (Full NIPS).

**Weaknesses:**

The main methodological weakness is inherent to the chosen approach: the inference is greedy. The algorithm is not guaranteed to find the globally optimal tree structure and is susceptible to getting stuck in local optima. The authors acknowledge this limitation and propose using multiple random restarts as a practical mitigation strategy. While this is a reasonable heuristic, the solution lacks the theoretical guarantees of asymptotic correctness that MCMC methods provide. However, I view this as a well-motivated and acceptable trade-off. For many applications, a very fast algorithm that finds a "good" solution is far more useful than a slow one that theoretically targets the "perfect" solution.

Another limitation is that the merge criterion is based on a local Bayes factor, considering only the data within the sub-trees being merged. This ignores the broader network context and could potentially lead to suboptimal merges. This is another explicit trade-off for computational efficiency, as it allows for the O(1) updates that make the algorithm fast.

Finally, the model relies on a set of fixed hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). A fully Bayesian treatment would involve placing priors over these parameters and inferring them from data. The performance of the model could be sensitive to their settings, and this aspect was not explored in the paper. This, however, feels like a minor point that can easily be addressed in future extensions of this promising work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a fundamentally new direction for inference in Bayesian network models, trading the completeness of MCMC for massive gains in speed via a principled greedy search. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Practical Impact:** The method's efficiency makes Bayesian hierarchical community discovery practical for datasets that were previously out of reach for similar models.
*   **Clarity and Elegance:** The model and algorithm are presented clearly, and the underlying probabilistic formulation is sound and elegant.
*   **Stimulates New Research:** This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on faster, approximate Bayesian inference for a variety of complex probabilistic models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Greedy Inference:** The algorithm is heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees of finding the optimal model.
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The comparison is primarily against IRM and could be strengthened by including other scalable community detection methods or variational approaches.
*   **Fixed Hyperparameters:** The model is not fully Bayesian as hyperparameters are fixed, potentially affecting performance and generalizability.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a bold and creative approach to a long-standing problem. While the greedy nature of the inference is a clear limitation from a purist's perspective, the immense practical benefits in terms of speed are undeniable. The authors have successfully engineered a method that makes rich, hierarchical Bayesian modeling accessible for large-scale network analysis. The novelty of the idea and its potential impact far outweigh the methodological trade-offs. This is precisely the type of innovative, direction-setting research that NIPS should be highlighting. I strongly recommend acceptance.
