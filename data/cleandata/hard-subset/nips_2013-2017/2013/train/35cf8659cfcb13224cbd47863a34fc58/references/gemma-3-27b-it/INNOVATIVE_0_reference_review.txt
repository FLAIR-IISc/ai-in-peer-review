## Review of Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy algorithm for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The core idea – extending Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data – is a compelling one, addressing a significant gap in scalable Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection. While existing methods like the IRM offer a fully probabilistic treatment, they often suffer from prohibitive computational cost, particularly for larger networks. BHCD aims to bridge this gap with a fast, greedy algorithm while retaining a Bayesian framework.

The paper clearly outlines the model, building upon stochastic blockmodels and leveraging a tree-based prior on partitions. The authors demonstrate a clever use of dynamic programming to efficiently calculate marginal likelihoods, leading to a runtime that is quadratic in the number of vertices, independent of the number of communities – a significant advantage over many existing methods. The comparison to the IRM in terms of speed (two orders of magnitude faster per iteration) is particularly striking. The inclusion of variations (sparse BHCD, binary tree restrictions) and the discussion of predictive performance further strengthens the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of combining Bayesian hierarchical clustering with relational data and a greedy inference scheme is genuinely novel. This isn’t simply an incremental improvement; it's a different direction in tackling a challenging problem.
* **Scalability:** The claimed quadratic runtime is a major strength, especially compared to the often intractable inference in more expressive Bayesian models.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The formulation in terms of stochastic blockmodels and the use of dynamic programming demonstrate a strong theoretical understanding.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data provide preliminary validation of the method's effectiveness, showing competitive performance with existing techniques.  The speed comparison to IRM is compelling.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited scope of experiments:** While the experiments are a good start, they lack extensive comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., spectral methods, agglomerative clustering with different linkage criteria).  A more comprehensive benchmark would better establish BHCD’s position within the broader field.
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameters were selected to “work well,” but doesn't provide any sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen values. This could be a significant concern, as performance might be heavily reliant on these settings.
* **Predictive performance – room for improvement:**  While BHCD performs comparably to LFRM and better than IRM, it’s outperformed by ILA. Investigating why and potential modifications to improve predictive accuracy would be valuable.
* **Tree structure limitations:** The use of a greedy approach might lead to suboptimal tree structures. A discussion of the potential for getting trapped in local optima and possible strategies to mitigate this would be beneficial.
* **Missing details:** Some implementation details are glossed over. For example, the heap maintenance process in Algorithm 1 could be elaborated for greater clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
* The scalability advantage is significant and opens the door to applying Bayesian hierarchical community detection to larger networks.
* The theoretical foundation is solid.
* The initial empirical results are promising.
* The potential impact on the field is high, as it offers a practical alternative to computationally expensive Bayesian methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental validation is somewhat limited.
* The lack of hyperparameter analysis is a concern.
* The predictive performance, while competitive, isn't state-of-the-art.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty of the approach, the potential for scalability, and the solid theoretical foundation outweigh the concerns regarding limited experiments and hyperparameter sensitivity. The authors have identified a significant bottleneck in Bayesian hierarchical community detection and proposed a promising solution.  I believe this work has the potential to stimulate further research in this area, and the minor flaws can be addressed in future work.  I view this as a bold and significant contribution, prioritizing a novel idea with good foundational work over absolute methodological perfection.  



**Score:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
