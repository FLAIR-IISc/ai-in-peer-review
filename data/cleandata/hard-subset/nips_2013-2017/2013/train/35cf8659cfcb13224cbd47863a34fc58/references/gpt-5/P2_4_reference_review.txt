Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian agglomerative procedure to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels: a tree T over vertices induces a set of tree-consistent partitions; a prior over partitions is defined via a stopping probability πS at each internal node, and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) decomposes recursively with on-diagonal blocks modeled by f (within-community) and off-diagonal blocks by g (between-community), using conjugate beta–Bernoulli terms. Given T, the marginal likelihood can be computed by dynamic programming. To learn T, the paper uses a bottom-up agglomerative search akin to Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT), considering join and absorb merges, scoring each local merge by a Bayes factor computed from local sufficient statistics that are cached to yield O(1) inner-loop updates. The overall complexity is O(n^2 log n), with a sparse variant that forbids merging trees with no edges between them. A simple predictive scheme traverses the learned tree to mix within- and between-cluster posteriors.

The approach builds directly on Bayesian hierarchical clustering (BHC) and BRT for i.i.d. data (Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.) and on the IRM for graphs, but restricts the partition space via a tree to allow exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions. It is closely related conceptually to hierarchical random graphs/dendrogram-based network models (e.g., Clauset–Moore–Newman) and to multiscale/hierarchical blockmodels, but differs by its specific Bayesian marginal likelihood and agglomerative search mechanics with conjugate f/g and partition prior πS.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic model that cleanly separates within and between community likelihoods and yields an exact marginal over tree-consistent partitions.
- Efficient inference: well-engineered caching and local scoring make the agglomerative procedure practical; complexity and implementation details are explained.
- Prediction procedure is simple and fast (linear in depth), and the paper reports competitive AUC/log-predictive performance versus IRM, comparable to LFRM and below ILA, with markedly lower wall-clock time.
- Empirical demonstrations on a classical small network (Sampson) and a medium-sized co-authorship graph (NIPS), plus a qualitative large-tree visualization for the full NIPS dataset.
- Sensible practical variations (sparse BHCD; binary-only merges) and discussion of their effects.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: the inference algorithm mirrors BRT (including join/absorb choices and πS form) and BHC’s marginal-likelihood recursion; the main contribution is adapting these to relational data with a blockmodel f/g and caching statistics. This is valuable, but should be framed as such.
- Limited comparisons: experiments primarily compare to IRM (Gibbs and greedy). Cited results for LFRM and ILA are indirect; there is no side-by-side runtime/memory comparison under matched code/hardware. Important baselines like hierarchical random graphs/dendrogram models and degree-corrected SBMs (and hierarchical variants) are not evaluated.
- Hyperparameters are fixed (α,β,δ,λ,γ) without sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter learning; the strong prior α>β and δ<λ may drive behavior and could disadvantage cross-method comparisons.
- The “sparse BHCD” heuristic (forbidding zero-edge merges) breaks the stated generative story and introduces a model–inference mismatch; some discussion of its impact on consistency/bias is warranted.
- Reproducibility: algorithmic description is good, but some implementation details (e.g., exact heap update policy under ties, memory complexity, code availability) are missing.
- Minor clarity issues: predictive complexity is described as linear in depth but later as Θ(n); please clarify. Some typos and dangling references (e.g., “BHCD global” without definition in the excerpt) should be cleaned up.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The model is technically sound; the marginal likelihood recursion and Bayes factor merge score are well justified. Claims about runtime are plausible and supported by design; empirical speedups vs IRM are convincing. However, the scope of empirical evaluation is modest and missing several relevant baselines and sensitivity studies. The sparse heuristic departs from the model.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized; equations and recursion are clear. A more explicit contrast to BRT/BHC and to hierarchical random graph models would help. Please resolve the predictive complexity inconsistency and better motivate γ.
- Originality: Moderate. The main idea is a thoughtful adaptation of BRT/BHC machinery to blockmodels, with efficient caching. The partition prior and merge operators are inherited; the novelty lies in the f/g decomposition for relational data and engineering aspects.
- Significance: Practical significance is reasonable: a scalable, simple Bayesian method for hierarchical communities that gives useful predictions and interpretable trees. The contribution is incremental but could be adopted by practitioners needing fast hierarchical structure discovery in networks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add direct comparisons to hierarchical random graphs (Clauset–Moore–Newman) and to scalable degree-corrected/hierarchical SBMs where possible; report wall-clock, AUC, and calibration.
- Provide hyperparameter sensitivity (α,β,δ,λ,γ), or incorporate empirical Bayes/MAP-II for γ and Beta parameters; discuss identifiability when edge density varies.
- Clarify and justify the “sparse” constraint theoretically, or present it as a post-hoc speed heuristic with an ablation on its effect on likelihood/prediction.
- Expand experiments to 1–2 additional benchmark networks with varying sparsity and known hierarchical structure; include variance over restarts and tie-breaking effects.
- Release code and detail memory usage; document the exact tie-breaking policy and heap invalidation strategy.

Pros
- Simple, fast, and well-motivated Bayesian hierarchical community model.
- Exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions; efficient dynamic program.
- Strong speed–performance trade-off; interpretable hierarchies.
- Clear exposition of inference and practical variants.

Cons
- Incremental novelty relative to BRT/BHC; missing key baselines (HRG, degree-corrected SBMs).
- Fixed hyperparameters; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Heuristic sparse variant departs from generative model.
- Experiments limited in breadth and scale relative to claims.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. While the conceptual novelty is modest, the paper offers a clean and practical adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas to relational data with solid engineering, good clarity, and compelling speed advantages with acceptable predictive performance. Strengthening comparisons and addressing hyperparameter sensitivity would further bolster the case.
