This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative clustering method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The work positions itself as a solution to the poor scalability of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for network analysis, such as those based on the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which typically rely on slow MCMC inference. The proposed model builds upon the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012), adapting it to relational data. The core of the method is a probabilistic model that defines the marginal likelihood of network data given a tree structure by summing over all partitions consistent with that tree. This marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently via a dynamic programming recursion. Inference of the tree structure is then framed as a greedy model selection problem, where a hierarchy is built bottom-up by iteratively merging subtrees that provide the largest increase in the Bayes factor. The authors demonstrate experimentally that BHCD is orders of magnitude faster than IRM inference, achieving comparable link prediction performance in a fraction of the time.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Scalability:** The primary strength of this paper is its contribution to scalable Bayesian network analysis. The proposed method is significantly faster (two orders of magnitude in the experiments) than comparable MCMC-based approaches like the IRM. This drastic speed-up makes hierarchical Bayesian analysis practical for much larger networks, addressing a major bottleneck in the field. The O(n^2 log n) complexity, independent of the number of communities, is a strong result for dense graphs.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the recursive formulation for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant and correct. The greedy search algorithm based on maximizing local Bayes factors is a principled, albeit approximate, inference strategy. The authors' attention to computational detail, such as caching sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) updates in the inner loop, is commendable.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, building the model from the simpler stochastic blockmodel, and clearly explaining the inference algorithm. The figures, particularly the illustrations of the model (Fig 1) and merge operations (Fig 2), are effective aids to understanding.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The qualitative analysis on the classic Sampson's monastery dataset shows that BHCD can recover meaningful, interpretable structures. The quantitative comparison against the IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset provides compelling evidence for the method's speed advantage, clearly illustrating the speed-accuracy trade-off. The demonstration on the full NIPS dataset showcases the method's applicability to larger-scale problems.

### Weaknesses

*   **Greedy Inference Limitations:** The method's main weakness is its reliance on a greedy search, which is susceptible to finding poor local optima. The final hierarchy is highly dependent on the sequence of merges, and an early suboptimal merge cannot be undone. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of this approach is warranted. For instance, it is unclear how sensitive the results are to the tie-breaking strategy, which the authors mention is random.
*   **Limited Novelty:** While the application is novel and significant, the core methodology heavily borrows from previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering ([13], [14]). The primary contribution is the successful adaptation of this framework to a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for relational data, rather than a fundamentally new modeling or inference concept.
*   **Hyperparameter Treatment:** The hyperparameters for the Beta priors and the tree prior are fixed to values that "work well". This is a pragmatic choice for demonstrating the method, but it leaves questions about the model's sensitivity to these settings. The paper would be strengthened by including a sensitivity analysis or at least a discussion of how these hyperparameters could be set in a more data-driven manner (e.g., via empirical Bayes).
*   **Comparative Analysis:** The experimental comparison is focused exclusively on other Bayesian nonparametric models. Given that a key claim is computational efficiency, it would be highly informative to compare BHCD against fast, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., the Louvain method). Such a comparison would help to better position the paper's contribution within the broader community detection literature, clarifying the trade-offs between speed, statistical rigor, and the quality of the discovered hierarchies.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Addresses the critical and practical problem of scalability in Bayesian network modeling.
2.  Provides a method that is orders of magnitude faster than established MCMC techniques, making hierarchical analysis feasible for larger networks.
3.  The proposed method is technically sound, principled, and retains the benefits of a full probabilistic model (e.g., for model comparison and prediction).
4.  The paper is clearly written and supported by strong, well-executed experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The greedy nature of the inference is a significant approximation with no performance guarantees.
2.  The conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, as it is a direct application of an existing framework to a new problem domain.
3.  Predictive performance, while achieved quickly, does not advance the state-of-the-art compared to other recent models like ILA [10].
4.  The treatment of hyperparameters is underdeveloped, and the experimental comparison could be broader.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   Please add a caption to Table 1 to explain the variables used in the complexity analysis (e.g., n, m, K, I, R), which would improve its readability.
*   In the discussion of the Sampson's monastery results, the text refers to "BHCD global", but this variant is not explicitly named in the figure or methods. Please clarify this.
*   The statement that BHCD performs "better" than IRM should be nuanced. It seems to reach a comparable performance level much faster, which is the key point. The final performance of a long IRM run may be slightly better according to Figure 4.
*   To strengthen the paper, I encourage the authors to add a discussion section that more explicitly acknowledges the limitations of the greedy search and speculates on potential failure modes. Further, adding a brief experimental comparison to a widely-used fast heuristic method like the Louvain algorithm would significantly enhance the paper's impact by providing a more complete picture of where BHCD stands in the landscape of community detection methods.
