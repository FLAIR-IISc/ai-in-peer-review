This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to define a probabilistic model over tree-structured partitions of the network's vertices. The likelihood of the network data is based on a stochastic blockmodel (SBM), where different priors are used for the probability of edges within a community versus between communities. The model avoids the computational intractability of summing over all partitions, as done in the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), by restricting the model to partitions that are consistent with a single, learned hierarchy.

The main technical contribution is a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm for learning this hierarchy. Starting with each vertex as its own community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of sub-hierarchies (trees) that result in the largest increase in the marginal likelihood, as measured by a local Bayes factor. This approach is a direct extension of prior work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering for non-relational data [13, 14] to the domain of network analysis. The authors demonstrate that by carefully caching sufficient statistics, the algorithm achieves a time complexity of O(n^2 log n), which is notably independent of the number of inferred communities and significantly faster than MCMC-based competitors like the IRM. The method is evaluated on several standard network datasets, demonstrating its ability to recover sensible hierarchies and achieve competitive predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than the IRM.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses the critical challenge of scalability in Bayesian network analysis. Many powerful Bayesian nonparametric models, such as the IRM, rely on MCMC inference, which can be prohibitively slow for even moderately sized networks. By proposing a greedy inference scheme that is substantially faster while maintaining a principled Bayesian formulation, this work offers a practical tool for exploratory analysis of large networks. The two-orders-of-magnitude speedup shown in Figure 4 is a compelling result that makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling accessible to a wider range of problems.

*   **Originality:** While the inference algorithm is adapted from previous work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [14], its application to relational data with a hierarchical SBM likelihood is a novel and valuable contribution. The formulation of the merge score as a Bayes factor (Eq. 12) and the efficient update rules for the SBM's sufficient statistics (Eq. 13) represent a non-trivial adaptation. The introduction of non-binary merges (absorptions) and the "sparse" variant that leverages network sparsity are also valuable, practical innovations.

*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is correct. The agglomerative algorithm is a principled, if greedy, approach to model selection in the space of hierarchies. The experimental evaluation is well-executed, providing both qualitative validation on the well-understood Sampson's monastery network and quantitative comparison against a strong and relevant baseline (IRM) on a standard benchmark (NIPS-234). The claims of computational efficiency are strongly supported by the experimental results.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It logically progresses from the basic SBM to the hierarchical model and then to the inference algorithm. The use of figures to illustrate the model (Figure 1) and the merge operations (Figure 2) is effective. The description of the algorithm and the experimental setup is sufficient for an expert to understand the work.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

*   **Limited Novelty of Inference Scheme:** The core agglomerative search algorithm is structurally identical to that of Blundell et al. [14]. While the authors are transparent about this, it does mean that the novelty of the paper rests primarily on the application of this existing inference framework to a new likelihood model for networks.

*   **Limitations of Greedy Search:** The primary trade-off made for speed is optimality. Greedy algorithms are susceptible to local optima and cannot explore the full posterior over hierarchies in the way that an MCMC sampler can. While multiple restarts are used to mitigate this, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of this trade-off. For instance, the poor result for "BHCD global" in Figure 3 is attributed to "out of date scores on its heap," which is an unclear implementation-level detail. A more insightful explanation would be to frame this as a potential failure mode of the greedy search.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values. There is no analysis of the model's sensitivity to these settings or any discussion of how they might be chosen in a data-driven manner. For a Bayesian model, the lack of hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis is a weakness.

*   **Complexity Analysis:** The O(n^2 log n) complexity is stated without detailed justification, relying on the reader's familiarity with the cited work [14]. A brief explanation of how the caching of sufficient statistics for the relational likelihood leads to O(1) updates per merge consideration would strengthen the paper's self-containedness and clarity.

In summary, this paper presents a well-executed adaptation of an existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithm to the problem of community detection in networks. Its main strength is the significant computational speed-up it achieves over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, making it a valuable and practical contribution. While the novelty of the inference procedure itself is limited and the greedy approach has inherent drawbacks, the paper's significance, clarity, and strong empirical results make a compelling case for acceptance.
