## Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel, greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The authors address the limitations of existing Bayesian approaches, which often suffer from high computational cost or restrictive assumptions (e.g., fixed hierarchy depth). BHCD builds upon Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering, offering a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach to inferring nested community structures. The core idea involves modeling the network as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, combined with an efficient inference scheme that leverages dynamic programming for likelihood calculation and a greedy search for optimal tree structures.  The authors demonstrate the speed and effectiveness of BHCD on both synthetic and real-world datasets (Sampson’s monastery network, and a subset/full version of the NIPS co-authorship network), showcasing its ability to discover meaningful hierarchies and make accurate link predictions, significantly faster than established methods like IRM.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional stochastic blockmodels (requiring known K), greedy hierarchical clustering (lacking probabilistic foundations), and existing Bayesian approaches (e.g., IRM, Mondrian process) regarding scalability. It builds directly on prior work in Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and adapts these techniques to the relational data context. The comparison with IRM and LFRM is appropriate, given their relevance in the community detection space.  The paper clearly identifies the gap in efficient Bayesian methods for *hierarchical* community detection, making a compelling case for its contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** This is the standout strength. The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity (and even lower for sparse networks with the BHCD sparse variant) is a significant advantage over existing Bayesian approaches which often scale poorly with network size. The empirical results on the NIPS dataset strongly support this claim.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The model is grounded in a solid Bayesian framework, providing a probabilistic account of the discovered structure.  The derivation of the marginal likelihood calculation using dynamic programming is elegant and contributes to the algorithm’s efficiency.
* **Flexibility:**  The approach doesn’t impose a fixed number of communities or hierarchy depth, allowing it to adapt to the underlying network structure.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are easy to follow.  The inclusion of illustrative examples (Sampson's monastery network) helps to solidify understanding.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of BHCD and its competitive performance compared to alternative methods. The speedup compared to IRM is particularly impressive.
* **Sparse Network Optimization:** The inclusion of BHCD sparse variant, tailored for sparse networks, demonstrates a thoughtful consideration for real-world data characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention setting hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ), they don’t discuss the sensitivity of the results to these choices. A more thorough exploration of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would strengthen the work. A justification for the chosen values beyond "found to work well" is needed.
* **Predictive Performance vs. State-of-the-Art:**  While BHCD’s speed is a major win, its predictive performance on the NIPS dataset is *comparable* to LFRM but *worse* than ILA. This needs to be acknowledged more explicitly.  The paper highlights speed, but a deeper analysis of the trade-off between speed and accuracy is warranted.
* **Greedy Nature:** The greedy search algorithm may not always find the globally optimal tree structure. Although the authors use restarts to mitigate this, the potential for suboptimal solutions remains a concern. The impact of different tie-breaking strategies on the final tree structure isn’t fully investigated.
* **Limited Scalability Analysis:** While the claimed complexity is promising, a more detailed discussion of scalability limitations for extremely large and dense networks would be helpful. Specifically, how does memory usage scale with network size?
* **Connection to Existing Hierarchical Models:** While the paper mentions existing hierarchical models (e.g., [8], [9], [10], [11]), the discussion feels somewhat cursory. A more detailed comparison of the underlying assumptions and advantages/disadvantages of BHCD compared to these alternative approaches would be valuable.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant improvement in computational efficiency for Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
* Sound theoretical foundation and clear mathematical formulation.
* Demonstrably effective performance on multiple datasets.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in network analysis.
*  Well-written and clearly presented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Predictive performance is not state-of-the-art.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Potential for suboptimal solutions due to the greedy search.
* Scalability limitations for extremely large networks are not fully addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. The computational efficiency of BHCD is a significant achievement and addresses a critical limitation of existing Bayesian methods. While the predictive performance isn’t leading-edge, the speed advantage and the solid theoretical foundation make this work worthy of acceptance. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Discuss Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Include a brief analysis of how the choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) affects the results.
2.  **Clarify Predictive Performance:** Provide a more nuanced discussion of the trade-off between speed and accuracy, and explicitly acknowledge that BHCD’s predictive performance is not superior to the best-performing methods.
3. **Scalability Discussion:** Briefly address memory usage and potential bottlenecks for very large networks.
4. **Expand Related Work:** Elaborate on the connections between BHCD and existing hierarchical community detection models.
