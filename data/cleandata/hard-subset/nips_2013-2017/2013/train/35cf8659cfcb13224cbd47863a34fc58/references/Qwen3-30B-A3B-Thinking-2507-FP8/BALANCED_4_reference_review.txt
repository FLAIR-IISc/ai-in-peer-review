

**Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"**

**Summary**  
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The approach extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14] to relational data by modeling communities as a tree-structured partition of vertices, where each internal node represents a community and leaves are individual vertices. BHCD uses a dynamic programming scheme to compute marginal likelihoods in O(n² log n) time, claiming to be two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy. The method is evaluated on Sampson’s monastery network, a NIPS co-authorship subset (NIPS-234), and the full NIPS co-authorship network (2864 vertices). The work builds on hierarchical Bayesian models [8,12] but addresses scalability limitations of prior approaches through efficient sufficient statistic caching.

**Strengths**  
1. **Technical Contribution**: The dynamic programming approach with cached sufficient statistics (Section 4) is a clear, well-executed innovation for scaling Bayesian hierarchical community detection. The O(n² log n) complexity is rigorously derived and empirically validated, offering a significant practical advantage over IRM (O(n²K²)) and other Bayesian methods.  
2. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions (e.g., tree-consistent partitions, sufficient statistics), algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1), and thorough experimental validation. The ablation of BHCD variants (sparse, binary) provides useful insights.  
3. **Empirical Results**: BHCD achieves competitive predictive performance (AUC, log predictive probability) on NIPS-234 while being 100× faster than IRM. The qualitative analysis on Sampson’s network (Figure 3) demonstrates coherent hierarchical structure, aligning with known community labels.  
4. **Practical Utility**: The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) is well-motivated for real-world sparse networks, and the tie-breaking strategy (50 restarts) ensures robustness. The predictive probability framework (Section 4.2) is elegantly integrated.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Originality Gap**: The core algorithmic structure (greedy agglomerative selection) is nearly identical to [14], with the primary novelty being the adaptation to relational data and caching for efficiency. The paper underemphasizes distinctions from [14] and fails to compare against non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering (e.g., HDBSCAN [11]), which could better contextualize the Bayesian contribution.  
2. **Methodological Limitations**:  
   - The hyperparameters (α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, γ=0.4) are arbitrarily chosen without sensitivity analysis or justification, weakening reproducibility.  
   - The "BHCD sparse" variant prohibits merging disconnected components, but the rationale is unclear—why should disconnected components *not* merge (e.g., if they share similar structural roles)? This needs empirical validation.  
   - The claim of "two orders of magnitude faster" lacks precise benchmarks (e.g., absolute runtime on large networks) and ignores non-Bayesian baselines.  
3. **Theoretical Weaknesses**: The paper does not address whether the tree structure is identifiable from data or how the model handles noise/missing edges (e.g., Sampson’s network uses negative edges as "absent," but this is not rigorously justified).  
4. **Significance**: While scalability is improved, the paper does not convincingly argue *why* hierarchical Bayesian structure is necessary over simpler flat models (e.g., IRM) for the tasks studied. The NIPS hierarchy (Figure 5) appears interpretable but does not demonstrate unique insights unavailable from flat communities.

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. The work presents a technically sound and practical extension to existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering, with clear empirical advantages in speed. However, the novelty is marginal given the close relationship to [14], and the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work or justify the Bayesian approach’s necessity for hierarchical community detection. The lack of hyperparameter analysis, ambiguous sparse variant rationale, and underdeveloped significance arguments prevent it from meeting NIPS’ bar for impactful contributions.

**Suggestions for Improvement**  
1. **Clarify Novelty**: Explicitly contrast BHCD with [14] and [8] (e.g., "While [14] handles general data, BHCD adapts it to relational networks via [X], achieving [Y] speedup"). Add a comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering (e.g., [11]) to contextualize the Bayesian contribution.  
2. **Strengthen Methodology**: Include a hyperparameter sensitivity study (e.g., varying α, β, γ) and justify choices via cross-validation. Provide a clear rationale for prohibiting merges between disconnected components (e.g., "Disconnected merges increase false positives; we validate this on [dataset]").  
3. **Enhance Significance**: Demonstrate *why* hierarchical structure matters for downstream tasks (e.g., link prediction in hierarchical vs. flat models). Show how BHCD’s hierarchy reveals insights unattainable with flat models (e.g., "The NIPS hierarchy uncovers subfields *within* machine learning, whereas flat models conflate them").  
4. **Improve Clarity**: Simplify tree notation (e.g., replace σ¬chSS with clearer terms) and add a small example of the sufficient statistic caching (Equation 13) in the main text.  

The paper’s technical execution is strong, but its contribution lacks the originality and significance required for NIPS. With the above revisions—particularly a sharper novelty statement and deeper analysis of *why* Bayesian hierarchical modeling is advantageous—it could become a compelling submission.
