Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian procedure to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model uses a stochastic blockmodel likelihood with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors, distinguishing within- vs between-cluster edge densities via different hyperparameters. A tree T induces a restricted family of partitions Φ(T); the marginal likelihood p(D|T) sums exactly over tree-consistent partitions via a dynamic programming recursion. Inference is cast as greedy agglomerative model selection over trees: at each step, merge two subtrees via one of three operators (join, absorb left, absorb right), scored by a local Bayes factor derived from a forest likelihood. Efficient caching of sufficient statistics yields O(1) update cost per candidate merge and overall O(n^2 log n) time; a “sparse” variant forbids merging disconnected components. A predictive distribution for missing edges is obtained by a top-down recursion on the learned tree. Experiments show qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery data, predictive comparisons to the IRM (Gibbs and greedy) on a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset, and a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS graph.

This work is closely related to Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (BHC) and Bayesian Rose Trees, from which it borrows the tree prior πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| and the dynamic-programming marginalization over tree-consistent partitions. The novelty is to adapt this framework to relational data via blockmodel likelihoods and to engineer a fast agglomerative search for trees using local Bayes factors and cached relational sufficient statistics. It also relates to IRM and extensions to hierarchies, the Mondrian process for trees with relational likelihoods, and shallow hierarchical network models (e.g., ILA). Greedy hierarchical clustering in networks (e.g., Girvan-Newman, Clauset-Newman-Moore) is discussed as fast but non-probabilistic; BHCD aims to retain speed while providing a probabilistic model and link prediction.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation: exact marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions with a simple recursive computation.
- Algorithmic contribution: efficient local scoring with caching; overall O(n^2 log n) runtime independent of the number of communities; practical sparse variant.
- Empirical speed: on NIPS-234, reaches reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than IRM; qualitative structure on Sampson and a large NIPS graph.
- Simplicity: conjugate likelihood enables closed-form scoring and fast predictions.

Weaknesses
- Originality is incremental: core tree prior and DP marginalization are taken from BHC/Rose Trees; main novelty is plugging in a blockmodel likelihood and engineering details.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: comparisons focus on IRM; no empirical comparison to other hierarchical network models (e.g., hierarchical random graph (HRG), Mondrian-based RL, nested SBMs) or degree-corrected SBMs; no ablation/sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Some clarity/consistency issues: prediction complexity is said to be linear in depth but later claimed Θ(n); treatment of missing data vs “sparse” merging rule; “BHCD global” appears in figure text but is not defined; minor notation typos.
- Model restrictions: undirected, no self-edges; fixed hyperparameters α,β,δ,λ,γ with no learning; no discussion of degree heterogeneity; greedy search with random tie-breaking and restarts lacks theoretical guarantees.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The recursion for p(D|T) (Eq. 10) follows from the prior over tree-consistent partitions and conjugate marginal likelihoods; the merge score (Eq. 12) is a correct local Bayes factor under the forest factorization. The caching identities (Eq. 13) are sensible and enable the stated complexity. The “sparse” rule is reasonable for fully observed simple graphs but could be problematic under missingness (merging disconnected components might still be warranted if many entries are unobserved).
- Experiments support speed claims and basic predictive utility. However, the scope is limited (one medium dataset for quantitative comparison), and baselines omit several relevant hierarchical network models. Hyperparameters are fixed ad hoc without sensitivity analysis. No reporting of variability across restarts, and hardware/settings for timing are not detailed.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. The derivations are concise and explicit. Suggestions:
  - Clarify Section 4.2: if prediction traverses a single root-to-leaf path, complexity is O(depth), not Θ(n).
  - Define “BHCD global” if used in figures, or remove.
  - Be precise about handling missing entries in D across all components (likelihood, sufficient statistics, sparse merging).
  - Provide pseudocode for Algorithm 1 or more implementation details (heap invalidation, restart strategy).
  - Fix minor typos/formatting (e.g., “wherem n2”; consistent notation for σ).

Originality
- The combination of BHC/Rose Trees with blockmodel likelihoods for networks and the specific merge operators/caching is a useful, but modest, extension. Related hierarchical network models (HRG; nested SBMs) are not empirically compared; Mondrian-process-based relational models are cited but not evaluated against.

Significance
- Practical significance stems from speed: a fast, probabilistic hierarchical clustering method that yields reasonable predictions and interpretable trees on medium-to-large graphs. As a tool or initializer, it could see use. Methodological advance is incremental relative to prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering and network models; the absence of stronger baselines and broader datasets tempers impact.

Pros/cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, efficient Bayesian hierarchical model with exact marginalization per tree.
- Strong speed-accuracy trade-off versus IRM; scalable implementation details.
- Clear exposition; qualitative structures on canonical datasets.

Cons:
- Limited novelty beyond adapting existing BHC/Rose Tree machinery to blockmodels.
- Narrow empirical evaluation; missing comparisons to key hierarchical network baselines.
- Fixed hyperparameters and no sensitivity or degree-correction; some clarity issues.

Suggestions to improve
- Add quantitative comparisons to hierarchical network baselines (e.g., HRG; nested SBMs; Mondrian-based hierarchical relational models) and degree-corrected SBMs.
- Provide sensitivity analysis over α,β,δ,λ,γ and ablations (e.g., join-only vs absorb, effect of sparse rule).
- Clarify prediction complexity, missing data handling, and define all variants used in figures.
- Consider learning hyperparameters or using empirical Bayes; discuss degree heterogeneity and potential extensions.
- Release code to support reproducibility and adoption.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Rationale: The paper presents a well-executed, fast, and practical adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas to blockmodel-based network analysis, with convincing speed benefits. However, the methodological novelty is moderate, and the empirical evaluation is too limited given the space of relevant hierarchical network models. With expanded comparisons and clarity improvements, this work could be strong.

Reviewer confidence: High on methodology and evaluation standards; medium on coverage of all related hierarchical network literature.
