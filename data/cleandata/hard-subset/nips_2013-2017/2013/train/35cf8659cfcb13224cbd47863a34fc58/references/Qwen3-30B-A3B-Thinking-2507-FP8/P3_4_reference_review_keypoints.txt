- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery that models nested communities and uses a greedy agglomerative algorithm with O(n² log n) time.  
- Demonstrates BHCD is much faster than Bayesian nonparametric methods (e.g., IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive performance on tested networks.  
- Clearly identifies the gap between computationally expensive Bayesian hierarchical methods and non-probabilistic greedy clustering, positioning BHCD as a bridge.  
- Provides a strong theoretical foundation: hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, marginal likelihood derivation, and efficient dynamic programming for computation.  
- Presents thorough experimental comparisons with Bayesian methods (IRM, LFRM, ILA) and complexity analysis showing BHCD’s worst-case advantage.  
- Addresses practical issues like a sparse-optimized BHCD variant and tie-breaking in merge scores, showing empirical benefits on sparse networks.  
- Lacks quantitative metrics for assessing hierarchy quality and relies mainly on visual inspection of block-diagonal structure.  
- Does not sufficiently compare BHCD to state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods to justify efficiency vs. structure-quality trade-offs.  
- Makes ambiguous performance claims (e.g., "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM") without detailed timing methodology or implementation specifics.  
- Uses fixed hyperparameters without sensitivity analysis, leaving robustness of results to parameter choices unclear.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written, and a valuable contribution that offers practical computational advantages for large networks.  
- Recommended improvements: add quantitative hierarchy-quality metrics, compare with non-Bayesian methods, include hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and provide concrete timing comparisons.  
- Recommendation: Accept — BHCD is a strong, impactful contribution to hierarchical community detection with notable computational benefits.
