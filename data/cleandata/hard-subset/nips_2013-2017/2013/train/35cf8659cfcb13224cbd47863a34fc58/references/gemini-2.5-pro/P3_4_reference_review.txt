### Summary

This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to adapt the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework, previously applied to feature-based data, to relational data using a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) likelihood. The model defines a probability distribution over tree-consistent partitions of the network's nodes. Inference is performed via a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges subtrees to maximize the marginal likelihood of the data. The authors demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than comparable Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving competitive predictive performance on link prediction tasks.

The work elegantly bridges the gap between fast, non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., modularity optimization) and principled but computationally expensive Bayesian nonparametric approaches. The main contribution is the formulation of the SBM within a recursive marginal likelihood calculation for tree structures and an efficient greedy search algorithm with a novel caching scheme to learn the hierarchy.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and important problem: the prohibitive computational cost of fully Bayesian inference for hierarchical network structures. The proposed solution, which combines the mathematical elegance of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework [13, 14] with the SBM, is both novel and practical. The demonstrated speed-up over MCMC-based IRM is substantial and could make Bayesian hierarchical analysis feasible for a much wider range of networks. This represents a significant step forward in scalable Bayesian network modeling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood in Equation (10) is a correct and clever application of dynamic programming on trees, which allows the sum over an exponential number of partitions to be computed efficiently. The agglomerative search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-defined, and the caching scheme for sufficient statistics, which reduces the inner loop complexity to O(1), is a crucial contribution to the method's overall efficiency of O(n² log n).

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the model is built up logically from the SBM, and the inference algorithm is explained in sufficient detail. The figures, particularly Figure 1 illustrating tree-consistent partitions and Figure 4 showing the time-vs-performance trade-off, are highly effective at conveying the paper's core ideas and results.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is strong and convincing.
    *   The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network provide a good sanity check and demonstrate that the method finds interpretable, meaningful hierarchies that align with sociological findings.
    *   The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 dataset is the paper's highlight. The direct comparison of predictive performance versus wall-clock time against a key baseline (IRM) clearly substantiates the main claim of achieving good results much faster.
    *   The application to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's scalability to networks with thousands of nodes.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The primary weakness is the reliance on a greedy search algorithm. This approach finds a single point estimate (a MAP tree) and is susceptible to local optima. While the use of multiple random restarts mitigates this to some extent, it does not capture the posterior uncertainty over hierarchies, which is a key theoretical advantage of fully Bayesian methods. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this trade-off: BHCD sacrifices posterior exploration for massive gains in speed. It would be useful to know how variable the results are across different restarts.

2.  **Ad-hoc Nature of the "Sparse" Variant:** The BHCD-sparse variant, which prohibits merges between disconnected components, is introduced as a practical improvement. While intuitively appealing and empirically effective, this constraint feels somewhat ad-hoc from a pure modeling perspective. The generative model itself does not forbid such merges. This modification should be framed more clearly as a heuristic search constraint designed to improve performance on sparse graphs, rather than a direct consequence of the model.

3.  **Comparison to Other Fast Methods:** The experimental comparison focuses on IRM, a flagship Bayesian nonparametric model. However, to fully situate BHCD in the broader landscape, a comparison to other widely used (though non-Bayesian) and fast hierarchical community detection algorithms, such as those based on modularity optimization (e.g., the Louvain method), would have been beneficial. This would provide a clearer picture of where BHCD sits on the spectrum of speed versus principled probabilistic grounding.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values "found to work well". A sensitivity analysis or a more detailed discussion on how to set these parameters would improve the paper's utility for practitioners. For instance, how does the choice of γ influence the "bushiness" of the resulting tree?

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Provides a novel and elegant synthesis of Bayesian hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels.
*   Addresses the critical challenge of scalability in Bayesian network analysis, offering a practical and principled alternative to slow MCMC methods.
*   The claimed speed-up of two orders of magnitude over IRM is highly significant and well-supported by experiments.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and presents a complete piece of work with strong empirical validation.
*   The work is likely to be influential and built upon by others seeking to develop scalable probabilistic models for network data.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The greedy inference approach provides only a point estimate of the hierarchy, forgoing the uncertainty quantification that is a hallmark of Bayesian methods.
*   The comparison to related work could be broader, including fast non-Bayesian hierarchical methods.
*   The "sparse" variant, while effective, is a heuristic modification to the search procedure.

### Recommendation

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed BHCD method is a significant and timely contribution to the field of network analysis. It offers a compelling solution to the scalability problem of Bayesian hierarchical models, making such analyses practical for larger datasets. The technical contribution is solid, and the empirical results are impressive. I strongly recommend acceptance.
