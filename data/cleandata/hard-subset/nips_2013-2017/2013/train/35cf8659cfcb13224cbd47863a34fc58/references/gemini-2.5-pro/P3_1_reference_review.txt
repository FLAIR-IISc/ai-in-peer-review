### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative algorithm for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core contribution is the elegant extension of the Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering framework, particularly the "Bayesian Rose Trees" model of Blundell et al. (2010), to relational data. The method starts with each node as its own community and iteratively merges pairs of communities (trees) that yield the largest increase in the marginal likelihood of the data. The model defines a probability distribution over all partitions consistent with a given hierarchy (tree), allowing for the exact computation of the tree's marginal likelihood, `p(D|T)`, via a dynamic program. This likelihood serves as a principled score for the greedy merge operations.

The work is well-positioned within the literature. It contrasts itself with classic flat community detection models like the Stochastic Blockmodel (SBM) and its mixed-membership and nonparametric (Infinite Relational Model - IRM) variants. It also distinguishes itself from algorithmic hierarchical methods (e.g., Newman-Girvan) by providing a full probabilistic model. Crucially, it offers a computationally efficient alternative to more complex Bayesian nonparametric hierarchical models (e.g., nested CRPs or the Mondrian process), which often rely on slow MCMC inference. The primary selling point of BHCD is its speed, claiming a runtime of O(n² log n) and demonstrating performance orders of magnitude faster than a standard IRM implementation.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Algorithmic Elegance and Principled Foundation:** The paper presents a very clean and well-motivated approach. Extending the Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering framework to relational data is a natural and powerful idea. Using the marginal likelihood of the tree as the objective for a greedy search provides a solid Bayesian foundation for what would otherwise be a heuristic clustering procedure. The recursive calculation in Equation (10) is a key component and is explained clearly.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The main strength of the paper is the remarkable computational performance. The claim of being two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of an IRM is significant and well-supported by the experiments in Figure 4. The careful caching of sufficient statistics (Equation 13) to achieve O(1) updates within the main loop is a non-trivial and important implementation detail that enables the O(n² log n) complexity. This makes the method practical for moderately large networks where MCMC-based approaches would be prohibitive.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to showcase the method's strengths.
    *   The qualitative analysis on Sampson's Monastery network (Figure 3) demonstrates that the method produces interpretable and sensible hierarchies that align with established sociological findings.
    *   The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 dataset is compelling. The paper not only compares against a key baseline (IRM) but also uses standard metrics (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC) and a public train/test split, making the results directly comparable to other recent work (e.g., LFRM, ILA). The speed vs. performance trade-off is starkly illustrated.
    *   The application to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's scalability.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It systematically builds from the SBM to the hierarchical model and then to the agglomerative inference algorithm. The notation is consistent and the figures are informative.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Greedy Search Limitations:** The most significant weakness is the reliance on a greedy search algorithm. This finds a single point estimate of the hierarchy `T`, ignoring the posterior uncertainty over structures. While the authors mitigate this by averaging predictions over multiple random restarts, this is not a substitute for proper Bayesian posterior averaging. The paper should be more explicit about this limitation. The claim of providing a "full probabilistic account" is slightly overstated; it provides a probabilistic *model*, but inference is based on optimization (model selection) rather than full posterior inference.

2.  **Locality of the Merge Score:** The Bayes factor score in Equation (12) is local, considering only the data within the sub-clusters being merged (`p(DMM|M)`). This makes an implicit assumption that the best local merge will lead to a globally optimal structure. This is not guaranteed and can lead to classic greedy algorithm pitfalls. A brief discussion of this assumption and its potential consequences would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are fixed to values that "work well". For a Bayesian model, this is a point of concern. A sensitivity analysis, even a brief one on a smaller dataset, showing how performance varies with different hyperparameter settings would significantly improve the paper's rigor. Alternatively, placing priors on these hyperparameters and integrating them out or performing MAP estimation could be discussed as future work.

4.  **Justification for the "Sparse" Variant:** The `BHCD sparse` variant is introduced as a heuristic fix ("does not seem to be a reasonable behaviour") that empirically improves results. While the intuition is clear, the paper would be stronger if this constraint could be motivated from a more principled or probabilistic standpoint. Does this correspond to a different prior on the tree structure, for example?

5.  **Comparison to Other *Hierarchical* Models:** The experimental comparison is primarily against the *flat* IRM. While the paper mentions other hierarchical models (e.g., [8], [12]) in the introduction and dismisses them as computationally expensive, the empirical section would be more complete with a direct comparison, even on a small dataset where those methods are tractable. This would help to better situate BHCD's performance not just against flat models, but against its direct hierarchical competitors.

### **Final Assessment**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. It successfully combines the principled framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering with the practical need for scalable community detection. The proposed BHCD method is elegant, fast, and empirically effective. The strengths, particularly the impressive speed-up over established Bayesian nonparametric methods while maintaining competitive predictive performance, are highly significant for practitioners and researchers working with network data. While the greedy nature of the inference is a notable limitation, the authors are transparent about the algorithm and its practical benefits are clearly demonstrated. The paper is well-written, the experiments are convincing, and the work is likely to be influential and built upon by others.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
