## Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. Building upon previous Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, the authors propose a model that organizes communities into a nested hierarchy represented by a tree.  The key contribution lies in an efficient inference scheme that allows for fast computation of the marginal likelihood, enabling practical application to larger networks. The paper demonstrates the speed and effectiveness of BHCD on both synthetic (Sampson’s Monastery network) and real-world (NIPS co-authorship network) datasets, showcasing significant speedups compared to the IRM while maintaining competitive performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the existing landscape of community detection algorithms. They acknowledge the limitations of both parametric methods like stochastic blockmodels (requiring known community number) and fully Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM (suffering from scalability issues).  They effectively highlight how their method bridges the gap, offering a Bayesian approach with a practical computational cost.  The connection to previous Bayesian hierarchical clustering work ([13, 14]) is well-established, and the extensions to relational data are clearly articulated. The references to related work (e.g., [1, 2, 5, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]) are comprehensive and demonstrate a strong understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** This is the most significant strength of the paper. The authors achieve remarkable speedups compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, particularly the IRM. The O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity (and potentially O(n+m) for sparse networks with the `BHCD sparse` variant) makes the method applicable to larger networks than previously feasible.
* **Bayesian Framework:** The Bayesian formulation provides a principled approach to community detection, offering uncertainty quantification and the ability to incorporate prior knowledge.
* **Hierarchical Structure:** The ability to discover hierarchical community structure is valuable, as many real-world networks exhibit such organization. The tree representation provides an intuitive and interpretable visualization of the community hierarchy.
* **Clear and Well-Defined Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm (BHCD) is clearly explained and the optimization strategies (caching of sufficient statistics) are well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson’s Monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness.  The comparison to IRM is particularly insightful.
* **Practical Improvements:** The `BHCD sparse` variation demonstrates a clear understanding of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost for sparse networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors state that they found the chosen hyperparameters to work well, a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.  How do changes in α, β, δ, λ, and γ impact the resulting community structure?
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper lacks strong theoretical guarantees on the convergence or optimality of the greedy algorithm.  While the empirical results are promising, a more rigorous analysis of the algorithm's behavior would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** The paper primarily compares against other Bayesian methods. A comparison to well-established non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., spectral clustering, Louvain algorithm) would provide a more complete picture of the method's performance.
* **Link Prediction Details:** The description of link prediction is relatively brief. Providing more detail on the specific implementation and evaluation metrics would enhance the reproducibility of the results.
* **Scalability Bottleneck (Potential):** While the inference scales well, the authors acknowledge a potential bottleneck in calculating the likelihood for sparse networks, and mention further work is needed. This is an area to focus on in future research.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the experimental results.  The authors are careful to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. It appears to be a complete piece of work, with a clear methodology and thorough evaluation.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. However, some sections (e.g., the mathematical derivations) could benefit from further simplification and explanation.  The use of notation is consistent throughout the paper.

**Originality:**

The work presents a novel combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering and relational data modeling. The efficient inference scheme is a significant contribution and distinguishes this work from previous efforts.

**Significance:**

The proposed method has the potential to significantly advance the field of community detection by providing a computationally efficient and principled approach to discovering hierarchical community structure. The method is likely to be used by researchers and practitioners working with large social networks and other relational data.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Significant speedup over existing Bayesian methods (IRM).
* Principled Bayesian framework with hierarchical structure.
* Well-defined algorithm with practical improvements (BHCD sparse).
* Strong empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* Addresses a challenging problem in a novel and effective manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of extensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Absence of theoretical guarantees.
* Limited comparison to non-Bayesian methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.  The strengths of the paper—particularly the computational efficiency and the novel approach to Bayesian hierarchical community detection—outweigh the weaknesses. The work represents a significant contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to a wide audience at NIPS. Addressing the suggested improvements (hyperparameter sensitivity, theoretical guarantees, and comparisons to non-Bayesian methods) in a future version of the paper would further strengthen the work.
