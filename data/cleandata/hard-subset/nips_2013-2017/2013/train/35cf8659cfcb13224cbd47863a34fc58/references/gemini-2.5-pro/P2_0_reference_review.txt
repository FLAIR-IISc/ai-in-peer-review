This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to extend the framework of Bayesian hierarchical clustering, previously developed for i.i.d. data, to relational data. The model defines a marginal likelihood for the network data given a specific hierarchical tree structure, which can be computed efficiently via dynamic programming. The algorithm then performs a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative search to find a high-likelihood tree, starting with each node as its own community and iteratively merging the pair of subtrees that provides the largest increase in the Bayes factor. The main contribution is a computationally efficient method that scales quadratically with the number of nodes, offering a significant speed advantage over existing Bayesian non-parametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), which often rely on slower MCMC or variational inference.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive computational efficiency. Many Bayesian non-parametric models for network structure, while theoretically elegant, are notoriously slow, limiting their application to small or medium-sized networks. By adopting a greedy search strategy, BHCD offers a practical tool that can produce meaningful results on networks with thousands of nodes in seconds or minutes. The claim of being "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" is well-substantiated in the NIPS-234 experiment and represents a significant practical advance. The O(n² log n) complexity, which is independent of the number of communities, is a major advantage over methods whose complexity depends on the number of inferred clusters (K), which can be large.

2.  **Model Elegance and Soundness:** The probabilistic formulation is clear and well-grounded. The paper builds logically from the stochastic blockmodel, incorporates a tree-based prior over partitions, and provides a clean recursive formula (Eq. 10) for the marginal likelihood p(D|T). This allows the greedy search to be framed as model selection, where each merge is chosen to maximize a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12). The use of conjugate priors with different hyperparameters for on-diagonal vs. off-diagonal blocks is a simple but effective way to encode the community-detection objective.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the method's strengths.
    *   The qualitative analysis on Sampson's Monastery network shows that BHCD recovers known social structures, providing confidence in the model's output.
    *   The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 co-authorship network is the paper's strongest point. It directly compares against a key baseline (IRM) on standard link prediction metrics (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC) and clearly illustrates the speed-performance trade-off. The results show that BHCD achieves comparable (and in some cases, better) predictive performance to the IRM in a fraction of the time.
    *   The application to the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's scalability, a key claim of the paper.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The progression from the stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical model is logical. The description of the algorithm, including the caching of sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) updates in the inner loop, is clear and provides enough detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Greedy Approach Limitations:** The main weakness, which the authors are transparent about, is the greedy nature of the inference. Agglomerative clustering is prone to finding local optima, and decisions made early in the search cannot be undone. While the paper uses multiple random restarts to mitigate this, there is no characterization of how far the discovered solutions might be from the global optimum. A discussion of these limitations, perhaps with a small-scale experiment comparing the greedy solution to one from a "gold standard" MCMC run (if computationally feasible), would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Originality in Context:** The core algorithmic idea of using a greedy search over a recursively defined marginal likelihood is a direct application of Heller and Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012). The novelty lies in adapting this framework to relational data using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. While this is a valuable and non-trivial contribution, the paper could be more explicit in delineating what parts of the framework are directly inherited and what constitutes the novel contribution for network data.

3.  **Limited Baselines:** The experimental comparison is focused almost exclusively on the IRM. While the IRM is a very relevant baseline, the field of community detection is vast. It would be beneficial to compare BHCD against:
    *   Other fast, greedy hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., those based on modularity optimization like Clauset et al., 2004) to see how the Bayesian model-selection criterion compares to heuristic objectives.
    *   Other scalable Bayesian models mentioned in the introduction, such as the Mondrian process, if implementations are available. While the paper's comparison to ILA [10] and LFRM [18] is done by citing their reported numbers on the same data splits, a direct comparison of runtime would have been illuminating.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed to values that "work well". A sensitivity analysis or a more principled method for setting/inferring these parameters would make the method more robust and easier for others to apply. For instance, how much does the performance change if the prior belief about community density (α > β, δ < λ) is weakened?

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** A highly scalable and practical Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection.
*   **Pro:** Orders-of-magnitude faster than standard Bayesian non-parametric competitors like the IRM.
*   **Pro:** The model is technically sound, and the greedy search is well-justified as a model selection procedure.
*   **Pro:** Strong and clear experimental validation on both qualitative and quantitative tasks.
*   **Con:** The greedy search is susceptible to local optima.
*   **Con:** The core algorithm is an adaptation of existing methods; the novelty is in the application to relational data.
*   **Con:** The range of experimental baselines is somewhat narrow.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant practical contribution to the field of network analysis. It addresses a well-known bottleneck—the scalability of Bayesian community detection models—with an effective and well-explained solution. While the greedy nature of the algorithm is a limitation, the demonstrated speed and competitive performance make it a valuable tool that is likely to be used and built upon by other researchers. The strengths, particularly the impressive empirical results and the clear presentation, far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is a solid contribution to the NIPS community.
