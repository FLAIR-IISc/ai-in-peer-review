## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel, greedy Bayesian approach (BHCD) for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, adapting them to relational data by framing the problem as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. Crucially, they focus on computational efficiency, achieving significant speedups compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while maintaining competitive performance. The paper details the model, an efficient inference scheme (leveraging caching for speed), experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network), and demonstrates the method’s potential for link prediction.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of community detection and Bayesian nonparametrics. It acknowledges the limitations of earlier work, such as the parametric nature and fixed community number requirements of Stochastic Blockmodels, the computational cost of IRM-based methods, and the depth limitations of other Bayesian approaches ([9, 10, 11]).  The authors build on Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering [13, 14], extending these methods to relational data, which is a natural and significant extension. The comparison to IRM and LFRM [18] in the experiments is particularly relevant and highlights the advantages of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** This is the most compelling strength. The reported speedups (two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration) are substantial and address a critical bottleneck in applying Bayesian methods to large networks. The caching scheme described seems well-motivated and contributes significantly to this efficiency.
* **Scalability:** The claimed time complexity of O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) is favourable, particularly for dense networks, and the potential for further improvement on sparse networks is noted.
* **Principled Bayesian Framework:**  The use of a Bayesian approach provides a probabilistic interpretation of the discovered community structure, allowing for uncertainty quantification and potentially more robust predictions.
* **Clear Presentation of the Model:** The authors provide a detailed and mathematically sound description of their model, including the key equations for likelihood calculation and the hierarchical prior.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network demonstrate the method’s ability to recover known community structures. The comparison to IRM on the NIPS dataset demonstrates the speed-accuracy trade-off favourably.
* **BHCD Sparse:**  The introduction of the "sparse" variant is a valuable addition, addressing the performance limitations on sparse networks and further improving scalability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention setting hyperparameters, the justification for choosing α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4 isn't thoroughly discussed. Exploring the sensitivity of results to these parameters would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Exploration of Hierarchy Depth:** The experiments focus primarily on reasonably shallow hierarchies. Investigating the method’s performance on datasets with deeper and more complex hierarchical structures would be beneficial.
* **Predictive Performance vs. State-of-the-Art:** While BHCD performs comparably to LFRM and better than IRM, it falls short of ILA [10] in terms of predictive accuracy. A deeper analysis of why this difference exists, and potential avenues for improvement, would be valuable.
* **Tie-Breaking Randomness:** The random tie-breaking during merges, while acknowledged, could introduce variability in results. Exploring alternative tie-breaking strategies (e.g., based on edge density or modularity) might improve consistency.
* **Lack of a Comprehensive Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** While the paper focuses on comparison to Bayesian approaches, a brief comparison to fast, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., those based on modularity optimization) would provide a more complete picture of the method’s performance.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery.
* The focus on computational efficiency is a significant contribution, making Bayesian hierarchical clustering more practical for large networks.
* The experimental results demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The predictive performance, while good, does not reach state-of-the-art levels.
* The hyperparameter choices and sensitivity analysis could be more thorough.
* The scope is somewhat limited by the focus on primarily shallow hierarchies.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong submission** that makes a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The authors have addressed a significant challenge – the computational cost of Bayesian hierarchical clustering – with a clever and effective solution. While there are some areas for improvement (hyperparameter sensitivity, deeper hierarchy exploration, and more comprehensive comparison), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. The method’s speed and scalability make it a promising tool for analyzing large-scale social networks.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I'd suggest adding a section discussing hyperparameter sensitivity, and expanding the NIPS experiments to include a more detailed comparison to the ILA model.
