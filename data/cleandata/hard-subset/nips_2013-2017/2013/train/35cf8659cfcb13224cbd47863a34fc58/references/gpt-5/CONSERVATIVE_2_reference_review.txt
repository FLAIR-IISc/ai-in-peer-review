Paper summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering procedure for network data. The model uses a tree to restrict the set of partitions over vertices and defines a marginal likelihood by summing over tree-consistent partitions. Each internal node corresponds to a community; the marginal over edges is a mixture of “on-diagonal” (within-group) and “off-diagonal” (between-group) likelihood terms, with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli instantiation used in experiments. The prior over partitions is derived from a node-wise stop/continue process, with πS = 1−(1−γ)|ch(S)|, following Bayesian Rose Trees. The marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed via a dynamic-programming recursion. Inference is performed by greedy agglomeration with three merge types (join and two absorptions), using local Bayes factors and cached sufficient statistics for O(1) inner-loop updates, yielding O(n^2 log n) complexity. A “sparse” variant forbids merges between components with no observed edges. The paper also derives predictive link probabilities by traversing a path in the inferred tree.

Empirically, the authors present: (i) qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery, (ii) predictive performance on a 234-author subset of the NIPS coauthorship network versus IRM (MCMC and greedy), and (iii) a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS 1–17 network (2864 nodes).

This work closely builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010), adapting those ideas to relational data via a blockmodel likelihood and within/between priors. It also relates to IRM and hierarchical extensions (e.g., Herlau et al.), and to hierarchical network models such as hierarchical random graphs/nested SBMs (e.g., Clauset–Moore–Newman; subsequent work by Peixoto), which are not discussed in detail.

Strengths
- Clear, modular formulation that marries rose-tree style priors with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood; the recursion for p(D|T) is elegant and computationally attractive.
- Greedy agglomerative inference with cached statistics is well engineered and appears practically fast; the three-way merge operator is a natural extension of rose-tree merging.
- Predictive computation is simple (path traversal) and exploits conjugacy.
- On NIPS-234, BHCD achieves reasonable predictive performance quickly; qualitative structures on Sampson and NIPS are sensible.
- The worst-case inference complexity is independent of an explicit number of communities, which is appealing for model selection.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is incremental. The core construction follows Bayesian Rose Trees almost verbatim (πS choice, join/absorb merges, DP recursion), with the main change being to use within/between blockmodel likelihoods. The paper should more carefully delineate contributions relative to [13,14] and to hierarchical SBMs/HRG.
- Limited empirical validation against appropriate baselines. For hierarchical community discovery, comparisons to hierarchical random graphs (Clauset–Moore–Newman), nested SBMs, or hierarchical IRM variants are missing. For predictive performance, the paper reports only IRM (and cites numbers for LFRM/ILA from other work). Without running strong baselines under a shared setup, it is difficult to assess significance.
- Assortativity built into the likelihood. Fixing α>β and δ<λ assumes communities are assortative; no experiments on disassortative or core–periphery graphs, and no sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters or γ. This is a strong modeling bias that could degrade performance on many real networks.
- Greedy local model selection with restarts but no global guarantees. The Bayes factor uses local data only; while computationally convenient, the paper does not justify that this is an exact marginal improvement under the full model or bound the suboptimality. The method relies on R random restarts and tie-breaking; there is no analysis of stability or variance across runs.
- Claims of scalability and speed are not rigorously substantiated. “Two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration” is an unusual and arguably unfair comparison. Modern IRM/hSBM implementations with split-merge MCMC or variational methods can be much faster per effective update. Memory and wall-clock details, and end-to-end comparisons on larger graphs, are lacking.
- No synthetic experiments. There is no controlled study of recovery (tree topology, depth, cluster accuracy) under known ground truth, nor robustness to noise, sparsity, or degree heterogeneity.
- Hyperparameter learning is absent. Fixed α,β,δ,λ,γ are used without inference; this can materially affect both hierarchy shape and predictions. The discussion mentions nonconjugate likelihoods but does not evaluate them.
- Clarity and completeness. Some notation/typo issues and missing citations (e.g., hierarchical random graphs; nested SBM literature). Code availability is not specified; reproducibility beyond high-level settings is unclear.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to α,β,δ,λ and γ? Can you provide a brief sensitivity analysis or perform empirical Bayes for these parameters?
2. Can you compare BHCD to a hierarchical SBM/HRG baseline (and to ILA/LFRM you cite) on NIPS-234 with shared folds and runtime budgets?
3. Is the local Bayes factor in Eq. (12) an exact marginal improvement for the global model under the factorization in Eq. (11), or an approximation? Please provide a derivation or clarify assumptions.
4. How stable are the inferred hierarchies across the R restarts and tie-breaks? Any quantitative measure (e.g., variation of information over trees)?
5. How does BHCD behave on disassortative or core–periphery networks? Can the model adapt by swapping priors or using a mixture over within/between types?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, fast, and well-motivated adaptation of rose-tree Bayesian clustering to relational data.
- Clean recursion enabling exact marginal likelihood under the restricted partition family.
- Practical speed with decent predictions on a standard benchmark; interpretable hierarchies.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental contribution with limited novelty over prior hierarchical clustering and network models.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow and lacks strong hierarchical baselines and sensitivity studies.
- Modeling assumptions (assortativity, greedy local scoring) are restrictive and insufficiently validated.
- Claims about speed/significance are not convincingly demonstrated across diverse settings.

Overall assessment
While the approach is neat and potentially useful for quick exploratory hierarchical community discovery, the novelty is limited and the validation is not at the standard I expect for NIPS. Stronger comparisons to hierarchical network models, synthetic recovery studies, and hyperparameter/sensitivity analyses are needed to substantiate the claims.
