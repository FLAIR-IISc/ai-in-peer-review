Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a hierarchy T over vertices; each node in the tree induces a set of tree-consistent partitions Φ(T). Given conjugate block models for within- and between-cluster edges (Beta-Bernoulli in the experiments), the marginal likelihood p(D|T) can be computed exactly via a dynamic program with recursion p(DTT|T) = πT f(σTT) + (1−πT) g(σ¬chTT) ∏C∈ch(T) p(DCC|C). The prior over partitions given the tree uses a stopping probability πS that depends on the branching factor (following Bayesian Rose Trees), encouraging non-binary splits when appropriate.

Inference proceeds via a bottom-up greedy merge procedure akin to Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (BHC; Heller & Ghahramani, Blundell et al.), but extended to relational data. At each step, the best merge among three possibilities (join two subtrees with a new parent; or absorb one as a child of the other) is chosen by a local Bayes factor that only uses data among the leaves in the candidate merged subtree, with cached sufficient statistics enabling O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) complexity. A sparse variant disallows merging trees with no edges between them. Predictive link probabilities are computed along a path in the tree via a mixture of on- and off-diagonal posteriors.

Experiments include qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network; predictive performance on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subgraph versus IRM (MCMC and greedy), with BHCD obtaining comparable AUC/accuracy to IRM much faster and roughly on par with LFRM but below ILA (using published folds); and a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS 1–17 network.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation marrying BHC/Rose Trees with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood; exact tree marginal likelihood via DP is elegant and useful.
- Practical, scalable inference with careful caching; O(n^2 log n) independent of unknown number of communities is attractive for medium/large graphs. Sparse variant is sensible and accelerates on sparse networks.
- Reasonable predictive performance with substantially lower wall-clock time than IRM; understandable trade-off versus more expressive models (e.g., ILA).
- The three-way merge move (join/absorb) is a nice extension that improves interpretability by allowing non-binary hierarchies.
- Predictive distribution derivation is clean and useful; responsibilities rS are interpretable.
- Paper situates itself with IRM, BHC/Rose Trees, and hierarchical IRM-like approaches.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- The model uses a single off-diagonal likelihood g(·) across all inter-cluster pairs (i.e., no θpq heterogeneity if p≠q). This favors assortative communities and may underfit disassortative or core–periphery patterns. Please discuss this limitation and consider a variant with multiple between-block parameters or depth-dependent parameters.
- Greedy local merges with a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12) can be myopic; no guarantees are provided. Restarts help, but the cost of R restarts is not consistently accounted for in timing plots. Clarify whether timing includes R=50 restarts, and provide sensitivity of results to R.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference is shown, though these strongly influence assortativity and branching. Please add sensitivity plots or simple empirical Bayes updates, and discuss γ’s effect on non-binarity and predictive performance.
- Evaluation is limited: comparisons are primarily to IRM; LFRM/ILA comparisons are indirect via published numbers. Including additional baselines (e.g., HRG-style models, modularity-based hierarchical clustering, or the hierarchical IRM of Herlau et al.) would strengthen claims. Synthetic experiments with known hierarchical ground truth would help assess structural recovery.
- The sparse variant forbids merges without inter-tree edges; while intuitive, it can preclude discovering hierarchies where communities are linked only through higher-level structure. Please justify and quantify the impact (ablation).
- The paper mentions “BHCD global” and stale heap scores; this is confusing. Clarify the difference among variants and ensure all compared methods are well-defined and fair.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and organized, with a clear derivation of the DP recursion and merge scoring. However:
  - Include Algorithm 1 explicitly in the paper, not just referenced, and detail the heap maintenance/update policy to avoid ambiguity about stale scores.
  - Define all symbols when they first appear (e.g., ancestorT(φ), subtreeT(φ)).
  - Figures are central; ensure they are legible and self-contained (captions should map tree operations to matrix blocks).
  - Explain handling of missing entries and signed edges (in Sampson) more explicitly.
  - State precisely whether results shown are for binary vs non-binary, sparse vs dense variants, and whether reported likelihoods are comparable across variants.

Originality
- The main novelty is importing Bayesian rose-tree ideas into relational data via a blockmodel likelihood, plus the absorb/join merge options and efficient caching. This is a meaningful but incremental contribution building on BHC/Rose Trees and SBMs/IRM. Related hierarchical graph models such as hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman 2008) and hierarchical IRM-like approaches could be discussed more thoroughly to position differences in modeling assumptions (e.g., BHCD’s exact partition sum over a tree vs. HRG’s likelihood and MCMC).
- The prediction procedure and DP marginalization are neat and practically useful.

Significance
- The method is simple, fast, and yields interpretable hierarchies and useful predictions; this combination is valuable for practitioners analyzing medium-sized networks.
- The scalability claim is credible and fills a gap between heuristic hierarchical clustering and fully Bayesian nonparametric models that do not scale well.

Questions for the authors
1. Do timing curves include all R restarts? How were ties broken and how sensitive are results to random seeds?
2. How does performance vary with γ? Does constraining to binary merges help or hurt across datasets?
3. Can you report results on a synthetic hierarchical SBM to quantify structure recovery and compare to hierarchical IRM/HRG?
4. Would allowing a small set of between-block parameters (e.g., per-parent-node off-diagonal parameter) improve fit without sacrificing speed?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, well-motivated probabilistic model with exact tree marginal likelihood.
- Scalable, practical algorithm; strong speed-accuracy trade-off.
- Clear extension of BHC to relational data; absorb/join options add value.
- Useful predictions and interpretable hierarchies on real datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical comparison and analysis; no synthetic recovery experiments.
- Modeling restrictions (single between-block parameter; assortativity bias).
- Greedy inference without guarantees; unclear accounting of restarts in timing.
- Some clarity issues and missing related work discussion (e.g., HRG).

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a practical and conceptually clean contribution with good empirical speed and reasonable accuracy. Addressing evaluation breadth, hyperparameter sensitivity, and clarity would strengthen it further. Confidence: medium.
