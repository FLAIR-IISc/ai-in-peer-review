### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller and Ghahramani (2005) and the "rose tree" model of Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data. The model is effectively a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where a tree defines a prior over a set of nested partitions. The marginal likelihood of the data given a tree can be computed exactly and efficiently via dynamic programming. Inference of the tree structure itself is performed using a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges sub-trees to maximize a local Bayes factor score. The primary contribution is an efficient Bayesian inference scheme for hierarchical community detection, with a claimed complexity of O(n^2 log n), which is demonstrated to be significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The main strength of the paper is the method's computational efficiency. The greedy agglomerative approach, combined with careful caching of sufficient statistics, leads to a practical algorithm that is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based competitors like the IRM. The O(n^2 log n) complexity, which is independent of the number of communities, is a significant advantage for analyzing larger networks.
2.  **Probabilistic Foundation:** Unlike many fast hierarchical clustering algorithms, BHCD is grounded in a coherent Bayesian framework. It provides a marginal likelihood for the entire hierarchy (p(D|T)), which allows for principled model selection and a well-defined probabilistic prediction mechanism for link prediction.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written. The model is developed logically, building from the standard stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical version. The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is clear and central to the method.
4.  **Novel Application of an Existing Framework:** The application of the Bayesian rose tree framework to community detection is a natural and valuable extension, bridging the gap between non-probabilistic hierarchical methods and slower, more complex Bayesian nonparametric models.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Methodological and Technical Imprecision:** The paper contains several minor but collectively troubling technical inaccuracies and unsubstantiated claims.
    *   **Formatting:** The footnote on the first page regarding the author's prior affiliation is non-standard for conference submissions and suggests a lack of attention to formatting guidelines.
    *   **Prediction Complexity:** Section 4.2 states the prediction complexity is Θ(n). This is imprecise. The complexity is linear in the *depth* of the tree, which is O(n) only in the pathological case of a chain-like hierarchy. For more balanced trees, it would be closer to O(log n). This distinction is important and should be stated correctly.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper claims that binary tree variants "tend to produce better predictive results as they average over a larger number of partitions." This is a strong and counter-intuitive claim that is not explained or empirically supported. How does restricting merges to be binary lead to averaging over more partitions? The model *always* averages over all partitions consistent with the inferred tree. Furthermore, in the Sampson experiment, the binary trees achieve a lower log-likelihood, which contradicts the notion that they provide a better model fit.
    *   **Superfluous Generalization:** The generalization to the exponential family in Equation (3) is introduced and then immediately abandoned. While not incorrect, it adds little value and could be removed for conciseness.

2.  **Inconsistent Experimental Results and Analysis:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating speed, raises more questions than it answers.
    *   **Contradictory Performance of BHCD-sparse:** In Section 4.1, the `sparse` variant is motivated as being more reasonable and is claimed to "empirically... produce better results." However, in the Sampson's Monastery experiment (Section 5), BHCD-sparse yields a log-likelihood of -22.80, which is dramatically worse than the -6.62 of the standard BHCD. This severe contradiction is not mentioned, let alone explained. How can a model with an exponentially lower marginal likelihood be considered better? This undermines the credibility of the `sparse` variant and the thoroughness of the analysis.
    *   **Confusing Algorithmic Artifact:** In the Sampson experiment analysis, the paper states "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap". This is alarming. An agglomerative algorithm using a priority queue should not have "out of date" scores if implemented correctly; stale entries should be ignored. This offhand comment suggests either an implementation bug or a poorly understood algorithmic behavior that warrants a much more detailed explanation.
    *   **Lack of Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The hyperparameters are fixed to values that "we found to work well." For a Bayesian model, this is insufficient. A sensitivity analysis is required to assess how the choice of α, β, δ, λ, and γ affects the resulting hierarchies and performance.

3.  **Limitations of the Greedy Approach:** While speed is the main selling point, this comes at the cost of a greedy inference procedure. The local nature of the Bayes factor score (Eq. 12) is a strong heuristic that is susceptible to local optima. The paper does not adequately discuss the implications of this, nor does it compare to less greedy search strategies. The use of R=50 restarts acknowledges the algorithm's stochasticity but also inflates the true runtime by a large constant factor (O(R * n^2 log n)), a detail that is understated.

### Justification for Recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important problem of scalable inference for hierarchical community detection.
*   The proposed method is extremely fast compared to relevant Bayesian baselines, which is a significant practical contribution.
*   The extension of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational data is novel in this context.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a number of minor flaws, including technical imprecision, unsubstantiated claims, and formatting issues, which together signal a lack of meticulousness.
*   The experimental analysis is weak and contains a major, unaddressed contradiction regarding the performance of the `sparse` variant.
*   The discussion of algorithmic details (e.g., "out of date scores") is confusing and raises concerns about the implementation's correctness or the authors' understanding of it.
*   The work, while useful, is a relatively direct application of existing methods ([13], [14]) to a new domain, making its conceptual originality somewhat limited.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising and computationally efficient approach to an important problem, the accumulation of minor technical flaws, unsubstantiated claims, and particularly the unaddressed contradictions in the experimental results prevent me from recommending acceptance. The work feels premature and lacks the scientific rigor and polish expected for a NIPS publication. The authors should address the inconsistencies in their results, provide justification for their claims, and be more precise in their technical descriptions before the work is ready for publication.
