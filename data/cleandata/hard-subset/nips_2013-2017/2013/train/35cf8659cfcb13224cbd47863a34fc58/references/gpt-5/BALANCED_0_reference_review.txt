Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian method to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels: a tree T constrains the set of admissible partitions, and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) can be computed exactly by dynamic programming via a recursive decomposition (Eq. 10). The prior over tree-consistent partitions uses a stopping probability πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| that biases toward non-binary (“rose”) trees, following Bayesian Rose Trees. Inference is an agglomerative model-selection scheme: at each step, two subtrees are merged (by join or absorption), choosing the merge with the largest Bayes factor that depends only on local sufficient statistics (Eq. 12). With careful caching, per-iteration work is O(1), yielding O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant disallows merges between disconnected components. Predictions for held-out edges are computed by a top-down recursion (Eq. 14).

The work builds on stochastic blockmodels and the IRM, and on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees for exchangeable i.i.d. data, extending these ideas to relational data. Related hierarchical relational models include hierarchical IRM variants, the Mondrian process with relational likelihoods, and two-level hierarchical block models. The paper’s emphasis is scalability and a simple likelihood with conjugate priors for within- vs. between-community edges.

Experiments include: qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery network; predictive evaluation on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset (BHCD vs. IRM using Gibbs and greedy search, with cross-validation folds from prior work); and qualitative structure on the full NIPS 1–17 graph (2,864 nodes). BHCD achieves useful predictions orders of magnitude faster than the IRM, with performance comparable to LFRM and below ILA (as reported in prior work), and reveals interpretable hierarchies.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated extension of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to networks via a blockmodel likelihood and tree-constrained partitions.
- Exact marginal likelihood for a given tree via DP; elegant recursion and clean use of sufficient statistics.
- Fast, simple greedy inference with local Bayes factors and effective caching; worst-case O(n^2 log n) independent of the number of communities.
- Practical sparse variation and multiple restart strategy; the method empirically finds interpretable hierarchies.
- Demonstrated substantial speed advantages over IRM while maintaining reasonable predictive accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited in breadth and depth: main baseline is IRM; comparisons to other scalable link-prediction baselines (e.g., LFRM, ILA, spectral/embedding methods) are indirect rather than side-by-side on identical folds and codebases.
- No sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference; α, β, δ, λ, γ are fixed without justification beyond empirical convenience, and the prior strongly encodes “assortative” structure.
- Model rigidity: all within-block probabilities share a common prior, as do all between-block probabilities; no hierarchical prior over θpp or θpq to capture heterogeneous densities across communities.
- Greedy search can be unstable; results depend on random tie-breaking with R restarts. No analysis of variability or uncertainty in the inferred hierarchies beyond picking the best run for trees and averaging for prediction.
- Some clarity issues: “BHCD global” appears in the experiments without prior definition; predictive complexity is described as both linear in depth and Θ(n); memory complexity and practical runtime/memory trade-offs are not discussed.
- Limited discussion of when forbidding merges of disconnected components (BHCD-sparse) is appropriate; in some settings with missing data or disassortative structure, this may be too restrictive.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The likelihood/prior factorization and dynamic programming are correct and well related to prior rose-tree work. The merge scoring and caching are carefully described (Eq. 13). However, evaluation would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer experimental protocols (including exact metrics and held-out schemes), and sensitivity to hyperparameters. The claim of “two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration” seems strong; reporting absolute times, hardware, and memory usage would make the claim more concrete.

Clarity
Generally well organized with precise notation. The recursive definitions and sufficient-statistics caching are clear. Noted issues:
- Define “BHCD global” or remove it.
- Fix predictive complexity: it is O(depth), not Θ(n) in general.
- Provide explicit pseudo-code for Algorithm 1 (here it’s referenced but not fully shown).
- Improve references (several incomplete), and briefly summarize competing hierarchical models’ differences.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is marrying rose-tree-style Bayesian hierarchical clustering with a blockmodel likelihood and devising a fast greedy search specialized to relational data. Related hierarchical IRM/Mondrian approaches exist but are more computationally intensive. The simple within/between conjugate setup is not novel per se, but the particular combination and efficient realization for networks is useful.

Significance
Practical significance is promising: a fast, simple, hierarchical community discovery method that scales beyond what many Bayesian nonparametric models can handle. The approach is likely to be a good baseline and exploration tool. The lack of stronger empirical comparisons and sensitivity analysis dampens the impact.

Constructive suggestions
- Add side-by-side comparisons with LFRM and ILA on NIPS-234 using the same splits and code/runtime reporting; include simple scalable baselines (e.g., spectral clustering, node2vec/embedding-based link prediction) for context.
- Report runtime, memory, and scaling curves (n vs. time) for BHCD, BHCD-sparse, and IRM on synthetic and real graphs.
- Provide hyperparameter sensitivity and/or empirical Bayes or hierarchical priors over α, β, δ, λ, and γ; consider learning γ or adapting πS.
- Analyze stability: variance across restarts, consensus trees, and uncertainty quantification for the hierarchy (e.g., posterior responsibilities rS).
- Consider community-specific parameters (e.g., θpp with node-specific or subtree-specific priors) to capture heterogeneous densities while maintaining tractability.
- Clarify BHCD-sparse’s applicability: discuss missing data and disassortative cases.
- Extend to directed/weighted graphs; discuss missing data handling formally.
- Fix clarity issues noted above and complete references. Release code.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Pros: elegant, fast method; clear modeling; promising qualitative results; useful practical tool.
Cons: limited empirical validation and baselines; fixed hyperparameters; stability/sensitivity not assessed; some clarity issues.

I believe the paper is close; with stronger experiments, sensitivity analyses, and clarified presentation, it could make a solid contribution.
