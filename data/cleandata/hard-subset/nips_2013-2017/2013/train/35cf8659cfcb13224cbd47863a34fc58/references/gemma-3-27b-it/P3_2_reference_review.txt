## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The approach builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels, offering a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate that BHCD can achieve comparable performance to IRM, but with significantly faster runtimes – two orders of magnitude faster for a single iteration – and a worst-case runtime independent of the number of communities.  The paper details the probabilistic model, the efficient inference scheme leveraging dynamic programming, and provides experimental results on Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship dataset, and the full NIPS network.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on community detection. It effectively contrasts BHCD with: (1) flat community detection methods (stochastic blockmodels, mixed membership stochastic blockmodels), (2) greedy hierarchical clustering (lacking probabilistic grounding), and (3) Bayesian nonparametric approaches (IRM, Mondrian process) which often suffer from computational scalability issues. The discussion of the trade-offs between conjugate priors for efficiency versus non-conjugate priors for potentially improved accuracy is relevant and well-stated. The citation of relevant works is generally comprehensive.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** This is the most significant strength. The authors convincingly demonstrate that BHCD provides a substantial speedup compared to existing Bayesian methods, particularly IRM. This is achieved through a clever caching scheme and a greedy, dynamic programming-based inference approach.  The claim of O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) worst-case runtime is a valuable contribution, as it addresses a key limitation of many Bayesian nonparametric models.
* **Probabilistic Framework:**  Unlike purely greedy hierarchical clustering, BHCD operates within a fully Bayesian framework, providing a probabilistic account of the discovered structure and enabling predictions. 
* **Clear Model Description:** The probabilistic model is presented clearly, building logically from the stochastic blockmodel and extending it to hierarchical structures.  The use of the tree structure to define a prior on partitions is a clever and efficient way to explore the space of possible community structures.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network demonstrate the qualitative ability of the method to recover known community structures. The comparison to IRM on the NIPS dataset provides quantitative evidence of its performance and speed advantage. The full NIPS experiment showcases scaling to larger datasets.
* **Variations and Sparse Optimization:** The introduction of BHCD sparse, specifically designed for sparse networks, is a practical improvement and acknowledges the common characteristics of real-world social networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors state they found parameters that worked well, the paper lacks a thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity (α, β, δ, λ, γ).  A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the results. How does performance change with different priors?
* **Limited Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:**  The primary comparison is against IRM. A comparison to faster, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., Leiden algorithm) would further highlight the trade-offs between computational cost, probabilistic modeling, and accuracy.
* **Predictive Performance:** While BHCD performs reasonably well in terms of predictive accuracy, it doesn't decisively outperform LFRM.  Given the computational advantage, further investigation into improving the predictive capabilities, perhaps through modifications to the likelihood function or prior specification, would be beneficial.
* **Tree Structure Exploration:** The greedy nature of the algorithm means it might get stuck in local optima in the space of tree structures. While the restarts help, exploring alternative search strategies (e.g., simulated annealing) to improve tree exploration could be considered.
* **Interpretation of Larger Hierarchies:**  The visualization of the full NIPS hierarchy, while illustrating scalability, is challenging to interpret.  More sophisticated methods for visualizing and summarizing large hierarchical structures would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Offers a significant speed improvement over existing Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods.
* Presents a well-defined probabilistic model grounded in sound statistical principles.
* Demonstrates reasonable performance on both qualitative and quantitative benchmarks.
* Addresses a computationally challenging problem with a novel and efficient solution.
* The inclusion of BHCD sparse shows a focus on practical considerations for real-world data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of a comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Limited comparison to non-Bayesian methods.
* Predictive performance is good, but not state-of-the-art.
* The greedy nature of the algorithm may limit the exploration of the solution space.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of community detection. The computational efficiency of BHCD is a significant advantage, making it a valuable tool for analyzing large-scale social networks. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe it meets the criteria for acceptance to NIPS. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept**
