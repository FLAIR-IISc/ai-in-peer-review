## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The authors build upon existing Bayesian approaches to hierarchical clustering, leveraging stochastic blockmodels and a tree-based prior on partitions to efficiently infer community structure. They demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable performance, particularly on larger datasets.  A sparse version of BHCD, optimized for sparse graphs, is also introduced.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of community detection techniques. It acknowledges the limitations of flat community models (stochastic blockmodels, IRM) and the computational cost of existing Bayesian hierarchical approaches. It builds upon previous work in greedy hierarchical clustering and Bayesian approaches like those in Heller & Ghahramani (2009) and Blundell et al. (2013), extending them to relational data. The comparisons to IRM and LFRM highlight the method's competitive position regarding both speed and accuracy.

**Strengths:**

* **Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work is its computational efficiency. The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity, coupled with the caching scheme, positions BHCD as a viable option for larger networks where traditional Bayesian methods struggle. The empirical results (Table 1, Figure 4) convincingly demonstrate a substantial speed advantage over IRM.
* **Sound Methodology:** The approach of using a tree-based prior on partitions coupled with efficient dynamic programming for marginal likelihood computation is well-motivated and appears theoretically sound. The authors clearly explain the recursive formulation for calculating the likelihood.
* **Practical Improvements (Sparse BHCD):** Recognizing the prevalence of sparse networks, the introduction of the "sparse" variant is a practical and effective optimization, further enhancing the method’s applicability.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network provide qualitative validation, demonstrating that the method recovers known community structure. The NIPS co-authorship network experiments provide quantitative comparison to relevant baselines (IRM, LFRM).
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The equations are clearly presented and explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors state the hyperparameters they used, a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these parameters is missing. It's unclear how robust the performance is to different choices of α, β, δ, λ, and γ.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to IRM and LFRM is valuable, a broader comparison to other scalable community detection algorithms (e.g., Louvain, Leiden) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s performance.
* **Predictive Performance Detail:** While the results on predictive performance (Figure 4) are good, the nature of the held-out edges isn’t described.  Knowing the density of the held-out edges would provide more context.
* **Complexity Analysis Caveat:** The authors note that while inference is O(n<sup>2</sup> log n), the likelihood calculation is O(n+m). This discrepancy isn’t fully explored, and it could be a bottleneck for extremely sparse networks.
* **Hierarchical Interpretation:** The full NIPS hierarchy is acknowledged as large, but the process of summarizing/filtering it for visualization (rT condition) could benefit from more justification.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness. The approach is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis appears correct. The empirical results support the claims made. However, the lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis weakens this assessment.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is clear.
* **Originality:** The combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering with the efficient inference scheme for relational data is novel and represents a valuable contribution.  The sparse BHCD variant is a clever and practical adaptation.
* **Significance:** This work addresses the challenge of scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection. The demonstrated efficiency and competitive performance suggest that BHCD could be a useful tool for researchers and practitioners working with large network datasets. The focus on scalability is particularly significant.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant speedup compared to existing Bayesian methods.
* Sound theoretical foundation.
* Practical improvement with the sparse variant.
* Positive empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.

**Con:**

* Lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Limited comparison to non-Bayesian scalable algorithms.
* Missing details on the held-out edge characteristics in predictive performance.
* Potential bottleneck for extremely sparse networks (discrepancy between likelihood and inference complexity).




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS. The method presented is a significant contribution to the field of community detection, particularly regarding scalability.  While some minor weaknesses exist (primarily regarding sensitivity analysis and broader comparison), the strengths of the work – its efficiency, sound methodology, and practical improvements – outweigh these concerns. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and addresses a relevant and important problem.  The authors have made a convincing case for the value of their approach.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would suggest the authors address the hyperparameter sensitivity issue and consider a broader comparison to other scalable algorithms in a revised version of the paper.
