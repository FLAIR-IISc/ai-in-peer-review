This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The approach builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering and aims to provide a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate the method on Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, showing speed advantages over IRM.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its computational efficiency. The authors correctly identify a key bottleneck in existing Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection – the slow convergence and unfavorable scaling with network size. BHCD’s claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity, combined with the observed two orders of magnitude speedup compared to a single IRM iteration, is a significant contribution, particularly for larger networks. The caching scheme to achieve O(1) inner loop computation is a clever optimization. The inclusion of a "sparse" variant, tailored for sparse networks, further enhances practicality. 

However, my concerns outweigh the positives. While speed is valuable, it shouldn't come at the expense of thorough methodological rigor and compelling evidence of superior performance beyond simply being faster. The presented experimental evaluation feels incomplete and lacks sufficient justification for the design choices.

Firstly, the reliance on a single dataset (NIPS co-authorship) for demonstrating performance against IRM is insufficient. While the results are promising, they don't establish generalizability. A broader range of benchmark datasets, encompassing different network structures and sizes, is crucial. The chosen dataset itself is a subset of the full NIPS dataset, which raises questions about whether the observed speedups translate to the complete graph.

Secondly, the evaluation metric – log predictive probability – is not thoroughly justified. The paper mentions AUC and accuracy, but these are not central to the reported results. Why prioritize log predictive probability in this context, and are there alternative metrics that might provide a more comprehensive assessment of community quality?

Thirdly, the sensitivity analysis is weak. The hyperparameters are set without a clear rationale and are not varied to assess their impact on performance. The choice of α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4 appears arbitrary, and it's unclear how robust the results are to different settings.

Fourthly, the comparison to IRM feels somewhat superficial. While the speed advantage is highlighted, a more detailed comparison of the *quality* of the inferred community structures is needed. Are the communities identified by BHCD qualitatively comparable to those found by IRM? Is there a systematic bias in either method? The paper lacks a careful, side-by-side evaluation of the resulting partitions. The qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery network is a good start, but needs similar rigour when dealing with larger datasets.

Fifthly, the theoretical contributions feel incremental. Extending existing Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data is a natural progression, but the paper doesn't present fundamentally new theoretical insights. The connection to the Mondrian process and other related work is acknowledged but not deeply explored.

Finally, the code is not available, hindering reproducibility and further evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Computational efficiency compared to existing Bayesian methods.
*   Potential for scalability to larger networks.
*   Clear presentation of the method and its implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental validation beyond a single dataset.
*   Lack of thorough hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis.
*   Superficial comparison to IRM and other relevant methods.
*   Incremental theoretical contributions.
*   Lack of publicly available code.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a technically sound but ultimately underwhelming contribution. While the speed improvements are notable, they are not sufficient to justify acceptance to NIPS without a more comprehensive and convincing evaluation. The paper feels premature – it presents a promising approach but lacks the empirical support and rigorous analysis required for a high-impact publication. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in this review and resubmit a revised version with a more thorough evaluation and a stronger justification for its claims. The potential is there, but it hasn’t been fully realized.
