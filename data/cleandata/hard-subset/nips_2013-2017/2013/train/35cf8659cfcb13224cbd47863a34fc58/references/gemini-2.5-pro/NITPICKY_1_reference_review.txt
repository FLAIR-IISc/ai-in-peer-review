**Summary**

This paper proposes a greedy agglomerative method for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery, termed BHCD. The approach builds upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] by adapting the framework to relational data. The model uses a tree to define a prior over partitions consistent with that tree, with a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) likelihood for the graph edges. The tree structure is learned via a bottom-up greedy procedure where pairs of subtrees are merged at each step based on the locally optimal Bayes factor. The authors claim a time complexity of O(n² log n), which is independent of the number of communities, and demonstrate experimentally that their method achieves comparable predictive performance to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) but is orders of magnitude faster.

**Critique**

The paper addresses the important problem of scalable inference for hierarchical network models. The proposed greedy approach offers a compelling alternative to computationally intensive MCMC methods. However, the manuscript suffers from a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its technical exposition, experimental evaluation, and overall presentation, which collectively undermine the contribution.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary contribution is a significant speed-up over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection. The greedy algorithm avoids the slow mixing and convergence issues of MCMC, and its O(n² log n) complexity is advantageous, particularly its independence from the number of communities K. The experimental comparison against IRM convincingly illustrates this practical benefit.
2.  **Model Formulation:** The adaptation of the Bayesian Rose Tree framework [14] to the SBM likelihood is elegant. The use of dynamic programming to compute the marginal likelihood of a tree (Eq. 10) is a key component that enables the efficient greedy search.
3.  **Problem Relevance:** Scalable analysis of large networks is a core topic of interest for the NIPS community. This work provides a pragmatic approach that could be useful for practitioners needing a fast, "good-enough" hierarchical clustering of a network.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The manuscript is marred by formatting inconsistencies and a general lack of polish. The bibliography is incomplete for numerous key citations (e.g., [1], [2], [5], [7], [20]), with missing years, venues, or author information. This is a serious oversight.
    *   A footnote on the first page (`∗Part of the work was done whilst at the Gatsby Unit...`) violates the principles of double-blind review.
    *   The exposition is overly dense in places. For example, the generalization to exponential families in Eq. (3) is mentioned but immediately dropped, adding little value.
    *   The description of the model and algorithm is difficult to follow without the referenced figures (Figures 1-5 were not available in the submitted PDF). This is a critical flaw that renders large parts of Sections 3, 4, and 5 incomprehensible and unverifiable.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Details:**
    *   The complexity analysis appears to contain inaccuracies. The claim that the inner loop of Algorithm 1 takes O(1) time seems incorrect. After merging trees X and Y into I, new potential merges between I and all other trees J in the forest F must be computed and added to the heap. This requires iterating over O(|F|) = O(n) trees, making the cost of a single merge step O(n log n). While the overall O(n² log n) complexity may still hold, the justification provided is flawed.
    *   In Section 4.2, the predictive complexity is stated as Θ(n). This is the worst-case complexity for a degenerate tree; for a reasonably balanced tree, it should be proportional to the tree depth, i.e., O(log n). This lack of precision is concerning.
    *   The locality assumption underpinning the greedy Bayes factor score (Eq. 12) is very strong and is a major simplification. The paper fails to discuss the potential failure modes of this assumption, where non-local information might be crucial for determining the correct community structure.

3.  **Originality:** The conceptual novelty is somewhat limited. The core modeling idea is a direct application of the framework from Blundell et al. [14] to a standard beta-Bernoulli SBM likelihood. The main contribution is therefore more of an engineering one—demonstrating that this combination is effective and fast for network data—rather than a fundamentally new probabilistic modeling principle.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   Hyperparameters are set to fixed values that "work well," without any justification, sensitivity analysis, or discussion of how a user might set them for a new dataset.
    *   The discussion of the results on the Sampson monastery network is confusing. Section 4.1 claims the "sparse" variant "produces better results," yet the log-likelihoods reported in Section 5 show the standard BHCD achieves a dramatically better score (-6.62 vs. -22.80). The authors must clarify this contradiction and define what "better" means (e.g., predictive accuracy vs. marginal likelihood).
    *   The algorithm relies on random tie-breaking and requires a large number of restarts (R=50) to find a good solution. This suggests that the greedy search landscape is highly multimodal and that a single run may yield a poor local optimum. This procedural instability is a significant practical weakness that is not adequately discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a very fast algorithm for a popular and important problem.
*   The empirical speed-ups are impressive and could enable the analysis of much larger networks than is feasible with sampling-based Bayesian methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is sloppily prepared, with an incomplete bibliography and formatting errors (including a de-anonymizing footnote).
*   There are technical inaccuracies in the complexity analysis.
*   The experimental reporting is confusing and lacks rigor regarding hyperparameter selection and evaluation metrics.
*   The reliance on a strong, undiscussed locality assumption and the need for many random restarts point to weaknesses in the core greedy method.

**Decision:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper's goal of a fast Bayesian method for community detection is laudable and the empirical speed is impressive, the manuscript in its current form falls well below the standards for NIPS. The combination of poor presentation, technical inaccuracies, and lack of experimental rigor makes it impossible to confidently assess the method's value. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous flaws identified.
