Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a scalable Bayesian approach to learning hierarchical community structure in networks. The model views a hierarchy (tree) as defining a set of tree-consistent partitions and assigns a prior over these partitions via a simple generative process with stop probabilities πS. Conditioned on the tree, the marginal likelihood of the adjacency matrix under a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors can be computed exactly via a dynamic program (Eq. 10). Building on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees, the authors introduce an agglomerative, greedy model selection procedure that merges trees using three operations (join and two absorptions), with careful caching of sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) inner-loop updates, yielding O(n^2 log n) total time. A sparse variant forbids merging disconnected components. The method provides a simple predictive recursion for link prediction by traversing the tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS co-authorship (a 234-node subset and the full graph) show coherent hierarchies and predictive performance comparable to LFRM (via published numbers), worse than ILA, and better than IRM, while being substantially faster.

Relation to prior work
- Extends stochastic blockmodels and IRM-style nonparametrics to hierarchical structure, but not via MCMC over partitions: instead, uses a tree-constrained partition prior (as in Bayesian rose trees) to enable exact marginal likelihood for a fixed tree and a greedy agglomerative search (as in BHC).
- Prior hierarchical network work includes IRM with trees (Herlau et al.), depth-limited hierarchies (Koutsourelakis & Eliassi-Rad; Palla et al.), and Mondrian process likelihoods. Compared to these, BHCD trades some modeling richness for a much more scalable greedy procedure.
- Distinct from modularity-based hierarchical clustering (e.g., Girvan–Newman, Clauset–Newman–Moore) by providing a probabilistic model and exact marginal for a given tree.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and appealing: a tree defines a distribution over partitions; exact marginal likelihood via dynamic programming; predictive probabilities via a simple recursion.
- Algorithmic contribution: three-way merges with a caching scheme enabling O(1) updates per candidate; overall O(n^2 log n), independent of number of communities, and a sparse variant for faster practice.
- Interpretability: hierarchical structure over communities; the visualization on NIPS co-authorship is persuasive.
- Practicality: very fast; appears to deliver reasonable predictions far sooner than IRM.
- Clear derivation and useful connections to BHC/Bayesian rose trees adapted to relational data.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited. The NIPS-234 comparison to LFRM and ILA is indirect (re-using splits but not re-running baselines), making fairness and variance unclear. No error bars. No degree-corrected baselines or modern scalable hierarchical SBMs.
- The model is a standard (non-degree-corrected) SBM with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors; degree heterogeneity is unmodeled, which often limits performance on real networks.
- The greedy search is heuristic; no guarantees or analysis of sensitivity to restarts, tie-breaking, or γ. While restarts are used, the impact on final quality is not quantified.
- Some practical choices are ad hoc (e.g., prohibiting merges with zero edges, selection criteria for cutting the tree), with limited justification/ablation.
- Scope limitations: undirected, no self-edges; no exploration of directed or weighted networks; no hyperparameter inference.
- Clarity/Completeness: minor typos and incomplete references; Algorithm 1 is referred to but not fully specified in the text; some complexity claims (e.g., O(n+m) for likelihood) could use a more explicit argument.

Quality
Technically sound in spirit: the recursion for the marginal likelihood given a tree is well motivated, and the adaptation of BHC/rose-tree machinery to block models is reasonable. The caching and merge scoring are plausible and well explained. However, the empirical section should be strengthened with direct, apples-to-apples comparisons and sensitivity analyses. Modeling limitations (no degree correction, conjugacy constraints) are acknowledged but not explored.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a clear flow from model to inference to experiments. A few derivations could be expanded (proof sketch of Eq. 10), and the algorithmic details (priority queue, invalidation of merges, complexity constants) could be made more explicit. References need completion.

Originality
Moderate but meaningful. The paper synthesizes ideas from BHC/rose trees and SBMs to yield a new, efficient approach to hierarchical community discovery with exact marginals for a fixed tree. While each component has precedent, the combination for relational data and the specific agglomerative machinery (three merge types plus caching) seem novel and practically impactful.

Significance
High practical significance for scalable, interpretable hierarchical community discovery. The method offers a compelling speed/quality trade-off that many practitioners would value. Scientifically, it opens a path to probabilistic hierarchical network models that are usable at scale.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include direct comparisons to LFRM, ILA, degree-corrected SBM, and a fast modularity-based hierarchical method on shared splits with error bars.
- Add sensitivity to γ and hyperparameters; explore basic hyperparameter learning.
- Consider a degree-corrected likelihood variant; discuss how the DP and caching extend.
- Provide more details on Algorithm 1 and the O(n+m) DP derivation.
- Explore directed/weighted variants and show at least one such dataset.
- Release code to support reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a fast, elegant Bayesian framework for hierarchical communities with exact tree-marginals and scalable greedy inference.
- Strong practical relevance; interpretable outputs; significant speed gains over IRM with competitive accuracy.
- Clear methodological contribution connecting BHC/rose trees with SBMs in a novel way.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and partially indirect empirical validation; missing strong modern baselines and degree correction.
- Greedy nature without theoretical guarantees; limited analysis of robustness to restarts/hyperparameters.
- Some modeling restrictions (conjugate, undirected) and ad hoc heuristics.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite empirical limitations, the paper offers a fresh, scalable Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery that is likely to spur follow-up work and be useful in practice. The novelty and direction outweigh the incomplete validation.
