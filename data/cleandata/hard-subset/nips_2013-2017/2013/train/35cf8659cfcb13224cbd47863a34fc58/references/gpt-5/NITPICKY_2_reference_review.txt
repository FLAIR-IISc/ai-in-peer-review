Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes BHCD, a greedy Bayesian procedure for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model places a tree T over vertices; each internal node represents a community, and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) sums over all partitions consistent with T. The likelihood is a blockmodel with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli on within- and between-community edges, with distinct on-/off-diagonal marginals f and g. A dynamic program computes p(D|T) via a recursion, and an agglomerative “Bayesian Rose Tree”-style search (join/absorb merges) greedily maximizes a local Bayes factor score. A “sparse” variant forbids merges between disjoint components. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS coauthorship data suggest that BHCD yields interpretable hierarchies and competitive link prediction faster than the IRM, with qualitative results compared to IRM, LFRM, and ILA.

This work closely builds on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010) by adopting their tree prior and dynamic programming to a relational block likelihood. It is also related to hierarchical IRM extensions (e.g., Herlau et al. 2011), the Mondrian process for relational data (Roy & Teh 2009), and to nested/ hierarchical SBMs (e.g., Peixoto 2014), as well as nonparametric latent feature models (Miller et al. 2009) and ILA (Palla et al. 2012).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual extension: combining a tree-structured prior over partitions with a conjugate blockmodel and greedy agglomeration is natural and potentially useful.
- Efficient inference by local scoring and cached sufficient statistics; the independence of the worst-case time from the number of communities is attractive.
- Practical variants (sparse, binary) and the use of restarts are sensible.
- Empirical speed advantages versus the IRM code on coauthorship data are plausible and practically relevant.
- The predictive recursion is straightforward and potentially efficient (linear in tree depth).

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Hyperparameters contradict stated modeling intent. The paper requires α>β for within and δ<λ for between (to encode higher within density). However, experiments use α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, which implies the same Beta(1,0.2) for both within and between, and also violates δ<λ (1.0<0.2 is false). This undermines both the stated prior and interpretability. Sensitivity analysis is absent.
- Complexity claims are inconsistent and insufficiently justified. The text claims p(D|T) can be computed in O(n+m), but for nonbinary nodes computing σ¬ch may require O(|ch(S)|^2) across internal nodes, potentially O(n^2). The greedy algorithm is claimed O(n^2 log n) but earlier the introduction states “quadratic and independent of the number of communities”; elsewhere O(n^2 log n R) is quoted. Memory complexity (caching σIJ for all I,J) is not analyzed and could be O(n^2).
- The merge score (Eq. 12) assumes locality; the derivation that the forest likelihood factorizes to yield this exact Bayes factor given the current forest should be shown. It likely holds due to the model’s factorization but is not proved.
- Predictive complexity is stated as “linear in depth,” but later says “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n).” These are contradictory; it should be O(depth(T)).
- Several definitions are imprecise: σ{x}{x}=0 is odd notation; better to say no self-edges so n1pp excludes diagonal. The definition of Φ(T) in Eq. (5) is terse; a small example would help.
- “Algorithm 1” is referenced, but the pseudocode is not provided here; the presentation relies on it.
- The “BHCD global” variant is referenced in the Sampson discussion but never defined.

Experimental methodology
- Baselines and fairness: The IRM comparisons (MCMC and greedy) lack detail (number of steps, burn-in, hyperparameters, initialization, hardware, stopping criteria). Comparing “one BHCD run” to “one IRM Gibbs step” is not an apples-to-apples comparison; a fair wall-clock comparison at matched time budgets and convergence diagnostics is needed.
- Evaluation metrics and splits: The held-out protocol (edge fraction, stratification, negative sampling, missingness handling) is not specified. Accuracy on sparse graphs is misleading; AUROC is reported but AUPRC would be more informative.
- No synthetic data where ground-truth hierarchies and depths are controlled to test recovery and scaling; this is standard for hierarchical community discovery.
- Competing hierarchical models (hierarchical IRM variants, Mondrian process relational likelihood, nested SBM) are not empirically compared despite being highly relevant.
- The “sparse” variant forbids merges without edges, which may be a strong bias when absent edges are informative; this should be discussed and quantified.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed without justification; no sensitivity analysis.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos/grammar issues (e.g., “The Mondrian process [12] propose”, “wherem n2”, repeated periods, inconsistent spacing “leaves(T )”). Several claims are overstated or incorrect (e.g., “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” in the abstract/introduction cannot be literally true).
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years/venues/pages; author misspellings such as “Erix”; dangling commas; URLs with spaces). Many citations to recent and highly relevant hierarchical community detection works are missing (e.g., Peixoto, Phys. Rev. X 2014).
- Figures are critical to understanding but not sufficiently described in text; quantitative results are minimally summarized.

Quality
- The model is a reasonable and sound combination, but the hyperparameter mismatch, unsubstantiated complexity claims, and incomplete experimental protocol weaken technical rigor.

Clarity
- The paper is readable, but definitions, algorithmic details, and presentation need tightening; many formatting issues.

Originality
- Moderate. The main novelty is applying Bayesian Rose Tree-style DP and greedy merges to a block relational likelihood. Closely related ideas exist in hierarchical IRM and nested SBM; differences and advantages need to be articulated more clearly.

Significance
- Practical speed is promising, but without stronger baselines, clearer correctness, and better experiments, the impact is limited.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Fix hyperparameters to reflect the stated prior (δ<λ) and report sensitivity to (α,β,δ,λ,γ).
- Provide a rigorous complexity and memory analysis, including the cost of computing σ¬ch at nonbinary nodes.
- Clarify and include Algorithm 1; define all variants including “BHCD global.”
- Provide fair, time-matched comparisons with well-tuned IRM, and add comparisons to hierarchical IRM/Mondrian/nested SBM. Include synthetic recovery experiments.
- Specify the held-out protocol; report AUPRC; analyze robustness across restarts R.
- Discuss handling of missing data vs observed zeros.
- Clean up writing and references; correct incorrect claims (e.g., “single iteration of IRM”).

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data.
- Efficient greedy inference with caching; practical variants.
- Promising speed in practice; interpretable hierarchies on real data.

Cons
- Hyperparameter inconsistency; several technical and clarity issues.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair experimental evaluation.
- Missing comparisons to the most relevant hierarchical models; limited novelty.
- Numerous presentation and reference problems.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is sensible and potentially useful, the accumulation of technical inconsistencies, incomplete methodology, and presentation issues outweighs the contributions in the current form. With corrected hyperparameters, rigorous analysis, stronger baselines, and improved clarity, this work could be much stronger.
