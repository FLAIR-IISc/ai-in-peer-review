

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches, extending them to relational data using a tree-based prior on partitions. BHCD efficiently computes the marginal likelihood through dynamic programming, achieving O(n² log n) time complexity, which the authors claim is two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on community detection by addressing a key limitation of previous Bayesian hierarchical methods: computational inefficiency. The authors thoroughly contextualize their work by comparing with relevant prior approaches, including the IRM, Mondrian process, and hierarchical clustering methods, demonstrating how BHCD fills a gap in the literature for scalable Bayesian hierarchical community discovery.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-motivated probabilistic model that correctly extends stochastic blockmodels to hierarchical structures. The derivation of the marginal likelihood using dynamic programming is mathematically sound, and the time complexity analysis is thorough and convincing.

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm achieves a significant speed advantage over existing Bayesian methods, as demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks. The authors clearly articulate why BHCD is faster (e.g., fixed number of iterations, no dependence on number of communities).

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data provide strong evidence for BHCD's effectiveness. The comparison with IRM (a state-of-the-art Bayesian method) is particularly compelling, showing BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance in a fraction of the time.

4. **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient mathematical detail for replication. The algorithm pseudocode and explanations of key concepts (e.g., tree-consistent partitions) are particularly accessible.

5. **Practical Considerations:** The authors thoughtfully address implementation details and provide useful variants (BHCD sparse, binary versions) that improve performance on real-world networks. The discussion of limitations and future work is honest and constructive.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the paper compares BHCD to Bayesian methods, it doesn't adequately benchmark against non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches (e.g., greedy hierarchical clustering) that are commonly used in practice. This makes it difficult to assess whether the speed advantage comes at a meaningful cost in terms of predictive quality.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Variants:** The paper introduces several variants (sparse, binary) but doesn't provide a thorough comparative analysis of when each variant is most appropriate. For instance, it's unclear when the binary restriction would be beneficial or detrimental to the hierarchical structure discovered.

3. **Limited Scalability Analysis:** The paper demonstrates BHCD's performance on moderate-sized networks (NIPS-234 with 234 nodes, full NIPS with 2864 nodes), but doesn't explore how it scales to extremely large networks (e.g., millions of nodes), which is a common scenario in network analysis.

4. **Ambiguous Interpretation of Hierarchical Structure:** While the paper demonstrates BHCD can discover hierarchical structure, it doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how well the discovered hierarchies match domain knowledge or how they compare to human-annotated hierarchies.

5. **Theoretical Limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the theoretical guarantees of BHCD, such as whether the greedy approach can find the globally optimal tree or how sensitive the results are to hyperparameter choices beyond the ones tested.

**Recommendation and Suggestions:**

This is a strong technical contribution that addresses an important problem in network analysis. While the paper is well-written and the method is clearly novel and useful, the current version has some limitations that would strengthen the contribution.

**Suggested improvements:**
1. Add a comparison with non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods as a baseline for speed/accuracy trade-offs.
2. Include a more thorough analysis of the different BHCD variants, with specific guidance on when each should be used.
3. Provide more detailed scalability analysis on larger networks (e.g., using synthetic data with increasing size).
4. Include more qualitative analysis of the hierarchical structures discovered, comparing them to domain knowledge or human annotations.
5. Consider adding a brief discussion of theoretical properties, such as how the greedy approach affects the quality of the discovered hierarchy.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of community detection, particularly for applications requiring computational efficiency. The authors have successfully demonstrated that their approach achieves a compelling balance between speed and accuracy. With the suggested improvements, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**
Quality: 8/10 (Strong technical contribution with sound methods, but limited theoretical analysis)
Clarity: 9/10 (Well-organized and clearly written)
Originality: 8/10 (Novel approach to Bayesian hierarchical community discovery)
Significance: 8/10 (Addresses a practical problem with meaningful computational advantages)

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above)
