## Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The authors build upon existing Bayesian approaches to hierarchical clustering, offering a method that is significantly faster than alternatives like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining competitive performance. The core idea is to model the network as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, leveraging a tree structure to define a prior on partitions and enabling efficient computation of the marginal likelihood. The authors demonstrate the method on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS network, showcasing its speed and accuracy in both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the landscape of community detection and Bayesian hierarchical modeling. They acknowledge the limitations of flat community models like the Stochastic Blockmodel and the Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel, as well as the computational challenges of fully Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM and Mondrian process. BHCD distinguishes itself by offering a practical balance between Bayesian rigor and computational efficiency, addressing a key limitation of existing methods.  The work builds directly upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering [13, 14], extending them to relational data. The comparison to LFRM [18] and ILA [10] is pertinent and highlights the trade-offs between model complexity and runtime.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** This is the primary strength of the paper. The authors convincingly demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster than IRM, particularly on larger datasets. The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity and the efficient caching scheme are notable contributions.
* **Bayesian Framework:** Maintaining a Bayesian framework provides a principled approach to community discovery, offering a probabilistic account of the data and discovered structure.
* **Hierarchical Structure:** The method naturally captures hierarchical community organization, a common feature of real-world networks.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, model formulation, and inference procedure. The figures are helpful in illustrating the tree structure and community assignments.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on three datasets – a small, well-understood network (Sampson), a medium-sized benchmark (NIPS subset), and a large-scale network (full NIPS) – provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
* **Practical Improvements (BHCD Sparse):** The introduction of the "sparse" variant, which avoids computations on non-existent edges, further enhances the method’s practicality for sparse networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). A more thorough investigation of the impact of these parameters on the inferred community structure would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** While the comparison to IRM is valuable, a comparison to simpler, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., those based on modularity optimization) would provide further context for the method’s performance.
* **Tie-Breaking:** The random tie-breaking mechanism during the merge step could introduce variability in the results.  While the authors address this with restarts, a more systematic exploration of tie-breaking strategies might be beneficial.
* **Scalability Beyond Density:** While the O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is good, it still presents a limitation for extremely large, dense networks.  The authors acknowledge this, but future work could explore strategies for scaling to even larger graphs.
* **Predictive Performance:** While competitive, the predictive performance on the NIPS dataset doesn’t convincingly surpass other methods. More detailed analysis, including statistical significance testing, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery, addressing a significant challenge in network analysis.
* The method demonstrates promising empirical results on multiple datasets, showcasing its accuracy and scalability.
* The clear writing and well-organized presentation make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The authors identify limitations and suggest avenues for future work, indicating a thoughtful and realistic assessment of their contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and comparison to non-Bayesian methods weakens the empirical evaluation.
* The reliance on random tie-breaking could introduce variability in the results.
* The predictive performance, while competitive, doesn't decisively outperform existing methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis.  The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The primary strength of the paper is its computational efficiency, which addresses a major limitation of existing Bayesian approaches. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10.
