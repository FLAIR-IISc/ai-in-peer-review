### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The work is motivated by the limitations of existing approaches: flat community models like the stochastic blockmodel require a predefined number of communities, while existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical structures (e.g., extensions of the Infinite Relational Model or IRM) suffer from high computational cost and slow convergence, limiting their applicability to large networks.

The proposed method frames the problem as one of Bayesian model selection. It defines a probabilistic model over hierarchical partitions (represented as trees) and computes the marginal likelihood of the data given a specific tree structure, p(D|T). This marginal likelihood is calculated efficiently via a dynamic programming recursion by summing over all partitions consistent with the tree. The core of the inference algorithm is a greedy agglomerative procedure that builds the hierarchy bottom-up. Starting with each node as its own community, the algorithm iteratively merges pairs of communities that result in the largest increase in the local Bayes factor, until a single tree containing all nodes is formed. The authors demonstrate that this approach is significantly faster than competing methods, with a time complexity of O(n² log n), and achieves comparable or superior predictive performance on link prediction tasks.

### **Strengths**

*   **Significance and Scalability:** The paper addresses a crucial and highly relevant problem: scalable inference for hierarchical network structure. The primary contribution is a method that is orders of magnitude faster than a key Bayesian nonparametric competitor (IRM). The O(n² log n) complexity, which is independent of the number of inferred communities, makes Bayesian hierarchical analysis practical for networks of a size that would be prohibitive for MCMC-based methods. This represents a significant practical advancement.
*   **Technical Quality and Elegance:** The proposed method is a technically sound and elegant combination of ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2010) and the stochastic blockmodel framework for relational data. The formulation of the marginal likelihood of a tree via the dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) is a key insight that makes the approach tractable. The careful caching of sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) computation in the inner loop of the greedy search is a well-executed piece of algorithmic engineering.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, and the model is built up logically from the familiar stochastic blockmodel. The figures, particularly Figure 1 illustrating tree-consistent partitions and Figure 2 showing merge operations, are effective aids to understanding. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the experimental setup and results are well-explained.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the method's claims. The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network are sensible and interpretable. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS co-authorship dataset is particularly strong, demonstrating that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance (in terms of log probability, accuracy, and AUC) to the IRM in a fraction of the time (seconds vs. thousands of seconds). This highlights the excellent speed-accuracy trade-off offered by the method.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Greedy Inference:** The main limitation of the proposed method is its greedy nature. The agglomerative search is deterministic (up to tie-breaking) and susceptible to finding poor local optima. The quality of the final hierarchy depends heavily on the sequence of local, myopic merge decisions. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple random restarts, the fundamental issue of a limited search over the vast space of hierarchies remains. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of this limitation and potential failure modes.
*   **Originality as a Combination of Existing Ideas:** While the application and specific formulation are novel, the core components—greedy Bayesian agglomerative clustering and stochastic blockmodels—are established techniques. The contribution lies in the successful synthesis and extension of these ideas to a new domain, rather than a completely new theoretical concept. The paper is transparent about this, but it is worth noting that the novelty is primarily in combination and application.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are set to fixed values that "work well". A more thorough evaluation would include a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate how robust the method is to the choice of these parameters. This would provide valuable guidance for practitioners seeking to apply the model to new datasets.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Discussion on Greedy Search Limitations:** In the discussion section, it would be valuable to add a paragraph acknowledging the limitations of greedy search more directly. For example, are there specific network topologies (e.g., those with significant overlap or ambiguous structure) where this greedy approach is likely to fail compared to an MCMC method that can escape local optima?
2.  **Expanded Experimental Comparison:** The comparison to IRM is excellent for demonstrating the speed advantage. To better position the work, a comparison against a fast, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithm (e.g., Clauset et al., 2004) would be informative. This would help disentangle the performance gains from the Bayesian model structure versus those from the agglomerative algorithm structure itself.
3.  **Clarification of the DP Recursion:** While clear to an expert, the connection between the sum over partitions in Eq. 6 and the recursion in Eq. 10 could be made more explicit for a broader audience. A brief sentence explaining that the factorization of the prior (Eq. 7) and likelihood (Eq. 8) over the tree structure is what enables the sum to be efficiently computed via dynamic programming would enhance clarity.
4.  **Hyperparameter Justification:** Even a small experiment showing results for a few different settings of a key hyperparameter, like γ, would strengthen the paper by demonstrating the robustness (or sensitivity) of the model and providing more confidence in the reported results.

### **Summary of Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. It provides a practical and scalable solution to the important problem of discovering hierarchical communities. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical evidence demonstrating a dramatic speed-up over existing Bayesian methods with comparable performance. While the greedy nature of the inference is a notable limitation, the authors offer a pragmatic and effective trade-off between computational cost and model accuracy. The work is likely to be influential and widely used by researchers and practitioners working with network data. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
*   Presents an elegant and efficient algorithm with a significant (orders of magnitude) speed advantage over relevant baselines.
*   The model is technically sound, well-explained, and the claims are strongly supported by empirical results.
*   Offers a highly compelling point on the speed-accuracy trade-off curve, making Bayesian analysis feasible for larger networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core inference algorithm is greedy and prone to local optima, a significant approximation whose quality is not theoretically characterized.
*   The novelty is primarily in the effective combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamental new concept.
*   The experimental validation could be strengthened with more baselines and a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
