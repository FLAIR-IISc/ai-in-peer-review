Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy Bayesian method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The model views a tree T as a prior over a subset of partitions Φ(T), and defines a marginal likelihood over T by summing p(φ|T)p(D|φ,T), where within-cluster and between-cluster blocks use conjugate beta–Bernoulli marginals f and g with hyperparameters favoring assortativity (α>β, δ<λ). A dynamic programming recursion yields p(D|T) in O(n+m), while an agglomerative model-selection algorithm builds T bottom-up using a local Bayes factor SCORE over three merge operations (join and two absorptions), caching sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” heuristic forbids merging disconnected trees. Link prediction is done by a recursive mixture along the path from the root. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network, a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subgraph, and the full NIPS 1–17 graph demonstrate qualitative recovery of blocks and competitive predictive performance relative to IRM at substantially lower runtime.

This work extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees [Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010] to relational data, contrasting with flat models like the IRM [Kemp et al.], mixed-membership variants [Airoldi et al.], and other nonparametric latent feature models [Miller et al.]. It is related to hierarchical network models such as hierarchical random graphs (HRG) [Clauset, Moore, Newman, Nature 2008], the Mondrian process for relational data [Roy & Teh], and multiscale community blockmodels [Ho et al.], but differs in its specific tree prior, use of conjugate block marginals, and greedy agglomerative inference.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation: a tree-induced prior over partitions with an exact DP for the tree marginal likelihood; principled reuse of rose tree ideas for networks.
- Very efficient inference: O(n^2 log n) independent of the number of communities, with careful caching and locality of the merge score; the sparse variant is practically faster on sparse graphs.
- Practical utility: produces reasonable hierarchies quickly and supports link prediction. Empirically, two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while achieving comparable or better predictive metrics on NIPS-234.
- Interpretable output: hierarchical communities and an explicit mixture interpretation for prediction along the tree path.

Weaknesses
Quality and technical soundness
- The merge score uses only local data (among leaves of the candidate merged tree). This locality assumption is heuristic; no analysis of its impact or guarantees (e.g., consistency, approximation quality) is provided.
- The model is geared to assortative structure via fixed hyperparameters (α>β, δ<λ); no degree correction is included. Standard stochastic blockmodels are known to struggle on heterogenous-degree networks (cf. degree-corrected SBM; Karrer & Newman 2011).
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; there is no hyperparameter learning or sensitivity analysis.
- Uncertainty is handled via restarts and model selection; there is no posterior over trees or assessment of variability beyond ad hoc tie-breaking.
- Minor technical issue: the link-prediction recursion states “computational complexity is Θ(n)”; it appears to be O(depth(T)), not O(n).

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized, but a few notational points could be improved: definitions of σ¬chSS and their updates deserve more explicit derivations. Algorithm 1 is referred to but not fully detailed in the text provided.
- Handling of missing data is only briefly mentioned; the likelihood treatment (counting only observed present/absent) should be stated explicitly.
- The effect of random tie-breaking and restarts on variability is noted but not quantified.

Originality
- The main novelty is the adaptation of Bayesian rose-tree agglomeration to blockmodel likelihoods for relational data. While this is a useful and pragmatic contribution, conceptually it is a modest extension of prior BHC/rose-tree frameworks.
- Related hierarchical network models (e.g., HRG [Clauset et al. 2008]) are not empirically compared, and the distinction relative to Mondrian-based relational models and multiscale SBMs could be sharpened.

Significance
- The speed and simplicity are compelling for practitioners; obtaining useful hierarchical structure and competitive link prediction far faster than IRM is practically valuable.
- However, the empirical scope is limited: two datasets with quantitative evaluation (one very small, one moderate), no synthetic benchmarks with known ground truth hierarchies, and no comparison to other hierarchical network baselines (HRG, Mondrian, multiscale SBMs, nested SBMs).
- Lack of degree correction and hyperparameter learning may limit adoption on modern large and heterogeneous networks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Baselines: Include comparisons to hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman 2008), Mondrian process relational models, and multiscale community blockmodels; if appropriate, also comment on more recent nested SBMs (e.g., Peixoto) to position the contribution.
- Model robustness: Add a degree-corrected variant of f and g (e.g., node effects or degree-corrected Bernoulli) and explore disassortative/bipartite cases by relaxing the α>β, δ<λ assumption or learning these hyperparameters (empirical Bayes or MCMC).
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: Learn or tune α,β,δ,λ,γ; report sensitivity analyses and ablations (e.g., effect of “sparse” merge restriction, number of restarts R, binary vs non-binary merges).
- Synthetic experiments: Evaluate recovery of known hierarchical structures (report dendrogram purity, ARI/NMI at different cuts, and tree distance metrics), and stress-test degree heterogeneity and disassortativity.
- Uncertainty and search: Consider lightweight post-agglomerative refinements (local split–merge, subtree swaps) and/or bagging across restarts; quantify variability across runs.
- Clarify details: Provide full pseudocode, explicit treatment of missing data, correct the link-prediction complexity statement, and expand the DP derivation.

Pros for acceptance
- Fast, principled, and practical algorithm with clean derivations.
- Strong runtime advantages and reasonable predictive performance.
- Produces interpretable hierarchical structures; simple to implement.

Cons for acceptance
- Modest conceptual novelty; close to existing BHC/rose-tree ideas applied to blockmodels.
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing comparisons to key hierarchical network baselines.
- No degree correction or hyperparameter learning; heuristic locality in merge scoring without guarantees.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper offers a useful and efficient approach with clear promise, but the empirical evaluation and positioning relative to established hierarchical network models are not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Addressing the suggested comparisons, robustness, and clarity issues would significantly strengthen the submission.
