Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy, Bayesian agglomerative method to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model uses a conjugate beta–Bernoulli stochastic blockmodel with different priors for within- and between-community edges (Eq. 2), and defines a prior over partitions via a tree T that constrains which partitions are allowed (Eqs. 5–8). This yields a dynamic-programming recursion for the marginal likelihood of any tree (Eq. 10). Inference constructs T bottom-up by iteratively merging subtrees, scoring local merges by a Bayes factor (Eq. 12). Three merge types (join and two absorptions) follow the Bayesian Rose Tree framework. A “sparse” variant forbids merges between disconnected components. Predictive link probabilities are computed by a recursive mixture along the tree (Eq. 14). Empirically, BHCD is applied to Sampson’s monastery, a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset (compared to IRM), and the full NIPS 1–17 graph.

This work sits between IRM-style Bayesian nonparametric models for flat clusters [Kemp et al.] and greedy hierarchical Bayesian clustering [Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.], extending the latter to blockmodels. It relates to hierarchical blockmodels and hierarchical random graphs (e.g., Clauset–Moore–Newman, 2008; nested SBMs and degree-corrected SBMs by Karrer–Newman, Peixoto), latent feature models (LFRM; ILA), and Mondrian processes for trees. Compared to MCMC/variational approaches (IRM, ILA, HSBM), BHCD trades some modeling flexibility for significant speed via greedy search and local scoring.

Strengths
- Clear and principled adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to relational data, with a conjugate blockmodel likelihood and a coherent tree-conditioned prior over partitions.
- Efficient recursion for p(D|T) (Eq. 10) and a well-engineered agglomerative search leveraging cached sufficient statistics; the sparse variant is particularly attractive for large sparse graphs.
- Practical, interpretable hierarchies with non-binary nodes (rose trees), avoiding brittle binary cascades; the πS parameterization encourages appropriate arity.
- Link prediction routine (Eq. 14) is simple and efficient.
- Empirical results show competitive prediction against IRM with orders-of-magnitude less wall-clock time and reasonable qualitative structure on standard datasets.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- The approach is heuristic at the search level: greedy, local Bayes factors (Eq. 12) without global guarantees or split moves. This is common but should be discussed in terms of potential local optima and failure modes.
- The likelihood assumes assortativity via α>β and δ<λ. This biases against disassortative, bipartite, or core–periphery structure. No degree correction is modeled; high-degree heterogeneity can confound blockmodels.
- No hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis for α,β,δ,λ,γ; all are fixed. Performance and inferred structure can be sensitive to these choices.
- Experimental comparisons are limited. IRM is a reasonable baseline, but missing comparisons to stronger and widely used baselines: hierarchical random graphs, nested/degree-corrected SBMs (MDL/Bayesian), and recent scalable SBM inference. Claims of being “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” need clarification and more careful, apples-to-apples wall-clock comparisons.
- Some implementation details are under-specified: the “BHCD global” mentioned in Fig. 3 is not defined earlier; tie-breaking and heap staleness are acknowledged but not mitigated (e.g., lazy recomputation strategies).

Clarity
- Generally well written, with explicit equations and notational consistency around σ. Eq. (14) would benefit from a brief derivation or intuition for rS. Define all variants (BHCD global, binary/sparse) consistently when first introduced.
- Algorithm 1 is referenced but not fully shown in the excerpt; ensure pseudocode includes cache updates and complexity justifications.
- Reference formatting is incomplete (many entries lack years/venues) and some key related works are missing (see below).

Originality
- The novelty is primarily in adapting Bayesian rose trees to a blockmodel likelihood with efficient caching and in demonstrating scalability to medium-sized graphs. Conceptually incremental relative to [13,14], IRM, and hierarchical random graphs, but the practical algorithmic contribution is useful.

Significance
- As a fast, interpretable baseline for hierarchical community discovery with probabilistic scoring, BHCD is valuable. However, given the current state of the art (degree-corrected/nested SBMs with scalable inference), the impact hinges on broader comparisons and robustness (hyperparameters, sensitivity, different network regimes).

Missing/under-cited related work
- Hierarchical random graphs and nested SBMs (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008; Peixoto, 2014–2019), degree-corrected SBMs (Karrer & Newman, 2011), and scalable Bayesian/MDL inference for SBMs should be discussed and compared where possible.
- Overlapping/mixed-membership hierarchical models (beyond depth-2), and alternative hierarchical priors (tree-structured stick-breaking, diffusion trees) are relevant.

Questions/clarifications
- Clarify the “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” claim (do you mean a full run vs one full Gibbs sweep?).
- How are held-out edges selected for CV, and are non-edges subsampled? Provide exact splits/ratios.
- How sensitive are results to γ and to the within/between hyperparameters? Any empirical Bayes or cross-validation for these?
- How do different tie-breaking seeds and number of restarts R affect variability of trees and predictions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add degree correction and an option for disassortative priors to broaden applicability.
- Include comparisons to nested/degree-corrected SBMs (e.g., graph-tool implementation), and to hierarchical random graphs.
- Provide a hyperparameter selection strategy (empirical Bayes, cross-validation), and report sensitivity.
- Consider split-and-merge search or limited lookahead to mitigate local optima; or ensemble multiple trees for uncertainty quantification.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce timing and prediction metrics; standardize wall-clock measurements against well-tuned baselines.
- Improve references and add missing related work; define all variants clearly; fix minor typos and notational issues.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, fast, and interpretable hierarchical community discovery with a coherent Bayesian objective.
- Strong practical speedups with reasonable predictive performance.
- Clear exposition of the core recursion and algorithmic engineering.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing hierarchical SBMs/HRG; missing key comparisons to current state of the art.
- Modeling restrictions (no degree correction, assortativity bias) and lack of hyperparameter inference/sensitivity.
- Greedy search without stronger validation or uncertainty handling.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper is promising as a fast, practical method, but needs stronger empirical comparisons (especially to nested/degree-corrected SBMs), hyperparameter treatment, and broader discussion of limitations. With these additions, it could be competitive.

Reviewer confidence: High on methodological context; medium on exact empirical details absent from the excerpt.
