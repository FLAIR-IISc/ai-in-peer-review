This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to extend Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering by adapting them to relational data, framing the problem as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors claim significant speed improvements over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while maintaining competitive performance.

The paper is generally well-written and the motivation for exploring faster, scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection is clear. The connection to stochastic blockmodels provides a solid foundation, and the proposed approach builds logically upon existing Bayesian greedy clustering methods. The authors correctly identify the computational bottlenecks of existing methods and attempt to address them through a greedy, dynamic programming-based inference scheme. The experiments are a good start, showcasing the speed advantage of BHCD and demonstrating reasonable performance on both a small, well-studied dataset (Sampson’s monastery network) and a larger co-authorship network (NIPS).

However, despite the authors’ claims, I find the paper’s contributions to be incremental, and the evidence presented insufficient to warrant strong endorsement at NIPS. My primary concern revolves around the lack of a truly compelling demonstration of *superiority* beyond speed. While faster than IRM, the performance on NIPS-234 is comparable to, but not consistently better than, the Latent Factor Relational Model (LFRM). Given the relative maturity and established track record of LFRM, merely achieving comparable results with a faster algorithm doesn’t constitute a significant advance.

Furthermore, the theoretical justification for the model, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The authors mention the importance of conjugate priors for computational efficiency, but don’t fully articulate the trade-offs involved. The choice of hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) appears somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification based on the data or theoretical considerations. A more thorough sensitivity analysis of these parameters would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation also has limitations. The comparison to IRM is somewhat unfair, as the IRM is notoriously slow. More importantly, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to other established community detection algorithms, particularly non-Bayesian methods like Louvain or Leiden algorithms, which are often used as benchmarks due to their speed and scalability. Demonstrating superiority over these widely used alternatives would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims. The use of a fixed cut-off (rT ∏ A∈ancestorT (1−rA) > 0.5) for visualizing the NIPS hierarchy is also somewhat ad-hoc and could influence the observed results.

The discussion of variations, like BHCD sparse and the binary tree restrictions, is insightful, but again lacks a strong justification for the observed performance differences. The claim that binary trees yield more interpretable hierarchies is subjective and not convincingly supported.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem (scalable hierarchical community detection).
*   Presents a novel, theoretically grounded approach.
*   Demonstrates a significant speed advantage over existing Bayesian methods.
*   Provides preliminary experimental validation on relevant datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution – performance not demonstrably superior to existing, established methods.
*   Limited comparison to non-Bayesian benchmarks.
*   Lack of thorough parameter sensitivity analysis.
*   Ad-hoc choices in experimental setup and visualization.
*   Theoretical justification feels somewhat cursory.



**Overall:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and the idea is reasonable, it doesn’t represent a substantial enough advance to warrant inclusion in NIPS. The authors need to provide a more compelling demonstration of the method’s superiority, ideally through more comprehensive comparisons to a wider range of algorithms and a more rigorous theoretical analysis. The speed advantage is valuable, but insufficient on its own to justify acceptance in a highly competitive venue like NIPS. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation, exploring the impact of different hyperparameters, and providing a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs involved in their approach.
