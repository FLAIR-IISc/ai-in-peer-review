This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach is framed as a Bayesian model selection problem, where a hierarchy is represented by a tree built in a bottom-up fashion. At each step, the algorithm merges the pair of subtrees that results in the largest increase in the marginal likelihood of the data, calculated via a local Bayes factor. The underlying generative model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs), where the marginal likelihood for a given tree structure can be calculated exactly and efficiently using dynamic programming. This work builds directly on prior research in Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2010), adapting that framework from i.i.d. data to relational network data using an SBM-style likelihood. The primary contribution is a computationally efficient and principled alternative to more costly MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods for community detection, such as hierarchical extensions of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM).

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic model is sound, and the derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Eq. 10) is clear and correct. The core of the technical contribution lies in the efficient implementation of the greedy search algorithm (Algorithm 1), which leverages a caching scheme for sufficient statistics to achieve an O(n^2 log n) time complexity. This makes the method significantly more scalable than many competing Bayesian approaches. The claims are well-supported by a solid set of experiments covering qualitative interpretation (Sampson's monastery), quantitative predictive performance against a key baseline (NIPS-234), and scalability (full NIPS dataset). The authors are honest about the method's greedy nature and propose using multiple restarts to mitigate the issue of local optima.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The progression from the standard SBM to the hierarchical model and the agglomerative inference procedure is logical and easy to follow. The use of figures to illustrate the tree-consistent partitions (Fig. 1) and merge operations (Fig. 2) is effective. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the experimental setup and results are well-described. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel and clever combination of existing techniques. While neither Bayesian hierarchical clustering nor the SBM are new, their synthesis into an efficient, greedy model-selection algorithm for network data is a novel contribution. The primary originality lies in the adaptation of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework to relational data and the development of an efficient algorithm to perform inference. The paper does a good job of positioning itself relative to prior work, clearly distinguishing its approach from MCMC-based inference for models like the IRM and other hierarchical SBMs.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant. The ability to perform Bayesian inference for hierarchical community structure "two orders of magnitude faster" than a single iteration of a comparable MCMC-based method (IRM) is a major practical advantage. This makes sophisticated Bayesian modeling accessible for larger networks where MCMC would be computationally prohibitive. The demonstrated predictive performance, which is competitive with or better than the IRM given a reasonable time budget, suggests that the speed does not come at an excessive cost to accuracy. Researchers and practitioners looking for a fast, principled, and interpretable method for exploring network hierarchies are likely to find this work very useful.

**Strengths:**
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The main strength of the paper. The O(n^2 log n) algorithm, independent of the number of communities, offers a dramatic speed-up over MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods, as compellingly demonstrated in Figure 4.
*   **Principled Framework:** The greedy algorithm is not based on a heuristic score but is a well-founded model selection procedure that attempts to maximize the data's marginal likelihood.
*   **Model Flexibility:** The model naturally determines the structure and depth of the hierarchy from data, without needing to specify the number of communities beforehand.
*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, providing qualitative, quantitative, and scalability assessments that effectively highlight the method's advantages.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Greedy Search Limitations:** The most significant weakness is the greedy nature of the inference, which is prone to finding local optima. While the use of multiple restarts helps, the algorithm does not explore the full posterior over hierarchies in the way that an MCMC sampler could.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) that are fixed in the experiments. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how these were chosen and an analysis of the model's sensitivity to their settings. For a Bayesian method, the lack of hyperparameter inference is a notable omission.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The experimental comparison focuses primarily on the IRM. While this is a relevant baseline, comparing against fast, state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection algorithms would provide a more complete picture of where BHCD stands in the broader landscape of community detection methods.
*   **Clarity on Merge Types:** The motivation for including non-binary "absorption" merges could be clearer. The paper notes that binary versions "tend to produce better predictive results," which raises the question of the primary benefit of the more complex non-binary "rose tree" structures. The text seems to contain a typo ("more difficult to interpret than the non-binary hierarchies found"), which obscures the authors' intended point.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  A brief sensitivity analysis or discussion regarding the choice of hyperparameters would make the experimental results more robust.
2.  The trade-off between the binary-only and non-binary versions of BHCD should be clarified. If the non-binary version offers better interpretability at the cost of predictive performance, this should be stated and demonstrated more explicitly.
3.  While likely out of scope for a revision, future work should definitely consider comparisons to fast non-Bayesian methods (e.g., those based on modularity) to better situate the performance-complexity trade-off.
4.  For the "BHCD sparse" variant, is it possible to state a tighter time complexity bound for sparse graphs where m << n^2? This is mentioned as future work but is a key question for scalability on most real-world networks.

**Decision:**
This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. It addresses the important problem of scalable inference for hierarchical community models with a novel, efficient, and principled approach. The strengths, particularly the impressive computational gains demonstrated without sacrificing much performance, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
