The paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to build a hierarchical tree representing the community structure, leveraging a stochastic blockmodel framework and a Bayesian nonparametric approach. The authors address the scalability issues of existing Bayesian methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) by using a greedy algorithm and efficient caching of sufficient statistics, resulting in significant speedups. They present experiments on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS network, demonstrating both qualitative and quantitative results.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The Bayesian model is clearly defined, building upon existing stochastic blockmodel and hierarchical Bayesian approaches. The greedy algorithm for model selection is well-motivated, and the caching scheme seems effective for improving computational efficiency. The experiments demonstrate that BHCD is significantly faster than the IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance. However, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the greedy algorithm, or bounds on the error introduced by the greedy approach compared to a fully Bayesian solution. The choice of hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4) is stated to work well, but lacks justification or sensitivity analysis. The claim of quadratic worst-case runtime is reasonable given the described algorithm. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and potential future directions.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and related work. The model description is relatively clear, although some sections (e.g., the mathematical derivations) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The notation is mostly consistent, but can be dense at times. The experimental section is well-structured, and the results are presented clearly. However, the descriptions of variations of BHCD (sparse, binary) are brief, and it’s not immediately obvious how those changes impact the algorithmic details and interpretability of the hierarchy. Figures 3 and 5 are helpful for visualizing the community structures discovered, but more explanation on the selection criteria for which tree structures to show would be valuable.

**Originality:** The combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering with a greedy algorithm and efficient caching is a novel contribution. While the individual components (stochastic blockmodels, Bayesian inference, greedy algorithms) are not new, their integration offers a practical and scalable solution for hierarchical community detection. The paper builds on prior work [13, 14] extending it to relational data. The algorithm's speed advantage over the IRM and other Bayesian methods is a significant improvement. The comparison with existing methods (IRM, LFRM, ILA) provides a good benchmark for evaluating the performance of BHCD.

**Significance:** The results are important and address a challenging problem in network analysis. The ability to discover hierarchical community structure efficiently has broad applications in various domains, including social network analysis, bioinformatics, and recommender systems. The speedup achieved by BHCD makes it applicable to larger networks than previously feasible with Bayesian methods. The method provides a probabilistic account of the community structure, which is valuable for uncertainty quantification and prediction. The demonstration on the NIPS co-authorship network is particularly relevant, showcasing the ability to uncover meaningful hierarchical relationships among researchers. The predictive performance is comparable to state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant speedup compared to existing Bayesian methods (IRM).
*   Scalable to larger networks.
*   Novel combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering and greedy algorithm.
*   Demonstrated performance on multiple datasets.
*   Provides a probabilistic account of community structure.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm's convergence and error bounds.
*   Limited justification for hyperparameter choices.
*   Brief descriptions of variations of BHCD.
*   Could benefit from more in-depth discussion of the interpretability of the hierarchical structures.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The technical soundness, originality, and significance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
