Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for community detection in relational (network) data. The model defines a hierarchy T whose internal nodes are communities and leaves are vertices. The likelihood is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels: within-community edges are modeled by an on-diagonal marginal likelihood f and between-children edges at each internal node are modeled by an off-diagonal marginal likelihood g. For concreteness, the paper uses a conjugate beta–Bernoulli instantiation with distinct hyperparameters for on- and off-diagonal blocks (encouraging assortative structure). A prior over partitions consistent with T is defined via a stopping probability πS at each node (following Bayesian rose trees), enabling exact marginal likelihood computation for a given tree via a dynamic program: p(DTT|T) = πT f(σTT) + (1−πT) g(σ¬chTT) ∏C p(DCC|C). 

Inference is performed by greedy agglomeration starting from singletons, considering three merge operations (join and two absorptions), scored by a local Bayes factor that uses only data among the leaves of the two candidate subtrees. Efficient caching of sufficient statistics yields O(1) per update inside the inner loop, giving an overall O(n^2 log n) time algorithm with O(n^2) memory. A “sparse” variant forbids merging trees with no edges between them. Link prediction is done by a recursive mixture along the root-to-leaf paths using responsibilities rS.

Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network show qualitatively coherent hierarchies; on a NIPS-234 coauthorship subset, BHCD variants reach competitive predictive performance much faster than the IRM (both MCMC and greedy), and on the full NIPS 1–17 dataset (2864 nodes) BHCD discovers a large hierarchy and an illustrative top-level cut. The paper positions BHCD relative to stochastic blockmodels, mixed-membership SBMs, the IRM and its hierarchical variants, and Bayesian hierarchical clustering and rose trees, and discusses speed advantages due to the greedy approach.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Model: The hierarchical mixture construction is well defined. The prior over tree-consistent partitions based on node-wise stopping probabilities matches the Bayesian rose tree framework. The decomposition of the likelihood into on-diagonal (f) and between-children (g) terms avoids double-counting by assigning between-child edges to g at the split level and passing within-child edges down the tree.
- Inference: The Bayes factor score for merges uses only local data, enabling efficient updates with cached sufficient statistics; the update rules are clearly specified. The dynamic program for evaluating p(D|T) (given T) is correct given f and g are conjugate and can be computed from sufficient statistics. The complexity claims for a run (O(n^2 log n)) and per-tree likelihood evaluation (O(n+m)) are plausible given the stated caching, though memory is O(n^2).
- Predictions: The recursive predictive distribution and responsibility rS are consistent with the generative process; complexity is linear in tree depth (the text also states Θ(n); clarifying that this is a worst-case bound would help).
- Caveats: The “sparse” variant forbids some merges, which improves practice but breaks exact adherence to the stated generative prior (a heuristic constraint). The greedy, local nature of the search can get trapped; multiple restarts mitigate this for prediction but do not quantify uncertainty over trees.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The core equations, prior definition, recursion, and sufficient-statistic updates are explicit. The relation to Bayesian rose trees is properly explained.
- Minor clarity issues: 
  - The O(n+m) claim for likelihood evaluation would benefit from a brief explanation of how σ¬chSS is accumulated efficiently at all nodes.
  - The statement “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n)” in Sec. 4.2 appears to refer to worst-case path length; clarifying expected vs worst-case depth would avoid confusion.
  - Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown in detail; including a compact pseudocode would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The main novelty is adapting Bayesian rose trees to relational data via a blockmodel with separate on/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods, together with an efficient, local Bayes-factor merge scoring and caching scheme. This is a reasonable and concrete extension of [13,14].
- Relative to prior Bayesian models for hierarchical community structure (e.g., hierarchical IRM, Mondrian process, depth-limited models), the contribution is a simpler, much faster greedy approach with exact tree-marginal computations, at the cost of not performing posterior inference over trees.
- A relevant prior work not cited is the hierarchical random graph model (Clauset, Moore, Newman, Nature 2008), which also infers hierarchical structure in networks with a dendrogram and uses likelihood-based inference. Clarifying similarities/differences (e.g., prior structure, likelihood, and optimization) would strengthen the positioning.

Significance
- Practical significance is strong: the method scales to a few thousand nodes with competitive link prediction and orders-of-magnitude faster runtime than IRM-based baselines. Independence from the number of communities in the complexity is attractive.
- Scientific significance is moderate: it provides a coherent probabilistic framework for hierarchical community detection with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions and a clear, efficient inference heuristic. However, it is an incremental methodological advance relative to known Bayesian hierarchical clustering, trading statistical sophistication for speed.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic model with exact marginal likelihood for a fixed tree.
- Efficient, well-engineered greedy inference with O(n^2 log n) time and simple caching.
- Competitive link prediction on NIPS-234 much faster than IRM; qualitative results on standard benchmark and large real dataset.
- Straightforward predictive computation and path-wise interpretability.
- Sensible discussion of binary vs non-binary trees and practical “sparse” variant.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: largely an adaptation of Bayesian rose trees to blockmodels.
- Strong assortativity assumption via fixed Beta priors; no handling of disassortative or bipartite structure unless hyperparameters are tuned; no hyperparameter inference.
- Heuristic “sparse” constraint departs from the stated prior and may bias results.
- Greedy local merges with random tie-breaking; no guarantees or posterior uncertainty over trees; dependence on number of restarts.
- Empirical evaluation omits comparisons to hierarchical random graphs and other hierarchical SBMs; limited sensitivity analysis to γ and Beta hyperparameters; memory/runtime scaling not quantified beyond big-O.
- Minor clarity gaps as noted.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to α,β,δ,λ and γ? Could you learn these hyperparameters or adapt them per-node to capture assortative vs disassortative structure?
- What is the memory footprint (in practice) of caching all σIJ for large n? Any sparse data structures used?
- Can you compare against the hierarchical random graph (Clauset et al., 2008) or discuss differences?
- Does forbidding merges with no edges ever harm performance on datasets with missing edges? Could a soft penalty replace the hard constraint?
- What is the typical tree depth observed, and how does it scale with n?

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a clear, fast, and practically useful Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery, with exact marginal likelihood for a tree and strong runtime advantages.
- Sound derivation and implementation details; empirical results show competitive accuracy with much lower compute.

Arguments against acceptance
- Methodological novelty is limited; core ideas closely follow Bayesian rose trees with a standard blockmodel likelihood.
- Evaluation lacks some key hierarchical baselines and robustness analyses; the “sparse” heuristic deviates from the model.
- Assortativity bias and lack of hyperparameter learning limit applicability across diverse networks.

Overall, this is a technically sound and clearly written paper with practical impact through speed and scalability; its main limitation is incremental novelty and somewhat narrow evaluation.
