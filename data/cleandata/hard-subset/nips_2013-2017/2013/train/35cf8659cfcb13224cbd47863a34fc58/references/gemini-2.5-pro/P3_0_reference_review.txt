**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in network data. The core contribution is the adaptation of the Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering framework, previously developed for i.i.d. data, to the relational setting of networks. The authors define a probabilistic model where a tree structure defines a prior over partitions of the network's vertices. The marginal likelihood of the network data given a tree is calculated efficiently via dynamic programming. Inference of the tree structure itself is performed using a greedy, bottom-up agglomerative algorithm that iteratively merges sub-trees to maximize the Bayes factor, a proxy for the marginal likelihood. The method is demonstrated to be significantly faster than MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving competitive predictive performance on link prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its clever and effective synthesis of two powerful ideas: the stochastic blockmodel (SBM) for relational data and the efficient Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (BHC) framework [13, 14]. The extension of the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Eq. 10) to handle on-diagonal (within-community) and off-diagonal (between-community) block statistics is non-trivial and well-executed. This provides a principled, fully Bayesian approach to hierarchical clustering of graphs that avoids the heuristic nature of many classic algorithms (e.g., Newman-Girvan).

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The most significant practical contribution is the method's speed. By replacing computationally intensive MCMC sampling with a deterministic greedy search, BHCD achieves a dramatic speed-up. The complexity of O(n² log n) is a major improvement over methods whose runtime depends on the number of iterations and the number of latent communities, which can be large. The experimental results in Figure 4 compellingly demonstrate this, showing that BHCD achieves strong performance in seconds, while the IRM requires thousands of seconds to converge to a similar level. This makes the method applicable to much larger networks.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors build their argument logically, starting from the basic SBM, defining the hierarchical model and its marginal likelihood, and then describing the agglomerative inference scheme. The notation is consistent, and the figures are informative. This clarity makes the work accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed to showcase the method's strengths. The qualitative analysis on the classic Sampson's Monastery dataset shows that BHCD recovers meaningful and interpretable structures. The quantitative comparison on the NIPS co-authorship benchmark provides a direct and impressive head-to-head against a relevant baseline (IRM) on speed and predictive accuracy. The inclusion of the full NIPS dataset demonstrates the method's scalability.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Greedy Inference:** The primary limitation is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. While fast, this bottom-up approach is susceptible to finding local optima and is not guaranteed to find the tree structure that globally maximizes the marginal likelihood. The authors acknowledge this and use multiple random restarts to mitigate the issue, but it remains a fundamental trade-off against the more exhaustive exploration of MCMC methods. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the random tie-breaking in the initial stages would have strengthened the paper.

2.  **Limited Comparison Baselines:** The main comparison is against the IRM. While the IRM is a canonical Bayesian nonparametric model for networks, the field has other relevant methods. It would have been beneficial to compare against:
    *   Faster inference techniques for SBMs, such as variational inference, which can also be quite scalable.
    *   Other scalable hierarchical community detection methods, even if non-Bayesian (e.g., those based on modularity optimization or the minimum description length principle), to better contextualize the performance. The authors honestly report that their method is outperformed by ILA [10] in accuracy, which frames BHCD as a "fast-but-not-best" method, a perfectly valid but important positioning.

3.  **Handling of Sparsity:** The "BHCD sparse" variant, which prohibits merges between disconnected components, is presented as an ad-hoc heuristic. While it empirically improves performance and speed on sparse networks, it feels less principled than the core model. As the authors note in the discussion, the O(n²) component of the complexity is a bottleneck for sparse graphs where m << n². A more fundamentally sparse implementation, where the complexity depends on the number of edges (m) rather than nodes (n), would be a significant next step and is a current weakness for very large, sparse networks.

4.  **Local Merge Criterion:** The Bayes factor calculation for a merge (Eq. 12) is local, considering only the data within and between the two sub-trees being merged. This implicitly assumes that the optimal local merge is also part of the optimal global structure. In networks with complex, long-range dependencies, this assumption might not hold, and a locally optimal merge could lead the greedy search down a suboptimal path.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses for the Program Committee**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and principled Bayesian framework for hierarchical community detection.
*   The method is computationally very efficient, offering a significant speed-up (orders of magnitude) over standard MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods.
*   The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and easy to understand.
*   Experimental results are strong, demonstrating both qualitative interpretability and quantitative advantages in speed and predictive power against a key baseline.
*   The work addresses an important and widely studied problem, and the proposed method is likely to be used by practitioners and researchers.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The greedy inference scheme is a significant simplification and is not guaranteed to find the optimal solution.
*   The set of experimental comparisons could be broader, including modern variational methods or other scalable hierarchical clustering algorithms.
*   The handling of sparse graphs is heuristic, and the worst-case complexity remains quadratic in the number of nodes.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The elegant adaptation of the BHC framework to relational data is a significant contribution, and the practical benefits in terms of computational speed are substantial. It presents a complete piece of work with a clear methodology, strong empirical backing, and honest discussion of its limitations. This is a high-quality paper that would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings. I strongly recommend acceptance.
