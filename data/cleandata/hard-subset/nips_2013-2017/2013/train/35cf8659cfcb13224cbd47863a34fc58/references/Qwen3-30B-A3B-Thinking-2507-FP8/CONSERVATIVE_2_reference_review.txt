

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method that extends greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data. The authors claim their method is two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable performance, with worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n). The approach builds upon previous work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14] and the stochastic blockmodel.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a well-structured method with clear mathematical formulations. The authors provide a sound theoretical foundation for their approach, including proper probabilistic modeling of hierarchical community structure. The algorithm description is detailed, and the authors demonstrate their method on three datasets (Sampson's monastery, NIPS co-authorship subset, and full NIPS network). The computational complexity analysis is technically sound, and the paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the methodological soundness, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and validation. The most critical issue is the lack of thorough experimental validation against established baselines. The authors claim BHCD "attains comparable performance" to IRM but provide insufficient evidence. The comparison to IRM is limited to a single dataset (NIPS-234), and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences. The paper also fails to compare against established hierarchical clustering methods like [3,4] that are specifically designed for hierarchical community structure.

The experimental section is particularly weak. The authors claim BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster" than IRM, but they don't provide sufficient context about the hardware, implementation details, or specific conditions under which this speedup holds. The paper doesn't address whether this speedup comes at the cost of accuracy on real-world networks. The results on Sampson's monastery network show BHCD finding reasonable structure, but the authors don't demonstrate how this structure is superior to what could be achieved with simpler flat community detection methods.

The paper's claim of "comparable performance" to IRM is particularly problematic. The results in Figure 4 show that BHCD initially performs worse than IRM but catches up after about 10 seconds. However, the paper doesn't provide evidence that BHCD's final performance is statistically indistinguishable from IRM's, nor does it demonstrate that this is consistent across multiple datasets and network types.

The paper also lacks a thorough analysis of the limitations of their approach. For instance, the authors mention that BHCD may consider merging disconnected trees, but they don't provide evidence of how often this happens in practice or how it affects the results. The paper doesn't address whether the hierarchical structure discovered is meaningful for downstream tasks, which is a critical concern for community discovery methods.

**Originality:**
While the specific combination of greedy agglomerative approach with Bayesian hierarchical modeling is somewhat novel, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from previous work. The authors cite [13,14] as building upon, but don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from those prior methods. The paper also doesn't adequately address how their method differs from other greedy hierarchical clustering approaches [3,4], which are explicitly mentioned in the introduction.

**Significance:**
The claimed speed advantage is potentially significant for large networks, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this speed-accuracy tradeoff is favorable for real-world applications. The paper doesn't provide evidence that hierarchical community structure is necessary for the tasks they address, nor does it show that the hierarchical structure discovered is more useful than flat community structure for downstream applications.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a methodologically sound approach that builds upon established work, but the lack of thorough validation and insufficient evidence for the claimed performance advantages make it difficult to recommend for acceptance. The authors make bold claims about performance and speed without providing the necessary experimental evidence to support them. The paper would be significantly improved with more extensive validation against multiple established baselines across diverse network types, statistical significance testing, and a clearer demonstration of the value of hierarchical structure over flat community detection.

I recommend rejection. While the approach has potential, the current evidence is insufficient to support the paper's claims, and the lack of thorough validation against established methods contradicts the high standards expected at NIPS. The authors should conduct more comprehensive experiments and provide stronger evidence for their claims before resubmitting.
