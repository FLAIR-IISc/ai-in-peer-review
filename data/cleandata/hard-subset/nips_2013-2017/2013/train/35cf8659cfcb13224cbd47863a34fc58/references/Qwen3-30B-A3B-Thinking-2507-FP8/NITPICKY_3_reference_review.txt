

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper introduces BHCD, a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for discovering community structure in social networks. The authors extend Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data by modeling communities as a nested hierarchy of stochastic blockmodels, with the key contribution being an efficient inference algorithm with O(n² log n) time complexity.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in network analysis with a well-motivated approach. The experimental results demonstrate BHCD's computational advantages over existing Bayesian methods, with a clear 2-order-of-magnitude speedup compared to IRM. The hierarchical structure discovery on Sampson's monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks is visually compelling.

**Major Technical Concerns:**

1. **Contradictory Complexity Claims:** The paper states "worst case run-time is quadratic in the number of vertices" but then claims O(n² log n) complexity in Algorithm 1 and Table 1. This inconsistency undermines the paper's core technical contribution. The authors must clarify whether they mean Θ(n² log n) or O(n²).

2. **Inconsistent Notation:** Multiple instances of unclear notation appear throughout (e.g., "n 0 pq" in equations (1) and (2) should be "n^0_{pq}"). Equation (9) uses confusing "σ¬chSS" notation that should be properly formatted as "σ^{\neg ch}_S" or similar.

3. **Unjustified Hyperparameters:** The authors set α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4 without justification. This is particularly concerning since the paper claims these hyperparameters "work well" without any sensitivity analysis or ablation studies.

4. **Theoretical-Algorithmic Mismatch:** The paper claims "the marginal likelihood (6) may be calculated by dynamic programming, in O(n + m) time" but then states the algorithm complexity as O(n² log n). This disconnect between theoretical likelihood calculation and algorithm complexity is not resolved.

5. **Implementation Ambiguity:** The paper states "BHCD sparse... runs faster for sparse networks" but doesn't provide theoretical analysis of its sparse complexity. The claim that it "has the same time complexity O(n² log n) as BHCD" in the worst case contradicts the stated advantage for sparse networks.

**Minor Issues:**

1. Algorithm 1 is referenced but not provided in the excerpt, making it impossible to assess the implementation details.

2. The paper references "BHCD global" in Figure 3 without defining what this variant is, creating confusion for readers.

3. The paper claims "the algorithm for finding T is described in Algorithm 1" but the algorithm isn't included in the provided text.

4. Inconsistent reference formatting (some with page numbers, others without) and missing publication venues for some references.

**Significance Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting computational approach to hierarchical community detection, the technical inconsistencies and lack of theoretical justification for key claims significantly undermine the contribution. The paper claims to build upon [13, 14] but doesn't clearly articulate how it extends these works beyond the vague statement "extending these approaches to relational data." The experimental results, while promising, don't sufficiently demonstrate that BHCD provides novel insights beyond existing methods.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper contains substantial technical inconsistencies and methodological shortcomings that prevent it from being accepted. The contradictory complexity claims, unjustified hyperparameters, and theoretical-algorithmic mismatch are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission. The paper would require significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.

The reviewers recommend rejection due to the technical inconsistencies that undermine the core claims of the paper. The authors would need to resolve the complexity contradictions, provide justification for their hyperparameters, and clarify the theoretical basis for their algorithm's efficiency before the paper could be reconsidered.
