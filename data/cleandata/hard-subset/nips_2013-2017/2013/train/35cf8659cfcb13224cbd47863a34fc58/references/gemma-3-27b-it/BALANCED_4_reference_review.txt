This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to extend existing Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data, leveraging a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors highlight the computational efficiency of their approach, claiming two orders of magnitude faster performance than a single iteration of the IRM and a worst-case runtime independent of the number of communities. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, related work, and the proposed method. It builds upon a solid foundation of previous work in stochastic blockmodels, Bayesian nonparametrics, and hierarchical clustering. The motivation for developing a faster and more scalable method for hierarchical community discovery is clear, given the computational limitations of existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches like the IRM. The comparison to IRM and other methods in terms of computational complexity (Table 1) is a strong point, effectively demonstrating the potential benefits of the proposed method. The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS network provide a good evaluation of the method’s performance. The inclusion of both qualitative and quantitative results, including predictive performance, adds to the paper’s strength.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its computational efficiency. The claimed speedup compared to IRM is significant, and the quadratic runtime complexity (independent of the number of communities) is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the explanations are accessible.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The method builds on established concepts in stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian nonparametrics, providing a strong theoretical basis.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate the method's effectiveness and provide insights into its performance characteristics.
* **Variants and Analysis:** The exploration of variations like BHCD sparse and the binary-tree restriction, alongside the analysis of their tradeoffs, showcase a thoughtful approach to method development. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states hyperparameters were set as α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4 "which we found to work well." This is insufficient justification. A more systematic exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity, potentially with a sensitivity analysis or cross-validation approach, is needed. How do these choices affect the resulting hierarchies and predictive performance?
* **Limited Comparison Depth:** While the comparison to IRM is valuable, the comparison to other state-of-the-art hierarchical community detection methods is somewhat limited.  More thorough comparison with recent methods specifically designed for hierarchical community detection (beyond LFRM and ILA) would strengthen the claims.
* **Model Assumptions:** The choice of Beta priors and the assumption of conjugate priors, while contributing to computational efficiency, might limit the flexibility of the model. A discussion of the potential impact of these assumptions on the results would be beneficial.
* **Interpretation of Hierarchies:** While the paper visually presents the discovered hierarchies (e.g., Figure 5), more discussion on how to interpret these hierarchies and assess their meaningfulness would be valuable. How can we evaluate the quality of the discovered hierarchical structure beyond predictive performance?
* **Scalability Nuance:** While the runtime is quadratic, the authors acknowledge that the performance on sparse networks can be improved. A more detailed discussion on adapting the algorithm to exploit sparsity further would enhance the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method offers a significant improvement in computational efficiency for Bayesian hierarchical community detection, addressing a key limitation of existing approaches.
* The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and based on a strong theoretical foundation.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the method's effectiveness on multiple datasets.
* The authors identify potential areas for future work, demonstrating a forward-looking perspective.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of a thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis weakens the robustness of the results.
* The comparison to other state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.
* The model assumptions and their potential impact on the results are not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of hierarchical community detection. The proposed method addresses a significant practical challenge – computational efficiency – while maintaining a solid theoretical foundation. While some weaknesses exist, particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and comparative analysis, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

1. **Hyperparameter Analysis:** Perform a sensitivity analysis or cross-validation to determine the impact of hyperparameters on the results and provide a more robust justification for the chosen values.
2. **Expanded Comparison:** Include a more comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art hierarchical community detection methods.
3. **Discussion of Assumptions:**  Discuss the potential limitations imposed by the choice of Beta priors and conjugate priors.
4. **Hierarchy Interpretation:** Provide a more detailed discussion on interpreting the discovered hierarchies and assessing their quality beyond predictive performance.
5. **Sparsity Optimization:** Expand the discussion on adapting the algorithm to exploit sparsity further and potentially incorporate sparsity-aware optimizations.
