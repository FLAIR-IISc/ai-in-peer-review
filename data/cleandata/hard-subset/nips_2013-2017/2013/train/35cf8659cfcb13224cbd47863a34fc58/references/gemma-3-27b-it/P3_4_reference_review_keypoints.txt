- Presents BHCD: a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method framing community discovery as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels.
- Key contribution: an efficient inference scheme that avoids IRM’s bottlenecks, showing ≈100× speedup per iteration and runtime independent of number of communities.
- Positions itself well in prior work: bridges flat SBMs and greedy clustering while addressing computational costs of Bayesian nonparametrics.
- Strength — computational efficiency: claims O(n^2 log n) complexity and scales much better than existing Bayesian nonparametric alternatives.
- Strength — probabilistic framework: provides a full Bayesian account enabling principled model selection and uncertainty quantification.
- Strength — hierarchical structure: naturally uncovers multi-level community organization, richer than flat detection methods.
- Strength — clear presentation and reproducibility: clear derivations, detailed algorithm, and specified hyperparameters/experimental details.
- Strength — empirical validation: strong experiments on Sampson and NIPS datasets demonstrating effectiveness and speed, with insightful qualitative hierarchy analysis.
- Strength — BHCD Sparse variant: practical consideration for real-world sparse graphs that improves performance.
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: limited exploration of the impact and robustness of α, β, δ, λ, γ.
- Weakness — tie-breaking randomness: random tie-breaking may induce variability; more principled strategies not investigated.
- Weakness — limited theoretical analysis: lacks deeper theoretical justification and convergence analysis for the greedy approach and locality assumptions.
- Weakness — missing comparisons to non-Bayesian methods: no evaluation against state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical algorithms (e.g., Leiden).
- Weakness — likelihood computation bottleneck: O(n+m) likelihood step could be a practical bottleneck for sparse networks; optimizations not explored.
- Arguments for acceptance: significant scalability gains, sound probabilistic framework, compelling experimental results, clear presentation, and addresses an important challenge.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient hyperparameter study, limited theoretical backing, and missing non-Bayesian comparisons.
- Overall assessment: strong, practical, and efficient contribution to hierarchical community detection; recommended for acceptance (rating 8.5/10).
