Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian procedure for learning hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a prior over tree-consistent partitions via a stopping probability πS at each internal node, and a blockmodel marginal likelihood that distinguishes on-diagonal (within-cluster) and off-diagonal (between-cluster) statistics with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli terms. Given a fixed tree T, the marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed exactly via a dynamic programming recursion that marginalizes over all partitions consistent with T in O(n+m). The hierarchy is learned with an agglomerative model selection scheme akin to Bayesian Rose Trees, evaluating local Bayes factors for three merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right) with cached sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time (times R restarts). A “sparse” variant forbids merging components with zero inter-edges. The method provides a simple recursive predictive distribution for link prediction by mixing along a path in the tree.

Experiments include qualitative structure recovery on Sampson’s monastery network, predictive performance and speed on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subgraph (compared against IRM via MCMC and greedy), and a large-scale hierarchy on the full NIPS graph (2864 nodes). BHCD is substantially faster than IRM and delivers predictive performance comparable to LFRM and better than IRM (worse than ILA), with the sparse variant particularly effective on sparse graphs.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Extends Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data by integrating a stochastic blockmodel marginal likelihood and a tree-induced prior over partitions. The exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions is elegant and contrasts with typical MCMC over partitions.
- Scalability and simplicity: The caching scheme and local Bayes factor enable practical O(n^2 log n) inference, independent of the number of communities, with likelihood computation in O(n+m). The sparse variant is a pragmatic and useful restriction for real networks.
- Practical utility: Produces interpretable hierarchies and fast link predictions; competitive predictive performance at a fraction of the runtime of standard nonparametric baselines.
- Clear modeling choices: The on/off-diagonal separation with conjugate priors is transparent and leads to closed-form updates and exact DP.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Positioning vs prior hierarchical network models: The paper does not compare against or cite hierarchical random graph models (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman, PNAS 2008) or other hierarchical SBMs, nor does it discuss differences in the induced generative assumptions and scaling/inference trade-offs. The IRM-with-tree extension [8] is mentioned but with limited empirical comparison.
- Evaluation breadth: Quantitative evaluation is limited to IRM; comparisons to ILA and LFRM are indirect via previously reported numbers. Including a direct run of ILA/LFRM on the same splits (or at least the released code baselines the authors cite) would strengthen claims about accuracy/speed trade-offs.
- Greedy inference and local scoring: The Bayes factor uses only local data among the merged trees; while this enables speed, it risks suboptimal global structure. There is no analysis of the impact of local decisions or of restart sensitivity beyond R=50. An ablation showing the effect of the three merge operations, γ, and tie-breaking would help.
- Hyperparameters and robustness: α,β,δ,λ,γ are fixed and not inferred. No sensitivity analysis is provided; prior choices can materially affect on/off-diagonal density contrasts and thus the hierarchy. Non-conjugate likelihoods are discussed only as future work.
- BHCD “sparse” variant: Forbidding zero-edge merges is intuitive but heuristic. Some networks have meaningful structural holes; a short discussion of when this restriction might hurt would help.
- Clarity details: A few notational points could be clarified (e.g., explicit treatment of undirected vs directed graphs and self-edges in σpq, cost of building/maintaining σ¬ch per node, and when prediction depth can be large). The claim that prediction is Θ(n) should be stated as linear in tree depth.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to γ and to the Beta hyperparameters? Can you provide a brief sensitivity plot or at least report performance across a small grid?
2. How often do absorptions vs joins get selected in practice, and how much do they contribute to performance vs speed? What happens if only joins are allowed (beyond the qualitative comments)?
3. Can you comment on memory usage for caching σ statistics on large graphs?
4. How does BHCD perform on networks with disassortative structure (e.g., bipartite-like blocks) given the asymmetric priors α>β, δ<λ?
5. Could you include a direct comparison against a hierarchical random graph or nested SBM baseline, at least on the small datasets?

Relation to prior work
- The paper is well grounded in blockmodels, IRM, LFRM/ILA, and Rose Trees, but it should also discuss hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008) and related hierarchical block models, clarifying differences in priors, likelihoods, and inference complexity.

Pros
- Elegant integration of tree-structured priors with blockmodel marginalization.
- Fast, scalable, and simple inference; clear path to practical use.
- Hierarchical outputs are interpretable and useful for exploration.
- Solid results vs IRM with significant speedups.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparisons; missing key hierarchical baselines.
- Greedy, local nature of inference with limited analysis of suboptimality or stability.
- No hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline-strong). While the methodology is not flawless and the empirical section could be broader, the central idea—a fast, exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions for relational data coupled with an efficient greedy search—is a meaningful and novel contribution. Given the clear speed/quality trade-off and the potential impact for scalable hierarchical community discovery, I believe it merits inclusion.
