Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model treats a hierarchy (a rose tree) as a prior over a subset of partitions consistent with the tree, and defines a marginal likelihood by analytically integrating out Bernoulli edge probabilities with conjugate Beta priors. A recursion akin to Bayesian Rose Trees (Blundell et al., 2010) allows efficient computation of the marginal likelihood for a fixed tree. Inference is performed via agglomerative model selection: at each step, the algorithm merges two subtrees by maximizing a local Bayes factor score computed only from the induced subgraph on the leaves of the merged node, considering three merge types (join and two absorptions). The method scales as O(n^2 log n) with caching, and the authors also introduce a sparse variant that forbids merging disconnected subtrees. For link prediction, predictive probabilities are obtained by a recursive mixture along the root-to-leaf path.

Empirically, BHCD is evaluated on Sampson’s monastery network and on NIPS co-authorship data (a 234-node subset and the full graph). Compared to IRM (both MCMC and greedy), BHCD achieves much better speed with reasonable predictive performance (comparable to LFRM, worse than ILA), and produces interpretable hierarchies on the full NIPS graph.

The work builds on stochastic blockmodels (SBMs), the IRM, Bayesian hierarchical clustering, and Bayesian Rose Trees. It is closely related in spirit to Clauset–Moore–Newman’s hierarchical random graph (Nature 2008), which also uses dendrograms with link probabilities and performs link prediction; this important line of work is not cited and should be discussed. More recent hierarchical SBMs (e.g., multiscale or nested SBMs) are tangentially referenced but not directly compared.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: a simple, conjugate, analytically tractable hierarchical mixture over tree-consistent partitions, with a clean dynamic program for p(D|T).
- Practical algorithm: a local Bayes factor–driven agglomerative procedure with careful caching yields good speed; the sparse variant is sensible in practice.
- Empirical impact: On NIPS-234, BHCD attains competitive predictive metrics much faster than IRM; full-NIPS hierarchy is large and qualitatively meaningful.
- Clear exposition of inference and prediction, and a useful discussion of computational complexity.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality:
- Prior specification inconsistency: The text states δ < λ for the between-cluster Beta(δ, λ) prior (to encode lower density across communities), but experiments fix α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2. This implies δ>λ, giving a high prior mean ~0.83 for between-community edges, contradicting the stated modeling intent. This needs correction or justification, plus a sensitivity analysis to hyperparameters (including γ).
- Modeling limitations: At any internal node, all between-child edges are governed by the same marginal likelihood g (shared hyperparameters), i.e., no pair-specific affinities at the same level. This restricts expressivity vs. a standard SBM where θpq varies per pair; the model is tailored to assortative communities and may struggle with more general block structures (e.g., bipartite or core–periphery patterns among children).
- Greedy, local objective: The Bayes factor in Eq. (12) uses only local data. While computationally attractive, it can be myopic and sensitive to tie-breaking. The paper uses multiple restarts but provides no guarantees or diagnostics for suboptimal merges. Synthetic experiments with known hierarchies would help quantify recovery and robustness.
- Complexity and memory: While per-merge updates are O(1) with caching, maintaining σIJ for all forest pairs implies O(n^2) memory and update traffic; this should be discussed quantitatively. The claim that likelihood can be computed in O(n+m) is plausible given cached subtree statistics, but the cost to maintain these during agglomeration deserves a clearer derivation and empirical memory/runtime profiles (including hardware).
- Evaluation: Comparisons are limited to IRM (plus literature references to LFRM/ILA). There is no direct comparison to hierarchical random graphs or other hierarchical SBMs that target similar goals. No synthetic validation or ablation (e.g., BHCD vs BHCD-sparse, binary vs non-binary) beyond brief commentaries. Runtime/accuracy plots should include error bars, hardware, and implementation details.

Clarity:
- Generally well written, with a coherent walkthrough of the model and algorithm. A few issues:
  - Missing or incomplete references (years/venues), and omission of the Clauset–Moore–Newman HRG line.
  - “BHCD global” appears in the Sampson discussion but is not defined earlier; clarify variants and the “out-of-date heap” issue.
  - The directed/weighted extension is alluded to but not specified; at minimum, note how σ and conjugacy would change.
  - Notation could be tightened in Section 3 (e.g., explicitly define σSS and σ¬chSS earlier and give an intuition).

Originality:
- The main novelty is to bring the Bayesian rose-tree framework to relational data via a tailored on/off-diagonal conjugate likelihood and a local Bayes-factor agglomerative search. This is a meaningful combination, but conceptually close to existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering and to HRG approaches. The difference from HRG (e.g., integrated-out Beta-Bernoulli, prior over partitions given T, three-way merge operations) should be made explicit.

Significance:
- The algorithm is fast and simple, and likely useful as a scalable baseline for hierarchical community discovery and link prediction. The empirical results suggest practical utility. However, the lack of comparisons to closer hierarchical baselines and the modeling limitations temper claims about advancing the state of the art.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix and justify hyperparameters; include sensitivity analyses for α,β,δ,λ,γ.
- Add synthetic experiments with known hierarchies to assess recovery and mis-specification (e.g., disassortative blocks).
- Compare against hierarchical random graphs and at least one hierarchical SBM; discuss when BHCD is preferred (speed, interpretability).
- Provide memory/runtime scalings with n,m; clarify the O(n+m) vs O(n^2 log n) discrepancy.
- Discuss probabilistic justification (or impact) of the “sparse” heuristic; could it be seen as a prior or constraint?
- Provide code and detailed experimental settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, fast, and practical Bayesian method with clear derivation.
- Competitive predictive performance at orders-of-magnitude lower runtime than IRM.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies; appealing for large sparse graphs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing key related work (HRG) and limited comparative evaluation.
- Prior inconsistency in reported hyperparameters; no sensitivity analysis.
- Restricted expressivity vs. general SBMs; greedy/local search may be brittle.
- Some clarity and reproducibility issues (variant definitions, references, resource reporting).

Recommendation: Weak reject. With corrected hyperparameters, stronger baselines (HRG/hierarchical SBMs), and added analyses (sensitivity, synthetic recovery), this would be a solid contribution.
