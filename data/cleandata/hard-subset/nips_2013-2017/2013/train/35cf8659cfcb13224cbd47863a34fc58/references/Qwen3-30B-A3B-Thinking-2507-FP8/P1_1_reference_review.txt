

# Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a gap in the literature: while previous work has focused on flat community detection (e.g., stochastic blockmodels), and some have explored hierarchical structures, most Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection are computationally expensive or limited in their applicability.

The paper builds upon the stochastic blockmodel (SBM) framework, extending it to a hierarchical mixture of SBMs where communities form a nested tree structure. The key innovation is a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithm that efficiently infers the tree structure through local model selection, with a worst-case runtime of O(n² log n) where n is the number of vertices. This is a significant improvement over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods (like the Infinite Relational Model [IRM] or Mondrian process), which typically require many iterations that scale poorly with the number of communities.

The authors position their work clearly in relation to prior research. They acknowledge that greedy hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., [3, 4]) are computationally efficient but lack a full probabilistic account, while Bayesian approaches (e.g., [5-12]) either have high computational costs or are limited to specific hierarchy depths. The paper's contribution is a method that combines the computational efficiency of greedy approaches with the probabilistic rigor of Bayesian methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Algorithmic Approach**: The paper introduces a genuinely novel inference algorithm that efficiently computes the marginal likelihood of hierarchical structures through dynamic programming, with the clever caching mechanism that allows O(1) updates during each merge step. This is a significant technical contribution that addresses a real computational bottleneck in Bayesian hierarchical community detection.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence of BHCD's effectiveness. On the NIPS co-authorship network, BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to IRM but is approximately 100x faster. The qualitative results on Sampson's monastery network also show that BHCD discovers meaningful hierarchical community structures.

3. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and intuitive explanations of the algorithm. The figures (Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate the key concepts.

4. **Practical Considerations**: The authors thoughtfully address practical implementation details, such as the sparse variant (BHCD sparse) for sparse networks and the handling of tie-breaking in the greedy selection process. The computational complexity analysis is thorough and well-justified.

5. **Comparative Analysis**: The comparison with other methods (particularly IRM) is well-executed, with clear benchmarks on both speed and predictive performance. The authors provide a helpful complexity table (Table 1) that demonstrates BHCD's scalability advantages.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the algorithm is well-motivated and empirically validated, the paper lacks deeper theoretical analysis. For instance, it would be valuable to have a proof of convergence or a theoretical guarantee about the quality of the solution found by the greedy algorithm. The paper states that BHCD "produces good results" but doesn't provide evidence of why the greedy approach is effective or under what conditions it might fail.

2. **Comparison with Non-Bayesian Methods**: The paper focuses primarily on comparing with Bayesian methods, but it would be valuable to include comparisons with state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods (e.g., [3, 4]) to better establish the relative strengths of the Bayesian approach. This would help clarify whether the Bayesian framework provides tangible benefits over simpler greedy approaches.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that they set α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4 as "working well," but doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the results are to these hyperparameters. A more systematic analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.

4. **Interpretability of Results**: While the paper shows the hierarchical structure discovered on the full NIPS dataset (Figure 5), it doesn't provide detailed analysis of why the hierarchy takes the form it does. More qualitative discussion of the interpretability of the discovered hierarchy would help readers understand the practical value of the method.

5. **Algorithm Details**: The paper states that "BHCD sparse" is faster for sparse networks, but doesn't provide clear guidance on when to use which variant. A more detailed explanation of the trade-offs between the variants would be helpful for practitioners.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Theoretical Justification**: Include a brief discussion of why the greedy approach is effective for this problem, perhaps by relating it to known properties of the likelihood function or by providing a small theoretical analysis of the greedy selection process.

2. **Expand Comparative Analysis**: Add a comparison with at least one state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection method to better establish the value of the Bayesian approach.

3. **Hyperparameter Analysis**: Include a small experiment showing how performance varies with different hyperparameter settings, particularly γ, which controls the tree structure.

4. **Improve Figure 5 Caption**: The caption for Figure 5 mentions "part of the hierarchy" but doesn't specify which part is shown or why that particular portion was selected for display. Adding this information would improve clarity.

5. **Clarify Computational Complexity**: While the paper states that the likelihood can be computed in O(n+m) time, the algorithm's complexity is O(n² log n). It would be helpful to explain why there's this discrepancy and how the caching mechanism enables the efficient updates.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of community detection by providing an efficient Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated on multiple datasets. The computational speedup over existing Bayesian methods is impressive and will likely make the approach widely applicable to larger networks.

The paper meets all the criteria for a NeurIPS publication:

- **Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental results are thorough and well-organized. The authors are careful to note the limitations of their approach (e.g., the greedy nature of the algorithm).

- **Clarity**: The paper is clearly written with good organization and effective use of figures. The mathematical formulations are precise and well-explained.

- **Originality**: The approach represents a novel combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering with efficient inference algorithms. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from previous work.

- **Significance**: The method addresses a real computational bottleneck in hierarchical community detection, making it practical for large-scale networks. The speedup of two orders of magnitude over existing Bayesian methods is significant and will likely enable new applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a solid contribution to the field of network analysis with clear practical advantages. While there are areas for improvement (particularly in theoretical analysis), the paper's strengths far outweigh these weaknesses. The method is likely to be widely adopted due to its efficiency and good performance.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel algorithm with significant computational advantages
- Strong empirical validation on multiple datasets
- Clear exposition and well-organized paper
- Addresses a real problem with practical importance
- Provides a clear path for practitioners to implement the method

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of the greedy algorithm
- Comparison with non-Bayesian methods could be more thorough
- Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is limited
- Some details about implementation could be clearer

Overall, the strengths of this paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
