This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to extend existing Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering by adapting them to relational data, framing the problem as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors claim significant speed improvements over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, particularly the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while maintaining comparable performance.

The paper is well-structured and the motivation for exploring faster alternatives to IRM-style approaches is clear. The connection to existing stochastic blockmodel literature is appropriately established. However, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough comparative evaluation against well-established, *non*-Bayesian, hierarchical clustering methods. While the speedup compared to IRM is highlighted, the authors don't convincingly demonstrate that BHCD outperforms simpler, faster alternatives like standard greedy hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., those cited in the introduction [3, 4]) *in terms of community quality*, especially given the additional Bayesian modeling assumptions.

A significant weakness is the limited scope of experimentation. The initial demonstration on Sampson’s monastery network is qualitative, and while visually informative, lacks quantitative evaluation. The comparison to IRM on the NIPS co-authorship dataset is more rigorous, but focuses solely on link prediction accuracy and runtime. This neglects other crucial evaluation metrics for community detection, such as Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) or modularity, which assess the quality of the discovered community structure directly, independent of a specific downstream task like link prediction.  Furthermore, the authors don't provide sufficient detail regarding the hyperparameter choices (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4) and justification for these values.  A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the results to variations in hyperparameters would strengthen the paper. 

The claim of a quadratic worst-case runtime is mentioned but not fully substantiated with a rigorous analysis – a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity in relation to network density would be helpful. The “BHCD global” variant and its struggles with coherent structure are acknowledged but not fully explained.  While the caching scheme is highlighted as a key optimization, its practical impact beyond runtime isn't fully explored.

The discussion of variations, such as the sparse versions and binary-tree restriction, is useful, but feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic exploration of the trade-offs between computational efficiency and community quality in these variants would be beneficial. The predictive probabilities calculation is presented but its actual impact on performance isn't sufficiently highlighted. The final section showcasing the hierarchy discovered in the full NIPS dataset lacks detailed analysis of the discovered communities and their relevance to the research landscape. Simply showing a portion of the tree doesn’t provide convincing evidence of the method’s utility in uncovering meaningful hierarchical structure.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem: Scalable hierarchical community detection is important.
*   Presents a novel approach: Combining Bayesian methods with greedy hierarchical clustering is a reasonable idea.
*   Demonstrates speedup over IRM: The reported runtime improvements are significant.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative evaluation: Lacks comparison to established non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods.
*   Insufficient quantitative evaluation: Relies heavily on link prediction accuracy and lacks community quality metrics.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity not addressed: Limited discussion of hyperparameter choices and robustness.
*   Limited analysis of discovered hierarchies: The NIPS full dataset example lacks in-depth analysis.
*   Unsubstantiated runtime claims: Need for more detailed complexity analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of convincingly demonstrating its superiority to existing methods. While the speedup compared to IRM is valuable, the lack of comprehensive evaluation and detailed analysis raises concerns about the practical utility and generalizability of BHCD. The paper feels somewhat incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by including more thorough comparative evaluation, conducting a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and providing a more in-depth analysis of the discovered community structures.
