

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper proposes a "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" (BHCD) method for inferring hierarchical community structures in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches [13,14] to create a greedy agglomerative algorithm that claims to be two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while maintaining comparable performance. The method uses a tree-structured prior on community partitions and computes marginal likelihoods via dynamic programming.

**Strengths:**
The paper is clearly written with precise mathematical formulations. The algorithm description is thorough, and the implementation details for the dynamic programming approach are well-explained. The comparison to existing methods on Sampson's monastery network is reasonably well-executed, and the empirical demonstration on the NIPS co-authorship dataset provides some evidence of the claimed speed advantages.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The paper's main claim of "two orders of magnitude faster" lacks rigorous validation. The runtime comparison in Table 1 is misleading - it only reports theoretical complexity (O(n² log n) for BHCD vs. O(mK²) for IRM) but does not provide actual runtime measurements across different network sizes. The experiments on NIPS-234 use a small subset (234 vertices), and the paper fails to demonstrate scalability to larger networks where the claimed advantages would matter most. The paper also lacks thorough analysis of how BHCD compares to established baselines on standard community detection metrics (e.g., normalized mutual information, modularity) - the comparison is limited to predictive performance on held-out edges.

*Methodological Soundness:* The authors claim to "build upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering" but fail to clearly articulate how their method differs from existing approaches. The "greedy" aspect of the algorithm is not sufficiently justified - the paper does not demonstrate that the greedy approach is theoretically sound or that it consistently finds better solutions than alternative approaches. The paper also lacks a proper discussion of when BHCD might fail, particularly for networks where hierarchical structure is not well-defined or where the data contains significant noise.

*Originality:* The core algorithm is a straightforward extension of previous work [13,14] with a novel caching scheme for efficiency. However, the paper does not clearly articulate how this represents a meaningful advancement over prior work. The authors cite [13,14] as the foundation for their approach but don't sufficiently differentiate their contribution. The paper's novelty appears to be primarily in the implementation details rather than in a fundamentally new methodological insight.

*Significance:* The claimed speed advantage is not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper does not establish that BHCD discovers more meaningful hierarchical structures than existing methods. The method is limited to hierarchical community detection, which is a narrower problem than flat community detection (the focus of most prior work). The paper does not convincingly argue why hierarchical structure is more valuable for most real-world applications than flat structure.

**Specific Concerns:**

1. The paper claims BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" but provides no actual timing measurements to support this. The table comparing time complexities (Table 1) is misleading as it only shows theoretical complexity, not empirical runtime.

2. The paper states that BHCD "produces good results" but does not provide evidence that the results are better than those of established methods. The comparison to IRM only shows predictive performance on a small subset of NIPS data.

3. The method's limitation to hierarchical structures (rather than flat structures) is not well-justified. Most social network analysis applications would benefit from flat community detection, and the paper fails to explain why hierarchical structure is the more important problem.

4. The paper does not address how BHCD handles sparse networks, which are the norm in social network analysis. While the authors mention "BHCD sparse" as a variation, they don't provide evidence that this variant actually performs better on sparse networks than established methods.

5. The paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of the method's properties. For instance, it does not discuss the convergence properties of the greedy approach or how the model selection criterion relates to the true hierarchical structure of the network.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a technically sound algorithm that builds on established work, the claims of significant speed advantages and improved performance are not sufficiently supported by rigorous experimental validation. The method appears to be an incremental improvement rather than a significant advancement in the field. The lack of thorough empirical validation across diverse network sizes and the limited comparison to established baselines on appropriate metrics make it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work.

The paper would benefit from: (1) actual runtime measurements demonstrating the claimed speed advantages across multiple network sizes, (2) comprehensive comparisons of BHCD to established community detection methods on standard metrics, (3) a clearer articulation of how the method differs from prior work, and (4) a stronger justification for why hierarchical community detection is more valuable than flat community detection for most applications.

Given the lack of rigorous validation for the main claims and the incremental nature of the contribution, I recommend rejection. The paper would be better suited for a venue focused on algorithmic efficiency rather than a top-tier machine learning conference like NIPS, where methodological innovation and empirical validation are paramount.
