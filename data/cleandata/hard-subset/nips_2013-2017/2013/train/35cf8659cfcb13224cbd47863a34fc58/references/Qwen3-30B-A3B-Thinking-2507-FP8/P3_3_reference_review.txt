

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

## Summary
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors extend Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data, building upon the stochastic blockmodel framework. The key contribution is an efficient greedy inference algorithm that computes the marginal likelihood of hierarchical partitions through dynamic programming with a novel caching scheme, achieving O(n² log n) time complexity. The method is empirically shown to be orders of magnitude faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant contribution to the computational efficiency of hierarchical community detection. The caching scheme for sufficient statistics (Equation 13) is particularly clever and well-executed, enabling the O(n² log n) complexity that the authors claim. This is a substantial practical improvement over existing methods like the IRM (which scales as O(n²K²)) or Mondrian process approaches. The experimental results on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data provide convincing evidence of the computational advantage, with BHCD achieving comparable predictive performance to IRM in 1/100th of the time.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, with clear connections to the Infinite Relational Model [5-7], Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14], and stochastic blockmodels [1]. The authors effectively acknowledge their position relative to previous work and clearly articulate the limitations of prior approaches (particularly computational inefficiency).

## Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper lacks theoretical analysis of the model's statistical properties. There's no discussion of consistency, convergence rates, or statistical guarantees of the greedy algorithm. While computational efficiency is valuable, the paper doesn't address whether the greedy approach finds the global optimum or how it compares to the full Bayesian model in terms of statistical quality.

2. **Incomplete Comparison**: The comparison with existing methods focuses primarily on computational complexity rather than predictive performance. The paper doesn't compare with other hierarchical community detection approaches beyond IRM, such as those in [3, 8, 11], which would better contextualize the contribution. For instance, [3] proposes a greedy hierarchical clustering method that is conceptually similar but lacks the Bayesian probabilistic framework.

3. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The Sampson's monastery network example (Figure 3) shows qualitative results but doesn't provide quantitative metrics to assess how well BHCD captures the known community structure. The NIPS experiments focus on computational speed and predictive performance but don't analyze the interpretability or quality of the discovered hierarchical structure.

4. **Ambiguous Claims**: The paper claims BHCD "finds the most likely tree" (Section 5.1), but doesn't provide evidence that the greedy approach consistently finds the maximum a posteriori tree rather than a local optimum. The tie-breaking procedure (Section 4.1) is described as random, yet the paper doesn't quantify how often different runs produce different hierarchical structures.

## Critical Evaluation

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results support the computational claims. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and limited statistical validation weakens the quality assessment. The paper is a complete piece of work but could be strengthened with more rigorous theoretical grounding.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, though some sections (like the tree representation in Section 3) could benefit from additional examples. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly clear.

**Originality**: The core innovation is the efficient inference algorithm with the caching scheme, which is novel and well-executed. The hierarchical extension of stochastic blockmodels isn't entirely new (see [8, 11]), but the computational approach is distinctive.

**Significance**: The computational efficiency represents a significant practical contribution to the field, making hierarchical community detection feasible for larger networks. However, the paper doesn't demonstrate that the method discovers qualitatively better hierarchical structures than previous approaches, which limits its theoretical significance.

## Recommendations
The paper makes a valuable contribution to computational efficiency in hierarchical community detection. While the theoretical depth could be improved, the practical utility of the method is compelling. The authors should:

1. Add a brief theoretical discussion of the statistical properties of the greedy approach
2. Include a comparison with at least one other hierarchical community detection method (beyond computational complexity)
3. Provide more detailed analysis of the hierarchical structure discovered in the Sampson network

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS due to its computational focus and relevance to network analysis. While not a breakthrough in theory, it addresses a practical bottleneck in an important problem domain with a clever algorithmic solution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical discussion and experimental comparison.

## Final Assessment
**Quality**: 8/10 (Strong technical contribution, limited theoretical analysis)
**Clarity**: 9/10 (Well-written and organized)
**Originality**: 7/10 (Novel algorithm, less novel model)
**Significance**: 8/10 (Practical computational improvement with clear applications)

Overall, this is a strong submission that advances the state of the art in computational efficiency for hierarchical community detection, with the potential to enable new applications on larger networks.
