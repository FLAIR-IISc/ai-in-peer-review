

# NIPS Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a key limitation in existing Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection: computational inefficiency. Building upon stochastic blockmodels and Bayesian hierarchical clustering, the authors propose a tree-based model where each internal node represents a community and leaves represent individual vertices. The key innovation is an efficient greedy inference algorithm that runs in O(n² log n) time, making it significantly faster than previous Bayesian methods (e.g., IRM) while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work, particularly the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches. It clearly articulates how BHCD differs from prior work, especially in terms of computational efficiency. The authors acknowledge that their method is a greedy approximation to the full Bayesian model, but demonstrate that this trade-off yields substantial speed improvements without significant loss in performance.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The model is well-formulated with clear mathematical definitions, and the inference algorithm is carefully derived. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity (O(n² log n)), which is significantly better than the O(n²K²) complexity of IRM (where K is the number of communities) or the O(n²F²) complexity of LFRM. The paper includes a thorough comparison of the theoretical complexity with existing methods in Table 1, which is a valuable contribution.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating BHCD's performance on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS co-authorship network. The results show that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to IRM but is approximately 1000 times faster. The paper also provides insightful visualizations of the hierarchical structures discovered.

One minor quality concern is the limited exploration of the method's theoretical guarantees. While the authors show empirical results, a more rigorous analysis of when the greedy approach would be guaranteed to find the optimal tree structure would strengthen the paper.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem and motivation, and the technical sections flow logically from the stochastic blockmodel to the hierarchical extension. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the figures (particularly Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate the model and results.

The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and well-structured, and the authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The paper also thoughtfully discusses variations of the method (e.g., BHCD sparse) and their trade-offs.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear original contribution by introducing a computationally efficient greedy Bayesian approach to hierarchical community detection. While the authors build upon previous work (stochastic blockmodels, IRM, Bayesian hierarchical clustering), they specifically address a key limitation of existing Bayesian methods—computational intractability for larger networks.

The key novelty lies in the combination of the tree-based hierarchical structure with a greedy inference algorithm that uses dynamic programming and sufficient statistics caching to achieve O(n² log n) time complexity. This is a significant improvement over the O(n²K²) complexity of IRM, and the authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work (e.g., [8] and [9] are limited to specific tree structures or depths).

The paper thoroughly reviews related work and clearly identifies how BHCD advances the state of the art.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Hierarchical community detection is a fundamental problem in network analysis with applications in social network analysis, scientific collaboration networks, and beyond. The computational bottleneck of previous Bayesian approaches has limited their applicability to large networks, and this paper directly addresses that limitation.

The empirical results demonstrate that BHCD can achieve comparable predictive performance to existing methods while being orders of magnitude faster. This makes hierarchical community detection feasible for much larger networks than previously possible. The paper's demonstration of the method on the full NIPS 2864-vertex co-authorship network is particularly compelling.

The paper also makes an important contribution to the understanding of the trade-off between computational efficiency and model complexity in Bayesian network analysis. The authors show that a greedy approach can yield good results without the need for expensive MCMC or variational inference.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clearly articulated novel contribution to a significant problem in network analysis
- Strong theoretical foundation with careful complexity analysis
- Well-designed experiments demonstrating substantial speed advantages without significant performance loss
- Excellent writing and organization
- Practical implementation details and algorithm description
- Clear comparison to relevant baselines

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited exploration of the method's theoretical guarantees (beyond empirical results)
- The random tie-breaking strategy (50 restarts) raises reproducibility concerns, though the authors address this
- Limited exploration of the method's performance on extremely large networks (beyond the 2864-vertex NIPS dataset)
- Minimal discussion of the sensitivity to hyperparameters (beyond the values used in experiments)

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a computationally efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community detection that is orders of magnitude faster than existing approaches while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The technical quality is strong, the writing is clear, and the significance of the contribution is substantial.

The paper is well-positioned to be of interest to the NIPS community, as it addresses a fundamental problem in network analysis with a novel and practical solution. The method's efficiency makes it applicable to larger networks than previous Bayesian approaches, potentially enabling new applications in social network analysis.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method would scale to networks with millions of vertices, beyond the 2864-vertex NIPS dataset. This would strengthen the argument for the method's practical utility.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) to help practitioners understand how to tune the method.

3. Consider including a brief discussion of the method's limitations for networks with very specific structural properties (e.g., networks where the hierarchical structure is not well-defined).

4. The paper mentions that "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap" in the Sampson's monastery example. A brief explanation of why this happens would help readers understand the algorithm's behavior better.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of network analysis. The computational efficiency of the proposed method is particularly noteworthy and will likely make it widely adopted by practitioners.
