Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian agglomerative procedure that builds a tree-structured hierarchy of communities for relational data. The model uses a stochastic blockmodel likelihood with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors, with different hyperparameters for within- vs across-community edges. A tree T induces a prior over tree-consistent partitions, enabling an exact marginalization over partitions via dynamic programming; the resulting recursion (Eq. 10) mirrors Bayesian hierarchical clustering/Bayesian rose trees. Inference is a bottom-up merge process with three merge types (join and two absorptions), scored by a local Bayes factor that only depends on data among the candidate merged leaves. With careful caching of sufficient statistics, the method runs in O(n^2 log n) time (per restart) and computes p(D|T) in O(n+m). A “sparse” variant forbids merging subtrees with no edges between them. The method is applied to Sampson’s monastery, a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset (compared to IRM), and qualitatively to the full NIPS graph.

The work is positioned against IRM and its hierarchical variants, Bayesian HRC (Heller & Ghahramani), Bayesian Rose Trees, and the Mondrian process. It is close in spirit to Bayesian rose trees extended to blockmodels. Related prior work on hierarchical structure in networks (e.g., Clauset et al. hierarchical random graphs) and hierarchical SBMs is not discussed.

Strengths:
- Clear probabilistic formulation that reuses well-understood conjugate blockmodel components.
- Exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions via a dynamic program is elegant and leveraged effectively for scoring/merging.
- Practical algorithmic engineering: local Bayes factor scoring and caching yields reasonable computational complexity; the sparse variant reduces work on sparse graphs.
- Sensible prediction procedure along the learned tree; computational cost proportional to tree depth.
- The qualitative structure on Sampson’s network is plausible; on NIPS-234, BHCD attains reasonable predictive performance quickly compared to an IRM implementation.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental. The main contribution is to plug a blockmodel likelihood (with on/off-diagonal f/g) into the Bayesian rose tree framework and adapt the merge scoring and caching; the inference structure is essentially that of BRT. Prior work on hierarchical random graphs and hierarchical SBMs is not compared or even cited.
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Quantitatively, the only direct baseline is IRM (MCMC and greedy). Claims of being “comparable to LFRM” and “worse than ILA” rely on reusing folds rather than direct side-by-side runs; this is not a fair comparison. There is no comparison to known hierarchical network models (e.g., HRG; IRM with tree augmentation; hierarchical SBMs), modularity-based dendrograms, or degree-corrected variants that often matter in coauthorship graphs.
- The “sparse” variant forbids merges with no inter-tree edges. This heuristic conflicts with the stated generative model (which allows off-diagonal g blocks to explain absent edges) and appears introduced for speed/quality without a principled derivation. The paper should either justify it probabilistically (e.g., via a hard prior) or treat it as a separate heuristic with ablations.
- No sensitivity analysis of the Beta hyperparameters or γ. The chosen α,β,δ,λ strongly bias denser within-block edges; results may be driven by these settings. There is no hyperparameter inference.
- Some claims appear overstated or inconsistent. The introduction claims “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM,” which is puzzling; later plots suggest two orders of magnitude speedup to reach similar predictive accuracy, not per-iteration. Clarify wall-clock measurements, hardware, implementations, and constants.
- Prediction complexity (Sec. 4.2) is said to be Θ(n); earlier it is described as linear in tree depth. It should be O(depth), not Θ(n); please correct.
- Scalability: while O(n^2 log n) is reasonable, the method still scales quadratically and includes an R-restarts factor; memory/time for large graphs is not detailed. The full-NIPS result is only qualitative; no held-out likelihood/AUC is reported.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Algorithm 1 is described at a high level; more precise pseudocode and complexity accounting (including data structures, memory) would help. References are incomplete/inconsistent, and some notational pieces (e.g., σ partitions for undirected graphs) could be clearer.

Quality:
Technically sound in its core derivations and consistent with prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering frameworks. However, deviations such as the sparse merge rule are ad hoc. The experimental support is insufficient for strong claims: limited baselines, minimal datasets, and lack of sensitivity analysis/hyperparameter learning.

Clarity:
Generally readable and well organized. Some notational/complexity statements need correction/clarification; references require fixing.

Originality:
Modest. The main idea is a straightforward and reasonable extension of Bayesian rose trees to a blockmodel likelihood with on/off-diagonal terms. Prior hierarchical network models are not adequately contrasted.

Significance:
Potentially useful as a fast heuristic for discovering hierarchical community structure and for quick predictions. Without stronger empirical validation against established hierarchical network models and larger-scale studies (with careful hyperparameter treatment), the impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add rigorous comparisons to hierarchical random graphs and hierarchical SBMs; include degree-corrected and mixed-membership baselines where relevant.
- Provide ablations: effect of the sparse restriction; binary-only vs non-binary merges; number of restarts R; tie-breaking.
- Do hyperparameter inference or at least sensitivity analyses for α,β,δ,λ,γ; consider non-conjugate likelihoods if performance claims hinge on flexibility.
- Report wall-clock, memory, and scaling on larger sparse graphs; exploit the O(n+m) likelihood to further reduce inference complexity (e.g., candidate pair pruning).
- Correct complexity statements and strengthen algorithmic details for reproducibility; release code.
- Clarify treatment of negative ties in Sampson (equating negatives to absent edges is questionable).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean probabilistic framing; exact marginalization via DP.
- Simple, fast inference with practical engineering; promising speed/quality trade-off.
- Clear, interpretable hierarchical outputs.

Cons against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty; key related hierarchical network work not addressed.
- Limited and arguably unfair experimental evaluation; weak baselines.
- Heuristic deviations from the model; lack of sensitivity analyses and hyperparameter treatment.
- Overstated claims and minor inconsistencies.

Overall recommendation: Reject. The approach is interesting and may be useful in practice, but the empirical validation and positioning w.r.t. established hierarchical network models are too weak for acceptance at this time.
