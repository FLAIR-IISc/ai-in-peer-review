## Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a new greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The method builds upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, leveraging a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The core contribution lies in an efficient inference scheme that allows BHCD to scale to larger networks – specifically, the authors claim it's two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM and has a worst-case runtime independent of the number of communities. The paper demonstrates the method on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS network, showcasing its speed and predictive performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on community detection. They clearly distinguish their approach from flat community models like the stochastic blockmodel and IRM, highlighting the limitations of these methods (requiring known number of communities, computational cost).  They also contextualize it against other Bayesian hierarchical methods, noting the computational burden of methods like [8] and the depth limitations of [9, 10, 11].  The connection to prior work on Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering [13, 14] is also well-established, clarifying how BHCD extends these approaches to relational data.

**Strengths:**

* **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength is the speed of the algorithm. The authors convincingly demonstrate significant speedups over existing Bayesian methods (IRM) on the NIPS dataset, particularly for sparse networks with the 'sparse' variant.  The claimed time complexity of O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) is attractive and supported by the experimental results.
* **Scalability:** The independence of worst-case runtime from the number of communities is a significant advantage, addressing a key limitation of many other approaches.
* **Principled Bayesian Approach:**  Using a hierarchical Bayesian framework provides a probabilistic account of the discovered structure, allowing for uncertainty quantification and potentially more robust predictions.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the algorithm is explained in a logical manner.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on three datasets, including the widely-used NIPS co-authorship network, provide evidence for the method's effectiveness. The qualitative results on Sampson’s network are insightful.
* **Practical Improvements:** The introduction of the ‘sparse’ variant of BHCD further enhances performance on sparse networks, demonstrating a good understanding of the data characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance is likely sensitive to the chosen hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ). While the authors state they found values that “worked well,” a more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results. Justification for the chosen values is minimal.
* **Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** The paper focuses primarily on comparison with Bayesian methods.  Comparing performance against faster, non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., those based on modularity optimization) would provide a more complete picture of BHCD's competitive position.
* **Predictive Performance:** While BHCD performs comparably to LFRM and better than IRM, the predictive performance isn’t dramatically superior. A more detailed analysis of *why* BHCD performs as it does, and where it excels or falls short, would be valuable.
* **Limited Depth of Hierarchical Structure:** While the method *can* discover hierarchical structure, the visualisations (e.g., Fig. 5) suggest the identified hierarchies aren't exceptionally deep or complex. It's unclear if this is a limitation of the method or a characteristic of the NIPS data itself.
* **Model Assumptions:** The reliance on the stochastic blockmodel, even within a Bayesian framework, is a strong assumption. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a discussion of the potential impact of violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of community detection. The primary strength is the computational efficiency of the proposed BHCD algorithm, making it a practical option for analyzing large networks. The weaknesses are relatively minor and don’t detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  The paper addresses a relevant problem, provides a novel and efficient solution, and demonstrates its effectiveness through empirical evaluation. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Significant speed improvements over existing Bayesian methods.
* Scalability to larger networks.
* Clear presentation and well-motivated approach.
* Empirical validation on multiple datasets.
* Practical improvements (sparse variant).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Lack of comparison to non-Bayesian methods.
* Predictive performance is good but not dramatically superior.
* Depth of discovered hierarchies is not deeply explored.



**Rating:**  **Accept (7/10)**

I believe this paper meets the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. While not groundbreaking in terms of theoretical novelty, the practical improvements in computational efficiency and scalability are significant. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.  Addressing the suggested improvements (hyperparameter sensitivity, comparison to non-Bayesian methods) in a revised version would further strengthen the paper.
