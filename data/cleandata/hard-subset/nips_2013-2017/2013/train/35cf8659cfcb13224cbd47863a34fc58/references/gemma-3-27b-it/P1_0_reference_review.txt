## Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and efficient greedy Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery (BHCD). The core idea of extending Bayesian greedy clustering to relational data is sound and addresses a significant need in network analysis – scalable inference of hierarchical community structure. While the paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, the experimental evaluation, though promising, could be more comprehensive and provide a deeper comparison against established Bayesian non-parametric methods. I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions, primarily concerning expanded experimental analysis and clarification of certain implementation details.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. Existing approaches fall into three main categories: stochastic blockmodels (parametric, requiring known number of communities), greedy hierarchical clustering (efficient but lacking probabilistic grounding), and Bayesian non-parametric methods (flexible but computationally expensive).  The authors propose BHCD, a greedy Bayesian approach building on previous work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, aiming to bridge the gap between efficiency and probabilistic rigor. The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing literature, correctly identifying the strengths and weaknesses of alternative methods like the IRM and Mondrian process. The connection to previous Bayesian greedy clustering work [13, 14] is clearly established, and the extension to relational data is well-motivated.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Efficiency:** The primary strength of BHCD is its speed. The authors convincingly demonstrate that it is significantly faster (two orders of magnitude) than a single iteration of the IRM and has a favorable runtime complexity (O(n^2 log n) or O(n+m) for sparse networks) independent of the number of communities. This addresses a critical bottleneck in applying Bayesian methods to large-scale networks.
* **Probabilistic Foundation:** Unlike purely greedy methods, BHCD provides a probabilistic framework for community discovery, allowing for uncertainty quantification and potentially more robust results.
* **Clear Methodology:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained. The formulation based on a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels is intuitive, and the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood is elegant. The explanation of the greedy model selection process is also very clear.
* **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide details regarding implementation and highlight the use of caching techniques to further improve efficiency.  The introduction of BHCD sparse targeting sparse networks is a sensible and practical extension.
* **Promising Experimental Results:** The experiments on Sampson’s monastery network qualitatively demonstrate the ability of BHCD to recover known community structures. The NIPS co-authorship network experiments show competitive performance compared to the IRM, with a substantial speed advantage.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the comparison with the IRM is valuable, it would be stronger with a more comprehensive benchmark against other state-of-the-art community detection algorithms, particularly Bayesian non-parametric methods like LFRM [18] or ILA [10] on a wider range of datasets. Simply reporting that BHCD’s performance is “similar” to LFRM is insufficient; a quantitative comparison is needed.  
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states hyperparameters were “found to work well” without detailing a sensitivity analysis. Discussing the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the results would strengthen the work.  Specifically, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of α, β, δ, and λ?
* **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The results on the NIPS data are presented graphically (Figure 4) without statistical significance testing. This makes it difficult to determine if the observed performance differences between BHCD and IRM are statistically meaningful.
* **Implementation Details:** While the caching scheme is mentioned, a more detailed description of its implementation (e.g., data structures used) would be helpful for reproducibility.  Also, the choice of R (number of restarts) is not fully justified.
* **Scalability to extremely large graphs:** While the O(n^2 log n) complexity is better than many alternatives, it might still be limiting for truly massive graphs (millions or billions of nodes).  A discussion of potential future optimizations to address this limitation would be valuable.



**4. Originality & Significance:**

The originality lies in the adaptation of Bayesian greedy clustering to relational data and the efficient implementation details (caching). It's not a completely novel theoretical breakthrough, but a clever and practical application of existing techniques to a challenging problem. The significance is moderate but potentially impactful.  Scalable Bayesian hierarchical community discovery is a valuable tool for analyzing large social networks, and the proposed method offers a compelling trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.  The ability to make predictions based on the learned hierarchy is also a significant contribution.

**5. Pro & Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a critical need for scalable Bayesian hierarchical community discovery.
*   Demonstrates significant speed improvements over existing Bayesian methods.
*   Well-written and clearly explained methodology.
*   Promising experimental results on benchmark datasets.

**Con:**

*   Limited experimental comparison to other state-of-the-art algorithms.
*   Lack of detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
*   Missing statistical significance testing.
*   Relatively limited discussion of scalability to extremely large graphs.



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include a quantitative comparison with LFRM and/or ILA on a wider range of datasets.
2.  **Perform a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the results.
3.  **Add statistical significance testing:**  Provide p-values or confidence intervals for the performance comparisons.
4.  **Provide more implementation details:** Elucidate the caching scheme and the justification for the choice of R.
5.  **Discuss scalability limitations:** Briefly address the potential limitations of the method for extremely large graphs and suggest possible future optimizations.



This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis, and addressing the suggested revisions will further strengthen its impact.
