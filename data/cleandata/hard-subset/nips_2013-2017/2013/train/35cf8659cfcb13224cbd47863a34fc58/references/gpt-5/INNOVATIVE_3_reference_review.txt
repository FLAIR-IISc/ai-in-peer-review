Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a scalable Bayesian procedure to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model combines a stochastic blockmodel likelihood (beta–Bernoulli with distinct within- vs between-cluster hyperparameters) with a tree-structured prior over partitions induced by a “stop/split” process (πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|). Critically, the tree defines a restricted set of partitions; the marginal likelihood over all tree-consistent partitions can be computed exactly via a dynamic program (Eq. 10). Inference is done by a greedy agglomerative procedure over a forest with three merge types (join and two absorptions), scored by a local Bayes factor; careful caching yields O(1) inner updates and an overall O(n^2 log n) per run, independent of the number of communities. Variants include a “sparse” restriction prohibiting merges with zero inter-tree edges. A simple recursive predictive scheme supports link prediction.

The work builds directly on Bayesian hierarchical clustering/Rose Trees [Heller & Ghahramani ’05; Blundell et al. ’10], extending the framework from i.i.d. vectors to relational data via a blockmodel likelihood and an off-diagonal term that depends on the tree structure. It contrasts with the IRM (flat partitions) and its hierarchical extensions, the Mondrian process for trees with relational likelihood, and latent-feature/attribute models (LFRM, ILA). Compared to MCMC/greedy inference in nonparametric block/feature models, the greedy model selection here is designed to be much faster. The paper also relates to hierarchical random graphs and hierarchical blockmodels (not cited), where hierarchical structure is modeled explicitly but typically with heavier inference.

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: an elegant extension of Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data via a hierarchical mixture of blockmodels, yielding exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Scalability: clear algorithmic choices (local Bayes factor scoring, three merge moves, caching) produce O(n^2 log n) behavior per run and constant-time inner updates. The “sparse” variant is practical for large sparse graphs.
- Practicality and interpretability: produces human-readable hierarchies and supports fast prediction via a simple recursion.
- Empirical evidence of speed: on NIPS-234, comparable link prediction to LFRM and better than IRM within seconds, with two orders of magnitude speedup (per authors’ plots) relative to IRM iterations.
- Independence from the number of communities and guaranteed termination after n − 1 merges are attractive engineering properties.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical validation and baselines: comparisons are primarily to IRM; no comparisons to hierarchical random graphs (Clauset–Moore–Newman, 2008), degree-corrected/assortative variants, or nested hierarchical SBMs. The full NIPS experiment is qualitative; additional diverse datasets and quantitative metrics would strengthen claims.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: fixed α,β,δ,λ,γ without sensitivity analyses or learning; the performance and discovered hierarchy may depend on these choices.
- Heuristic variants: the “sparse” no-edge merge restriction is reasonable but breaks the generative semantics; its effect on accuracy/consistency is not analyzed.
- Greedy nature and stability: reliance on random tie-breaking and R restarts is acknowledged, but variance across runs and the impact of the three merge types (ablations) are not reported.
- Some clarity/consistency issues: (i) predictive complexity is said to be linear in tree depth but later stated as Θ(n); (ii) “BHCD global” appears in the Sampson discussion without prior definition; (iii) citations have missing years and some references are incomplete.

Quality:
- Generally sound and well-motivated derivations. The dynamic program (Eq. 10) and scoring function are sensible. Experimental support is adequate for a methods paper emphasizing scalability, but the evaluation could be broader and more rigorous (stronger baselines, sensitivity, ablations).

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. Please fix the noted inconsistencies, define all variants, and improve reference formatting. A brief pseudocode block for Algorithm 1 would help.

Originality:
- Moderate to good. The core novelty is the specific integration of Rose Tree priors with a blockmodel likelihood for relational data and a tailored greedy/cached inference scheme. While based on known components, the combination and resulting exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions are nontrivial and practically useful.

Significance:
- Moderate. If broadly adopted, BHCD could provide a fast, interpretable Bayesian baseline for hierarchical community discovery and link prediction at scale. Stronger comparisons to hierarchical graph models would increase impact.

Questions for the authors:
- Sensitivity/learning of α,β,δ,λ,γ: how robust are results? Can γ be estimated (e.g., by maximizing marginal likelihood) during agglomeration?
- Ablations: how much do absorption moves help vs. join-only (binary) merges? Show performance and runtime trade-offs.
- Stability: report variance across R restarts and how R scales with n to achieve stable solutions.
- Sparse variant: does forbidding zero-edge merges hurt on noisy or partially observed graphs where communities may be weakly connected? Any theoretical guarantees?
- Directed/weighted/multi-graphs: how easily does the framework extend (e.g., to Poisson or Gaussian likelihoods) and what changes in the dynamic program?
- Relation to HRG/nested SBMs: please discuss differences and ideally include a baseline if feasible.

Minor comments:
- Clarify the Θ(n) vs O(depth) statement for prediction.
- Define “BHCD global” or remove.
- Ensure σIJ symmetry handling is explicit for undirected graphs to avoid double counting.
- Clean up and complete the references.

Recommendation: Weak Accept.
This is a thoughtful, fast, and interpretable Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery with a neat exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions and a carefully engineered greedy inference scheme. While the empirical section is not exhaustive and some variants are heuristic, the paper opens a useful, scalable direction. With minor clarifications and, ideally, a stronger comparison to hierarchical graph models, it would make a solid contribution.

Confidence: Medium.
