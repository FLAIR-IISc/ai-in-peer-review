### Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core contribution is a greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm where merge decisions are guided by a Bayesian model selection criterion. The method builds upon prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering (Heller & Ghahramani, 2005; Blundell et al., 2012) by extending it to relational data using a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The primary motivation is to combine the speed of greedy methods with the principled probabilistic foundation of Bayesian non-parametric models, thereby addressing the significant computational cost of methods like MCMC inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The authors demonstrate empirically that their method is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than a comparable MCMC-based approach, while achieving similar predictive performance.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic model is sound and well-formulated. The key idea of defining the model likelihood over a tree `T` by summing over all tree-consistent partitions (Eq. 6) is elegant. The derivation of the recursive formula for the marginal likelihood of a tree (Eq. 10) is a crucial component that enables the efficient greedy search, and it appears correct. The inference algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a direct adaptation of previous work on Bayesian agglomerative clustering, but its application to relational data, including the necessary caching of sufficient statistics for `O(1)` updates in the inner loop, is a solid piece of engineering.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
1.  The qualitative analysis on the classic Sampson's monastery dataset provides a good sanity check and demonstrates that the discovered hierarchies are interpretable and align with known social structures.
2.  The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 co-authorship network is the paper's strongest point. The authors compare against the IRM, a highly relevant baseline, on multiple metrics (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC). The results compellingly support their primary claim of achieving comparable accuracy at a fraction of the computational cost.
3.  The authors are careful and honest in their evaluation, noting that their method performs worse than the state-of-the-art Infinite Latent Attributes (ILA) model, which is appropriate given the trade-off BHCD makes for speed.
4.  The demonstration on the full NIPS dataset shows the method's applicability to larger networks.

A potential weakness is the reliance on fixed hyperparameters (`α, β, δ, λ, γ`). While common in such papers, a discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters or a strategy for their inference would have made the work more complete. The greedy nature of the inference is an inherent limitation, as it is susceptible to local optima. The authors acknowledge this by using random restarts, which is a standard and reasonable mitigation strategy.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
-   The introduction clearly situates the work within the existing literature, motivating the need for a fast, probabilistic method for hierarchical community detection.
-   The model is built up logically, starting from the standard stochastic blockmodel and then introducing the hierarchical extension in Section 3. The use of figures to illustrate the tree-consistent partitions (Figure 1) and merge operations (Figure 2) is very effective.
-   The algorithm is described clearly, both in text and via pseudocode. The explanation of the efficient caching of sufficient statistics is a key detail that is well-communicated.
-   The experimental setup and results are presented with clarity, and the plots (especially Figure 4) provide a powerful visual summary of the method's speed-accuracy trade-off.

The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

### Originality

The work is a novel and clever synthesis of existing ideas. The core concept of Bayesian hierarchical clustering using a marginal likelihood score for greedy merges is from Heller & Ghahramani (2005). The use of non-binary ("rose") trees is from Blundell et al. (2012). The use of stochastic blockmodels for community detection is classical.

The main originality lies in:
1.  The successful application and adaptation of this Bayesian agglomerative framework to relational data for community detection. This is a non-trivial extension that required developing the appropriate likelihood model (Eq. 2), the recursive marginal likelihood calculation for relational data (Eq. 10), and the efficient update scheme for the sufficient statistics.
2.  The formulation of the problem in a way that directly competes with, and significantly outperforms in terms of speed, established Bayesian non-parametric models like the IRM for this task.
3.  The introduction of the `BHCD sparse` variant, a simple but effective heuristic for sparse networks that is well-suited to the problem domain.

While not introducing a fundamentally new paradigm, the paper presents a novel combination of techniques that results in a new, highly practical algorithm for a well-known problem. Related work is adequately cited and distinguished.

### Significance

The significance of this paper is high, primarily due to its practical impact. Many Bayesian non-parametric models for network analysis are powerful in theory but often too slow for moderately large datasets. This work provides a pragmatic and principled alternative.
-   **Practical Utility:** Researchers and practitioners with networks of a few thousand nodes can use BHCD to quickly obtain a meaningful hierarchical clustering, a task that would be computationally prohibitive with MCMC-based methods. The claimed two-orders-of-magnitude speedup is a significant practical contribution.
-   **Advancement of the State-of-the-Art:** The paper advances the state of the art by demonstrating how to make Bayesian hierarchical modeling for networks computationally feasible without sacrificing too much performance. It provides a strong baseline for future work on scalable Bayesian network analysis.
-   **Building Block for Future Research:** The ideas presented could be extended in several directions, as the authors note in their discussion (e.g., improving sparse network handling, incorporating non-conjugate models). The work provides a solid foundation that others are likely to build upon.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
*   **Significant Speed Improvement:** The method is substantially faster than key Bayesian non-parametric baselines like the IRM, making hierarchical community detection practical for larger networks.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines and demonstrating the method's effectiveness on both qualitative and quantitative grounds.
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, technically sound, and presents a clear, compelling narrative.
*   **Novel Synthesis:** It represents a novel and impactful combination of ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering and network modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Greedy Inference:** The algorithm is greedy and finds a point estimate of the tree structure, forgoing a full posterior exploration and risking local optima.
*   **Fixed Hyperparameters:** The method relies on manually set hyperparameters, and their sensitivity is not explored.
*   **Complexity on Sparse Graphs:** The `O(n^2 log n)` complexity is not ideal for very large, sparse networks, where methods that scale with the number of edges `m` are often preferred. The proposed `BHCD sparse` variant is a heuristic that does not change the worst-case complexity.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable and practical contribution to the field of network analysis. It addresses a clear need for faster inference methods for Bayesian community detection models. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and backed by convincing empirical results. Despite the inherent limitations of a greedy approach, the significant computational gains make this a noteworthy advance that is likely to be widely used and built upon. It is a very good fit for NIPS.
