

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel approach for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a key limitation of existing Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods: their poor scalability. While previous work has focused on Bayesian methods for flat community structure (e.g., IRM) or hierarchical structure with high computational costs, BHCD proposes an efficient greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method that avoids the computational bottlenecks of prior approaches.

The key innovation is the development of a tree-structured prior on partitions that allows exact computation of the marginal likelihood through dynamic programming, combined with an efficient greedy agglomerative algorithm that leverages sufficient statistics caching. The authors demonstrate that BHCD achieves O(n² log n) time complexity, which is two orders of magnitude faster than IRM per iteration while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by presenting a method that significantly improves the scalability of Bayesian hierarchical community detection without sacrificing much predictive accuracy.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The theoretical foundations are well-explained, with careful attention to the generative process and marginal likelihood calculation. The algorithm is presented in pseudocode with sufficient detail, and the experimental validation on Sampson's monastery network, NIPS co-authorship network, and full NIPS dataset demonstrates the practical utility of the approach. The authors also provide thoughtful variations (BHCD sparse, binary restrictions) and discuss their trade-offs.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its bold approach to scalability. While many papers focus on improving predictive accuracy with more complex models, BHCD takes a different path: it proposes a simpler model with an efficient inference procedure that makes hierarchical community detection feasible for larger networks. This represents a significant shift in the design philosophy for these methods, prioritizing practical applicability over marginal improvements in predictive performance.

The paper's main strengths are its clear novelty, strong computational efficiency claims, and practical validation. The authors effectively demonstrate how their method bridges the gap between the theoretical elegance of Bayesian hierarchical models and the practical constraints of real-world network analysis.

Some minor weaknesses include:
1. The experimental comparison is primarily with IRM, but could include more recent methods for hierarchical community detection
2. The theoretical analysis of predictive performance, while good, could be more comprehensive
3. The paper doesn't deeply explore the interpretability of the hierarchical structures found

However, these are minor concerns compared to the paper's core contribution. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for tie-breaking in some cases) and present a clear path forward for future work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Hierarchical community detection has long been recognized as important for understanding complex networks, but previous Bayesian approaches were computationally prohibitive for practical use. BHCD makes this approach feasible for larger networks, potentially enabling new applications in social network analysis, biology, and other domains where hierarchical structure matters.

The paper stands out in the crowded field of community detection by offering a genuinely novel computational approach rather than incremental improvements to existing methods. It clearly distinguishes itself from previous work, particularly in terms of computational efficiency, while maintaining theoretical rigor.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel computational approach that addresses a significant limitation in Bayesian hierarchical community detection
- Strong empirical evidence of computational efficiency (2 orders of magnitude faster than IRM)
- Clear theoretical foundation with well-motivated prior and likelihood
- Practical applicability to real-world networks
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

Con arguments for rejection:
- Limited comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods
- Some theoretical aspects could be more thoroughly analyzed
- The predictive performance, while comparable, isn't significantly better than existing methods

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a scalable Bayesian approach to hierarchical community detection. As a reviewer who values bold, novel approaches over methodological perfection, I find this work particularly compelling. The authors have successfully bridged a critical gap between theoretical elegance and practical applicability in community detection, which is exactly the kind of contribution NIPS should recognize.

The paper meets all four key criteria for NIPS acceptance: it's technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), presents a genuinely novel approach (originality), and offers a significant practical contribution (significance). I recommend acceptance.
