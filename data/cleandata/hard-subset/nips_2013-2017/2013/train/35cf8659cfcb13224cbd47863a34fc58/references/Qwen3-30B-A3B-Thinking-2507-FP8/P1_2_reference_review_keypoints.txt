- Introduces BHCD, a Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery that uses a tree representation for relational data.
- Key innovation: an efficient greedy inference algorithm computing marginal likelihood in O(n² log n), far faster than IRM while matching predictive performance.
- Maintains Bayesian probabilistic interpretation and scales to larger networks, with a sparse variant for sparse graphs.
- Strong technical contribution: novel dynamic programming approach (Equation 10) for marginal likelihood computation.
- Clear theoretical foundation, linking the model to consistent partitions and Bayesian nonparametrics (e.g., Chinese Restaurant Process).
- Thorough experimental evaluation on Sampson’s monastery, NIPS subset, and full NIPS showing large speed gains and comparable predictive accuracy.
- Practical impact: computational complexity makes the method applicable to much larger networks than prior Bayesian approaches.
- Well-presented paper with clear notation and helpful figures illustrating concepts and results.
- Lacks comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods, weakening contextual evaluation.
- Uses random tie-breaking for equal-score merges without analyzing variability or providing principled selection of final hierarchies.
- Does not explore sensitivity to fixed hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) used across experiments.
- Insufficient guidance on when to use BHCD sparse versus the non-sparse variant in practice.
- Limited formal evaluation of hierarchical structure quality against alternatives (e.g., hierarchical NMI or similar metrics).
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses scalability, strong theory, clear empirical speed advantage, well-written, and practically useful.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing non-Bayesian comparisons and insufficient analyses of tie-breaking, hyperparameter sensitivity, sparse trade-offs, and hierarchy quality.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add non-Bayesian comparisons, analyze tie-breaking variability, include hyperparameter sensitivity, and clarify sparse vs. non-sparse usage.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original, significant, and clear contribution that meets NIPS criteria; recommend acceptance with suggested improvements.
