

# Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors propose a Bayesian nonparametric approach that organizes communities into a nested hierarchy, extending Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering. The key innovation is an efficient greedy model selection procedure that computes the marginal likelihood of the hierarchical structure using dynamic programming, resulting in an O(n² log n) time complexity, where n is the number of vertices. This is significantly faster than existing Bayesian approaches, which often require expensive MCMC sampling or have computational complexity that depends on the number of communities.

The paper builds upon the stochastic blockmodel framework, extending it to a hierarchical mixture model. The authors develop a greedy agglomerative algorithm that merges communities based on Bayes factor improvements, with three possible merger types (joining two trees, or absorbing one as a child of the other). The approach is evaluated on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS dataset, demonstrating that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art Bayesian methods while being orders of magnitude faster.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear technical contribution by developing an efficient algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. The caching scheme for sufficient statistics that enables O(1) computation per inner loop is particularly elegant and well-executed.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The claimed speed advantage (two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of IRM) is compelling, especially for large networks. The paper thoroughly addresses the scalability issue that plagues many Bayesian nonparametric methods.

3. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical notation and a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly well-structured.

4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments cover multiple datasets and compare with relevant baselines (IRM, LFRM, ILA). The authors provide detailed analysis of both qualitative (Sampson's monastery) and quantitative (NIPS co-authorship) results.

5. **Adequate Related Work**: The paper properly situates itself within the literature, clearly distinguishing its contributions from previous work on hierarchical community detection (e.g., [8-11]) and Bayesian methods (e.g., IRM [5-7]).

6. **Practical Considerations**: The authors address practical concerns like tie-breaking during merges, the sparse variant for sparse networks, and the binary tree restriction, showing careful consideration of real-world implementation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Non-Bayesian Methods**: While the paper compares well with Bayesian methods, it doesn't adequately compare with non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches (e.g., [3,4]), which might be faster than BHCD but lack probabilistic guarantees. This weakens the claim of being "the fastest" method.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper claims the method is efficient but provides no theoretical guarantees about the quality of the discovered hierarchy (e.g., approximation guarantees, convergence properties).

3. **Insufficient Speedup Quantification**: The claim of "two orders of magnitude faster" is somewhat vague. The paper should provide concrete timing comparisons for the specific datasets used in the experiments, not just claim the speedup.

4. **Limited Analysis of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges some limitations (e.g., binary tree restriction), it doesn't thoroughly analyze when BHCD might fail or what types of network structures it handles poorly. For example, how does it perform on networks with overlapping communities?

5. **Sparse vs. Non-sparse Variants**: The paper states that BHCD sparse runs faster on sparse networks but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of when the sparse variant should be preferred over the non-sparse version.

6. **Predictive Performance Details**: While the paper shows that BHCD performs similarly to LFRM and better than IRM, it doesn't provide enough detail on the specific predictive performance metrics (e.g., exact log predictive probabilities, AUC scores) to fully assess the quality of the results.

## Arguments for Acceptance

* **Strong Technical Contribution**: The caching scheme for efficient sufficient statistic computation is a significant technical advance that addresses a major bottleneck in Bayesian hierarchical clustering.
* **Practical Impact**: The speed advantage makes this method applicable to much larger networks than current Bayesian approaches, which is a significant practical contribution.
* **Well-Executed Experiments**: The experiments are comprehensive and properly contextualized against relevant baselines.
* **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to the NIPS community.
* **Addresses a Real Problem**: Hierarchical community structure is an important problem in network analysis, and the paper provides a practical solution.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: The paper lacks theoretical guarantees about the quality of the discovered hierarchies, which is a significant gap for a NIPS submission.
* **Vague Performance Claims**: The claim of "two orders of magnitude faster" needs more concrete evidence from the experimental results.
* **Incomplete Comparison**: The comparison with non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods is insufficient to fully validate the claim of being the fastest approach.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the limitations of the method.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The technical contribution is strong, the method is well-motivated and clearly presented, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the approach. While the paper has some limitations in its theoretical analysis and comparative evaluation, these are not severe enough to outweigh the significant contribution of an efficient, scalable Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of network analysis, particularly for practitioners who need to analyze large social networks where computational efficiency is critical. The method's ability to achieve comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art Bayesian methods while being orders of magnitude faster is a significant advancement.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include concrete timing comparisons (in seconds) for BHCD versus IRM on the NIPS datasets to quantify the "two orders of magnitude faster" claim.

2. Add a comparison with a non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (e.g., [3]) to better establish the relative speed of BHCD.

3. Provide more detailed predictive performance metrics (e.g., exact log predictive probabilities, AUC scores) in the results section.

4. Add a subsection discussing limitations and failure cases of the method, including what types of networks might be challenging for BHCD.

5. Consider adding a brief theoretical analysis of the approximation properties of the greedy algorithm, even if it's just a discussion of how the greedy approach might differ from the optimal solution.

6. Clarify the relationship between the binary tree restriction and the quality of the discovered hierarchies, as the paper states that non-binary hierarchies are "typically better" but binary versions "tend to produce better predictive results."

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent NIPS submission.
