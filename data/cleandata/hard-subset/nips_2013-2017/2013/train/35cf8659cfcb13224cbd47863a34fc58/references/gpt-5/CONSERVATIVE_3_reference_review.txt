Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), an agglomerative, greedy Bayesian method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model defines a prior over tree-consistent partitions via a rose-tree-style stopping probability πS and uses a conjugate beta-Bernoulli blockmodel with different priors for within- vs between-cluster edges. Given a fixed tree T, the marginal likelihood p(D|T) factorizes and can be computed exactly via dynamic programming. Inference greedily merges subtrees based on a local Bayes factor considering three merge types (join/absorb), using cached sufficient statistics to achieve O(1) per inner loop and overall O(n^2 log n) time. Variants include a “sparse” heuristic that forbids merging disconnected components. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery data and NIPS coauthorship show qualitatively sensible hierarchies and predictive performance competitive with IRM and LFRM (but worse than ILA), with significantly lower runtime; a large NIPS example illustrates discovered hierarchies.

Relation to prior work
BHCD extends Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering (BHC) and Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data by adopting a stochastic block model likelihood, and is conceptually related to hierarchical random graph models (Clauset et al., PNAS 2008) and hierarchical/nonparametric SBMs (e.g., IRM; nested SBMs/MDL). Compared to IRM-based hierarchical extensions and the Mondrian process, BHCD emphasizes greedy, scalable inference. However, the paper omits key comparisons to HRG and nested SBMs (e.g., Peixoto’s nested DC-SBM and efficient MDL-based inference), which already provide hierarchical structure with strong scalability and are widely used. Degree-corrected SBM variants (Karrer & Newman 2011) and more recent scalable Bayesian/nonparametric network models are also not discussed.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea: a tree prior that enumerates only tree-consistent partitions, enabling exact marginalization over those partitions and a simple dynamic program.
- Inference is simple, fast, and pragmatic; the caching scheme and local Bayes factor scoring are well described.
- The method provides hierarchical structure with interpretable internal nodes and efficient predictive computations.
- Empirical results demonstrate substantial wall-clock advantages over IRM while maintaining reasonable predictive quality.
- The paper’s exposition of the recursion and sufficient statistics is generally clear and technically correct.

Weaknesses
- Missing baselines: no comparison to hierarchical random graphs or nested (degree-corrected) SBMs with modern, highly scalable inference. This is a significant omission given the paper’s focus on hierarchical structure and scalability.
- Modeling restrictiveness: the use of a single conjugate g term for all inter-child edges at a node (via aggregated σ¬ch) enforces the same between-group rate across all child pairs at that level, which is a strong and potentially unrealistic constraint. It limits expressivity relative to SBMs that allow distinct between-block parameters or degree correction.
- Ad hoc “sparse” variant: prohibiting merges between components without edges improves results but lacks a principled probabilistic justification within the presented model (it effectively changes the prior/search space).
- Limited validation: no synthetic experiments to assess recovery of known hierarchical structure or robustness; no sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) or for restarts R; no ablations (e.g., join vs absorb).
- Claims vs details: the abstract/introduction claim “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of IRM” is confusing (faster than a single iteration?). Later claims (10s vs 1000s) are clearer, but hardware, implementation details, and fairness of comparison (including R restarts) are not fully specified.
- Clarity/consistency issues: “BHCD global” appears in the Sampson discussion but is not defined earlier. Some references are incomplete or contain typos.
- Lack of modern considerations: no discussion of degree correction, heterogeneity in degrees, or overlapping communities, all of which are known to impact community detection and predictive performance.

Quality
The core derivations (tree-consistent partition prior, likelihood recursion) are sound and align with prior BHC/Rose Tree formulations. The greedy inference is reasonable but lacks guarantees and relies on random tie-breaking and restarts. The sparse heuristic departs from a clean Bayesian story. The experimental support is limited in scope and missing critical baselines.

Clarity
Generally readable, with helpful notation and equations. However, some inconsistencies (e.g., “BHCD global,” complexity statements oscillating between O(n^2) and O(n^2 log n)) and incomplete references detract from polish. Figures and Algorithm details are referenced but not fully included in the text provided.

Originality
The main novelty is adapting BHC/Rose Trees to a relational blockmodel with an exact dynamic program and a tailored merge scoring procedure. While sensible, this is an incremental combination of established ideas; many related hierarchical network models exist, some with richer likelihoods and scalable inference.

Significance
The contribution is primarily pragmatic: a simple, fast approach that can provide hierarchical structure and decent predictions quickly. Without comparisons to stronger hierarchical baselines and without addressing degree heterogeneity, the broader impact is limited. As presented, it is unlikely to change practice in network modeling where nested/DC-SBM-type methods are standard.

Questions for the authors
- How does BHCD compare to hierarchical random graphs and nested SBMs (with and without degree correction) in both accuracy and speed?
- What is the empirical impact of the restrictive inter-child g assumption? Could you allow distinct g per child-pair or incorporate degree correction while retaining efficiency?
- Provide sensitivity analyses for α,β,δ,λ,γ and R. How robust are results?
- Can the “sparse” constraint be derived from a prior (e.g., forbidding merges when the posterior over g strongly favors zero rate)?
- Clarify the “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” statement and provide precise runtime environments and fairness details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to HRG and nested/DC-SBM with efficient inference; include synthetic recovery experiments.
- Explore degree correction and/or per child-pair parameters at internal nodes, or justify the current choice empirically.
- Provide ablations and hyperparameter sensitivity; report variability across restarts.
- Clean up references and clarify undefined variants (“BHCD global”), algorithm pseudocode, and complexity statements.

Minor issues
- Several typos and incomplete references (years/pages missing).
- Grammar issues (e.g., “The Mondrian process [12] propose…”).
- Notation spacing (e.g., “wherem n2”).

Recommendation
Reject. While the method is clear, fast, and potentially useful, the modeling assumptions are restrictive, the validation is limited, and key hierarchical baselines are missing. Strengthening empirical comparisons (especially to HRG and nested/DC-SBM), adding robustness analyses, and addressing degree heterogeneity would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence
High.
