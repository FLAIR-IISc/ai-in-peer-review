Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for relational data that discovers a tree-structured hierarchy of communities. The model starts from a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors but, instead of inferring a single flat partition, places a prior over all partitions consistent with a given tree (a “rose tree”), following ideas from Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees. Given a tree T, the marginal likelihood p(D|T) can be computed exactly via a dynamic program that mixes “on-diagonal” (within-cluster) and “off-diagonal” (between-children) marginal likelihood terms at each internal node, with a tree-structured prior on partitions controlled by a per-node stopping probability πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)|. The paper then performs agglomerative model selection over trees using local Bayes factors, permitting three merge types (join and two absorptions), and caches sufficient statistics so each inner-loop update is O(1), yielding overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant forbids merges between components with no observed edges. Predictive link probabilities are computed by a top-down recursion along a single path.

Experiments include qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network, a speed/accuracy comparison to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) on a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset (using the same folds as prior work on LFRM and ILA for quantitative comparison), and qualitative structure on the full NIPS 1–17 graph (2864 nodes). BHCD reaches reasonable predictive accuracy orders of magnitude faster than IRM, performs similarly to LFRM and worse than ILA (per authors’ comparison), and yields interpretable hierarchies on real data.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian rose trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010), extending them from i.i.d. data to relational matrices via a blockmodel factorization.
- Relates to the IRM (Kemp et al. 2006) and its hierarchical extensions, but uses a greedy model selection and an exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions for a fixed tree.
- Close in spirit to hierarchical random graph/dendrogram models for networks (e.g., Clauset, Moore, Newman, PNAS 2008), which infer a dendrogram with edge probabilities at internal nodes via MCMC; BHCD differs by using only two likelihood components (f for within, g for between-children) and a different tree prior, enabling a fast greedy search.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound probabilistic construction: the factorization of p(D|φ,T) across subtrees and ancestors, and the dynamic programming recursion for p(D|T), are clear and plausible.
- Efficient algorithmic design: local Bayes factor scoring, careful caching of block sufficient statistics, and a well-analyzed O(n^2 log n) time complexity. The sparse variant is practically motivated.
- Predictive evaluation with cross-validation and wall-clock comparisons shows strong speed advantages.

Weaknesses:
- Model expressiveness is limited: only two global likelihoods f and g are used across the entire tree (no per-internal-node parameters), which may be too inflexible for heterogeneous networks and limits comparison to HRG-style models.
- The merge score is entirely local. While computationally attractive, it risks myopic decisions; no analysis of suboptimality or safeguards (beyond restarts) is provided.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; no sensitivity analysis or learning of γ is attempted. The choice of πS from rose trees is imported without justification for networks.
- The “sparse” variant forbidding merges with zero observed edges may preclude discovering legitimate structures (e.g., bipartite patterns or missing-data regimes). A discussion of when this is appropriate would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with concrete definitions of σSS and σ¬chSS and a clear derivation of the recursion (Eq. 10).
- Some notational points could be clarified: explicitly state directed/weighted generalizations and the exact symmetry handling; ensure Algorithm 1 pseudocode is fully specified (only described in prose here).
- There is an inconsistency: predictive complexity is said to be linear in depth but later described as Θ(n); it should be O(depth).
- The mention of “BHCD global” in the Sampson section is unexplained.
- Provide details on train/validation splits, held-out protocol, and hardware for time comparisons; ideally release code to support reproducibility.

Originality
- The combination of rose-tree style priors with a blockmodel marginal likelihood for relational data and a tailored agglomerative search is, to my knowledge, novel.
- Prior Bayesian hierarchical models for networks exist (IRM extensions; HRG), but the proposed inference is much simpler and faster. The paper would benefit from a more explicit contrast to HRG.

Significance
- The main contribution is a scalable, simple-to-implement method for hierarchical community discovery with exact marginalization for a fixed tree and fast greedy search. This is practically useful for exploratory analysis and rapid link prediction at scale.
- The empirical results indicate competitive predictive performance per unit time, though absolute performance lags the best latent attribute models.
- If adopted, BHCD could serve as a strong baseline for large graphs where MCMC-based nonparametric methods are too slow.

Pros
- Clear probabilistic model and exact DP for p(D|T).
- Very fast agglomerative search with sensible engineering (caching, heap).
- Empirical speedups of 100x over IRM while achieving reasonable prediction quality.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies on real networks.

Cons
- Limited expressiveness (global f/g, no per-node parameters); no hyperparameter learning or sensitivity study.
- Local, greedy merges without theoretical guarantees; reliance on restarts and tie-breaking.
- Sparse variant may disallow true structures; conditions for its use are not analyzed.
- Missing key related work discussion (e.g., hierarchical random graphs) and no empirical comparison to such models.
- Some clarity issues and minor inconsistencies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare and contrast more directly with HRG (Clauset et al., PNAS 2008) and, if feasible, provide an empirical comparison on at least one dataset.
- Add a sensitivity analysis for γ and the Beta hyperparameters; consider simple empirical Bayes or cross-validation to set them.
- Discuss memory complexity and practical limits (caching σIJ is O(n^2)).
- Clarify predictive complexity as O(depth), define all variants (e.g., “BHCD global”), and include full pseudocode in the appendix.
- Consider a middle ground between BHCD and HRG: per-internal-node parameters with conjugate priors, possibly with shrinkage, to improve expressiveness while retaining DP structure locally.
- Evaluate on synthetic data with known hierarchical structure to quantify recovery and the effect of the sparse constraint.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a neat, well-motivated, and very fast Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery with clear practical value. With stronger related work positioning, clarity fixes, and some analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity/expressiveness, it would be a solid contribution.
