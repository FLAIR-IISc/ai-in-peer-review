Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast greedy Bayesian method to infer hierarchical community structure in graphs. The model builds a hierarchy (tree) where each internal node corresponds to a community, and leaves are vertices. Conditioned on a given tree T, the marginal likelihood over all partitions consistent with T is computed exactly via dynamic programming. The likelihood is based on a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate beta–Bernoulli priors, using different hyperparameters for within- vs between-cluster edges (assortative prior). A tree-structured prior over partitions arises from a generative process that either “stops” at a node (using within-block likelihood f) with probability πS or “splits” (using an off-diagonal likelihood g between children) with probability 1 − πS. The resulting recursion yields p(D|T) in O(n + m) given T.

To learn T, the authors use a greedy agglomerative procedure akin to Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010): iteratively merge two subtrees among three merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right), choosing the locally best Bayes factor score computed from local sufficient statistics. Careful caching enables O(1) per candidate update and an overall O(n^2 log n) algorithm. Variants include “sparse BHCD” (forbidding merges between subtrees with zero inter-edges) and binary-only merges. A simple recursive predictive distribution enables link prediction by traversing a path from the root.

Experiments: (i) Sampson’s monastery network (qualitative structure), (ii) NIPS-234 coauthorship subset: BHCD vs IRM (MCMC and greedy) on held-out link prediction (log predictive probability, AUC, accuracy) showing BHCD reaches reasonable accuracy orders of magnitude faster; performance comparable to LFRM and below ILA as reported in prior work, (iii) full NIPS 1–17 (2,864 nodes), demonstrating an interpretable hierarchy.

The work relates to IRM and hierarchical extensions (Kemp et al.; Herlau et al.), greedy hierarchical clustering for networks (e.g., modularity-based methods), and Bayesian tree priors (Bayesian Rose Trees, Mondrian process). Compared to hierarchical IRM-like models, BHCD trades some modeling flexibility for speed and scalability.

Strengths
- A clear, principled probabilistic model that ties a tree prior on partitions to blockmodel likelihood with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Elegant dynamic programming recursion for p(D|T) and a practical greedy agglomerative search with local Bayes factor scoring and three merge types.
- Strong emphasis on scalability with a careful caching scheme; time complexity O(n^2 log n) and per-tree likelihood O(n + m).
- Empirical evidence that BHCD reaches useful predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than an IRM implementation on a standard benchmark; interpretable hierarchies on a large coauthorship network.
- Variants (sparse, binary) are sensible and helpful in practice.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is relatively limited: two datasets (one small, one moderate) and comparisons mainly to IRM, with indirect comparisons to LFRM/ILA via prior reports. Missing comparisons to other hierarchical community models (e.g., hierarchical random graphs) and strong non-Bayesian baselines (e.g., Louvain with multi-level structure).
- The greedy local merge objective is a heuristic for global model selection; no analysis of approximation quality, stability, or sensitivity of the resulting hierarchies. The need for multiple restarts underscores this.
- The assortative Beta-Bernoulli SBM may be brittle under degree heterogeneity or disassortative structure; no degree-correction or mixed-membership extension is considered.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter learning.
- Some clarity/consistency issues: predictive complexity is said to be “linear in the depth” and then “Θ(n)”; the latter should be O(depth), typically much smaller than n. References are incomplete and contain typos.

Quality
- Technically sound at a high level: the recursion (Eq. 10), local Bayes factor (Eq. 12), and sufficient-statistics updates are coherent. The dynamic programming argument is plausible given the factorization.
- The inference is a greedy search; the paper is transparent about this, but lacks theoretical guarantees or diagnostics beyond restarts.
- Experiments support the speed claims and provide reasonable predictive metrics; however, broader and fairer comparisons would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-written; the model and algorithm are explained clearly with equations and algorithmic structure. Some notation is dense; adding a brief worked example of the recursion would help.
- A few minor issues: incomplete references, typos, and the predictive complexity statement.

Originality
- The main novelty is adapting Bayesian Rose Trees/greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data with a blockmodel likelihood and a tree-induced prior over partitions enabling exact marginalization. This is a neat and useful combination; methodological novelty is moderate but impactful.

Significance
- The contribution is practically useful: fast, interpretable hierarchical community detection with predictive capabilities. While not state-of-the-art in predictive performance, the speed/interpretability trade-off is attractive and likely to be adopted.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please compare to hierarchical random graph models (e.g., Clauset et al., PNAS 2008) and to strong scalable community methods that yield hierarchies (e.g., Louvain’s multilevel dendrogram), at least qualitatively and on runtime/fit.
- Discuss and, if possible, evaluate degree-corrected likelihoods; degree heterogeneity can confound blockmodels.
- Add a brief sensitivity analysis for γ and the Beta hyperparameters; or perform empirical Bayes.
- Clarify memory complexity: storing all σIJ for candidate merges is O(n^2) memory; is this a practical bottleneck?
- Correct the predictive complexity statement: it should be O(tree depth).
- Consider reporting variability across restarts and a consensus tree construction.
- Ensure references are complete and correct; fix typos.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant probabilistic formulation with exact marginalization given T.
- Simple, scalable inference with strong empirical speed advantages.
- Produces interpretable hierarchies and reasonable predictions.
- Extends well-known Bayesian hierarchical clustering ideas to networks.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth and missing key hierarchical baselines.
- Greedy search without guarantees; sensitivity/restarts not deeply analyzed.
- Modeling simplicity (assortative, non-degree-corrected) may limit applicability.
- Some clarity and presentation issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The paper presents a useful and well-executed scalable method with clear contributions and promising empirical behavior; strengthening comparisons and clarifications would make it even more compelling.

Reviewer confidence: High.
