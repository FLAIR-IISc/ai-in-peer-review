Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian procedure to infer hierarchical community structure in networks. The model starts from a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate beta-Bernoulli marginals and defines a hierarchy T over vertices. A key idea is to restrict the sum over partitions in the marginal likelihood to partitions that are consistent with T, and to place a tree-structured prior over these partitions via a “stop or split” process with node-specific stopping probability πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| (in the spirit of Bayesian Rose Trees and Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering). Given T, the marginal likelihood p(D|T) obeys a dynamic programming recursion combining an on-diagonal term f(·) and off-diagonal term g(·). The authors then perform bottom-up agglomerative model selection: at each step they merge two trees using one of three merge operators (join or two kinds of absorption), scoring candidates via a local Bayes factor. With appropriate caching of sufficient statistics, scoring is O(1) per candidate, giving O(n^2 log n) inference. A “sparse” variant forbids merging subtrees with no interconnecting edges. They also derive a recursive predictive distribution for link prediction. Experiments include qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery, predictive comparisons to IRM (Gibbs and greedy) on NIPS-234 coauthorship, and a large hierarchy on the full NIPS coauthorship (n≈2.8k).

This work is closely related to BHC/Bayesian Rose Trees [Heller & Ghahramani; Blundell et al.], extending them from i.i.d. vectors to relational data via blockmodel marginals. It also connects to IRM and its hierarchical extensions, the Mondrian process for trees with relational likelihoods, and to hierarchical community detection (e.g., Clauset–Moore–Newman’s Hierarchical Random Graph). Compared to MCMC/variational inference in IRM-like models, the contribution is a faster, greedy, tree-constrained approach with predictive capabilities.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: a clear adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to blockmodel data with a well-defined recursion and local Bayes factors.
- Speed: Greedy agglomeration with cached sufficient statistics results in practical scalability; the sparse variant further helps in sparse graphs.
- Predictive distribution: A simple, interpretable recursion for link prediction along a path in the inferred tree.
- Empirical demonstration: Reasonable qualitative structure on Sampson; competitive predictive performance vs IRM on NIPS-234 at substantially lower wall-clock time; a large-scale hierarchy on full NIPS.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Prior inconsistency: The paper motivates β-Bernoulli hyperparameters with α>β for within-cluster and δ<λ for between-cluster (to encourage within>between density), but then uses α=δ=1.0 and β=λ=0.2 in experiments, making within and between priors identical and, moreover, with high mean (≈0.83). This contradicts the stated modelling intention and undermines the claim that priors encode community assumptions. At minimum, a sensitivity analysis or learning of these hyperparameters is needed.
- Baselines: The comparison is limited to IRM; more relevant baselines for hierarchical community structure include Hierarchical Random Graph (Clauset–Moore–Newman, PNAS 2008) and more recent hierarchical blockmodels (even if post hoc for some versions), or at least a discussion of differences. The cited ILA/LFRM results are not directly replicated (only compared via previously reported numbers), making fairness hard to assess.
- Claims vs evidence: The statement that BHCD is “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” seems implausible as written; elsewhere the text says BHCD produces good predictions before a single Gibbs sweep completes. Please clarify these claims, report hardware, implementation details, and comparable stopping criteria.
- Objective and guarantees: The greedy procedure optimizes local Bayes factors; there is no discussion of consistency, approximation quality, or when the local criterion might mislead (e.g., due to myopic merges). Ties are broken randomly with R restarts, but sensitivity to R and variance across runs are not reported.
- Modeling limitations: The likelihood uses a single pair of f/g across all nodes and levels; it cannot capture heterogeneity across communities or depth-specific sparsity/density without hyperparameter learning. The “sparse” variant forbids merging components without edges, which may be sensible but could hurt in the presence of missing data or for disassortative/bipartite structures; this trade-off isn’t analyzed.
- Clarity gaps: “BHCD global” is referenced in the Sampson figure discussion but not defined earlier. Some notation (e.g., DTT) is terse; more guidance to reproduce dynamic-programming computations and caching would help.

Quality
Technically sound in its core derivations (the recursion mirrors BHC/rose trees). However, the hyperparameter inconsistency is a serious issue. The experiments are suggestive but limited. Reporting lacks detail needed for exact reproducibility (code availability, hardware, runtime per phase, number of restarts vs performance variance, sensitivity to γ and Beta priors).

Clarity
Generally clear, with helpful equations and algorithmic description. Recommendations:
- Define all algorithm variants clearly (BHCD, BHCD sparse, “global”, binary-only) before experiments.
- Provide pseudocode or a more explicit description of cached sufficient statistics and memory costs.
- Clarify the complexity of the sparse variant in terms of m, not only worst-case.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is adapting Bayesian rose trees to stochastic blockmodel marginals and designing an efficient local scoring/caching scheme. This is a principled, useful combination rather than a fundamentally new model.

Significance
Moderate. A fast, scalable Bayesian approach to hierarchical communities can be valuable for exploratory analysis and link prediction on medium-sized graphs. Broader impact would be strengthened by stronger baselines, hyperparameter learning, and larger-scale evaluations.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, fast method with clear Bayesian semantics.
- Practical recursion for predictions and reasonable empirical performance at low cost.
- Bridges BHC to relational data in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance
- Hyperparameter inconsistency with stated modeling assumptions.
- Limited and partially incomparable baselines; unclear fairness of runtime claims.
- No analysis of sensitivity to restarts/γ or discussion of failure modes.
- Modeling expressivity limited by shared f/g across hierarchy and fixed priors.

Questions for authors
1. Please clarify and correct the prior settings: why set α=δ and β=λ if the intent is α>β and δ<λ? Provide sensitivity analyses and/or hyperparameter learning.
2. What is the precise hardware, implementation details, and stopping criteria underlying the runtime plots? How many candidates are scored per step?
3. Can you compare against Hierarchical Random Graph (Clauset–Moore–Newman) or discuss differences empirically?
4. How sensitive are results to γ and to the number of restarts R? What variance is observed across runs?
5. Does forbidding merges without edges harm performance on disassortative or bipartite graphs or when data is missing?
6. Can the model accommodate level-specific or node-specific parameters (e.g., different f/g per node) without losing tractability?

Minor issues
- Several citation/placeholders are incomplete; ensure full bibliographic entries.
- Define “BHCD global” and “binary” variants earlier.
- Clarify that p(D|T) can be computed in O(n+m) given precomputed sufficient statistics, and specify memory usage.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and the method is likely useful, but the inconsistent priors, limited baselines, and clarity/experimental gaps should be addressed to meet NIPS standards. I would be inclined to accept a revised version that rectifies these issues. Confidence: medium.
