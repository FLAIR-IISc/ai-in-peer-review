Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for relational data. The model treats a tree T over vertices as a prior over a restricted set of partitions Φ(T); the marginal likelihood p(D|T) sums exactly over these tree-consistent partitions using on-diagonal and off-diagonal marginal likelihoods (f, g) of a blockmodel with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors. A dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) enables efficient evaluation of p(D|T). The hierarchy is learned via bottom-up agglomerative model selection that considers three merge types (join vs. two absorptions), scored by a local Bayes factor (Eq. 12) computable from cached sufficient statistics. A “sparse” variant forbids merges between disconnected trees. Link prediction is performed by a top-down mixture along a root-to-leaf path (Eq. 14). Experiments on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks show qualitative hierarchies and competitive predictive performance relative to IRM, at notably lower runtime; performance is similar to LFRM and below ILA.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds directly on Bayesian hierarchical clustering and Bayesian Rose Trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010), replacing i.i.d. data likelihoods with a stochastic blockmodel marginal and redefining f/g to handle within/between-block edges. Compared with IRM/hIRM (Kemp et al.; Herlau et al.), BHCD trades full posterior inference for greedy model selection and a restricted prior over partitions defined by a learned tree, yielding large speedups. The method is distinct from classical divisive/greedy hierarchical clustering in graphs (e.g., Girvan-Newman), and from flat Bayesian nonparametric link models (LFRM; ILA). A discussion of hierarchical random graphs/hierarchical SBMs (e.g., Clauset-Moore-Newman’s dendrogram model) would strengthen positioning, as would clarifying differences from Mondrian process-based relational models.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation: exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions with a well-motivated prior on “stopping” probabilities πS, and an interpretable dynamic program.
- Efficient and simple inference: local Bayes factor merges with O(1) updates via cached sufficient statistics; worst-case O(n^2 log n), independent of the number of communities, and a sparse variant for real-world networks.
- Practicality: C implementation, restarts for robustness, and a straightforward predictive distribution along the hierarchy.
- Empirical evidence: qualitatively sensible structures on Sampson; competitive link prediction on NIPS-234 vs. IRM (and by proxy comparable to LFRM), at much lower wall-clock time; demonstration on the full NIPS network.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling assumptions limit expressivity:
  - Beta-Bernoulli blockmodel without degree correction will struggle on networks with high degree heterogeneity, assortative/disassortative mixtures, or overlapping memberships. This likely explains the gap to ILA and other SOTA methods on link prediction.
  - Single global hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) fixed a priori; no hyperparameter learning or sensitivity analysis. Results may be sensitive to these settings, especially γ which shapes arity.
- Search and identifiability:
  - Greedy local merges (and local scoring) can be myopic; no guarantees on approximation quality. Multiple restarts partially address this but also indicate instability. There is no ablation on the effect of restarts, tie-breaking, or merge-type choices.
  - The sparse variant forbids merges with zero observed edges. While pragmatic, this can be problematic under missing-not-at-random observation or for bipartite/disassortative structures.
- Evaluation methodology:
  - The claim “two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration” is surprising and needs clarification; later plots compare wall-clock time, which is appropriate, but the text should avoid iteration-level comparisons across algorithms with different iteration semantics.
  - Comparisons omit hierarchical generative baselines (e.g., hierarchical random graphs/dendrogram models; hierarchical SBMs). IRM is flat; ILA and LFRM are stronger baselines but comparisons are indirect (via reused splits and external numbers).
  - No uncertainty quantification around predictive metrics (e.g., error bars) and limited detail (held-out fraction, missingness handling for Sampson, number of restarts per fold).
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Algorithm 1 is referenced but not fully presented in the text; including pseudocode would help reproducibility.
  - Some notation is dense; e.g., σ¬chSS deserves a small example to avoid double-counting confusion.
  - References are incomplete and a few key related works are missing or under-discussed.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The recursion (Eq. 10) and the Bayes factor (Eq. 12) are consistent with the generative story and with the rose tree literature. The caching scheme for O(1) inner-loop updates is a useful practical contribution. The experimental evidence supports the speed claims and shows reasonable predictive accuracy, though broader benchmarks and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Mostly clear, but could benefit from: (i) explicit pseudocode; (ii) a worked toy example of the dynamic program and merge scoring; (iii) clearer experimental protocol details; (iv) improved references.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is adapting Bayesian rose trees to relational data via blockmodel marginals and engineering the inference to be fast. The conceptual combination is natural but useful.

Significance
Practical significance is good: a fast, interpretable, hierarchical community discovery tool with competitive predictions and scalable runtime. Theoretical significance is more modest due to the simplicity of the likelihood and greedy search.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to α,β,δ,λ,γ? Can γ be learned (e.g., via evidence maximization) or integrated out?
2) How does BHCD perform on networks with known degree heterogeneity or disassortativity? Could a degree-corrected blockmodel be used as f/g while retaining conjugacy?
3) Can you report variability across restarts and provide confidence intervals on predictive metrics?
4) Please clarify the “two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration” claim.
5) Could you compare against hierarchical dendrogram/hierarchical SBMs (or discuss expected differences)?

Pros
- Elegant, interpretable Bayesian formulation with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Very fast inference; strong engineering for locality and caching.
- Competitive predictive performance vs. IRM/LFRM with far less compute.
- Demonstrates meaningful hierarchies on real datasets.

Cons
- Limited expressivity (no degree correction, no mixed-membership); no hyperparameter learning.
- Greedy search with restarts; limited analysis of stability/optimality.
- Incomplete comparisons and experimental details.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The method is technically sound, fast, and practically useful for hierarchical community discovery, with clear contributions over existing Bayesian approaches in scalability. Strengthening comparisons and adding degree correction or hyperparameter inference would further increase impact.
