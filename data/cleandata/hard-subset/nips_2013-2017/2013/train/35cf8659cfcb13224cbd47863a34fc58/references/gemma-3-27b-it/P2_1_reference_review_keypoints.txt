- Introduces BHCD: a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method using a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels with a tree-based prior for faster inference.  
- Claims significant speed and practicality improvements over Bayesian nonparametric methods like IRM, tested on Sampson’s monastery and NIPS co-authorship networks.  
- Situates work within prior literature, addressing limits of flat community methods and building on Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering foundations.  
- Compares experimentally to related models (LFRM, ILA) showing awareness of alternative network analysis techniques.  
- Major strength: computational efficiency with reported complexity O(n^2 log n) and effective caching, enabling scalability beyond IRM.  
- Strength: provides a full probabilistic framework allowing predictions and uncertainty quantification, unlike purely greedy methods.  
- Strength: clear exposition with helpful equations and figures explaining likelihood computation and recursive prediction.  
- Strength: empirical validation demonstrates recovery of known structure on Sampson’s network and competitive link-prediction on NIPS.  
- Strength: includes practical variants (e.g., BHCD sparse) and discussion of tie-breaking strategies.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (α, β, δ, λ, γ) beyond reporting values that “worked well.”  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical guarantees about when BHCD recovers true hierarchical structure.  
- Weakness: comparisons are limited—needs benchmarks against popular non-Bayesian algorithms (Louvain, Leiden).  
- Weakness: scalability discussion omits memory requirements and how they grow with network size.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important scalable hierarchical community detection problem with a novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated Bayesian approach.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing extensive hyperparameter sensitivity study, theoretical recovery guarantees, and broader algorithmic comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with notable speedup over existing Bayesian methods; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, requesting hyperparameter analysis, comparisons to Louvain/Leiden, and a brief discussion of memory requirements.
