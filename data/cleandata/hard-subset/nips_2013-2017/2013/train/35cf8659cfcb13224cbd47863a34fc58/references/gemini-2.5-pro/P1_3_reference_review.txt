### Summary

This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative clustering method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The work extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data by using a stochastic blockmodel likelihood. The core technical contribution is a dynamic programming recursion that efficiently calculates the marginal likelihood of the data given a specific hierarchy by marginalizing over all tree-consistent partitions. Inference is performed via a greedy bottom-up search, where at each step, two subtrees in the current forest are merged to maximize the local Bayes factor. The authors demonstrate that this approach is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than MCMC-based inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), a prominent Bayesian nonparametric model, while achieving comparable predictive performance on link prediction tasks. The method's scalability is showcased on both small (Sampson's Monastery) and large (NIPS co-authorship) networks.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the recursive marginal likelihood in Equation (10) is a direct and clever application of the principles from previous work on Bayesian rose trees to the relational data setting. The proposed greedy search algorithm is well-defined and the caching scheme for sufficient statistics to achieve O(n^2 log n) complexity is a solid engineering contribution. The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors provide both qualitative results on a classic dataset (Sampson's Monastery) and quantitative results on a standard benchmark (NIPS-234), directly comparing speed and accuracy against a relevant and widely-known baseline (IRM). The inclusion of a larger-scale experiment (full NIPS dataset) effectively demonstrates the method's practical scalability. The authors are honest about the performance relative to other models like ILA, stating that BHCD is outperformed in accuracy but makes up for it in speed.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. Section 2 provides a clear introduction to stochastic blockmodels, which serves as the necessary foundation. Section 3 logically builds upon this to introduce the hierarchical model, explaining the key concepts of tree-consistent partitions and the recursive calculation of the marginal likelihood. The agglomerative algorithm in Section 4 is described clearly, supplemented by Algorithm 1. The figures are effective, particularly Figure 4, which compellingly illustrates the speed-performance trade-off against the IRM. The notation is consistent and the mathematical developments are clear.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel and impactful combination of existing ideas. While greedy hierarchical clustering and Bayesian nonparametrics for networks are not new, the synthesis of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework (which marginalizes over partitions) with a stochastic blockmodel likelihood for a fast, greedy search is original. It cleverly bridges the gap between fast, heuristic methods (like Clauset et al., 2004) and more principled but computationally expensive Bayesian methods (like hierarchical extensions of the IRM). This contribution is significant because it provides a practical, "off-the-shelf" Bayesian method that can be applied to medium-to-large networks where MCMC-based methods would be prohibitively slow.

**Significance:** The primary significance of this work lies in its practicality and performance. The problem of discovering hierarchical structure in networks is of broad interest in machine learning and applied sciences. Existing Bayesian methods often struggle to scale. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster than a key competitor while maintaining a full probabilistic footing and delivering comparable results, this paper makes a valuable contribution. Researchers and practitioners looking for a fast, principled method for exploring hierarchical network structure are likely to use and build upon this work. The demonstrated efficiency makes Bayesian hierarchical analysis accessible for much larger datasets than was previously feasible with models like the IRM.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Greedy Search Limitations:** The most significant weakness is the greedy nature of the inference, which is susceptible to finding local optima. While the authors mitigate this with multiple random restarts, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this limitation. For example, how much variance is there in the likelihoods of the final trees across different restarts? How sensitive are the results to the random tie-breaking scheme mentioned in Section 4.1? Providing some measure of this variability would give a clearer picture of the algorithm's robustness.

2.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The main empirical comparison is against the IRM. While this is a crucial baseline, the paper's related work section and complexity table mention several other relevant models (e.g., LFRM, ILA). The authors note that ILA [10] achieves better predictive performance on the NIPS-234 dataset. A direct comparison of runtime vs. performance against ILA, similar to the one performed for IRM in Figure 4, would provide a more complete picture of where BHCD stands among state-of-the-art models. As it is, the comparison feels slightly narrow, focusing on a baseline that BHCD is designed to beat in terms of speed.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters are fixed to values that "work well". While understandable for a conference paper, a brief sensitivity analysis or a more detailed justification for their choice would be beneficial. How does the choice of the concentration-like parameter γ, or the Beta priors, affect the resulting hierarchy's depth and granularity? This is important for practitioners seeking to apply the method.

4.  **Motivation for the "Sparse" Variant:** The BHCD-sparse variant is introduced with the heuristic motivation that "communities that are completely disconnected should not be merged." While this is intuitive and empirically effective, it could be connected more formally to the model. Does this constraint correspond to a specific prior belief about community structure (e.g., a prior that places zero mass on merging disconnected components)? Framing it this way would make the variant feel less ad-hoc.

### Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** Addresses the important and practical problem of scalable Bayesian inference for hierarchical community detection.
*   **Pro:** The proposed method is a novel and elegant combination of the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework and stochastic blockmodels.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating a two-order-of-magnitude speed-up over a key MCMC-based baseline (IRM) with comparable predictive accuracy.
*   **Pro:** The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and easy to understand.
*   **Con:** The greedy inference algorithm is susceptible to local optima, and the paper could discuss this limitation in more depth.
*   **Con:** The experimental comparison, while strong against IRM, is narrow and omits direct comparisons with other modern, relevant methods mentioned in the related work.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significant practical contribution in terms of speed and scalability for a principled probabilistic model, far outweigh its weaknesses. It represents a solid piece of work that is likely to be influential and widely used.
