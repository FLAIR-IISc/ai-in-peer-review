The paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to extend Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, leveraging stochastic blockmodels and a tree-based prior on partitions. The authors claim substantial speedups over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while maintaining competitive performance.

While the motivation for faster hierarchical community discovery is strong, and the proposed method builds on a solid foundation of existing work (stochastic blockmodels, Bayesian nonparametrics, greedy clustering), the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a concerning lack of polish, leading me to recommend rejection. My assessment is not due to a lack of novelty – the idea of a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering approach is reasonable – but rather the pervasive nature of the issues throughout the manuscript.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty (Moderate):** The combination of Bayesian principles with a greedy hierarchical clustering approach for relational data represents a potentially valuable contribution.
*   **Speed:** The reported speed advantage over the IRM is significant, if accurate. The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is attractive.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of stochastic blockmodels and a tree-based prior provides a solid probabilistic framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The writing is frequently convoluted and lacks precision. The transition between sections feels abrupt, and the mathematical notation, while generally standard, is occasionally presented without sufficient explanation. For example, the definition of σ<sub>pq</sub> is given after its first use in equation (2), making initial comprehension difficult.
*   **Technical Rigor:** The assertion that the marginal likelihood can be calculated "exactly" (Section 3) is dubious and requires further justification. While dynamic programming might facilitate efficient computation, claiming "exactness" implies an absence of approximation, which seems unlikely given the complexity of the model.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is weak. The analysis of Sampson’s Monastery network is largely descriptive and lacks quantitative evaluation. The comparison to IRM on NIPS data is better, but the experimental setup isn't fully described (e.g., details on cross-validation folds). The claim that BHCD's speed is "two orders of magnitude faster" requires a more rigorous benchmarking methodology. The "R restarts" approach is mentioned but the rationale for the value of R=50 is lacking.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) is arbitrary. No justification is provided for these values, and a sensitivity analysis is missing. How do different hyperparameter settings affect the results?
*   **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the paper's credibility. For example, the equation numbering seems erratic. The meaning of "DSS" is not defined when it appears in equation (14). The term "rose trees" is used without proper definition or context. There's a discrepancy between the claimed runtime complexity and the discussion of caching benefits—if caching provides O(1) access, wouldn't the overall complexity be lower?
*   **Algorithm 1:** The description of Algorithm 1 relies heavily on references to previous work ([14]). The paper should be self-contained and provide a more detailed explanation of the algorithm's steps.
* **BHCD global:** This variant is mentioned as performing poorly, but there is no explanation as to why.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Potential for significant speedups in hierarchical community discovery.
*   Sound theoretical foundation.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in network analysis.

**Con:**

*   Lack of clarity and precision in writing.
*   Questionable technical claims regarding exact computation.
*   Weak experimental validation.
*   Arbitrary hyperparameter settings and lack of sensitivity analysis.
*   Pervasive minor errors and inconsistencies.
*   Insufficient detail in algorithmic description.

**Overall:**

Despite the potential novelty and theoretical merits of BHCD, the numerous shortcomings in clarity, technical rigor, and experimental validation outweigh its strengths. The paper reads as a preliminary exploration of ideas rather than a polished, conclusive contribution. While the authors demonstrate a grasp of the underlying concepts, the presentation and execution leave much to be desired. The sheer volume of minor issues suggests a lack of careful attention to detail. I believe that a significant revision, addressing the points outlined above, would be necessary before this paper could be considered for acceptance. As it stands, I recommend **rejection**.
