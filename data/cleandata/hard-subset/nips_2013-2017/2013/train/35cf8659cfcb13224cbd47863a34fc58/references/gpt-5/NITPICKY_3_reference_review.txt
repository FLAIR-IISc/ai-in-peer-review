Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian procedure for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels: a “rose tree” T (non-binary tree) defines a set of partitions consistent with the hierarchy, and the marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed exactly via dynamic programming using a recursion akin to Bayesian Rose Trees (Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010). Within/between-block edges are integrated under conjugate Beta–Bernoulli models (with distinct on/off-diagonal hyperparameters). Inference uses an agglomerative procedure that, at each step, chooses the merge maximizing a local Bayes factor among three options (join or absorb), with careful caching for O(1) inner-loop updates and overall O(n^2 log n) time. A “sparse” variant forbids merging trees with no edges. Prediction uses a top-down mixture recursion along a path in T. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery data and NIPS co-authorship networks (NIPS-234 subset and full) show hierarchical structures and faster link prediction than IRM, with comparable accuracy to LFRM and worse than ILA.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual formulation: a rose-tree prior over partitions coupled with a blockmodel likelihood; exact marginal over tree-consistent partitions via DP.
- Practical, fast inference with local Bayes factors and caching; sensible engineering to achieve O(n^2 log n) time.
- Simple predictive recursion; averaging across multiple restarts for uncertainty.
- Qualitative results on Sampson; promising predictive speed/quality on NIPS-234; large-scale hierarchy on full NIPS.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Hyperparameter inconsistency that undermines the modeling claim: the paper states α>β (dense within) and δ<λ (sparse between), but experiments use α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2. This violates δ<λ (1.0<0.2 is false) and, more importantly, makes on-/off-diagonal priors identical, removing the intended preference for community structure. This is serious: it directly affects all results and interpretations. Please correct and re-run (e.g., α>β and δ<<λ, or infer hyperparameters).
- Complexity inconsistencies: the text alternately claims worst-case quadratic and O(n^2 log n). The prediction section says “linear in depth,” then concludes Θ(n). Please unify and prove the stated bounds (and discuss memory, which appears to be O(n^2) for cached σIJ/pairs).
- Sparse variant: forbidding merges with no edges discards negative evidence (absences), which is integral to the Beta–Bernoulli likelihood—particularly in sparse networks. This introduces bias and should be analyzed (ablation, conditions under which it helps/hurts).
- Empirical evaluation lacks crucial details/quantification:
  - No numerical values, error bars, or statistical tests in NIPS-234; figures alone are insufficient for NeurIPS standards. Provide the exact log-predictive probabilities/AUC/accuracy with confidence intervals.
  - Fairness of speed comparisons is unclear: BHCD uses R=50 restarts (costly), while IRM runs are plotted per step. Report wall-clock with identical hardware, implementations, and stopping criteria. Clarify whether the reported BHCD time includes all restarts.
  - Missing comparisons to closely related hierarchical Bayesian methods (e.g., hierarchical IRM variants, multiscale community blockmodels, Mondrian-process-based approaches) despite being discussed in related work.
- Algorithmic details: Algorithm 1 is referenced but not fully spelled out; “BHCD global” is mentioned in Sampson results but never defined. The heap/”stale” score handling is mentioned informally; please specify invariant and correct handling rigorously.

Clarity
- Notation and minor errors:
  - O(2n) in Sec. 3 likely means O(2^n).
  - σ{x}{x}=0 is confusing; clarify treatment of self-edges and how missing data are excluded from counts.
  - Prediction complexity should be O(depth(T)), not Θ(n).
  - The forest likelihood (11) and merge score (12) deserve a short derivation (or pointer) so readers can verify that only local terms remain.
- Formatting/grammar issues abound (typos, punctuation, missing spaces, duplicated periods). References are incomplete (missing years/venues, typos; e.g., “Erix”).
- In Sec. 5 you say “six variants” but only define four; “BHCD global” appears only there.

Originality
- The main novelty is the adaptation of Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data via a blockmodel likelihood plus an efficient agglomerative merge operator and caching. This is a reasonable, incremental contribution. However, the paper should more clearly delineate what is new beyond [13,14] (e.g., exact form of the Bayes factor for blockmodels, the caching scheme, theoretical properties).

Significance
- If the empirical claims hold with corrected priors and rigorous evaluation, a fast hierarchical community discovery method that yields reasonable accuracy could be useful. As written, due to the hyperparameter error and underdeveloped evaluation, the significance is not yet established. The method also lacks robustness to degree heterogeneity; comparisons to degree-corrected blockmodels or non-conjugate variants would strengthen the case.

Pros
- Conceptually clean synthesis of rose-tree priors with blockmodels.
- Efficient greedy inference with local scoring and caching; scalable and simple to implement.
- Qualitative hierarchies on real data; promising speed.

Cons
- Critical hyperparameter error invalidates the “community” prior in experiments.
- Inconsistent complexity claims; incomplete algorithmic detail.
- Evaluation lacks numerical rigor, fair timing protocol, and comparisons to relevant hierarchical baselines.
- Sparse variant heuristics may bias results; no analysis.
- Numerous clarity/formatting/reference issues.

Questions for the authors
- Please correct the on/off-diagonal priors and re-run experiments. How sensitive are results to α,β,δ,λ, and γ? Why fix rather than infer them?
- What is the memory footprint of the caches for large n? Can BHCD-sparse guarantee subquadratic memory/time on sparse graphs?
- Define “BHCD global” and reconcile the “six variants” claim.
- Provide exact numbers (with error bars) and fair wall-clock comparisons including restarts.
- How does BHCD compare to hierarchical IRM extensions and multiscale blockmodels on NIPS-234?

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is appealing and potentially useful, but the paper contains a major experimental flaw (on/off priors), several internal inconsistencies, and insufficiently rigorous empirical evaluation. With corrected priors, clearer algorithmic exposition, stronger/ fairer experiments, and improved writing, this work could be competitive.
