### Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a method called Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The approach extends the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2012) to relational data modeled by a stochastic blockmodel (SBM). The core idea is to define a probabilistic model over tree structures, where each tree corresponds to a specific hierarchy of communities. The marginal likelihood of a tree can be computed efficiently via dynamic programming. To find the optimal tree, the authors employ a bottom-up, greedy agglomerative clustering algorithm. At each step, the algorithm merges the pair of subtrees that yields the largest increase in the Bayes factor, a local approximation to the model evidence. The main claimed advantage of this method is its computational efficiency, scaling as O(n^2 log n) for n vertices, which is reported to be significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) that typically rely on MCMC for inference. The authors demonstrate the method's performance on the Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset, comparing its speed and predictive accuracy against the IRM.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength of this paper is the proposed method's speed. The greedy, deterministic nature of the algorithm, combined with an efficient caching of sufficient statistics, allows it to scale to larger networks than traditional MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric models. The experimental results in Figure 4, showing BHCD achieving reasonable performance in seconds while the IRM takes thousands of seconds, effectively highlight this advantage. This makes the method a potentially valuable tool for exploratory analysis of large-scale network data.

2.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written and the proposed model is clearly formulated. The authors do a good job of building up from the standard stochastic blockmodel to their hierarchical extension. The recursive formula for the tree's marginal likelihood (Eq. 10) is elegant and the greedy search algorithm is explained well. The connection to prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering is explicitly and correctly stated.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core technical contribution appears to be an application of the existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework ([13], [14]) to relational data. While this is a valid and useful extension, the underlying probabilistic model and the greedy inference algorithm are not new. The main innovation is in adapting the sufficient statistics and likelihood calculations for an SBM-type model, which feels more like an incremental contribution rather than a fundamentally new approach.

2.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The reliance on a greedy agglomerative search is a major methodological weakness. This approach finds a single point estimate of the tree structure and is susceptible to getting trapped in poor local optima. The authors acknowledge this by using multiple random restarts, but this is an ad-hoc solution that does not fully address the problem. Established Bayesian methods, such as MCMC for the IRM, are slower precisely because they aim to approximate the full posterior distribution over structures, which provides a more principled way to handle uncertainty and avoid local optima. By sacrificing this probabilistic rigor for speed, the model loses a key advantage of the Bayesian paradigm.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the speed-up, is not comprehensive enough to establish the method's superiority or even consistent competitiveness in terms of quality.
    *   The primary baseline is the IRM, which is a foundational but relatively old model. The paper acknowledges more recent and relevant work like LFRM [18] and ILA [10], and the results in Figure 4 show BHCD's performance is comparable to LFRM but worse than ILA. This suggests that while BHCD is fast, it does not advance the state of the art in predictive accuracy. A stronger paper would have included direct, head-to-head comparisons with these more modern baselines.
    *   The analysis of the full NIPS dataset is purely qualitative. While Figure 5 provides an interesting visualization, it offers no quantitative assessment of the discovered hierarchy's quality or utility. It is difficult to judge whether the found structure is meaningful or an artifact of the greedy algorithm.
    *   The sensitivity of the model to the fixed hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) is not discussed or evaluated. A robust Bayesian model should either be insensitive to these choices or provide a mechanism for their inference. This work does neither, which is a notable omission.

4.  **Strong Modeling Assumptions:** The model makes a strong locality assumption in its merge criterion (Eq. 12), stating that "local community structure should depend only on the local connectivity structure." This may not hold in complex networks where long-range dependencies or higher-order structures (e.g., motifs beyond edges) define communities. The model's simplicity, which contributes to its speed, also limits its representational power compared to more flexible models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses the important practical problem of scaling community detection in a Bayesian framework.
*   The proposed method is demonstrably orders of magnitude faster than a standard MCMC-based IRM, making it a pragmatic choice for large datasets.
*   The model is simple, clearly presented, and easy to understand.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The methodological contribution is incremental, largely applying an existing greedy algorithm [13, 14] to the network domain.
*   The greedy inference approach lacks the rigor of established Bayesian inference techniques and provides no guarantees of solution quality.
*   The experimental evaluation is not thorough. The method does not outperform more recent, relevant baselines on predictive tasks and lacks quantitative validation on the larger dataset.
*   Key aspects, such as hyperparameter sensitivity, are not addressed.

**Recommendation**

While the paper presents a computationally efficient method for a relevant problem, its weaknesses in methodological novelty and the rigor of its inference scheme are significant. The greedy approach is a well-known heuristic, and its application here trades principled Bayesian posterior exploration for speed. The experiments confirm this trade-off: the method is fast, but its accuracy does not surpass existing, more sophisticated models. Given the high standards at NeurIPS for methodological innovation and thorough validation, I am leaning towards rejection. The work is a solid piece of engineering but does not represent a sufficient advance in the state of the art to warrant publication at this conference.
