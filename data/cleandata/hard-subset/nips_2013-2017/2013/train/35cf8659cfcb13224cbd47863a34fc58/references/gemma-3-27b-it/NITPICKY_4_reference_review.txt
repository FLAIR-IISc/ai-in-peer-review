The paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to build a hierarchy of stochastic blockmodels, leveraging a tree structure to define a prior on partitions, allowing for efficient marginal likelihood computation via dynamic programming. The authors claim substantial speed improvements over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the IRM, while maintaining competitive performance.

While the problem addressed – scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection – is undeniably important, and the proposed approach has potential, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to more substantial concerns regarding experimental methodology and claims of efficiency. The presentation, while generally clear, lacks the meticulousness expected for a NIPS submission.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of greedy hierarchical clustering with a Bayesian framework and efficient marginal likelihood calculation is a novel contribution. The caching scheme for sufficient statistics is a clever optimization.
*   **Potential for Scalability:** The claimed O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is attractive, especially compared to methods with higher complexity or those suffering from slow convergence.
*   **Empirical Results:**  Demonstrating good performance on Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship dataset is a positive sign, although the results require closer scrutiny (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation. For instance, ‘φ’ is used to represent both a partition and a set of vertices. ‘T’ represents both the tree structure and a subtree. This ambiguity hinders readability.
*   **Lack of Rigorous Complexity Analysis:** The claim of O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity is stated without sufficient justification. The heap maintenance operations and potential for recomputation of sufficient statistics after merges are not fully accounted for in the complexity analysis. The claimed O(n+m) time for likelihood computation is also not fully substantiated given the recursion involved.
*   **Experimental Design Concerns:** The choice of hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4) is seemingly arbitrary and lacks justification. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of these hyperparameters on the results is missing.
*   **IRM Comparison:** The comparison with the IRM is not entirely fair. The IRM implementation used is not clearly specified (details on MCMC settings, convergence criteria are missing). Stating BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a *single iteration* of the IRM" is a deliberately low bar. A fairer comparison would assess BHCD's performance against a converged IRM solution.
*   **NIPS Dataset Subset:** Restricting the analysis on the NIPS dataset to 234 vertices is questionable. The scalability benefits of BHCD are most relevant for large networks; evaluating on a smaller subset obscures this advantage.
*   **BHCD Variants:** The rationale behind the four BHCD variants tested (with and without sparsity, binary vs. non-binary trees) is not clearly articulated. The benefits and drawbacks of each variant are not thoroughly discussed.
*   **Figure 3 Clarity:** The visual representation of the communities found in Sampson’s monastery network (Figure 3) lacks clarity. The coloring scheme and labels are difficult to interpret.
*   **Missing Details:** The supplementary material is mentioned but not provided. Critical details regarding the full NIPS hierarchy and further experimental results should be included.
*   **Minor Issues:** Typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies are present throughout the manuscript (e.g. “rose trees typically better explain the data” is an odd phrasing).



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous technical flaws and inconsistencies detract from the paper’s credibility.
*   The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous to support the strong claims made about performance and scalability.
*   The lack of a thorough sensitivity analysis and justification for the hyperparameter choices raises concerns about the robustness of the results.
*   The presentation, despite being generally clear, is marred by notational ambiguity and minor errors.

**For Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is novel and addresses an important problem.
*   The potential for scalability is significant.
*   The initial empirical results are promising, albeit needing more validation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I find the paper falls short of NIPS standards. While the idea is interesting, the numerous flaws in methodology, presentation, and analysis outweigh the potential benefits. The paper requires substantial revision, addressing the issues raised above, before it can be considered for acceptance.  I recommend **rejection** at this time. A significant rewrite and more thorough experimental validation are necessary to establish the paper's contribution and credibility.
