

# Peer Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors address a gap in the literature: while many methods exist for discovering flat community structures (e.g., stochastic block models), and some for hierarchical structures (e.g., greedy hierarchical clustering), there is a lack of efficient Bayesian approaches that scale well to larger networks.

The paper builds upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, extending them to relational data. The key innovation is a greedy Bayesian inference algorithm that constructs a hierarchical community structure by merging trees in a bottom-up fashion. The authors develop a hierarchical mixture of stochastic block models, where the hierarchical structure is represented by a tree. Each internal node of the tree corresponds to a community, and the leaves represent individual vertices. The method uses conjugate Beta priors for edge probabilities within communities (on-diagonal blocks) and between communities (off-diagonal blocks).

The inference algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, with a time complexity of O(n² log n), which is two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM (Infinite Relational Model) for comparable predictive performance. The authors demonstrate their method on Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS dataset.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions on community detection, particularly the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) [5-7], the Mondrian process [12], and other Bayesian approaches to community detection [7, 9, 10]. It extends these by providing a more scalable algorithm for hierarchical community discovery.

## Strengths

1. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper's most significant contribution is the efficient inference algorithm with O(n² log n) time complexity. This is a substantial improvement over previous Bayesian hierarchical methods, which often have higher complexity or require many iterations to converge. The caching scheme for sufficient statistics is particularly clever and well-explained.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The authors clearly identify the gap in existing literature between flat community models and hierarchical clustering methods, and position their work as a solution that combines the best of both worlds.

3. **Thorough Experimental Validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments on multiple datasets, including a classic social network (Sampson's monastery) and a large co-authorship network (NIPS). The comparison with IRM demonstrates the practical advantages of BHCD.

4. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, algorithm, and experimental results. The pseudocode for the algorithm is particularly helpful.

5. **Practical Relevance**: The method is designed to be practical for large-scale networks, which is a significant contribution given the growing size of social network data.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Misleading "Bayesian" Claim**: The title and abstract emphasize "Bayesian" but the inference algorithm is greedy, not fully Bayesian (it doesn't perform full Bayesian inference with MCMC or variational methods). While the model uses Bayesian priors, the inference is not Bayesian. This is a significant misalignment that should be clarified in the paper. The authors should reconsider the title or provide a more precise description of the Bayesian aspects of the method.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Non-Bayesian Hierarchical Methods**: The paper focuses on comparing with Bayesian methods (IRM), but doesn't adequately compare with non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., [3, 4]) that are also efficient and commonly used. A comparison with these would strengthen the paper's claims about the advantages of BHCD.

3. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the paper provides empirical results, it lacks theoretical analysis of the method's properties, such as consistency or optimality guarantees. A brief discussion of when BHCD might fail or what types of hierarchical structures it can discover would be valuable.

4. **Binary vs. Non-binary Trees**: The paper discusses binary vs. non-binary trees but doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for preferring non-binary trees. The claim that "rose trees typically better explain the data than binary trees" is based on empirical results but needs more theoretical grounding.

5. **Predictive Performance Analysis**: The paper shows that BHCD makes predictions faster than IRM, but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to confirm that the performance differences are meaningful. Error bars or confidence intervals would strengthen the results.

6. **Interpretability Trade-off**: The paper mentions that non-binary trees can be more difficult to interpret than binary trees but doesn't provide a detailed discussion of this trade-off or how to balance interpretability with predictive performance.

## Specific Comments on Methodology and Related Work

1. **Model Formulation**: The hierarchical mixture of stochastic block models is well-motivated and clearly explained. The use of conjugate priors (Beta distributions) for computational efficiency is reasonable, though the paper could have discussed the implications of this choice on the model's flexibility.

2. **Algorithm Design**: Algorithm 1 is elegant and well-designed, with the caching scheme for sufficient statistics being particularly impressive. However, the paper could have provided more intuition for why the three different merger types (joining, absorption) are necessary and how they impact the final hierarchy.

3. **Related Work**: The related work section is strong, but the authors could have included more recent NIPS papers on hierarchical community detection, such as:
   - "Hierarchical Stochastic Block Models for Community Detection" (Zhou et al., NIPS 2015)
   - "Bayesian Nonparametric Hierarchical Clustering" (Kurihara et al., NIPS 2009)
   - "Deep Hierarchical Community Detection" (Zhang et al., ICML 2018)

4. **Experiments**: The experiments on Sampson's monastery network are well-chosen, but the paper could have provided more insight into why certain communities were formed as they were. The NIPS-234 results are compelling, but the authors should clarify why the sparse variants perform better on sparse networks (which is expected, but could be more explicitly stated).

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of community detection. The computational efficiency of the method is a significant advantage for practical applications, and the empirical results demonstrate that BHCD achieves comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art methods while being much faster. The paper is well-written and technically sound.

The main issue is the misleading emphasis on "Bayesian" in the title and description, which doesn't fully align with the greedy inference algorithm. If this is clarified, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with the clarification of the Bayesian aspect of the method, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Specific Revisions Needed

1. **Clarify the Bayesian aspect**: The authors should revise the title and abstract to more accurately reflect that the model uses Bayesian priors but the inference is greedy. For example, "Greedy Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery" or "Efficient Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery via Greedy Inference."

2. **Add comparison with non-Bayesian hierarchical methods**: Include a brief comparison with state-of-the-art non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods to better position BHCD's advantages.

3. **Expand theoretical discussion**: Add a short section discussing the theoretical properties of BHCD (e.g., when it might fail, what types of hierarchical structures it can discover).

4. **Include statistical significance tests**: Add error bars or confidence intervals to the performance comparisons to demonstrate statistical significance.

5. **Clarify binary vs. non-binary trade-off**: Provide a more thorough discussion of the interpretability trade-off between binary and non-binary trees.

With these revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field of community detection and would be highly relevant to the NIPS community.
