**Summary**

This paper presents Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structure in networks. The approach extends previous work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] to relational data by using a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) likelihood. The core idea is to treat the problem as one of model selection over a space of possible hierarchies (trees). The marginal likelihood of the data given a tree is calculated via a dynamic programming recursion that sums over all tree-consistent partitions. The hierarchy is constructed in a bottom-up fashion by iteratively merging subtrees that yield the highest local Bayes factor. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their method is significantly faster—by two orders of magnitude—than MCMC inference for the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving comparable link prediction performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Scalability and Speed:** The primary strength of the paper is the computational efficiency of the proposed method. The greedy agglomerative approach, with careful caching of sufficient statistics, avoids the slow mixing and high per-iteration cost of MCMC-based nonparametric models like the IRM. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate a massive speed-up, making the method potentially applicable to much larger networks.
2.  **Principled Framework:** The method is grounded in a Bayesian framework. The use of marginal likelihoods to score trees and Bayes factors to guide the greedy search is a principled way to perform model selection, even if the search itself is heuristic.
3.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated, clearly positioning itself as a fast, deterministic alternative to more computationally expensive Bayesian nonparametric methods for network analysis. The problem of discovering hierarchical structure is important, and the need for scalable methods is well-established.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, while presenting a promising direction, is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, technical ambiguities, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Clarity:**
    *   The notation is frequently imprecise. In Eq. (2), the product over `p,q∈φ, p≠q` double-counts pairs for an undirected graph; it should be over `p<q`. In Eq. (13), the update `σMM = σII + σJJ + σIJ` is notationally loose, as these are tuples of sufficient statistics.
    *   The description of Algorithm 1 is too high-level and potentially misleading. The claim that each inner loop takes O(1) time is questionable. While updating a single sufficient statistic may be O(1), an entire merge step requires updating potential merges with all other `k-1` trees in the forest, which takes O(k) time. This detail, which is central to the complexity claim, is glossed over.
    *   The explanation of the three merge types (join, absorb left, absorb right) and the corresponding update for `σ¬chMM` in Eq. (13) is terse and difficult to follow without a diagram or more detailed exposition.
    *   In the Sampson's Monastery experiment results, the authors state: "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap and so finds less coherent structure." This is an alarming and unexplained statement. What is "BHCD global"? Why would scores on the heap become "out of date"? This suggests either a bug in the implementation or a poorly understood algorithmic artifact that is not addressed. Such a casual dismissal of a clear failure case is not acceptable.

2.  **Limited Originality and Discussion of Limitations:**
    *   The core algorithmic contribution is a direct application of the method in [14] to an SBM likelihood. While a useful combination, the novelty is limited. The paper would be stronger if it focused more on the unique challenges and insights gained from this specific application.
    *   The greedy nature of the algorithm is a major limitation. It is susceptible to local optima, and the paper's solution—multiple random restarts—is a standard heuristic. The paper makes a strong locality assumption in Eq. (12) that "local community structure should depend only on the local connectivity structure," but does not discuss the potential negative consequences of this assumption.

3.  **Experimental Weaknesses:**
    *   The choice of hyperparameters (`α, β, δ, λ, γ`) is presented without justification or sensitivity analysis. The claim that they "found [them] to work well" is insufficient for a rigorous scientific paper.
    *   The comparison is primarily against IRM, a model known to be slow. While demonstrating a speed advantage is valuable, the comparison set is narrow. It would be more insightful to compare against other fast hierarchical clustering algorithms (e.g., [3, 4]) to demonstrate what benefit the Bayesian formulation provides over purely algorithmic approaches.
    *   The criterion for cutting the tree in the full NIPS experiment is heuristic. Choosing a threshold of 0.5 is arbitrary and the results are therefore sensitive to this choice.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation:**
    *   The list of references is poorly formatted. Multiple entries are incomplete, missing venues, years, or page numbers (e.g., [1], [2], [5], [17]). Reference [20] is just a URL. This lack of attention to detail is concerning.
    *   The footnote on the first page regarding the author's affiliation is unconventional for a NIPS submission and should be in the acknowledgements section.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important problem of scalable hierarchical community detection.
*   The proposed method is extremely fast and achieves competitive predictive performance against a relevant baseline.
*   The core idea is simple, well-motivated, and builds on established work in Bayesian machine learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The technical contribution is incremental, being a direct application of an existing algorithm [14] to a standard model (SBM).
*   The paper suffers from a lack of technical precision in its notation and algorithmic description.
*   Key algorithmic behaviors (the "out of date scores" artifact) are mentioned but not explained, casting doubt on the robustness and understanding of the method.
*   The experimental evaluation is weakened by a lack of hyperparameter analysis and a narrow set of baselines.
*   The overall presentation, particularly the reference list, lacks the polish and care expected for a top-tier conference.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend **rejection**. While the direction of research is valuable and the headline result (two orders of magnitude speedup) is impressive, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards of NIPS. The accumulation of minor issues in clarity, technical precision, experimental rigor, and presentation is substantial. These issues prevent me from fully trusting the details of the contribution. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous flaws before it is ready for publication.
