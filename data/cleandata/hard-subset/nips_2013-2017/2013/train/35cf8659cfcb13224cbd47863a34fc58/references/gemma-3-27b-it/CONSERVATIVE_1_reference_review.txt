This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to extend existing Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, framing the problem as a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels. The authors claim significant speed advantages over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while maintaining reasonable predictive performance.

While the motivation for faster hierarchical community detection is sound, and the problem is relevant to the NIPS audience, I find the paper lacking in several crucial aspects. The presentation feels somewhat disjointed, jumping between model description, algorithmic details, and experimental results without sufficient connective tissue. The primary concern is the limited and arguably unconvincing validation of the proposed method.

The authors repeatedly emphasize the speed advantage of BHCD compared to IRM. However, the experimental comparison (Table 1 & Figure 4) isn’t entirely fair. The IRM implementation used appears to be a standard MCMC approach, and it's well-known that IRM can be slow. A more robust comparison would involve state-of-the-art IRM inference techniques, such as variational inference or more efficient MCMC samplers. The claim that BHCD’s runtime is independent of the number of communities is also potentially misleading; while the *worst-case* complexity is stated as such, the practical runtime will undoubtedly be affected by the number of clusters, even if the algorithm doesn't explicitly require it as an input.

Furthermore, the experiments rely heavily on a relatively small subset of the NIPS co-authorship network. While demonstrating speedup on this dataset is positive, it doesn’t sufficiently justify the claims of general applicability. The qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery network is weak. Simply showing that BHCD finds *some* block structure isn't particularly insightful, as many community detection algorithms can achieve this. The authors don’t rigorously compare the discovered communities to the known ground truth, merely stating that the results are "reasonable". 

The modifications to the algorithm (BHCD sparse, binary trees) are presented without substantial justification. The benefit of the sparse version is intuitively clear, but the rationale for exploring binary-only trees is less convincing. The discussion of the predictive performance also lacks depth. The authors mention improvements over the IRM but don't adequately contextualize these results relative to other established methods in link prediction.

The technical details, while generally sound, are presented in a rather dense and difficult-to-follow manner. The notation is cumbersome, and the recursion for the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. The reliance on caching for performance optimization is a positive aspect, but the explanation is somewhat superficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in network analysis.
*   Presents a novel approach combining Bayesian methods with greedy hierarchical clustering.
*   Demonstrates potential speed advantages over existing Bayesian nonparametric methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited and unconvincing experimental validation.
*   Comparison to IRM is not comprehensive, using a naive implementation.
*   Lack of comparison to other state-of-the-art community detection algorithms.
*   Qualitative analysis is weak and lacks rigorous evaluation.
*   Presentation is dense and difficult to follow.
*   Claims of independence from the number of communities are potentially misleading.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is currently not suitable for acceptance to NIPS. While the idea is interesting and the authors demonstrate some promising initial results, the lack of thorough validation and the limitations of the experimental evaluation raise serious concerns. The paper feels premature and would benefit significantly from more extensive experimentation, a more rigorous comparison to existing methods, and a clearer presentation of the technical details. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing these concerns. The potential for a faster, scalable Bayesian hierarchical community detection method is significant, but this paper doesn't yet demonstrate a convincing realization of that potential.
