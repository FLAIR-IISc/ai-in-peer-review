

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for discovering community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian approaches to hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and extend them to relational data, addressing limitations of previous Bayesian nonparametric methods that scale poorly with network size. The paper presents a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels, where communities are organized into a nested hierarchy, and develops an efficient inference algorithm that scales as O(n² log n) in the number of vertices n.

The paper's main technical contributions include: (1) a Bayesian hierarchical model that organizes communities into a nested hierarchy, (2) an efficient greedy inference algorithm using dynamic programming and sufficient statistics caching, and (3) a method for making predictions about missing edges. The authors demonstrate that BHCD is two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), with worst-case complexity independent of the number of communities.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound methods with clear theoretical derivations. The marginal likelihood calculation (equation 10) and inference algorithm (Algorithm 1) are well-supported by the paper's mathematical framework. The authors provide experimental validation on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship dataset, demonstrating BHCD's computational advantages over IRM and other Bayesian approaches. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for tie-breaking in cases of equal merge scores, and provides empirical evidence for the effectiveness of BHCD sparse for sparse networks. However, the paper could strengthen its claims by providing more detailed analysis of how hyperparameters affect performance, particularly the choice of α, β, δ, λ, and γ.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent, and key concepts (e.g., tree-consistent partitions, sufficient statistics) are well-defined. Figures 1-3 effectively illustrate the hierarchical structure and experimental results. The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is clear and well-commented. Some sections, particularly the description of the prior on partitions (equation 7), could benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid reader comprehension.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by addressing the computational limitations of existing Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant prior research, including the Infinite Relational Model [5, 6, 7], the Mondrian process [12], and previous Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches [13, 14]. The key novelty lies in the efficient greedy inference algorithm that avoids the computational bottlenecks of previous Bayesian methods. While the hierarchical mixture model concept builds on existing work, the specific implementation with dynamic programming and caching of sufficient statistics represents a novel technical contribution.

**Significance:** The method addresses a practical need for efficient community detection in large networks, with demonstrated speed improvements over state-of-the-art Bayesian approaches. The paper shows BHCD can make reasonable predictions in seconds, while IRM requires minutes to reach comparable performance. This makes BHCD particularly valuable for applications where computational resources are limited or real-time analysis is required. The method's applicability to large dense networks (with worst-case complexity O(n² log n)) is a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- Clear presentation of a technically sound method addressing a real problem in network analysis
- Significant computational advantages over existing Bayesian approaches (two orders of magnitude faster)
- Well-justified theoretical framework with proper mathematical derivations
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple datasets
- Practical implementation details (e.g., BHCD sparse for sparse networks)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison of hierarchical structure quality beyond log likelihoods
- NIPS dataset experiments focus on a subset (234 vertices) rather than the full network
- Insufficient discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and selection
- Limited analysis of directed network applications (common in social networks)
- Comparison to non-Bayesian approaches (e.g., greedy hierarchical clustering) is minimal

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a computationally efficient solution to hierarchical community detection
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing Bayesian methods
- Well-grounded in the literature with proper contextualization
- Presents a novel inference algorithm with sound theoretical underpinnings
- Includes thorough experimental validation on real-world networks

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited exploration of hyperparameter effects on performance
- Experiments on the NIPS dataset are restricted to a small subset
- The paper doesn't fully address the practical utility of non-binary hierarchies
- No comparison to recent non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of community detection by providing an efficient Bayesian method for hierarchical community discovery. The technical contributions are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the method addresses a clear practical limitation of existing approaches. The computational advantages are particularly noteworthy for applications involving large networks. While some limitations exist in the experimental scope and hyperparameter analysis, these do not undermine the core contribution of the paper. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis.
