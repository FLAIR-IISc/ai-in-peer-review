

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

## Summary

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structures in social networks. The authors address a key limitation of existing Bayesian approaches to hierarchical community detection, which typically suffer from poor scalability due to expensive inference algorithms. BHCD builds upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods but extends them to relational data, using a tree structure to represent hierarchical community organization. The key innovation is an efficient greedy inference algorithm that computes the marginal likelihood of the tree structure in O(n² log n) time, which is two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM (Infinite Relational Model) while achieving comparable predictive performance.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on community detection by providing a Bayesian approach that scales well to larger networks while maintaining the probabilistic interpretation that is a key advantage of Bayesian methods over purely heuristic approaches. The authors situate their work within the broader context of stochastic blockmodels, hierarchical clustering, and Bayesian nonparametric methods, clearly differentiating their approach from previous work (particularly [5-11] and [12]).

## Strengths

1. **Strong Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a genuinely novel and efficient inference algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery. The dynamic programming approach for computing the marginal likelihood (Equation 10) is well-justified and represents a significant improvement over previous Bayesian approaches.

2. **Clear Theoretical Foundation**: The authors provide solid theoretical grounding for their method, explaining how the tree structure defines a set of consistent partitions and how the marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently. The connection to the Chinese Restaurant Process and other Bayesian nonparametric methods is well-established.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The paper includes comprehensive experiments on three datasets: Sampson's monastery network, a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network, and the full NIPS dataset. The results demonstrate BHCD's superior speed (Figure 4) while maintaining comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art methods.

4. **Practical Impact**: The O(n² log n) time complexity (with O(n+m) for likelihood computation) makes the method suitable for larger networks where previous Bayesian approaches would be computationally prohibitive. The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) is particularly well-suited for sparse networks like co-authorship graphs.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical notation, well-structured sections, and helpful figures (Figures 1-5) that illustrate key concepts and results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Non-Bayesian Hierarchical Methods**: While the paper compares BHCD to Bayesian methods (IRM, LFRM, ILA), it does not compare to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches (e.g., [3, 4]). This is a significant omission, as these methods are commonly used for hierarchical community discovery and could provide valuable context for the Bayesian advantage.

2. **Tie-Breaking Issue**: The paper acknowledges that BHCD uses random tie-breaking when multiple merges have the same score (Section 4.1), which can lead to different results across runs. While the authors mitigate this by using multiple restarts, the paper doesn't explore whether the quality of the final hierarchy varies significantly with different restarts or provide a principled way to select the best hierarchy.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses fixed hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) across all experiments, but doesn't investigate how sensitive the results are to these choices. A more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.

4. **Sparse vs. Non-Sparse Trade-off**: The paper introduces BHCD sparse for sparse networks but doesn't provide clear guidance on when to use the sparse variant versus the non-sparse version. The theoretical advantages of the sparse variant are clear, but the practical decision boundary for choosing between them is not discussed.

5. **Limited Discussion of Hierarchical Structure Quality**: While the paper demonstrates that BHCD finds reasonable hierarchical structures (e.g., on Sampson's monastery), it doesn't provide a formal comparison of the quality of the hierarchical structures (e.g., using metrics like normalized mutual information for hierarchical clustering) against other methods.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- The method addresses a significant practical limitation in Bayesian hierarchical community detection (computational scalability)
- The theoretical foundation is strong and the algorithm is well-designed
- The experimental results clearly demonstrate the speed advantage while maintaining reasonable predictive performance
- The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature
- The method has clear practical applications in social network analysis

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- The comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods is missing, which weakens the justification for a Bayesian approach
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address the tie-breaking issue or hyperparameter sensitivity
- The sparse vs. non-sparse trade-off could be better explained
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the quality of the hierarchical structures discovered

## Recommendations

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a scalable Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery. The main weaknesses are not fundamental to the method's contribution but rather represent opportunities to strengthen the paper's presentation and evaluation.

Specific revisions I would recommend:

1. Add a comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods (e.g., [3, 4]) to better justify the Bayesian approach and contextualize the results.

2. Expand Section 4.1 to discuss the tie-breaking issue more thoroughly, including an analysis of how different restarts affect the final hierarchy quality.

3. Add a brief sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) to show how robust the results are to reasonable variations in these values.

4. Clarify the conditions under which BHCD sparse should be preferred over BHCD, including a discussion of the sparsity threshold where the sparse variant becomes advantageous.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of community detection. The authors have developed a novel and efficient inference algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community discovery that addresses a key limitation of previous approaches. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the method has clear practical advantages in terms of computational efficiency.

The paper meets all the NIPS criteria:
- **Quality**: Technically sound with strong theoretical justification and thorough experimental validation
- **Clarity**: Well-written and well-organized with clear mathematical notation
- **Originality**: Presents a novel inference algorithm that significantly improves upon previous Bayesian approaches
- **Significance**: Provides a scalable solution to an important problem in social network analysis

The minor weaknesses noted above can be addressed with relatively minor revisions, and I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance with the suggested revisions.
