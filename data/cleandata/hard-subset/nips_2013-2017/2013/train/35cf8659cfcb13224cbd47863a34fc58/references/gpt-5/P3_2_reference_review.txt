Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for networks. The model defines a prior over partitions that are consistent with a given tree T (via a stopping probability πS at each internal node), and a likelihood that marginalizes stochastic blockmodel parameters using conjugate Beta-Bernoulli priors with different hyperparameters for within- and between-cluster blocks. The marginal likelihood p(D|T) is computed by a dynamic program (Eq. 10), enabling local Bayes-factor merge scores (Eq. 12) and an agglomerative search over rose-tree structures with three merge types (join and two absorptions). The authors present a sparse variant that forbids merging disconnected subtrees. They report strong speedups over IRM and competitive link prediction on NIPS co-authorship data, plus qualitative structure on Sampson’s monastery.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity: Adapts Bayesian hierarchical clustering (BHC/Bayesian Rose Trees) to relational data with a clean derivation of the tree-marginal likelihood and predictive distribution. The recursion in Eq. (10) is elegant and computationally attractive.
- Scalability: The greedy algorithm with cached sufficient statistics yields O(n^2 log n) complexity (with restarts factor R), independent of the number of communities. The sparse variant is well-motivated for real-world sparse networks.
- Practicality: The Bayes-factor merge score is local and easy to compute; the three-way merge design (join or absorption) increases flexibility over strictly binary trees and tends to yield interpretable rose trees.
- Empirical results: BHCD attains reasonable link prediction quickly on NIPS-234, outperforming IRM in time–accuracy trade-offs and providing sensible hierarchies on Sampson and full NIPS.

Weaknesses and questions
- Prior hyperparameters inconsistency: The text states α>β and δ<λ to encode higher within- than between-cluster edge probabilities. However, experiments use α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, which imply identical within and between priors with mean 0.83 (and actually violate δ<λ). This undermines a core modeling assumption and may materially affect results. Please clarify or correct, and provide sensitivity analysis to α,β,δ,λ,γ.
- Predictive complexity: Section 4.2 states “Hence the computational complexity is Θ(n)” for a single edge prediction; it should be O(depth(T)), which is typically much smaller than n. Please correct and characterize typical depths observed.
- Model expressiveness: The likelihood assumes homogeneous within-block density (no degree correction) and a single between-block prior g at each node. This can struggle on degree-heterogeneous or disassortative/bipartite structure. Comparison to degree-corrected SBMs and discussion of limitations would be valuable.
- Relation to prior hierarchical network models: The paper cites [8, 11], but should more carefully position against hierarchical random graphs (e.g., Clauset et al. 2008) and multiscale/nested SBMs, explaining how BHCD’s mixture-over-tree-consistent partitions differs in modeling capacity and inference cost.
- Evaluation breadth and fairness: The main quantitative comparison is to IRM (MCMC/greedy). The comparison to LFRM/ILA is via published numbers on the same folds, which is informative but not a controlled head-to-head. Consider including direct comparisons to other fast baselines (e.g., modularity-based multilevel clustering, degree-corrected SBM with greedy merging), and to hierarchical IRM variants when feasible. Also report variance over restarts and the impact of tie-breaking.
- The “BHCD global” variant in Fig. 3 appears without description in the methods section; please define it if it is part of the study.
- Memory and caching details: While each inner loop is O(1) with cached σ, storing σIJ for all pairs is O(n^2) memory. Please discuss memory footprint and how the sparse variant reduces it in practice.
- The dynamic program complexity claim p(D|T) in O(n+m) is stated but not justified; a short proof or a clear accounting of sufficient-statistics updates per edge would strengthen the computational claims.
- Hyperparameter γ: Fixed at 0.4; its effect on arity and on the depth/branching of trees should be analyzed. Empirical Bayes or cross-validation for γ would increase robustness.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a plausible and efficient objective and search strategy. However, the hyperparameter inconsistency is a substantive flaw that needs correction. The empirical section supports the speed claim and shows reasonable predictive performance, but broader baselines and sensitivity analyses are needed to firmly establish accuracy.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The derivations are concise and readable. Minor issues: define “BHCD global”; correct the Θ(n) predictive complexity; ensure references include years and are complete.

Originality
The core idea—bringing BHC/Rose trees to relational data with a SBM marginal and a tree-constrained mixture over partitions—is a neat and useful combination of known ingredients. The three-way merge operator and sparse variant are practical contributions. While related hierarchical network models exist, the computational framing and agglomerative scheme here are distinctive.

Significance
A fast, simple hierarchical community discovery procedure is valuable both as a standalone tool and as an initializer for richer models. If the hyperparameter issue is corrected and evaluation broadened, BHCD could be a useful addition to the toolkit.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix and analyze the within/between hyperparameters; include sensitivity plots and perhaps empirical Bayes estimation.
- Add degree-corrected or attribute-aware baselines; discuss failure modes on degree-heterogeneous graphs.
- Expand comparisons to hierarchical network models; clarify positioning and trade-offs.
- Report memory usage and wall-clock scaling on larger sparse graphs; quantify variability across restarts.
- Correct the predictive complexity statement; provide a short proof of the O(n+m) DP cost.

Pros
- Clear, efficient method with principled Bayesian objective.
- Strong scalability and practical implementation details.
- Meaningful empirical speedups; interpretable hierarchies.

Cons
- Hyperparameter inconsistency; limited analysis of sensitivity.
- Narrow set of quantitative baselines; missing degree correction.
- Some clarity and completeness issues in methods and references.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject/weak accept. With corrected hyperparameters and strengthened evaluation, this would be a strong submission.
