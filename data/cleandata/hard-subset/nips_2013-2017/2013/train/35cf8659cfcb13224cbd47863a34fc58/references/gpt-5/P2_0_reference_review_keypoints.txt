- Proposes BHCD: a greedy Bayesian agglomerative method using a tree prior and blockmodel likelihood with efficient exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.  
- Inference greedily merges subtrees with three merge types using a local Bayes factor and caching to achieve O(n^2 log n) time; sparse variant forbids merging disconnected subgraphs.  
- Link prediction is handled by a top-down recursion on the learned tree.  
- Empirically, BHCD is much faster than IRM and achieves predictive performance similar to LFRM but below ILA (based on reported numbers); experiments include Sampson, NIPS-234, and full NIPS (qualitative).  
- Extends Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering/Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data with a πS prior choice from prior work.  
- Compares experimentally to IRM and situates relative to LFRM and ILA, but omits citation and empirical comparison to Hierarchical Random Graph (HRG) and recent hierarchical IRM/Mondrian variants.  
- Probabilistic model is well specified; Beta priors for within/between edges provide conjugacy and capture assortativity.  
- The dynamic program for p(D|T) and the local Bayes-factor merge score are correct under the model and prior.  
- Merge operations and caching formulas are appropriate and support the complexity claims; sparse variant is clearly defined.  
- Limitation: aggregating all across-child edges into a single g block per split ties inter-child rates and favors assortative structure, reducing flexibility vs. full SBM.  
- Limitation: local merge score ignores effects outside merged subgraph, so greedy optimality is not guaranteed; multiple restarts help but no guarantees or diagnostics provided.  
- Empirics: predictive comparison to IRM is sensible and shows speed gains, but wall-clock fairness is unclear without hardware/implementation details.  
- Empirics: comparisons to LFRM/ILA are indirect (via reported numbers) rather than direct re-runs; largest-graph results are qualitative and Sampson lacks quantitative metrics.  
- Paper is generally clear with precise notation; πS choice and role are well explained.  
- Clarity issues: undefined “BHCD global,” missing full Algorithm 1, unspecified memory complexity (heap/caching footprint), and ambiguity about predictive recursion complexity.  
- Originality: combines known pieces (BHC/BRT priors + SBM likelihood); novelty is mainly algorithmic/engineering (exact marginal, cached greedy inference) rather than a fundamentally new model.  
- Needs stronger differentiation from HRG and hierarchical IRM variants to clarify originality.  
- Practical significance: method is fast, simple, and yields interpretable hierarchies with useful link prediction; sparse variant is attractive for large graphs.  
- Scientific impact limited by missing baselines and insufficient empirical depth to claim state-of-the-art performance.  
- Pros: principled model with exact marginalization, scalable greedy inference with caching, strong speed advantages, interpretable hierarchies and link-prediction method.  
- Cons: missing HRG/related comparisons, limited empirical evaluation (few datasets, indirect baselines, no ablations or hyperparameter sensitivity), modeling constraint of single g block per split, and clarity/implementation reporting gaps.  
- Suggestions: compare directly to HRG and hierarchical IRM, report hardware/implementation and fair timing, analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters and restarts, quantify Sampson with NMI/ARI and add more benchmarks, discuss memory usage and scaling guidance, and include full pseudocode/supplement details.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject—technically sound and useful but insufficiently novel relative to existing hierarchical models and lacking key baselines and deeper experiments.
