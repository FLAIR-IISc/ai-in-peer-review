Summary and positioning
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy, Bayesian, agglomerative method for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model uses a tree to induce a prior over partitions (following Bayesian Rose Trees/Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering), and a stochastic blockmodel likelihood with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli terms for within- and between-community edges. For a fixed tree, the marginal likelihood over all tree-consistent partitions can be computed exactly via a dynamic program (Eq. 10). Inference builds the tree bottom-up by repeatedly merging a pair of subtrees, choosing among three merge types (join and two absorptions) using a local Bayes factor that depends only on data among the candidate leaves. Careful caching yields O(1) per inner-loop update and overall O(n^2 log n) time per run. A sparse variant disallows merging disconnected subgraphs. Link prediction is handled via a simple top-down recursion on the learned tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network (qualitative), a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset (predictive performance vs. IRM), and the full NIPS graph (qualitative) show that BHCD is much faster than IRM and achieves predictive performance similar to LFRM and below ILA as reported by prior work.

Relation to prior work
- The approach explicitly extends Bayesian Hierarchical Clustering/Bayesian Rose Trees [13,14] to relational data using a blockmodel likelihood and a tree-induced prior over partitions with the πS choice from [14].
- It compares experimentally to the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and situates itself relative to LFRM and ILA.
- However, closely related hierarchical network models such as the Hierarchical Random Graph (HRG; Clauset, Moore, Newman, Nature 2008) are not cited or compared, despite strong conceptual overlap (tree/dendrogram with edge probabilities governed by the LCA). Likewise, recent hierarchical IRM variants and Mondrian-process-based relational models are mentioned but not compared empirically.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Soundness: The probabilistic model is well specified. Using different Beta priors for within- vs. between-cluster edges captures assortativity with conjugacy for efficiency. The dynamic program for p(D|T) (Eq. 10) is correct given the tree-consistent partition prior and the block-aggregating g term for inter-child edges. The merge score (Eq. 12) follows from a consistent local Bayes factor criterion.
- Algorithmic details: The three merge operations (join/absorptions) mirror BRT and are appropriate. The caching formulas (Eq. 13) make the complexity claims plausible; the distinction between BHCD and the “sparse” variant is clear.
- Limitations/assumptions: At each split the model aggregates all across-child edges into a single g block (σ¬ch), effectively tying all inter-child edge probabilities at that split. This favors assortative community structure and reduces flexibility vs. a full SBM where each child pair can have distinct rates. The local merge score ignores effects outside the merged subgraph, so optimality is not guaranteed; multiple restarts partially mitigate this but no diagnostics or guarantees are provided.
- Empirics: Predictive comparisons to IRM (MCMC and greedy) on NIPS-234 are sensible and show clear speed gains. However, the fairness of wall-clock comparisons is hard to assess without hardware and implementation details, and IRM is not the most suitable hierarchical baseline. The comparisons to LFRM and ILA are indirect via previously reported numbers on the same folds; direct re-runs would be stronger. Sampson’s example is illustrative but lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., NMI vs. known groups). The full NIPS results are qualitative only.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with precise notation and clear derivations. The role of πS and the choice πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| are explained and tied to BRT.
- Minor clarity issues: “BHCD global” is referenced without definition; Algorithm 1 is referred to but not fully reproduced; memory complexity and implementation details (heap size, caching footprint) are not discussed; predictive recursion complexity is stated as Θ(n) but practically equals tree depth—worth clarifying.

Originality
- Conceptually, the paper combines known pieces—BHC/BRT-style tree priors over partitions and blockmodel likelihoods—with a greedy, cached agglomerative search. The specific dynamic program and local Bayes-factor merge scoring are sensible extensions to relational data.
- However, the overall modeling idea of a dendrogram-based probabilistic network model is close to HRG. The novelty is primarily algorithmic/engineering (exact marginal for tree-consistent partitions, merge operations with caching to achieve O(n^2 log n) per run) and pragmatic (fast, scalable heuristic inference), rather than a fundamentally new probabilistic formulation. The paper would benefit from a thorough differentiation from HRG and hierarchical IRM variants.

Significance
- Practical impact: The method is fast, simple, and provides a hierarchical summary useful for exploration and link prediction. The speedups over IRM are notable, and the sparse variant is attractive for large sparse graphs.
- Scientific impact: As presented, the empirical evidence is not sufficient to claim state-of-the-art predictive performance or superiority to existing hierarchical models; missing key baselines limit the strength of conclusions. Nonetheless, the approach could be a useful tool in the community due to its speed and simplicity.

Pros
- Clear, principled probabilistic model with exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions.
- Elegant, scalable greedy inference with effective caching; well-motivated merge operations.
- Strong speed advantages; reasonable predictive performance per unit time.
- Useful link prediction recursion and interpretable hierarchies.

Cons
- Missing comparison and discussion of HRG and other hierarchical SBMs; originality vs. these is underspecified.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: few datasets, largely qualitative for the largest graph, indirect comparisons to LFRM/ILA, no ablations or sensitivity to hyperparameters γ, α,β,δ,λ or number of restarts R.
- Modeling restriction of a single g block per split may underfit heterophilic or nuanced between-group patterns.
- Some clarity gaps (Algorithm 1 details, “BHCD global,” hardware/time reporting, memory complexity).

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Add direct comparisons to HRG (and, if feasible, to hierarchical IRM variants), both in link prediction and hierarchy quality.
- Provide hardware specs, implementation details, and report wall-clock vs. iterations fairly across methods; consider normalizing by operations if possible.
- Explore sensitivity to hyperparameters and R; consider learning γ (or a prior over πS).
- Quantify Sampson with standard metrics (NMI/ARI) vs. known groups; add at least one more benchmark network with ground truth communities and a large modern sparse graph to stress-test the sparse variant.
- Discuss memory usage of caching sufficient statistics and heap size; provide guidance for scaling beyond ~3k nodes.
- Clarify “BHCD global” mention and include full pseudocode (even in the supplement).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is technically sound, clear, and practically useful, with clear speed advantages. However, the originality relative to established hierarchical network models (notably HRG) is insufficiently articulated, and the empirical evaluation lacks key baselines and depth. With a strengthened related-work discussion, direct comparisons to HRG/hierarchical IRM, and more comprehensive experiments, this would be a strong submission. Confidence: medium.
