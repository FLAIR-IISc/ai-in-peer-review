Summary
This paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a fast, greedy Bayesian procedure for discovering hierarchical community structure in networks. The model places a prior over partitions that are consistent with a given tree, and marginalizes over all such partitions exactly via a dynamic programming recursion that generalizes Bayesian Rose Trees [Blundell et al.] to relational data. The likelihood is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels with conjugate Beta-Bernoulli terms for within- vs between-cluster edges, with a stopping probability πS at each internal node that biases toward non-binary trees. Inference is a bottom-up agglomerative procedure that considers three merge types (join, and two “absorption” options), scored by a local Bayes factor. With careful caching, per-merge updates are O(1) and the overall complexity is O(n^2 log n). A “sparse” variant prohibits merging disconnected subtrees. The method supports link prediction via a recursive mixture along the path in the tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery network and NIPS coauthorship (234 nodes and full dataset) show qualitative hierarchical structure and predictive performance comparable to or better than IRM at a tiny fraction of the time; performance is reportedly similar to LFRM and worse than ILA based on shared splits.

Relation to prior work
BHCD is positioned between flat blockmodel approaches (SBM, IRM) and hierarchical extensions (e.g., Herlau et al.’s hierarchical IRM, Mondrian process models) while leveraging the greedy Bayesian clustering ideas of Heller & Ghahramani and Bayesian Rose Trees. The novelty is in marrying a tree-consistent partition prior and dynamic programming recursion (Eq. 10) to a relational likelihood and an efficient agglomerative search with absorptions, yielding non-binary trees and strong scalability. Compared to nonparametric relational models like IRM/LFRM/ILA, BHCD trades modeling flexibility for speed and interpretability.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Extends Bayesian hierarchical clustering to relational data with an elegant prior over tree-consistent partitions and a clean recursion that permits exact marginalization over partitions induced by the tree.
- Scalability: O(n^2 log n) greedy inference with O(1) local updates is appealing; likelihood evaluation in O(n+m) is also attractive. The sparse variant is sensible and empirically faster.
- Practical utility: Produces interpretable non-binary hierarchies; absorption merges are a nice idea. Link prediction is straightforward and efficient.
- Empirics: On NIPS-234, achieves reasonable predictive performance orders of magnitude faster than IRM; qualitative structure on Sampson; a large-scale hierarchy on full NIPS.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: Comparisons are primarily to IRM (MCMC and greedy); results for LFRM/ILA are indirect via shared folds rather than head-to-head runs. No synthetic experiments to test recovery of known hierarchical structure or depth.
- Modeling limitations: Uses vanilla SBM without degree correction or edge covariates. This can hurt performance on networks with degree heterogeneity. The claim of generality to any exponential family is not demonstrated beyond Beta-Bernoulli.
- Heuristic elements: The sparse constraint (disallowing merges with zero edges) is a heuristic; tie-breaking with 50 random restarts suggests instability; there’s no uncertainty quantification over trees beyond selecting the best run.
- Hyperparameters and learning: γ and Beta hyperparameters are fixed; no hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis. πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| is reasonable but its impact is not explored.
- Theory: No guarantees on the greedy search (e.g., approximation to MAP tree), nor analysis of consistency. The assumption that the Bayes factor can be computed locally may be myopic in some structures.
- Clarity/minor: A few notational glitches and missing details (e.g., Algorithm 1 specifics, exact caching scheme). In Sec. 4.2, predictive complexity is said to be linear in depth but then stated as Θ(n); please clarify.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please provide direct comparisons (runtime and predictive metrics) against LFRM and ILA on the same hardware, or at least include re-runs with the public code if available, to substantiate the relative position more convincingly.
- Include synthetic benchmarks with known hierarchical ground truth to assess recovery (tree structure and depth) and sensitivity to noise and degree heterogeneity.
- Explore degree-corrected likelihoods or non-conjugate priors (as noted in the discussion) and report the trade-off in speed vs performance.
- Add hyperparameter learning (e.g., empirical Bayes for α,β,δ,λ and γ) or at least a sensitivity analysis; γ likely has strong influence on non-binarity.
- Report variability across the R restarts: distribution of likelihoods, predictive metrics, and structural stability (e.g., tree edit distance). Consider simple ensemble averaging for predictions (you already mention averaging R trees).
- Clarify: (i) the O(1) update mechanics more concretely; (ii) the discrepancy in predictive complexity (depth vs Θ(n)); (iii) the “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” phrasing; and (iv) how missing edges are handled in training/testing splits.
- Consider an ablation contrasting join-only vs join+absorption merges quantitatively (both accuracy and interpretability) and the effect of the “sparse” constraint.

Minor issues
- Several references lack years/venues; please standardize. Check [8], [11], and others for precise citations.
- Typographical issues and punctuation around equations and figures; ensure Algorithm 1 is fully specified.
- The assertion that the space of tree-consistent partitions is O(2^n) could be contextualized; the DP recursion is the key point.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and, in my view, novel contribution that opens a useful path: a principled yet very fast Bayesian approach to hierarchical community discovery with interpretable non-binary trees. While the model is simpler than state-of-the-art relational latent feature models and the experimental evaluation is not exhaustive, the idea and resulting algorithm are compelling and likely to be useful to the community. Given NeurIPS’ interest in scalable probabilistic modeling and hierarchical structure, I recommend acceptance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel formulation linking tree-consistent partitions to relational likelihood
- Very fast, simple, and interpretable; non-binary hierarchies via absorptions
- Strong scalability and promising empirical speed/quality

Cons against acceptance
- Limited baselines and empirical depth; no synthetic recovery study
- Simplistic likelihood (no degree correction), fixed hyperparameters
- Heuristic components and no theoretical guarantees

Recommendation: Accept (good novelty and practical impact despite some empirical and modeling limitations)
