

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and extend it to relational data, addressing limitations of existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches that scale poorly with network size. BHCD represents communities as a tree structure, with each internal node corresponding to a community and leaves representing individual vertices. The key contribution is an efficient greedy inference algorithm that computes the marginal likelihood of the tree using dynamic programming, with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), where n is the number of vertices. This represents a significant computational improvement over existing methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) and its extensions, which typically scale quadratically with the number of communities.

The paper relates well to prior work. It properly cites and differentiates from the stochastic blockmodel [1], mixed membership variants [2], and Bayesian hierarchical approaches [5-12]. The authors correctly identify that existing Bayesian hierarchical methods either require expensive inference (IRM extensions [8]) or are limited to specific tree structures (depth-2 hierarchies [9, 10] or fixed-depth hierarchies [11]), making BHCD's approach to efficient inference a meaningful contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model is well-defined, with clear mathematical formulation of the tree-consistent partitions, marginal likelihood, and inference algorithm. The dynamic programming approach for efficient computation is elegant and properly justified.

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that BHCD is two orders of magnitude faster than IRM while achieving comparable predictive performance. The analysis of time complexity (O(n² log n)) is thorough, and the comparison to other methods (Table 1) is appropriate.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on Sampson's monastery network, NIPS co-authorship data, and the full NIPS dataset provide compelling evidence of BHCD's effectiveness. The results demonstrate that BHCD discovers meaningful hierarchical structures that align with domain knowledge.

4. **Practical Utility:** The paper's focus on computational efficiency for large networks addresses a real need in network analysis. The sparse variant (BHCD sparse) specifically targets sparse networks, which are common in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Non-Bayesian Methods:** While the paper compares favorably to Bayesian methods, it doesn't benchmark against popular non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches (e.g., greedy agglomerative clustering [3, 4]), which might be faster but lack the probabilistic framework. This weakens the claim of "two orders of magnitude faster" without context.

2. **Binary vs. Non-Binary Trees:** The paper mentions that non-binary trees typically explain the data better (log likelihoods), but the discussion of interpretability trade-offs is superficial. The binary-only variants produce more coherent predictive results but less interpretable hierarchies. A more thorough analysis of when to use binary vs. non-binary trees would strengthen the paper.

3. **Tie-Breaking Mechanism:** The paper acknowledges that multiple merges can have identical scores (e.g., at the first iteration) and simply breaks ties randomly. This leads to inconsistent results across runs, which is problematic for reproducibility. While the authors suggest using multiple restarts, the paper doesn't provide evidence that this consistently yields better results.

4. **Scalability Claims:** The claim that BHCD "always terminates after a fixed number of steps" is slightly misleading. The number of steps is fixed (n-1 merges), but the computational cost per step depends on the graph structure. The paper should clarify that while the worst-case complexity is O(n² log n), the actual runtime depends on graph density.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a technically sound and computationally efficient approach to hierarchical community detection that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The method addresses a real problem with clear practical value, and the experimental results support the claims of improved efficiency without sacrificing predictive performance.

The primary weaknesses relate to insufficient comparison with non-Bayesian methods and underdeveloped discussion of the binary vs. non-binary tree trade-offs. These could be addressed with additional experiments and analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel efficient inference algorithm for Bayesian hierarchical community detection
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear probabilistic model
- Demonstrated computational advantage over state-of-the-art Bayesian methods
- Comprehensive experimental validation on multiple datasets
- Clear presentation of the relationship to prior work

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited comparison to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering methods
- Superficial discussion of binary vs. non-binary tree trade-offs
- Inconsistent results due to random tie-breaking without sufficient justification

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of community detection with a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically useful method. The weaknesses identified are important but not severe enough to outweigh the paper's strengths. The authors should address the points above in the camera-ready version to further strengthen the contribution.
