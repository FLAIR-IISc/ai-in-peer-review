### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a novel method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The core idea is to frame the problem as a Bayesian model selection task over the space of possible hierarchies (represented as trees or dendrograms). The authors adapt the Bayesian hierarchical clustering framework of Heller & Ghahramani (2005) to relational data by using a stochastic blockmodel (SBM) as the likelihood for partitions of the network. Inference is performed via a greedy bottom-up agglomerative algorithm, which iteratively merges sub-trees that provide the largest increase in the marginal likelihood of the data. The primary contribution is a fast and scalable alternative to more computationally intensive MCMC-based Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), offering comparable predictive performance at a fraction of the computational cost.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a compelling and novel synthesis of two established lines of work: Bayesian hierarchical clustering and stochastic blockmodels for network analysis. While greedy agglomerative clustering for networks is not new, casting it within a principled Bayesian model selection framework is a significant contribution. The resulting algorithm is not just another heuristic but is optimizing a well-defined objective function (the marginal likelihood of the tree structure).

2.  **Scalability and Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work is its computational efficiency. The authors clearly demonstrate that their method is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based inference for the IRM. The O(n^2 log n) complexity, which is independent of the number of inferred communities, is a major advantage over methods whose per-iteration cost depends on the number of clusters (K), which can be large. The time-vs-performance plots in Figure 4 are particularly effective at illustrating this key advantage. This makes the method practical for moderately large networks where MCMC might be infeasible.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of building up their model, starting from the standard SBM (Section 2), introducing the hierarchical extension and the recursive marginal likelihood calculation (Section 3), and finally detailing the greedy inference algorithm (Section 4). The mathematical notation is clear, and Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the procedure.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims.
    *   The qualitative analysis on Sampson's monastery network shows that the method produces interpretable and meaningful hierarchies that align with known social structures.
    *   The quantitative comparison on the NIPS-234 dataset against the IRM is thorough, using standard metrics (log predictive probability, accuracy, AUC). It clearly establishes the method's superior speed and competitive accuracy.
    *   Demonstrating the method on the full NIPS dataset showcases its scalability to larger, real-world problems.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limitations of Greedy Search:** The main methodological weakness is the greedy nature of the inference algorithm. Agglomerative clustering is known to be susceptible to local optima, where an early, suboptimal merge cannot be undone later. The authors acknowledge this and use random restarts as a mitigation strategy. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of this limitation. For instance, how variable are the results across different random restarts? Is there a risk of finding a hierarchy with high marginal likelihood that is qualitatively very different from and inferior to one found by a slower but more exhaustive method like MCMC?

2.  **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The experimental comparison is focused almost exclusively on the IRM. While the IRM is a key and highly relevant baseline, the field of hierarchical community detection is broader. The introduction mentions greedy methods like that of Clauset, Newman, and Moore (2004), which are also very fast. A comparison against such a method, even on a single metric like speed and a structural quality metric (e.g., modularity, though its limitations are known), would better position BHCD within the wider literature. It would help answer whether the Bayesian formulation provides benefits over faster, non-probabilistic heuristics beyond just providing a probabilistic output.

3.  **Fixed Hyperparameters:** The model's hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) are fixed across all experiments. The paper states that the chosen values "work well," but this lacks rigor. The performance of Bayesian models can be sensitive to hyperparameter settings. A sensitivity analysis, even a brief one in an appendix, would significantly increase the confidence in the results and the robustness of the method. Alternatively, a discussion on potential empirical Bayes or full Bayesian approaches to setting these parameters would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Model Assumptions:** The model generates a strict, non-overlapping hierarchy. Each node belongs to exactly one community at any given level of the tree. This is a strong assumption, as real-world networks often feature individuals with mixed memberships in multiple communities (as acknowledged by the mention of the mixed-membership SBM). A discussion of this limitation and the types of network structures the model might fail to capture would be appropriate in the Discussion section.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed BHCD method is a clever and practical combination of existing ideas that results in a highly efficient algorithm for a significant problem. The claims of speed and comparable accuracy are well-supported by solid experimental evidence. While the greedy nature of the inference is a limitation, the authors' approach is principled, and the resulting scalability makes it a very attractive tool for practitioners and researchers. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for future work and do not detract from the core contribution of this paper. It is well-suited for the NIPS audience and is likely to be influential.
