This paper introduces a Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method for finding community structure in social networks. The core idea is to combine Bayesian approaches with greedy hierarchical clustering, building a tree-based model where each node represents a community. The authors aim to achieve a balance between the probabilistic rigor of Bayesian methods and the scalability of greedy algorithms. The method is compared to IRM and other relevant approaches in terms of speed and predictive performance on several datasets, including Sampson’s monastery network and a subset of the NIPS co-authorship network.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the existing literature on community detection, highlighting the limitations of both flat and hierarchical approaches. The mathematical formulation of the model, particularly the use of stochastic blockmodels and the hierarchical extension, is reasonably clear. The description of the greedy inference procedure (Algorithm 1) is detailed, and the explanation of the caching scheme for efficiency is commendable. The experimental results are presented in a comprehensive manner, with quantitative comparisons against other methods.  The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of greedy hierarchical clustering with a Bayesian framework is a valuable contribution. While both components are known, integrating them effectively to achieve scalability and probabilistic modeling is novel.
*   **Scalability:** The reported O(n<sup>2</sup> log n) complexity, along with the efficient caching scheme, is a significant advantage over many existing Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection. The reported speed-up compared to IRM is impressive.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the model. The figures and tables are helpful in understanding the results.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors provide a detailed description of the model, the inference procedure, and the experimental setup. They also consider variations of the algorithm (e.g., sparse version, binary trees) and compare them comprehensively.
* **Well-contextualized:** The paper thoroughly reviews the existing landscape of community detection algorithms, including those based on stochastic blockmodels, IRM, and Mondrian processes, correctly positioning their contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) is not extensively discussed.  A more thorough investigation of the impact of these parameters on the results would be beneficial. The justification for the chosen values is brief.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the complexity analysis is provided, a deeper theoretical understanding of the convergence properties of the greedy algorithm would be valuable.  The authors acknowledge that the greedy nature may lead to suboptimal solutions.
*   **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:**  While IRM is used as a baseline, more recent and competitive methods for hierarchical community detection, beyond those cited (e.g., recent deep learning approaches), could be considered for a more robust comparison.  The comparison to LFRM and ILA is good, but a broader range of baselines could further strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Interpretability of Hierarchies:** The paper acknowledges the challenges in interpreting the discovered hierarchies, particularly for the full NIPS dataset. Providing tools or guidelines for visualizing and understanding these hierarchies would be helpful.
* **Lack of Discussion on Model Assumptions**: The method relies on the SBM as its building block. Discussing the limitations of this assumption, and potential issues arising from the real-world networks not perfectly conforming to this model, would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
*   The method achieves significant speed-up compared to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods while maintaining competitive performance.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and thoroughly evaluated.
*   The work addresses a significant problem in network analysis and has the potential to be used by other researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of deeper theoretical analysis are weaknesses that could be addressed with further work.
*   The comparison with stronger baselines and a more in-depth discussion of the model assumptions would further strengthen the paper.
* While the speed-up is significant, the algorithm's core complexity remains quadratic, potentially limiting scalability for extremely large networks.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of community detection. The proposed method is novel, scalable, and well-supported by experimental results. While some weaknesses remain, they do not overshadow the overall quality and significance of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and a more comprehensive comparison to related work.
