Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering algorithm tailored to network data. The model places a tree prior over partitions, akin to Bayesian Rose Trees (BRT), but with a likelihood based on a stochastic blockmodel with separate conjugate priors for within-community and between-community edges (Beta-Bernoulli). The marginal likelihood of a tree is computed via a dynamic-programming recursion over the tree that mixes “on-diagonal” and “off-diagonal” block contributions. Inference is agglomerative bottom-up: at each step, two trees are merged to maximize a local Bayes factor, considering “join” and “absorption” moves (as in BRT). A “sparse” variant forbids merging components with zero inter-edges. Predictive link probabilities are computed by a top-down recursion on the tree. Experiments on Sampson’s monastery, a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset, and the full NIPS graph (2864 nodes) suggest reasonable hierarchies and faster-than-IRM wall-clock times, with predictive performance similar to LFRM, worse than ILA, and better than IRM (per authors’ report).

Relation to prior work
The approach is a clear adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering/Bayesian Rose Trees [Heller & Ghahramani 2005; Blundell et al. 2010] to relational data via blockmodel likelihoods. It contrasts with the (hierarchical) IRM and related nonparametric network models (e.g., LFRM, ILA, degree-corrected SBMs) by favoring a greedy model-selection procedure with tree-constrained partitions and conjugate marginal likelihoods. The paper claims substantially better scalability than MCMC/variational methods that perform many small updates.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, computationally attractive adaptation of BRT to networks, with a clean recursion for tree marginal likelihood and local Bayes-factor merge scores.
- Reasonable scalability and practical performance; updates are local and cacheable; the approach is straightforward to implement.
- The predictive recursion is efficient (linear in depth) and interpretable.
- The idea of separate on/off-diagonal conjugate priors is natural for assortative community detection and computationally convenient.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/methodological
1) Novelty vs prior work: The core inference and merge machinery follow BRT closely; the main novelty is plugging in a blockmodel likelihood and defining the off-diagonal term. The paper could better delineate what is genuinely new theoretically (beyond an application of BRT to a different likelihood and some caching). A formal comparison to [14] is missing.

2) Complexity claims: 
- The O(n + m) claim for computing p(D|T) via (10) needs a precise derivation/algorithmic sketch (what is precomputed, how σSS and σ¬chSS are updated across the tree, and how to avoid double counting in undirected graphs). 
- The agglomeration phase requires maintaining σIJ for all pairs in the forest; memory and time to initialize/update these statistics likely scale as O(n^2). This is not discussed, yet is critical for large graphs. 
- The “two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM” claim is puzzling and likely misstated (an iteration of IRM is already relatively cheap).

3) Model assumptions:
- The separate Beta priors for within/between edges bake in assortativity. The method may struggle on disassortative or core-periphery networks; this limitation is not discussed nor tested.
- The statement “generalise to any exponential family” is inaccurate unless conjugacy is maintained; otherwise the recursion and closed-form integrals break.
- πS = 1 − (1 − γ)^{|ch(S)|} requires γ ∈ (0,1); this is not stated, and γ is fixed without sensitivity analysis or inference. The choice is crucial for the bias toward non-binary trees.

4) BHCD-sparse variant: Forbidding merges between components with zero inter-edges is an algorithmic convenience but changes the model class and may bias inference (e.g., forbidding latent communities weakly connected through higher levels). No theoretical or empirical justification beyond speed is provided.

5) Predictive recursion: The text says prediction is “linear in the depth of the tree” and then concludes “Hence Θ(n)”, which is inconsistent. It should be O(depth), worst-case O(n).

6) Derivations and notation:
- Equation (2): for undirected graphs, clarify whether products are over p ≤ q to avoid double counting; currently σpq = σqp is mentioned but the indexing remains unclear.
- Equation (12): Provide a short derivation from (11); confirm the denominator’s g(σIJ) term is exactly the contribution removed by merging.
- The dynamic programming recursion (10) is central but not proved; a brief inductive argument would help.
- Merging operations (“join,” “absorb”) should be specified with precise tree manipulations and how σ¬ch updates follow (13). Algorithm 1 is referenced but not provided.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: Only IRM is run; comparisons to LFRM/ILA are indirect (reused folds but different implementations/hardware). Provide direct runs and quantitative tables with mean±std of AUC/accuracy/log predictive across folds, and statistical tests.
- Hyperparameters: α=δ=1.0, β=λ=0.2, γ=0.4 are fixed with no sensitivity analysis or learning; performance may be sensitive.
- Runtime reporting: Specify hardware, implementation details (single-thread vs parallel), memory consumption, and wall-clock per run including the R restarts (R=50).
- Sampson: Results are mostly qualitative; provide ARI/NMI against known groups and calibrated predictive metrics.
- Full NIPS: The ad hoc tree cut criterion rT ∏(1−rA) > 0.5 is not justified; no evaluation beyond visualization.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos/grammar: e.g., “Physics Review E”→“Physical Review E”; “Erix P. Xing”; “wherem n2”; subject-verb agreement (“The Mondrian process [12] propose…”). Many references are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), inconsistent formatting, and some dangling punctuation. Please clean up.
- “BHCD global” appears in the Sampson discussion but is never defined earlier. Binary vs non-binary variants are not crisply formalized.
- Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown; include pseudocode.
- Notation sometimes shifts between uppercase/lowercase p/P.

Pros
- Elegant, fast, and practical greedy Bayesian approach for hierarchical community discovery.
- Clear linkage to BRT with a sensible network likelihood.
- Promising speed-accuracy trade-offs; useful for large graphs.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond adapting BRT machinery; missing proofs/derivations for key claims.
- Weak experimental rigor and baselines; limited metrics and ablations; unclear runtime/memory reporting.
- Several methodological choices (γ fixed, BHCD-sparse constraint) are unjustified.
- Poor polish: typos, incomplete references, missing algorithm details.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The idea is interesting and practically valuable, but the paper needs substantially stronger theoretical treatment (proofs, clarity, and precise complexity analysis), more rigorous and fair empirical comparisons (with full metrics, ablations, hyperparameter studies, and runtime/memory reporting), and significant improvements to clarity and presentation. With these addressed, the work could be competitive in a future submission.
