Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method adapted to relational data. The model places a tree prior over hierarchical communities and, for a fixed tree, marginalizes over all tree-consistent partitions using a dynamic program, yielding a closed-form marginal likelihood when using conjugate Beta–Bernoulli block models with separate priors for within- and between-community edges. Inference proceeds via bottom-up agglomeration with a local Bayes factor scoring function that considers three merge types (join or absorptions), with cached sufficient statistics enabling O(1) update per candidate and overall O(n^2 log n) runtime. A sparse variant prohibits merges with no inter-tree edges. The method provides predictive link probabilities by traversing the hierarchy and mixing on-diagonal and off-diagonal posteriors.

Relative to prior work, BHCD connects Bayesian hierarchical clustering (e.g., rose trees) to blockmodels, offering a scalable alternative to fully Bayesian nonparametric models like the IRM and its hierarchical extensions, as well as depth-limited hierarchical models and the Mondrian process approach. It aims to fill a gap between probabilistic hierarchical models with expensive inference and fast but non-probabilistic hierarchical clustering.

Empirically, the paper presents: (i) a qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery data; (ii) predictive performance and speed on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset, comparing to IRM (both MCMC and greedy); and (iii) a visualization of the top of the hierarchy for the full NIPS 1–17 network.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation: A principled mixture over tree-consistent partitions with conjugate marginalization and an interpretable tree prior.
- Efficient dynamic programming for p(D|T) and careful caching for fast greedy inference. The local merge score is data-local and computationally inexpensive.
- Practical scalability: O(n^2 log n) inference with constants small enough to handle thousands of nodes; the sparse variant is empirically faster on sparse graphs.
- Competitive predictive accuracy per wall-clock time: on NIPS-234, BHCD variants achieve useful predictions orders of magnitude faster than IRM.
- Useful predictive machinery: link prediction via hierarchical responsibilities; mixture of f/g terms is intuitive.
- Non-binary trees increase interpretability in some cases; the absorb operations add useful flexibility beyond strictly binary agglomeration.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Degree heterogeneity is not modeled (plain Beta–Bernoulli SBM). This can bias community discovery and hurt prediction compared to degree-corrected or attribute/latent feature models; it likely explains the performance gap to ILA.
- The local Bayes factor merge criterion is heuristic relative to the full model evidence over all trees; there is no analysis of consistency or approximation error introduced by locality. No ablation on the impact of allowing absorptions vs joins on recovery/accuracy.
- The claim that the predictive computation is Θ(n) contradicts the preceding statement that it is linear in tree depth; it should be O(depth), which is typically O(log n) for balanced trees but could be O(n) in worst-case degenerate trees.
- Hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) are fixed ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter inference is provided, despite their clear influence on density biases and tree arity.
- Experimental evaluation lacks synthetic benchmarks with known hierarchical ground truth to assess structural recovery, not only link prediction.
- Comparisons are limited: IRM is a dated baseline for flat partitions. Missing are hierarchical SBMs and degree-corrected/nested SBMs (e.g., hierarchical/nested SBM), HRG-style models, or modern scalable community detection with hierarchical structure. No comparison to strong non-Bayesian baselines (e.g., Louvain/Infomap with hierarchical variants).
- Sampson experiment uses negative ties as “absent edges,” conflating antagonism with non-observed ties; this choice merits justification or robustness analysis.

Clarity
- Overall presentation is clear, but some notation could be tightened (e.g., σ¬chSS and its updates), and the DP derivation (Eq. 10) would benefit from a short proof sketch.
- The complexity discussion mixes likelihood evaluation O(n+m) with inference O(n^2 log n); the distinction and practical constant factors could be made crisper.
- Figures: the paper refers to multiple figures and a table; quantitative values (wall-clock times, hardware, memory) should be reported explicitly in the main text.

Originality
- The main novelty is adapting Bayesian rose-tree ideas and DP marginalization to a blockmodel likelihood with on/off-diagonal conjugate terms, plus the specific local merge scoring and caching for efficiency. This is incremental relative to prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering and relational models, but still a useful design contribution.
- The prior p(φ|T) with πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| is borrowed from rose trees; the contribution is in applying it to relational blocks and working out the DP. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- As a fast, interpretable baseline for hierarchical community discovery with probabilistic predictions, BHCD is practical. However, the lack of degree correction and limited comparisons constrain its impact relative to current state-of-the-art hierarchical network models.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to γ and to the within/between Beta hyperparameters? Can you learn them (e.g., via empirical Bayes or MCMC over γ)?
- What is the worst-case and typical depth of learned trees, and the actual time for single-edge prediction?
- How robust is BHCD-sparse when true communities have weak, few inter-node edges (risk of missing merges)?
- Can the method handle weighted or directed networks straightforwardly? How would σ and f/g change?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add degree-correction (e.g., node-specific propensities or DC-SBM likelihood) and compare.
- Include synthetic hierarchical benchmarks with known structure to quantify recovery (ARI/NMI at multiple cuts); report uncertainty over R restarts.
- Provide hyperparameter sensitivity and, ideally, hyperparameter inference.
- Compare against hierarchical/nested SBMs and HRG-style models, and hierarchical variants of Louvain/Infomap; add a modularity-based hierarchical clustering baseline.
- Clarify the predictive complexity (O(depth)); report wall-clock and memory with hardware details; add ablations for join-only vs absorptions and for the sparse constraint.
- Consider modestly richer likelihoods (e.g., non-conjugate priors via Laplace/VI) and triangle-based sufficient statistics as discussed.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a clear, efficient, and useful adaptation of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to blockmodels with solid engineering and promising speed/accuracy trade-offs. However, the methodological novelty is incremental, the evaluation is limited (missing key modern hierarchical baselines and structural recovery analyses), and important modeling aspects (degree heterogeneity, hyperparameter learning) are not addressed. With stronger empirical comparisons, robustness analyses, and modest modeling enhancements, this work could be competitive for a future submission.
