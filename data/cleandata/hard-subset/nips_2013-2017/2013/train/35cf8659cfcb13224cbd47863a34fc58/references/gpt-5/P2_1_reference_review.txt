Summary
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method for networks. The model defines a hierarchy T over vertices and a prior over tree-consistent partitions, assigning on-diagonal and off-diagonal marginal likelihoods (conjugate beta–Bernoulli in the main instantiation). A dynamic programming recursion computes the marginal likelihood p(D|T) in linear time in the number of edges given T. Inference builds T greedily by agglomeration with a local Bayes factor score that considers three merge types (join and two absorptions). Variants include a “sparse” constraint disallowing merges between disconnected components. Experiments show qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery data, link prediction performance on a 234-node NIPS coauthorship subset compared to IRM (and to LFRM/ILA via published results), and a qualitative hierarchy on the full NIPS 1–17 network. The method is claimed to be orders of magnitude faster than IRM with worst-case O(n^2 log n) time and independence of the number of communities.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on stochastic blockmodels and the IRM; it adapts Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data by replacing i.i.d. point likelihoods with block sufficient statistics and on/off-diagonal marginal likelihoods. It is closely related to hierarchical network models such as hierarchical random graphs (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008) and to the Mondrian process for tree priors; hierarchical IRM variants and multiscale SBMs are also discussed. The paper appears to miss the HRG literature, which is conceptually very close: a dendrogram with link probabilities at internal nodes and MCMC inference. The novelty here is a conjugate marginal likelihood with a tree-consistent partition prior enabling an efficient dynamic program and a fast greedy agglomeration.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic setup and the recursion (Eq. 10) are plausible extensions of Bayesian rose trees to blockmodels. The prior over partitions p(φ|T) and the factorization of p(D|φ, T) are clearly specified. The Bayes factor used for merging relies on the forest decomposition so that only local terms change; this is reasonable given Eq. 11. The sufficient statistics caching and O(1) inner-loop updates are laid out.
- Claims vs. support: The complexity claims are argued (inference O(n^2 log n), likelihood O(n+m)), and the empirical runtime advantage over IRM is shown. However, important details are missing: no space complexity, no proof sketch of the DP recursion, and only limited analysis of convergence/stability (tie-breaking is handled by restarts).
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are limited. The main quantitative comparison is to IRM on one dataset; LFRM/ILA comparisons are indirect via published numbers rather than re-running baselines. No comparisons to HRG or modern nested SBMs. No sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) or ablations of the three merge types. The “sparse” variant is somewhat ad hoc (a hard constraint) and its effect is only qualitatively discussed. Link prediction performance is promising but lacks statistical tests.
- Minor technical issue: In Sec. 4.2, the predictive computation is said to be Θ(n); it seems linear in tree depth, typically O(depth) ≤ O(n), and often much smaller. Please clarify.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The model, recursion, and algorithm are described clearly with explicit equations and careful notation for sufficient statistics. Figures (not included here) are referenced appropriately. A few notational/terminology issues should be cleaned up (e.g., “BHCD global” appears without prior definition). The relationship to prior hierarchical graph models (especially HRG) needs to be clarified and contrasted. Reproducibility would benefit from more implementation details and making code available.

Originality
Moderate. The contribution is a novel and useful combination: bringing Bayesian rose-tree ideas to relational data with a conjugate blockmodel likelihood and a tailored greedy agglomeration. However, the high-level idea of tree-structured priors for hierarchical communities is not new (HRG, Mondrian, hierarchical IRM). The main novelty is the specific prior/likelihood factorization enabling an exact DP for p(D|T) and a fast merge score in the relational setting.

Significance
Moderate. The method offers strong practical value: scalable hierarchical community discovery with competitive link prediction at a fraction of the compute cost of IRM. The ability to handle large networks and produce interpretable hierarchies is attractive. That said, without stronger empirical comparisons to HRG/nested SBMs and broader datasets, it is hard to conclude a clear advance in state-of-the-art modeling performance, though the scalability improvement is meaningful.

Pros
- Clear, principled model with exact marginal likelihood over tree-consistent partitions.
- Elegant and fast greedy inference with local Bayes factors and cached sufficient statistics.
- Strong runtime advantage; complexity independent of number of communities.
- Interpretable hierarchies; practical “sparse” variant for large sparse graphs.

Cons
- Missing comparison and discussion of closely related HRG and nested SBM work.
- Limited experimental scope; indirect comparisons to some baselines; no significance tests.
- No hyperparameter inference or sensitivity analysis; ad hoc constraint in sparse variant.
- Theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency, approximation quality of greedy search) are not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and compare empirically to HRG and nested/ hierarchical SBMs on standard benchmarks.
- Include sensitivity analyses for γ, α,β,δ,λ, and ablate join vs. absorption merges.
- Provide proofs or at least derivation sketches for Eq. 10 and the O(1) update claims; report memory usage.
- Clarify predictive complexity (depth vs. n) and the effect of averaging over restarts on overfitting.
- Consider simple hyperparameter inference (e.g., gamma priors on α,β,δ,λ) and directed/weighted extensions.

Overall assessment
Quality: good; Clarity: good; Originality: moderate; Significance: moderate. This is a solid and practically useful contribution on scalable hierarchical community discovery, but the empirical evaluation and positioning vs. closely related hierarchical network models need strengthening. I lean toward a borderline/weak reject in its current form, with a clear path to acceptance after additional comparisons and analysis.
